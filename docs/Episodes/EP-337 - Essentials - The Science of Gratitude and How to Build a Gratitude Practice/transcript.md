## [00:00:00] Gratitude

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today, we are talking all about the science of gratitude. There's now a wealth of data showing that having an effective gratitude practice can impact a huge number of health variables, both mental health and physical health, in positive ways.

## [00:00:30] Gratitude Practice Benefits

However, in researching this episode, I was completely surprised as to what constitutes an effective gratitude practice. I think, like many of you, would've thought that an effective gratitude practice simply involves writing down a few things or many things that we're grateful for, or thinking about those, or really making an effort to somaticize or feel some of the elements of gratitude while writing out that list or thinking about that list. It turns out that an effective gratitude practice doesn't resemble that at all. The neuroimaging data, the physiological data looking at things like inflammatory markers, other studies purely looking at the psychology, and the long and short-term effects of an effective gratitude practice, point to a completely different approach to using gratitude to positively impact health metrics.

There are studies showing that performing a gratitude practice twice or three times, or even just once a week, can lead to a pervasive, long-lasting impact on subjective well-being. People report feeling happier, more meaning, joy, even awe for their life experience, simply in response to adding a gratitude practice. There are also studies showing that a regular gratitude practice can provide resilience to trauma in two ways: it can provide a reframing and resilience to prior traumatic experiences, buffering people against the negative physiological and psychological effects of earlier trauma, but also inoculating them in many ways to any traumas that might arrive later in life.

The other thing that a gratitude practice does is it's been shown to benefit social relationships — not just for the relationship in which you express gratitude. A regular gratitude practice can enhance one's social relationships across the board: in the workplace, at school, with family, in romantic relationships, and even one's relationship to themselves, which is really what the subjective feelings of wellbeing are. And for those who are coming to this conversation thinking that a gratitude practice is kind of wishy-washy or woo — buckle up, because the data actually point to the fact that a gratitude practice is a very potent way in which you can steer your mental and physical health in positive directions, and those effects are very long-lasting.

## [00:03:22] Pro-Social vs Defensive Behaviors, Gratitude

Before we dive into the tools and mechanisms and scientific studies around gratitude, I'd like to just set the framework for the discussion. Gratitude is what we call a "prosocial behavior" or a "prosocial mindset." Prosocial behaviors are basically any behavior or mode of thinking that allow us to be more effective in interactions with other people, including ourselves. There are actually neural circuits in the brain that are specifically wired for prosocial thoughts and behaviors. We have circuits in the brain that are what we call "appetitive" — they are designed to bring us closer to things and to bring us into closer relation to the details of that sensory experience, whether that's a delicious food, a loved one, a friend, or even our relation to ourselves.

The neural circuits in the brain associated with aversive or defensive behaviors are actually antagonized — meaning they are reduced — when the prosocial circuits are more active. So the framework here is that we have this kind of seesaw of neural circuits in the brain: one set that are prosocial and designed to bring us closer to others and to certain sensory experiences, and another set, the defensive circuits, that involve areas of the brain associated with fear, freezing, or backing up. Gratitude falls under this category of prosocial behaviors, which are designed to bring us closer to different types of things and to enhance the level of detail that we extract from those experiences.

The key thing for today's discussion is that gratitude turns out to be one of the most potent wedges by which we can shift the balance between these two circuits, giving more weight to the side associated with positive prosocial feelings. And what's really beautiful about gratitude practices is that if they're performed repeatedly — and not even that often, but repeatedly — one can actually shift their neural circuits such that the prosocial side can start to dominate the physiology and mindset in ways that can enhance many aspects of physical and mental health, by default. So we don't always have to constantly be in practice trying to be happy.

## [00:06:25] Serotonin, Brain & Context; Gratitude Mindset

Now I'd like to talk about some of the neurochemistry and neural circuits associated with gratitude and prosocial behaviors. Neuromodulators are chemicals released in the brain and body that change the activity of other neural circuits — making certain brain areas more likely to be active, and other brain areas less likely to be active. These neuromodulators have names like dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, epinephrine, and so on. The main neuromodulator associated with gratitude and prosocial behaviors tends to be serotonin. Serotonin is released from a very small collection of neurons in the brainstem called the "raphe nucleus," and those neurons send axons out to numerous places in the brain, tending to increase the activity of neural circuits that lend themselves to more approach toward particular types of experiences.

Two main brain areas are activated by these serotonergic systems when people experience gratitude, and the amount of activation scales with how intensely the person experienced the feeling of gratitude. Those two areas are the "anterior cingulate cortex" and the "medial prefrontal cortex." Many of you have probably heard of the medial prefrontal cortex because this is the area of the brain involved in planning, deep thinking, and evaluation of different types of experiences — past, present, or future. The reason it's especially important for gratitude is that the medial prefrontal cortex sets context — it literally defines the meaning of your experience.

If you were to deliberately place yourself into an ice bath, it would be uncomfortable. However, if you are doing it because you want to, or because you have knowledge that there are particular health benefits, the medial prefrontal cortex can then control areas of your deeper brain — like the hypothalamus — to positively impact the neurochemicals released into your system. Your knowledge that you are making the choice, that it's you deciding to put yourself through this discomfort, has been shown to create a very different and positive effect on things like dopamine and anti-inflammatory markers, compared to if someone pushes you into an ice bath or if you are doing it because someone insists that you do it and you really don't want to.

If you take a mouse and it runs on a running wheel — which mice really like to do — there are many positive effects on reducing blood pressure, improvements in neurochemistry, et cetera. However, if there's a mouse in the cage next to it that's trapped in the running wheel and it has to run every time the other mouse runs because the wheels are linked, the second mouse experiences negative shifts in overall health metrics: blood pressure goes up, stress hormones go up, because it's not actually making the choice. The medial prefrontal cortex is the switch that can take one experience and allow us to frame it such that it creates positive health effects — and the exact same experience framed as something we don't want to do can create negative health effects. Gratitude is a mindset that activates the prefrontal cortex, and in doing so, sets the context of your experience such that you can derive tremendous health benefits.

## [00:12:21] Context, Can You Lie to Yourself?

What kind of gratitude practice is going to accomplish this? You can't simply lie to yourself. You can't simply say, "Oh, well, every experience is a learning experience," or, "A terrible thing happened — I'm just going to say it's good," and have your body react as if it's good for you. That's a myth — and frankly, it's a myth that's fairly pervasive in the self-help and self-actualization literature. We can't simply lie to ourselves or "fake it until we make it." Neural circuitry is very powerful and very plastic. It can be modified, and it's very context-dependent, but it's not stupid. And when you lie to yourself about whether or not an experience is actually good for you, your brain knows.

## [00:13:06] Effective Gratitude Practice, Tools: Receiving Gratitude, Story

So what does an effective gratitude practice look like? Well, let's examine what a poor gratitude practice looks like, because therein lies some really important information — including the fact that I, and I think millions of other people out there, are doing it wrong. Most gratitude practices that you see online involve something like writing down, or reciting, or thinking about five, or ten, or twenty things that you're especially grateful for, and then really trying to feel into some of those. Most studies actually point to the fact that that style of gratitude practice is not particularly effective in shifting your neural circuitry, your neural chemistry, or your somatic circuitry — because you literally have organs and neural circuits that need to be activated toward enhanced activation of the prefrontal cortex and these prosocial neural networks.

It turns out that the most potent form of gratitude practice is not a gratitude practice where you give gratitude or express gratitude, but rather where you receive gratitude — where you receive thanks. There are a number of studies about this now. One in particular is called "Prefrontal activation while listening to a letter of gratitude read aloud by a coworker face-to-face: A NIRS study." In this experiment, coworkers wrote a letter of gratitude to another coworker unbeknownst to them, then sat down together as the letter was read aloud, and brain activity was imaged as the letter was being heard and received. It showed very robust effects on the prefrontal networks, pointing to the fact that receiving gratitude is actually much more potent — in terms of the positive shifts it can create — than giving gratitude.

For many people who want to experience the positive effects of gratitude, it's probably not the most advantageous approach to just sit around waiting, hoping that someone's going to deliver all these letters or words of gratitude. That brings us to the important work of Antonio Damasio, who explored the neural correlates of gratitude to define the areas of the brain associated with prosocial behaviors like the prefrontal cortex. Rather than have people express gratitude, they had subjects go into a scanner — so their brains are being imaged — and they watched narratives, stories about other people experiencing positive things in their life. In this case, these were powerful stories about survivors of genocide.

The subjects were not survivors of genocide — they were watching videotapes of people that had survived genocide, with others helping them along the way as part of their story of survival. Within these stories, there was a conveyance of a lot of struggle, but also small, highly significant features of their history that had led to their own feelings of gratitude. For instance, someone had been sick for weeks, and then another prisoner who was a doctor finds a particular medicine and literally saves her life. Just hearing this in the context of nothing but a scientific paper, it's probably not that impactful — but what's really important about this study is that these stories of other people receiving things that were powerful for them are embedded in story, and the human brain is so oriented towards story.

We have neural circuits that like to link together past, present, future — different characters, protagonists and antagonists. From the time we're very young until the time we're very old, story is one of the major ways that we organize information in the brain. The human subject in this scientific study is watching these powerful stories, and the neural circuits associated with prosocial behaviors and with gratitude become robustly active when they start to feel some affiliation with the person telling the story. In many ways, the subjects are receiving a sense of gratitude through the narrative of one of these other subjects. To really activate these circuits for gratitude, the serotonin and probably the oxytocin system as well, and their prefrontal networks, one has to powerfully associate with the idea of receiving help.

## [00:19:36] Tool: Find & Revisit Inspirational Stories

Let's take a moment and start to think about how we are going to build out the ultimate gratitude practice. It's very clear that receiving gratitude is powerful, but it's also very clear that waiting around to receive that gratitude is an impractical approach. What we know for sure is that there has to be a real experience of somebody else's experience, and that the best way to do that is story. So in thinking about how to build out an effective gratitude practice, it's very worthwhile to find someone's narrative that's powerful for you — a story that inspires you because of the beauty of the human spirit or the ability of humans to help other humans.

The circuits for gratitude are such that we can exchange gratitude — we can actually observe someone else getting help, someone else giving help, and that observation of our species doing that for one another allows us to experience the feeling of a genuine chemical and neural circuit activation lift. How would you do this? People digest stories in a number of ways: they watch movies, listen to podcasts, read books. An effective gratitude practice has to be repeated from time to time, so what I would not suggest is that we build a protocol in which you're constantly foraging for inspirational stories over and over again. Rather, the most effective protocol is to either think into — and you could write this out — when somebody was thankful for something that you did, and really start to think about how you felt in receiving that gratitude, or imagining or thinking deeply about the emotional experience of somebody else receiving help.

The story that you select does not have to have any semblance to your own life experience — it's just about what happens to move you. The way one could do this is to find a story that's particularly meaningful for you, take some short bullet-point notes — just list out what the struggle was, what the help was, and something about how that impacts you emotionally — and then read those out and think into the richness of that experience. This could be done literally for one minute, or two minutes, or three minutes. This is not an extensively long practice. If you have an experience or a story that's very potent for you, it becomes a sort of shortcut into the gratitude network, into these prosocial networks, where the activation of these circuits becomes almost instantaneous.

There's another very clear and positive effect of using this narrative or story-based approach: what story does for our physiology. Having a story that you can return to over and over again — even just the shorthand bullet-point version — will create a perceptible and real shift in your heartbeat and in your breathing. An effective gratitude practice is one that can rapidly shift not just the activation of the circuits in your brain for prosocial behaviors, but also the activation of particular circuits in your heart and lungs and the other organs of your body such that you can get into a reproducible state of gratitude each time. The key thing is that you want to use the same story — whether it's your own experience or somebody else's — and keep coming back to it over and over again. That makes it a very potent tool that you can get a tremendous amount of benefit from with even as short as 60 seconds of practice.

## [00:24:19] Heartfelt Intention, Genuine Thanks

There's a really interesting study published in "Scientific Reports," which is a Nature Research journal. The title of it is "Neural responses to intention and benefit appraisal are critical in distinguishing gratitude and joy." Basically, what they did is they used functional magnetic resonance imaging so they could look at brain circuitry activation with very high precision, and they had people receiving money in the context of this experiment. They had subjects with some knowledge as to whether or not the money they were receiving was given to them wholeheartedly or reluctantly. They looked at whether or not the sense of gratitude scaled with the amount of money received and/or the intention of the benefactor.

What's remarkable is that while the amount of money given was a strong component in whether or not somebody felt that they had received gratitude — which makes sense — the stronger variable, the bigger impact, came from whether or not the person giving the money was doing it with a wholehearted intention versus a reluctant intention. This tells us that genuine thanks are what count. You can't tell yourself that an experience was great, or that it justified the receipt of gratitude, if you think the other person gave it reluctantly. And that tells us that if we are the giver, we'd better be giving wholeheartedly, or we are undermining the sense of gratitude that someone is going to receive from us.

## [00:26:31] Gratitude Practice Benefits for Anxiety, Fear & Motivation

We are gradually building up the ultimate gratitude practice based on the variety of scientific literature that's out there. I want to turn our attention to a really interesting study entitled "Effects of gratitude meditation on neural network functional connectivity and brain-heart coupling." To make a long story short, repeated gratitude practice changes the way that your brain circuits work, and it also changes the way in which your heart and your brain interact. You're familiar with the fact that your brain controls your heart — you could be stressed about something perceived with your brain and then your heart rate will speed up. You're also probably familiar with the fact that if your heart rate speeds up for some reason, you'll think, "What's making my heart rate speed up?" — because the brain and the heart are reciprocally innervated; they're talking to one another in both directions.

This study looked at changes in so-called "functional connectivity" within the brain and between the brain and the heart in response to gratitude practices. What they found is that a repeated gratitude practice could change the resting state functional connectivity in emotion and motivation-related brain regions. A regular gratitude practice could shift the functional connectivity of emotion pathways in ways that made anxiety and fear circuits less likely to be active, and circuits for feelings of well-being and motivation to be much more active. A lot of people who are highly motivated also have issues with anxiety and fear, and so this study really points to the fact that it's a twofer: if you have a good gratitude practice and repeat it regularly, you reduce the fear and anxiety circuits, increase the efficacy of the positive emotion and feel-good circuits, and the circuits associated with motivation and pursuit are actually enhanced as well.

## [00:28:58] Gratitude Practice Benefits for Immune System

Thus far we've mainly talked about the effects of gratitude on neural circuit activation and changes, a little bit about changes in terms of the body, heart rate, and breathing. But we haven't talked a lot yet about the changes in health metrics, in things like inflammation, or reductions in inflammation, and immunity. So with that in mind, I'd like to describe the results of a really interesting study published in the journal "Brain, Behavior, and Immunity" in 2021. The title of the study is "Exploring neural mechanisms of the health benefits of gratitude in women: A randomized controlled trial." The first author is Hazlett. What this paper showed was that women who had a regular gratitude practice of the sort we've been talking about showed reductions in amygdala activity — a brain area associated with threat detection, an intimate part of the fear network in the brain.

They also showed large reductions in the production of TNF-alpha (tumor necrosis factor-alpha) and IL-6 (interleukin-6). TNF-alpha and IL-6 are inflammatory cytokines — chemicals that exist in your body and are released from cells when there is damage or systemic stress. In the short term, they can be beneficial: they can call in signals for wound healing and repair of cells. But you don't want TNF-alpha and IL-6 levels to be too high, and you don't want those levels to be up for too long. This study showed significant effects in reducing TNF-alpha and IL-6 in response to a gratitude practice, and these reductions occurred almost immediately after the gratitude practice was completed. Based on the biology and circuitry of the amygdala and the biology of TNF-alpha and IL-6 performing this inflammatory role in both men and women, there is no reason to think the results of that study wouldn't pertain to both men and women.

## [00:31:31] Recap: Establishing an Effective Gratitude Practice

I'd like to just highlight the key elements of the most effective gratitude practice. First of all, that practice has to be grounded in a narrative — a story. You don't have to recite or hear that story every single time you do the gratitude practice, but you have to know what that story was and what the gratitude practice references back to. Second, that story can be one of you receiving genuine thanks — where you are the one receiving the thanks, given to you genuinely and wholeheartedly. Or it can be a story of you observing someone else receiving thanks or expressing thanks, and that has to be a genuine interaction as well, both between the giver and the receiver.

After you've established the story you want to use for your gratitude practice, write down three or four simple bullet points that can serve as salient reminders of that story for you. Include something about the state that you or the other person were in before they received the gratitude, the state after they received the gratitude, and any other elements that lend some sort of emotional weight or tone to the story. This could be three pages of text or just a couple of bullet points — the important thing is that it's embedded in your memory and really associated with this genuine exchange of thanks and the receival of thanks.

Then it's very simple: the entire practice involves reading off these bullet points as a cue to your nervous system of the sense of gratitude, and then for about one minute — or maybe two minutes, or if you're really ambitious, up to five minutes — just really feeling into that genuine experience of having received gratitude, or observed someone else receiving gratitude. If we take a step back from this protocol and compare it to what's typically out there — making a list of all the things you're thankful for, counting your blessings — I think everybody should be counting their blessings all the time; there's always something to be thankful for. But in terms of a scientifically grounded gratitude practice that is demonstrated to shift your physiology at the level of your immune system and your neural circuitry, reducing anxiety, increasing motivation, a gratitude practice reveals itself to be an immensely powerful tool. Thank you for your time and attention today, learning about the science of gratitude. And last, but certainly not least, thank you for your interest in science.
