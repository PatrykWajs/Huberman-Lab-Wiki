# Episode 182: Tools to Reduce & Manage Pain | Dr. Sean Mackey

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Sean Mackey, Chief of the Division of Pain Medicine and Professor of Anesthesiology, Perioperative and Pain Medicine and Neurology at Stanford University School of Medicine, joins Andrew Huberman for a comprehensive exploration of pain — what it is, how it works in the brain and body, and the full spectrum of tools available to manage it. The conversation spans basic neuroscience (nociceptors, gate control theory, conditioned pain modulation, central sensitization), the role of emotions and psychology in amplifying or dampening pain, practical behavioral and nutritional interventions, prescription medications including the nuanced use of opioids, complementary approaches (acupuncture, chiropractic, physical therapy), targeted supplements, and cognitive behavioral therapy. Mackey also discusses the opioid crisis in clinical context, the emerging evidence on kratom and cannabis, and his ongoing work building a free digital platform to make evidence-based pain care universally accessible.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Pain is always both sensory and emotional — and entirely subjective.** The IASP definition describes pain as a complex, subjective experience that is both sensory and emotional. No single brain center produces it; it is a distributed, top-down construction shaped by memory, cognition, and emotion that differs measurably between individuals. Chronic pain affects ~100 million Americans and costs ~$500 billion per year. [00:06:13](transcript.md#000613-pain-unique-experiences-chronic-pain)

2. **NSAIDs reduce hyperalgesia around an injury, not baseline pain sensitivity.** Drugs like ibuprofen and naproxen are anti-inflammatory and anti-hyperalgesic, not true analgesics — they do not raise a normal pain threshold. Blocking the inflammatory response can also delay fracture and tissue repair, so the clinical goal is to use the minimum dose that restores functional sleep and activity. Ibuprofen is short-acting (up to 3×/day); naproxen is longer-acting (2×/day). Acetaminophen/Tylenol is more centrally acting and safer on the stomach but requires care not to exceed 4 g/day due to liver risk. Caffeine can potentiate the analgesic response of NSAIDs. [00:16:15](transcript.md#001615-treating-pain-medications-nsaids-analgesics)

3. **Gate control theory explains why rubbing, shaking, and running water over a wound reduces pain.** Fast A-beta touch fibers activated by mechanical stimulation converge on nociceptive pathways in the spinal cord dorsal horn and inhibit pain signal transmission upward to the brain. Swearing aloud after injury has also been shown in controlled studies to meaningfully reduce pain. The TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation) device exploits this same mechanism by electrically activating A-beta fibers via skin electrodes. [00:32:34](transcript.md#003234-pain-touch-gate-control-theory)

4. **Pain inhibits pain — conditioned pain modulation is a real neurological phenomenon.** A painful stimulus applied to a site distant from the primary pain engages a brainstem circuit with descending pathways to the spinal cord that inhibit the primary pain. First described by Le Bars in rodents (late 1970s) as Diffuse Noxious Inhibitory Control (DNIC); the human version is called conditioned pain modulation (CPM). Notably, CPM is impaired in fibromyalgia and similar central sensitization syndromes. [00:44:53](transcript.md#004453-pain-in-children-pain-modulation-pain-inhibits-pain)

5. **Pain thresholds differ reliably between individuals and sexes, but within-group variation dwarfs between-group differences.** Men on average tolerate heat pain longer than women, but individual variation is far larger. Context, expectation, anxiety, and prior experience all powerfully modulate the threshold — anxiety reliably lowers it; positive emotional states raise it. Exercise over time raises pain thresholds, likely by building inhibitory tone. [00:38:56](transcript.md#003856-pain-threshold-gender)

6. **Cold and heat modulate pain through distinct mechanisms; individual preference should guide use.** Cold reduces prostaglandin and cytokine release and slows nociceptor firing velocity — most effective in the first ~48 hours after acute injury. Heat increases blood flow and relaxes muscle — preferred for subacute and chronic musculoskeletal pain. Mackey's clinical advice: use whichever provides durable relief and apply it within safe time limits (no continuous ice pack for more than ~20 minutes on bare skin). [00:53:20](transcript.md#005320-tool-heat-cold-pain-changing-pain-threshold)

7. **Distinguishing "hurt" from "harm" is a foundational clinical and self-management tool.** Mackey's Morton's neuroma patient had become wheelchair-bound and depressed believing that pain meant damage. Once he understood the pain was not causing structural harm, he walked out of the clinic unaided. Asking "is this actually harming me?" — ideally with clinical confirmation — is the cognitive reframe that can restore activity and quality of life. [01:08:29](transcript.md#010829-tool-hurt-vs-harmed-chronic-pain)

8. **Catastrophizing is the strongest psychological predictor of poor pain outcomes; anger is the most underappreciated amplifier.** Catastrophizing amplifies perceived pain intensity, worsens treatment response, and correlates strongly with disability. Suppressed or internalized anger ("anger in") is a major driver of chronic pain amplification, more so than outwardly expressed anger ("anger out"). Treating the whole person — including emotional state — is not optional in chronic pain care. [01:12:38](transcript.md#011238-emotional-pain-anger-medication)

9. **Elimination diets and anti-inflammatory nutrition can dramatically reduce certain chronic pain conditions.** Mackey describes his own post-infectious visceral pain fully resolved by eliminating FODMAPs (specifically the onion/allium family: onions, shallots, chives, scallions, leeks). Research suggests gut infections can alter genetic expression related to food antigen sensitization. A systematic elimination-and-reintroduction approach identifies individual triggers more reliably than general dietary advice; notably, pain from onion exposure can last ~2 weeks, making pattern recognition difficult without structured journaling. [01:20:43](transcript.md#012043-tool-nutrition-pain-food-sensitization-elimination-diets)

10. **Romantic love and positive social connection measurably reduce pain through reward circuits, not distraction.** In Mackey and Art Aron's fMRI study, viewing a photograph of a romantic partner during a pain stimulus activated the nucleus accumbens and substantia nigra (reward and descending inhibition circuits) and reduced pain ratings — distinct from the prefrontal/cingulate distraction network activated by cognitive tasks. The intensity of passionate love correlated with degree of pain reduction. [01:40:23](transcript.md#014023-romantic-love-pain-addiction)

11. **Opioids are appropriate for specific patient populations; the current crisis is driven by illicit fentanyl, not therapeutic prescribing.** Mackey distinguishes end-of-life cancer pain, severe acute post-surgical pain, and carefully selected chronic pain patients from the general population. The majority of current overdose deaths are driven by illicitly manufactured fentanyl, not prescribed opioids. Keith Humphreys's three-physician framework: most docs do the right thing for the right reasons; a small group did wrong things for right reasons (undertrained, bought marketing); a tiny group did wrong things for wrong reasons (pill mills). Cutting patients off opioids abruptly drove a doubling of deaths in California as patients turned to illicit substitutes. [01:53:17](transcript.md#015317-opioid-crisis-prescribing-physicians)

12. **Pacing — structured activity management — is essential to break the boom-bust cycle in chronic pain.** Chronic pain waxes and wanes. Patients who overdo activity on good days and crash into rest afterward enter a negative reinforcement loop that increases fear-avoidance and worsens long-term function. Pacing establishes consistent, sustainable baselines — incrementally increasing by small amounts each day — that the nervous system can adapt to over time. [02:31:35](transcript.md#023135-physical-therapy-chronic-pain-tool-pacing)

## Tools & Protocols

### Gate Control Activation (Rubbing, Shaking, Running Water, Swearing)
- **What:** Mechanical stimulation of skin near a painful area activates fast A-beta touch fibers that converge on nociceptive neurons in the spinal cord dorsal horn and inhibit pain signal transmission. Swearing aloud also reduces pain by a separate (unclear) mechanism. TENS devices exploit this same A-beta activation principle electrically.
- **How:** Rub, shake, or run water (warm or cool — the movement matters more than temperature) over the injured area immediately after acute injury. Swearing loudly at the moment of injury is supported by research. For chronic applications, TENS devices (electrodes placed over the area, producing a buzzing sensation) can be used.
- **When:** Immediately following acute minor injury; TENS for ongoing acute or chronic pain management.
- **Ref:** [00:32:34](transcript.md#003234-pain-touch-gate-control-theory)

### Heat & Cold Application
- **What:** Cold reduces prostaglandin/cytokine/histamine release and slows nociceptor firing rate. Heat increases local blood flow and relaxes muscle. Both operate on distinct mechanisms.
- **How:** Apply cold for approximately the first 48 hours post-acute injury (ice pack, cold water — limit continuous application to ~20 minutes on bare skin to avoid frostbite). Transition to heat thereafter, or use heat for chronic musculoskeletal pain. For chronic conditions, use whichever provides greater durable relief per individual preference.
- **When:** Acute injury (cold, first 48 hrs); subacute and chronic musculoskeletal pain (heat or cold per preference).
- **Ref:** [00:53:20](transcript.md#005320-tool-heat-cold-pain-changing-pain-threshold)

### Hurt vs. Harm Cognitive Reframe
- **What:** A patient education and self-management tool that separates pain signaling danger/damage (harm) from pain that is real but not causing structural damage (hurt). Eliminating the fear that pain equals damage can immediately restore function and activity.
- **How:** For any persistent pain, ask: is this pain causing tissue damage or structural harm, or is it hurting without harming? Obtain clinical confirmation when uncertain. Once harm is ruled out, use this cognitive distinction to justify resuming normal or modified activity despite ongoing pain.
- **When:** Chronic pain management; fear-avoidance; before limiting activity due to pain.
- **Ref:** [01:08:29](transcript.md#010829-tool-hurt-vs-harmed-chronic-pain)

### Attentional Distraction
- **What:** Engaging prefrontal and cingulate cortex networks through absorbing activities (reading, socializing, walking) diverts attentional resources away from pain processing and measurably reduces pain intensity. Most effective during waking hours; night remains the hardest period for chronic pain patients.
- **How:** Schedule regular distracting activities: reading, time with friends and family, community engagement, outdoor walks. For nighttime pain, distraction alone is insufficient — pair with additional strategies (MBSR, medication, CBT).
- **When:** Daytime chronic pain management; as a complement to other treatments.
- **Ref:** [01:00:54](transcript.md#010054-tools-psychology-mindfulness-based-stress-reduction-catastrophizing)

### Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)
- **What:** Jon Kabat-Zinn's structured 8-week mindfulness program, originally validated in low back pain patients. Teaches non-judgmental, accepting awareness of pain — observing it without amplifying it through catastrophic appraisal. Operates through different neural mechanisms than attentional distraction. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) is a related evidence-based modality used in pain psychology.
- **How:** Formal MBSR programs (clinical, online, or app-based). Core practice: observe pain with curiosity and without judgment. Note its presence, quality, location — without labeling it as bad or fighting it.
- **When:** Chronic pain with significant psychological overlay; adjunct to other therapies; sleep-disrupting chronic pain.
- **Ref:** [01:00:54](transcript.md#010054-tools-psychology-mindfulness-based-stress-reduction-catastrophizing)

### Elimination Diet / FODMAP Identification
- **What:** Systematically removing potential dietary triggers to identify individual food-pain relationships. Highly relevant for visceral and abdominal pain; also relevant for inflammatory joint and widespread pain. Mackey's own chronic abdominal pain fully resolved by eliminating the allium/onion family.
- **How:** Remove suspected triggers (begin with the allium/onion family and other high-FODMAP foods) for 2–4 weeks. Reintroduce one category at a time with extended observation periods — pain from onion exposure can persist for ~2 weeks, so standard 3–5 day windows may be insufficient. Maintain a food-pain diary.
- **When:** Abdominal/visceral chronic pain; inflammatory pain; suspected food-pain relationships.
- **Ref:** [01:20:43](transcript.md#012043-tool-nutrition-pain-food-sensitization-elimination-diets)

### Pacing
- **What:** Structured activity management for chronic pain that avoids the boom-bust cycle. Establishes a consistent, sustainable activity baseline that the nervous system can adapt to over time, breaking the fear-avoidance reinforcement cycle.
- **How:** Set a baseline activity level achievable even on moderately bad pain days. Gradually increase that baseline in small increments (e.g., walk one block; next day one block plus 50 feet; continue incrementally). On good days, hold to the baseline — do not exceed it. On bad days, rest as needed, then restart at the prior baseline. Work with a physical therapist or pain psychologist trained in chronic pain for individualized guidance.
- **When:** Any chronic pain condition with variable intensity and activity cycling.
- **Ref:** [02:31:35](transcript.md#023135-physical-therapy-chronic-pain-tool-pacing)

### Acetyl-L-Carnitine (Neuropathic Pain)
- **What:** OTC supplement with disease-modifying evidence in peripheral neuropathy. Supports mitochondrial metabolism and nerve health. The Sydney trials (Australia) showed improved nerve conduction velocity — a structural endpoint, not just symptom relief — in diabetic peripheral neuropathy.
- **How:** Available OTC (Vitamin Shoppe, online). Dose per Sydney trials; consult a clinician for appropriate dosing. Generally well tolerated.
- **When:** Peripheral neuropathic pain, especially diabetic neuropathy.
- **Ref:** [02:36:35](transcript.md#023635-supplements-acetyl-l-carnitine-alpha-lipoic-acid-vitamin-c-creatine)

### Alpha Lipoic Acid (Neuropathic Pain)
- **What:** OTC antioxidant and T-type calcium channel modulator with evidence for neuropathic pain. Dual mechanism: free radical scavenging reduces oxidative nerve damage; calcium channel modulation dampens nociceptor excitability.
- **How:** Available OTC. Generally well tolerated; may cause mild GI upset. Caution: T-type calcium channels are also present in the heart — Mackey personally noted reduced maximum heart rate during HIIT and discontinued use. High-intensity exercisers should be aware.
- **When:** Peripheral neuropathic pain; general antioxidant support.
- **Ref:** [02:36:35](transcript.md#023635-supplements-acetyl-l-carnitine-alpha-lipoic-acid-vitamin-c-creatine)

### Omega-3 Fish Oil (Chronic Pain)
- **What:** Omega-3 fatty acids have demonstrated benefit for chronic pain. Anti-inflammatory mechanism. Caution: high doses can inhibit platelet aggregation — disclose to surgeons before any procedure.
- **How:** Standard fish oil supplementation. Available OTC. Pause before surgery per clinician guidance.
- **When:** Chronic inflammatory pain; as adjunct supplement.
- **Ref:** [02:36:35](transcript.md#023635-supplements-acetyl-l-carnitine-alpha-lipoic-acid-vitamin-c-creatine)

### Vitamin C (CRPS Prevention Post-Trauma)
- **What:** Evidence suggests Vitamin C supplementation after orthopedic trauma or surgery reduces the risk of complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS), a severe, difficult-to-treat chronic pain condition.
- **How:** 500 mg/day for 50 days post-fracture or orthopedic surgery, based on CRPS prevention trials. OTC. Consult a clinician before and after surgery.
- **When:** Post-orthopedic trauma or surgery, as prophylaxis against CRPS.
- **Ref:** [02:36:35](transcript.md#023635-supplements-acetyl-l-carnitine-alpha-lipoic-acid-vitamin-c-creatine)

### Creatine (Pain-Associated Deconditioning)
- **What:** Well-established supplement for maintaining and recovering muscle mass and strength. Small pilot studies show benefit in fibromyalgia and other conditions. Relevant for chronic pain patients who are deconditioned due to activity avoidance.
- **How:** 3–5 g/day creatine monohydrate, daily, no cycling required. Widely available OTC.
- **When:** Chronic pain with associated deconditioning; fibromyalgia; neuropathic pain with muscle weakness.
- **Ref:** [02:36:35](transcript.md#023635-supplements-acetyl-l-carnitine-alpha-lipoic-acid-vitamin-c-creatine)

### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for Pain
- **What:** CBT targets maladaptive thought patterns (catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, anger) and behaviors that amplify chronic pain. Evidence-based for multiple chronic pain conditions. Dr. Beth Darnall's "Empowered Relief" condenses 8 weeks of CBT into a single ~2-hour session and has been shown in NIH-funded trials to significantly reduce pain catastrophizing.
- **How:** Full CBT: 8–12 weeks with a pain-trained psychologist. Brief interventions (Empowered Relief): available through some pain clinics and the forthcoming free Mackey/Darnall digital CHOIR platform. Core targets: identifying and challenging catastrophic thought patterns; behavioral activation; anger management; relaxation training.
- **When:** Chronic pain with catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, depression, or anger; as a core component of comprehensive pain management.
- **Ref:** [02:42:25](transcript.md#024225-pain-management-cognitive-behavioral-therapy-cbt-biofeedback)

### Biofeedback
- **What:** Real-time physiological feedback (heart rate variability, skin conductance, muscle tension EMG) teaches patients to consciously modulate autonomic responses that amplify pain. Evidence base is strongest for headache and temporomandibular pain. Particularly useful for analytical patients who respond to closed-loop feedback.
- **How:** Administered by a trained therapist with biofeedback equipment; also available through some consumer devices. Typically 6–10 sessions for initial training.
- **When:** Headache (especially migraine and tension-type), temporomandibular disorders, stress-related pain amplification.
- **Ref:** [02:42:25](transcript.md#024225-pain-management-cognitive-behavioral-therapy-cbt-biofeedback)
