# Essentials - How Hearing & Balance Enhance Focus & Learning

## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Hearing & Balance

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. Today, we're going to talk all about hearing and balance and how you can use your ability to hear specific things and your balance system in order to learn anything faster. The auditory system, meaning the hearing system, and your balance system, which is called the vestibular system, interact with all the other systems of the brain and body and, used properly, can allow you to learn information more quickly, remember that information longer and with more ease. We're going to talk about tools for all of that.

## [00:00:55] Ears, Sound Waves, Cochlea

If you can hear this, that's amazing because what it means is that sound is causing little tiny changes in the airwaves wherever you happen to be and that your ears and whatever's contained in those ears and in your brain can take those sound waves and make sense of them. What we call ears have a technical name — they're called pinna (P-I-N-N-A), and this outer part made of cartilage is arranged such that it can capture sound in the best way for your head size. The shape of these ears is such that it amplifies high-frequency sounds — those are the squeakier, shriller sounds — and sound waves themselves are literally just fluctuations or shifts in the way that air is moving toward your ear and through space. In the same way that water can have waves, air can have waves; it's a reverberation of air.

Those sound waves come in through your ears and reach what's called your eardrum, and on the inside of your eardrum there's a little bony structure shaped like a hammer — the malleus, incus, and stapes — that hammers on a snail-shaped structure called the cochlea in your inner ear. The cochlea is where sound gets converted into electrical signals that the brain can understand. One end of the cochlea is more rigid than the other, which is crucial for decoding low-frequency versus high-frequency sounds, and within the cochlea you have thousands of tiny hair cells that, when they move, send signals into the brain that a particular sound is present in the environment.

Your cochlea essentially acts as a prism — it takes all the sound in your environment and splits those sounds into different frequencies, and then the brain takes that information and puts it back together and makes sense of it. The hair cells in each of your two cochleae send axons that convey their patterns of activity into the brain, passing through a number of processing stations before reaching conscious awareness. More important than knowing what you're hearing, your brain needs to know where a sound is coming from — and the auditory and visual systems collaborate to help locate the position of things in space.

## [00:04:42] Sound & Direction, Ventriloquism Effect, Cupping Ears

The way you know what direction a sound is coming from — whether a car, a bus, or a person — is because sound lands in one ear before the other. You have neurons in your brain that calculate the difference in time of arrival for those sound waves in your right versus your left ear: if they arrive simultaneously, the source is directly in front of you; if the sound arrives first to one ear, the source is on that side. But what about up and down? A sound from above and a sound from below both land on both ears at the same time, so the brain uses the frequency characteristics modified by the shape of the pinna to determine elevation.

Disruptions in this auditory-visual matching are the basis of the ventriloquism effect — where you think a sound is coming from a location it's not actually coming from. This all happens very fast and subconsciously. You now know why, if people really want to hear something, they make a cup around their ear — they're giving themselves a bigger pinna, like the large ears of a fennec fox, which has excellent sound localization. Cupping your hand at your ear is not just a gesture; it actually serves a mechanical role by capturing sound waves and funneling them better, increasing the effective size of your pinna.

## [00:08:09] Binaural Beats, Alertness, Calmness, Learning, Anxiety

Binaural beats involve playing one frequency of sound to one ear and a different frequency to the other ear. The idea is that the brain will take those two frequencies, and because the pathways from the ears eventually cross over and share information with both sides of the brain, the brain will average that information and come up with a sort of intermediate frequency. The rationale is that these intermediate frequencies place the brain into a state that is better for learning — either more focused for encoding new information or more relaxed to reduce anxiety.

Sound waves are measured in hertz or kilohertz. Delta waves (1–4 Hz) can help with the transition to sleep and staying asleep. Theta rhythms (4–8 Hz) bring the brain into a state of subtle sleep or deep meditation — deeply relaxed but not fully asleep. Alpha waves (8–13 Hz) can increase alertness to a moderate level, a great state for recall of existing information. Beta waves (15–20 Hz) are great for bringing the brain into focus states for sustained thought or incorporating new information. Gamma waves (32–100 Hz, the highest frequency) are associated with learning and problem-solving.

There is quality evidence from peer-reviewed studies for anxiety reduction from the use of binaural beats, and this effect seems most pronounced when the beats bring the brain into delta, theta, and alpha states. There is also good evidence that binaural beats can be used to treat chronic pain. That said, binaural beats are not uniquely special for learning — they are yet another way of bringing the brain into states of deep relaxation through low-frequency sound or highly alert states for focused learning with higher-frequency sound. They can help some people bring their brain into the state that allows them to learn better, but the mechanism is indirect.

## [00:12:27] Tool: White Noise & Learning

There's an excellent study entitled "Low-intensity white noise improves performance in auditory working memory task: An fMRI study" that explored whether learning could be enhanced by playing white noise in the background. The strength of the study is that researchers looked at the underlying neural circuitry activation as participants performed the learning task, and it showed that white noise, provided it is of low enough intensity — not super loud — could enhance learning to a significant degree. This has now been demonstrated for a huge number of different types of learning.

A 2014 paper published in the Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience, titled "White noise improves learning by modulating activity in dopaminergic midbrain regions and the right superior temporal sulcus," is particularly illuminating. Dopamine is a neuromodulator released largely in the brain that controls the likelihood that certain brain areas will be active, and it is associated with motivation and craving. What's so interesting is that white noise itself can raise the baseline levels of dopamine being released from areas like the substantia nigra. Turning on white noise at a low level — but not too loud — can allow you to learn better because of the ways it modulates your brain chemistry toward heightened dopaminergic activation.

## [00:15:54] White Noise, Hearing Loss & Child Development

White noise actually can have a detrimental effect on auditory learning and possibly even the development of the auditory system in very young children, in particular in infants. Data published in the journal Science showed that when very young animals were exposed to white noise, it disrupted the maps of the auditory world within the brain. In the auditory cortex we have what are called tonotopic maps, where different sound frequencies — from high to low — are organized in a very systematic spatial arrangement, much like the keys of a piano.

White noise essentially contains no tonotopic information — the frequencies are all intermixed. One of the reasons why hearing a lot of white noise during development for long periods of time can be detrimental is that these tonotopic maps may not form normally; it's like taking the keys on a piano and taping a few of them together. Every scientist consulted on this question noted that neuroplasticity is elevated during sleep, and that exposing a developing child to white noise throughout the entire night might degrade that tonotopic map.

Once the auditory system has formed and established its tonotopic maps, the presence of background white noise should not be a problem. For adults, low-level white noise supports learning by bringing the brain into a heightened state of alertness and dopaminergic activation — but for infants and young children, it is advisable to limit continuous overnight white noise exposure to protect the developing auditory system.

## [00:20:02] Auditory Learning, Cocktail Party Effect, Tool: Remember New Names

The cocktail party effect describes what happens when you are in an environment rich with sound — many sound waves from many different sources — and you must attend to specific components of those sounds while filtering everything else. Your brain is exquisitely good at creating a cone of auditory attention, a narrow band of attention with which you extract the information you care about and suppress the rest. This takes work and real attentional effort, which is why you can come home from a loud gathering feeling exhausted — paying strong attention burns literal caloric energy.

We can expand and contract our auditory window just as we can expand and contract our visual field of view. One of the key mechanisms is attending to both the onset and the offset of words. When you meet someone new at a loud party and cannot remember their name moments later, it's often because the signal-to-noise ratio was not high enough — not because you didn't care. The next time you ask someone's name, listen carefully to the onset of what they say and also to the offset — for example, the "juh" at the start and the "fff" at the end of "Jeff" — and you'll be far more likely to remember it.

Using the attentional system in this targeted way allows us to learn much faster and to activate neuroplasticity in the adult brain, which is otherwise very challenging to do. The auditory system is one of the main channels through which we can access neuroplasticity more broadly. While attending to every onset and offset in a full sentence could be disruptive, applying this technique selectively — for specific important pieces of information — is highly effective.

## [00:24:06] Balance, Ears, Vestibular System

Our sense of balance is controlled by structures in our ears — specifically the vestibular system — as well as by our brain and elements of our spinal cord. Right next to the two cochleae in your inner ear, you have what are called semicircular canals. The semicircular canals can be visualized as three hula hoops with marbles (actually small calcium-like stones called otoliths) in them — one positioned vertically with respect to gravity, one roughly horizontal, and one tilted about 45 degrees in between.

Your head can move in three planes: pitch (nodding up and down), yaw (shaking side to side), and roll (tilting side to side as a curious puppy might). Each of these movements causes the stones to shift within one or two of those hula hoops, deflecting little hair cells that send information up to the brain. Any animal that has a jaw has this vestibular balance system.

The vestibular system works in concert with the visual system. When you quickly turn your head, the stones activate hair cells in the relevant semicircular canal, sending a signal that your head just moved, while simultaneously visual information slides past your field of view. When those two signals combine, your eyes lock to a particular location. You can experience the interplay of these systems by standing on one leg and looking at a point 10–12 feet away — easy — and then closing your eyes. Suddenly you'll feel what scientists call postural sway, because visual information actively feeds back onto the vestibular system to help stabilize balance.

## [00:29:17] Improve Dynamic Balance, Tool: Improve Mood & Learning, Tilted Exercise

Static balance — standing on one leg — is an artificial situation. Most people who want to enhance their sense of balance for sport, dance, or other endeavors want to engage balance in a dynamic way, moving through many different planes of movement. For dynamic balance, the vestibular system also cares about acceleration: head position, eye position, direction of movement, and how fast. The semicircular canals, the visual system, and what we call linear acceleration must all work together.

One of the best ways to cultivate a better sense of balance is to enter modes where you are accelerating forward while also tilted with respect to gravity. This is the carve on a skateboard, surfboard, or snowboard; taking a corner on a bike while leaning into the turn so your head is tilted relative to the Earth. The head and body being tilted during forward acceleration has a profound and positive effect on mood and well-being, because the cerebellum outputs to areas of the brain that release neuromodulators like serotonin and dopamine, making us feel really good.

These modes of tilted acceleration have an outsized effect both on well-being and on the ability to learn information in the period after performing them. Getting into modes of acceleration while tilted every once in a while — provided you can do it safely — is an immensely powerful way to build balance skills, enhance mood, and prime the brain for learning. It feels very pleasing precisely because of the chemical relationship between forward acceleration, head tilt, and the release of serotonin and dopamine via cerebellar pathways.
