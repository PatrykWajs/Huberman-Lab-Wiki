## [00:07:03] Intermittent Fasting, Exercise & Women

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. Stacy Sims, welcome. Our podcast puts out a lot of content about nutrition, fitness, cold exposure, heat exposure, and hydration — topics for which you have a ton of expertise, but with an extra degree of expertise as it relates to female-specific physiology. Just to kick things off, fasting — is intermittent fasting something that differs in terms of its impact and how well it works for men versus women?

**Stacy Sims:** That's a short answer — yes. We need to distinguish between intermittent fasting, where you're holding the fast up till noon or having days of really low calorie restriction, versus time-restricted eating, which is the fancy way of saying normal eating where you have breakfast and stop eating after dinner — eating aligned with your circadian rhythm. If we look at intermittent fasting where you're holding the fast up till noon in active women, it's very detrimental unless you have PCOS or some other subclinical issue. The reason is that we as women have more oxidative fibers, so we are already metabolically more flexible than men. Women already have that advantage fasting is supposed to give. When we look at fasting or fasted workouts trying to improve metabolic flexibility, it actually increases cortisol and stress on the woman. They can't hit intensities high enough with no fuel to invoke the post-exercise responses of growth hormone and testosterone, which are what drop cortisol.

**Andrew Huberman:** Could you elaborate on more oxidative fibers and how it relates to metabolic flexibility?

**Stacy Sims:** Sure. Oxidative fibers are muscle fibers with more aerobic capacity — the ones you can use long and slow for a very long period of time because they use a lot of free fatty acids. When a woman starts to exercise, she goes through blood glucose first and then gets into free fatty acid use; she doesn't tap so much into liver and muscle glycogen. So when we're talking about fasted workouts trying to improve metabolic flexibility, it increases stress on the woman, cortisol goes up, and she can't hit the intensity she needs for the post-exercise hormonal responses that would counter-regulate that cortisol.

**Stacy Sims:** From a hypothalamic point of view, there are two areas of kisspeptin neurons in the brain for women but only one for men. One controls appetite and luteinizing hormone; the other looks at estrogen and thyroid. If you start having an exercise stress or a daily stress of getting up and going on with your day without fuel, you perturb those kisspeptin neurons and downregulate them. After just four days you have dysregulation of thyroid and a change in luteinizing hormone pulse — which is really important to maintain endocrine function. People may say "I've been fasting for years and it does great for me," but the question is: how much better would you be if you fueled according to the stress at hand? We see better brain improvements, more cognitive function, less thyroid dysfunction when women fuel properly rather than fast. Population research now shows that people who hold their fast till noon then eat from noon to 6 p.m. have more obesogenic outcomes than people who break their fast at 8 a.m. and finish by 4 or 5 p.m. It comes back to chronobiology — we need to eat when our body is under stress and needs it. The exception: if a woman has obesity, inactivity, PCOS, or other metabolic conditions, fasting can be used as a strategic intervention.

## [00:12:50] Cortisol & Circadian Rhythm, Caffeine & Training

**Andrew Huberman:** It sounds like intermittent fasting or time-restricted feeding, unless very well aligned to the circadian rhythm, is not going to be advantageous for women. I'm also hearing that if a woman trains while fasted, that will further exacerbate the stress response in a way that's not going to be good.

**Stacy Sims:** Exactly. And it gets worse as women get into perimenopause — in their 40s — because there's more fluctuation of hormones and an increase in baseline cortisol. Fasted training increases that cortisol drive and that sympathetic drive. Not having fuel before a high-intensity workout puts them into moderate intensity — they just can't hit the intensities they need. The same with resistance training: women working on sessional RPE (rating of perceived exertion) where they need to hit an eight will miss around 2 to 5% of that top load when they're not fueled. They're not really lifting in the zone they need to be.

**Andrew Huberman:** I have to imagine that if she's also drinking caffeine in order to do that training — because caffeine is a stimulant of the sympathetic arm of the autonomic nervous system — that will further exacerbate all these issues.

**Stacy Sims:** Exactly. Cortisol peaks about half an hour after you wake up. If you're having that cortisol peak but not eating, that's a higher baseline sympathetic drive for women. They walk around more "tired but wired" and have a really difficult time accessing any kind of parasympathetic responses. Having even something small that brings blood sugar up signals to the hypothalamus: "yes, there's some nutrition on board — we can start our day."

## [00:17:25] Reps in Reserve, Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE); Age & Women

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's get people up to speed on RPE because this is a term starting to circulate more outside the physical training community.

**Stacy Sims:** Reps in reserve is when you go in and say "eight" — it means you have two reps in reserve. You finish your eight and you should be able to complete two more in really good form before you hit failure. We can correspond that with your rating of perceived exertion: if we need you to hit an eight on a scale of 1 to 10, it correlates with two reps in reserve. It's a way of quantifying what you're doing in the moment for a squat or deadlift or some other really heavy lift, as opposed to looking at percentage of one-rep maximum. The benefit is you don't need to know your 1RM, and testing 1RM can be dangerous if you're not skilled at compound movements.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there an across-the-board recommendation for most people whether they should train their sets to failure or leave a couple reps in reserve?

**Stacy Sims:** That depends on the age of the woman. If we're looking at the reproductive years — 20 to 40 — then it doesn't matter so much; you can periodize pretty much how normal periodization works with your mesocycles and microcycles. But when we start to get to perimenopause, losing estrogen — which is woman's testosterone, the key driver for strength and power — we have to look at lifting heavy. We want them to do two to three reps in reserve because their 1RM changes depending on what training block they're doing. When you're younger, the key is working to failure; when you're older, the key is working heavy. Heavy-load training invokes a big central nervous system response, which is what we want as women age — it feeds forward into better proprioception, attenuation of cognitive decline, and there's interesting research linking strength and power-based training when older with more neural growth patterns and even some protection against Alzheimer's.

## [00:21:06] Pre-Training Meal & Brain, Kisspeptin

**Andrew Huberman:** If the bad situation is a woman fasting, drinking caffeine, and training intensely but not as intensely as she would otherwise be able to — what's the solution? What is a good example of a pre-training meal?

**Stacy Sims:** I make a double espresso and put some almond milk and a scoop of protein powder in there. If I'm going to do an ocean swim, I need carbohydrate and protein on board. If I'm just going to the gym, I'll probably just have the protein powder in the coffee. Yes, I'm caffeinating, but I'm also getting the calories for the hypothalamus and getting more circulating amino acids. Abby Smith-Ryan out of UNC did specific work looking at carbohydrate and protein before strength or cardio and found that if you're going to do a true strength training session, you only need around 15 grams of protein before to really help ensure you have fuel on board — it also increases your post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC) so your resting metabolism stays elevated. If you're going to do any kind of cardiovascular work up to an hour, add 30 grams of carbs to that. It doesn't have to be a full meal; just enough to bring blood sugar up and stimulate the hypothalamus to say "yes, nutrition is coming in." Then have your real breakfast within 45 minutes after training.

**Andrew Huberman:** As a neuroscientist I find it so interesting that a lot of what you're talking about with this pre-workout meal relates to how ingesting those calories impacts the brain — protects those kisspeptin neurons — as opposed to strictly the calories you'll burn.

**Stacy Sims:** Exactly. It's the neural aspects: being able to generate intensity, blunt cortisol, and get the most out of training without putting the body into an emergency state. The longer someone withholds food after exercise, the more the brain perceives a low-energy state — and the first thing to go is lean mass. It's really hard for women to put on lean mass, so once you nail the idea that you just need 15 grams of protein to really help conserve lean mass, it's a small simple fix and people feel amazing.

## [00:26:45] Post-Training Meal & Recovery Window

**Andrew Huberman:** What does the post-workout meal look like and what is the timing?

**Stacy Sims:** The recovery window is really important. We want protein post-exercise — about 35 to 40 grams — within 45 minutes of finishing training. Women should aim for high-leucine protein sources or whey protein. If you're plant-based, combining pea protein with rice protein gives you a complete amino acid profile. The post-training meal also needs carbohydrates because we need to replenish glycogen stores and support muscle repair. The idea that the body is in a catabolic state post-training and you should delay eating to "burn more fat" is counterproductive, especially for women — it leads to muscle protein breakdown and a prolonged stress response.

## [00:31:48] Hormones, Calories & Women

**Andrew Huberman:** Training fasted burns more body fat — I think now most people accept that's not the case. The percentage of fat as fuel may be increased during fasted training, but in terms of overall loss of body fat it doesn't matter if you train fasted or fed.

**Stacy Sims:** Correct. The easiest way for people to understand the basic idea of low energy availability and how this affects men and women: the tipping point for endocrine dysfunction for men is 15 calories per kilogram of fat-free mass. For women it's 30 — double. So women need a much higher baseline caloric intake before seeing endocrine dysfunction. This is why men do better in a fasted or low-calorie state but for women, our carbohydrate needs are so much higher because we have so many other functions reliant on kisspeptin up-regulation. The interplay of estrogen, luteinizing hormone, progesterone, and thyroid through the circadian rhythm requires more calories for women than men.

## [00:34:24] Women, Strength Improvements & Resistance Training

**Andrew Huberman:** Nowadays there's such a push for women to resistance train — which is great. What is it about resistance training that makes it so important for women specifically?

**Stacy Sims:** The number of fast-twitch fibers women have is similar to men on a relative basis, but the expression of those fibers and how they respond to training differs. Women can actually make strength gains very rapidly, partly because the neural drive changes quickly. The misconception that lifting weights will make women "bulky" is simply not true because women don't have the testosterone levels to support large amounts of hypertrophy in the same way men do. The strength gains come from neural adaptations first. Women tend to recover faster between sets compared to men as well. What we're finding is that the type of contraction matters: for women wanting body composition changes, we want them getting close to or going to failure in the reproductive years — but as they move into perimenopause, we want them lifting heavier with fewer reps in reserve.

## [00:39:10] Tool: Women & Training Goals by Age Range

**Stacy Sims:** Let me lay out a framework. For women in their 20s to 40s: resistance training two to three times a week emphasizing compound movements, periodizing mesocycles, training to failure or near failure. For cardiovascular work: some zone 2 is fine but we want some sprint interval training — true high-intensity — at least once a week. For women in perimenopause and beyond (40s through 50s and older): three minimum resistance training sessions with compound movements, one or two sprint intervals and one HIIT session per week. The emphasis shifts to heavier loads with reps in reserve rather than training to failure — because we want that central nervous system response and strength adaptation that estrogen used to support.

## [00:44:16] Women, Perimenopause, Training & Longevity

**Stacy Sims:** Men age more in a linear fashion whereas women have a definitive point in their late 40s to early 50s where everything changes — that perimenopausal state. Women are having less and less sex hormone circulating, more anovulatory cycles with no or very low progesterone, and changes in the estradiol pulse. Because every system in the body is affected by estrogen, this is why you see more soft tissue injuries in the 40s. Two of the biggest things women in their 40s are going to physical therapists about are frozen shoulder and plantar fasciitis — both really indicative of what's happening in perimenopause. So that mid-40s to early 50s range is a definitive aging point where I really try to get women into heavy lifting and polarizing their training — not emphasizing zone 2, but asking "How am I affecting my central nervous system?" So that when they reach that perimenopause inflection point, their body is already conditioned for the hormonal changes that are coming.

## [00:47:14] Women & Training for Longevity, Cardio, Zone 2

**Andrew Huberman:** What about zone 2 cardiovascular training — which many experts recommend for metabolic health and longevity? Is the prescription different for women?

**Stacy Sims:** Zone 2 is fine, but it's not the emphasis for women, particularly in perimenopause. The emphasis should be on polarized training — very easy recovery work and very high intensity work, with not much in between. For body composition changes and cardiometabolic improvements, women need the high-intensity stimulus. Zone 2 alone doesn't provide the hormonal stimulus that women need, especially as estrogen is declining. Walking is great for recovery but if you're looking for that big metabolic and cardiovascular change, we're talking true sprint interval training.

## [00:51:42] Tools: How to Start Resistance Training, Machines; Polarized Training

**Stacy Sims:** If someone has never done resistance training and they're in their 40s, we take between two weeks to four months to really learn how to move well, because there's a higher incidence of soft tissue injury in the 40s due to perturbations of estrogen. What works for them matters — if you have a short amount of time in the gym, do a split routine. If you can allocate an hour to an hour and a half, do total body with adequate rest. Machines are absolutely valid especially for beginners — they teach the movement pattern and reduce injury risk. The key distinction between younger and older women: younger women should work to failure; older women should work heavy with reps in reserve. For polarized training: have very very low intensity for recovery and super super high intensity for metabolic and cardiovascular changes. What I have a lot of women do is a 20-minute lower body heavy set and then they'll go on the assault bike and do as hard as they can for 30 seconds, recover as much as they need to go again, then do another 30 seconds. Most people think they can do four or five of those — after two they're completely gassed.

## [00:59:10] Menstrual Cycle & Training, Tool: Tracking & Individual Variability

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the menstrual cycle and how that impacts training at the level of psychology and physiology. Is there a particular phase where a woman should expect that motivation or recovery would be more challenging?

**Stacy Sims:** This is the sticky point of recent science. We see meta-analyses saying there is no effect of the menstrual cycle on anything — but those studies use small subject pools of eumenorrheic women with supposedly normal cycles. When we look more deeply and also use mixed methods — qualitative as well as quantitative — we need women to track their own cycles and find their own patterns. There are times where you feel terrible and can't push intensity, but that might be day eight for one woman and day 18 for another. From a molecular standpoint, the low hormone phase — day one (first day of bleeding) up through ovulation, which is midway through your cycle — you have a greater capacity for pulling in and accommodating stress, both physical and mental. So the low hormone phase is really optimal for hitting a PR or a new speed. The high hormone or luteal phase is associated with more cortisol, pro-inflammatory immune response, inability to access carbohydrates as well, and higher sympathetic drive. The goal of the luteal phase is to build tissue — shuttling carbohydrate and amino acids toward the endometrial lining. So in the luteal phase, especially the week before your period, you really need to be amping up carbohydrate and protein. Not every woman ovulates in every cycle, which is why tracking is so important.

## [01:04:31] Tool: 10-Minute Rule; High-Intensity Training & Menstrual Cycle

**Stacy Sims:** The 10-minute rule is a really useful practical tool. If you wake up and feel terrible — maybe it's PMS, maybe you slept badly — you get dressed and start your warm-up. If after 10 minutes you still feel terrible, give yourself permission to stop and have an active recovery day. But most of the time after 10 minutes you'll feel better. The high-intensity training caveat during the menstrual cycle: in the follicular phase (low hormone), hit your hardest workouts. In the luteal phase, you can still do high-intensity work but you may need more recovery — you might need to dial back volume slightly and make sure you're eating more, especially carbohydrates, to support that phase.

## [01:08:36] "Train Hard & Eat Well"; Appetite, Nutrition & Menstrual Cycle

**Stacy Sims:** The motto I live by is "train hard and eat well." Women have been conditioned to think about food restriction, especially around exercise, but this is counterproductive. In the luteal phase, appetite naturally increases — your body is asking for more fuel. Honor that. You need more protein and more carbohydrate, not less. The misconception that "eating more will make me gain weight" is backwards when we're talking about active women — eating appropriately supports training, reduces cortisol, and improves body composition over time. The brain needs to see incoming nutrition to maintain its kisspeptin signaling; without it, it down-regulates thyroid and reproductive hormones.

## [01:12:22] Oral Contraception, Hormones, Athletic Performance; IUD

**Andrew Huberman:** What about oral contraceptives — how do they impact training and athletic performance?

**Stacy Sims:** Oral contraceptives (OCs) blunt the natural hormone fluctuations. We know there are effects on VO2 max — some women see a reduction in their top-end aerobic capacity when on OCs. There was a study in early 2024 looking at changes in the amygdala that happens with oral contraceptive use: it's reversible in adults but for young girls whose brains are still developing, we don't know if it's reversible. The amygdala changes made women less willing to take chances; when they went off the OC they said, "Why couldn't I do that before?" OCs have a significant effect on the body well beyond the reproductive system, and we don't know enough about all the other effects. The IUD — specifically a progestin-laced Mirena-type IUD — is what a lot of high-performance tactical athletes use because it doesn't have a systemic effect on adaptation, inflammation, mood, or performance. It's a fit-and-forget option for up to three to five years. The copper IUD is different — non-hormonal — and causes heavier bleeding for the first three cycles but then attenuates.

## [01:20:57] Evaluating Menstrual Blood, PCOS; Hormones & Female Athletes

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned pioneering studies on evaluating menstrual blood as a window into what's going on physiologically.

**Stacy Sims:** Menstrual fluid is often thought of as a discard product, but it's a very good indicator of what's happening from an endocrine standpoint and gives a really good indication of what's happening from an endometrial standpoint. Looking at the cytokines, proteins, and tissue that come from it — it's a huge indicator that's naturally discharged. We're now looking at it for determining whether you have HPV, whether we can identify PCOS proteins, whether we can identify endometriosis markers. PCOS — polycystic ovarian syndrome — is associated with elevated androgens and is becoming more commonly diagnosed. We also see rebound PCOS that happens when someone gets off the oral contraceptive pill — it's not necessarily true PCOS, because the ovaries are producing eggs that have been downregulated for so long. Under ultrasound it might look like PCOS but it's not. True PCOS requires specific diagnosis and often responds well to addressing low energy availability and increasing carbohydrate intake.

## [01:26:31] Iron, Fatigue; Blood Testing & Menstrual Cycle

**Stacy Sims:** Iron is a massive issue for women — menstrual bleeding means women lose iron every month. Fatigue, poor performance, brain fog — these are often signs of low iron, not overtraining. Blood testing timing matters: test iron and ferritin about a week after your period ends, not during or right after, because serum iron fluctuates significantly across the cycle. Ideally test full iron panel including ferritin, serum iron, and TIBC (total iron binding capacity). Ferritin ideally should be above 50 ng/mL for active women — many labs flag "normal" at anything above 12, but that's too low for performance. Vitamin D3 also affects iron absorption, so deficiency in D3 can compound iron issues.

## [01:29:33] Caffeine & Perimenopause; Nicotine, Schisandra

**Stacy Sims:** Caffeine in perimenopause is a significant issue. As baseline cortisol is already elevated in perimenopause, adding caffeine — which further stimulates the sympathetic nervous system — can be quite problematic. Some women in perimenopause find they need to reduce caffeine or cut it off earlier in the day to avoid disrupted sleep and amplified hot flashes. Nicotine is something I get asked about — there's evidence for cognitive benefits but for women in perimenopause it further drives sympathetic activity and is not something I recommend. Schisandra is an adaptogen that some research supports for improving athletic performance and stress tolerance in women — it has some interesting properties around cortisol modulation and liver enzyme activity. I consider it an adaptogen worth looking at, particularly for perimenopausal women managing high training loads.

## [01:34:24] Deliberate Cold Exposure & Women, Endometriosis; Tool: Sauna & Hot Flashes

**Andrew Huberman:** What about deliberate cold exposure for women? You've expressed some nuance about this.

**Stacy Sims:** There's a real sex difference in how women respond to cold. Women have better thermal insulation due to higher body fat percentage and different vasoconstrictive responses — when men go into very cold water, they maintain core temperature better initially; when women go into very cold water, their vasoconstriction can be quite severe, cutting off peripheral blood flow and actually reducing their ability to exercise effectively. The sweet spot for women doing deliberate cold exposure for its benefits — dopamine, norepinephrine response, and everything else — is around 16°C (55-56°F). That's chilly but not the extremely cold temperatures (38-50°F) that are sometimes used. We did a pilot study: using deliberate cold water exposure around ovulation and holding it for 10 days over three menstrual cycles attenuated endometriosis symptoms because endometriosis is an inflammatory disease — dampening that inflammation and creating a learned anti-inflammatory response helped. For women managing endometriosis, cold exposure at the right temperature and timing is worth exploring.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about sauna and hot flashes?

**Stacy Sims:** Sauna has really significant benefits for women, particularly perimenopausal women. The mechanism: when you do heat exposure, you're sending a stronger stimulus to the hypothalamus — specifically the medial preoptic area, which acts like a thermostat. You're also getting better serotonin production from the gut (95% of serotonin is gut-derived), which leads to better temperature control and actually shuts down hot flashes. Frequent sauna exposure essentially "resets" the hypothalamic thermostat. Additionally, regular sauna use improves insulin and glucose control, better expression of heat shock proteins, cardiovascular adaptations, and metabolic changes — all of which are especially beneficial for women.

## [01:42:19] Tools: "Sims' Protocol": Post-Training Sauna & Performance; "Track Stack"

**Stacy Sims:** Post-training sauna exposure — what I call the Sims Protocol — is really good for improving performance. When you go into the sauna after a workout, you're already in a state of passive dehydration from training. Your heart rate is elevated and your body is under stress. The sauna extends that training stimulus: your body responds by upregulating blood volume — it stimulates the production of more red blood cells, increasing cardiovascular capacity and oxygen delivery. This is akin to going to altitude for the blood volume boost, but more accessible. You can use it post-cardio as well as post-resistance training. I'm also a fan of what I call the "Track Stack" — used for track athletes and anyone doing significant high-intensity work. It's 200 milligrams of caffeine, low-dose aspirin (for blood thinning), and beta-alanine. Beta-alanine has vasodilatory properties and carnosine-related benefits for muscle contraction. When you do sprint sessions with this stack it increases your training stress, so your adaptation is to a higher stress level. Make sure you're recovering well and not stacking two days of high intensity in a row.

## [01:49:37] Women, Hormones & Sleep, Perimenopause & Sleep Hygiene

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there female-specific requirements for sleep?

**Stacy Sims:** Yes. First, the obvious — sleep temperature. Women and men have variations in their sleep temperature preferences, so creating an environment that is cool and comfortable for you matters. During the menstrual cycle, there are definitive changes in sleep architecture: in the mid-luteal to premenstrual phase — about 10 days before your period — there is significantly less slow-wave sleep, increased sleep latency, and more light sleep. This is when women tend to have more mood issues too, because of estrogen's interplay with serotonin in the brain. Optimize sleep hygiene during this window: L-theanine and apigenine (chamomile extract) can help, as well as cool room temperature and limiting screens. In perimenopause, significant insomnia results from hot flashes and night sweats disrupting sleep architecture. Strategies: use adaptogens like rhodiola stacked with theanine, use NSDR (non-sleep deep rest) or Yoga Nidra, manage room temperature, and potentially work with a sleep specialist. Men don't have these same hormonal disruptions to sleep architecture, which is why women need to be more aligned with their hormonal profile when managing sleep.

## [01:52:54] Supplements: Creatine, Water Weight, Hair Loss; Vitamin D3

**Stacy Sims:** Number one supplement for women regardless of age: creatine. It's really important for brain function, mood, and actually gut health — we're seeing a lot of interesting research in all those areas beyond muscle. Dose: 3 to 5 grams of monohydrate per day, preferably Creapure (the German company that uses a water-based wash to produce it, unlike other brands that use an acid-based wash and can cause gastric distress). Common concern from women: creatine brings water into the muscle, so they see a small transient weight gain — this is muscle water, not fat, and it actually helps with hydration and muscle function. Hair loss has been a reported concern linked to creatine and DHT levels, but the data on this is not conclusive — the study that generated this concern was poorly designed. Vitamin D3 is the second key supplement: really important for cardiovascular, muscle, and brain health, and it also helps with iron absorption and maintenance of iron stores.

## [01:57:21] Protein Powder; Adaptogens & Timing

**Stacy Sims:** Protein powder is a food-based supplement and absolutely valid for women who struggle to hit their protein targets through whole food alone. Aim for whey protein if you can tolerate dairy — high leucine content. If plant-based, pea and rice protein combination is a solid complete protein. Regarding adaptogens: Schisandra for stress and cortisol modulation, rhodiola for sleep support especially in perimenopause, and ashwagandha is another option but watch timing — taken at night it can be sedating, which is either useful or not depending on your situation. Adaptogens work best when cycled — used during periods of high training load and stress, then tapered off — rather than taken indefinitely.

## [02:00:11] Pregnancy & Training; Cold & Hot Exposure

**Andrew Huberman:** What about training during pregnancy? And can pregnant women do deliberate cold exposure?

**Stacy Sims:** When you get pregnant, your body tells you what you can do. You have a reduction in anaerobic capacity on purpose — your body is being protective. Endurance capacity is actually good because of blood volume expansion, but high-intensity work is not well tolerated. Guidelines have gotten rid of the old heart rate rules; women are now told to be as active as they can be without creating injury and without trying to make gains — maintain, don't improve. The worst scenario is a highly active woman stopping everything because she's afraid, becoming deconditioned. On cold exposure during pregnancy: I advise caution, especially in the first 12 to 20 weeks, because anything incredibly stressful for the body raises the risk of miscarriage. We simply don't have good data on deliberate cold exposure during pregnancy. Hot yoga (around 40°C / 100°F) appears acceptable based on case studies — there's actually a vascularization benefit for the placenta from moderate heat. Sauna-level extreme heat during pregnancy is more cautionary. Men who want to conceive should avoid saunas because heat is detrimental to sperm viability.

## [02:06:19] Tool: Women in 50s & Older, Training & Nutrition for Longevity

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the most important advice for women in their 50s and older?

**Stacy Sims:** Three key training modalities: jump training (plyometrics), heavy resistance training, and sprint interval training. These are the three external stressors that invoke change without pharmaceuticals. Jump training is particularly important for bone density and neural drive. Heavy resistance training with compound movements preserves and builds strength. Sprint intervals generate lactate, which is increasingly understood to be neuroprotective and important for cognitive health. From a nutrition standpoint: protein is paramount — 1 to 1.1 grams per pound of body weight per day (2 to 2.3 grams per kilogram). That equates to a few scrambled eggs at breakfast, chicken at lunch, a small steak at dinner plus other things. It doesn't all have to be animal products — beans and legumes contribute too. Getting adequate protein becomes increasingly difficult as appetite decreases in older women, which is why protein powder as a supplement makes practical sense.

## [02:09:38] Tool: Women in 20s-40s & Training, Lactate

**Andrew Huberman:** What about women in their 20s to 40s?

**Stacy Sims:** In your 20s to 40s, you have more room to get away with things that might not be optimal. The big rock is still resistance training — periodize, work to failure or near failure, build your base so you arrive at perimenopause with a solid foundation. For cardiovascular training, include lactate-producing high-intensity work because we know there's a misstep in brain lactate metabolism in women — the brain hasn't been exposed to sufficient lactate, especially historically when women were not culturally encouraged to do that kind of intense work. The younger you are and the more you can keep your glycolytic fibers active by doing high-intensity work, the more you're exposing your brain to lactate, and the better the forward-looking attenuation of cognitive decline and reduction of Alzheimer's plaque development.

## [02:12:18] Tool: What is High-Intensity Training?, Cardiovascular Sets & Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** When you say high-intensity you don't mean a class or a run where you're drenched in sweat at the end — you mean something more specific. Let's disambiguate.

**Stacy Sims:** True high-intensity interval training for a runner means going to the track and doing sets of 400s or 800s — between one and four minutes of work at 80% or more, with variable recovery. If you do one lap and walk half a lap to recover, then do it again, that's adequate. You might get through it in 30 to 40 minutes max. In a gym setting, I like "every minute on the minute" (EMOM) training — for example, 10 deadlifts at moderate weight in 50 seconds, 10 seconds to transition to thrusters for 8 reps, then to kettlebell swings. You're doing explosive compound work with short structured rest rather than continuous moderate effort. What most people call "high intensity" — a tough class, moving continuously, circuit training — is actually moderate intensity because you're never going above about 75-80% of your capacity and you're not allowing full recovery between efforts.

## [02:17:22] Training for Longevity, Cellular & Metabolic Changes

**Stacy Sims:** From a longevity standpoint, we're looking at cellular and metabolic changes that come from high-intensity work: mitochondrial biogenesis, improved insulin sensitivity, lactate as a signaling molecule for brain health, heat shock protein expression with sauna, and increased blood volume from the Sims Protocol (post-training sauna). The combination of heavy strength training, sprint intervals, jump training, and strategic sauna use covers all the major longevity pathways without requiring pharmaceutical interventions. The key is starting these habits early — the earlier you build the CNS adaptations and the muscle mass base, the better you weather the perimenopausal storm.

## [02:19:30] Nutrition, 80/20 Rule

**Andrew Huberman:** What does an ideal nutritional day look like for an active woman?

**Stacy Sims:** I live by the 80/20 rule: 80% of the time, eat really well — prioritize whole foods, adequate protein (1 to 1.1 g/lb), complex carbohydrates timed around workouts, healthy fats mostly from plant-based sources (avocados, nuts, seeds, olive oil) but not excluding dairy fats or occasional animal fats. 20% of the time, eat what you enjoy — dark chocolate, whatever makes you feel good. Fats should predominantly come from whole food plant-based sources, not ultra-processed. The fear-mongering around saturated fatty acids from dairy has been disproven — a conglomerate of fat sources with most from whole food is the goal. The overall frame: eat to fuel your training, not to restrict calories. Women who eat more — of the right quality foods — are consistently leaner, stronger, and healthier than those in chronic caloric restriction.
