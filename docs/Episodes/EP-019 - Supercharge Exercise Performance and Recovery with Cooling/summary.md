# Episode 19: Supercharge Exercise Performance & Recovery with Cooling

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Huberman explains the science of thermoregulation and how strategically cooling specific body surfaces — the palms, bottoms of the feet, and face — can increase exercise output by 200–600% compared to baseline. He covers the biology of AVAs (arterio-venous anastomoses), why whole-body cold immersion is counterproductive for both performance and hypertrophy, and how caffeine, alcohol, and NSAIDs interact with body temperature to affect training outcomes. The episode opens the podcast's physical performance and skill-learning series.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Heat is the primary limiter of physical performance** — not motivation, not willpower. The enzyme pyruvate kinase, a rate-limiting step in ATP production, denatures above ~39–40°C, halting muscle contraction. This is why you stop before you consciously decide to. [00:13:42](transcript.md#001342-heat-the-enemy-of-all-performance--why)

2. **Three body surfaces (glabrous skin) are the body's primary heat-exchange portals** — palms, bottoms of feet, and face. These sites have arterio-venous anastomoses (AVAs) that allow direct artery-to-vein blood routing, bypassing capillaries, enabling far faster heat transfer than anywhere else on the body. [00:31:38](transcript.md#003138-face-palms-bottoms-of-feet-glabrous-skin)

3. **Palmar cooling between sets can increase pull-up volume from ~100 to ~600 reps per session** — demonstrated by Craig Heller's lab at Stanford, originally funded by DARPA and now used by NFL teams and the military. A bench press control group taking testosterone cypionate improved ~1% per week; the cooling group far outpaced them. Huberman calls it "several-fold better than anabolic steroids." [00:40:55](transcript.md#004055-palmer-cooling-outperforms-anabolic-steroids-several-fold)

4. **Cardiac drift is why you quit** — heat raises heart rate independently of effort. The brain sums the heat-driven and effort-driven heart rate contributions; when they hit a combined threshold, it stops you. Palmar cooling prevents this drift and has produced statistically significant performance extensions every time it has been tested. [00:46:33](transcript.md#004633-cardiac-drift---moving-the-i-quit-point)

5. **Ice baths and cold showers are suboptimal for performance cooling** — total-body immersion constricts the very AVA portals needed to dump heat. Cooling the back of the neck, top of the head, or wearing ice vests are similarly inferior. Targeted glabrous skin cooling (palms, feet, face) is consistently more effective. [00:59:23](transcript.md#005923-how-to-use-cold-to-recover-faster--more-thoroughly)

6. **Cold immersion after training can block hypertrophy** — it suppresses mTOR (mammalian target of rapamycin) and the inflammation signals required for muscle growth adaptation. Cold plunges have their place for deliberate brown fat thermogenesis or mental resilience, but not post-strength-training when hypertrophy is the goal. [01:02:05](transcript.md#010205-ice-baths--cold-showers-can-prevent-training-progress-mtor-etc)

7. **The cooling temperature matters: slightly cooler than body temperature, not ice cold** — ice-cold water causes vasoconstriction of AVAs, closing the heat-exchange portals. Cool (not cold) water keeps them open. This is also why passing a cool can back and forth between palms is effective — the motion prevents sustained single-point contact from causing vasoconstriction. [00:53:20](transcript.md#005320-protocols-for-self-directed-cooling-to-vastly-improve-performance)

8. **Caffeine's effect on performance depends on adaptation status** — in non-adapted users, caffeine causes vasoconstriction and heat retention, limiting performance. In chronic users (100–400 mg/day), it causes vasodilation and aids heat dumping. All users should avoid caffeine after exercise, as the rebound vasoconstriction as caffeine wears off impedes recovery. [01:12:00](transcript.md#011200-the-caffeine-rule--caffeine-adaptation)

9. **NSAIDs lower core temperature and are used by some endurance athletes to sustain output** — ibuprofen, naproxen, and acetaminophen all drop body temperature. Some athletes exploit this pharmacologically for desert races and summer events. Risks include kidney and liver stress, amplified when water/salt balance are already stressed during long exertion. [01:14:20](transcript.md#011420-nsaids-for-training-performance-enhancements--risks)

10. **Alcohol is a vasodilator that lowers body temperature** — post-session it can aid recovery by reducing core temp. Huberman cites a competitive endurance athlete friend who drinks a beer or two after long runs for this effect. Anything post-exercise that raises body temperature impedes recovery; anything that safely lowers it accelerates it. [01:06:29](transcript.md#010629-alcohol-caffeine-nsaids-their-temperature-effects-matter)

11. **The 49ers athlete case study and real-world adoption** — a member of the 49ers who could do 40 strict full-range dips on his first set, declining steeply to ~10 by the 10th set, tripled his output within less than a week using palmar cooling — and maintained that higher performance level even without the cooling, confirming it produced genuine training adaptation, not just a performance crutch. [00:25:29](transcript.md#002529-proper-cooling-can-double-triple-quadruple-or-more-your-ability)

12. **To accelerate inter-session recovery, return body temperature to resting baseline as quickly as possible** — the sooner you hit resting temperature post-workout, the sooner muscles and tendons recover and the sooner you can train again. Glabrous skin cooling achieves this faster and without the mTOR-blocking side effect of full immersion. [00:59:23](transcript.md#005923-how-to-use-cold-to-recover-faster--more-thoroughly)

## Tools & Protocols

### Palmar Cooling Between Sets
- **What:** Apply cool (slightly below body temperature) water to the palms between work sets to dump heat and sustain output.
- **How:** Fill a sink or bucket with cool tap water (not ice water). Submerge or rest palms for 10–30 seconds between sets; extend to 30–60 seconds as fatigue accumulates over the session. Alternatively, pass a cool (not frozen-solid) can back and forth between hands — the passing motion prevents sustained contact from triggering vasoconstriction. Ice-cold water closes AVA portals; avoid it.
- **When:** During resistance training, running, cycling, or any effort where heat is limiting output.
- **Ref:** [00:53:20](transcript.md#005320-protocols-for-self-directed-cooling-to-vastly-improve-performance)

---

### Face Cooling
- **What:** Apply cool (not ice cold) water or a damp cloth to the face to rapidly dump core heat.
- **How:** Splash cool water on the face or press a cool damp cloth for 15–30 seconds. Avoid prolonged ice-pack contact (vasoconstriction risk). Firefighters use cool face masks between high-exertion bouts and remove them before vasoconstriction sets in.
- **When:** Between exercise bouts, at halftime, between rounds, or whenever heat is spiking.
- **Ref:** [00:53:20](transcript.md#005320-protocols-for-self-directed-cooling-to-vastly-improve-performance)

---

### Foot Cooling
- **What:** Expose the bottoms of bare feet to cool water during rest intervals.
- **How:** Remove shoes and socks. Place bare feet just at or above the surface of a bucket of cool water between sets. Huberman self-tested this alongside palmar cooling and reported a 60% increase in dip volume in a single session.
- **When:** During resistance training rest periods, especially in warm environments.
- **Ref:** [00:53:20](transcript.md#005320-protocols-for-self-directed-cooling-to-vastly-improve-performance)

---

### Post-Workout Recovery Cooling
- **What:** Cool glabrous skin surfaces (palms, feet, face) after training to return core temperature to resting baseline as quickly as possible.
- **How:** Cool water on palms, feet, and/or face for several minutes post-session. Avoid full cold immersion (ice bath, cold shower) — it blocks mTOR-driven muscle growth signaling. Cold plunges are appropriate when the goal is brown fat thermogenesis or mental resilience, not post-strength hypertrophy recovery.
- **When:** Immediately after training sessions, especially strength-focused sessions where hypertrophy is the goal.
- **Ref:** [00:59:23](transcript.md#005923-how-to-use-cold-to-recover-faster--more-thoroughly)

---

### Caffeine Timing Rule
- **What:** Time caffeine intake based on adaptation status to avoid heat-retention impairment.
- **How:** Caffeine-adapted users (100–400 mg/day): take before exercise — vasodilation aids heat dumping. Non-adapted users: avoid before exercise — vasoconstriction traps heat, making it "about the worst thing you could possibly do." All users: avoid after exercise — rebound vasoconstriction impedes recovery.
- **When:** Pre-workout only for habituated users; non-users should avoid it around exercise entirely.
- **Ref:** [01:12:00](transcript.md#011200-the-caffeine-rule--caffeine-adaptation)

---

### NSAID Use for Endurance (Advanced/Caution)
- **What:** NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, acetaminophen) lower core body temperature and are used by some endurance athletes to pharmacologically extend performance.
- **How:** Some athletes take OTC NSAIDs before long events to suppress core temperature rise. Risks: kidney and liver stress, amplified during heavy exertion when water/salt balance are already stressed. Huberman does not recommend for typical training use.
- **When:** Potentially for high-stakes endurance events only, under medical supervision.
- **Ref:** [01:14:20](transcript.md#011420-nsaids-for-training-performance-enhancements--risks)
