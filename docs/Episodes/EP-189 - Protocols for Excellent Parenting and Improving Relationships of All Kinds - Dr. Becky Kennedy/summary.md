# Episode 189: Protocols for Excellent Parenting & Improving Relationships of All Kinds | Dr. Becky Kennedy

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Becky Kennedy, clinical psychologist and founder of Good Inside, joins Andrew Huberman to discuss evidence-based protocols for raising emotionally resilient children and building healthy relationships of all kinds. The conversation covers the core framework of "sturdiness" — the ability to remain connected to yourself and to another person simultaneously — and translates it into concrete, real-world tools for parents, partners, teachers, and anyone navigating emotionally charged relationships. Topics include setting genuine boundaries, repairing after conflict, handling rudeness, supporting deeply feeling or ADHD kids, navigating adolescence, managing entitlement, building frustration tolerance, co-parenting differences, and the role of mentors in development. While the frame is parent-child, virtually every tool applies to adult relationships, workplaces, and self-relationship.

## Guest: Dr. Becky Kennedy

Dr. Becky Kennedy (also known as "Dr. Becky") is a clinical psychologist specializing in parent-child relationships. She trained at Duke University and Columbia University and is the founder of **Good Inside**, an online education platform for parents. She is the author of the bestselling book *Good Inside: A Guide to Becoming the Parent You Want to Be* and hosts the Good Inside podcast. Her approach centers on the belief that all children (and adults) are fundamentally good inside, and that behavior problems reflect a lack of skill or an unmet need — not a character deficit.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Sturdiness is the foundation of healthy relationships.** Being sturdy means staying connected to yourself (your values, needs, limits) and to the other person at the same time. It is the opposite of either giving in or shutting down. [00:07:35](transcript.md#000735-healthy-relationships-sturdiness-boundaries-empathy)

2. **Boundaries are actions you take, not demands you make.** A boundary is something you will do — not something you ask the other person to do. "I'm going to turn off the TV" is a boundary. "Stop watching TV" is a request. Boundaries require no cooperation from the other person. [00:14:34](transcript.md#001434-tool-establishing-boundaries)

3. **Kids aren't bad — they're underskilled.** Reframing misbehavior as a skills deficit rather than a character flaw changes the parental response from punishment to teaching. Children are born with all the feelings and none of the skills to manage those feelings; behaviors are surface signals of an underlying unmet need. [00:31:16](transcript.md#003116-kids-inherent-good)

4. **"I believe you" is one of the most powerful phrases in parenting.** Validating a child's inner experience — not the behavior, but the feeling — builds genuine confidence and psychological safety. Confidence is self-trust, and it comes from having one's experience consistently believed. [00:44:48](transcript.md#004448-tool-i-believe-you-confidence-safety-other-relationships)

5. **Trauma is aloneness, not the event itself.** Events processed alone become traumatic; events processed in safe connection do not. Children who are not repaired with after a rupture default to "it must be my fault" — the origin of many adult self-critical and self-doubt patterns. [00:52:15](transcript.md#005215-trauma-aloneness-repair)

6. **Repair is more important than perfection.** Ruptures in relationship are inevitable. A good repair starts with repairing with yourself first ("I'm a good parent having a hard time"), then approaching the child simply: "I'm sorry I yelled. It's not your fault." Even a minimal repair is powerful. [00:57:07](transcript.md#005707-tool-repair-apologies-rejecting-apology)

7. **"Most Generous Interpretation" reframes rudeness.** When a child says "I hate you," the most generous interpretation is that they love you so much that the disappointment is unbearable. Pausing to ask "what is the most generous interpretation of this behavior?" shifts the parent from reactive punishment to curious problem-solving. [01:04:37](transcript.md#010437-tool-rudeness-disrespect-most-generous-interpretation)

8. **Sturdy leaders don't walk on eggshells.** When parents fear a child's emotional outbursts and capitulate to avoid them, children lose the sturdy leader they need. Like a pilot who does not change course because a passenger is upset, the parent's calm non-reactivity is the most stabilizing thing they can offer. [01:12:32](transcript.md#011232-walking-on-eggshells-pilot-analogy-emotional-outbursts-sturdy-leadership)

9. **"Deeply feeling" kids are super-sensors who need co-regulation, not suppression.** Some children have a larger emotional range, heightened sensory sensitivity, and a deep fear of being "too much." These children reject typical validation but desperately need it; side-door approaches and stating "I am not scared of your feelings" is transformative. [01:20:49](transcript.md#012049-deeply-feeling-kids-fears-sensory-overload)

10. **Frustration tolerance is a learnable skill that must be practiced early.** Shielding children from all frustration — through immediate gratification or rescue — builds intolerance circuits. The learning space inherently feels frustrating; that feeling is correct. Deliberately inserting small doses of frustration builds the anterior mid-cingulate cortex circuits associated with persistence and resilience. [01:41:20](transcript.md#014120-tool-tolerating-frustration-screen-time-learning)

11. **Adolescents need both autonomy and connection — and parents need to keep showing up.** The difference between an Explorer and a Nomad is whether they have a home base. Teens who say "get out of my room" still need the note slipped under the door — and even when they rip it up, it resonates. [02:01:15](transcript.md#020115-adolescence-critical-needs-explorers-vs-nomads)

12. **Entitlement is the fear of frustration, not selfishness.** Entitled behavior is the result of a child encoding frustration next to fear because adults always rescued them from it. The antidote is building frustration tolerance, assigning real responsibilities without pay, and allowing natural consequences to unfold. [02:34:26](transcript.md#023426-tool-entitlement-fear-frustration)

## Tools & Protocols

### Establishing a Boundary
- **What:** Identify what *you* will do — not what you want the other person to do.
- **How:** State the boundary as an action: "I'm going to close the laptop now." Then do it. Do not negotiate or explain at length. Follow with empathy: "I know you're upset. I get it."
- **When:** Whenever you feel pulled to give in to avoid conflict, or when a clear line has been reached.
- **Ref:** [00:14:34](transcript.md#001434-tool-establishing-boundaries)

### "I Believe You"
- **What:** Validate the child's (or person's) internal experience without necessarily agreeing with the behavior.
- **How:** When someone is upset, say "I believe you" — meaning: I believe your feelings are real, I believe this is hard for you. No "but." No pivot to correction. Optionally follow with "tell me more."
- **When:** During emotional moments, meltdowns, expressions of pain or fear — and in adult relationships and the workplace equally.
- **Ref:** [00:44:48](transcript.md#004448-tool-i-believe-you-confidence-safety-other-relationships)

### Repair After Rupture
- **What:** Return to the relationship after a blow-up, mistake, or moment of poor parenting.
- **How:** Use a self-regulating phrase first ("I'm a good parent having a hard time"). Then approach the child: "I'm sorry I yelled. That wasn't okay. It's not your fault." Keep it simple. Slip a note under the door if they won't engage directly. If they reject the repair, try again later.
- **When:** As soon as you are regulated enough to approach without escalating further.
- **Ref:** [00:57:07](transcript.md#005707-tool-repair-apologies-rejecting-apology)

### Good Apology Framework
- **What:** A realistic apology that works under real-world conditions.
- **How:** Minimum viable apology: "I'm sorry I yelled." Optional additions: "Just like you, I'm working on managing my emotions. Next time, even when I'm frustrated, I'll try to stay calm." Optionally: "It's not your fault." Avoid "I'm sorry you felt that way" and "I'm sorry but…" — neither is a real apology.
- **When:** After any rupture. Even brief apologies are powerful.
- **Ref:** [01:01:04](transcript.md#010104-tool-good-apologies)

### Most Generous Interpretation (MGI)
- **What:** Before reacting to rudeness or difficult behavior, ask: what is the most generous interpretation of what this person is doing?
- **How:** When a child says "I hate you" or a teen slams a door, pause and ask internally: "What might be going on for them that would make this make sense?" Then respond to the underlying need, not the surface behavior. Doing nothing in the moment is often the best first move.
- **When:** Whenever you feel triggered by someone's behavior.
- **Ref:** [01:04:37](transcript.md#010437-tool-rudeness-disrespect-most-generous-interpretation)

### Pilot Analogy for Sturdy Leadership
- **What:** Conceptual frame for staying calm during others' emotional storms.
- **How:** Think of yourself as the pilot of a plane in turbulence. Passengers panic; the pilot stays calm — not because there is no turbulence, but because their job is to navigate it. Your calm is what stabilizes others. If a pilot reroutes because a passenger threatens to be upset, everyone is less safe.
- **When:** During tantrums, outbursts, or any emotional escalation in a child or adult.
- **Ref:** [01:12:32](transcript.md#011232-walking-on-eggshells-pilot-analogy-emotional-outbursts-sturdy-leadership)

### Deeply Feeling Kid Protocol
- **What:** Specialized approach for children with intense emotional range and sensory sensitivity.
- **How:** Before the next triggering event (e.g., family movie night where they normally melt down), tell the child exactly what will happen: "Tonight it is your sister's turn. I know you will be upset. If you are screaming, I will bring you to your room, sit with you, and stay there. I am not scared of your feelings." Practice saying this aloud — record yourself to assess how sturdy you sound. Hold the limit while staying present.
- **When:** With children who hiss, growl, scratch, or sustain tantrums far longer than typical; children who push parents away when they need them most.
- **Ref:** [01:20:49](transcript.md#012049-deeply-feeling-kids-fears-sensory-overload)

### "I'm Noticing" Check-In
- **What:** A non-pressuring way to open a door for emotional communication.
- **How:** Instead of "What's wrong?" say "I'm noticing you seem off today — I'm here if you want to talk." Walk away. Do not require a response. Use "I wonder…" phrasing to lower defensiveness further.
- **When:** With teens, introverts, or anyone who shuts down under direct questioning.
- **Ref:** [01:55:24](transcript.md#015524-tool-im-noticing-asking-questions-emotional-regulation)

### Relationship to Self — Driver and Passengers
- **What:** Apply the same boundaries-and-validation framework internally.
- **How:** When a strong feeling arises, say "it makes sense" — validate it without letting it dictate behavior. Picture yourself as the driver and your feelings as passengers: "You're a part of me, not all of me." Set an internal boundary when the feeling pushes toward harmful action.
- **When:** When overwhelmed by anxiety, anger, hurt, or any intense emotion — especially in parenting moments that trigger personal history.
- **Ref:** [01:51:57](transcript.md#015157-grace-parenthood-parenting-job-description-relationship-to-self)

### Family Meetings
- **What:** Structured, brief, regular family check-ins that normalize communication and problem-solving.
- **How:** Short (15–20 min), with a loose agenda. Frame it as "same team against the problem, not me against you." Bring a pad of paper, write down everyone's ideas, and cross out the unreasonable ones together. Each person can share something going well and something hard.
- **When:** Weekly or biweekly, especially during high-stress periods or with teens navigating curfews, screen-time, or ongoing conflicts.
- **Ref:** [02:09:58](transcript.md#020958-saying-i-love-you-teenagers-family-meetings)

### Frustration Tolerance Practice
- **What:** Deliberately allow children (and yourself) to experience manageable frustration without rescuing.
- **How:** When a child is frustrated by a task, be present but do not immediately solve it. Acknowledge the feeling: "That's hard. I believe you. That's the exact right feeling to be having." Draw the "learning space" — the gap between not knowing and succeeding — and name it as the place where growth happens. Resist the impulse to eliminate the discomfort. Lengthen the amount of time the child tolerates being in that space.
- **When:** During homework, chores, screen-time limits, puzzles, drawing — any moment where the child hits resistance.
- **Ref:** [01:41:20](transcript.md#014120-tool-tolerating-frustration-screen-time-learning)

### Handling Wayward Teens and Substance Use
- **What:** Protocol for when a teen is disengaging, withdrawing, or using substances.
- **How:** Assess impact on overall developmental functioning (grades, social connections, family engagement, activities). If functioning is declining across domains, seek additional help. Frame getting help as a strength, not punishment: "I love you so much that I'm willing to do things that make you unhappy with me." Maintain connection — a teen who feels disconnected from family has more risk. Do not give teens full control of decisions about their own safety.
- **When:** When withdrawal, substance use, or high-risk behavior appears; when walking on eggshells around a teen is the norm.
- **Ref:** [02:22:08](transcript.md#022208-tool-wayward-teens-marijuana-substance-use-getting-additional-help)

### Addressing Entitlement
- **What:** Reframe and redirect entitled behavior by understanding it as fear of frustration, then building tolerance.
- **How:** "I notice you seem really frustrated that you didn't get what you wanted. That's real." Then hold the limit. Do not collapse the boundary to relieve the discomfort. Build in small doses of frustration deliberately (boring errands, waiting, chores without pay). Use the phrase "just because I can doesn't mean I will."
- **When:** When children (or adults) react disproportionately to not getting their way; in families where financial resources make it easy to eliminate all frustration.
- **Ref:** [02:34:26](transcript.md#023426-tool-entitlement-fear-frustration)

### Chores Without Pay
- **What:** Assign household responsibilities that are intrinsically motivated rather than reward-driven.
- **How:** Frame chores as "sometimes your life involves doing boring things as part of being a good human." Do not attach payment to basic household participation. If a separate allowance is desired, keep it structurally separate from chores so the motivation for contributing remains internal.
- **When:** As early as possible; ongoing throughout childhood and adolescence.
- **Ref:** [02:41:57](transcript.md#024157-tool-experiencing-frustration-chores-allowance)
