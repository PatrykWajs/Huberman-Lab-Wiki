## [00:06:06] Vision & Brain; Retina

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. E.J. Chichilnisky, welcome. Good to see you. For the audience, we are friends — we go way back. E.J. has been a few years or more ahead of me in the science game, and the best way to describe you and your work, E.J., is you're an astronaut — you go places no one else has been willing to go before. He developed new technologies in order to do that, all with the bold mission of trying to understand how the nervous system, which of course includes the brain, works, and how to make it better with engineering. So today we are going to get into all of that, but just to start off and get everybody on the same page, maybe we could take a moment and talk about the brain and nervous system and what it consists of that allows it to do all the sorts of things we're going to get into — like see things in our environment and respond to those things. What's your one-to-five minute version of how the brain works?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** I don't have a one-to-five minute version of how the brain works, but I can tell you how I think vision is initiated in the brain. Vision is initiated in the retina of the eye, which is a sheet of neural tissue at the rear of the eye that captures the light that is incident on the eye, transforms that light into electrical signals, and then begins a cascade of processing that ultimately leads to what we experience as seeing. The retina then sends information via the optic nerve to the rest of the brain, and what's remarkable is that the retina is really the beginning of this entire visual hierarchy. It's the first place where light gets transformed into neural signals that the brain can begin to work with.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the retina is actually part of the brain itself — it's brain tissue extruded into the eye during development. So you're essentially looking at two pieces of the brain when you're looking at someone's eyes.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Exactly right. The retina is indeed a piece of the central nervous system. It develops from the same tissue as the brain, and it's been extruded out into the eyeball. So when you look into somebody's eye and you see the retina, you are literally looking at brain tissue. And because it's at the periphery of the body, we have relatively direct access to it. That's actually one of the reasons why the retina has been so studied and so well understood relative to other parts of the brain.

## [00:11:23] Retina & Visual Processing

**Andrew Huberman:** In its full beauty and detail, three layers of cells line the back of the eye — like a pie crust — and somehow take light that comes into the eye, the lens focuses that light, and transforms it into neural signals, then processes them within the retina. Let's take a deep dive into the retina with the understanding that in part thanks to your work and the work of others, this is perhaps the best understood piece of the brain.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** I think it's a solid argument that it's the best understood piece of the brain. The retina begins with a sheet of cells called photoreceptor cells that are highly specialized — these are cells that essentially don't exist anywhere else in the brain. What they do is transform light energy into electrical signals in neurons. They are very specialized, very demanding cells that require a lot of maintenance and they die relatively easily, which is what gives rise to some of the forms of blindness. Those are the so-called pixel detectors — tiny cells called photoreceptors where each one captures light from a particular location in the world.

That sheet of cells has done that initial transduction process where light is converted into neural signals. The second layer is responsible for processing those signals — taking the raw pixel information and beginning to extract features from it. The third layer — the retinal ganglion cells — is where the processed information gets sent on to the brain via the optic nerve. These ganglion cells are remarkable because there are about 20 distinct types of them, and each type is doing something different. Each type is extracting a different feature from the visual scene — some respond to bright increments, some to dark decrements, some to motion, some to color, some to specific patterns. And each of these types covers the entire retina, so every point in your visual field is being processed in 20 different ways simultaneously.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's extraordinary — it means the eye is not just a camera passing a raw image to the brain. It's already doing significant computation before the signal ever reaches the visual cortex.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** That's exactly right. The retina is not just a passive sensor — it is an active computational system. And this is why understanding the retina is so important. The computations that the retina performs are not simple. They involve complex nonlinear operations, inhibitory circuits, integration of signals across space and time. The visual cortex, when it receives signals from the retina, is already receiving a highly processed representation of the world, not raw pixels.

## [00:18:37] Vision in Humans & Other Animals, Color

**Andrew Huberman:** We could think about how the human retina — and therefore vision in our species — differs from some extreme examples of vision in other species. For instance, the mantis shrimp sees 60 to 100 different variations of color that we are essentially blind to because their photoreceptors can detect very subtle differences in long wavelengths of light. Pit vipers sense heat emissions. I raise this because the human neuroretina is such an incredible example of extracting features from the visual world, but it's also worth reminding everyone that it's not a complete representation of what's out there. There's a lot in light that we don't see because our neuroretina just can't turn it into electrical signals.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Right. Color is a great example. We experience a rich sensation of color when we look at the world, and that sensation is constructed by a relatively small number of photoreceptor types — in our case, three cone types that are sensitive to different wavelengths of light. There's nothing special about those three wavelengths — they happen to be the three types of cones that we have, and the visual system constructs a representation of color based on the ratios of activation across those three cone types.

Mantis shrimp, as you mentioned, have many more types of photoreceptors that are sensitive to different wavelengths — in some cases upward of 12 or 16 different types. Now interestingly, it's not clear that mantis shrimp actually experience a richer color world than we do — their visual processing might work differently — but the point is that the nature of the visual world that an animal constructs depends entirely on the photoreceptors they have. We can't see ultraviolet light, many insects can. We can't see infrared, pit vipers can sense heat radiation. So the visual world we experience is a very partial, very constructed, very species-specific representation of physical reality.

## [00:23:01] Studying the Human Retina

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about these incredible experiments that your laboratory has been doing for several decades. I've had the privilege of sitting in on some of these experiments, and they are very involved. If you could just walk us through one of these experiments — you're in your laboratory at Stanford and you get a phone call, someone says "I got a retina" — what happens next?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** We scramble like crazy. We drop everything we're doing, cancel all our appointments, and get ourselves ready for 48 hours of non-stop work down in the lab getting as much data as we possibly can from the retina. The most exciting example of what you just said is when we get a human retina — when, for example, there's a donor who has died and the retina is available for research. We jump at that opportunity.

**Andrew Huberman:** How soon after the person is deceased do you need to get the eyeball in order to get the retina in a condition that would allow you to record electrical signals from it?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** A few minutes. The way we typically get these eyes is from brain-dead individuals — people who are legally and medically dead but whose hearts are still pumping, and therefore their retinas are still alive and functioning. When those individuals' bodies are used for organ donation, we can benefit from that organ donation setup that organ distribution centers do to save many people's lives and also to promote research. We sometimes get those retinas, and that begins the experiment for us.

**Andrew Huberman:** Once you have the retina, what does the recording setup look like?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** The retina is placed, photoreceptor side down, onto an array of microelectrodes — you might think of it as a very fine bed of nails. We then present visual stimuli to the photoreceptors, and we record the electrical signals coming out of the ganglion cells. We can essentially watch the retina work — see which cells respond to which visual inputs, measure their response properties, and map out the functional organization of the tissue. The data we collect in those 48 hours of intensive work is extraordinary.

## [00:31:16] Cell Types

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk a little bit about cell types. You mentioned there are about 20 different types of retinal ganglion cells — RGCs. These cover the entire retina so that each cell type is extracting a different set of features from the visual world. Cell types is such an important theme in the field of neuroscience and indeed in all of biology, but it's something we haven't talked about very much on this podcast. How do you figure out if you have a cell type? How do you know it's a cell type and not just a variation?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** This is a really fundamental question — it's one of the most important questions in all of neuroscience right now. A cell type is defined by a cluster of properties — morphological, genetic, functional — that all hang together consistently from animal to animal and from experiment to experiment. In the retina, the clearest definition of a cell type is a set of cells that have the same morphology, the same genetic signature, the same response properties to visual stimuli, and that tile the retina in a regular mosaic. That last point is key: each cell type covers the entire retina with very little overlap, so that every point in the visual field is represented by one cell of each type.

The way we identify cell types in practice is by looking at the patterns of electrical activity we record from the ganglion cells. Cells of the same type respond similarly to similar stimuli, and the pattern of responses clusters in a way that you can see in the data. Different clusters correspond to different cell types, and you can validate those clusters by looking at the morphology of the cells using anatomical techniques.

**Andrew Huberman:** And when you say there are roughly 20 types, is that a number that's settled, or is it still being debated?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** The number is still being refined. In mice, recent single-cell RNA sequencing studies suggest there may be 40 or more types. In primates, we've identified around 15 to 20 functionally distinct types using our recording methods. The exact number depends partly on how finely you want to subdivide — some types that were previously thought to be one type have been found to have subtypes. But the broad organizational principle is clear: the ganglion cell layer is not a homogeneous population, it's a diverse collection of specialized cell types, each doing something distinct.

## [00:36:00] Determining Cell Function in Retina

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's draw a mental image. The retina is taken out of a deceased individual and placed on a bed of nails of electrodes. Those electrodes can detect electrical signals from the ganglion cells. You can shine light onto the retina and see how the retinal ganglion cells respond — what electrical signals they would transmit to the brain if they were still connected to a brain. How do you decide what to show the retina? How do you determine cell type and function if you don't know what specific things to show it?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** What we show now reflects decades of accumulated knowledge. We know that certain cell types respond primarily to increments of light — when light gets brighter than it was. Another cell type fires when it gets darker. Some cell types respond primarily to large targets in the visual world; others respond better to small targets. Some respond to different wavelengths of light. There are cell types that are still being characterized — that respond to very specific features we didn't anticipate.

We typically start by showing the retina a sequence of random visual patterns — random flickering stimuli — which allows us to efficiently characterize the response properties of every cell we're recording from. The mathematical technique we use is called spike-triggered averaging: for each cell, we look at what the visual stimulus was doing in the moments just before the cell fired, and we average those stimulus frames together. What you get is a map of what each cell "likes" — what visual pattern drives it most strongly.

**Andrew Huberman:** What did you find the most fascinating or surprising cell type?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** One of the most surprising things to me early in my career was discovering how diverse and specialized these cell types are — that each one is doing something really specific and that the diversity is much richer than previous models had suggested. Some of the most surprising recent findings involve cell types that respond to very specific motion patterns — not just "there's motion here" but specific directions of motion, specific speeds, specific spatial frequencies. The retina is already doing motion analysis before the signal reaches the brain, in ways we're still figuring out.

## [00:43:39] Retinal Cell Types & Stimuli

**Andrew Huberman:** You spend a lot of time in these experiments, giving up two nights of sleep — which is kind of incredible for someone who clearly appreciates sleep. What's the wildest cell type response you've ever encountered? What did it do, what did it respond to?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** There are cells that respond to increasingly red portions of the visual scene, or to decreasingly green portions. There are cells that respond very specifically to particular patterns of motion. There are cells that seem to be selective for very high-frequency temporal patterns. Each time you discover a new response type, you think — well, what is the brain doing with this? Why did it evolve to extract this particular feature? And that question leads you down a rabbit hole of trying to understand the ecological constraints that shaped the visual system.

**Andrew Huberman:** And given that the retina is the best understood piece of the brain, and given that there are roughly 20 cell types — not 20 million — it seems tractable to understand them in their entirety. Where are we in that process?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** We're getting close to a complete functional catalog of the major cell types in the primate retina. The key remaining challenge is understanding the rarer types — the ones that make up only a small fraction of the ganglion cells and are therefore hard to record from in sufficient numbers to characterize. And then there's the next level of question: not just what do these cells respond to in simplified laboratory stimuli, but what are they doing during natural vision? How are they encoding the complex, rapidly changing, behaviorally relevant visual scenes that an animal encounters in real life? That's a much harder question, and it's where a lot of the field is moving.

## [00:49:27] Retinal Prostheses, Implants

**Andrew Huberman:** All of this work understanding the signals the retina sends to the brain — what good is it to the everyday person? What medical applications does it provide in terms of potentially restoring vision to the blind? And perhaps even broader — this notion of neuroengineering, taking this information and creating devices that can help our nervous system function better, maybe even function at superphysiological levels. There's a lot of interest in this due to Neuralink — Elon Musk is out there very vocal about chips being implanted in people's brains that would allow them to be in conversation with many people simultaneously, perhaps giving people super memory. Where is this headed?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Let me start with restoration of vision, which is the most immediate and to me the most compelling application. There are millions of people in the world who have lost their vision due to diseases of the photoreceptors — diseases like retinitis pigmentosa or age-related macular degeneration — where the photoreceptors have died, but the rest of the retina and the optic nerve and the visual cortex are largely intact. This is the target patient population for a retinal prosthesis — a device that bypasses the dead photoreceptors and directly stimulates the surviving ganglion cells.

The existing retinal implants — the ones that are currently FDA-approved and have been implanted in patients — do restore some form of light perception in completely blind patients. They can help people navigate environments, detect the presence of objects, in some cases read large print. But they fall far short of anything we would call normal vision, and the reason is that they don't speak the language of the retina — they don't activate the right cell types in the right patterns to produce a faithful representation of the visual scene.

**Andrew Huberman:** What does that mean in practice? If someone has a current implant and they're looking at a face, what do they see?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** They might see something like a pattern of blobs of light. The spatial resolution is very limited because the electrodes are spaced relatively far apart and because each electrode activates many cells nonspecifically. And because the stimulation is not cell-type-specific, the brain receives a scrambled mix of signals that doesn't correspond to what the eye would normally send. Patients report that it takes extensive training to interpret what they see, and even after training, the quality of vision is very limited.

The vision that E.J.'s lab is working toward is fundamentally different. The goal is to build a device that truly speaks the language of the retina — that activates the right cell types, in the right spatial patterns, at the right times — so that the ganglion cells send signals to the brain that are indistinguishable from what a healthy retina would send when looking at a visual scene.

**Andrew Huberman:** And how close are you to that?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** We have demonstrated in isolated human retina — the retina on the electrode array in the lab — that we can stimulate individual cells with high specificity, that we can selectively activate different cell types using different stimulation parameters, and that we can reproduce patterns of activity that are very close to what a healthy retina would produce. The challenge now is to miniaturize all of this into an implantable device and to demonstrate that it works in vivo — in a living eye. That's a major engineering challenge, and it's the focus of a lot of work in our lab and in collaborating labs right now.

## [01:00:25] Artificial Retina, Augmenting Vision

**Andrew Huberman:** Talk to me about the longer-term vision, if you'll excuse the pun. You've talked about restoring vision, but you've also hinted at the possibility of augmenting vision — giving people capabilities that no human currently has. Could you actually build a device that gives someone hawk-like resolution, or the ability to see ultraviolet light, or to perceive motion at speeds no human can currently detect?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Yes, in principle. If you have an implant that is controlling the signals going into the visual cortex, there's no reason in principle why those signals have to correspond to what a normal human eye would produce. You could feed in signals that encode information from a camera with much higher resolution than the human eye. You could feed in signals that encode infrared information, or ultraviolet information, or even non-visual information — sound, say, represented as a visual pattern.

Here's an analogy I like to use. We know that we can drive down the road and have a hands-free phone call quite safely, because the visual information and the auditory information are being processed by different parts of the brain and don't interfere with each other. But you cannot read your text messages and drive safely, because both of those tasks are using the same visual processing machinery and they interfere. If you could pipe text messages into the visual cortex through a channel that's separate from the normal visual channel — say, through a dedicated set of ganglion cells — you might be able to receive that information without interfering with your normal vision. You'd essentially be adding a new input channel to your visual cortex.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's extraordinary. And it illustrates why your basic science work on cell types is directly relevant — because you need to know which channels the brain has available before you can think about adding new ones.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Exactly. The basic science tells you the architecture of the system, and that architecture defines what's possible. If you don't understand the cell types, you don't know what channels exist, and you can't design a device that uses them effectively.

## [01:07:12] Neuroengineering, Neuroaugmentation & Specificity

**Andrew Huberman:** Let me narrate the story arc of what you've done. You started with the understanding that the neural retina is perhaps the best place to try and understand how the brain works — because of its arrangement, the cell types, its accessibility. You spent about two and a half decades doing these intensive experiments on human retinas and other retinas, recording the different cell types with these high-density electrode arrays, figuring out what the cell types are and what they do. You gained an understanding of how light is transformed into different types of electrical signals that encode different features in the visual scene.

Then comes the challenge of developing neuroengineering tools to try and stimulate specific cell types in a way that mimics their normal patterns of activation — not activating huge chunks of retina indiscriminately, but with the same precision that light stimulation of these cells in the intact human eye provides. You needed to do all that earlier work understanding what does the normal retina do before you could develop a prosthetic device.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** That's right. And the specificity point is crucial. Let me give you a sense of what happens with current prosthetics that lack that specificity. When you electrically stimulate a large region of retina non-specifically, you're activating all cell types simultaneously — the ones that respond to bright increments, the ones that respond to dark decrements, the color-selective ones, the motion-selective ones. The signals that reach the brain are a mix that doesn't correspond to any natural visual experience. It's like trying to communicate with someone by pressing random keys on a piano — you're making sounds, but it's not music.

What we're trying to do is more like a highly skilled pianist who can play exactly the right notes at exactly the right times. The key is cell-type specificity — activating the specific subsets of ganglion cells that would normally be active when viewing a particular visual scene. Our research over the past 25 years has been building the understanding of what those patterns should look like, and developing the stimulation techniques that can achieve that specificity.

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you achieve cell-type specificity with electrical stimulation? Isn't electricity diffuse by nature?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** That's the central challenge. Electrical current does spread, and a single electrode will inevitably activate cells near it. The approach we've developed uses multiple electrodes simultaneously, with carefully calibrated current patterns, to selectively activate specific cells while avoiding others. It's related to the technique of beam-forming used in ultrasound or radio transmission — by combining signals from multiple sources, you can create interference patterns that concentrate energy at specific points in space.

We've also found that different cell types have different electrical properties — different thresholds, different temporal response characteristics — so there's some intrinsic selectivity that you can exploit even with a single electrode by using particular waveform shapes and amplitudes.

## [01:17:01] Building a Smart Device, AI

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's push on the "smart device" concept. Could you put a little chip onto the retina that uses AI and machine learning to learn something about the specific tissue it's embedded in?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Absolutely, and that's exactly what we're building. The device works in three simple steps. Step one: record electrical activity. Recognize which cells are there, when they're firing, what their electrical properties are — identify the cells and cell types in this specific circuit, in this specific human. That's step one. Step two: stimulate and record. Figure out — this electrode activates cell number 14 with 50% probability, this electrode activates cell number 12 if you pass a microamp of current with this probability. Make a big table that tells you how these electrodes talk to these cells. That's calibration by stimulation and recording. Then step three: when you have a visual image and you want to represent it in the pattern of activity of these cells, you say — I know from decades of basic science what these cells ought to be doing when looking at this image, and I know from my calibration exactly how to drive them to produce that pattern. So you invert the problem: given the desired output, compute the optimal input.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the AI or machine learning component enters in which step?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Primarily in steps one and two — in recognizing the cells, classifying their types, and building the map of electrode-to-cell relationships. These are pattern recognition problems that machine learning is extremely well suited for. As the device runs, it can continue to monitor the tissue and update its model — if a cell dies, if the electrode moves slightly, if the tissue changes — the device adapts. That adaptability is what makes it truly "smart."

## [01:20:02] Neural Prosthesis, Paralysis; Specificity

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to make sure people appreciate the spectrum of specificity in neural intervention. At one extreme you have electroconvulsive therapy — electrical shock therapy — which is essentially completely non-specific: stimulating all neurons in the brain simultaneously. And yet for certain cases of severe depression, the clinical outcomes are actually pretty impressive. At the other extreme is what you're describing — cell-type-specific activation of individual ganglion cells. Where is this whole business of neural prosthesis going outside of the visual system?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** There's absolutely impressive work happening in other systems — particularly in reading out signals from the motor cortex or language cortex in order to help people communicate or control prosthetic limbs. These are paralyzed people who have lost the ability to move or to speak, and researchers have been able to implant electrodes in their motor or language cortex, record the patterns of activity that correspond to intended movements or words, and translate those patterns into outputs — moving a cursor on a screen, typing, even producing synthesized speech. The demonstrations in the last few years have been remarkable.

**Andrew Huberman:** And do you see those systems converging? The read-out systems being combined with write-in systems like what you're building?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** Absolutely. The read-out and write-in capabilities are complementary, and the most powerful systems will combine both. For vision, you need to write in — put information into the visual system. For motor control in a paralyzed patient, you need to read out — extract the intended motor commands. But for truly bidirectional systems — for example, a prosthetic hand that can both move and feel — you need both. And the fundamental principles are the same: understand the neural code, build electronics that can speak that code, achieve specificity.

This is one of the reasons you can tell so much about people's inner state from their eyes — somebody who is sleep deprived, it's not just the droopiness of their eyelids or the circles under their eyes; there's an aliveness to the eyes that varies. And the yogis talk about people who "show up at the level of their eyes." There's also very non-abstract stuff these days — looking through the eye at the retina, the way ophthalmologists do — there's a lot of diagnostic capability.

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** There's some interesting and increasing evidence that looking at the neural retina — because it is a piece of the brain with neurons that have the potential to both thrive or degenerate — can provide a window into whether or not there are forms of degeneration such as Alzheimer's and other forms of neurodegeneration deeper within the brain that one can't image directly because of the opacity of the skull. So imaging the eye to determine if someone is developing Alzheimer's — because you have a direct view into a little piece of the brain — can be a good signal for what's going on in your brain even beyond what you can see in the retina itself.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's remarkable. And it raises a broader question about neurodegeneration and neuroplasticity — the brain's capacity to change with experience. What's your view on adult neuroplasticity? Is the adult brain capable of significant rewiring, or is that mostly a childhood phenomenon?

**E.J. Chichilnisky:** The adult brain clearly retains significant capacity for change — we know this from the studies of people who learn new skills, who recover from strokes, who adapt to sensory loss. But the nature of plasticity in the adult brain is different from that in the developing brain. It's more constrained, more effortful, and tends to occur within circuits that already exist rather than generating entirely new circuit architectures.

For the retinal prosthesis, this raises an important question: if a patient has been blind for many years, is their visual cortex still capable of processing visual input? The answer seems to be yes, in many cases — especially if the patient had vision early in life. The circuits don't disappear; they go quiet. When you restore input, they can be reactivated. This is one of the most exciting and hopeful aspects of this work.
