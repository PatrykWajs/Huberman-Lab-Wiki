## [00:07:57] Back Anatomy: Spine, Vertebrae, Spinal Cord

For many people, the back simply means the spine — the bony stuff that runs down your middle. Of course, your spine doesn't just run down the middle: you've got your cervical spine up near your neck (don't ever forget that your neck is the upper part of your spine), you've got the thoracic spine through the chest and rib region, then the lumbar spine right below that, then the sacral spine in the pelvic region, and finally the coccyx, which is the very lowest part. It's important to understand that the back is made up not just of bony stuff, but it's also got stuff in between — which are the discs. The discs are the soft tissue that sit between the vertebrae: the vertebrae are the bones, and those discs have some durability to them but they can be compressed — they're a soft tissue that allows for mobility of the bony stuff. So you've got basically bone, disc, bone, disc, bone, disc along the spine.

Those discs allow for movement of the spine from side to side and also allow for some compression along the length of the spine. Sometimes today I'll refer to this as vertical compression. Running down through the middle of all these vertebrae and discs is the spinal cord — a tube-like neural structure that carries motor and sensory information between the brain and the body. The spinal cord is encased within those vertebrae, so the bony structure of the spine is actually doing double duty: it gives the body structure and it also protects the spinal cord. This is crucial to understand because when discs bulge or herniate, they can impinge on the nerves traveling through or exiting the spinal cord, and that impingement is often the direct cause of pain. On the outside of the vertebrae and discs, you also have a set of powerful muscles — the spinal erectors — that run the length of the spine and are critical for supporting posture, generating power, and stabilizing the entire column during movement.

## [00:12:07] Spinal Cord & Nerves; Herniated Discs

The spinal cord is a snake-like extension out the back of the brain. The brain and spinal cord together make up what we call the central nervous system, or CNS. The brain is encapsulated within the skull, and extending out from the brain stem you have the spinal cord — this tube-like neural structure that extends all the way down through the vertebrae and discs to your pelvic region. At multiple levels along the spine, nerve roots branch off to the left and right and travel out to innervate — that is, provide sensory and motor connection to — virtually every part of the body. This is why back problems can cause pain, tingling, or numbness not just in the back itself, but in the arms, legs, glutes, and even the feet. The specific distribution of where you feel those symptoms often tells a clinician which spinal level is affected.

Disc herniation occurs when the soft interior of the disc — the nucleus pulposus — pushes outward through the tougher outer ring — the annulus fibrosus. Think of the classic Oreo cookie analogy: the two cookies represent the vertebrae, the cream filling represents the disc's soft interior. When you squeeze the cookies evenly from top and bottom, the cream moves outward all around the circumference equally. But if you squeeze more on one side, the cream filling moves predominantly toward the opposite side — that's a herniation. That bulging disc material can then press against a nearby nerve root, causing the nerve to fire inappropriately and producing pain, tingling, or weakness along the distribution of that nerve. Not all herniations cause pain, and many resolve over time as the disc material retracts or the body reabsorbs it, but when they do cause pain the location and character of that pain provides important diagnostic information. The key point is that the spinal cord and nerve roots are extremely sensitive to pressure, and even a modest herniation in exactly the wrong location can produce significant symptoms.

## [00:19:50] Build Strong Pain-Free Back; Bulging Discs

It's important to understand that not all back pain is neural in nature — or rather, while all pain is ultimately a neural perception, the causes of that perception are varied. As Dr. Sean Mackey's biopsychosocial model of pain teaches us, pain involves psychology, prediction, history, inflammation, and many other factors. What you'll also learn today is that there are many structural and biomechanical factors you can address: creating stability of your feet and toes, the position of your pelvis, your neck, your chin — all these things, as well as the spinal erector muscles in your lower back and your abdominal core, combine to create the right milieu for the spine and back to function at its best. The vertebrae, the bony segments surrounding the spinal cord, and between those the discs — the soft tissue that also have the spinal cord traveling through them — work together with all the surrounding musculature to allow for movement while maintaining stability.

The key classification that matters most in everyday practice is the distinction between a bulging disc and a ruptured disc. A bulging disc (herniation) means the disc material has moved outward but the outer ring is still intact — this is often reversible. A ruptured disc means the outer ring has torn and nuclear material has actually escaped — this is less reversible and sometimes requires surgical intervention. Most back pain from disc pathology involves bulging rather than rupture, and much of today's episode is focused on what you can do conservatively — through specific exercises, posture adjustments, and daily habits — to reverse bulging, relieve impingement, and build the surrounding musculature so that your spine is robust against future injury.

## [00:24:26] Back Pain & Professional Evaluation; Tool: Spine Self-Assessment

Before getting into the specific protocols and exercises for strengthening and pain-proofing your back, it's worth mentioning something that was first told to me by Dr. Stuart McGill, who is a world expert in spine physiology and anatomy and has worked with elite athletes as well as everyday people. In discussions with Stu, he raises some really important points. First of all, if you have back pain, you need a proper assessment and diagnosis — it would be irresponsible to try to diagnose your own back pain in this format because there are just so many things that are specific to each circumstance. Some people get back pain because of overuse of certain motor patterns — maybe they are avid golfers who always swing a certain way, building strength in certain pathways while developing weaknesses on the opposite side. Other people have an injury — a spinal compression injury or even a ruptured disc. Ruptured discs often need surgery, and I don't want to give the impression that all back pain can be resolved without surgery. If you have severe or persistent back pain that you are not able to alleviate with non-surgical methods, please talk to a back expert, perhaps even a spine surgeon.

That said, Dr. McGill has pointed out that anyone can use a simple self-assessment to start understanding their own back. The self-assessment involves methodically moving through your spine's range of motion — forward flexion (bending forward), extension (arching backward), lateral flexion (bending to each side), and rotation (twisting) — while paying attention to when and where pain occurs and whether it increases or decreases with specific movements. This information is useful: pain that worsens with flexion and improves with extension often suggests a disc herniation that may benefit from extension-based movements like cobra push-ups. Pain that worsens with extension and improves with flexion suggests a different cause, possibly facet joint issues. Pain with lateral flexion to one side that eases when bending the other direction may indicate compression of one side versus the other. By systematically testing these movements and noticing the pain pattern, you can begin to identify which directional bias your spine prefers and use that knowledge to choose the most appropriate rehabilitative approach. The important caveat is that this self-assessment is a starting point, not a diagnosis — professional evaluation remains essential for persistent or severe pain.

## [00:36:29] Tool: McGill Big 3 Exercises, Curl-Up

The protocols that allow you to strengthen your back begin with the so-called McGill's Big Three — named after Dr. Stuart McGill, who has done a tremendous amount of work in the research and practical realms and has developed a number of different protocols for strengthening and de-paining the spine. Everyone Huberman spoke to in the physical therapy community, the medical community, and the sports community agrees these three exercises are extremely useful for everybody. The one caveat is that McGill himself has noted he doesn't want the Big Three to be viewed as the be-all, end-all of back strengthening and pain relief — they are a foundational starting point, and today's episode covers many additional protocols as well.

The first of the Big Three is called the curl-up. The curl-up is in many ways a replacement for the sit-up. Most people are familiar with ab crunches and sit-ups where you lie down, bend your knees, put your hands behind your head, and essentially sit up to contract the abdominals — but it turns out that if you have back pain, and even if you don't, that is probably the least efficient and perhaps even the worst thing you can do because it loads the discs in a way that can worsen herniation. The curl-up is different: you lie on your back with one knee bent and one leg flat, or both legs flat. You place your hands palms-down underneath the natural arch of your lower back — not pressing into it, just maintaining that natural lumbar curve — and then you lift your head and upper shoulders off the ground only slightly, hold briefly, then lower back down. The key points are: maintain that neutral lumbar arch by keeping your hands underneath your back, do not flatten your back into the floor, keep your neck in a neutral position without straining, and generate the contraction from your abdominals rather than your neck muscles. A typical set involves about eight repetitions with a six-second hold at the top, followed by brief rest between sets — though the specific protocols can be found in McGill's books.

## [00:44:40] Tool: McGill Big 3 Exercises, Side Plank

The second exercise in the McGill Big Three is the side plank. Huberman confesses he has always disliked side planks, which — as he notes — tells him he should be doing them. He has started doing side planks after talking to Dr. McGill, recognizing that even without current back pain, building a strong spine is essential for healthspan and lifespan. Most people think a side plank is simply lying on one side, putting one arm down on the floor, and stacking your feet to make yourself as stiff as a plank as possible. While that is one way to do it, most people will get the most benefit in terms of strengthening the spine and potentially alleviating back pain if they build up through a set of incremental steps, making sure the form on each step is absolutely perfect.

The proper progression begins with knees slightly bent at roughly a 30–40 degree angle. You lie on your side on the floor, put your forearm down, and lift your hips off the ground so that your body forms a diagonal line from knee to shoulder. Hold this position for a brief period, then lower and repeat. As you develop strength, you progress to having straighter legs — eventually reaching the full side plank with legs fully extended and stacked. The key points of proper side plank form are: keep your body in a straight line from head to feet (no sagging at the hips), keep your top hand either on your hip or extended upward for an added challenge, breathe normally throughout rather than holding your breath, and avoid rotating your torso — your chest should face directly to the side, not toward the floor or the ceiling. McGill's recommended approach involves shorter holds (ten seconds) done for multiple repetitions rather than one long continuous hold, because the shorter hold-and-release pattern builds both endurance and neural stability while minimizing the risk of compensation and breakdown of form.

## [00:53:13] Tool: McGill Big 3 Exercises, Bird Dog; Back Pain

The third exercise in McGill's Big Three is the bird dog. The bird dog is performed on all fours on the ground: you extend one arm forward and simultaneously extend the opposite leg backward. So if you are down on all fours and extend your right hand in front of you, you extend your left leg behind you. A couple of key points: make the extended hand into a fist, because generating that strong neural contraction keeps your upper body parallel to the floor with no tilting from side to side. You also want to make sure you don't raise the leg too high — you are not trying to arch your lower back. The goal is to raise the arm and leg to approximately parallel with the floor or just slightly above, generating a stable, controlled position. The extended arm and opposite leg should be in the same plane as your torso — imagine a tabletop resting on your back; you want to keep everything level enough that nothing would fall off that tabletop.

Hold the position for roughly eight to ten seconds, then lower and switch sides. As with the curl-up and side plank, McGill recommends sets with brief holds rather than one long sustained contraction. An important addition to the bird dog is to trace a small rectangle with the extended hand (and optionally the extended foot) before returning to the starting position — this introduces a small amount of controlled dynamic challenge while maintaining spinal stability. The set-and-rep scheme recommended by McGill for all three Big Three exercises follows a pyramid pattern: for example, you might do one set of six reps, then one set of four reps, then one set of two reps, with brief rest between sets. The rationale is that front-loading the most reps when muscles are freshest — and tapering to fewer reps as fatigue accumulates — maximizes the strength-building stimulus while minimizing the risk of injury from form breakdown under fatigue. Together, the curl-up, side plank, and bird dog target the anterior, lateral, and posterior aspects of spinal stability, respectively, making them a remarkably complete foundational program.

## [01:05:37] Tool: Back Pain & Oreo Analogy, Bar Hang

Before discussing other strengthening protocols, Huberman describes two approaches that many people find beneficial specifically for alleviating existing back pain. The first relates back to the Oreo cookie analogy: vertebrae are the hard cookies, and the disc is the soft cream filling. Uneven compression on one side pushes the cream filling (disc material) to the opposite side, causing herniation and potential nerve impingement. One of the most immediately effective tools for relieving that compression and giving the disc material a chance to rehydrate and retract is a simple bar hang. You find any bar or overhead horizontal structure, grasp it with both hands using an overhead grip, and simply hang. The key instruction is to keep your toes lightly touching the ground — not taking all the weight off your legs, but maintaining just a modest fraction of your body weight support through the toes. This toes-touching modification allows you to relax your spine into decompression gradually without the jarring effect of a full deadhang, and it also makes it easier to maintain the hang for a useful duration.

The rationale is straightforward: gravity compresses the spine throughout the day — every step you take, every load you carry, all of it adds compressive force that pushes the disc material outward. Hanging from a bar directly counteracts this by applying gentle longitudinal traction, allowing the disc space to widen slightly, encouraging disc material to retract away from nerve roots, and providing immediate pain relief in many cases. Huberman has used this protocol himself with considerable success. A good protocol is to hang for 30–60 seconds at a time, multiple times throughout the day, particularly after periods of prolonged sitting or standing. This is one of the lowest-barrier, highest-payoff interventions for acute disc-related back pain.

## [01:10:34] Time & Back Pain; Tool: Reversing Disc Herniation, Cobra Push-Ups

Huberman shares a personal anecdote: a few years ago while in Seattle giving a talk, he went to the gym that morning and performed weighted reverse bench dips. At some point something felt off in his back. As the morning progressed, he developed increasingly severe pain on the lower and mid right side of his back, down through the hip and leg — the classic distribution of sciatic-type pain. By the time he needed to walk back to the Space Needle for the talk, he was bent over at 20–30 degrees, barely able to stand upright. That experience motivated him to learn as much as possible about both the acute management and the long-term prevention of disc-related back pain.

One of the most powerful tools for reversing disc herniation — particularly posterior or posterolateral herniations where disc material bulges toward the back of the spine — is the cobra push-up, a movement borrowed from yoga and physical therapy. The cobra push-up involves lying face down on the floor (prone position), placing both hands flat on the ground approximately at chest level (like the top of a push-up), and then pressing your upper body up while keeping your hips and pelvis on or near the floor, allowing your lower back to arch passively. The rationale: in a prone position, the anterior (front) compression on the vertebrae increases slightly, which tends to push disc material in the posterior direction initially — but if you then extend the spine by pressing up into cobra, you reverse the pressure gradient and encourage disc material to migrate anteriorly (toward the front), away from the posterior nerve roots that are causing pain. Consistency is key: many clinicians, including Dr. Robin McKenzie (whose McKenzie Method is widely referenced), recommend performing cobra push-ups multiple times per day — sometimes as frequently as every hour or two during acute phases — to continuously encourage disc retraction. Huberman found that within a day or two of consistently performing cobra push-ups and bar hangs, his acute pain began to resolve substantially, and within a week he was largely pain-free. He emphasizes this anecdote represents his personal experience and that professional evaluation remains important for anyone with back pain.

## [01:21:28] Sciatica, Referred Pain, Herniated Disc

Many people hear about back pain and immediately think of sciatica. Sciatica is most commonly thought of as tingling, pain, or numbness — or some combination of the three — in the lower back, the glute region, the hip, and down the leg. Relieving sciatica involves a number of different approaches, and Huberman notes he will likely do an entire episode specifically about sciatica and other nerve pathway pain. For the time being, much sciatica is caused by exactly the sorts of things discussed so far — herniation or bulging of the disc, and impingement of the nerve. When that bulging is occurring within the lower lumbar region of the spine, it will often impinge on a nerve or nerves that innervate the glutes, the hip, and down the leg.

There's also something called referred pain — where you experience pain in one part of the body due to an impingement or disruption of a neural pathway elsewhere. The location of the pain is not always where the cause resides. This is why back pain can manifest as hip pain, knee pain, or even foot pain without any primary pathology in those locations. Much of what Huberman experienced personally — the severe hip and leg pain — could be characterized as sciatic-type pain arising from the lumbar spine. The practical takeaway is that pain shooting down the leg does not necessarily mean the leg is the problem; the spine — specifically a herniated disc impinging on a nerve root — is often the culprit, and treating the disc pathology is what resolves the peripheral symptom. This understanding guides the choice of interventions: cobra push-ups, bar hangs, and the McGill Big Three all work on the spine itself, which is the root cause.

## [01:24:21] Tool: Improve Spine Stability, Strengthen Neck

A set of protocols shared by Dr. Sean Wheeler addresses how structures both near and far from the spine — from the neck all the way to the feet — contribute to spinal stability. The first of these involves strengthening the muscles in the front of the neck. This might seem counterintuitive when talking about lower back health, but the neck and lower back are mechanically linked. When you look down repeatedly — at a phone, at a desk — you create a forward head position that shifts the center of gravity of your head forward significantly, placing enormous additional load on the cervical spine and, through the chain of the spinal erectors, increasing tension all the way down to the lumbar region. Strengthening the deep cervical flexors — the muscles at the front of the neck — helps counteract this forward head posture.

The simplest exercise involves performing what is called a chin tuck or cervical retraction: while sitting or standing, you gently retract your chin straight back (not downward), creating a slight double-chin appearance. Hold this position for a few seconds, then release. Doing this multiple times throughout the day — particularly after periods of looking down at a phone or computer — strengthens the cervical stabilizers and gradually pulls the head back over the spine to a more neutral position. You can also use gentle manual resistance: place one or two fingers on your forehead and press gently backward as you push your head forward into that resistance and hold, or vice versa — press fingers on the back of your head and push forward while resisting with the neck muscles. These isometric neck exercises build the deep cervical flexors and extensors without compressing the discs. Over time, a stronger neck in better alignment reduces the compensatory load on the mid- and lower-back musculature.

## [01:29:23] Tools: Strengthen Feet, Toe Spreading

The feet are the foundation of your entire body. Strengthening your feet has many benefits — it is something Huberman has been focusing on in the last couple of years and it has made a huge difference in terms of eliminating shin splints when running and alleviating a certain amount of lower back pain. The starting point for foot strengthening is an assessment: can you spread your toes such that none of them are touching one another, on both feet? Many people find they can do this on one foot but not the other. The ability to spread your toes says a lot about your ability to activate distal neural pathways — the motor circuits that control the small intrinsic muscles of the foot — and the health and strength of the arch and the plantar fascia.

If you cannot currently spread your toes, practice daily. Simply sit comfortably, look at your feet, and attempt to spread all five toes apart simultaneously. This sounds simple but requires neural relearning for many people, particularly those who have spent their lives in narrow, constricting footwear. Over weeks, the neural pathways strengthen and the ability to actively control the toes improves. Toe spreader devices — small silicone spacers worn between the toes — can also help by passively widening the toe box and allowing the intrinsic foot muscles to activate in their natural spread position. A stronger, more articulated foot improves proprioception (your sense of where your foot is in space), which feeds into better balance, better gait mechanics, and reduced load compensation in the ankles, knees, hips, and ultimately the lower back. Choosing footwear with a wider toe box for both running and daily wear is another low-effort, high-impact intervention for long-term foot and back health.

## [01:34:35] Tools: Belly Breathing; Stagger Stance

During discussions with Dr. Wheeler, Dr. Starrett, and Dr. McGill, all three emphasize that during resistance training you should brace — essentially not doing a big belly breath but instead filling your body with air so that you create firmness within the abdominal walls, the spinal erectors, and the obliques. This bracing creates the most stable canister of musculature around the spine, which avoids injury and allows lifting the greatest amount of weight. However, when at rest — just standing around, sitting, or engaging in light activity — you actually want to do the opposite: relax that musculature and belly breathe. Belly breathing means that when you inhale (ideally through the nose), the belly moves outward as the diaphragm descends, rather than the chest lifting. When you exhale, the belly moves inward as the diaphragm ascends.

Belly breathing at rest is important for back health because excessive chronic bracing or tightening of the abdominal wall and spinal erectors when not necessary creates tonic (persistent) muscular tension that can compress the lumbar discs over time and limit the natural movement and decompression of the spine. You can practice belly breathing simply by placing one hand on your chest and one hand on your belly: a good belly breath should move the lower hand without much movement of the upper hand. Another practical tool discussed is the stagger stance — particularly relevant for anyone who must stand for extended periods. Instead of standing with both feet parallel and side by side, stagger one foot slightly forward with about a third of your body weight shifted to the front foot. This small adjustment reduces the degree of hip extension and lumbar lordosis required to maintain an upright posture, relieving compressive load on the lumbar discs and facet joints. Alternating which foot is forward every several minutes throughout a long period of standing distributes the load evenly and significantly reduces cumulative lower back fatigue.

## [01:42:03] Tools: Relieve Low Back Pain, Medial Glute Activation; Rolled Towel

Among the final four protocols to strengthen and pain-proof the back, the first involves activating and strengthening the medial glute — the gluteus medius — a muscle that most people don't think about. The gluteus medius is involved in stabilizing the hips as you walk and run, preventing the pelvis from rocking from side to side. When it weakens or when you have trouble activating it, this can lead to what is perceived as lower back pain — particularly a belt-like region of discomfort around the lower back and the top of the glutes, around the area of the posterior superior iliac spine (the two bony nodules you can feel at the top of the pelvis). A simple protocol to relieve and retrain this area: lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor, feet parallel, and then perform glute bridges — pressing through the heels to lift your hips off the floor. Focus on activating both glutes symmetrically, paying special attention to the gluteus medius by thinking about your hips moving straight upward rather than twisting. More specific medial glute exercises include the toe-stab hip raise: standing on one foot, you stab the big toe of the opposite foot into the ground beside you and then raise the hip on the working side, essentially performing a hip abduction against the body's own weight. Doing these bilaterally and regularly re-activates the gluteus medius and tends to substantially relieve the lower back and top-of-glute discomfort associated with its weakness.

The rolled-towel protocol involves rolling a firm bath towel to roughly the diameter of a forearm and placing it horizontally in the small of the lower back while seated or lying, so as to maintain the natural lumbar curve. This is particularly useful for people who spend many hours seated and find themselves habitually rounding their lower back. The rolled towel simply serves as a passive physical reminder and support: it is not a substitute for strengthening, but it can relieve acute discomfort during periods of prolonged sitting and gradually retrain the lumbar spine to maintain a more neutral posture. Used consistently — in a car seat, office chair, or on a couch — the rolled towel can produce meaningful relief for lower back discomfort associated with chronic sitting-induced lumbar flexion.

## [01:50:59] Tool: Psoas Stretching

The psoas (pronounced "so-az") muscle is involved in connecting the spine to the pelvis; it also has an important interaction with the diaphragm. Many people experience tightening of the psoas and hip flexors from sitting too much, being in a hip-hinge position during heavy lifting, or simply carrying a great deal of tension through the midsection. Being able to stretch the psoas muscle provides a tremendous amount of relief and allows you to stand in a more upright, long-spine posture afterward. The protocol Huberman recommends can be done by essentially everyone, regardless of how flexible or inflexible you are, and it feels excellent.

The psoas stretch resembles the yoga Warrior 1 or low lunge position. Start in a standing position, then step one foot forward and drop the other knee to the floor so you're in a kneeling lunge. Both knees should be at roughly 90 degrees. Then, take the hand on the same side as the knee that is on the floor, reach it up toward the ceiling, and gently rotate the pinky side of that hand back and away from you (clockwise for the right hand). This rotation at the shoulder creates a spiral of tension along the lateral chain of the torso that deepens the stretch into the psoas on that same side. Hold for 30–60 seconds per side, breathing deeply and allowing the hip flexors and psoas to lengthen with each exhale. Huberman describes this as one of those exercises that "just feels so good" and functions as both a structural intervention for back health and a stress reliever — the psoas is a muscle many people hold emotional and physical tension in, and releasing it can have noticeable effects on overall sense of ease and well-being.

## [01:57:00] Tool: Back Awareness; Strengthen & Pain-Proof Back

The final protocol is purely mental — purely cognitive. It doesn't involve any specific movement, but rather developing an awareness of movement patterns as you move through daily life, including exercise. Armed with knowledge from today's episode about the different components of back anatomy — the vertebrae, the discs, the nerves, the muscles and how they relate to one another — you are now in a position to self-observe: to notice when herniating of the discs or inflammation at a particular spinal segment, or tonic contraction at the level of the neck, or lack of strength in the feet, is creating back pain, limiting back mobility, or limiting overall mobility.

Practically, this means paying attention to any pain you have in your back region and any limitation in your ability to manage loads or generate the kinds of movement you need. If you have lower back stiffness or pain, ask yourself: when I drive, when I sit at a standing desk, what is the position of my feet — are they directly underneath my pelvis? Am I rounding my back? If you notice you're rounding your back a lot of the time — because you're texting or typing — perhaps you need to take a towel, roll it up, and put it in the lower back region. Maybe you just need to spend a little bit of time each day being consciously aware of maintaining a slight lower-back arch. If you're someone with a lot of mid-back stiffness, observe whether your thoracic extension is limited and whether you can correct that with active postural adjustments. The key insight is that developing body awareness converts knowledge into behavior change: understanding the anatomy makes you more likely to notice problems early, choose the right interventions, and adapt your habits to prevent recurrence. Over time, this awareness becomes more automatic — you intuitively know when you've been sitting too long in a poor position and you take action before pain develops.
