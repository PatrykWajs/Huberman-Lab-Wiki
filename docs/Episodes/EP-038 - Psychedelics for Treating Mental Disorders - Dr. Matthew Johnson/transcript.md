## [00:06:40] 'Psychedelics' Defined

**Andrew Huberman:** And now, my conversation with Dr. Matthew Johnson. Well, Matthew, I've been looking forward to this for a long time. I'm a huge fan of your scientific work, and I'm here to learn from you. My first question is a very basic one, which is: what is a psychedelic? We hear this term all the time, but what qualifies a substance as a psychedelic?

**Matthew Johnson:** Nomenclature is a real challenge in this area of psychedelics. Starting with the word psychedelic — if you're a pharmacologist, it's not very satisfying, because that term really spans different pharmacological classes. In other words, if you're really concerned about receptor effects and the basic effects of a compound, it spans several classes of compounds. But overall, it's really more of a cultural term, or it does have a relationship to drug effects, but it's at a very high level.

So all of the so-called psychedelics across these distinct classes — the way I put it — is they all have the ability to profoundly alter one's sense of reality, and that can mean many things. Part of that is profoundly altering the sense of self acutely, so when someone's on the psychedelic.

The specific pharmacological classes — the classic psychedelics — are the tryptamines and phenethylamines. So psilocybin, which is in mushrooms, and LSD, are tryptamines. And then you have the phenethylamines, which are typified by mescaline. And these all hit the serotonin 2A receptor primarily. And then you have other classes — MDMA, ketamine — that are often discussed in the same breath as psychedelics, though they work through different mechanisms.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you just do a brief rundown of what those different classes are, and what compounds fall under each?

**Matthew Johnson:** So the classic psychedelics hit the serotonin 2A receptor — psilocybin, LSD, DMT, mescaline. Then there are the NMDA antagonists — ketamine, PCP — which are more dissociative. And then you have MDMA, which is an entactogen or empathogen, hitting serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine through a releasing mechanism. And then there are the anticholinergics — atropine, scopolamine — though those are rarely discussed in the therapeutic context.

**Andrew Huberman:** And these compounds all share the ability to profoundly alter consciousness and sense of self?

**Matthew Johnson:** Right. And I think that's probably the best working definition — the ability to profoundly alter one's sense of reality, including the sense of self.

## [00:14:09] Hallucinations, Synesthesia, Altered Space-Time Perception

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you tell us what hallucinations are, and more specifically, what kinds of perceptual changes occur with these compounds?

**Matthew Johnson:** People often misuse the word hallucination. In psychiatry, a hallucination is a perception in the absence of external stimuli — you think you're talking to someone who isn't there, or you see a pink elephant that isn't there. Classic psychedelics don't typically cause true hallucinations in that sense. What they cause are pseudo-hallucinations or illusions — distortions of reality that's there, enhanced perception, synesthesia, where senses bleed together. You might hear music and see colors.

And there's a fascinating alteration of space-time perception. Time can seem to dramatically slow down or speed up. Many people on a six-hour psilocybin experience say that the beginning seems like it was weeks ago by the time they're at the end. Space can seem to distort and expand. And there's also the dis-habituation effect — things you'd normally tune out become astonishing. A hand becomes the most miraculous thing in the universe.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's so interesting from a neuroscience perspective. Normally, if you look at any one thing long enough, it actually perceptually disappears — we have these microsaccades that prevent that. The brain is always jumping around. It sounds like psychedelics somehow disable that perceptual jumping and allow you to stay with one thing?

**Matthew Johnson:** Right. And it can be enormously enriching or enormously challenging, depending on what you're attending to. Someone could be on a strong psilocybin experience staring at their hand in the middle of Manhattan traffic and feel as if their hand is the most miraculous thing in the universe, completely unaware of the danger around them.

## [00:19:56] Serotonin & Dopamine

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the neurotransmitter systems involved. We've mentioned serotonin. How does psilocybin relate to serotonin, and what's the interplay between serotonin and dopamine in these experiences?

**Matthew Johnson:** Psilocybin is converted in the body to psilocin, which is a tryptamine — structurally similar to serotonin. It's an agonist at the serotonin 2A receptor. So it's hitting the same parking spot as serotonin, but having an alternate response at the receptor level — a biased agonism. This leads to a cascade of downstream effects, including increased glutamate transmission in certain brain areas.

Serotonin, at a very high level, tends to be associated with an inward, more interoceptive, present-moment-focused state. Dopamine is more exteroceptive — looking outward, seeking, motivated toward things in the world outside oneself. These are somewhat opposing states, and they're always at different relative levels. With MDMA, you get robust increases in both dopamine and serotonin simultaneously, which is a very unusual neurochemical situation.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the fact that classic psychedelics look structurally similar to serotonin — psilocybin, LSD — is part of why they engage these receptors?

**Matthew Johnson:** Yes. Psilocybin looks a lot like serotonin. Mescaline, interestingly, looks more like dopamine, yet at the receptor activation level, its pharmacological effect is similar to the tryptamines. The chemistry doesn't always neatly line up with receptor effects. This is also why people shouldn't assume they can cowboy their own chemistry — what you see on paper is often vastly different from what you predict in terms of biological effect.

## [00:23:50] Ketamine & Glutamate

**Andrew Huberman:** What about ketamine? It seems to be taking a very different path to similar outcomes.

**Matthew Johnson:** Ketamine is an NMDA receptor antagonist — it blocks glutamate receptors rather than hitting serotonin receptors. It's more dissociative than the classic psychedelics. Someone on a high dose of ketamine can go into what's called a K-hole — a state of profound dissociation, behavioral inactivity, very different from the mobile, internally active state of psilocybin.

At lower doses, like the bumps used recreationally, you get something closer to an alcohol-level relaxation with some psychedelic coloring. Go higher — 75-100 milligrams — and you're looking at floor-level dissociation.

Clinically, Spravato — esketamine, the intranasal form marketed by Janssen — has been FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression. The interesting thing is that it's not being used in a psychedelic therapy model. The model is essentially: you'll probably feel different — just ignore that, it's a side effect. Whereas the older work by Krupitsky in Russia in the '90s and early 2000s used very high doses of ketamine and treated it like psychedelic therapy — telling people this is going to be a meaningful experience, pay attention to it, process it afterwards. They found incredibly high rates of success for heroin addiction and alcohol addiction. Spravato gives antidepressant effects that last about a week. With psilocybin and classic psychedelics, we're seeing effects a year or more later.

## [00:28:00] An Example Psychedelic Experiment

**Andrew Huberman:** Maybe you could walk us through what a psychedelic experiment in your lab looks like from start to finish?

**Matthew Johnson:** Sure. So first, there's screening — a couple of days of structured psychiatric interviews, going through someone's whole history and symptoms across the DSM, to see if they might have various disorders that could disqualify them — mainly the psychotic disorders like schizophrenia, and bipolar disorder, particularly the manic component. There's also cardiovascular screening.

After that screening comes preparation. You develop a therapeutic rapport with the people who are going to be in the room with you — your guides. You're also didactically explained about what the psychedelic could be like, and it's more known by its variability. I call them uppers, downers, and all-arounders — psychedelics are the all-arounders. You could have the most beautiful experience of your life, or the most terrifying experience of your life. So there's a laundry list of things that could happen, so there are no surprises.

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's say someone qualifies and it's the day. Are they eating mushrooms, or taking it in capsule form?

**Matthew Johnson:** They receive pure psilocybin — not the mushroom. Psilocybin is the compound — a single molecular entity, a white powder. It's administered in a small capsule. It'll take anywhere from 15 minutes to an hour to kick in, on average around 30 minutes. Most of our studies look at a dose in the 20 to 30 milligrams range. We've found you don't need to adjust by body weight — brain size doesn't vary that much between individuals, and this is primarily a brain effect.

The session day itself is not full of cognitive tasks for the therapeutic studies. We want a meaningful experience. Research has found that you get a less meaningful experience when you're in an fMRI or doing a lot of cognitive tasks. So in a therapeutic model, you have that preparation so the person feels very comfortable with their guides. The guides are in the room throughout. I tell people: any emotional response is welcome. You could be crying hysterically — that's what you should be doing if that's what you feel like.

We monitor blood pressure and pulse every half hour or so. If blood pressure goes over a certain level, a physician comes in and gives a little nitroglycerin under the tongue to bring it down — it doesn't affect the experience. We've had to do this only a few times.

## [00:37:30] 'Letting Go' with Psychedelics

**Andrew Huberman:** The letting go of control is an interesting feature. One of the common themes of good psychotherapy is trust built between patient and analyst. The psychedelic experience seems to involve chemically being under a new set of conditions where space and time are altered, sense of self changes. What allows people to go further down that path?

**Matthew Johnson:** The preparation — developing a real trusting relationship with the guides — is key. In the street context, people with psychedelic experience often learn the rule "hold your shit." If several friends split a bag of mushrooms at a party, there's social pressure not to be the one in the corner crying about your mother. In the clinical context, it's the opposite. Any emotional response is not only welcome, it's encouraged.

The other big tool is the eye shades — we don't call them blindfolds, because people are seeing a lot in there. When you close your eyes, the levels of activity in the retina are maintained through spontaneous activity. With the eyes closed and music playing, the person is invited to go inward, to let whatever arises arise. This seems to reduce the anxiety of being in an unfamiliar external environment and allows people to engage with whatever comes up internally.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think about it as an expansion of one's perceptual bubble — to the point where you're not worried that it's going to pop. You're not worried about what people think of you. You can pay attention to a somatic experience, like waves of heat through the body, and just go deeper and deeper into it without pulling back.

**Matthew Johnson:** Exactly. And there's this dis-habituation component — normally, the simplest form of learning is habituation. Keep applying stimuli and there's less response. Psychedelics seem to reverse that. Things that you'd normally habituate to become vivid, astonishing, worthy of infinite attention.

## [00:44:10] Our Mind's Eye

**Andrew Huberman:** What's happening in terms of visual experience with eyes closed?

**Matthew Johnson:** With eyes closed, the mind's eye can be on rocket booster. For some people on psilocybin, with eyes open, the room looks much the same — maybe things seem a little brighter, or there's some wavering in the curtains. But with eyes closed, there can be extraordinarily vivid, complex, geometrical, sometimes narrative imagery. This isn't occurring in the external world — it's spontaneous activity of the visual system amplified enormously.

For some people, even at a high dose, there's no perceptual effect with eyes open. Others are seeing waves in the walls. So there's enormous variability. But that's why the eye shades are so useful — they direct people inward, toward that amplified inner visual and emotional experience, rather than getting distracted by the external environment.

**Andrew Huberman:** It's interesting because the anticholinergic compounds produce true hallucinations — people having conversations with people who aren't there. That sounds terrifying, quite different from the serotonergic experience.

**Matthew Johnson:** The anticholinergics — atropine, scopolamine — do produce true hallucinations, and they're notoriously unpleasant and disorienting in ways that make recovery very difficult. There's a whole history around witches believing they were flying — these agents were used in flying ointments applied transdermally. That's a very different experience from the serotonergic compounds where what you call "hallucinations" are more accurately pseudo-hallucinations or illusions — you know on some level that you're in a particular experience, even if that knowledge is tenuous at high doses.

## [00:48:00] Redefining Your Sense of Self

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's say someone goes into this expanded perceptual bubble — could be the beauty of their connection to things in the world, could be relived trauma, could be falling in love with a pen. Those vastly different experiences seem to have a similar export: a better relationship to the world and to oneself. How can the pen and processing childhood trauma both lead to that?

**Matthew Johnson:** So this is definitely in the terrain we're figuring out. Educated speculation is the best I can provide. But I think the best answer is: the common denominator are persisting changes in self-representation — the way one holds the sense of self, the fundamental relationship of a person in the world. The self is the biggest model — I am a thing that is separate from other things, I have a certain personality, I'm a smoker having a hard time quitting, I'm a depressed person who views myself as a failure. Those are models.

I think the change in self-representation may be an endpoint for all these different experiences. The falling in love with the pen — the whole idea that you could have such a deep connection with this random aspect of the universe could potentially lead to a transformed understanding of the self. The pen may be a proxy for the miracle of reality in a way that relies on nothing supernatural. You can be a hard atheist and have that experience. And that extrapolation might be equivalent to the extrapolation that comes from reprocessing a childhood trauma.

Chris Letheby, a philosopher in Australia, has a forthcoming book — "Psychedelics and Philosophy" — and his conclusion is that changes in self-representation may be the naturalistic explanation for what psychedelics achieve. That can go along with plant spirits, the Buddha, chakras, Jesus — whatever model system you bring. But it can also be completely devoid of any supernatural thinking. We see all of these varieties in our participants.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think about the "I am" construction — what follows "I am" tends to have this feedback effect on how you are in the world. And you're saying that the psychedelic experience actually allows people to reshape that self-representation?

**Matthew Johnson:** Right. I remember one participant during the session who held onto this afterward — he said, "My god, it's like I can really just decide, like flicking off a button, I can decide not to smoke." I call these "duh" experiences with psychedelics. With cancer patients, someone might say, "I'm causing most of my own suffering. I can follow my appointments, do everything medically required, but I'm not getting outside in the sunshine, I'm not playing with my grandkids — I'm choosing to do that." They've told themselves this before. But during and after the psychedelic experience, there's this felt sense of the gravity of agency that's supercharged. Normally, you tell a depressed person: don't think of yourself as a failure. They can't take that in. But in one of these states, they can actually have an experience where they realize it.

**Andrew Huberman:** And that's the key distinction from affirmations and the self-help literature, which claims that language can reshape neural networks. The neuroscience suggests that neural networks don't change in response to language — they change in response to experience. And a psychedelic, combined with the right therapeutic container, is providing an actual experience.

**Matthew Johnson:** Exactly. Neural networks, the brain, and the networks that underlie emotionality and perception and sense of self, they don't change in response to language. They change in response to experience. And there are prerequisites — certain neuromodulators need to be present at sufficient levels. You don't need a drug to do it — give a kid their first puppy, and the levels of dopamine and serotonin are probably elevated, and that experience will reshape them. The psychedelic is doing something similar but in a more concentrated, controllable way.

## [00:58:56] Exporting Psychedelic Learnings to Daily Life

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the transition out of the psychedelic state and the export into life — because this is really where the power of psychedelics seems to be in the therapeutic sense.

**Matthew Johnson:** So as they're coming back from the experience — around five or six hours in — the guides do some initial discussion. Not completely unpacking it, just kind of initially having a little bit of conversation before they go home. Their homework that night is to write something — could be a few bullet points, could be 20 pages. We get everything in that range. Don't be self-critical. It's not graded. This is just to process and provide a point of discussion the next day.

They come in the next day for a one to two hour integration session — basically, let's discuss your experience. What might that mean for your smoking? What might it mean for how you relate to your illness? We encourage them to take the experience seriously. This is something that could be the antithesis of how a lot of social users approach psychedelic experiences — where the social reinforcement is dismissal: "Dude, you were screwed up. Too much acid for you!"

In terms of major life decisions — don't end any relationship, don't quit your job, don't make any big decisions for at least a week or two. Let your sober mind catch up. If they feel inclined to apologize to a sibling about some things, go ahead. But with something big, sit on it.

## [00:01:04:36] Flashbacks

**Andrew Huberman:** What about flashbacks? One of the things you hear is that people get flashbacks, and the street lore is that the compound gets stored in body fat and then released later. Is that complete nonsense?

**Matthew Johnson:** No evidence for that storage in body fat — so probably complete nonsense. Whether flashbacks are completely nonsense, we have to define the term, because there are multiple distinct constructs falling under it.

There is a phenomenon that appears real, called Hallucinogen-Persisting Perceptual Disorder — HPPD — which is in the DSM. A very small number of people who have used psychedelics will have persisting perceptual disturbances: halos around objects, trails following things in motion, distortions in color. Very rare, very mysterious. And amazingly, it has never been seen in thousands of participants in clinical studies — neither in the older era of the late '50s to early '70s, nor in the modern era. So it seems to be something happening specifically in illicit use. Possible factors: polypharmacy — you don't know what you actually took, the dose, the purity. But I think more importantly, some sort of rare neurological susceptibility. There's even a case series showing people developing HPPD-like symptoms who never took a psychedelic — prompted by alcohol, benzodiazepines, cannabis, even tobacco.

The most common thing people experience is state-dependent learning — returning yourself to a similar context can bring back the same thoughts and emotions as the experience. Someone used mushrooms a week ago, now they smoke cannabis or take a warm bath or follow a thought trail, and they find themselves back in that experience. That's not perceptual and not typically distressing.

And then there are perceptual anomalies within a day or two following the experience — some trails or halos the day after. Most people joke that it's a free trip. It doesn't last longer than that, and it doesn't impair functioning.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think one very common misconception about neuroplasticity is that it's an event. It's not — it's a process. The trigger sets in motion a series of dominoes that fall, and the falling of those dominoes is neuroplasticity. The reshaping of neural circuits could take years. So some of what you're describing could literally be the reordering of circuitry that in some individuals might extend longer than in others.

## [01:12:10] Ayahuasca, & ASMR, Kundalini Breathing

**Andrew Huberman:** A number of people I know who've done ayahuasca, as well as people who've done MDMA, report an increased sense of what is sometimes called ASMR — autonomic sensory meridian reflexes — this ability to generate shivers or waves of sensation through the spine more readily after the experience.

**Matthew Johnson:** Yeah, that's really interesting. The ayahuasca connection makes sense — it's DMT that's the active compound, combined with MAO inhibitors that allow the DMT to be orally active. The serotonergic stimulation from those experiences could potentially increase sensitivity to these kinds of autonomic sensory responses.

**Andrew Huberman:** And some people connect this to what's called Kundalini — people who do long duration Kundalini breathing sessions will report feeling as if their perception of self is outside of their head, that their sense of self is extended beyond the body. That's a clinically described neurologic phenomenon.

**Matthew Johnson:** Yes, and that kind of out-of-body or extended-body perception of self is fascinating territory that connects to some of the psychedelic-induced changes in self-representation we were discussing.

## [01:15:54] MDMA, DMT

**Andrew Huberman:** My understanding is that MDMA is a purely synthetic compound — you're not going to find it in nature?

**Matthew Johnson:** So far. DMT was first synthesized in the lab, and then we thought it didn't exist in nature, and then Richard Schultes found it everywhere in South America. So who knows — a plant out there might be making MDMA. But as far as we know now, no.

**Andrew Huberman:** My understanding is that MDMA leads to very robust increases in both dopamine and serotonin simultaneously, which from a neural networks perspective is very unusual. Normally, dopamine puts us in an exteroceptive, seeking state, and serotonin focuses us inward — those are almost mutually exclusive neurochemical states. Why would having both elevated provide a beneficial experience?

**Matthew Johnson:** Yeah, in terms of the therapeutic effects, one thing we know is that the amygdala is less reactive under the acute effects of MDMA. That may play a role — there's less fear-processing control from the amygdala on one's experience of memory. So it may be part of this reprocessing, this reconsolidation of memories in a different way, where the amygdala is not going into fight-or-flight. The person can access the memory of a trauma without the overwhelming fear response that normally accompanies it.

That's why MDMA is being used primarily for trauma. It may be that MDMA is better for trauma for a broader number of people because the chances of having an extremely challenging experience — a bad trip — is much lower with MDMA. People can have bad experiences on MDMA, but they're of a different nature — not that sense of reality shattering, "I'm going insane."

**Andrew Huberman:** What about DMT? I was told that DMT is like a high-speed locomotive into the psychedelic experience and out of the psychedelic experience, and there's no ability to hold onto the self while you're in the peak phase?

**Matthew Johnson:** A lot of people say that. Terence McKenna, who's kind of the classic bard on DMT effects, would say the sense of self was intact, but everything else — the sensorium, what you navigate, what you orient toward — everything else changed, basically. And certain drugs like smoked DMT can be so strong that they force people — there is no choice to try to "hang on." That may be part of why it can be so extraordinary compared to others.

McKenna traveled to South America at 19, found psilocybin mushrooms there along with the DMT snuff he was looking for, and essentially became a highly eloquent spokesperson for the psychedelic experience in the '80s and '90s. He was the next generation's Tim Leary in a way. His brother Dennis McKenna, who is still very much alive, is an ethnobotanist and a founding member of the Heffter Research Institute.

## [01:26:00] Dangers of Psychedelics, Bad Trips, Long-Lasting Psychosis

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to talk about the dangers of psychedelics — it's something you don't hear a lot about these days. If a kid or adult has a predisposition toward psychotic thinking or auditory hallucinations, or is on the Asperger's side of the autism spectrum — are there increased risks?

**Matthew Johnson:** These can be profoundly de-stabilizing experiences. There are two big categories of danger.

The first, and I've already mentioned, is people with very severe psychiatric illness — psychotic disorders like schizophrenia, or mania as part of bipolar disorder. It seems very clear that folks with a predisposition or active disease could be destabilized. Some of the cases we know of — I always think of Syd Barrett, the first singer of Pink Floyd — he developed what looks like schizophrenia, and was doing a lot of LSD. But it looked like he was already showing hints of that susceptibility before. This is hard to disentangle, because when do people typically develop schizophrenia? The modal period for first break is adolescence, early adulthood. And when do people start playing with drugs? Same exact time period. But it seems pretty clear that for those with predisposition, psychedelics can be de-stabilizing in the same way that other intense life experiences can destabilize someone who's already at the edge.

Fortunately, it's really easy to identify these people through proper psychiatric screening. In our studies, we eliminate people with a first-degree relative with schizophrenia or bipolar, out of an abundance of caution.

The far more likely danger is the bad trip — and anyone can have this. The most psychologically healthy person in the world, if you jack the dose high enough, especially in a less than ideal environment, can have a bad trip. Even in our ideal environment, about a third of people at a high dose of around 30 milligrams of psilocybin will say that at some point they had a sense of strong anxiety, fear, feeling like they were losing their mind or feeling trapped. They could have one of the most beautiful experiences of their life a couple of minutes later — but at some point, there was distress.

When people have that in uncontrolled settings, they're more likely to be okay if they're not navigating the streets of Manhattan, and if they're with other people who are not also on their own psychedelic experience. Context is everything.

I have a file folder that grows larger every year of cases — from the medical literature or from the news — of people who freak out on a psychedelic and get hurt or die. They run into traffic. They fall from a height. Sometimes it's unclear whether they thought they could fly or just fell. One of the saddest cases was a young man in Oregon who intentionally took a heroic dose, ended up totally disoriented and in a neighbor's house, and was killed by police — a tragic outcome at every level.

**Andrew Huberman:** It's analogous to the reason I use examples of bodybuilding culture — people have taken excessive amounts of anabolic steroids and diuretics and died. At the extremes, any powerful tool can be lethal. One of those extremes is the sheer number of people with different biological makeups taking the same drug. You create extremes through numbers, through dosage.

## [01:38:15] Micro-Dosing

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about microdosing versus macrodosing. I'm a little "micro-cynical" about this term, because many people talking about microdosing are taking dosages of compounds like LSD that are tremendously powerful at microgram levels. The word "micro" can be confusing — it implies less than something, and yet these compounds are tremendously powerful at very small concentrations. What constitutes a microdose, and what is the value of so-called microdosing?

**Matthew Johnson:** LSD would be the prototypical example. The entry point for psychedelic-type effects with LSD is around 100 micrograms — a 10th of a milligram. People might mistakenly call that a microdose because it's micrograms, but that's actually a macrodose of LSD. When people microdose LSD, they're shooting for something like 10-20 micrograms — about a 10th to a 5th of that entry-level psychedelic dose.

One of the most common mistakes in microdosing is intending it to be a microdose but it ends up being a full-blown dose. People's ability to measure accurately outside of the clinical setting varies enormously. The sophisticated approach is to dissolve a known-quantity blotter of acid in water and take a volumetric fraction of that solution, so you know you're getting a 10th of the dose. With mushrooms, people grind everything into a homogenous powder so the psilocybin content is evenly distributed, then pack it into capsules.

The claims for microdosing fall into two general categories: one, a better version of Adderall — improved focus, treating ADHD-like symptoms. Two, a better version of Prozac — improved mood, treating depression. The idea is that these benefits are available even to people without a clinical diagnosis — not just correcting ADHD, but supercharging focus and mood for anyone.

As for the evidence — none of the peer-reviewed studies that have much credibility have shown a benefit. There are only four or five placebo-controlled studies, and they've ranged from finding no effect whatsoever to just a little bit of impairment — like impairing someone's ability to do accurate time estimation. Time perception is something you need if you're navigating the real world and claiming you're a better CEO because of it. People do feel a little bit high, which isn't surprising — take a little bit of a drug that causes a high, and you feel a little high.

However, an important caveat — the studies done so far have not modeled what the real aficionados are claiming. Paul Stamets and others have particular protocols — take it one day, skip a certain number of days, use it every four days. The claim is you feel the benefits on the off days, a few weeks into the regimen. No study has actually modeled that.

There's also a concern with chronic use: most of these compounds are serotonin 2B receptor agonists to some degree. Agonizing serotonin 2B has been shown to cause heart valve formation problems — valvulopathy. This is why fen-phen was pulled from the market — the component with serotonin 2B activity. Taking a compound that has some serotonin 2B activity twice a week for years is a very different risk profile than taking something a few times therapeutically within a lifetime.

My best guess about microdosing, based purely on anecdote: there's probably a real antidepressant effect — not as interesting or powerful as what we're doing with high-dose psychedelics, more like a better SSRI. We need more tools in the toolbox. But in the big picture, the most interesting thing about psychedelics are the heroic doses — one, two, three times, and you see improvements in depression months later, in addiction over a year later, in people dealing with potentially terminal illness. That's where the truly transformative effects live.

## [01:56:45] Risks for Kids, Adolescents & Teenagers; Future Clinical Trials

**Andrew Huberman:** What about kids? The brain is very plastic early in life, and becomes less plastic as we age — with around age 25 being sort of an inflection point where rigidity of the nervous system seems to take off. There could be great risks for young people taking psychedelics, particularly given the potential to exacerbate psychotic symptoms. Are there any trials looking at people under 18?

**Matthew Johnson:** There's no formal research yet. Although there's a very high chance that there will be. The FDA has actually, in multiple instances, signaled that they want to see those studies. The MAPS group developing MDMA for PTSD has already signaled that pediatric use is on the list of interest. The FDA is very concerned about what they call pseudo-specificity — the idea that you approve a drug only for certain types of people when there's no good rationale for excluding others. Age is one of those factors.

So if something really helps people who are 25 or 30, what's the rationale that it won't help a younger person? The developing brain concerns cut both ways — it's more susceptible to disruption, but also more plastic and potentially more resilient and adaptable. We're going to be seeing research eventually. Treatment-resistant adolescent depression and suicidality would likely be the first target — you'd want to start with the most severe, most treatment-resistant cases.

I went to Gunn High School, which at one point had a very high suicide rate — it was written up in the Times and elsewhere. The idea of kids being prescribed psychedelics for therapeutic purposes might make some people balk. But the idea of kids killing themselves should also make people balk. And I'm relieved to know there's going to be a rational, scientific, safe clinical trial-based exploration of this.

## [02:03:40] Legal Status: Decriminalization vs. Legalization vs. Regulation

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the current legal status of these compounds in the US? What does decriminalization actually mean? And what is the trajectory toward legalization?

**Matthew Johnson:** So federally, these are all Schedule I compounds — illegal. The word decriminalization itself can mean many things. Some forms are close to legalization. Others are pretty weak — just saying, we suggest the police make this their lowest law enforcement priority. Even in a decriminalized city, cops can still choose to arrest, and a local prosecutor may still choose to pursue charges.

The key point that's similar to early cannabis is that 99% of drug enforcement is done at the local and state level. The DEA — federal level — is a tiny fraction of arrests. So the feds could always come in, but in practice, selective enforcement is where it lands. They tend to go after high-profile, vocal cases — like they went after Tommy Chong for selling bongs, while everyone else on the Santa Monica boardwalk was selling bongs unmolested.

Oregon has passed a state-level legalization of psilocybin therapy — really interesting. There's a two-year period where they're figuring out implementation and how to integrate with federal law. Without rewriting the Controlled Substances Act, the best you're likely to get is federal tolerance, which could change with administrations.

I think over large spans of time — not five years — you're going to see something like broad access. The current model of criminalizing drugs feeds a black market and exacerbates a lot of harm. I'd eventually like to see smart regulation for all drugs — including methamphetamine, heroin, cocaine. For psychedelics specifically, something like: even for personal exploration, you need to get a kind of driver's license equivalent, perhaps have your first experiences with trained guides who can facilitate, and there's public health information about risks. That seems much more sensible than the current approach.

My best guess: MDMA will be FDA-approved for PTSD within the next three years, administered in specific clinic settings, like the MAPS trials are being run — not "take two and call me in the morning," but in a therapeutic framework. Psilocybin will follow, probably approved for depression. These approvals won't mean you can buy and sell freely — they'll be very specific clinical protocols.

But I want to make an important point: even if psychedelics had never been made illegal, the medical research would still need to happen. If it's effective as an antidepressant, we need the evidence. The efficacy is going to be increased, and the risks mitigated drastically, with proper screening, preparation, and integration. This isn't a race between decriminalization and medical development — they're parallel and complementary tracks.

## [02:18:35] Psychedelics for Treating Concussion & Traumatic Brain Injury

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to ask about another really important area you're involved in — not depression per se, but neurological injury, TBI, concussion. The major source of traumatic head injury isn't football or hockey — it's construction workers. And then military. What do you think is the potential for these compounds for the treatment and possible reversal of neurological injuries?

**Matthew Johnson:** This is definitely on the more exploratory end. It's based on anecdotes of people saying psychedelics have helped heal their brain — athletes in sports with repetitive head impact claiming improvement in cognitive function, including memory improvement and mood improvement — and on rodent research from several labs, including David Olson and Brian Roth, showing different forms of neural plasticity unfolding in the days following administration of various psychedelic compounds and even some non-psychedelic structural analogs. You see growth of dendrites, new connections forming between neurons.

Whether that neural plasticity underlies the psychiatric improvements we're already seeing — we don't know. But another potential is that maybe what's going on with these claims of improvement from neurological injuries is actual repair of the brain. Perhaps there's potential for helping folks recover from stroke, from disorders related to repetitive head impact.

What I'm hoping to do is work with retired athletes who've been exposed to repetitive head impacts — MMA athletes in the UFC, for example — who are retired and suffering from depression, which can result impart from that history of head impact. We'd see if we can fix the depression, and then as a more exploratory aim, see if we can find evidence of improvement in cognitive function, use MRI to see if it affects gray matter over time.

The UFC has been very gracious — had myself and a few colleagues out to their headquarters in Vegas. There's a dialogue going on there. I'm impressed by the organization's commitment to athlete health.

**Andrew Huberman:** From a mechanistic perspective — if we assume that the basis of improved mood and cognition involves neuromodulators like serotonin and dopamine creating conditions for neural plasticity, and you couple that with a meaningful experience — the rationale is strong. Right now for TBI, people are dabbling in hyperbaric chambers, sauna, breathwork, clipping at the margins of a problem that resides deep to the skull. The exploration you're doing seems well-grounded.

## [02:27:45] Shifting Trends in Psychedelic Research, Academic Culture

**Andrew Huberman:** A few years ago, if someone submitted a grant saying "I want to study how psilocybin impacts human depression," the response might have been, "Let's do a lot of rodent studies first." How has the culture shifted, and what's been the role of philanthropy in making this work possible?

**Matthew Johnson:** The large majority of the most interesting psychedelic work has been funded by philanthropy, not NIH. I've gotten some funding from the National Institute on Drug Abuse — NIDA — but only for work geared toward understanding these things as drugs of abuse.

The Heffter Research Institute, which Dennis McKenna is a founding and active board member of, funded a lot of our early work. There's also the Beckley Foundation based in England, headed by Amanda Feilding — they provided the first funding for our psilocybin smoking cessation research, and then Heffter provided subsequent funding. The Council on Spiritual Practices — a great guy named Bob Jesse — funded some of the original work at Hopkins looking at the nature of mystical experience outside of treating disease states. Just understanding: people take these compounds and astonishingly frequently say, "That was the most important thing I've ever experienced." What the hell is that?

And then most recently, we got a $17 million gift that allowed us to create a nominal center. That was split between the Cohen Foundation — Steven and Alexandra Cohen covered half — and the Tim Ferriss collaborative, Tim and a few friends who divided the other half. That has completely transformed our ability to fully delve into this area.

**Andrew Huberman:** Psilocybin has been designated as a Breakthrough Therapy by the FDA for two separate indications, and MDMA has been designated as a Breakthrough Therapy for PTSD. That's a very big deal. Pharma companies would pay millions for that designation. It means early research shows high potential for treating disorders without good existing treatments. MDMA is probably a year or two away from approval. Psilocybin will probably follow, approved for depression. Even if the effect size is halved from what you're seeing now, it's still going to be much larger than with traditional medications.

**Matthew Johnson:** And what I feel — and I feel lucky in this — is that this work is actually having a positive impact in the world in a way that a lot of academic work never gets to. I remember a colleague who did great memory research and said, "I really don't know that I'm making a difference in the world." Unfortunately, for a lot of academia, the work stays in the ivory tower. Thankfully, this work has disseminated. The psychedelic startup industry now has billions of dollars of investment. That's going to turn into both good and bad — any new technology does. But we've got psilocybin and MDMA as Breakthrough Therapies, and that's real.
