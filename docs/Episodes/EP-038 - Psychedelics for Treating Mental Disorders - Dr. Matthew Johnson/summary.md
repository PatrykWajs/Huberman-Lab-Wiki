# Episode 38: Psychedelics for Treating Mental Disorders | Dr. Matthew Johnson

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman speaks with Dr. Matthew Johnson, Professor of Psychiatry at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine and director of the Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research, about the science and clinical use of psychedelic compounds. They cover pharmacology and receptor mechanisms of psilocybin, LSD, DMT, mescaline, MDMA, and ketamine; what a clinical psilocybin session looks like from psychiatric screening through integration; hallucinations, synesthesia, altered time-space perception, and the mind's-eye experience with eyes closed. The conversation addresses dangers and absolute contraindications (psychosis, bipolar mania, predisposing family history), the mixed evidence on microdosing including a chronic heart-valve risk via 5-HT2B agonism, and MDMA's mechanism of reducing amygdala reactivity for trauma reprocessing. Later sections examine the evolving legal landscape — federal Schedule I status, decriminalization, Oregon's psilocybin therapy law, and the expected FDA approval timeline — plus exploratory research on using psychedelics for adolescent depression and traumatic brain injury in retired MMA fighters.

## Guest: Dr. Matthew Johnson

**Dr. Matthew Johnson** is a Professor of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine and director of the Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research. He has run clinical trials on psilocybin for smoking cessation, cancer-related depression and anxiety, and major depressive disorder, as well as survey research on naturalistic psychedelic use. He helped secure Breakthrough Therapy designation from the FDA for psilocybin in two separate indications. His work has been featured in The New York Times, Michael Pollan's "How to Change Your Mind," and a "60 Minutes" segment on psychedelic therapies.

## Key Takeaways

1. **"Psychedelic" is a cultural, not pharmacological, term.** Across distinct pharmacological classes the defining feature is the ability to profoundly alter one's sense of reality and sense of self. Classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT, mescaline) are tryptamines and phenethylamines acting primarily at the serotonin 2A receptor; MDMA (entactogen) and ketamine (NMDA antagonist) are mechanistically distinct but share this high-level effect. [00:06:40](transcript.md#000640-psychedelics-defined)

2. **Psychedelics do not cause true hallucinations — they cause pseudo-hallucinations and illusions.** True hallucinations are perceptions with no external stimulus. Classic psychedelics produce distortions of real stimuli, synesthesia (hearing music as color), profound dis-habituation (ordinary objects become astonishing), and dramatic slowing or speeding of perceived time. Anticholinergics (atropine, scopolamine) do cause true hallucinations and are notoriously unpleasant. [00:14:09](transcript.md#001409-hallucinations-synesthesia-altered-space-time-perception)

3. **Psilocybin is a biased serotonin 2A agonist that cascades into increased glutamate transmission.** It converts in the body to psilocin, structurally similar to serotonin. Mescaline structurally resembles dopamine yet produces serotonergic-type effects — demonstrating that molecular structure does not reliably predict receptor outcome. [00:19:56](transcript.md#001956-serotonin--dopamine)

4. **Ketamine (NMDA antagonist) produces dissociation, not classic psychedelia, and its clinical model matters enormously.** FDA-approved Spravato (esketamine) instructs patients to ignore the experience; Krupitsky's high-dose Russian trials treated it as full psychedelic therapy and achieved very high success rates for heroin and alcohol addiction. Classic psychedelics produce lasting effects over a year; Spravato effects last roughly a week. [00:23:50](transcript.md#002350-ketamine--glutamate)

5. **Clinical psychedelic sessions require extensive preparation and integration — the container is inseparable from the therapeutic outcome.** The Johns Hopkins protocol proceeds: psychiatric screening → preparation sessions building therapeutic rapport → dosing day (pure psilocybin capsule, eye shades, curated music, guides present, 6–8 hours) → same-day debrief → overnight writing homework → 1–2 hour integration session next day. [00:28:00](transcript.md#002800-an-example-psychedelic-experiment)

6. **Eye shades and curated music direct attention inward and support "letting go."** Participants are encouraged to welcome any emotional response — opposite to the street norm of social pressure to suppress distress. Eyes-closed experience can involve vivid geometric or narrative imagery generated by amplified spontaneous visual-system activity; eyes-open effects vary enormously by individual. [00:37:30](transcript.md#003730-letting-go-with-psychedelics)

7. **Persistent changes in self-representation are the likely common mechanism across wildly different psychedelic experiences.** Whether the experience involves childhood trauma reprocessing or intense attention to a pen, the shared export appears to be a restructured "I am" model — the core sense of who one is in the world. This is supported by philosopher Chris Letheby's naturalistic account and works without requiring any supernatural framework. [00:48:00](transcript.md#004800-redefining-your-sense-of-self)

8. **Neural networks change through experience, not language — psychedelics provide the experience.** Affirmations alone cannot reshape circuitry underlying emotion and self-perception. Psychedelics, combined with the right setting, generate actual somatic and perceptual experiences alongside elevated neuromodulators — the conditions that enable genuine neural plasticity, in the same way a child's first puppy can produce lasting transformation. [00:48:00](transcript.md#004800-redefining-your-sense-of-self)

9. **Schizophrenia, bipolar mania, and psychotic predisposition are hard contraindications.** People with these conditions can be severely destabilized (Syd Barrett is the referenced example). Clinical trials exclude participants with personal or first-degree family history of psychotic disorders. Even in ideal clinical conditions, about one-third of participants at 30 mg psilocybin experience a period of strong fear or feeling like they are losing their mind. [01:26:00](transcript.md#012600-dangers-of-psychedelics-bad-trips-long-lasting-psychosis)

10. **HPPD is real, extremely rare, and has never appeared in clinical settings — "flashbacks" are mostly state-dependent learning.** Hallucinogen-Persisting Perceptual Disorder (halos, trails, color distortions) appears specifically in illicit use and may involve polypharmacy or rare neurological susceptibility; even non-psychedelic substances have triggered HPPD-like symptoms. The much more common phenomenon is state-dependent learning: returning to a similar context briefly re-activates the emotional state. The storage-in-body-fat theory has no evidence. [00:01:04:36](transcript.md#00010436-flashbacks)

11. **No peer-reviewed evidence supports microdosing for cognition or mood — and chronic use carries a theoretical heart-valve risk.** Controlled studies show no benefit to mild impairment (e.g., time-perception errors). Most classic psychedelics are 5-HT2B agonists; chronic 2B agonism causes valvulopathy — the same mechanism that pulled fen-phen from the market. Johnson's best guess: a modest antidepressant effect probably exists, but the truly transformative effects live in heroic one-to-three-time dosing, not chronic microdosing. [01:38:15](transcript.md#013815-micro-dosing)

12. **MDMA reduces amygdala reactivity, enabling trauma reprocessing without overwhelming fear.** The simultaneous serotonin + dopamine surge produces an unusual neurochemical state. Reduced amygdala reactivity lets traumatic memories be accessed and reconsolidated without fight-or-flight, making MDMA particularly suited for PTSD and giving it a much lower bad-trip risk than classic psychedelics. [01:15:54](transcript.md#011554-mdma-dmt)

13. **No pediatric psychedelic trials exist yet, but they are coming — treatment-resistant adolescent depression and suicidality is the likely first target.** The FDA has signaled interest; MAPS has flagged pediatric MDMA use. The developing brain is simultaneously more susceptible to disruption and more plastic. Johnson attended Gunn High School — which had a very high suicide rate — and frames the research as a rational scientific response to adolescent suicide. [01:56:45](transcript.md#015645-risks-for-kids-adolescents--teenagers-future-clinical-trials)

14. **MDMA is projected for FDA approval for PTSD within a few years; psilocybin for depression will follow.** Both carry Breakthrough Therapy designation. Approval will mean specific clinical protocols (MAPS-style therapeutic framework), not open retail access. Even halved effect sizes will likely exceed traditional medications. The research infrastructure was made possible largely by philanthropy — Heffter, Beckley Foundation, Cohen Foundation, and Tim Ferriss's collaborative — not NIH. [02:27:45](transcript.md#022745-shifting-trends-in-psychedelic-research-academic-culture)

15. **The legal landscape is fragmented: federal Schedule I, locally shifting.** "Decriminalization" ranges from near-legalization to lowest-enforcement-priority with no formal protection. 99% of drug enforcement happens at local and state level — the DEA is a tiny fraction of arrests. Oregon has passed state-level psilocybin therapy legalization with a two-year implementation period. Johnson's long-term preference: smart regulation with a driver's-license equivalent for personal psychedelic use. [02:03:40](transcript.md#020340-legal-status-decriminalization-vs-legalization-vs-regulation)

16. **Psychedelics may have neuroregenerative potential for TBI — dendritic growth seen in rodent models.** Rodent data from Olson, Roth, and others shows dendritic growth and synaptogenesis following psychedelic administration. Johnson is planning psilocybin research with retired UFC MMA fighters for depression and cognitive outcomes, using MRI to track gray matter changes. Mechanistically plausible; early-stage. [02:18:35](transcript.md#021835-psychedelics-for-treating-concussion--traumatic-brain-injury)

## Tools & Protocols

### Clinical Psychedelic Session Protocol (Johns Hopkins Model)

- **What:** Full therapeutic protocol used in Hopkins clinical trials, from screening through post-session integration.
- **How:** (1) Screening — structured DSM psychiatric interview across all symptom domains; exclude schizophrenia, bipolar (especially manic component), first-degree family history of psychotic disorders; cardiovascular workup with nitroglycerin available on session day for BP spikes. (2) Preparation — 4–8 hours with guides building therapeutic rapport; full didactic walkthrough of the range of possible effects (beautiful to terrifying) so nothing is a surprise. (3) Session day — pure psilocybin 20–30 mg in a capsule (not mushrooms); patient on a couch with eye shades and curated music; guides present throughout; BP and pulse monitored every ~30 min; 6–8 hours total. Any emotional response welcomed and encouraged. (4) Same-day debrief before departure. (5) Overnight homework — write about the experience; bullet points to 20 pages, not graded, no self-criticism. (6) Integration session next day (1–2 hours) — discuss experience in relation to therapeutic target; hold major life decisions for at least 1–2 weeks.
- **When:** Formal clinical trial settings for depression, smoking cessation, cancer-related distress, PTSD, addiction.
- **Ref:** [00:28:00](transcript.md#002800-an-example-psychedelic-experiment) | [00:58:56](transcript.md#005856-exporting-psychedelic-learnings-to-daily-life)

### Eye Shades + Curated Music (Inward Focus Tools)

- **What:** Standard perceptual tools used in every Hopkins dosing session to direct attention inward.
- **How:** Eye shades (not blindfolds — significant internally generated imagery occurs) block external visual distraction. Curated music supports the emotional arc of the session. Together they reduce anxiety about the external environment and facilitate "letting go" into whatever arises internally.
- **When:** Throughout the dosing session.
- **Ref:** [00:37:30](transcript.md#003730-letting-go-with-psychedelics) | [00:44:10](transcript.md#004410-our-minds-eye)

### Integration Writing Exercise

- **What:** Post-session reflective writing assignment to begin processing the experience and anchor it for next-day integration.
- **How:** Evening after the session: write about the experience — could be a few bullet points or 20 pages. No self-criticism; not graded. Used as the basis for the next-day integration discussion.
- **When:** Night of the dosing session.
- **Ref:** [00:58:56](transcript.md#005856-exporting-psychedelic-learnings-to-daily-life)

### Psychiatric Screening Before Any Psychedelic Use

- **What:** Structured clinical interview to identify absolute contraindications before psychedelic administration.
- **How:** Full DSM screening for schizophrenia, bipolar disorder (particularly manic component), and family history of psychotic disorders. Cardiovascular screening also required. Even first-degree family history of psychotic disorders is an exclusion criterion in trials.
- **When:** Before any clinical psychedelic session; relevant baseline for anyone considering psychedelic use.
- **Ref:** [00:28:00](transcript.md#002800-an-example-psychedelic-experiment) | [01:26:00](transcript.md#012600-dangers-of-psychedelics-bad-trips-long-lasting-psychosis)

### Microdose Harm Reduction: Volumetric Dilution (LSD) / Homogeneous Grinding (Mushrooms)

- **What:** Methods for more accurately estimating psychedelic microdoses to avoid accidental full dosing.
- **How:** LSD — dissolve a blotter of known psychedelic potency fully in a measured volume of water (LSD is 100% aqueous soluble); take a volumetric fraction (e.g., 1/10th the volume) to get approximately 1/10th the dose. Mushrooms — grind entire batch into a homogeneous powder so psilocybin is evenly distributed; pack consistent-weight capsules.
- **When:** If someone is microdosing outside a clinical setting.
- **Ref:** [01:38:15](transcript.md#013815-micro-dosing)
