## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Emotions

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. My name is Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine.

So let's talk about emotions. Emotions are a fascinating and vital aspect of our life experience — it's fair to say that emotions make up most of what we think of as our experience of life. Even the things we do, our behaviors, the places we go, and the people we encounter all funnel into our emotional perception of what those things mean — whether or not they made us happy, sad, depressed, lonely, or awe-inspiring. One thing that is absolutely true is that everyone's perception of emotion is slightly different: your idea of happy is very likely different than my idea of a state of happiness, just as we know this for color vision — even though the cells in our eyes that perceive the color red are identical, your idea of the most intense red will differ from mine.

We need to agree at the outset that emotions are complicated and yet they are tractable — they can be understood. Today we're going to talk about tools to understand what emotions are, what your emotional states mean and what they don't, allowing you to place value on whether or not you should hold an emotional state as true or not, as well as whether the emotions of others are important to you in a given context. We're going to center a lot of our discussion around infancy and puberty, and we're also going to talk about tools for enhancing one's emotional range and navigating difficult emotional situations. I'm drawing from the psychology greats — those who studied emotion and emotional development — and linking that to the neuroscience of emotion, because nowadays we understand a lot about the chemicals, hormones, and neural circuits that underlie emotion.

While there's no one single universally true theory of emotion, at the intersection of many existing theories there are some ground truths. If we want to understand emotions, we have to look at where they first develop. The rule that every good neuroanatomist knows is that if you want to understand what a part of the brain does, you need to know what connections it makes and what is called the developmental origin of that structure. For years it was thought there might be circuits in the brain that generate the feeling of being happy or sad — that has been challenged — and yet there is good evidence for circuits such as limbic circuits that shift our overall states, our level of alertness or calmness, or bias us toward viewing the outside world versus paying more attention to what's going on inside our bodies. Emotions do arise in the brain and body, and if we want to understand how emotions work we have to look at how they are built — during infancy, adolescence and puberty, and continuing into adulthood — but the groundwork is laid down early in development.

## [00:03:01] Emotions & Childhood Development

You were born into this world without really any understanding of the things around you. There are two ways that you interact with the world and you're always doing both more or less at the same time: interoception — paying attention to what's going on inside you, what you feel internally — and exteroception — paying attention to what's going on outside you. Hold that in mind, because the fact that you're both interocepting and exterocepting is true for your entire life and it sets the foundation for understanding emotions.

As an infant, you didn't have any knowledge of what you needed — you didn't understand hunger, cold, heat, or any of that. When you needed something you experienced that as anxiety: you would feel an increase in alertness, and you would vocalize, cry out, act agitated, or make a number of different sounds. Then your caregiver, whoever that might have been, would respond to that. A baby doesn't have any sense of the outside world except that it responds to its acts of anxiety. All developmental psychologists agree that babies lack the ability to make cognitive sense of the outside world, but in this feeling of anxiety — registering one's own internal state and then crying out through vocalizations — we start to develop a relationship with the outside world in which our internal states and shifts in anxiety start to drive requests, and people come and respond to those requests.

This gets to the basis of what emotions are about: emotions are really about forming bonds and being able to predict things in the world. The fundamental rules of your emotional experience were laid down very early — as a young infant and toddler you were mainly focused inward, and you started to understand what was going on outward as a way of predicting what would bring you relief, what would remove your anxiety.

## [00:04:57] Infancy, Anxiety

When you were a baby you experienced all unmet needs as a form of anxiety — a generalized increase in alertness. You didn't yet have the language or cognitive ability to distinguish hunger from cold from the need for a diaper change. Every unmet internal need triggered the same core signal: an increase in arousal that drove you to vocalize and communicate distress to your caregiver.

As caregivers responded to those signals — bringing milk, warmth, comfort — you started to build a rudimentary prediction: if I signal distress, the outside world responds to relieve it. This basic feedback loop between your internal state and the external response is where the foundations of emotional processing were established. The reliability of those responses from caregivers shaped how you came to expect — or not expect — the world to meet your needs.

Babies are not doing this consciously, but they are doing it in order to relieve anxiety. As a young infant, you were mainly focused inward and started to understand what was going on outward as a way of predicting what would bring you relief. This is the origin of trust — the emotional understanding that the external world can be relied upon to address internal needs — and it has profound implications for how emotional bonds develop throughout the entire lifespan.

## [00:06:35] Understanding Emotions; Tools: Mood Meter; Emotions & 3 Key Questions

Emotions are about forming bonds and being able to predict things in the world. There is an interesting tool that addresses the question of what emotions are and what they consist of — it's an app developed by people at Yale called Mood Meter. What they're trying to do is put more nuance and subtlety on our words and language for emotions, and allow you to predict how you're going to feel in the future. The app asks how you are right now — you click "I feel" and can pick from four quadrants: high energy and unpleasant, high energy and pleasant, low energy unpleasant, or low energy pleasant. As you move your finger across a color gallery, words pop up — motivated, cheerful, inspired — and you select the one that best matches. It then collects data on you and links it to other features, helping you predict how you're going to feel at different times of day.

This points to a couple of really interesting features: we don't really have enough language to describe all the emotional states, and yet there are some core truths to what makes up an emotion. So the way this works is that at any point you can ask yourself three key questions. First, what is your level of autonomic arousal? — the continuum from alert to calm. If you're in a panic you're a 10 out of 10; if you're very drowsy, a 1 or 2. Second, what is your valence? — do you feel good or bad? Third, how much are you interocepting versus exterocepting — is your attention focused internally on what you're feeling, or primarily directed outward?

These three axes — alertness/calmness, valence (good/bad), and internal/external focus — make up much of what we call emotions. For instance, if you're really stressed, that often puts you in a position to be very in touch with what's going on in your body. If you're having somatic sensations like a racing heart you can't ignore, you're strongly interoceptive. Recognizing these three dimensions — how alert or sleepy, how good or bad you feel, and where your attention is directed — gives you a much richer framework for understanding your emotional state than simple labels like "happy" or "sad."

## [00:10:06] Infancy, Interoception & Exteroception

There's the baby in the crib, mostly interocepting. As caregivers bring what it needs — milk, diaper changes, warmth — it also starts to exteroceive: the baby begins looking into the outside world and making predictions. It starts wondering how much it needs to cry, or predicting "if I cry a little, mom comes over and I get milk." Babies are starting to evaluate all this unconsciously, doing it in order to relieve anxiety. As a young infant and toddler you were mainly focused inward and started to understand the outside world as a way of predicting what would bring relief and remove anxiety — and that is where the fundamental rules of your emotional experience were laid down.

Early in development you start off with this interoceptive bias, developing expectations and predictions about how the outside world is going to work, figuring out the reliability of outside events and people. Where things and people are reliable, you are able to give up more of your interoception — there is literally trust that your internal needs will be met through bonds and actions of others. This starts to veer toward the discussion about neglect and trauma: when reliability is absent in early caregiving, the interoceptive anxiety remains chronically elevated, shaping later emotional patterns. Understanding interoception and exteroception as a dynamic balance — one you carry throughout your entire life — is absolutely critical to understanding how emotions function.

## [00:11:10] Strange-Situation Task & Babies, Emotional Regulation

These are classic, actually famous experiments done by Bowlby and Ainsworth — this is the Strange Situation Task, in which a mother and child come into the laboratory, the baby and the mother or father play together for a bit, then the mother leaves for some period of time, and then comes back. A huge amount of research is devoted to understanding the response of the child when the caretaker returns. Bowlby and Ainsworth and many of their colleagues identified at least four patterns that babies display when their caretaker returns, grouping these into groups A, B, C, and D.

The A babies — when their caretaker would return, the infant would respond with happiness, what looked like delight — they would go to the caretaker, seemed happy. These are referred to as securely attached kids. The B babies were less likely to seek comfort from their caregiver when the caregiver returned — they would sometimes continue to play with toys or stay with the other adult who was in the room. These were referred to as avoidant babies. The C babies would respond to the return of the caregiver with acts of annoyance, seeming kind of angry — referred to as ambivalent babies. The D babies were the disorganized babies — the child avoided interactions with everyone and their behavior didn't really change whether or not the caregiver was there.

This classic work opened up a huge set of important questions about what the reestablishment of the bond is really about. What defines a really good bond — a secure attachment, an insecure attachment, or an avoidant attachment? The four things that define it are: gaze — literally eye contact; vocalizations — what we say and how we say it; affect or emotion — the way we express through crying, smiling, etc.; and touch. Gaze, vocalization, affect, and touch are really the core of what we call social bonds and emotionality. It's clear from most theories of emotional health that an ability to recognize when your own internal state is being driven primarily by external events is important for emotional regulation. People who are constantly being yanked around by external happenings are emotionally labile — not in control of their emotions. How much the outside environment disrupts your internal environment relates to this balance of interoception and exteroception, and very likely has roots in whether you were securely attached, insecurely attached, disorganized, or ambivalent as a baby.

## [00:15:12] Tool: Exteroception vs Interoception Focus?

While you can't travel back in time, there is an exercise you can do to address whether you have a bias for exteroception or interoception right now. Close your eyes and concentrate on the contact of any portion of your body, bringing as much of your attention to that point of contact as possible. From there, move your attention even more deeply inward: sense what's going on in your gut — are you full, empty, hungry? What is your heart rate? What is the cadence of your breathing? Bring your focus to everything at the surface of your skin and inward. After a moment of that, try something that is harder for most people: put your eyes or ears on anything in your immediate space — look across the room, pick a panel on the wall or a leg of a table, and try to bring as much of your attention to that as possible.

It is hard to place 100% of your attention on something externally unless it is really exciting or really novel. If you've ever watched a really great movie, you're exterocepting more than you're interocepting until something exciting happens — you feel something — and you're tethering your emotional experience to something external. You can also do this dynamically: you can decide to focus internally then externally, split it 50/50, or 70/30. One can develop a heightened ability to do this, and the power of doing so is that when you feel you're focused too much internally and you'd like to be more focused externally, you can actually do that deliberately. But as you notice, it takes work.

These exercises are at the core of developing emotional bonds, because the four things — gaze, vocalization, touch, and affect — are happening dynamically. If somebody winks at you, you're paying attention to their wink but then you also notice how you feel. Some people have a very hard time breaking out of a strongly interoceptive mode; others have a harder time breaking out of their exteroceptive mode. The extent to which we have biases in how interoceptive or exteroceptive we are connects to those three axes: valence (good or bad), alertness (alert or calm), and interoceptive or exteroceptive bias.

## [00:19:42] Puberty, Kisspeptin; Testing the World, Emotional Exploration

Arguably the second most — if not equally — important period in emotional development is puberty. Puberty is an absolute biological event: it has a beginning and a specific definition, which is the transition into reproductive maturity. There are a lot of hormonal changes, and there are also a lot of brain changes — and most people don't realize it, but the brain changes occur first. The brain turns on the hormone systems that allow puberty to occur. One of the more interesting molecules that triggers puberty in all individuals is kisspeptin (K-I-S-S-P-E-P-T-I-N). Kisspeptin is made by the brain and it stimulates large amounts of a different hormone called GnRH — gonadotropin releasing hormone — to be released. GnRH then causes the release of another hormone called luteinizing hormone (LH), which travels in the bloodstream and stimulates the ovaries of females to produce estrogen and the testes of males to produce testosterone. This gets triggered by leptin and kisspeptin, and from there the young child is now a different creature — not just because they are reproductively competent, but because there's a shift in a number of the things that underlie social bonds.

There is a marked shift in the things that allow children and adults to engage in predictive behavior about each other. Most of what consumes the minds and waking hours of adolescents is questions about how they relate to social structures, who they can rely on, and how they can make reliable predictions in the world now that they have more agency. You could argue that puberty is the fastest rate of maturation you'll go through at any point in your life — the largest change you'll ever go through in terms of who you are, because your biology has fundamentally changed at the level of your brain and every bodily organ from the skin inward.

There is a terrific review article published in the journal Nature about the biology of adolescence and puberty, as well as some of the core needs and demands that have to be met for successful emotional maturation during that time. One key connection highlighted is between the dopamine centers in the brain and an area involved in emotion and dispersal. Around the end of adolescence and during the transition to puberty — both because of changes in the brain and changes in hormones — there is an intense desire on the part of the child to get further and further away from primary caregivers. There's a desire to start spending more time with friends and peers and less time with adults. These hormones don't just allow sexual reproduction or change the brain and bodily organs — they also bias us toward dispersal. During puberty there is increased connectivity between the prefrontal cortex (involved in motivation, decision-making, and suppressing actions for long-term goals) and the dopamine centers and amygdala.

The key element here is testing — testing circuits for emotions and reward as they relate to decisions. Adolescents are testing social interactions, testing physical interactions with the world. Puberty is seen as the period of development in which one self-samples for the two elements we talked about at the beginning: how do I form bonds, and how do I make predictions about what will make me feel good at the level of interoception? The same core algorithm — alert or calm, feel good or feel bad, primarily exterocepting or primarily interocepting — is at play throughout the lifespan. It's a useful framework because it allows you to sort through all the data about this brain area being active, that hormone, and return to a kernel of ground truth from which you can evaluate how different behaviors and emotional responses might or might not make sense.

## [00:28:00] Creating Healthy Emotional Bonds; Dopamine, Serotonin & Oxytocin

There is a theory of emotional development from Allan Schore at UCLA that talks about how most of our testing of bonds and relationships is this seesawing back and forth between very dopaminergic states — driven by dopamine — and serotonergic states — driven by serotonin. This starts with infant and mother, or infant and father. Healthy emotional development clearly begins with an ability for the caretaker and child to be in calm, peaceful, soothing, touch-oriented, eye-gazing types of behaviors — those really drive serotonin, the endogenous opioid system, oxytocin — things that are very calming, centered around pleasure with the here and now. As well as excited states of what we're going to do next: there's a characteristic sign of the dopaminergic interaction where both caretaker and child are wide-eyed, the pupils dilate (a signature of arousal), and the baby will sometimes look away if it gets really excited — signatures of dopamine release.

In adolescence these same things carry forward where good bonds are achieved through hanging around, watching TV, just kind of being together, playing video games or texting — whatever the soothing local activity happens to be — as well as adventure and exciting things. This seesawing back and forth between different reward systems seems to be the basis from which healthy emotional bonds are created.

We can't have a complete conversation about emotions and bonds without talking about oxytocin. Oxytocin is released in response to lactation in females, in response to sexual interactions, and in response to non-sexual touch — it is released in males and females and is involved in pair bonding and the establishment of social bonds in general. How it does that seems to be by matching internal state: it seems to both increase synchrony of internal state — maybe setting a level of calmness or alertness that seems like a reasonable hypothesis — as well as raising people's awareness for the emotional state of their partner. Oxytocin brings us back to the alertness-calmness axis and the interoceptive-exteroceptive axis: in order to form good bonds, we can't just be thinking about how we feel — we also need to be paying attention to how others feel, evaluating a match, trying to see whether there seems to be some sort of synchrony between states.

Experiments involving the administration of intranasal oxytocin report increased positive communication among couples. That study, published in Biological Psychiatry, is titled "Intranasal oxytocin increases positive communication and reduces the cortisol stress hormone during couple conflict" — they have couples fight with and without oxytocin. This is very much in line with the idea that oxytocin is the "trust hormone."

## [00:31:54] Vasopressin; Vagus Nerve & Alertness

The other molecule that is extremely important for social bonds in emotionality is vasopressin. Vasopressin has effects on the brain directly — it actually creates feelings of giddy love and has very interesting effects on monogamous or non-monogamous behavior. There's a beautiful set of experiments done in a little rodent species called the prairie vole: it turns out there are two different populations of prairie voles — some are monogamous, always mating with the same other prairie vole, and some are robustly non-monogamous. It turns out that levels of vasopressin and/or vasopressin receptor dictate whether or not they're monogamous. There's also interesting evidence in humans — when people report their behavior accurately, vasopressin levels can relate to monogamy or non-monogamy in humans as well.

If we're talking about the neuroscience of emotions, we have to talk about the vagus nerve. The vagus nerve is these connections between the body and the viscera — including the gut, heart, lungs, the immune system — and the brain, and the brain is also controlling these organs, so it's a two-way street. There's a big myth that stimulating the vagus in various ways leads to calmness — that it's always going to calm you down — and that is false. This is interesting in light of emotionality because of work done by Karl Deisseroth at Stanford, who has developed tools to adjust the activity of neurons in real time using light and electrical stimulation.

There's a New Yorker article about this: Deisseroth is talking to an extremely depressed, suicidally depressed patient who has a small device implanted that allows her to adjust her vagus nerve activity. She describes how she has been "going pancake" — totally laid out flat, not much going on, not wanting to pursue a job, really depressed. He adjusts her vagus stimulation to 1.5 milliamps (she had been receiving 1.2 milliamps every 5 minutes for 30 seconds but was no longer feeling the effects). After the stimulation started, in the course of the next few minutes she underwent a remarkable change — her frown disappeared, she became cheerful, describing pleasure from the Christmas holiday and recounting YouTube videos she'd watched. Just by stimulating and activating the vagus.

The vagus is fascinating in terms of the brain-body connection, and this case helps dispel the myth that vagus stimulation is all about being calm — it's really about being alert. Level of alertness or calmness is impacting emotion: the alertness-calmness axis is one primary axis in emotion. It's not the only one — there's also the valence component of good or bad — and there's also the interoceptive-exteroceptive component we discussed earlier.

## [00:36:22] Recap & Key Takeaway

This business of how you conceptualize emotions is really the most powerful tool you can ever have in terms of understanding and regulating your emotional state. Rather than thinking of emotions as just labels — happy, sad, awe, depressed — think about emotions as elements of the brain and body that encompass levels of alertness, that include a dynamic with the outside world, and your perception of your internal state. Starting to think about emotions in a structured way can not only allow you to understand some of the pathology — when you might feel depressed or anxious, or when others are depressed and anxious — but also to develop a richer emotional experience. The three axes are: 1) alertness/calmness (autonomic arousal), 2) valence (how good or bad you feel), and 3) interoceptive versus exteroceptive focus.

The key takeaway is that emotions are not just labels; they are states of the nervous system that can be understood, navigated, and intentionally shaped. The groundwork for your emotional life was laid down during infancy through attachment patterns, and then substantially revised during puberty as hormonal changes drove dispersal, social testing, and exploration of bonds. Understanding the neurochemistry — dopamine and serotonin seesawing in healthy bonds, oxytocin synchronizing internal states, vasopressin influencing pair-bonding tendencies, and the vagus nerve modulating alertness — gives you a mechanistic handle on your own emotional life. I want to thank you for your time and attention, and thank you for your interest in science.
