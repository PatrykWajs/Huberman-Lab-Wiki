# Episode 239: How to Improve at Learning Using Neuroscience & AI | Dr. Terry Sejnowski

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Terry Sejnowski, computational neuroscientist at the Salk Institute, to explore how the brain processes and stores information at an algorithmic level and how that knowledge can be applied to dramatically improve learning. The conversation covers the basal ganglia's value function and dopamine-driven reinforcement learning, the distinction between procedural and cognitive learning and why schools are wrong to eliminate hands-on practice, and practical tools including sleep spindles for memory consolidation, the free "Learning How to Learn" Coursera course, high-intensity exercise for mitochondrial biogenesis, and cognitive velocity training. Sejnowski and Huberman discuss how psilocybin expands brain connectivity, how ketamine reveals the NMDA-receptor basis of schizophrenia and its antidepressant mechanism, and how large language models now function as "idea pumps" that dramatically accelerate scientific research and medical diagnosis. The episode closes with Sejnowski's personal practices — daily jogging on the Salk Institute beach, mind-wandering walks for idea generation, and using pre-sleep thinking to prime overnight problem-solving — and his frontier research project on self-attention and temporal context in both brains and AI Transformers.

## Key Takeaways

1. **All motivation runs on a single learning algorithm.** The basal ganglia implement a "value function" — a temporal-difference learning rule tied to dopamine — that governs how we approach rewards and avoid punishments across every domain, from motor skills to social relationships. Dopamine encodes prediction and expectation, not simply pleasure; every action updates this value function. [00:11:49](transcript.md#001149-basal-ganglia-learning--value-function)

2. **Negative punishment is far more potent than positive reward.** While small rewards build the value function gradually, a single powerful negative experience can produce one-trial learning that lasts a lifetime. PTSD is an extreme expression of the same mechanism. [00:15:23](transcript.md#001523-value-function-reward--punishment)

3. **Procedural and cognitive learning must coexist — schools removing practice are making a critical mistake.** Reading a book about tennis will never substitute for hitting balls on a court. The brain has two major learning systems — cortical (cognitive) and subcortical/basal ganglia (procedural) — and they reinforce each other. Removing hands-on practice from schools undermines the procedural system and degrades foundational learning capacity. [00:19:14](transcript.md#001914-cognitive-vs-procedural-learning-active-learning-ai)

4. **Active, trial-and-error engagement is what the brain encodes — being told the answer is not enough.** When solving a problem yourself, the brain stores the solution in a transferable, generalizable way. When someone tells you the answer, it is stored only as a fact. This is why AI models that merely memorize fail, and why human and AI learning both require active generalization. [00:19:14](transcript.md#001914-cognitive-vs-procedural-learning-active-learning-ai)

5. **Traveling waves during sleep spindles consolidate memory into long-term storage.** Sejnowski's lab discovered circular traveling waves (sleep spindles, 12–15 Hz, ~1–2 seconds duration) during non-REM transition states that coordinate hippocampal replay and cortical integration of daytime learning. These waves knead new experiences into the cortex without overwriting existing knowledge. [00:30:08](transcript.md#003008-traveling-waves-sleep-spindles-memory)

6. **Zolpidem (Ambien) doubles sleep spindle density and doubles next-day recall — but with a trade-off.** Research by Sarah Mednick at UC Irvine showed that taking zolpidem after learning doubles the number of sleep spindles and results in twice as much recall the following morning, with stable long-term retention. The cost: it wipes out memory encoding for experiences that occur after taking the drug, producing the "hotel room amnesia" effect. [00:34:57](transcript.md#003457-tool-increase-sleep-spindles-memory-ambien-prescription-drugs)

7. **Psilocybin dramatically increases global brain connectivity.** As people age, the brain prunes synapses and loses connectivity. Psilocybin reverses this temporarily by expanding the number of active connections across brain networks, dissolving rigid structure and allowing new associations to form — the likely mechanism behind its therapeutic potential and effects on creativity. [00:42:02](transcript.md#004202-psilocybin-brain-connectivity)

8. **The "Learning How to Learn" Coursera course is most impactful for adults aged 25–35.** Co-created with Barbara Oakley, this free MOOC was designed for students but has been taken by 4 million people in 200 countries. The 25–35 age peak is surprising: these adults are in the workforce, can't go back to school, and discover they can boost learning efficiency even as raw brain plasticity slightly declines. [00:45:58](transcript.md#004558-tool-learning-how-to-learn-course)

9. **AI should be treated like a human assistant to reduce cognitive fatigue.** A technical writer found that treating ChatGPT politely — as a human collaborator — eliminated end-of-day cognitive drain that occurred when treating it like a machine. This works because the brain's social interaction circuits engage naturally with human-like communication, requiring less effortful top-down cognitive processing. [01:01:06](transcript.md#010106-draining-experiences-ai--social-media)

10. **High-intensity interval exercise drives mitochondrial biogenesis and sustains cognitive vigor.** Sejnowski runs intervals on the beach daily and hikes in the Alps to drive mitochondrial biogenesis. Healthy, numerous mitochondria sustain the metabolic energy neurons need for high cognitive velocity. He views this as his primary anti-aging strategy for the brain, practiced consistently across decades. [01:06:52](transcript.md#010652-vigor--aging-continued-learning-tool-exercise--mitochondrial-function)

11. **Parkinson's disease reveals that cognitive and physical velocity share a set-point mechanism.** In Parkinson's, dopamine-producing neurons in the brainstem die, causing freezing of movement. Paradoxically, patients think they are moving at normal speed while physically still — the set point shifts rather than the subjective experience. L-DOPA (a dopamine precursor) was the first effective treatment and had near-miraculous results, restoring movement and speech. [01:50:04](transcript.md#015004-parkinsons-disease-cognitive-velocity--variables-amphetamines)

12. **Cognitive velocity training is a practical way to build mental stamina.** Deliberately reading or processing information slightly faster than your comfortable default — finding the edge where comprehension remains intact but effort is noticeable — acts like interval training for neurons. It stresses mitochondrial systems, drives adaptation, and can be used to counter the cognitive slowdown that comes with age or passive media consumption. [01:12:17](transcript.md#011217-tool-cognitive-velocity-quick-stressors-mitochondria)

13. **Ketamine reveals the NMDA-glutamate basis of schizophrenia and its antidepressant mechanism.** Ketamine blocks NMDA glutamate receptors, reducing inhibitory interneuron activity and creating excess cortical excitation — producing full-blown temporary psychosis clinically indistinguishable from a schizophrenic break. In sub-anesthetic doses, this same excitation "titrates" under-excited depressed circuits back toward balance, explaining ketamine's (Spravato's) remarkable antidepressant efficacy for treatment-resistant depression. [01:30:56](transcript.md#013056-schizophrenia-ketamine-depression)

14. **AI as "idea pump" accelerates research by foraging literature and generating novel hypotheses.** Rusty Gage's lab at the Salk Institute uses large language models to suggest new experiments based on their existing data and published literature. Sejnowski uses AI daily as an interactive research partner — giving it technical abstracts to explain at different levels, or loading it with papers to weight evidence and hypothesize outcomes in minutes rather than days. [01:36:15](transcript.md#013615-ai-idea-pump-analyzing-research)

15. **Human + AI outperforms either alone in medical diagnosis.** In a landmark Nature dermatology study, expert dermatologists and an AI each achieved ~90% accuracy classifying skin lesions. When doctors consulted the AI, accuracy jumped to 98%. The AI had broader rare-case pattern recognition; the doctor had deeper common-case nuance — their complementary weaknesses cancelled. [01:42:11](transcript.md#014211-ai-medicine--diagnostic-tool-predicting-outcomes)

16. **Mind-wandering walks are when top scientists get their best ideas — and sleep incubates overnight problem-solving.** Sejnowski gets his best insights on walks or jogs (never with music or podcasts), takes voice memos to capture ideas, and deliberately thinks about unsolved problems before sleep so his brain works on them overnight. He calls this "using the brain as a tool once you understand how it works." [02:12:40](transcript.md#021240-tool-idea-generation-mind-wandering-learning)

## Tools & Protocols

### Increasing Sleep Spindles for Memory Consolidation

- **What:** Sleep spindles are 12–15 Hz oscillatory bursts (~1–2 seconds) during non-REM transition states that consolidate daytime learning into long-term memory via hippocampal replay. Doubling spindle density doubles next-morning recall with stable long-term retention.
- **How:** Ensure adequate total sleep duration (7–9 hours) as the primary lever. Sarah Mednick's research showed zolpidem (Ambien) doubles spindle density and next-morning recall when taken after a learning session — but it blocks encoding of experiences that occur after ingestion. Avoid alcohol, which suppresses spindles.
- **When:** Nightly. Critical after any intense learning session, new skill acquisition, or heavy study.
- **Ref:** [00:34:57](transcript.md#003457-tool-increase-sleep-spindles-memory-ambien-prescription-drugs)

---

### "Learning How to Learn" Course (Free)

- **What:** A Coursera MOOC co-developed by Dr. Terry Sejnowski and Barbara Oakley teaching evidence-based learning strategies — focused vs. diffuse thinking modes, chunking, spaced repetition, exam anxiety reduction, procrastination management, and active self-testing. Approximately 50–60 bite-sized (~10-minute) videos over one month, with quizzes, tests, and student forums. Taken by 4 million people in 200 countries.
- **How:** Enroll for free at https://www.coursera.org/learn/learning-how-to-learn. Self-paced.
- **When:** Anyone seeking to improve how they absorb and retain new information — most impactful for adults aged 25–35 but beneficial across ages 10–90.
- **Ref:** [00:45:58](transcript.md#004558-tool-learning-how-to-learn-course)

---

### High-Intensity Exercise for Mitochondrial Biogenesis

- **What:** Brief bouts of maximal or near-maximal effort (sprint intervals, hill climbing) to drive mitochondrial biogenesis in neurons — generating new, efficient mitochondria that sustain the energy output required for high cognitive velocity and long-term cognitive health. Sejnowski runs beach intervals (10-second sprints, then jog) daily and hikes Alpine terrain seasonally.
- **How:** Sprint intervals alternating maximal effort with recovery jogging. Short daily sessions plus consistency over years are the key variables.
- **When:** Daily or near-daily. Sejnowski has maintained this practice for decades.
- **Ref:** [01:06:52](transcript.md#010652-vigor--aging-continued-learning-tool-exercise--mitochondrial-function)

---

### Cognitive Velocity Training

- **What:** Deliberately reading or processing information slightly faster than your comfortable default pace while still retaining content — creating a mild, controlled cognitive stressor analogous to physical interval training that drives mitochondrial adaptation and builds sustained mental stamina.
- **How:** During reading sessions, push pace just beyond the comfortable default. Find the edge where comprehension remains intact but effort is noticeable. Apply brief cognitive stress, then ease back — like sprint intervals applied to the mind.
- **When:** During focused study sessions. Particularly useful if reading speed or thinking pace feels like it is declining with age or after prolonged passive media consumption.
- **Ref:** [01:12:17](transcript.md#011217-tool-cognitive-velocity-quick-stressors-mitochondria)

---

### AI as Idea Pump for Learning and Research

- **What:** Using large language models as an interactive research and learning assistant to forage literature, generate hypotheses, stress-test ideas, weight evidence across papers, and suggest new experiments. Treat the AI like a human collaborator (polite, iterative dialogue) rather than a search engine to reduce cognitive fatigue and get richer responses.
- **How:** Engage in back-and-forth dialogue. Provide papers via drag-and-drop (tools like NotebookLM), ask the AI to identify connections, argue against your ideas, critique statistics, and hypothesize outcomes. Ask it to explain technical concepts at different levels of sophistication.
- **When:** Daily, as part of research, learning, writing, or creative work. Most powerful for synthesizing cross-disciplinary literature and generating novel hypotheses.
- **Ref:** [01:36:15](transcript.md#013615-ai-idea-pump-analyzing-research)

---

### Mind-Wandering Walks + Pre-Sleep Problem Incubation

- **What:** Two linked practices: (1) Unstructured walking or light jogging without podcasts, music, or screens — allowing the default mode network to activate, serotonin to rise, and unconscious processing to surface new ideas. (2) Deliberately thinking about an unsolved problem or complex paper before sleep, then capturing insights immediately on waking.
- **How:** (1) 20–60 minutes walking or jogging at a comfortable pace with no audio input. Carry a phone or voice recorder to capture ideas that surface. (2) Before bed, spend a few minutes actively engaging with a specific unsolved question — then sleep. Do not watch TV first, as this directs overnight processing toward irrelevant content.
- **When:** (1) Daily or after intense focused work sessions. (2) Any night when wrestling with a hard problem or trying to consolidate a complex paper.
- **Ref:** [02:12:40](transcript.md#021240-tool-idea-generation-mind-wandering-learning)
