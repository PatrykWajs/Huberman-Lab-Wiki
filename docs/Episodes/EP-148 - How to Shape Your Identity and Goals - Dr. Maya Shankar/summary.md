# Episode 148: How to Shape Your Identity & Goals | Dr. Maya Shankar

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman speaks with Dr. Maya Shankar — cognitive scientist, former White House senior advisor and Chair of the White House Behavioral Science Team, and creator and host of the podcast *A Slight Change of Plans* — about how human identity forms, shifts, and can be deliberately reshaped. Drawing on her own experience losing a professional violin career to a tendon injury while at Juilliard, Dr. Shankar walks through the science of identity foreclosure, identity paralysis, essentialism, cognitive closure, and the End-of-History Illusion, then provides a comprehensive toolkit for setting and sustaining meaningful goals. The episode covers both the philosophy of self-definition (anchoring identity to *why* rather than *what*) and the granular mechanics of approach vs. avoidance framing, agency in goal-setting, emergency reserves, temporal landmarks, temptation bundling, the middle problem, and the peak-end rule.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Anchor identity to "why," not "what."** When we define ourselves by roles (violinist, doctor, athlete), any loss of that role triggers identity paralysis. Anchoring instead to the underlying drive — e.g., "I am driven by human connection" — makes identity resilient across life transitions. Shankar's through line from violin to cognitive science to podcasting was always emotional connection. [00:05:15](transcript.md#000515-identity-foreclosure-identity-paralysis-throughlines)

2. **Identity foreclosure limits what we believe is possible.** Young people often inherit constrained identities from parents, peer groups, and society. This imposed framing can severely limit what a person believes they are capable of achieving. Recognising and naming the foreclosure is the first step to expanding it. [00:05:15](transcript.md#000515-identity-foreclosure-identity-paralysis-throughlines)

3. **Essentialism and shame are linked.** Believing we have fixed, immutable essences leads to shame ("I am bad") rather than guilt ("I did something bad"). Treating oneself as a collection of changeable behaviors and thoughts enables a growth mindset and reduces harmful self-narratives. [00:12:10](transcript.md#001210-identity--adolescence-essence--shame)

4. **Delight and awe are signals worth heeding.** The experience of delight — a felt sense of expansion and possibility within an activity — is a reliable internal cue pointing toward genuine interests. Awe (perceived vastness plus need for accommodation, per Dacher Keltner) can be positive or negative in valence and serves as an entry point for identity re-creation. [00:16:58](transcript.md#001658-delight--awe)

5. **Intrinsic motivation outlasts external reward.** Shankar's shift from extrinsic (perform for approval) to intrinsic (play because I love it) motivation reframed the violin as personal joy. Competitive environments can erode intrinsic motivation; reanchoring to curiosity and love of progress protects against that erosion. [00:35:58](transcript.md#003558-intrinsic-motivation-juilliard--courage)

6. **Curiosity is the engine of identity re-creation.** After her injury, Shankar followed curiosity as a seedling signal — not demanding the same passion as violin, just asking "do I want to learn more?" That low bar is sufficient to begin rebuilding. Reading *The Language Instinct* by Steven Pinker sparked her pivot to cognitive science. [01:06:32](transcript.md#010632-passions--curiosity)

7. **Cognitive closure and the End-of-History Illusion keep us stuck.** People underestimate how much they will change in the future (End-of-History Illusion, Dan Gilbert). High need for cognitive closure leads to premature identity lock-in and fear of change. Reducing cognitive closure — staying open to mystery and unbidden experiences — boosts well-being and resilience. [01:13:20](transcript.md#011320-change-cognitive-closure-end-of-history-illusion)

8. **Approach goals outperform avoidance goals.** Framing a goal as moving *toward* something ("I want to eat healthier foods") is consistently more motivating than moving *away* ("I want to avoid unhealthy foods"). Avoidance success produces only relief; approach success produces pride and a motivation boost. A single word change — "eligible" to "earned" — increased veterans' program uptake by 9%. [01:40:13](transcript.md#014013-tool-framing-goals)

9. **Who sets the goal matters enormously.** Goals set by coaches, bosses, or trainers undermine autonomy. Even partial ownership — choosing your own target within an externally imposed structure — dramatically increases intrinsic motivation and persistence. [01:47:13](transcript.md#014713-tool-agency-in-goal-pursuit)

10. **The middle problem is real and predictable.** Motivation spikes at the start and end of a goal pursuit but predictably dips in the middle. Shortening goal duration (weekly vs. annual) reduces the length of the middle. The goal gradient effect (monotonic increase in motivation as the finish line nears) can be leveraged by reframing the midpoint as a new beginning. [02:19:54](transcript.md#021954-tool-middle-problem-maintaining-motivation)

11. **The peak-end rule governs how we remember effort.** We do not average all moments of an experience; we weight the peak (most emotionally intense moment) and the end disproportionately. Deliberately engineering a slightly less intense end to a workout or difficult task — even by extending it briefly — reduces remembered aversion and improves follow-through. [02:24:55](transcript.md#022455-tool-aversion--memory-peak-end-rule)

12. **Flexible mindset and reframing are learnable skills.** Publicly valuing the ability to update one's beliefs creates cultures of honest feedback. Effective reframing (Ethan Kross) requires a genuine alternative interpretation — not suppression or venting. Venting without a "cognitive advisor" amplifies negative emotion. Third-person self-distancing decreases neural activity associated with hostility and aggression. [01:30:48](transcript.md#013048-tools-flexible-mindset-reframing--venting-gratitude)

## Tools & Protocols

### Anchor Identity to "Why"
- **What:** Identify the core emotional driver beneath your current roles — the through line that could survive the loss of any single role.
- **How:** Journaling exercise: list your current identities, then for each one ask "what does this give me?" until you reach a value (e.g., connection, mastery, curiosity). That value becomes the anchor.
- **When:** During major life transitions, or whenever a key role feels threatened.
- **Ref:** [00:05:15](transcript.md#000515-identity-foreclosure-identity-paralysis-throughlines)

### Delight Mapping
- **What:** Track what gives you genuine delight — the felt sense of expansion, surprise, and possibility — as a compass for unexplored parts of self.
- **How:** Keep a running list or journal. Periodically review what produced delight and ask what that reveals about latent values or capabilities.
- **When:** Ongoing; especially useful when feeling stuck or during identity transitions.
- **Ref:** [00:16:58](transcript.md#001658-delight--awe)

### Curiosity as a Seedling Signal
- **What:** Use curiosity — not passion — as the minimum viable signal when exploring a new identity or path after a loss.
- **How:** Ask only "do I want to learn more about this?" If yes, follow it. Do not hold the new domain to the same emotional standard as what was lost.
- **When:** Immediately after a forced identity change or when nothing feels as compelling as the lost pursuit.
- **Ref:** [01:06:32](transcript.md#010632-passions--curiosity)

### Approach vs. Avoidance Goal Framing
- **What:** Reframe every goal from avoidance language ("stop doing X") to approach language ("achieve Y").
- **How:** Review your current goal list. For any goal phrased as avoiding or stopping something, rewrite it as a positive destination state. E.g., "stop eating junk food" → "eat meals that energise me."
- **When:** At goal-setting sessions; when motivation for an existing goal is flagging.
- **Ref:** [01:40:13](transcript.md#014013-tool-framing-goals)

### Ownership of Targets
- **What:** Ensure you have genuine agency in setting at least the specific target within any goal structure, even if the goal category is externally imposed.
- **How:** If working with a coach or trainer, negotiate the specific number, deadline, or daily variation yourself rather than accepting a fully top-down assignment.
- **When:** Whenever entering a coached or managed goal context.
- **Ref:** [01:47:13](transcript.md#014713-tool-agency-in-goal-pursuit)

### Emergency Reserve (Slack)
- **What:** Build pre-approved "get-out-of-jail-free" days into a recurring goal to prevent one lapse from derailing the entire commitment.
- **How:** When setting a daily goal (e.g., gym every day this month), explicitly designate 2–3 acceptable miss days upfront. Missing on those days means you are still on track, not failing.
- **When:** For any multi-week daily habit where perfect adherence is unrealistic.
- **Ref:** [02:13:56](transcript.md#021356-tools-goal-setting)

### Psychological State-Matched Goal Setting
- **What:** Set goals in the same psychological and physiological state you expect to be in when pursuing them.
- **How:** Do not set ambitious early-morning exercise goals on a high-energy Saturday afternoon. Simulate the execution context — ideally, set the goal while already in that context.
- **When:** At goal-setting sessions.
- **Ref:** [02:13:56](transcript.md#021356-tools-goal-setting)

### Temporal Landmarks (Fresh Start Effect)
- **What:** Use meaningful calendar dates — New Year, birthday, first of a month, Monday — as psychological fresh starts to reboot goal pursuit after a lapse.
- **How:** When motivation dips, deliberately frame the next natural calendar landmark as a new beginning. Do not wait for motivation to return organically. Even shortening to "this afternoon" can work after a bad morning.
- **When:** After goal lapses or mid-pursuit motivation drops.
- **Ref:** [02:13:56](transcript.md#021356-tools-goal-setting)

### Middle Problem — Shorten Duration or Reframe the Midpoint
- **What:** Combat the predictable motivation dip in the middle of goal pursuit by shortening goal duration or artificially reframing the midpoint as a new beginning.
- **How:** Set weekly goals instead of annual ones to reduce middle length. At the halfway point of a longer project, explicitly mark it as a fresh start — reset self-monitoring and reframe remaining distance as a new race.
- **When:** Midway through any multi-week goal or project; at annual goal-setting time, consider breaking into weekly sub-goals.
- **Ref:** [02:19:54](transcript.md#021954-tool-middle-problem-maintaining-motivation)

### Temptation Bundling
- **What:** Pair an activity you only enjoy during goal pursuit (e.g., a specific podcast or playlist) exclusively with a difficult goal-related task (e.g., a hard workout or household chores).
- **How:** Choose one pleasurable thing you genuinely look forward to and restrict it only to times when you are doing the hard thing. Never consume the enjoyable thing outside the bundle context, or its motivating potency will diminish.
- **When:** For recurring effortful tasks where motivation is the barrier (exercise, studying, deep work, chores).
- **Ref:** [02:19:54](transcript.md#021954-tool-middle-problem-maintaining-motivation) — Katy Milkman research

### Peak-End Rule Engineering
- **What:** Deliberately design the end of a difficult experience to be less aversive in order to reduce remembered aversion and increase the likelihood of returning.
- **How:** End workouts or hard sessions with a few minutes of something slightly less intense than the hardest part — a lower-intensity cool-down or a moment of reflection. Avoid ending on the hardest moment. (For those who enjoy peak intensity, ending on that intensity works equally well for the same reason.)
- **When:** Any recurring effortful practice (exercise, cold exposure, intensive work blocks, medical procedures).
- **Ref:** [02:24:55](transcript.md#022455-tool-aversion--memory-peak-end-rule) — Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky

### Flexible Mindset Practice
- **What:** Explicitly value and model the ability to update your opinions when given good evidence, both personally and in team/leadership settings.
- **How:** When someone presents a counterargument, default response: "Based on what you've shared, I'm changing my mind." Make this statement publicly and reward others for doing the same.
- **When:** Ongoing — in leadership, relationships, and self-evaluation.
- **Ref:** [01:30:48](transcript.md#013048-tools-flexible-mindset-reframing--venting-gratitude)

### Third-Person Self-Distancing
- **What:** Shift perspective from first-person to third-person when facing a difficult problem or trying to identify blind spots, to gain objectivity and reduce hostility.
- **How:** Mentally play the role of a friend giving advice to someone in your exact situation (that "someone" being you). Identify the gaps and reframes you would offer a friend. Can also be done aloud or in writing.
- **When:** During conflict, after a criticism or failure, or when venting to a friend is not available or helpful.
- **Ref:** [01:30:48](transcript.md#013048-tools-flexible-mindset-reframing--venting-gratitude) — Ethan Kross research
