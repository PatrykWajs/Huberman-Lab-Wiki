# Episode 365: Using Play to Rewire & Improve Your Brain

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

In this Huberman Lab Essentials episode, Andrew Huberman covers the neurobiology and psychology of play across the entire lifespan. He explains why play is not just for children but a critical mechanism for neuroplasticity in adults, driven by the periaqueductal gray (PAG), endogenous opioids, and low epinephrine states. He identifies the specific forms of play most effective for expanding cognitive flexibility — dynamic multi-directional movement and chess — explains how early play identity shapes adult behavior, and closes with practical tools for adults to deliberately reintroduce play.

## Key Takeaways

- **Periaqueductal gray (PAG) drives play:** This brainstem area is rich in neurons that release endogenous opioids (e.g., enkephalin). During play, these opioids relax the prefrontal cortex and allow it to explore more contingencies and adopt new roles — the PFC doesn't get "stupid," it gets smarter. [00:00:37](transcript.md#000037-play-brain--exploring-contingencies-creatives)
- **Play is low-stakes contingency testing:** The core function of play is exploring "if I do A, what happens?" without fear of serious consequence. This unlocks neuroplasticity of the prefrontal cortex and expands the catalog of possible behaviors and thoughts. [00:03:27](transcript.md#000327-childhood-play--low-stakes-scenarios)
- **Adults need play too:** Deliberately reintroducing play — especially in activities you are not highly skilled at — expands prefrontal flexibility into all areas of life, not just during play. You only have one prefrontal cortex; making it more plastic benefits every domain. [00:05:18](transcript.md#000518-tool-adults--playful-mindset)
- **Play postures are universal:** Head tilt + open/soft eyes + slightly raised eyebrows signal playful intent in humans (equivalent to the dog's "play bow"). Partial postures — approaching while keeping body size small rather than puffing up — signal non-aggressive intent during rough-and-tumble play. Failures to read or produce these postures disrupt social play. [00:07:50](transcript.md#000750-play-postures-eyes)
- **Group play teaches rule-testing and rule-breaking:** All animals, including humans, gradually up the stakes during group play and inevitably one member breaks the rules. Adults who react aggressively to minor rule violations or playful sarcasm likely did not fully develop play contingency learning in childhood. [00:10:58](transcript.md#001058-group-play-testing--breaking-rules)
- **Role play forces the PFC to run more algorithms:** Taking on a different role means taking in more environmental inputs and making predictions from a new standpoint — one of the most powerful forms of play for building cognitive flexibility. [00:13:30](transcript.md#001330-role-play)
- **Genuine play requires high endogenous opioids + low epinephrine:** If you are stressed about the outcome — money on the line, Super Bowl pressure — adrenaline is too high and play circuitry is suppressed. True play is low stakes enough that opioids are liberated. Inside that neurochemical state, BDNF and other growth factors are deployed, physically rewiring brain circuits. Paradoxically, playfulness is also what allows peak performance because it opens access to novel behaviors unavailable under high-stakes pressure. [00:14:40](transcript.md#001440-play-mindset-low-stakes-situations-tool-adult-play-for-brain-health)
- **Richard Feynman as the model of adult playful spirit:** The Nobel Prize-winning physicist deliberately cultivated a mischievous, tinkering mindset throughout his life — picking locks at Los Alamos, writing playful books — and credited that spirit for his scientific creativity and ability to see the world differently. [00:20:36](transcript.md#002036-tinkerers--creatives-playful-spirit)
- **Best neuroplasticity play — dynamic movement:** Dance, soccer, martial arts, basketball, and any sport with jumping, lateral movement, angled movements, and varied speeds engages the vestibular system and cerebellum. This mimics the brain circuitry associated with play and opens portals for plasticity. Linear movement like running does not produce the same effect. [00:23:00](transcript.md#002300-tool-play-to-enhance-neuroplasticity-dynamic-movement-chess--mental-roles)
- **Best neuroplasticity play — chess and multi-role games:** Chess requires each player to simultaneously adopt multiple identities (each piece has different rules and capabilities), unlike video games where the player is locked into a single fixed avatar. Any game requiring adoption of multiple distinct roles within a single session is strongly neuroplastic. [00:23:00](transcript.md#002300-tool-play-to-enhance-neuroplasticity-dynamic-movement-chess--mental-roles)
- **Personal play identity shapes adult life:** Four components define it — how you play, personality, sociocultural environment, and economics/technology. Your play patterns from ages 10–14 (competitive vs. cooperative, solo vs. group, leader vs. follower, tolerance for role-switching mid-game) directly predict how you show up in adult work, relationships, and social hierarchies. [00:27:06](transcript.md#002706-personal-play-identity-adulthood)
- **Play circuits persist throughout the lifespan:** They are not pruned away in adulthood. Biology does not waste resources — if these circuits were unimportant in adults, evolution would have eliminated them. Their persistence is the neurobiological argument for lifelong play. [00:30:14](transcript.md#003014-recap)

## Tools & Protocols

### Adopt a Playful Mindset (Adult Play Reintroduction)
- **What:** Deliberately enter low-stakes activities you are not expert in — card games, board games, casual sport — with the explicit goal of exploring contingencies rather than winning.
- **How:** Lower internal stakes by reframing the goal from "win/perform" to "explore and observe." Notice how you and others react. Tolerate uncertainty and imperfect outcomes. Key marker: if you feel high adrenaline about the result, it is not play yet — reduce the stakes further.
- **When:** On a somewhat regular basis; especially useful when feeling cognitively rigid or stuck.
- **Ref:** [00:05:18](transcript.md#000518-tool-adults--playful-mindset)

### Dynamic Multi-Directional Movement
- **What:** Engage in physical play involving movements in multiple planes — lateral, angled, jumping, ducking, leaping — rather than strictly linear movement (e.g., running).
- **How:** Dance, soccer, martial arts, basketball, or any sport with rapid directional changes and varied speeds. Keep it genuinely low-stakes and exploratory rather than intensely competitive to maintain low epinephrine.
- **When:** Regularly as part of play; pairs well with social group play for added neuroplastic benefit.
- **Ref:** [00:23:00](transcript.md#002300-tool-play-to-enhance-neuroplasticity-dynamic-movement-chess--mental-roles)

### Chess or Multi-Role Mental Play
- **What:** Play chess or other games requiring simultaneous adoption of multiple distinct roles with different rules — not single-avatar video games.
- **How:** Learn chess or equivalent strategy games where each piece/player has different capabilities and rules. Focus on exploring different positional identities rather than grinding toward mastery. The neuroplastic benefit comes from role-switching within a single session.
- **When:** Periodically; especially valuable for those who prefer non-physical play.
- **Ref:** [00:23:00](transcript.md#002300-tool-play-to-enhance-neuroplasticity-dynamic-movement-chess--mental-roles)

### Identify Your Personal Play Identity
- **What:** Reflect on how you played at ages 10–14 — competitive vs. cooperative, solo vs. group, leader vs. follower, reaction to role-switching mid-game — to understand current adult behavioral defaults.
- **How:** Ask: Was I competitive? Did I prefer groups or solitude? How did I react when my role was switched mid-game — upset, delighted, or indifferent? How did I feel about being moved from the winning team to even things out? Use those answers to identify which play modes you have been avoiding as an adult.
- **When:** Once as a reflective exercise; revisit when navigating new social or professional situations.
- **Ref:** [00:27:06](transcript.md#002706-personal-play-identity-adulthood)
