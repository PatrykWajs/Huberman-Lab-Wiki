## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Build Endurance

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. This podcast is separate from my teaching and research roles at Stanford. It is, however, part of my desire and effort to bring zero-cost-to-consumer information about science and science-related tools to the general public.

Today, I'd like to talk about endurance and how to build endurance, and how to use endurance for the health of your entire body. Endurance, as the name suggests, is our ability to engage in continuous bouts of exercise, or continuous movement, or continuous effort of any kind. It is clear that cardiovascular exercise — exercise where you're getting your heart rate up continuously for a period of time — is vital for tapping into and enhancing various aspects of our biology in the body and in the brain, such that our brain can perform work for longer periods of time, focused work, learning, et cetera.

## [00:00:50] Energy Sources, ATP, Oxygen

The key thing to understand about energy production in the body is this thing that we call ATP. ATP is required for anything that requires energy, for anything that you do that requires effort. Our muscles and our neurons use different fuel sources to generate ATP. The ones that are used first for short bouts of intense activity are things like phosphocreatine. If you've only heard about creatine as a supplement, well, phosphocreatine actually exists on our muscles — that's why people take creatine, you can load your muscles with more creatine. Phosphocreatine is great for short, intense bouts of effort.

Then you start to tap into things like glucose, which is literally just carbohydrates, just sugar that's in your blood. Then if you keep pushing, you start to tap into other fuel sources like glycogen, and you have fats stored in adipose tissue. Even if you have very, very low body fat percentage, you can extract lipids and fatty acids from that body fat — it's a storage pack for energy that can be converted to ATP. When I say today "energy" or "ATP," just remember that regardless of your diet, regardless of your nutritional plan, your body has the capacity to use creatine, glucose, glycogen, lipids, and if you're ketogenic, ketones in order to generate fuel.

Now, the other crucial point is that in order to complete that process of taking these fuels and converting them into energy, most of the time you need oxygen. You need oxygen molecules in your system — they come in through your mouth and your nose, go to your lungs, and distribute via the bloodstream. Oxygen is not a fuel, but like a fire that has no oxygen, you can't actually burn the logs. When you blow a lot of oxygen onto a flame, it burns. Oxygen allows you to burn fuel.

Today we are going to ask the critical questions: What allows us to perform? What allows us to continue effort for long periods of time? We think of things like willpower — but willpower is neurons. It's neurons in our brain. We have this thing called the central governor which decides whether or not we should or could continue, or whether or not we should stop. We have to ask: what is the limiting factor on performance? What prevents us from enduring? What are the factors that say, "no more, I'm not going to continue this run?"

## [00:04:14] Neurons & Willpower, Glucose & Electrolytes

There are five main categories of things that allow us to engage in effort: nerve, muscle, blood, heart, and lungs. Let's talk about neurons and how they work. I want to tell you about an experiment that's going to make it very clear why quitting is a mental thing, not a physical thing. An experiment published in the journal Cell showed that there is a class of neurons in our brainstem — in the back of our brain — that if they shut off, we quit.

These neurons release epinephrine, which is adrenaline. Anytime we are engaged in effort of any kind, we are releasing epinephrine. This little group of neurons in the back of our brain — called the locus coeruleus — is churning out epinephrine all the time. If something stresses us out, it churns out more, and then it acts as an alertness signal for the whole brain. Think about epinephrine as a readiness signal. When we are engaged in effort, this readiness signal is being churned into our brain. When we're relaxed and falling asleep, epinephrine levels are low.

Our desire to continue — or put differently, our willingness to continue and our desire to quit — is mediated by events between our two ears. That doesn't mean that the body is not involved, but it means that neurons are critically important. We have two categories of neurons that matter: the ones in our head that tell us to get up and go out and take that run, and the ones that encourage us to continue that run; and we have neurons that shut things off and say "no more." We of course also have the neurons that connect to our muscles and control them.

The reason we quit is rarely because our body quits — our mind quits. When people say sports or effort is "90% mental, 10% physical," that whole discussion is actually missing the point. It's 100% nervous system. It's neurons. When people say mental or physical, understand: it's 100% neural.

What do nerves need in order to continue to fire? They need glucose. Unless you're fully ketogenic and keto-adapted, you need carbohydrate — that's what neurons run on — and you need electrolytes. Neurons have what's called a sodium-potassium pump that generates electricity. In order to get nerve cells to fire and contract muscle, you need sufficient sodium because the action potential — the actual firing of neurons — is driven by sodium entering the cell. Then there's a removal of potassium, and then there's a resetting of those levels by the sodium-potassium pump. This process is ATP-dependent — it requires energy. You need energy in order to get neurons to fire, and it is pH-dependent. Nerves need salt, potassium, and magnesium. You need glucose and carbohydrates to power those neurons, unless you are running on ketones.

Muscle will generate energy first by using the phosphocreatine system — high bouts of effort, really intense effort lasting seconds, literally a fuel source in the muscle you burn just like logs on a fire. Glycogen, which is stored carbohydrate in the muscle, converts into ATP. Then there's stuff in our blood that's available as an energy source. In blood we have glucose — literally blood sugar floating around. If you've fasted for three days, your blood glucose will be very low, so that's not going to be a great fuel source, but you will start to liberate fats from your adipose tissue. Fatty acids will mobilize into the bloodstream and you can burn those for energy.

## [00:09:19] Heart, Lungs; Physiology & Performance Limiting Factors

There are some other factors that are important: the heart, which moves blood. The more the heart can move blood and oxygen, the more fuel that's going to be available for you to engage in muscular effort and thinking effort. As I've mentioned oxygen a few times, it should be obvious that the lungs are very important. You need to bring oxygen in and distribute it to all these tissues because oxygen is critical for the conversion of carbohydrates and fats.

When we ask, "What's limiting for performance?" we need to ask which of those things — nerve, muscle, blood, heart, and lungs — is limiting. Put differently, we ask: what should we be doing with our neurons, our muscles, our blood, our heart, and our lungs that's going to allow us to build endurance for mental and physical work, and to be able to go longer, further with more intensity?

## [00:10:35] Muscular Endurance, Protocol, Concentric Movements, Mitochondria

Let's talk about the four kinds of endurance and how to achieve them. First of all, we have muscular endurance. Muscular endurance is the ability for our muscles to perform work over time. Our failure to continue performing that work is going to be due to muscular fatigue — not cardiovascular fatigue, not because we're breathing too hard or can't get enough blood to the muscles, or because we quit mentally, but because the muscles themselves give out.

One good example would be if you had to pick up a stone in the yard — a stone that's not extremely heavy for you — and needed to do that anywhere from 50 to 100 times: picking it up and putting it down, again and again. At some point, your muscles will fatigue. Muscular endurance is going to be something you can perform for anywhere from 12 to 25, or even up to 100 repetitions. A good example is pushups. It's no coincidence that a lot of military boot-camp style training is not done with weights — it's done with things like pushups, pull-ups, sit-ups, and running — because what they're really building is muscular endurance, the ability to perform work repeatedly over time for a given set of muscles and neurons.

A really good muscular endurance training protocol, according to the scientific literature, would be three to five sets of anywhere from 12 to 100 repetitions — 12 to 25 is more reasonable for most people. The rest periods are going to be anywhere from 30 to 180 seconds of rest. The one critical feature of building muscular endurance is that it has no major eccentric loading component. Concentric loading is when you are shortening the muscle — lifting a weight — and eccentric movements are when you are lengthening a muscle — lowering a weight. If you do a pull-up and get your chin over the bar, that's the concentric portion; as you lower yourself, that's the eccentric portion. The eccentric portion of resistance training is one of the major causes of soreness and creates more damage in muscle fibers.

Muscular endurance training should not include any movements that include major eccentric loads. If you're going to do pushups, you want your chest to touch the ground — that's a real pushup — but you don't want to deliberately slow the lowering phase. A pull-up is where you pull your chin above the bar. Neither of those should include a slow eccentric component if you are using those to train muscular endurance. The format is 3 to 5 sets of 12 to 100 repetitions with 30 to 180 seconds of rest in between, mainly concentric movement. Kettlebell swings work; isometrics like planks and wall sits also work.

What's interesting is that this doesn't seem at all like what people normally think of as endurance, and yet it's been shown in peer-reviewed studies that muscular endurance can improve our ability to engage in long bouts of long-duration low-intensity endurance work. It can support long runs, long swims, and it can build postural strength and endurance simultaneously.

Let's talk about the science of why this works. If you're doing a plank — maybe you can plank for a minute, two minutes, or three minutes — at some point you will fail. You're not going to fail because the heart gives out or because you can't get enough oxygen. You're going to fail because of local muscular failure. Which means that as you do this protocol of three to five sets to build muscular endurance, mainly what you are going to be building is the ability of your mitochondria to use oxygen to generate energy locally. That's called mitochondrial respiration. It's also going to increase the extent to which the neurons control the muscles and provide a stimulus for the muscles to contract. This is independent of power and strength. It's going to mainly create this ability to endure, to continually contract muscles or repeatedly contract muscles. Remember that you want the eccentric portion to be light and relatively fast — not so fast that you injure yourself, but certainly not deliberately slowed down.

## [00:17:14] Long-Duration Endurance, Efficiency, Mitochondria, Capillaries

Now let's talk about the other extreme of endurance: long-duration endurance. This is the type that people typically think about when they think about endurance — a long run, a long swim, a long bike ride. How long? Anywhere from 12 minutes to several hours, or maybe even an entire day — 8 or 9 hours of hiking, running, or biking. Some people are doing those kinds of really long events, marathons for instance.

Your ability to continue that effort is going to be dependent mainly on the efficiency of the movement, on your ability to strike a balance between the movement itself, the generation of the muscular movements required, and fuel utilization across the different sources of nerve, muscle, blood, heart, and lungs. Why would you fail on a long run? Why would you quit? Well, your mind is going to use more or less energy depending on how much willpower, how much of a fight you have to get into with yourself in order to generate the effort. Willpower, in part, is the ability to devote resources to things, and part of that is making decisions to just either do it or not. I think it is not good — it utilizes excessive resources — to churn over decisions excessively. You probably burn as much cognitive energy deciding about whether or not to do a given training as you do in the actual training itself.

When you go out for a run that's 30 minutes, you are building the capacity to repeat that performance the next time while being more efficient — actually burning less fuel. That might seem counterintuitive, but every time you do that run, what you're doing is building up mitochondrial density. It's not just about mitochondrial oxidation and respiration — you're building up mitochondrial density. You're actually increasing the amount of ATP that you can create for a given bout of effort. You're becoming more efficient; you're burning less fuel overall doing the same thing. That's really what these long, slow distance efforts are about.

Why do this long-duration effort? Why is it good for you? It does something very important: it builds the capillary beds within muscles. These are tiny little avenues — like little tiny streams and estuaries between the bigger arteries and veins. You can literally build new capillaries, create new little streams within your muscles. The type of long-duration effort — 12 minutes or more of steady effort — is very useful for doing that, and is very useful for increasing the mitochondria, the energy-producing elements of the actual muscle cells.

The reason is when blood arrives to muscles, it has oxygen; the muscles use some of that oxygen, and then some of the deoxygenated blood is sent back to the heart and to the lungs. The more capillaries you build into those muscles, the more oxygen available to those muscles. This long-duration work, unlike muscular endurance like planks, is really about building the capillary systems and the mitochondria — the energy utilization systems within the muscles themselves.

## [00:21:07] High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT), Anaerobic Endurance, Protocol

There are two kinds in-between that in recent years have gotten a lot of attention: high-intensity interval training. One is anaerobic endurance — so-called an-aerobic, no oxygen — and the other is aerobic endurance, both of which qualify as HIIT. Let's talk about anaerobic endurance first.

Anaerobic endurance, from a protocol perspective, is going to be 3 to 12 sets. These are going to be performed at whatever speed allows you to complete the work in good, safe form. What do these sets look like? High-intensity anaerobic endurance is going to be somewhere between 3 and 12 sets, and it's going to have a ratio of work to rest of anywhere from three-to-one to one-to-five. What would a three-to-one ratio look like? It's going to be 30 seconds of hard pedaling on the bike, or running, or on the rower — these are just examples, it could be swimming, air squats, weighted squats. 30 seconds on, 10 seconds off — that's a very brief rest.

The opposite extreme on that ratio would be one-to-five: 20 seconds on, 100 seconds off. You do the work for 20 seconds, then you rest for 100 seconds. In the three-to-one ratio, if you're going to do 30 seconds of hard pedaling on a bike followed by 10 seconds off, chances are you will be able to do anywhere from 1 to as many as 12 sets if you're in really good condition. The same movement done with kettlebells — 30 seconds on, 10 seconds off — the first set will probably be in good form. The second will be in pretty good form. But by the fifth and sixth set, the quality of your repetitions will likely degrade significantly, and you increase the probability of getting injured.

If quality of form is important — maybe you're doing squats — you're going to do 20 seconds on and 100 seconds of rest. The longer rest, even though it's only 20 seconds of intense effort, will allow you to perform more quality repetitions safely over time. It might be three sets of 20 seconds of hard effort followed by 100 seconds rest, done twice a week. In doing that, you will build anaerobic endurance.

Anaerobic endurance is going to take your system into greater than 100% of your VO2 max, bring your heart rate up very high, and maximize your oxygen utilization systems. That is going to lead to fatigue at some point in the workout, and that fatigue will trigger an adaptation. What adaptation is it triggering? It's triggering mitochondrial respiration — the ability of your mitochondria to generate more energy by using more oxygen, because you're maxing out, literally getting above your VO2 max. One adaptation will be that your mitochondria will shift such that they can use more oxygen. You're also going to increase capillary beds, but not as much as you're going to increase the amount of neuron engagement of muscle.

Normally, when we start to hit fatigue — when we're exhausted, when we're breathing really hard — because the systems of the body are linked and there's a mental and motivational component to this, after that third or fourth or sixth set, there's going to be a component of you that wants to stop. By pushing through and repeating another set — safely, of course — what you're doing is training the neurons to be able to access more energy, literally converting that into ATP, and therefore allowing the muscles to access more energy and ATP. The adaptation is in the mitochondria's ability to use oxygen, and this has tremendous carryover effects for other types of exercise. This can be beneficial in competitive sports or team sports where there's a sprinting component — dribbling the ball down the field, making the maximum effort to run to the net in tennis, things of that sort.

It is different than maximum power. Even though it feels like maximum effort, it is not the same as building power and speed into muscles — those are distinctly different protocols. The key elements are that you're bringing your breathing and oxygen utilization way above your max. It's not quite hitting failure, but you're pushing the system to the point where you are not ready to do another set, and yet you begin another set anyway.

## [00:27:47] High-Intensity Aerobic Endurance, Adaptations

I want to make sure I touch on the fourth protocol: high-intensity aerobic conditioning. HIIT has these two forms — anaerobic and aerobic. High-intensity aerobic conditioning also involves about 3 to 12 sets. A one-to-one ratio is powerful for building most of the energy systems involving nerve, muscle, blood, heart, and lungs.

A one-to-one ratio might be: you run a mile, and however long that takes — let's say the first mile is seven minutes — then you rest for seven minutes, then you run a mile again and it might take eight minutes, and you rest for eight minutes. You continue that for a total of four miles of running work. Many people find that using this type of training allows them to go run half marathons and marathons even though prior to the race date, they've never actually run a half marathon or marathon. How could it be that running a mile and then resting for an equivalent amount of time, for 7 miles, allows you to run continuously for 13 or 26 miles?

It improves ATP and mitochondrial function in muscle. It allows the blood to deliver more oxygen to the muscle and to your brain. It allows your heart to deliver more oxygen overall, and it builds tremendous lung capacity. What would this look like, and when should you do this? It's really a question of how much work can you do in 8 to 12 minutes, and then rest, and then repeat? This is pretty intense, and so you would probably only want to do this two, maybe three times a week if you're not doing many other things.

## [00:30:06] Brain & Body Adaptations, Heart

Let's talk about the heart and the lungs and oxygen, because that's something we can all benefit from understanding. The brain and the heart are probably the two most important systems that you need to take care of in your life. Maintaining or enhancing brain function and cardiovascular function are clearly key for health and longevity in the short and long term. The sorts of training I talked about today have been shown again and again to be very useful for enhancing the strength of the mind — as well as the health of the brain and the body.

If you are breathing hard and your heart is beating hard — in high-intensity anaerobic conditioning you're exceeding your VO2 max, and in high-intensity aerobic conditioning you're getting up near your VO2 max — what's going to happen is that as your heart beats faster, your blood is going to be circulating faster. Oxygen utilization in muscles is going to go up, and very quickly those capillary beds start to expand. Additionally, because of the amount of blood that's being returned to the heart when you engage in these really intense bouts of effort repeatedly, the amount of blood being returned to the heart actually causes an eccentric loading of one of the muscular walls of the heart.

Your heart is muscle — cardiac muscle. When more blood is being returned to the heart because of the additional work your muscles and nerves are doing, it has the effect of creating an eccentric loading, a kind of pushing of the wall. The left ventricle essentially gets pushed back and has to push back — a kind of eccentric loading of the cardiac muscle — and the muscle thickens. As more blood is returned to the heart, there's an adaptation where the heart muscle actually gets stronger and therefore can pump more blood per stroke, per beat. As it does that, it delivers more fuel — because blood contains glucose, oxygen, and other things — to your muscles, which allows you to do yet more work per unit time.

If you do this high-intensity type training where your heart is beating very hard — so maybe the one-to-one ratio mile-run repeats I described — pretty soon the stroke volume of your heart will really increase. As a consequence, you can deliver more fuel to your muscles and to your brain, and your cognitive functioning will improve. This has been shown again and again, because there's an increase in vasculature — literally capillary beds within the brain — in the hippocampus areas that support memory, but also areas of the brain that support respiration, focus, and effort.

Weight training does have some positive effects on brain function also. However, it's very clear why the standard strength and hypertrophy-type workouts are not going to activate the blood oxygenation and stroke volume increases for the heart that the sorts of training I'm talking about today will. It just doesn't have the same positive effects.

## [00:33:57] Hydration, Tool: Galpin Equation

The other thing that's really important to think about in terms of endurance-type work is hydration. Hydration is important for all forms of physical work and exercise, not just endurance. Typically, we're going to lose anywhere from 1 to 5 pounds of water per hour of exercise — that's going to vary tremendously based on weather and intensity, probably more like 5 pounds if it's a hot day and you're exercising very intensely.

If you think about your weight in pounds, once you lose about 1% to 4% of your body weight in water, you are going to experience about a 20% to 30% reduction in work capacity — in your ability to generate effort of any kind, whether strength or endurance. You are also going to experience a significant drop in your ability to think and perform mental operations. So hydration is key, and potassium, sodium, and magnesium are really key electrolytes.

It's true that you can die from drinking too much water — if you drink too much water, you'll excrete too many electrolytes, and your brain will shut off and your heart will stop functioning properly. You don't want to overconsume water to the extreme either. A simple formula — what I call the Galpin Equation — is your body weight in pounds divided by the number 30. That is how many ounces you should drink for every 15 minutes of exercise. If you are sweating a lot, you may need more. If you're already very well hydrated, you may need less. That's a good rule of thumb to start understanding the relationship between hydration and performance.

## [00:35:38] Supplements, Stimulants, Magnesium Malate

We didn't talk about supplements much today. In previous episodes I talked about the phosphocreatine system and supplementing with creatine, and beta-alanine for moderate-duration work. Really, the only things that have been shown to improve endurance work across the four varieties of endurance I described today essentially come in two forms. One are stimulants — things like caffeine will definitely improve endurance work and power output. Certain forms of magnesium — in particular magnesium malate (M-A-L-A-T-E) — have been shown to be useful for removing or reducing the amount of delayed onset muscle soreness. That form of magnesium is distinctly different from the sorts of magnesium that are good for getting us into sleep, things like magnesium threonate and bisglycinate.
