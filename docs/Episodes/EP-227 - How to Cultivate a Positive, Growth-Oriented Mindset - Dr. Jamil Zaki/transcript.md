## [00:06:59] Cynicism

**Andrew Huberman:** Delighted to have you here and to learn from you. You have decided to tackle an enormous number of very interesting and challenging topics — challenging because my read of it, not just your book but of these fields and the science that you've done, is that people default to some complicated states and emotions sometimes that in some way serve them well, in some ways serve them less well. So I'd like to talk about this at the level of the individual and interactions between pairs and larger groups and so on. But just to kick things off, what is cynicism? What does it serve in terms of its role in the human mind?

**Jamil Zaki:** The way that psychologists think of cynicism these days is as a theory — a theory about human beings. It's the idea that generally people at their core are selfish, greedy, and dishonest. Now that's not to say that a cynical person will deny that somebody could act kindly — for instance could donate to charity, could help a stranger — but they would say all of that kind and friendly behavior is a thin veneer covering up who we really are, which is self-interested. Another way of putting this is that there are these ancient philosophical questions about people: are we good or bad, kind or cruel, caring or callous? And cynicism is answering all of those in the relatively bleak way.

**Andrew Huberman:** I believe in your book you quote Kurt Vonnegut who says "we are who we pretend to be, so we need to be careful who we pretend to be." What do you think that quote means?

**Jamil Zaki:** To me that quote is enormously powerful because it expresses the idea of self-fulfilling prophecies. There's this subjective sense that people have that our version of the world is the world, that we are passively taking in information veridically, dispassionately. And in fact that's not the case. We each construct our own version of the world. So for instance if you think about cynicism — are people kind or cruel? — that's pretty much an unanswerable question at the level of science. It's a philosophical, some could argue even a theological question. But it turns out that the way you answer that goes a long way in constructing and shaping the life that you live, the decisions that you make. So cynics — maybe it's not so much about who they pretend to be but it's about who they pretend everybody else is.

If you decide that other people are selfish, for instance, you'll be far less likely to trust them. And there's a lot of evidence that cynics, when they're put in situations with new people, even when they interact with their friends, romantic partners, and families, they still have their guard up — they're not able to make trusting and deep connections with other people. But guess what — when you treat other people in that way a couple of things happen. One, you're not able to receive what most of us need from social connections. There's one really classic and very sad study where people were forced to give an extemporaneous speech about a subject they don't know much about — a very stressful experience that raised people's blood pressure. Some of these folks had a cheerleader — not an actual cheerleader but a friendly stranger who was with them while they prepared, saying "you've got this, I know you can do it, I'm in your corner." Other people had no support. Most people when they had this friendly person by their side, their blood pressure as they prepared for the speech went up only half as much as when they were alone. But cynical people had a spike in their blood pressure that was indistinguishable in magnitude whether or not a person was by their side or not.

One way that I think about this is social connection is a deep and necessary form of psychological nourishment, and living a cynical life — making the decision that most people can't be trusted — stops you from being able to metabolize those calories, leaves you malnourished in a social way.

A second thing that happens when you choose to pretend that others are selfish, greedy, and dishonest is that you bring out the worst in them. There's a lot of research that finds that cynical people tend to do things like monitoring others, spying on them, or threatening them to make sure that that other person doesn't betray them. But of course other people can tell how we're treating them and they reciprocate our kindness and retaliate against our unkindness. So cynical people end up bringing out the most selfish qualities of others — telling a story full of villains and then ending up stuck living in that story.

## [00:12:38] Children, Attachment Styles & Cynicism

**Andrew Huberman:** How early in life does cynicism show up? I'm thinking about Sesame Street characters, which to me embody different neural circuits. You've got Cookie Monster — some strong dopaminergic drive there, knows what he wants, knows what he likes and he's going to get it. You have Elmo who's all loving, and you have Oscar the Grouch, somewhat cynical but certainly grouchy. If one observes children, how early can you observe classically defined cynicism?

**Jamil Zaki:** That's a great question. Classically defined cynicism would be hard to measure very early in life because you typically measure it through self-report — so people have to have relatively well-developed, elaborated stories that they can tell you about their version of the world. That said, one early experience and one early phenotype that's very strongly correlated with generalized mistrust and unwillingness to count on other people would be insecure attachment early in life.

Insecure attachment is a way of describing how kids experience the social world. It's often tested using something known as the strange situation, where a one-year-old is brought to a lab with their caregiver. They're in a novel environment and researchers are observing how much do they explore the space, how comfortable do they seem. Then after that a stranger enters the room, a couple minutes after that their mother leaves the room, which is of course incredibly strange and stressful for most one-year-olds. The caregiver then returns after a minute, and what researchers look at is: how comfortable is the child exploring a space with their caregiver present? How comfortable are they when other people are around? How do they react when their caregiver leaves? And how do they react at the reunion with their caregiver?

The majority of kids — approximately two-thirds of them — are securely attached, meaning that they are comfortable exploring a new space, they get really freaked out when their caregiver leaves, but then they soothe quickly when their caregiver returns. The remaining third or so of kids are insecurely attached, meaning that they're skittish in new environments even when their parent or caregiver is there, they really freak out when their caregiver leaves, and they're not very soothed upon their return.

Researchers more recently have started to think about what are the cognitive schemas, the ways that children think when they are securely or insecurely attached. One brilliant study used looking time — in kids, looking time is a metric of what surprises them. Researchers found that insecurely attached kids, when they saw a video of a reunion of a caregiver and infant acting in a way that felt loving and stable, they looked longer, as though that was surprising. Kids who were securely attached didn't look very long at those stable interactions, but looked longer at interactions that were unstable. It's almost as though there's a setup that kids develop very early: can I count on people? Am I safe with people? And insecure attachment is a signal coming early in life — "no, you're not safe with people" — that, the data show, elaborates later in life into mistrust in other relationships.

## [00:17:29] Cynicism vs. Skepticism, Complexity

**Andrew Huberman:** How different is cynicism from skepticism? Cynicism seems to carry something of a lack of anticipation about any possibility of a positive future — is that one way to think about it?

**Jamil Zaki:** That's a very sharp way of thinking about it actually. Cynicism and skepticism — people often use them interchangeably. In fact they're quite different, and I would argue that one is much more useful for learning about the world and building relationships than the other. Again, cynicism is a theory that's kind of locked in — that no matter what, people show you their true colors are untrustworthy and self-oriented. It's a hyper-Darwinian view — ultimately people, red in tooth and claw.

Skepticism is instead the restlessness with our assumptions, a desire for new information. One way I often think about it is that cynics think a little bit like lawyers — they have a decision that they've already made about you and about everybody and they're just waiting for evidence that supports their point. And when evidence comes in that doesn't support their point, they explain it away. I think cynics actually are quite similar to the naive, trusting, gullible folks that they love to make fun of. Naive gullibility is trusting people in a credulous, unthinking way. I would say cynicism is mistrusting people in a credulous and unthinking way.

If cynics think like lawyers — sort of in the prosecution against humanity — skeptics think more like scientists. Skepticism, classically in philosophy, is the belief that you can never truly know anything. But as we think about it now it's more the desire for evidence to underlie any claim that you believe. And the great thing about skepticism is it doesn't require an ounce of naivety. You can be absolutely sharp in deciding I don't want to trust this person or I do want to trust this person, but it allows you to update and learn from specific acts, specific instances, and specific people.

**Andrew Huberman:** When I think about scientists, one of the first things I think about is not just their willingness but their excitement to embrace complexity. Whereas when I think about cynics — cynics are not embracing the complexity of disagreement. It seems like it's a heuristic, it's a way to simplify the world around you.

**Jamil Zaki:** That's exactly right. Phil Tetlock has a great term for this called integrative complexity — to what extent can you hold different versions of the world, different arguments in mind? And integrative complexity is a great way to learn about the world, whereas cynicism as you rightly point out is much more of a heuristic, it's a black-and-white form of thinking.

And the really sad thing is that cynicism puts us in a position where we can't learn very much. This is what in learning theory is called a wicked learning environment. Let's think in Bayesian terms. Bayesian statistics is where you have a set of beliefs about the world, you take new information in, and that new information allows you to update your priors into a new set of beliefs. A wicked learning environment is where your priors prevent you from gathering the information that you would need to confirm or disconfirm them.

Think about mistrust — it's easy to understand why people mistrust. Some of us are insecurely attached and we've been hurt in the past, we're trying to stay safe, we don't want to be betrayed. This is a completely natural response. But when we decide to mistrust, we never are able to learn whether the people who we are mistrusting would have been trustworthy or not. When we trust, we can learn whether we've been right or not. Somebody can betray us and that hurts. Or, more often than not, the data turn out to show us they can honor that trust — we can build a relationship, we can start a collaboration, we can live a full social life.

The problem is that trusting people incorrectly, you do learn from, but mistrusting people incorrectly you don't learn from, because the missed opportunities are invisible to us.

## [00:23:30] Culture Variability & Trust

**Andrew Huberman:** Do we find cultures where it's very hard to find cynics, or perhaps even more interestingly, cultures where there really isn't even a word for cynicism?

**Jamil Zaki:** I love that question. There is a lot of variance and the data on cynicism are much more local to the US typically. But that said, there's a lot of data on generalized trust, which you could say is an inverse of cynicism. There are national and international samples of major surveys which ask people whether they agree or disagree that most people can be trusted, and there's a lot of variance around the world.

In general, the cultures that are most trusting have a couple of things in common. One, they are more economically equal than untrusting cultures. There's great work from Kate Pickett and Richard Wilkinson — they have a book called *The Spirit Level* where they look at inequality across the world related to public health outcomes, and one of them is trust.

There's also variance in trust over time. In the US that's sadly a story of decline — in 1972 about half of Americans believed that most people can be trusted, and by 2018 that had fallen to about a third of Americans. That's a drop as big — just to put it in perspective — as the stock market took in the financial collapse of 2008.

One of the seeming characteristics of cultures that tracks that is how unequal they are, in part because research suggests that when you are in a highly unequal society economically, there's a sense of zero-sum competition that develops. There's a sense that anything that another person gets, I lose. And if you have that inherent sense of zero-sum competition, then it's very difficult to form bonds, it's very difficult to trust other people.

## [00:27:40] Negative Health Outcomes; Cynicism: Perception & Intelligence

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the relationship if any between cynicism and happiness or lack of happiness?

**Jamil Zaki:** The effect of cynicism on well-being is just really documented and quite negative. There are large prospective studies with tens of thousands of people — several of these studies — that measure cynicism and then measure life outcomes in the years and decades afterwards. The news is pretty bleak for cynics: absolutely lower levels of happiness, flourishing, satisfaction with life, greater incidence of depression, greater loneliness. But it's not just the neck up that cynicism affects. Cynics over the course of their lives also tend to have greater degrees of cellular inflammation, more incidence of heart disease, and they even have higher rates of all-cause mortality — so shorter lives than non-cynics.

This is consistent with the idea that really one of the great protectors of our health is our sense of connection to other people, and if you are unable or unwilling to be vulnerable around others, to really tap into that type of connection, it stands to reason that things like chronic stress and isolation would impact not just your mind but all through your body and your organ system.

So one of the best encapsulations of a cynical view of life comes from Thomas Hobbes the philosopher who in his book *Leviathan* said we need a restrictive government because left to our own devices human life is nasty, brutish, and short. And ironically, I think that might describe the lives of cynics themselves more than most people.

But point two — if cynicism hurts us so much, why would we adopt it? Well one of the answers is because our culture glamorizes cynicism. The very stereotype that your father pointed out — if you're happy-go-lucky, if you trust people, that kind of seems dull, it seems like maybe you're not that sharp, maybe you don't understand the world.

Susan Fiske and many other psychologists have studied warmth and competence — how friendly and caring does somebody seem, and how able do they seem to accomplish hard things? And it turns out that in many studies, people's perception is that these are inversely correlated. That if you're warm maybe you're not that competent, and if you're competent maybe you shouldn't be that warm.

There's data that find that when people are presented with a cynic and a non-cynic, 70% of respondents pick a cynical person over a non-cynic for difficult intellectual tasks, and 85% of people think that cynics are socially wiser. So most of us put a lot of faith in people who don't have a lot of faith in people.

And even more ironically, we're wrong to do so. Olga Stavrova, this great psychologist who studies cynicism, has this paper called the "cynical genius illusion" where she documents all these biases — the way we think cynics are bright and wise — and then uses national data, tens of thousands of people, to show that actually cynics do less well on cognitive tests, on mathematical tests. Trust is related with things like intelligence and education. And in other work, actually cynics do less well than non-cynics in detecting liars, because if you have a blanket assumption about people you're not actually attending to evidence in a sharp way.

**Andrew Huberman:** We should remind people that a hypothesis is not a question. Every great experiment starts with a question, and then you generate a hypothesis — a theory or conclusion made upfront — and then you go collect data and you see if you prove or disprove the hypothesis. And you can never really prove a hypothesis, you can only support it or not support it with the data.

## [00:35:59] Stereotypes, Threats

**Andrew Huberman:** If we view cynics as smarter — which clearly they're not as a group — why do we send cynics in, like razors, to assess what the environment is like? Is it that we're willing to accept some false negatives — meaning we're willing to let certain opportunities go by being cynical, or by deploying a cynic — as opposed to having someone get through the filter who really can't do the job?

**Jamil Zaki:** A couple of things. One, you said that if we know that cynics aren't smarter than non-cynics, why are we deploying them? Let's be clear — we know this, meaning you and I know this and scientists know this, but the data show that most people don't know this. We maintain the stereotype in our culture that being negative about people means that you've been around the block enough times that it is a form of wisdom. So that's a stereotype that I think we need to dispel first of all.

But I do think that when we deploy cynics out in the field — when we say "I'm going to be nice but I want somebody who's really pretty negative, pretty suspicious to protect me or to protect my community" — I think that's a really understandable instinct, almost from an evolutionary perspective. We are built to pay lots of attention to threats in our environment and threats to our community. If you wind the clock back 100 to 150,000 years, what is the greatest threat to early communities? It's people — people who would take advantage of our communal nature. The thing that allows human beings to thrive is that we collaborate. But that collaboration means that a free rider — somebody who chooses to not pitch in but still take from the common pool — can do exceptionally well. They can live a life of leisure on the backs of a community that's working hard, and if that type of person proliferates, the community collapses.

So it makes sense that we depend on cynics from a threat mitigation perspective, from a risk aversion perspective. But it doesn't make sense from the perspective of trying to optimize our actual social lives.

## [00:39:48] Cooperative Environments, Collaboration & Trust

**Andrew Huberman:** Is cynicism domain-specific? If somebody is cynical in one environment, do they tend to be cynical about other aspects of life?

**Jamil Zaki:** There's a little bit of data on this, and it suggests a couple of things. One, left to our own devices, our levels of cynicism tend to be pretty stable over time — and also decline in older adulthood, contra the stereotype of the cranky older person. But another is that cynicism does tend to be pretty domain general. People who score high on the Cook-Medley Hostility Scale tend to be less trusting of strangers, but they also tend to have less trust in their romantic partnerships, less trust in their friends, and less trust in their colleagues.

But I do want to build on a great intuition you have, which is that different environments might bring out cynicism or tamp it down. And it turns out that that's also very true. As trait-like as cynicism can be, there's lots of evidence that the type of social environment we're in matters a lot.

One of my favorite studies in this domain came from southeastern Brazil. There are two fishing villages separated by about 30 to 40 miles, similar in socioeconomic status, religion, culture. But there's one big difference: one of the villages sits on the ocean, and in order to fish on the ocean you need big boats, heavy equipment — you can't do it alone, you must work together. The other village is on a lake where fishermen strike out on small boats alone and they compete with one another.

About 10 years ago, in a study led by Andreas LeBrun, a really great economist, they went to these villages and gave the folks who work there a bunch of social games to play with strangers — games like would you trust somebody with some money and see if they then want to share dividends with you? They found that when they start in their careers, lake fishermen and ocean fishermen were equally trusting and equally trustworthy. But over the course of their careers they diverged. Being in a collaborative environment where people must count on one another to survive made people over time more trusting and more trustworthy. Being in a competitive, zero-sum environment over time made people less trusting and less trustworthy.

People in both of these environments are right — if you're in a competitive environment, you don't trust, and you're right not to trust. If you're in a collaborative environment, you do trust, and you're right to trust. So which of these environments do we want to be in?

## [00:44:05] Competition, Conflict, Judgement

**Andrew Huberman:** To what extent do you think having a mixture of cooperative learning, still competition perhaps between groups, as well as individual learning and competition, can foster an erosion of cynicism?

**Jamil Zaki:** I am not here to judge or impugn cynics. I should confess that I myself struggle with cynicism and have for my entire life. Part of my journey to learn more about it and even to write this book was an attempt to understand myself and to see if it is possible to unlearn cynicism, because frankly I wanted to. So you will get no judgment from me of people who feel like it's hard to trust.

Another point I want to make is that saying that competition over the long term — zero-sum competition — can erode our trust isn't the same as saying that we should never compete. Competition is beautiful. The Olympics are going on right now and it's amazing to see what people do when they are at odds trying to best one another. Often times we are driven to greatness by people we respect who are trying to be greater than us. So absolutely, competition can be part of a very healthy social structure and a very healthy life.

I think the broader question is whether we can orient that competition at the level of a task or at the level of the person. There's a lot of work in the science of conflict and conflict resolution that looks at the difference between task conflict and personal conflict. You can imagine in a workplace two people have different ideas for what direction they want to take a project. That's great if it leads to healthy debate and if that is mutually respectful. But the minute that turns into blanket judgments about the other person — "oh the reason that they want this direction is because they're not so bright" — that's when we go from healthy skeptical conflict into cynical and destructive conflict.

You see this with athletes as well. Athletes often are very good friends, and some of the people they respect the most are the folks who they're literally battling — but they can have immense and positive regard for one another outside of the ring. So there's a huge difference between competition oriented on tasks, which can help us be the best version of ourselves, and competition that bleeds into judgment, suspicion, and mistrust.

## [00:48:46] Cynics, Awe, "Moral Beauty"

**Andrew Huberman:** I've never thought about it in this way but cynics apply almost physics-like rules to social interaction — they talk in terms of like first principles of human interactions, strict categories, thick black lines between categories, as opposed to any kind of blending of understanding. One can see how that would be a really useful heuristic, but it's not good if our goal is to learn more about the world.

**Jamil Zaki:** Yes, and I appreciate you saying that. I think that many of us have the goals of having strong relationships, of flourishing psychologically, and of learning accurately about the world. And if those are your goals, I think it's fair to say that cynicism can block your way towards them.

I love that perspective. Human beings love explanatory power. We love to be able to have laws that determine what will happen, and the laws of physics are some of our most reliable. We all use theories to predict the world. Cynicism has a great function in simplifying, but of course in simplifying we lose a lot of the detail, we lose a lot of the wonder that maybe we experienced earlier in life.

I do want to touch on another aspect of what we lose to cynicism, which is the ability to witness the beauty of human action and human kindness. My friend Dacher Keltner studies awe — you know, this emotion of experiencing something vast and also experiencing ourselves as small and a part of that vastness. He wrote a great book on awe, and in it he talks about his research where he cataloged what are the experiences that most commonly produce awe in a large representative sample of people.

When I think of awe, my first go-to is Carl Sagan's pale blue dot — this image of a kind of nebula band or cluster, basically stardust really, and there's one dot in it with an arrow, and Carl Sagan says that dot is Earth, and every king and tyrant and mother and father and every person who's ever fallen in love and every person who's ever had their heartbroken — they're all on that tiny dot. I think of outer space, I think of groves of redwood trees, I think of drone footage of the Himalayas.

But Dacher finds that if you ask people what they experience awe in response to, the number one category is what he calls moral beauty — everyday acts of kindness, giving, compassion, and connection. This is also related to what Dacher and Jonathan Haidt talk about in terms of moral elevation — witnessing positive actions that actually make us feel like we're capable of more.

Moral beauty is everywhere if you are open to it. It is the most common thing that will make you feel the vastness of our species. And to have a lawful, physics-like prediction about the world that blinkers you from seeing that — that gives you tunnel vision and prevents you from experiencing moral beauty — seems like a tragic form of simplicity.

## [00:57:13] Cynicism, Creativity & Workplace

**Andrew Huberman:** It seems to me that cynicism and awe are also opposite ends of the continuum. To me it feels like awe is a more extreme example of delight. To what extent does a sense of cynicism divorce us from delight, awe, or their collaborator, which is creativity? Everything you're saying about cynicism makes it sound anti-creative, because you're by definition eliminating possibility, and creativity is the unique original combination of existing things or the creation of new things altogether.

**Jamil Zaki:** There is some work on this and a lot of it comes actually in the context of the workplace. One version of this is what's known as stack ranking — where managers are forced to pick the highest performing and lowest performing members of their team and in essence eliminate the people at the bottom 10% every six or twelve months. Stack ranking has thankfully mostly fallen out of favor in the corporate world, but it was very much in vogue in the late 20th and early 21st century.

The idea was: if you want people to be creative, tap into who they really are — and who are we really? We are really a hyper-individualistic, Darwinian species. Stack ranking is a social Darwinist approach to management. But the opposite is true. Stack-ranked workplaces are of course miserable. The people in them are quite unhappy and more likely to leave their jobs. And it turns out that if your job is to just not be at the bottom of the pile, the last thing you want to do is take a creative risk. You do not want to go out on a limb, you do not want to try something new, if other people are going to go after you for doing that.

So cynical environments are also highly conservative — not politically conservative, but conservative in terms of the types of choices that people make. There's also a cost at the level of what we might call group creativity. A lot of our best ideas come not from our minds but from the space between us, from dialogue or from group conversation. In stacked, zero-sum environments, people are less willing to share knowledge and perspective because doing so amounts to helping your enemy succeed.

Anita Woolley and colleagues have done terrific work looking at group intelligence or collective intelligence — the idea that groups when they get together have a type of intelligence that goes above and beyond the sum of their parts. And what predicts that type of collective intelligence? One factor is people's ability to understand each other's emotions — interpersonal sensitivity. But another is their willingness to pass the mic, to share the conversation, and to collaborate. So succeeding, thriving, optimizing, and being creative both at the individual and at the group level require environments where we feel free, where we feel safe, and where we feel that contributing to somebody else can also contribute to ourselves.

## [01:04:19] Assessing Cynicism; Assumptions & Opportunities

**Andrew Huberman:** To what extent are we all a little bit cynical, and how would we explore that? If I were in your laboratory and you had 10 minutes with me, what questions would you ask me to determine how cynical I might be?

**Jamil Zaki:** The first thing that I would do is give you that classic questionnaire from Cook and Medley, which would just ask you about your theories of the world. What do you think people are like? Do you think that people are generally honest? Do you think they are generally trustworthy? It's a series of 50 statements and you're asked in a binary way — do you agree or disagree? Since then Olga Stavrova and others have adapted the Cook-Medley and made it shorter, turning the questions into continuous 1-to-7 answers.

If you were in my lab, I might also ask you to play some different economic games. The trust game involves two players. One is an investor — they start out with some amount of money, let's say $10. They can send as much of that money as they want to a trustee. The money is then tripled in value, so if the investor sends $10, in the hands of the trustee it becomes $30. The trustee can then choose to give back whatever amount they want to the investor.

**Andrew Huberman:** In that type of study, what percentage of trustees do you think make the trustworthy decision of sending back the money?

**Andrew Huberman:** Gosh — 55%?

**Jamil Zaki:** So your prediction there is quite aligned with most people's. There's a great study by Fetchenhauer and Dunning that found that people when they're asked to forecast say "I bet 52 to 55% of people will send this money back." In fact, 80% of trustees make the pro-social and trustworthy decision. And again what Fetchenhauer and Dunning found is that when we have negative assumptions, we're less likely to send over the money and therefore less likely to learn that we were wrong.

It turns out that there's a lot of evidence that when asked to estimate how friendly, trustworthy, compassionate, or open-minded others are, people's estimates come in much lower than data suggest. And this to me is both the tragedy of cynical thinking and a major opportunity. It's a tragedy because we're coming up with these simple, black-and-white, physics-like predictions about the world and they're often wrong, they're often unduly negative. An opportunity because, to the extent that we can tap into a more scientific or curious mindset, pleasant surprises are everywhere. The social world is full of a lot more positive and helpful and kind people than we realize. The average person underestimates the average person.

## [01:11:11] Social Media & Cynicism, "Mean World Syndrome"

**Andrew Huberman:** Nowadays with social media we get a window into probably billions of social interactions in the form of comments and clapbacks and retweets. What if any data exists about how social media either feeds or impedes cynicism?

**Jamil Zaki:** It's a terrific question. Social media has been a tectonic shift in our lives. It has coincided with a rise in cynicism. But history is not an experiment so you can't take two temporal trends that are coincident with one another and say that one caused the other.

That said, my own intuition and a lot of the data suggest that in at least some ways social media is a cynicism factory. The average person goes through 300 feet of social media feed a day — approximately the height of the Statue of Liberty. And so the question becomes: what are we seeing when we scroll for that long? Who are we seeing? And are they representative of what people are really like? And the answer in a lot of ways is no.

There's a lot of evidence — a lot of this comes from William Brady now at Northwestern and Molly Crockett — that when people tweet in outrage and when they tweet about immorality, algorithmically those tweets are broadcast further, shared more. This does a couple of things. One, it reinforces the people who are already tweeting in that way — Brady has this great work using a reinforcement learning model showing that when people tweet in outrage and then get egged on, that person is more likely in their future tweets to turn up the volume on that outrage.

But a second question is what about the people watching? Claire Robertson has a great paper documenting that a vast majority — 90-plus percent — of tweets are created by the 10% of the most active users, and these folks are probably not representative of the rest of us in terms of how extreme and maybe how bitter their opinions are. So when we're scrolling that Statue of Liberty's worth of information, we think that we're seeing the world. In fact we're pulling from the fringes.

This is by the way not just part of social media — it's also part of legacy media. Communication theorists talk about something called the mean world syndrome, where the more time you spend looking at the news, the more you think violent crime is up in your area, the more you think you're in danger of violent crime, even during years when violent crime is decreasing. During the stranger danger era, if you asked people how many kids are being kidnapped by strangers in the US, they would in many cases say 50,000 children per year. The real number in those years was closer to 100.

So this leaves us all absolutely exhausted and also feeling alone — people who feel like "I actually don't feel that much outrage, I actually don't want to hate everybody who's different from me" — we feel like "I guess I'm the only one." But in fact most people are just like the exhausted majority. We're paying so much attention to what journalist Amanda Ripley calls conflict entrepreneurs — people who stoke conflict on purpose — that we're confusing them with the average.

## [01:18:35] Negativity Bias, Gossip

**Andrew Huberman:** This mean world syndrome — what's the inverse? The kind world syndrome, I suppose. Attempts at creating those sorts of social media platforms have been made. Why do you think people aren't really interested in those as much?

**Jamil Zaki:** We as a species are characterized by what we call negativity bias — negative events and threats loom larger in our minds. Our decision-making is negatively biased in that we'd prefer to avoid a negative outcome than to pursue a positive outcome — that's the classic work of Kahneman and Tversky. The impressions that we form are often negatively skewed. Classic work in psychology going back to the 1950s shows that if you list three positive qualities and three negative qualities about a new person, people will judge the person on their worst qualities and remember more about their negative qualities than about their positive qualities.

We also tend to speak and share in a negatively biased way. In my lab we had a study where people witnessed other groups of four playing an economic game where they could be selfish or generous. We asked them to share a piece of information about one of the people they were playing with for a future generation of participants. When somebody acted in a selfish way, people shared information about them three times more often than when they acted in a generous way. We gossip negatively.

And again that gossip is pro-social — if there's somebody out there harming my community, of course I'm going to shout about them. But we further found that when we showed a new generation of participants the gossip that the first generation shared and asked "how generous and how selfish were people in that first generation?" — they vastly underestimated that group's generosity. In trying to protect our communities, we send highly biased information and give other people the wrong idea of who we are.

But I don't think that we generally accept our instincts and biases as a life sentence. A lot of us — human beings in general — have the instinct to trust and be kinder towards people who look like us versus people who don't. None of us sit here and say "well I have that bias in my mind so I guess I'm always going to be racially biased" — we try to counteract those instincts. Depressed people have the bias to see themselves as worthless. Well, therapy is the attempt to say "I don't want to feel this way anymore, I want to fight the default settings in my mind." So to say that this toxic environment corresponds with some of our biases is to me not the same as saying we are destined to remain in that situation.

## [01:24:03] Social Media & Cynicism, Polarization, "Hopeful Skepticism"

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think social media could be leveraged to decrease cynicism, or as you referred to it, to generate hopeful skepticism? What would a feed on social media look like that fed hopeful skepticism as opposed to cynicism?

**Jamil Zaki:** I love this train of thought. I think that what I would want in a social media feed would be for it to have more data. If you could compel every person on Earth to post to social media about what they're doing today, what they're thinking about, what they want, what their values are — just as a thought experiment — and then people's feed was a representative sample of real people on the planet, I think that would drive hopeful skepticism.

By hopeful skepticism I mean skepticism as we've described — a scientific mindset, a scientific perspective, a curiosity, a hunger for information — and then the hopeful piece I simply mean skepticism that begins with the understanding that our defaults are often too negative. So I'm going to be open, and I'm going to realize that my gut instinct is probably leading me towards the negative and can be challenged.

I think the data show us that the we're all wearing a pair of soot-colored glasses all the time. And actually the best way to make people more hopeful is to ask them to look more carefully — not to look away, but to look towards in a more accurate and open fashion.

My lab has been surveying as many Stanford undergraduates as we can about their social health. A couple years ago we asked thousands of undergraduates to describe both themselves and the average Stanford student on a number of dimensions — how empathic are you, how much do you like helping people who are struggling, how much do you want to meet new people on campus. We discovered not one but two Stanfords. The first was made up of real students who are enormously compassionate, who really want to meet new friends, who want to help their friends when they're struggling. The second Stanford existed in students' minds — their imagination of the average undergraduate was much less friendly, much less compassionate, much pricklier and more judgmental than real students were.

We found that students who underestimated their peers were less willing to strike up a conversation with a stranger or confide in a friend, and that left them more isolated and lonelier. But more recently my lab, led by a great postdoc Ray Pak, tried an intervention — as simple as you can imagine. We put posters in a number of dorms that simply said "Did you know 95% of students at Stanford would like to help their friends who are struggling? 85% want to make friends with new students." We found that when students learned this information they were more willing to take social risks, and six months later they were more likely to have a greater number of friends. So here is a virtuous cycle that can replace the vicious one — if we just show people better information.

## [01:32:59] AI, Bias Correction

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think there's a version of AI that is less cynical than people tend to be? If we were to take what we know about ways to shift ourselves from cynicism to hopeful skepticism, do you think that's something that could be laced into these large language models?

**Jamil Zaki:** I love that question. I think there is an opportunity for that. One roadblock is that AI models are products of the data that we feed them. AI models eat the internet and give it back to us in some form. To the extent that the internet is overweighting negative content and cynical content, AIs that swallow that will reflect it as well.

But I could imagine retuning the way that AI takes information to account for negativity bias and to correct for it — to debias it — and then produce an inference that is less biased, more accurate, and less cynical, and give that as a kind of digest to people. Don't make me go through my social media feed — go through it for me, correct for that negativity bias, and then give it to me in a more accurate way. That's an incredible idea.

## [01:39:05] Tools: Mindset Skepticism; Reciprocity Mindset; Social Savoring

**Andrew Huberman:** If somebody is pretty cynical, older than 25 — outside the sort of developmental plasticity range — what are the things that they can do on a daily basis to either tune down their cynicism or create room for hopeful skepticism?

**Jamil Zaki:** I think about this a lot, in part because over the last several years I haven't just been studying cynicism, I've been trying to counteract it in myself and in others. So I've focused on practical everyday things that I can do.

The first has to do with our mindsets and the ways that we approach our own thinking. I like to engage in a practice that I call being skeptical of my cynicism. That is in essence taking tools from cognitive behavioral therapy and applying them to my cynical inferences. My default mode, my factory settings, are pretty suspicious — I want to lay my cards on the table. So I often find myself in new situations suspecting people, mistrusting people, wondering if they might take advantage of me. And what I do these days is say: "wait a minute, Zaki, where is this coming from? You're a scientist, defend your inference, defend your hypothesis. What evidence do you have to back it up?" And very often I find that the evidence is thin to non-existent. That challenge — just unearthing of "wait a minute, are you sure? No you're not" — can tap into a little bit of intellectual humility.

A second thing I try to do is apply what my lab and I call a reciprocity mindset — that is, understanding that yes, people vary in how trustworthy they are, but what you do also matters. Research finds that when you trust people, they're more likely to become trustworthy because they want to reciprocate — you've honored them in this small way and so they step up. It's known as earned trust in economics. And when you mistrust people, they become less trustworthy. So in my lab we found that when you teach people this, when you teach people to own the influence that they have on others, they're more willing to be trusting. And when you're more trusting then of course the other person reciprocates, which turns into a positive cycle.

A third thing is what I call social savoring. Savoring is a general term for appreciating good things while they happen. It's related to gratitude, but gratitude is more appreciating the things that have happened to us in the past that are good. Savoring is: let's grab this moment right now and think about it.

My kids and I started savoring practices a couple years ago — I call it "classes." So we'll do an ice cream eating class or a sunset watching class. We'll just sit there and eat ice cream slowly, and I'll say "what are you enjoying about this? Is it the texture, is it the flavor? What do you want to remember about this moment?" More recently I noticed that all of this was sensory, but it wasn't very social. And what they were hearing from me about other people was negatively skewed, because gossip is negatively skewed. So I started a practice of social savoring where I try to share with my kids positive things that I notice about other people. And one thing I noticed is that that habit changed my mental processing — it actually changed what I noticed, because if you're trying to tell somebody about something, you look for examples that you can tell them about.

So those three things — being skeptical of my cynicism, adopting a reciprocity mindset, and social savoring — those are three of the psychological pieces.

## [01:46:05] Tools: Leaps of Faith; Forecasting; Encounter Counting

**Andrew Huberman:** Would love to hear about some of the actions one can take as well.

**Jamil Zaki:** If you imagine the mindset shifts that I've talked about as thinking more like a scientist about the social world, then the second step to me is to act more like a scientist in the social world. The monk and author Pico Iyer has written beautifully about treating your life like an experiment — in this moment you could interrupt the defaults, you could interrupt the patterns, and look around more carefully.

One form of this is what I call taking leaps of faith on other people. Collecting more social data requires risk, so I try to take more risks — become less risk averse in a social context. Now this is not to say I share my bank information with a prince who's going to wire me $14 million. You need to be calculated and smart and safe in the risks that you take. But I would argue that many of us are far too risk averse in the social world.

I'm an introvert, Andrew — I think you've said you're an introvert as well?

**Andrew Huberman:** I am, yeah.

**Jamil Zaki:** So as introverts we tend to think that the social world is tiring and we need to recharge on our own. But I think that sometimes my introversion morphs into something else where I underestimate the joy of social contact. There are so many times that before a dinner party I would pay an embarrassing amount of money for the other party to cancel on me. But then while I'm there and afterwards I feel totally fulfilled by the experience. It's a little bit like running — there are many times that before a run I think "gosh I really don't want to do this," and then afterwards I'm so grateful to have done so.

There's a bunch of research that finds that if you ask people to forecast what it would be like to talk with a stranger, to open up about a problem to a friend, to express gratitude, to help somebody, even to have a disagreement on ideological grounds — people forecast that these conversations would be awful, awkward, cringe, painful. This is work from Nick Epley, Juliana Schroeder, and many others on something known as undersociality. And because we have these forecasts, we simply don't pursue the conversations — we don't go deeper, we stay on the surface. When you actually challenge people to have these conversations and then report back, their actual experiences are vastly more positive and more fulfilling than their forecasts.

The second piece is not just to take those risks but to document their effects. I call this encounter counting — gathering new data from the world is great, but if you forget those data, the effects might be short-lived. I try to really remember when a social encounter is a mismatch with my expectations. I have a relative who I disagree with politically quite a bit, and when I was working on this book I said "let me take a chance — we've known each other for 30 years and we've never talked politics." So I invited her to have this conversation about an issue we really disagree on. We did not agree by the end of the conversation, but it was an immensely deep and meaningful conversation, and I actually felt like I knew her better even though we've been close for decades. I tried to write down in my journal what happened, how it counteracted my expectations — to lock in that learning from the social world so that pleasant surprises hopefully aren't as surprising anymore.

## [01:51:33] Tool: Testing & Sharing Core Beliefs

**Andrew Huberman:** Byron Katie's work is about challenging beliefs by simply asking questions about our core beliefs. The practice she promotes is to take a core belief and then start challenging it from a number of different directions: is that always true? Are there cases where that's not true? What would that look like? As a way to really deconstruct one's own core beliefs. Does what I just described fit into the framework you're describing?

**Jamil Zaki:** Absolutely. It's in essence being skeptical about our beliefs, putting them through their paces — kicking the tires on our own beliefs. This reminds me of cognitive behavioral therapy. A person who's socially anxious might tell their therapist "I think all my friends secretly hate me." They might believe that to their core. And the therapist might challenge them: "what's the evidence you have for that? Are there any instances in your entire life where that seemed not to be true? And what would it mean if it weren't true?" This is the bedrock of one of the most successful forms of therapy for depression, anxiety, and phobia in the world.

I do want to zoom in on something that you're sharing about core beliefs, because I think that in addition to testing our core beliefs, one thing that I wish we would do more is share our core beliefs, because I don't think we know what each other's core beliefs are. The amount of people from every part of the political spectrum who want more peace and less conflict is north of 80% in surveys that my lab has conducted — but people don't know that.

This is also true in workplaces. I've done projects with school systems, hospital systems, businesses. One of the things I love doing is starting with an anonymous survey of everybody in the community — asking "how much do you value empathy and collaboration? How much would you prefer a workplace defined by cooperation versus competition?" Invariably — and I'm talking about some places where you might imagine people would be competitive — a super majority of individuals in those communities want compassion, cooperation, and collaboration much more than they want competition or isolation.

One of the actions we can take if we're in a leadership position is to collect those data and show people to themselves — unveil the core beliefs of your community. Often times those core beliefs are incredibly beautiful and surprising to the people in them. It gives people what I would call not peer pressure, but peer permission — to express who they've been all along.

## [01:58:09] Polarization vs. Perceived Polarization, Politics

**Andrew Huberman:** We're recording this about three months out from a presidential election. Without getting into discussions about political camps per se, what do your data and understanding about cynicism and hopeful skepticism tell us about that whole process?

**Jamil Zaki:** I think the headline for me when I look at the data on polarization — and I'm going to talk about perceived polarization as well — is twofold. One: it's tragic because we are underestimating one another. And two: there's a lot of opportunity here because the delta between the world that we think we're in and the one that we're actually in is great.

There's a bunch of work on political perceptions done by folks like Mina Cikara at Harvard, my colleague Rob Willer in sociology at Stanford, and others. A lot of this focuses on what people think the average member of the other side is like. And it turns out that in all cases we are dead wrong about who's on the other side. We're even wrong demographically — Democrats think that 25% of Republicans make more than $250,000 a year, the actual number is 2%. Republicans vastly overestimate the percentage of Democrats who are part of the LGBTQ community.

But we're even more wrong about what they believe and what they want. The data suggests that what we think the other side believes is much greater than polarization. My late friend Emile Bruneau collected data where he gathered Republicans' and Democrats' views on immigration — he plotted the distributions of what that actually looks like, and also what each side thought the other would respond. The distributions of our actual preferences are like a hill with two peaks — Republicans want more closed borders, Democrats want them more open, but they're not that far apart and there's a lot of overlap. The distributions of our perceptions are two hills on opposite sides of a landscape — Republicans think that Democrats want totally open borders and Democrats think Republicans want totally closed borders.

There's also work on meta-perceptions — what do you think the other side thinks about you? People on both sides imagine that their rivals hate them twice as much as their rivals really do. Rob has collected data on violence — "how much do you think the other side would support violence to advance their aims?" — and here the overestimates are 400%. So we think that the average person on the other side is four times as enthusiastic about violence as they really are.

What Rob and Mina and lots of other people find is that when you ask people to actually pay attention to the data — when you show them "actually the other side fears violence just as much as you do, actually the other side is terrified of losing our democracy, actually the other side doesn't hate you" — that mitigates these escalatory impulses. You can decrease the threat that people feel from the other side by showing them who the other side really is.

I'm not saying that hopeful skepticism will solve our divided political landscape. But it's worth noting how wrong we are, and that being a little bit less wrong can at least open a door.

## [02:06:06] Challenging Conversations, Questioning Perceptions

**Andrew Huberman:** Can the same tools you've described be used to defragment some of the cynicism circuitry that exists in us around this perceived highly polarized political landscape?

**Jamil Zaki:** Absolutely. There's lots of evidence that we are actively avoiding having conversations in part because of who we think the other side is. There was an amazing study conducted during Thanksgiving of 2016, which as you may recall was directly after a very polarizing election. Researchers used geotracking on people's cell phones to examine whether, in order to go to Thanksgiving dinner, they crossed between a blue county into a red county or vice versa — in other words, are they going into "enemy territory" for Thanksgiving dinner? It turns out that people who crossed county lines had dinners that were 50 minutes shorter than people who were dining with folks who presumably they agreed with. So we're talking about forsaking pie — they're giving up pie in order to not talk with people they disagree with.

The truth, though, is that when we can collect better data, often times we end up with better perceptions — more positive and more accurate. In our lab, my wonderful graduate student Louisa Santos ran a study where we had about 160 people from all over the country who took part in Zoom conversations. We made sure that they really disagreed about gun control, immigration, and climate change. We asked them to forecast what those conversations would be like. The forecasts went from neutral to negative — some people thought it wouldn't make any difference, others thought it would be counterproductive. Some folks in our survey said "dialogue is dead, there's no point in any of these conversations."

And I should say, among the people who were cynical about these conversations was us — the research team. Louisa and I spent hours talking about "what if people start to threaten each other?" Our IRB wanted all sorts of safeguards in place.

After the conversations occurred, we asked folks who had taken part in them to rate how positive they were on a 1 to 100 scale. The most common, the modal response, was 100 out of 100. And it wasn't just that they liked the conversation — they were shocked by how much they liked it. They also reported less negative emotion for the other side as a whole, not just for the person they talked with. And they reported more intellectual humility, more openness to questioning their own views.

So here are conversations that we as a culture are actively avoiding because of our priors. Our priors are wrong given the data. But we don't know that, and we don't give ourselves chances to learn that we're wrong. When we do collect the data, when we step in and take that leap of faith, we are shocked — humbled — and feel more positive, and maybe even feel a slightly greater sense of hope.

**Jamil Zaki:** Well, Dr. Zaki, thank you so much for sharing your incredible wisdom into this area of humanity. Humans are operating rationally and yet irrationally at the same time. But in the context of cynicism and in the context of being happier individuals, families, couples, and groups, it's so clear based on everything you've shared today that we have control — there's plasticity over this aspect of ourselves — if we adopt the right mindsets and apply the right practices. These aren't just ideas, they are data-supported ideas. I just want to thank you for your incredible generosity in coming here today. Your book is phenomenal, and I highly encourage people to read it.

**Jamil Zaki:** Andrew, this has been an absolutely delightful conversation. I will say my forecast of it was very high and it has exceeded that forecast. I also just want to take a moment to thank you for your work as a science communicator. I think it's absolutely one of the most important services that we can do — as folks who have been trained and learned all this stuff — to bring that information to as many people as we can. It's an incredible mission. It's an honor to be part of that conversation.

**Andrew Huberman:** Thank you. It's a labor of love and an honor and a privilege to sit here today with you. Please do come back again.
