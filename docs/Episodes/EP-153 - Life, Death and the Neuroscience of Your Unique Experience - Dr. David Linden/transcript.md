## [00:07:54] Sensory Touch & Genitals, Krause Corpuscles

**Andrew Huberman:** Professor Linden, welcome. Thanks so much for having me — I've been looking forward to our conversation. We have a lot to talk about: lots of different aspects of science, and lots of different aspects of personal journey and what you're confronting now as it relates to your health and your future. I want to start off with a question I learned from the one and only the great Karl Deisseroth, the first guest on this podcast, my colleague at Stanford. Anytime I've met with Karl, the first thing he says is, "What are you most excited about lately?" I'm going to steal that approach — what are you most excited about lately?

**David Linden:** Very very recently, the most interesting thing that I read in neuroscience is the answer to a scientific problem that I think is really dear to a lot of people's hearts — and that is: what are the nerve endings in the genitals that are responsible for sexual sensation? People can feel aroused from being touched on lots of different parts of the body, but there's something special about the genitals regardless of sex, orientation, or identity. You would think as biologists we would know this by now, but it's been a mystery for a long time.

If you go back to 1860, there was a German neuroanatomist named Krause who cut thin sections of tissue from the penis and the clitoris and looked at them under the microscope. He saw a particular kind of nerve ending there that has since been called the Krause corpuscle, and there were lots of them in these two places. These nerve endings are also in some other places that people can find erotic — the nipples, the lips, the anus — but they're also in places like the cornea or the lining of the joints. So the distribution never quite made sense, and it was never confirmed.

In a preprint from David Ginty's group at Harvard, they found a way to label, record from, activate, and inactivate these Krause corpuscles in mice. When they recorded from them they really are mechanical sensors, as you'd expect if they were involved in sexual sensation. Then they tried to artificially activate them: using optogenetic tricks to express one of Karl Deisseroth's channelrhodopsins, they found that if they shine blue light on the Krause cells in a male mouse, the mouse gets an erection. If you turn them off in a male mouse, he's just as interested in females in heat but won't mount and thrust and ejaculate as much. If you turn them off in a female mouse during her receptive period, she's much less likely to let him mount or finish.

So finally, after all these years since 1860, we know what the nerve endings are that convey sexual sensation. And like all good science, this opens up a lot of interesting questions: why do people like different things in bed, or have different propensity for orgasm? Is part of the reason individual variation in their Krause corpuscle structure? Does sexual sensation diminish with aging because Krause corpuscle density is lost from the skin of the genitals — just as fine touch sensors in the fingertips are lost with age? Ginty's lab has opened up a whole world of science.

**Andrew Huberman:** As I recall, Ginty was your neighbor at Hopkins before he moved to Harvard?

**David Linden:** That's right. He was one of several world leaders in the cellular basis of touch sensation at Hopkins — Ginty, Steven Shea, Michael Catarina, Shin Dong-hee. Do you recall if in the preprint there was an experiment where they activated these Krause corpuscles in females?

**Andrew Huberman:** It's funny you should mention that — I sent that exact email to David Ginty, and they said they are in the process of doing that right now. I asked: is erection of the clitoris even a thing in mice? He said they're really not sure. They're activating the Krause corpuscles in female mice and just kind of staring at it, looking to see if anything happens — does the body change shape, is there a color change? They don't even quite know what they're looking for because it's that much on the leading edge.

**David Linden:** Or perhaps the female mice would be more willing to mate outside the usual time frame of receptivity if these Krause corpuscles are stimulated. My guess would be not, because I think the hormonal regulation of receptivity is like a sledgehammer and very hard to overcome — but they might be more willing to continue mating or mate for longer during their fertile time.

**Andrew Huberman:** And I just want to remind people that Dr. Rena Malik, a urologist and reproductive and sexual health expert who was on recently, made clear that the clitoris and the penis come from the same embryonic origin — they are analogous tissues.

## [00:16:46] Sexual Experiences & Sensation

**Andrew Huberman:** I do have one more question about this. These are peripheral nerves — not of the brain and spinal cord, but in what we call the periphery. My understanding is that peripheral neurons regenerate and can remodel themselves extensively in ways that neurons within the brain and spinal cord tend to remodel less, especially as one gets older out of the so-called critical period. Is it possible that these Krause corpuscles and their patterns of innervation within the genitals change according to the stimulation that people experience? Is sexual sensation experience-dependent?

**David Linden:** That is a great question, and we don't know, because monitoring this in people is not technically possible — it requires cadaver tissue. In animals it will be possible. It could be for a couple of different reasons. What you're imagining is actual structural plasticity — you'd look at these Krause corpuscles and actually see them changing their shape, size, or density as a result of experience. But what can also happen is a phenomenon like desensitization, where when there's stimulation for a long time the receptors transiently become less sensitive. It's well known particularly in males that chronic masturbation can produce desensitization of sexual sensation in the penis — and that could be a result of a morphological change in the Krause corpuscles, but it's more likely to be a change in function that you wouldn't be able to simply see by looking at structure under the microscope.

## [00:19:14] Human Individuality & Variation; Senses & Odor Detection

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to talk about your recent book and the underlying basis of what led you to write it. "Unique" is a very interesting idea — that we are all different — especially coming from a neuroscientist. Focusing on human individuality is not something that modern neuroscience has really done much of. Tell us about "Unique" and about human individuality.

**David Linden:** You're absolutely right. When I look at experiments in my own lab — we work on highly inbred mice designed to be as genetically similar to each other as possible, raised in very similar conditions — and yes, so much of science is designed to try to find general principles of brain function and to ignore individual variation. But individual variation is so important to our human experience and to the process of evolution and natural selection. To me, what's really fascinating is that when you look at the variation in the way sense organs function, it's almost a miracle that we can agree on a common reality at all.

The place where this really stands out is the olfactory system. We have approximately 400 functional receptors for different odorant molecules in our nose. If you sequence the genomes of many people, you find that the DNA encoding these odorant receptors is unusually variable from individual to individual. On average, two different people will have functional differences in 30% of their odor receptors. Leslie Vosshall and her colleagues at Rockefeller University gave odor tests — giving people different things to smell and finding the threshold at which they can detect them — and found enormous variation between people both in general terms (some people are just better smellers than others) and in terms of individual odors. There's a secreted hormone called androstenone. Some people can't smell it at all. For some people it smells rather pleasant, like grass. For some people it smells foul, like urine or sweat. It depends on genetic variation in one particular odorant receptor.

**Andrew Huberman:** Catherine Dulac, a phenomenal researcher who studies olfaction among other things, once said that some people have a gene that makes the smell of microwave popcorn smell like vomit, while others who lack this gene don't find it aversive.

**David Linden:** The relevant chemicals there are butyric acid and isovaleric acid. Researchers — I think Rachel Herz is one — have given a mixture of these two chemicals to people. If they say "this is parmesan cheese," people go "yes, that's parmesan cheese." If they say "this is vomit," they go "yes, that's vomit." And if they tell people "we fooled you — it was the same vial," people say "no you didn't, you must have made a mistake." They're convinced it couldn't have been the same thing.

This points out not only genetic variation in odor perception but also how incredibly suggestible we are in terms of odors, and how dependent we are upon cultural context. This is central to our humanity in the sense that we humans are what I like to call the anti-pandas. Pandas live in one spot in southern China and eat one thing — bamboo. Humans are the opposite: we can live in any ecological niche in the world and eat an enormous variety of foods. As a result, we have to have a very plastic olfactory system.

There are only a handful of odors that even babies find innately aversive — rotting meat odors, molecules with the evocative names like cadaverine and putrescine. But other things have to be learned. Pretty much every adult finds fecal odors unpleasant, but babies happily play with their own poop — they have to learn culturally that it's disgusting.

We might say vanilla "smells sweet" — that's like saying it "sounds red," a statement about synesthesia. But in places where vanilla is used with sugar in sweet foods, people say vanilla smells sweet. In Vietnam, where mint is mostly used in savory dishes, people won't say that mint smells sweet. There's a paired dissociation. And there's no pure perception — perception is inference. All of our perception through all of our senses — both the outward-pointing senses and the inward-pointing senses like balance — is based on experience, expectation, and the situation of the moment.

## [00:31:22] Visual Individuality; Heat Tolerance; Early Life Experiences & Variation

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any examples of uniqueness in visual perception that come to mind?

**David Linden:** I recently did a social media post involving three rings — a blue ring, a red ring, and a blue ring in the center. I asked which ring is in front. Interestingly, it splits out into about thirds: some people see the blue ring in front, others see the red ring in front, others see them all in the same plane. We think this has to do with differences between individuals in the distribution of the cone photoreceptors — which is essentially random between individuals, maybe even between the two eyes — and that gives rise to chromatic aberration, the displacement of the visual image according to the wavelength of the light.

**David Linden:** Perhaps not what you had in mind, but one way experience modifies the visual world has to do with how much light you're exposed to in the first five years or so of life. Kids who don't get outside are much more likely to be myopic when they grow up. We know that light seems to stimulate the expression of trophic factors that actually change the shape of the eyeball — not the structure of the retina or the lens or the cornea, but the actual degree of elongation of the eyeball changes, changing the way the retina sits relative to the lens. This seems to be light-dependent early in life.

This gives rise to a really interesting general principle: there are situations where you would guess that something is genetic but it isn't — it's actually dependent on early life experience. In the early days of World War II, the Japanese army had a problem: enormous numbers of soldiers became incapacitated with heat stroke, and this was much more likely to happen in soldiers from the northern part of Japan (Hokkaido, where it snows) compared to the southern part (Kyushu, a semi-tropical environment).

The classical explanation would be that this happened genetically over many generations. And we know what the trait is: more heat-tolerant people have a higher fraction of a particular class of sweat gland — eccrine sweat glands — that are innervated, meaning signals from the brain saying "your core is too hot" can make you sweat. The total density of sweat glands between northern and southern soldiers wasn't different, but southern soldiers had a higher degree of innervation. However — in those rare cases where soldiers came from a long-established northern family whose parents had moved south, and they grew up in the southern location, they had high sweat gland innervation and were well heat-tolerant. Conversely, a well-established southern Kyushu family that moved to Hokkaido: their child developed the northern sweat gland innervation pattern — less innervation, lower heat tolerance.

This gives an advantage you can't get through evolution, that can happen right away in one generation. A wonderful additional example comes from field mice — voles — and Irving Zucker's work at Berkeley. If you take pregnant female wild-caught field mice and manipulate the lights to simulate artificial spring (day length getting longer during pregnancy), their pups are born with low density of fur, anticipating summer temperatures. In artificial fall (day length getting shorter), they are born with high density of fur, anticipating winter temperatures. No matter what the actual season is, you can do this by manipulating lights in the lab. Like the sweating Japanese soldiers, this is a great example of early life plasticity.

## [00:40:28] Auditory Variability, Perfect Pitch

**Andrew Huberman:** Are you aware of any examples in the auditory domain that particularly intrigue you?

**David Linden:** One really interesting example has to do with perfect pitch — the ability to hear a note played and say "oh, that's a C." This is a pretty rare trait. Even among highly trained musicians at Peabody Conservatory at Johns Hopkins, maybe one in ten trained musicians have perfect pitch. And parenthetically, having perfect pitch doesn't necessarily make you a better musician.

So the question is, is perfect pitch heritable? When you look at twin studies — which is what we use to estimate heritability — the answer is it's kind of low. There's a heritable component but it accounts for, my recollection is, on the order of 30 to 40% of the variability in perfect pitch. However, if people receive ear training starting at a young age, the chance that they will develop perfect pitch can improve drastically.

## [00:42:08] Heritability & Human Individuality: Cognitive & Physical Traits

**David Linden:** In your book "Unique," do you cover aspects of human individuality that extend beyond perception into the cognitive domain?

**Andrew Huberman:** Yeah, absolutely. Let me set the stage for heritability and human individuality. If you look at human traits — whether behavioral traits like shyness or straightforward morphological traits like height — you tend to find very few traits that are entirely heritable and very few that are completely unheritable, with most falling in between.

Everyone in the world has either wet or dry earwax, and this is determined by variation in a single gene called ABCC11. It doesn't matter how your parents raised you, what foods you ate, what diseases your mother had when you were in the womb — it's 100% heritable. At the other end of the scale, speech accent is 0% heritable. It is entirely dependent upon the speech you experience in childhood — and interestingly, it's the speech of your peers more than your family, which is why the children of immigrants sound like the place where they wound up rather than like their parents.

Most traits fall in the middle. One of the most heritable traits in humans is height — in the United States, height is about 85% heritable; 85% of the variation can be explained by what you inherit from your mother and father. The rest is nutrition, diseases fought off, and random variation. But if you go to rural Bolivia or rural India, where people routinely don't get enough nutrition and are fighting off infectious diseases, height is only about 50% heritable — because people in those situations can't live up to their genetic potential.

The same analysis applies to general intelligence. IQ test scores, in affluent countries where people tend to have good access to nutrition, medical care, and schooling, are heritable in the ballpark of 60 to 70%. But in communities facing consistent disadvantage — poor healthcare, poor schools, trauma — IQ is less heritable, for the very same reason as height: people can't live up to their genetic potential. The takeaway is that if you want to make things better for the people of the world, everyone needs the basic conditions — adequate nutrition, decent medical care, and schools — in order to fulfill their genetic potential for positive traits.

## [00:49:36] Heritability, Environment, Personality; Twin Studies

**Andrew Huberman:** If two identical twins are raised separately, only about 60-some percent of their IQ can be predicted on the basis of their genetic makeup alone. That makes sense — if one went to demanding schools and the other went to deficient schools, one would perform less well on an IQ test.

**David Linden:** The thing is, good schools correlate with many other things — better medical care, safer neighborhoods, books in the home. Things are very deeply interconnected. The way we get estimates of heritability is two ways: one is to compare identical (monozygotic) twins with fraternal (dizygotic) twins. Identical twins share nearly 100% of their gene variants; fraternal twins share on average 50%. A more powerful but much more difficult way is to look at twins reared apart. There was a landmark study called MISTRA — the Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart — which is really the gold standard for assessing the heritability of many human traits.

For example, most personality traits — psychologists use the acronym OCEAN: openness, conscientiousness, empathy, agreeableness, and neuroticism — on average tend to be about 50% heritable. Everyone was shocked when they actually did the analysis and found that family has almost nothing to do with the non-genetic portion. Parents can inculcate many things in their children — occupational trajectories, moral ideas, religious ideas — but in terms of these OCEAN personality traits, they have astonishingly little to do with it.

So then the question becomes: if 50% of the variation in these personality traits is not from your genetics and not from your family, where does it come from? And the answer seems to be that it comes from the random nature of the development of the body and the nervous system. The genome doesn't make a blueprint for making your body and brain — it's a rather vague recipe. It doesn't say "you, glutamate-using neuron in the thalamus, grow for 200 microns toward the top, then cross the midline." It says something like, "you bunch of glutamate neurons in the thalamus, about half of you cross the midline." So for some individuals, 40% of their axons will cross the midline, and for another individual, 60% will — even in identical twins.

Identical twins aren't really identical. If you take newborn identical twins and give them a CT scan just to measure the shape of their organs, they're not the same — one twin's spleen might be 30% larger than the other's, even though they have the exact same DNA and were lying right next to each other in the womb. The reason is the random, stochastic nature of neural development.

A great way to study this is with nine-banded armadillos — the only animal in North America that carries leprosy, and they are born as identical quadruplets. If you have four newborn identical nine-banded armadillos, this is a great model system for studying stochastic differences in development. Their brains are wired slightly differently, their bodies are slightly different, and if you test them behaviorally very early in life, they have different propensities — some are bolder and explore more, some tend to hide in the corner. This variation comes from the pseudo-random, stochastic nature of development.

## [01:01:19] Development, Chance; Transgenerational Epigenetic Inheritance

**Andrew Huberman:** Is the pseudo-random stochastic nature of development one of the major driving forces for evolution? Mutations provide the variation that can subserve adaptive traits. Genetic variation is at the heart of individuality. But there are also the effects of early life experience, and the stochastic nature of development — because if through the randomness of development you happen to have a particularly great liver, you're not going to pass that on to your children through the germline.

**David Linden:** That's right. The germline is the genes present in sperm and eggs. Going to the gym and getting fit does not make your children more fit, because the germline as far as we know is not modified in a direct way by your behaviors — in most but not all cases. As you correctly said about Oded Rechavi's work and others, there is what's called transgenerational epigenetic inheritance, where you can have traits passed not just one generation but even two generations to grandchildren, without requiring modification of DNA. To date, that has been shown very convincingly in worms and in plants. The evidence in mammals is really not there yet in my opinion.

A lot of it comes from epidemiology — most famously from famines in the Overkalix region of northern Sweden, where they had very good medical records. People said: if your grandfather went through the famine, then you're more likely to have this or that trait. But there are two problems. One is that there's no established biological mechanism. The other is that the way these things were discovered is by something called HARKing — Hypothesizing After Results are Known. They did very many statistical comparisons to try to find something significant, and when you make many post-hoc comparisons, you need to apply a Bonferroni correction to set the bar much higher. Most of those studies didn't apply that correction. I remain unconvinced of transgenerational epigenetic inheritance in mammals.

That said — just because it hasn't been shown convincingly now doesn't mean it won't be. There are good people working very hard on this and they may well describe a mechanism and show this convincingly in years to come. Right now, you may well inherit your grandma's trauma, but you're probably doing it socially — through parenting and upbringing — not through marks on your DNA.

## [01:07:37] Single Generation Epigenetic Inheritance & Stress; Autism

**David Linden:** What is fairly well established is that you can transfer things epigenetically over a single generation as a result of experiences the mother has during pregnancy. During the 1918 pandemic flu, many women were pregnant and got the flu. If you look at their children — for example, males who wound up going into the army for World War II, where the Army does a complete physical with very good records — you find that the male children who were in utero during the winter of 1918 during the pandemic flu are on average a millimeter or two shorter. More interesting: the incidence of schizophrenia went up about fourfold (from about 1% to about 4%), and what we would now retrospectively call autism also went up about fourfold.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do we know that it's stress per se? Because it's stressful to have the flu, but the flu is a bunch of other things — fever, breakdown of immune barriers. Could it be the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, elevated cortisol? Or a neuro-immune interaction of some other sort?

**David Linden:** It's probably the last thing you mentioned — a neuro-immune interaction. Gloria Choi at MIT has made a mouse model of this phenomenon. She takes pregnant female mice and injects them with a chemical on the coat of viruses that mimics viral infection. This produces — in a way that interestingly depends on the bacterial content of the mother's gut — a surge of an immune signaling molecule called interleukin-17. Interleukin-17 can pass through the placenta into the fetus. If it's present at a particular point in development — sort of the mouse equivalent of the first trimester — it causes disordered development of the layers of the cortex. Instead of looking like layers of a cake, you see balls and clumps of cells. Parenthetically, in some but not all post-mortem tissue from autistic people, you can also see those balls and clumps of cells.

Choi's group blocked the interleukin-17 signaling molecule, and it blocked the phenomenon. They artificially injected the signaling molecule into fetal brain when the mom hadn't been stressed, and they could reproduce the disorder. So it seems that at least in this mouse model, you can produce the increased vulnerability to schizophrenia or autism through this immune signaling pathway.

We don't know what the effects are on children who were in utero while their mothers were fighting off COVID. We won't know for a while. There might be nothing, or there might be something serious lurking there, as there was for the 1918 pandemic flu.

## [01:15:52] Sleep Paralysis; Cerebellum, Prediction

**Andrew Huberman:** I would be totally remiss if I didn't ask for your broad top-contour understanding of the cerebellum — the so-called mini brain. Whenever I do a PubMed search on cerebellum, I see an ever-expanding set of things it's implicated in — not just balance but also timing, cognition, learning. How should we think about the cerebellum?

**David Linden:** Cerebellar researchers like to joke that the cerebellum is a counterweight to keep your head from falling forward. But of course, the cerebellum is definitely involved in motor coordination. People who have damage to the cerebellum aren't paralyzed but they tend to be clumsy. They don't coordinate their movements, have a disturbed gait, and when reaching for an object they often overshoot it and have to make successive approximating motions to get back to their target.

But if you look through evolution, the cerebellum is connected to the thalamus and from there to many regions including the frontal cortex, where phenomena like planning, decision-making, moral sense, and many aspects of personality seem to be encoded. As time has gone on — I've been in this business for over 40 years — the cerebellum has been implicated in more and more functions far removed from the motor system. The theme of what the cerebellum does is that it is there to predict the immediate future. It's trying to determine what's going to happen in the next second or two to best guide behavior.

This kind of general computation could be applied very well to motor systems — like if you're trying to hit a baseball and anticipate what the pitch is. But it also comes up in the social realm: if we're trying to read someone and predict what they are going to do — is this person friend or foe, are they competent — a lot of this depends upon predictive circuitry, in part the cerebellum. It seems the cerebellum started out for prediction related to motor control, and through evolution its basic computation has been applied to other non-motor behaviors.

As an aside, this is very analogous to what happens during REM sleep. The brain is issuing commands to your muscles to behave in your dreams — to run away or go here or there — but those signals are blocked in the brain stem, prevented from reaching your muscles. The nerves that don't go through your brain stem, like those controlling eye movements, aren't subject to that blockade — that's why you can produce rapid eye movements in REM sleep. There are REM sleep behavior disorders where people thrash and move during REM because this outflow that's normally blocked isn't blocked. Have you ever had the reverse — you wake up but are still paralyzed?

**Andrew Huberman:** I have. There's that split second that feels like eternity where you are wide awake and cannot move. It's terrifying.

**David Linden:** Sleep paralysis. You can actually find ancient Greek depictions of people lying with a demon on their chest paralyzing them — that is from sleep paralysis. Later Hogarth did a drawing of exactly that.

## [01:23:47] Nature vs. Nature, Experience; Linden Hypothesis

**Andrew Huberman:** The term nature versus nurture is relevant but never so much as when thinking about the nervous system. What are your thoughts on nature versus nurture — should there even be a "versus" in there?

**David Linden:** I don't think there should be. Nature versus nurture was popularized by Francis Galton in the 19th century, a colleague of Darwin's, and I think it's wrong or misleading in a lot of ways. The "nature" in nature versus nurture means heritability — what you inherit in gene variations from your mother and father. "Nurture" means like how your parents or community raised you. But the problem I have with "nurture" is that it is too narrow a term. It should be replaced with the word "experience" — and experience in the broadest possible sense. Not just social experience, but the foods your mother ate when she was carrying you in utero, the diseases you or your mother fought off while she was carrying you, the bacterial population of your gut. Experience meaning anything that impinges on you starting from the earliest stages of fetal development through the last day of your life.

And I have a problem with "versus" because there's this idea that these things are in opposition. Actually, experience and heredity interact in all kinds of interesting ways — some oppositional, some reinforcing. A classic genetic example: phenylketonuria (PKU), an inability to metabolize the dietary amino acid phenylalanine. You have to inherit broken copies of this gene from both parents, so it's a recessive trait — but it only matters if you eat foods rich in phenylalanine. If you don't, it doesn't matter that you inherited these things. Think about athletic ability: a lot of athletic ability has a heritable component. If you are born fast, you're more likely to do sports and practice them and get better through your experience — so here genes and experience are feeding back on each other in a positive feedback loop.

So if I were to take the phrase "nature versus nurture" and reconfigure it, I would change it to read: "heritability interacting with experience filtered through the random nature of development." That doesn't fall off the tongue as elegantly as "nature versus nurture," but it's accurate.

**Andrew Huberman:** We can shorten that up and call it the Linden Hypothesis. From here on out, "heritability interacting with experience filtered through the randomness of development" is the Linden Hypothesis.

**David Linden:** All the geneticists will be gnashing their teeth about this being named after someone who isn't actually a geneticist. And their dentists will thank you.

## [01:30:37] Mind-Body Interaction; Chemical Signals

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm fascinated by the mind-body interaction. So much of neuroscience as I was coming up through the mid-1990s to 2010s was focused on the brain piece — very little on the body. There was nothing about the gut-brain axis in early discussions and coursework. In parallel, I've been interested in mental health, physical health, and performance, and got interested in meditation, respiration-based practices, things like Yoga Nidra, things that by experience I understood had a profound influence on nervous system states. Nowadays there's an entire institute at the NIH for complementary health and medicine, exploring things like Yoga Nidra, respiration practices, even supplements. What are your thoughts on mind-body?

**David Linden:** I think to me the most important thing for people to understand is that when you have a hypothesis — say, that meditation can attenuate chronic pain — there is a temptation to think this operates outside the realm of science and biology. There are a lot of people who describe it in exactly that way, with auras or co-opting scientific terms like "resonance" and "energy" without using them in a scientific way. But that fuzzy language shouldn't obscure the fact that when you have a hypothesis that some mental state like meditation or guided breathing affects some process in the body, you should be trying to understand this in terms of biological mechanisms, not some indistinct realm like manifestation.

I learned this initially from my father, a psychiatrist — a talking-cure, old-fashioned psychoanalyst in Los Angeles. One day I said, "Dad, it's really clear that a large fraction of your patients feel better and they conquer their depression or obsessive thoughts. How do you think it works?" He said, "We don't really know the mechanics, but ultimately when it works it's not working in some airy realm — it's working by changing the biology of the brain." That was like a lightning bolt: I could understand the underlying biology. Maybe I'll do that.

As you've correctly pointed out, when you say "mind-body" you're talking about two directions — mental functions affecting the body, and phenomena in the body affecting the mind. Generally speaking, there are two classes of pathways. One is through neurons that reach out into the body and sense things — interoception, as opposed to exteroception. While we can be consciously aware of a lot of that information, a lot is happening subconsciously, like breathing happening automatically without you thinking about it.

In addition to neural signals, there is also a whole realm of hormonal or diffusible immune signals — chemicals released into the bloodstream that move throughout the body and can activate neurons in the brain to produce changes in mental function. The class of molecules called cytokines — basically the signaling hormones of the immune system — can flow through the bloodstream and lymphatic fluid and reach many parts of the body. We've known for years that specialized receptors for these cytokines are found throughout the brain, and yet we know very little about what they do. That's going to be an astonishingly fruitful area of scientific research.

## [01:39:10] Inflammation & Depression

**David Linden:** There are a lot of things these days suggesting a link between inflammation in the body — whether in the gut or in other places — to depression. This could work through inflammation-sensing neurons sending electrical signals to the brain, or through immune signaling cytokine molecules produced at the site of inflammation that travel through the bloodstream and lymphatic system to reach the brain and have effects.

One mystery about depression is that it's not very tractable to pharmacological therapy. If you look at people who suffer with depression, about a third see significant benefit from modern SSRIs and related antidepressant drugs, about a third see very tiny benefit, and about a third see no benefit at all. Part of the reason is because our term "depression" is too big a bucket — depression is actually many different biological disorders, and only a subset are helped by SSRIs. Part of it might actually have to do with inflammation.

If you just have depressed people eat anti-inflammatory drugs for a few weeks and see if it relieves their depression — the answer seems to be no. But there are definitely links between inflammation and depression. One of the early treatments for Hepatitis C that has since been superseded by more modern drugs was a pro-inflammatory cytokine molecule, and when you gave it to people to treat their hepatitis C, almost everyone became depressed. Also, the incidence of depression as a comorbidity in multiple sclerosis is enormous — and it's not simply because if you're paralyzed from MS, you're bummed out about life. People who have spinal cord injuries from accidents have major depression at a rate similar to those with MS, so that doesn't explain it. In MS there's a cytokine including interleukin-6 (IL-6) that is massively elevated in cerebrospinal fluid — and that could be causative for depression.

What if, instead of looking at the general population of depressed people, you look at the subset that don't respond to SSRI antidepressants — are they helped by anti-inflammatories? There's a bit of a hint that maybe they are. It's not definitive yet, but I think this is a really good example of how we are going to see progress in the body-to-mind part of mind-body medicine.

## [01:43:35] Neuroplasticity, Inflammation & Mental Disorders; Microglial Cells, Exercise

**Andrew Huberman:** Could I get your thoughts on one candidate hypothesis? We know that psilocybin in clinical trials seems to lead to relief of depression in approximately 65 to 80% of people, compared to SSRIs which help about a third. Could we hypothesize that relief from depression has something to do with neuroplasticity — rewiring of neural circuits — that psilocybin can encourage, that SSRIs can encourage in some people but not all, and that inflammation is a barrier to neuroplasticity? This is the only thing I can think of that reconciles the current results.

**David Linden:** I think it's a completely reasonable hypothesis. I would be broader and say honestly the relief of any neuropsychiatric condition ultimately comes from neuroplasticity in some form or another. Let me step back a bit and talk about what neuroplasticity means.

To date, there has been a focus on synapses — the contacts between neurons — as the site of neuroplasticity. And that's warranted: synapses are plastic. They change as a result of experience, hormone changes, exercise, lots of things. But synapses are not the be-all and end-all. The ion channels involved in generating electrical signals are also plastic — we call that intrinsic plasticity, as opposed to synaptic plasticity. In addition, there are literal morphological changes — sometimes cell A wasn't connected to cell B and now it is, or cell A was connected to cell B but cell B wasn't responsive enough, and now there's a change in cell B's synapse or in cell A's intrinsic firing.

One of the key cell types that's going to be important for your hypothesis — linking inflammation to synaptic plasticity — is microglial cells. Microglia are non-neuronal cells in the brain. They're motile, can crawl around, have long processes, and can literally chew away and digest bits of the extracellular scaffolding that surrounds neurons and synapses, thereby rendering them plastic. They can destroy synapses. There is a lot of indication that certain disease states may involve over-exuberant microglial pruning of synapses, beyond what they should. And we know that microglia are chock full of cytokine receptors, so they are responsive to inflammatory signals.

When talking about inflammation and drugs, it's worthwhile to mention behavioral interventions. Exercise is about as good an antidepressant as SSRIs are, and the side effects are only good. The reason exercise works to relieve depression and maintain cognitive function as you age is because of biological pathways involving microglial cells and neurons, and involving the brain's vasculature. Exercise keeps blood flowing to the brain, and as you get older, if you exercise regularly, you can make your blood vessels including those in your brain more elastic — that is almost certainly protective against both depression and cognitive decline as we age.

## [01:52:15] Fads & Science

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm delighted to hear you say the word "microglia." My postdoc adviser, the late Ben Barres, would be especially delighted. He really championed — to the point that "championed" isn't even sufficient — he was beating the drum saying we have to pay attention to glia, for the longest time. In the last ten years there's been a kind of explosion of research exploring the role of microglia and other glial cell types. The most abundant cell type in the brain is the glial cell.

**David Linden:** We scientists like to think we're very rational creatures and not subject to fads, but we totally are. When I started out in this field in the early 1980s, everything was about opioid peptides. Then there was a period where gaseous neurotransmitters like nitric oxide were all the rage. Right now glia are in the spotlight, for good reason — I'm not saying it isn't worthwhile. But there is this phenomenon of things being faddish, and people jumping on bandwagons.

This happens both in terms of the subject we study and the techniques we use. Right now the most faddish technique involves single-cell expression profiling — creating a list of what genes are turned on and how strongly they're turned on in single cells, and seeing how that changes in different cell types and with experience. It's a very valuable technique, but one could argue it's perhaps a bit overused, and that 15 years from now people will go back and say, "Gosh, those folks in 2023 were really overdoing it with the single-cell profiling."

If anyone's thinking about getting into the field of neuroscience or another area of biological research: take a look at whatever fad is happening now, and just know that in five years it will be something different. It takes you about five years to finish your PhD or postdoc, so pick something different than what's faddish now and you'll land right on the money. The deletion test becomes relevant — if you look around and see one or more groups doing what you want to do very well, just pick something else. Your life's going to be a lot more pleasant.

## [01:55:16] Mind-Body Communication; Cancer

**Andrew Huberman:** Mind-body communication is bidirectional — mind informs the body, body informs the mind. We could break this down into respiration, thought patterns, meditation practices, Yoga Nidra, hypnosis, touch-based communication. Clearly this involves a thousand different neural pathways, not just the vagus nerve, as people tend to fixate on. How do you frame up mind-body communication, and what's most intriguing to you?

**David Linden:** Just as there are two potential pathways to convey signals from the body to the mind, there are also two potential pathways to convey signals from the brain to the body: neural signals and hormones, neurotransmitters, and cytokines released from the brain. Let me give an example that I think is really fascinating, from the cancer world.

Melanoma is a bad cancer — it kills a lot of people, is highly metastatic. We know that melanomas often become innervated — contacted by neurons and wrapped and receiving signals from them. If a melanoma becomes innervated, the prognosis for that patient is worse: it's more likely to grow and spread. How does that happen? Recently there have been reports showing that neurons that innervate the melanoma don't act directly on the tumor cells. Rather, they secrete a signaling molecule that has a receptor on immune cells patrolling the edges of the melanoma and nibbling away at it. When that signaling molecule is released from the neuron, it shuts down or reduces that immune patrolling function, and consequently the tumor can grow and spread more readily. The signal is something called CGRP — calcitonin gene-related peptide.

So if neurons can affect the progression of cancer through their activity, and these neurons in the periphery are ultimately connected to the brain through a couple of hops — then is it reasonable to hypothesize that mental processes could affect cancer progression? Let's say we hypothesize that through meditative practice you can slow the progression of certain tumors that tend to get innervated. Right now this is just a wild idea. But the important thing is that this is a wild idea with a biological substrate. It's not like you meditate and magic happens and force fields open and the angels sing and then your tumor shrinks. We are saying that activity in certain areas of the brain is increased by meditative practice, and that sends signals through neurons that actually reach the tumor and make something happen through a biochemical pathway we have defined.

Early on, psychedelics and conscious breathwork caused people to lose their jobs at major universities. Nowadays there are laboratories studying deliberate respiration on health, as well as psychedelics and meditation, at every major institution, with the goal of understanding what cytokines, what neurotransmitters change through defined pathways — including vagus but also phrenic nerves and the frontal cortex. We've entered a new era.

## [02:03:28] Mind-Body, Meditation, Breathwork

**Andrew Huberman:** What do you think needs to be done to really nail down the idea that how we think influences our biology?

**David Linden:** I think it is the job of biomedical researchers right now to reclaim a lot of this from the realm of nonsense. The problem is there has been a lot of nonsense — when someone says "do my breathwork and it'll realign your chakras and reduce your gut inflammation," I'm tempted to just dismiss it. But what if the chakras are collections of nerve innervation of bodily sphincters? It could make sense. In some cases these analogies are rooted in something real, and in some cases they're just made up. The challenge is to have rigorous scientific tests of these things — to be willing to say there have been a lot of claims about how mental processes can influence the body, and only a subset are going to be true, and of that subset it is our job to understand how they work, both to rationalize them and to optimize them.

There's no question that mental processes affect the body. If you just keep someone awake and don't let them sleep long enough, they'll die — they'll die from sepsis because the barrier between gut contents and the peritoneum breaks down. How does that happen through the nervous system? We're just starting to understand.

My colleagues who are interested in respiration tell me that you can record in many different places in the brain and find a signature of the breathing rhythm as a background to neural activity — in the cerebellum, the frontal cortex, the habenula (which is implicated in depression), lots of places. The idea that conscious modulation of your breathing could have manifold effects on neural function is reasonable given those kinds of observations.

## [02:07:30] Atrial Fibrillation, Synovial Sarcoma, Heart

**Andrew Huberman:** You've been so gracious in covering this wide array of topics with such eloquence. You are in a unique position these days, because you've been diagnosed with a fatal illness — though I suppose we've all been diagnosed with a fatal illness of sorts, because we're all going to die sooner or later. If you're willing, could you tell us the story of how that diagnosis came to be, what your initial reaction was, and where things stand now?

**David Linden:** Sure. In the summer of 2020, in the dark days of COVID, I developed profound shortness of breath. I couldn't get up a flight of stairs without huffing and puffing. I thought I had COVID but all the tests were negative. After a while, when this didn't go away, my wife Dena said, "Look, you've got to go into the doctor." They hooked me up to an electrocardiogram and said, "You've got atrial fibrillation" — your heart is doing two beats every time it should do one, so you have a very high heart rate, and when the heart beats that fast it can't work very effectively, there isn't enough time to recharge before the next beat.

There is a very straightforward therapy for atrial fibrillation: if you thread a catheter through your groin up through blood vessels, you can put in a little needle and ablate a tiny little strip of cells in the heart that creates a barrier preventing that aberrant return of electrical activity — and it cures atrial fibrillation. I had that ablation surgery and sure enough it cured my atrial fibrillation, and I was feeling terrific.

As a followup a few months later they did an echocardiogram and went, "Oh my God, there's this huge mass pressing against your heart — it's like the size of a Coke can." They thought it was a hiatal hernia — your stomach poking up through the diaphragm and nestling next to your heart. So they had me chug a can of diet Dr. Pepper, then quickly get up on the table — in the echocardiogram you can see a signature of the CO2 bubbles in your soda, and if you see those in the mass then you know it's your stomach. I chugged it, got up there — nope, it's not your stomach. They said they thought it was a teratoma — a developmental anomaly you carry usually from fetal life, where a group of different cells gets in a place where it shouldn't during development and then grows. They're not malignant, they don't spread.

I had to have open-heart surgery — a big hairy deal. I was told it would last about five hours; it turned out to last two days. I was on the heart-lung bypass machine longer than you're supposed to because of clot risks — fortunately I didn't have a stroke. There was so much bleeding that they couldn't close my chest, so they left me anesthetized with my chest open until the bleeding stopped. Then I got the pathology report back, and it was bad news: it's not a teratoma, it is a kind of cancer called synovial sarcoma. Synovial sarcoma usually affects the synovium — the lining of the joints — or some other places. It's pretty rare to have it happen in the heart. For common cancers like testicular cancer or breast cancer there are huge tables of statistics from millions of patients. For synovial sarcoma of the heart, there are only individual case reports. There are no statistics because it's that rare.

The oncologist said, "I think you've got 6 to 18 months to live." That was about 27 months ago. I've fortunately exceeded that lifespan estimate. I also want to be clear that being an oncologist has got to be a terrible job, in part because you've got to give a lifespan estimate even if you really don't have the data — you can't just say "I won't do it." The oncologist was trying to do something based on very little information and made his best guess. I'm not saying he was incompetent because I've outlived my estimate.

## [02:14:22] Gratitude & Anger; Chemotherapy, Curiosity & Time Perception

**David Linden:** I was furious. I was so angry — heart cancer? Who the hell gets heart cancer? Is that even a thing? I was 59 years old. I've got a lot to do. And what was transformative for me is that at the same time that I was feeling white-hot angry with the universe, I was also feeling a deep sense of gratitude for what I've had. I've had a terrific life. I've had great parents, wonderful friends growing up, a good career. And I have a wonderful wife named Dena — how do I deserve her?

In neuroscience we often think about states as a single axis — anxious or relaxed, fleeing or approaching. But it isn't. I didn't truly understand until that moment that I could feel profoundly grateful and profoundly angry in the very same moment.

Having cancer and getting chemotherapy and radiation — it's famously deeply unpleasant. The radiation burned my esophagus; I couldn't eat for weeks, it was painful to swallow for months. And it's a deeply disempowered situation to be a medical patient — things are being done to you, drugs go in you that make you feel really bad, and there isn't that much you can do. For me, the sense of agency came from being curious — from being a total nerd about things. Part of what made me curious was my own mental processes as they related to my cancer.

For example, day after day of feeling bad from chemo — even as a pretty upbeat person who doesn't have a tendency for depression — it's hard to be positive. I could tell myself, "This is going to be over, you won't feel this way forever" — but I couldn't talk myself out of the mood. Probably because my brain was awash in interleukin-6 and I couldn't overcome it. But at the same time I was sort of from a remove being a nerd about it, going: "I bet those cytokines are messing me up right now. I bet that's what's going on." That gave me some sense of agency.

Another thing it brought home is the issue of how malleable time perception is. If someone had said to me before I was diagnosed, "You're going to die in five years," I would have been very upset — I'm 59 years old, I should get way more than five years, I have a lot to do. But after my diagnosis of 6 to 18 months, if you told me "you get five years," I'd be like: five years? Yeah, that's pretty good. I can do a lot in five years. I can finish up in the lab, do some good work, spend time with my family and travel and savor life's pleasures. Of course it's the same five years — the only thing that's different is the context.

## [02:19:58] Death, Brain & Future Prediction, Religion & Afterlife

**David Linden:** The most important thing I realized is that I really couldn't — and still can't — genuinely engage with the idea of myself being gone. I can do practical things: update my will, write letters for the people in my lab so they've got them for their next job. But in terms of genuinely engaging with my own demise — as much as I try, I really can't do that.

At first I thought, well, that's just your own lack of imagination, Linden. But the more I thought about it, the more I thought: actually no, this is a human thing. And one of the things that's different in neuroscience compared to when I was first trained is this: when I was first trained, the brain was described as a reactive structure — something happens in the world, it comes to your sense organs, goes into your brain, triggers some things, you make decisions and actions. But what we have known in more recent years is that when the brain is waiting for something to happen, it's not just idling. The brain is at every moment subconsciously trying to predict the near future. And predicting the near future is predicated on the idea that there will be a near future — that you won't be dead and gone.

So my failure to truly engage with my own demise is actually a feature — it is a side effect of the fact that the brain is always trying to predict the future. And this made me think a lot about the world's religions. Religion is everywhere in the world — if you ask anthropologists, "Is there any society that doesn't have religious ideas?" they say no. Every place in the world has religion. Most religions have stories of afterlife or reincarnation in which your consciousness endures. Why would that be? In many religions they have a deal: follow these rules in life and you'll be rewarded in the afterlife. There's variance: in some religions you meld with the divine, in others you're reincarnated. But they share the idea that your consciousness endures.

I would hypothesize that this is a side effect of the fact that the brain can't help but always trying to predict the future. When we can't imagine the world without us in it, we are forced to concoct stories of the afterlife.

## [02:24:15] Life Advice; Time Perception & Gratitude

**Andrew Huberman:** My undergraduate, graduate, and postdoc advisors all died earlier than expected, and both of the latter two described a heightened sense of gratitude especially for things they had not previously paid attention to. And I've noticed something about the knowledge of one's impending death — it shifts our attention, leads to this sense of slowing down. We know from basic videography and photography that slowing down means an increase in frame rate. Have you noticed a shift in your perception of time?

**David Linden:** Definitely my perception of time is slower, and it seems like an age since I was diagnosed. Part of that is because it's been action-packed — so many emotionally salient things, so many intense discussions with my wife and friends and the people in my lab. My wife and I have taken a lot more vacations than we normally do — there's a certain sense of packing it in that influences time perception.

But I would say for me personally the gratitude isn't about the little things. The gratitude is about the very biggest things: the gratitude for being a sentient being and having that blessing. The gratitude for being able to have a life where I can follow my own ideas and creativity. And my gratitude — I get choked up — is for the profound love that I've felt from my wife and my children. It's not noticing the sip of tea — it's the big issues.

For me, I don't want to delay my death, of course, but when I think about it, the part that makes me upset is leaving people behind. It's not for myself — I've had a great life. I'm 61, I'd like to go longer, but that's a pretty good run. I've gotten to do lots of things and have wonderful, loving, interconnected experiences.

**Andrew Huberman:** What you've left behind is enormous, and has been the consequence of actions long before your diagnosis — which I think is a clear lesson to everyone: don't wait for the diagnosis. You've mentioned the sense of agency that you felt by being able to pay attention to and explore your experience of impending death. And at the same time you've amplified and accelerated the number of things you've put into the world — writing incredible articles about your experience of life and death that we will link to. Very few people have your insight into the nervous system at the mechanistic level but also at this more holistic level. If you had advice to give to any and all of us based on the whole experience, what would it be?

**David Linden:** The advice that is really universal is what everybody already knows and is a bit trite, but I'll say it anyway: appreciate what you got while you got it. For a subset of people, the way of the nerd is very empowering — turning that curiosity to your own mortality and your own medical situation can be empowering and useful. But I don't think that should be broad advice. I think that's only for a fraction of people. For some people, it's probably the worst thing they could do, and there's nothing wrong with that.

**Andrew Huberman:** This is normally the portion of a conversation with a guest where I list off the many things they've done and how grateful I am. In the case of you being here today, I think it's self-evident how much you've not just accomplished, but how much knowledge you've put into the world — not just scientific knowledge but knowledge about the human experience. So I just want to extend a giant thank you on behalf of the listeners and viewers and myself. Thank you for coming here today, thank you for doing what you do, and so great to still have you here.

**David Linden:** Thank you — that's very kind. It's been a pure pleasure to have this discussion with you.
