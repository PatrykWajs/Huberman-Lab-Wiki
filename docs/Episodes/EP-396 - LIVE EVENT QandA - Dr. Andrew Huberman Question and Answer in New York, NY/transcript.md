## [00:02:17] You've Said Before That Stress Can Be Good for Us. How Do We Know When It's Too Much?

There is some incredible work done by Dr. Alia Crum at Stanford, who is a tenured professor of psychology and a licensed clinical psychologist. She has made important discoveries around mindset — specifically that how you think about stress shapes the physiology that follows. When subjects watch a short video about the harmful effects of stress, they experience worse outcomes: elevated blood pressure, immune suppression. When they watch a video about the beneficial aspects of stress — how it can sharpen decision-making and actually mobilize the immune system — outcomes improve. The key insight is that stress does not deplete the immune system unless it is prolonged. In fact, the immune system is mobilized by the alertness side of the autonomic nervous system. The reason people get sick after a period of intense stress is usually that they finally rest — the immune system shuts down its heightened state, and pathogens get through. Sleep loss is the real culprit.

Short-term stress — lasting one to three days — is not a problem, even if it feels intense, provided you can get rest afterward. Long-term stress is defined by a different signature: it impedes your sleep-wake cycle, causes dreams to become more anxious or nightmarish, and persists. This kind of stress requires serious attention. The solution for accumulated, historical stress appears to be a supported environment that allows full catharsis — a full ride of autonomic intensity followed by relaxation. Whether that requires psychedelics or not is beside the point; the structure of the experience is what matters. Focusing directly on the traumatic narrative is important, and this work is best done with a clinician.

A practical marker: if stress is disrupting your sleep for three or more nights, or shifting your dream content toward anxiety and nightmares, you are in long-term stress territory. Sleep quality — specifically the regularity of sleep-wake cycles — is one of the most reliable clinical diagnostics for depression and anxiety. Language is a poor proxy for what is happening inside. Look to behavior and regularity.

## [00:07:44] How Has Hypnosis Been Impacting Your Life?

Hypnosis has impacted life in both a practical and a scientific way. Before live events, roughly an hour is spent in self-directed hypnosis. David Spiegel, whose father Herbert Spiegel was one of the originators of hypnosis as a valid psychiatric treatment, has been a key collaborator in understanding its mechanisms. There is even the idea that EMDR — the back-and-forth scanning of eyes while reporting a narrative — may be capturing some elements of the hypnotic state.

On the scientific side, the laboratory works on both vision and stress. These domains turn out to be deeply connected. Hypnosis takes advantage of the relationship between eye position and brain state. The Spiegel Eye Roll Test is a valid clinical measure: when you look upward while keeping your head still, you engage brainstem circuits involved in generating alertness. When you look downward and close your eyes, you engage circuits that move you into a calmer state. Inducing hypnosis involves looking upward and then closing the eyes while maintaining that upward gaze — a simultaneously alert and relaxed state. People who can easily roll their eyes upward behind closed lids are particularly susceptible to hypnosis.

Hypnosis is defined as a state of deep relaxation combined with alertness and focus. It is a contextual narrowing. In stage hypnosis, subjects are not under anyone's control — they simply lose awareness of surrounding context, focusing internally and on dialogue with the hypnotist. This narrowing of context is the mechanism.

The reason hypnosis is so useful is that it meets the conditions required for neuroplasticity: you need both focused attention and deep relaxation. Normally these are sequential — focus, then sleep or non-sleep deep rest. Hypnosis combines them simultaneously. The practical protocol is a 5-to-15-minute self-directed script, done daily or every other day, used to reprocess strong emotional states. The approach involves imagining the thing that provokes the emotion while the body is in a state of calm — coupling a calm bodily state to a previously agitating mental content. This is difficult to achieve in conventional talk therapy.

Breathwork can also be framed through this same lens: exhales longer than inhales move you toward calm; inhales longer than exhales move you toward alertness; box breathing maintains the current state.

## [00:12:51] What Are the Most Effective Protocols for Boosting the Microbiome?

Justin Sonnenburg at Stanford, together with his wife Erika, runs a lab that has defined much of what is known about the gut microbiome. A provocative hypothesis they have raised is that humans may be the vehicles for microbiota, not the other way around — that microbial intelligence may be driving our behaviors to serve their own propagation. Whether or not that framing is serious, it reframes the relationship.

The microbiome exists not just in the gut but along all mucosal linings: throat, nose, eyes, skin, genitalia. Nasal breathing supports the nasal microbiome and acts as a superior filter compared to mouth breathing. The book Jaws by Paul Ehrlich and Sandra Kahn at Stanford, with a foreword by Jared Diamond and Robert Sapolsky, documents the evidence that mouth breathers are more prone to illness. Nasal breathing during exercise is a useful way to build this habit.

We exchange microbiota through touch. Research from Noam Sobel's lab at the Weizmann Institute shows that within the first 10 to 15 seconds of a handshake, people typically wipe their eyes — transferring the other person's molecules directly to a mucous membrane.

The most important dietary intervention comes from a Stanford study by Chris Gardner and Justin Sonnenburg: one to four servings per day of low-sugar fermented foods — kimchi, natto, sauerkraut, kefir, kombucha — produced a dramatically more robust microbiome and reduced inflammatory markers. Fiber did not produce these microbiome improvements, though fiber is still correlated with other positive outcomes such as reduced cardiovascular disease and reduced colorectal cancer risk. The inflammatory marker increase seen with high-fiber diets in that study likely reflects a different mechanism.

Probiotics and prebiotics are probably only necessary when there is dysbiosis — following antibiotics or highly processed food consumption. Cold exposure also positively interacts with microbiome health. More broadly, sleep, nutrition, microbiome health, social interactions, and exercise form the "big five" that provide buoyancy to every other system in the body. They modulate rather than mediate — they set the baseline but do not directly cure specific conditions.

## [00:18:38] Why Do Humans Love/Need Dogs so Much?

A fear lab uses dog stimuli in VR exposure therapy for people with dog phobia — with success. For those not afraid of dogs, the question of why they are so beloved comes down to several mechanisms.

Eye contact matters. Dogs make sustained eye contact, and eye contact is genuinely meaningful in terms of oxytocin release. Data from better neuroimaging studies continue to confirm this. But a simpler answer is that dogs show up reliably in a loving state. They need proper care, but once that is met, the equation is simple. The bulldog Costello, for example, seemed perpetually disappointed — and then suddenly delighted by small actions — which trained the human to work for the dog's approval, creating a loop of mutual delight.

On a more mechanistic level, there is the C tactile fiber system. These are small nerve endings in the skin that respond to slow, gentle, non-sexual grooming touch. They feed directly into serotonin and oxytocin systems. Grooming touch among members of a species — or even across species — is one of the most powerful triggers of oxytocin release, comparable to data on romantic touch and parent-child touch. This type of touch activates oxytocin, which is not really a "love hormone" so much as a neurochemical signal for present-moment connection. Dopamine, by contrast, is about pursuit — anticipating something beyond the body's surface. Oxytocin is about what you already have: skin-surface inward.

Cats exploit this system diabolically. They allow touch intermittently, then withdraw — approximating the variable-ratio schedule of human social dynamics. Dogs, by contrast, let you pet them as much as you want. The relationship is more straightforward, and that consistency is probably the core reason humans bond with them so deeply.

## [00:23:21] How Can "Night Owls" Best Function in a Society Made For "Morning Birds"

A personal transition from confirmed night owl to morning person provides the framework here. During extended solo lab work, the circadian clock drifted; isolation amplified this. One thing not often discussed: seeing faces during the day is important for mental health. Eye contact and face-to-face interaction anchor mood and are more essential than is widely appreciated.

If you are a true night owl, your circadian genes — located in the hypothalamus — are fundamentally different. You are unlikely to become a genuine morning person without friction. However, the clock can be shifted by two to eight hours using a stacked protocol. The four major levers are:

Light is the most powerful. Get bright light when you want to be awake; minimize it when you want to sleep. Temperature follows the same logic: body temperature must rise to wake up and fall to go to sleep. A cold shower or ice bath causes an initial drop followed by a thermogenic rebound — body temperature rises afterward. Stack this with light exposure in the morning to signal wakefulness to the brain. Food is the third lever: the gut has its own clock, and eating at the target wake time synchronizes the peripheral clock with the brain clock. Exercise is the fourth. All four together — light, temperature, food, exercise — can meaningfully shift chronotype over time.

For shift workers or those who truly cannot align with a standard schedule, specific protocols have been covered in other episodes of the podcast. For napping: nap if you want to, but not if it disrupts nighttime sleep. If you cannot nap, non-sleep deep rest (NSDR) provides a partial substitute.

As we age, the ratio of slow-wave sleep to REM sleep shifts, and many people find they can function on shorter total sleep. This suggests a structured approach: limited nighttime sleep plus a brief nap.

## [00:31:12] What Is a Stress Inoculation Protocol for Workplace Anxiety?

Deliberate stress inoculation involves repeatedly exposing yourself to a safe, controllable adrenaline spike so that your nervous system learns to operate from a wider range and returns to baseline more easily. One accessible method: cyclic hyperventilation — 25 hyperventilated breaths followed by a short breath hold, repeated two or three times. Lab data supports this producing measurable shifts in baseline autonomic arousal within about a week.

Cold water exposure follows the same logic. The water should be cold enough to be genuinely uncomfortable — uncomfortable enough that you want to get out but can safely remain. Open bodies of water are not recommended (a cautionary story: a colleague at the National Institutes of Mental Health nearly drowned in a river in Bethesda). A cold shower or controlled bath is appropriate.

After a week or so of deliberate adrenaline release practice, people begin to function more like someone with a naturally calibrated nervous system — not blunted, but more capable of moving fluidly up and down the arousal continuum. The seesaw metaphor is useful: the hinge loosens, and you gain control of how far you swing in either direction. Placing yourself into actual high-stakes environments — public speaking, Toastmasters, or simply starting to talk and not stopping — is the necessary complement to the physiological training.

## [00:33:04] What Do You Think Will Be the Next Hot Topic/New Trend in the Field of Neuroscience and Behavioral Therapeutics Within the Next 10-20 Years?

Brain-machine interface is the obvious answer but probably not the right one for the general population. Eddie Chang, a neurosurgeon who chairs neurosurgery at UCSF and a childhood friend, is doing extraordinary work helping people with locked-in syndrome speak again through implanted devices. Combined with AI-generated facial expressions synced to the patient's actual face, these systems allow meaningful nonverbal communication — a genuinely positive use of avatar technology. Brain-machine interface for Parkinson's, movement disorders, epilepsy, and locked-in syndrome will grow substantially. But chip implantation to enhance memory in healthy people is many decades away, and there are good reasons for caution: the nervous system has finite real estate, and lopsided augmentation carries risks.

The more likely transformative shift in the next 10-20 years is a broader public understanding that the nervous system contains pre-existing circuits capable of remarkable things — circuits that work reliably without pharmaceuticals or devices. The blurring of divisions within psychology is already happening. The camp boundaries between psychoanalytic, cognitive-behavioral, dialectic, EMDR, and hypnosis are arbitrary from a neuroscientific standpoint. They are all governed by a smaller set of central principles. The same blurring happened in neuroscience itself — the old divisions between anatomist, physiologist, and computational modeler have largely dissolved in favor of question-driven, collaborative science.

The hope is that behavioral tools — many of which most people do not know exist — will reach broader populations for conditions like depression and anxiety, where they are highly effective but chronically underutilized. Medication remains essential for conditions like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and OCD, but for a wide range of mood and anxiety disorders, behavioral tools are the underused resource.

## [00:37:53] What Changes Have You Made to Your Fitness Protocol That Include Nutrition That You Wish You Knew Before Starting the Podcast?

As an omnivore who does not eat extremely, carbohydrates are part of the diet — consumed in moderation. Not being very hungry in the morning is partly a consequence of eating more at night. No extreme fasting protocol is followed.

The most significant change to the fitness protocol has been incorporating nasal breathing during cardiovascular work. This has eliminated sleep apnea symptoms. Sleep apnea is genuinely dangerous — research from the Stanford Sleep Lab, Penn Sleep Lab, and others confirms that untreated sleep apnea creates serious downstream problems. Nasal breathing during exercise dilates the nasal passages over time. The sinuses can expand. Medical tape over the mouth during sleep is another tool some people use. For exercise, the constraint is that nasal breathing limits intensity — you cannot breathe nasally while sprinting — but at moderate intensities it works and produces lasting structural and microbiome benefits.

A second change is adding a one-to-three-minute decompression at the end of training before looking at a phone or switching tasks. This trains the nervous system to transition from high-intensity focus to lower-intensity states deliberately. Task switching — how the mind moves between focused and defocused states — is an emerging area of neuroscience that is still poorly understood. There is a significant opportunity for new tools in this space.

## [00:40:11] For Things That Take a Long Time — Career, Pursuing a Degree, Etc — Is There a Way to Know We're on the Right Path?

A practical tool: the carbon dioxide tolerance test, used in the free-diving community and others. Take three or four breaths, then one big inhale, then execute a controlled exhale — through the nose if possible — making it as slow and long as possible until the lungs are empty. Time this exhale. This is the CO2 discard rate. It measures diaphragm control via the phrenic nerve, CO2 management, and overall stress regulation. Zero to 20 seconds indicates high stress; 20-40 seconds is moderate; above 40 seconds indicates good control. This number varies with context — after a good night's sleep it will be longer; after a run it will be shorter. Use it like a blood pressure reading: a periodic check alongside resting heart rate and heart rate variability. If the discard rate shortens steadily over time, combined with sleep disruption, the nervous system needs reestablishment of baseline buoyancy before meaningful career or life decisions can be made.

On the psychological dimension: humans are very good at assigning meaning retrospectively. Most people would prefer to find meaning in the process rather than only in hindsight. The practical signal for being on the right path is the frequency with which you tap into genuine excitement and delight in your work — even from small things, surprising things, or the social interactions the work generates.

Graduate school was an example of extreme isolation — tinfoil on windows, locking out the holidays. The advisor's one complaint was that the work style seemed unfriendly, which was not accurate — it was just absorbed. The significant social interactions during that period were with the janitorial staff. Those brief exchanges were genuinely meaningful at the time. Later came the broader social world. The lesson: look actively for the small moments of delight and connection. This is an active process, not a passive one.

Rick Rubin's framing of creativity is relevant here: access the "source" — see yourself as a portal for getting certain things done rather than being a careerist optimizing for outcomes. This requires mental flexibility. It requires enough calm to see the larger pattern. And it requires the ability to move between states: gasping focus when needed, deliberate rest when needed, and knowing you have control over that movement.

The central principle underlying everything discussed in this event: it is not about landing permanently in focus or permanently in rest. It is about being able to move along the continuums deliberately. If an organism or person feels they are in control of their internal physiological state — that they can escalate, sustain, and then return to baseline — all the language around meaning, happiness, and delight begins to emerge naturally.
