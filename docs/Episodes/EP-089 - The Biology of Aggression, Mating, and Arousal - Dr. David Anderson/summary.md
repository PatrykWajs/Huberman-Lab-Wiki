# Episode 89: The Biology of Aggression, Mating, & Arousal | Dr. David Anderson

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. David Anderson, a world-leading expert on emotions and motivated states at Caltech, joins Andrew Huberman to explain why "states" — not emotions — are the more useful neurobiological framework for understanding behavior. They cover the neural circuits behind aggression, fear, and sexual behavior in depth, including landmark optogenetics research in the ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH), the role of estrogen and progesterone (not just testosterone) in male and female aggression, how the same behavior (mounting) can reflect entirely different internal states, and a detailed discussion of tachykinins as a promising therapeutic target for social-isolation-induced anxiety and aggression. The episode closes with the somatic marker hypothesis, the vagus nerve, and how the body and brain mutually shape emotional experience.

## Guest: Dr. David Anderson

**Dr. David Anderson** is a Professor of Biology at the California Institute of Technology (Caltech), a member of the National Academy of Sciences, and a longtime investigator with the Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI) — a position requiring competitive renewal every five years, which Anderson has held since 1989. His laboratory studies the neural and genetic mechanisms of emotion, aggression, fear, sexual behavior, and motivational states, primarily using *Drosophila* and mice. ## Key Takeaways

1. **Emotions are a subtype of internal states, not the other way around.** States (including sleep, arousal, motivation) change the brain's input-to-output transformation. Reframing emotions as states shifts the analysis from psychology to neurobiology and sidesteps definitional arguments about "feelings." [00:08:10](transcript.md#000810-emotions-vs-states)

2. **States have three key dimensions beyond simple valence: persistence, intensity, and generalization.** Persistence means states outlast their triggering stimulus. Intensity determines how hard a state is to interrupt. Generalization means a state spreads across contexts — an angry person becomes generally irritable, not just angry at one thing. [00:10:36](transcript.md#001036-dimensions-of-states-persistence-intensity--generalization)

3. **Arousal and valence are independent axes, not a single spectrum.** You can be highly aroused and positively valenced (excited) or highly aroused and negatively valenced (terrified). The flip between positive and negative valence is a *circuit* switch, not a simple neurochemical one. Even dopamine is involved in both positively and negatively valenced arousal — the circuit, not the chemical, determines the sign. Research in fruit flies showed two separable forms of arousal (sleep-wake and startle) that both require dopamine but use completely different neural circuits. [00:14:38](transcript.md#001438-arousal--valence)

4. **The VMH (ventromedial hypothalamus) contains dedicated circuits for aggression — and male-mouse offensive aggression is rewarding.** Optogenetic stimulation of VMH aggression neurons causes mice to attack anything present, including inanimate objects (e.g., a glove). Male mice will press a lever to get the opportunity to fight, and they prefer environments where those neurons are activated. Defensive aggression (felt when attacked or cheated) is neurobiologically distinct and its circuit location remains less characterized. VMH acts as both antenna and broadcasting center — receiving input from ~30 brain regions (smell, vision, mechanosensation) and projecting to ~30 regions to coordinate the full physiological aggression response. [00:18:11](transcript.md#001811-aggression-optogenetics--stimulating-aggression-in-mice-vmh)

5. **Three types of aggression — offensive, defensive, and predatory — are neurobiologically distinct.** Offensive aggression is rewarding and VMH-encoded. Defensive aggression is actually *enhanced* by fear (unlike offensive, which is shut down by fear). Predatory aggression engages yet another circuit; the substantia innominata has been proposed as a possible final common pathway. Conflating these under one label misleads both science and policy. [00:24:42](transcript.md#002442-aggression-types-offensive-defensive--predatory)

6. **Hydraulic pressure is a useful model for how drives accumulate and release.** Neural activity in a circuit (e.g., hunger neurons in the hypothalamus) builds as deprivation increases, then collapses after behavior is executed. For aggression, higher VMH drive lowers the threshold for fighting. If no target is available, the animal may redirect onto inanimate objects — a model for displacement aggression. Importantly, stimulating VMH or mating neurons without a target present produces only mild wandering; a conspecific (or even a kumquat) is required to "ungate" the behavior. [00:39:46](transcript.md#003946-hydraulic-pressure--aggression)

7. **Hormones modulate aggression through VMH, but the key molecules are estrogen and progesterone — not just testosterone.** Estrogen receptor alpha-expressing neurons in VMH are critical for aggression in both sexes. Testosterone's effects on aggression are largely mediated by its conversion to estrogen via aromatase — castrated male mice stop fighting and can be rescued with either testosterone *or* estrogen implants. Aromatase inhibitors reduce both fighting and sexual activity in males. Progesterone also plays a role; VMH aggression neurons also express the progesterone receptor. [00:48:31](transcript.md#004831-aggression--hormones-estrogen-progesterone--testosterone)

8. **Female aggression is hormonally gated and organized by two divisible VMH neuron subsets.** Virgin female mice mate readily with males; nursing mothers attack those same males. Within VMH, there are two distinct subsets of estrogen receptor neurons — one controlling fighting, one controlling mating. In virgins, mating neurons dominate; in nursing mothers, fighting neurons dominate. Stimulating the fight-specific subset in a virgin can induce attack. Male VMH aggression neurons are only activated by male intruders; female VMH aggression neurons are activated equally by male and female intruders. [00:52:33](transcript.md#005233-female-aggression-motherhood)

9. **Mating and aggression circuits are interdigitated in the VMH and medial preoptic area (MPOA) and can co-activate or mutually inhibit.** Optogenetic stimulation can switch a male mouse from mating to attacking mid-behavior, or from attacking to attempting to mount (stimulating MPOA "make-love-not-war" neurons stops a fight and initiates mounting toward the former opponent). Dense interconnections between VMH and MPOA are partially mutually inhibitory. Stimulating MPOA neurons in females evokes male-type mounting behavior — demonstrating the circuits for male-type mounting are present in both sexes. [00:59:48](transcript.md#005948-mating--aggressive-behaviors)

10. **The same behavior can reflect entirely different internal states — as shown by dominance mounting versus sexual mounting.** Male mice mounting other males (dominance mounting) produce no ultrasonic vocalizations, whereas sexual mounting of females is always accompanied by ultrasonic song. VMH is maximally active during dominance mounting; MPOA is maximally active during sexual mounting. This demonstrates the danger of inferring an animal's state or intent from surface behavior alone. [01:15:25](transcript.md#011525-mounting-sexual-behavior-or-dominance)

11. **Tachykinins are a conserved neuropeptide family upregulated by social isolation — and osanetant is a promising therapeutic target.** Two weeks of social isolation massively increases Tachykinin-II (Neurokinin B) in the mouse brain, driving heightened fear, anxiety, and aggression. Tachykinin-I is Substance P (classically associated with inflammatory pain). Osanetant (a Tachykinin-II / NK3 receptor blocker with a good human safety profile) reverses all isolation effects non-sedatively — allowing previously hyperaggressive isolated mice to reintegrate peacefully with cage mates. In humans, serum Tachykinin-I levels correlate with self-reported aggression in borderline personality disorder. The drug failed in schizophrenia trials (for which there was little preclinical rationale), leaving it commercially abandoned despite strong potential for isolation and bereavement stress. [01:30:38](transcript.md#013038-tachykinins--social-isolation-anxiety-fear--aggression)

12. **The PAG (periaqueductal gray) acts as a topographic routing center for innate behaviors, including fear-induced analgesia.** The dorsal PAG drives panic/flight; the ventral PAG drives freezing. Both MPOA and VMH project to different sectors of the PAG. During high-arousal states (fighting, mating), the PAG's opioid and adrenal peptide systems activate — explaining why pain is often not felt during combat until afterward (fear-induced analgesia). A 22-amino-acid peptide from the adrenal medulla (bovine adrenal medullary peptide) has demonstrated endogenous analgesic activity via a receptor Anderson's lab identified. [01:24:40](transcript.md#012440-pag-periaqueductal-gray-brain-region-pain-modulation--fear)

13. **The somatic marker hypothesis and vagus nerve explain the body's contribution to emotional experience.** Bodily sensations (gut tightness, chest pressure, heart rate) feed back to the brain via the vagus nerve and shape emotional experience — the foundation of Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis. Vagal fibers are both afferent (sensing body states) and efferent (receiving signals from the brain). Heat maps of where people "feel" specific emotions show cross-cultural regularity, reflecting genuine body-brain feedback rather than cultural metaphor. [01:43:49](transcript.md#014349-brain-body--emotions-somatic-marker-hypothesis--vagus-nerve)

## Tools & Protocols

### Reframing Emotions as States
- **What:** Replace subjective emotion labels with a state-based analysis defined by arousal, valence, persistence, intensity, and generalization.
- **How:** When analyzing behavior (your own or others'), ask: How intense is this state? How long has it persisted beyond the trigger? Is it generalizing across contexts?
- **When:** Decision-making, conflict resolution, mental health self-monitoring.
- **Ref:** [00:08:10](transcript.md#000810-emotions-vs-states), [00:10:36](transcript.md#001036-dimensions-of-states-persistence-intensity--generalization)

### Recognizing Hydraulic Pressure Before Displacement
- **What:** Awareness that unmet drives accumulate and will find an outlet — in the correct behavior, displacement, or spontaneous release.
- **How:** When noticing a building drive (prolonged frustration, unmet social needs, suppressed anger), proactively provide a sanctioned outlet before displacement aggression occurs.
- **When:** Relevant for managing anger, sexual drive, hunger, or any motivated state that accumulates with deprivation.
- **Ref:** [00:35:38](transcript.md#003538-hydraulic-pressures-for-states--homeostasis), [00:39:46](transcript.md#003946-hydraulic-pressure--aggression)

### Avoiding Prolonged Social Isolation
- **What:** Social isolation upregulates tachykinin in the brain, increasing aggression, fear, and anxiety. This effect is evolutionarily conserved and observed in flies, mice, and likely humans.
- **How:** Maintain regular social contact. Even brief, lower-quality social interaction is preferable to total isolation during periods of grief, remote work, or incarceration.
- **When:** Especially critical during bereavement, periods of enforced solitude, or mental health vulnerability.
- **Ref:** [01:30:38](transcript.md#013038-tachykinins--social-isolation-anxiety-fear--aggression)

### Osanetant (NK3 / Tachykinin-II Receptor Blocker) — Investigational
- **What:** A small-molecule drug that blocks the Tachykinin-II (NK3) receptor. Demonstrated strong reversal of isolation-induced aggression, fear, and anxiety in mice without sedation.
- **How:** Not currently prescribed for these indications. Good human safety data exists from prior schizophrenia trials. Anderson is advocating for new trials in isolation-stress and bereavement populations.
- **When:** Potential future application; currently no approved clinical use for this indication.
- **Ref:** [01:30:38](transcript.md#013038-tachykinins--social-isolation-anxiety-fear--aggression)

### Body-State Awareness (Somatic Marker Approach)
- **What:** Attending to physical sensations — gut, chest, jaw, throat — as early signals of emotional state before cognitive labeling.
- **How:** Practice interoceptive scanning during stress or high-arousal situations. Use bodily cues as early warning signals to guide decisions before the emotion fully manifests behaviorally.
- **When:** During conflict, decision-making, or any high-arousal situation.
- **Ref:** [01:43:49](transcript.md#014349-brain-body--emotions-somatic-marker-hypothesis--vagus-nerve)

## People Mentioned

**David Anderson** — Professor of Biology at Caltech, HHMI Investigator, National Academy of Sciences member. Guest. Expert on emotions, aggression, fear, and sexual behavior at the circuit and genetic level. ([Lab](https://davidandersonlab.caltech.edu/davidanderson))

**Dayu Lin** — Former postdoc in Anderson's lab, now Principal Investigator at NYU Langone. Led landmark research identifying VMH neurons driving offensive aggression in male mice, and showed offensive aggression has positive valence (is rewarding) in male mice. ([NYU Lab](https://med.nyu.edu/research/lin-lab/))

**Annegret Falkner** — Collaborated with Dayu Lin on VMH aggression research, including work showing offensive aggression has positive valence in male mice. Now PI at Princeton Neuroscience Institute. ([Princeton profile](https://pni.princeton.edu/faculty/annegret-falkner))

**Mengyu Liu** — Graduate student in Anderson's lab. Led research showing two divisible subsets of estrogen receptor neurons in the female VMH — one controlling fighting, one controlling mating — explaining the virgin-to-maternal aggression switch. ([Caltech](https://davidandersonlab.caltech.edu/))

**Moriel Zelikowsky** — Former postdoc in Anderson's lab, now Assistant Professor at the University of Utah. Led the tachykinin/social isolation research in mice, demonstrating that osanetant reverses isolation-induced aggression, fear, and anxiety. ([Utah Lab](https://zelikowskylab.com/))

**Ann Kennedy** — Former computational/theoretical member of Anderson's lab, now PI at Northwestern University. Proposed the ultrasonic vocalization experiment that distinguished dominance mounting from sexual mounting in male mice. ([Northwestern](https://www.neuroscience.northwestern.edu/))

**Ralph Adolphs** — Psychologist and neuroscientist at Caltech. Co-authored *The Neuroscience of Emotion* with David Anderson. Collaborated on the multidimensional model of emotional states (persistence, intensity, generalization). ([Caltech profile](https://www.hss.caltech.edu/people/ralph-adolphs))

**Nirao Shah** — Professor at Stanford School of Medicine; former PhD student of Anderson's. Research demonstrated that estrogen (aromatized from testosterone) is necessary for male mouse aggression via VMH estrogen receptor neurons, and that progesterone receptor neurons in VMH also play a role. ([Stanford profile](https://shahlab.stanford.edu/))

**Menno Kruk** — Neuroscientist in the Netherlands who pioneered electrical stimulation of the hypothalamus to evoke aggression in rats. Provided critical input to Anderson's lab when electrical stimulation failed in mice, helping them identify the methodological gap that optogenetics ultimately solved. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menno_Kruk))

**Konrad Lorenz** — Austrian ethologist, Nobel laureate (1973). Proposed the hydraulic model of motivated behavior: drives accumulate like pressure and release through specific behaviors or displacement. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konrad_Lorenz))

**Antonio Damasio** — Neurologist at USC. Proposed the somatic marker hypothesis: bodily sensations are part of the substrate of emotional experience and guide decision-making. Author of *Descartes' Error* and *The Feeling of What Happens*. ([USC profile](https://dornsife.usc.edu/labs/damasio/))

**Walter Rudolf Hess** — Swiss physiologist, Nobel laureate (1949). Pioneered electrical stimulation of the hypothalamus to evoke rage, sleep, and other motivated behaviors in cats — and first described two distinct types of aggression (defensive rage vs. predatory) based on electrode location. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Rudolf_Hess))

**Karl Deisseroth** — Psychiatrist and bioengineer at Stanford. Co-inventor of optogenetics, whose tool enabled Anderson's lab to successfully evoke aggression in mice via targeted VMH stimulation after 50 years of failed electrical stimulation attempts. ([Stanford profile](https://profiles.stanford.edu/karl-deisseroth))

**Robert Sapolsky** — Neuroendocrinologist at Stanford. Prior Huberman Lab guest. Mentioned here for his finding that exogenous testosterone amplifies existing behavioral tendencies rather than uniformly increasing aggression. ([Stanford profile](https://profiles.stanford.edu/robert-sapolsky))

**Scott Sternson** — Neuroscientist (formerly Janelia, now Salk Institute). Demonstrated that hunger-circuit neurons in the hypothalamus increase firing proportionally with caloric deprivation and reset sharply upon feeding — a direct model for the hydraulic pressure concept. ([Salk profile](https://www.salk.edu/scientist/scott-sternson/))

**Catherine Dulac** — Neuroscientist at Harvard. Early work suggested that circuits for male-type mounting behavior are present in the female brain — a finding later confirmed by Anderson's lab through direct MPOA stimulation in females. ([Harvard profile](https://www.mcb.harvard.edu/department/news-events/faculty-profile/catherine-dulac/))

**Emil Coccaro** — Clinical psychiatrist at the University of Chicago. Published work showing a strong correlation between self-reported aggression levels and serum Tachykinin-I (Substance P) levels in patients with borderline personality disorder — a human correlate for Anderson's mouse tachykinin findings. ([University of Chicago](https://www.uchicago.edu/))

**Anna Lembke** — Stanford psychiatrist, author of *Dopamine Nation*. Mentioned in context of pornography addiction and how ready availability of supranormal stimuli can displace natural mate-seeking behavior. ([Stanford profile](https://profiles.stanford.edu/anna-lembke))

**William James** — 19th-century American philosopher and psychologist. Proposed that bodily changes precede conscious emotional experience — an early predecessor to the somatic marker hypothesis. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_James))
