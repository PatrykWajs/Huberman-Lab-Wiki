# How to Increase Your Speed, Mobility & Longevity with Plyometrics & Sprinting | Stuart McMillan

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Stuart McMillan, head coach at Altis and coach to dozens of Olympic medalists, presents a comprehensive framework for improving movement quality across the full speed spectrum — from walking to maximal sprinting. The conversation covers the five distinct gait stages (walking, jogging, running, striding, sprinting), the mechanics that differentiate them, and why skipping — which McMillan coins "plyrics" — is the single most valuable movement tool for people of all ages because it simultaneously trains joint health, eccentric loading, cross-body coordination, plyometric stiffness, and cardiovascular conditioning. McMillan argues that the ability to sprint maximally without injury is the single best proxy for overall health and longevity, subsuming VO2 max, grip strength, and walking speed as metrics. The episode also covers how to transfer weight-room work to locomotion via staggered-stance lifting and run-specific isometric training (Alex Terca protocol), the genetics-versus-environment debate in sprinting (both matter and interact), the current state of PED use in elite track and field (largely clean by McMillan's assessment), and a unifying philosophy that great movement is an expression of identity — authentic, expansive, and joyful rather than mechanically imposed. Throughout, McMillan returns to his concept of "pressure and peace" — the dual quality of full commitment without internal tension — as the hallmark of peak performance and a well-lived life.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Speed dictates foot strike — not the reverse.** Attempting to force a forefoot strike at slow speeds creates problems by working against natural mechanics. As pace increases, foot contact migrates toward the forefoot on its own. The key moment is flat-foot contact — foot flat, 15-degree knee bend, 5-10 degree hip bend, foot under center of mass — where maximum force is applied. [00:23:38](transcript.md#002338-walking-to-sprinting-gait-patterns-tool-flat-foot-contact)

2. **Skipping (plyrics) is the single best exercise for most people.** It simultaneously trains joint health, eccentric loading on landing, cross-body coordination, plyometric tendon stiffness, and cardiovascular conditioning — all in one accessible, scalable movement requiring no equipment. McMillan coined "plyrics" to overcome the public health barrier caused by the childlike connotations of the word "skipping." [00:36:00](transcript.md#003600-tool-skipping--benefits)

3. **Eccentric strength is the true differentiator of elite sprinters.** McMillan's testing in Calgary in the 1990s showed that what most differentiated elite from sub-elite sprinters was not concentric force production but eccentric force absorption — force per unit body mass on single-leg landing. The capacity to land hard and not collapse is the defining quality. [00:53:03](transcript.md#005303-concentric--eccentric-phases-running)

4. **Older adults need eccentric training most urgently.** Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death in older adults — not from insufficient concentric strength but from inadequate eccentric deceleration. McMillan's own father (late 70s) skips 2-3 times per week; his balance, gait, and movement confidence improved within a few months of gentle skipping. [00:03:11](transcript.md#000311-older-adults-eccentric-control-tool-skipping)

5. **Staggered stance in the weight room transfers to locomotion; parallel stance does not.** All human locomotion occurs with one foot in front of the other — never with both feet parallel. Nearly all lifts should be done in staggered, heel-toe, split, or single-leg stance, alternating the leading foot to develop bilateral symmetry. Exception: trap bar deadlifts for neural drive. [00:30:35](transcript.md#003035-visual-focus-body-position-running-lifting-weights)

6. **Run-specific isometric training (Alex Terca protocol) is the most transfer-efficient strength work for advanced athletes.** The position: foot flat, 15-degree knee bend, 5-10 degree hip bend, foot directly under center of mass — the exact mid-stance sprint position. Push maximally against an immovable bar for 3-5 seconds, 3-4 reps, 3 sets, single leg only. Most effective for athletes who have plateaued on maximal strength. [02:22:12](transcript.md#002212-resistance-training-to-improve-movement-sprinting-kinetics-individualization)

7. **The four kinetic variables of sprinting are force magnitude, rate of force development, direction of force, and body mass.** Recreational athletes below the strength threshold benefit most from increasing force magnitude (get stronger). Elite athletes already at high strength levels get more from optimizing rate and direction — where plyometrics, bounding, skipping, and isometric training take over from adding weight to the bar. [02:22:12](transcript.md#002212-resistance-training-to-improve-movement-sprinting-kinetics-individualization)

8. **The leg acts as a stiff spring at top speed — and stiffness is trainable.** At maximal sprint velocity the foot contacts the ground with very little ankle or knee collapse; elastic energy stored in the Achilles and calf complex is released to propel the body forward. Elite sprinters have high leg stiffness. Plyometric training — jumping, hopping, bounding, skipping — is the primary way to develop this quality in tendons. [00:08:27](transcript.md#000827-running-form-tools-high-knees-stiff-springs-hip-extension)

9. **Movement is expression — great coaching reveals rather than imposes.** Each person has a unique movement signature reflecting personality, history, and emotional state. McMillan uses mood words ("be powerful," "run as if in gold"), play, and unstructured observation to reveal authentic movement. Bolt, Greene, and Johnson all achieved elite results through completely different movement expressions. Charlie Francis coached Ben Johnson with almost no technical cues — he created environments and let athletes figure out how to be fast. [00:22:27](transcript.md#002227-expression-through-movement-playfulness-confidence)

10. **Event selection is an identity decision as much as a physical one.** Athletes often compete in the wrong event because they identify with a particular athletic archetype rather than optimizing for physiology. Bill Bowerman advised Steve Prefontaine based on personality matching event — Pre was a front-runner, not a kicker. The event should express who you are, not just what your body is capable of. [00:17:00](transcript.md#001700-athletes-identity-race-selection)

11. **The ability to maximally sprint is the single best physical proxy for overall health and longevity.** To sprint without injury requires healthy joints, muscular power, neuromuscular coordination, cardiovascular fitness, eccentric control, adequate mobility, and a functional gait pattern — none can be absent. VO2 max, grip strength, and walking speed are reasonable proxies but sprint capacity subsumes all of them. [00:52:14](transcript.md#005214-single-physical-metric--sprinting-pressure--peace)

12. **Elite track and field is largely clean of androgens now.** PED use was rampant through the 1980s and became minimal as out-of-competition testing tightened. Evidence: Trayvon Bromell ran 9.97 as a high school junior at 5'7" and 135 lbs; Bolt ran sub-10 at 19 before any professional support structure. McMillan's coached bobsled athlete Pav Jovanovic tested positive for nandrolone in 2001 from a contaminated supplement, was banned, and later died by suicide — illustrating the human cost. [00:36:52](transcript.md#003652-performance-enhancement-elite-athletes-androgen-reputation)

13. **Rhythm is fundamental to movement efficiency — and music can be a technical coaching tool.** All locomotion is rhythmic; when parts move in coordinated timing the system is efficient and fast. Music can be used not just for motivation but to teach rhythm, cue foot strikes, and help athletes access the flow state associated with best performances. [00:51:39](transcript.md#005139-music-art-rhythm-coaching-soccer-greatest-players--countries)

14. **Pressure and peace are the dual qualities of peak performance and a well-lived life.** The best athletes apply maximum effort while remaining free of internal tension — full commitment without anxiety or self-fighting. As athletes mature, peace becomes more accessible because experience generates trust in their own capacity. This balance extends beyond sport to every domain. [00:45:05](transcript.md#004505-pressure--peace-exercise-movement--age)

15. **Early sports specialization is harmful; multi-sport development builds the higher ceiling.** Early specialization leads to burnout, overuse injuries, compensatory movement patterns, and a lower ultimate performance ceiling. The evidence strongly supports multi-sport backgrounds; most elite athletes were multi-sport athletes when young. [00:12:13](transcript.md#001213-natural-sprinters-kids-sports-specialization)

16. **The big toe (first ray) is critical to efficient locomotion via the windlass mechanism.** Push-off rolls over the big toe, fully extending the metatarsophalangeal joint and loading the plantar fascia like a bowstring; that elastic energy propels the body forward. If the big toe is stiff this mechanism is lost. Training with the rear foot elevated on the big toe (yoga lunge) loads this joint through its full range of motion. [02:32:29](transcript.md#003229-transferring-weight-room-to-track-staggered-stance-stretching)

## Tools & Protocols

### Skipping — Beginner Protocol
- **What:** Rhythmic alternating single-leg bounds with relaxed arm drive
- **How:** 30 seconds skipping alternated with 30 seconds walking; 5-10 rounds (~5-10 minutes total); progress over weeks to longer continuous bouts; target 2-3 minutes continuously before advancing
- **When:** 2-3 times per week minimum; warm-up before lifting, running, or any athletic activity
- **Ref:** [00:45:01](transcript.md#004501-tools-skipping-beginners-jogging-incorporation)

### Skipping — Intermediate/Advanced Warm-Up Protocol
- **What:** 10-15 minute skipping warm-up with graduated amplitude before any athletic activity
- **How:** Begin with gentle skip for first few minutes; progress to higher knee drive and more vigorous arm drive for latter portion; arm to eye level, knee to hip height or above
- **When:** Before any athletic activity; replaces other warm-up modalities
- **Ref:** [00:45:01](transcript.md#004501-tools-skipping-beginners-jogging-incorporation)

### Skipping — Maximum Amplitude (Primary Workout)
- **What:** Maximal height and forward distance per bound — vertical and horizontal simultaneously; arm drives to eye level, driving knee to hip height or above
- **How:** 50-meter maximal amplitude bouts; full recovery between reps (walk back); 10-15 repetitions; treat as primary training stimulus
- **When:** Standalone power-plyometric workout 1-2 times per week; latter portion of warm-up after gentle skip warm-in
- **Ref:** [00:49:50](transcript.md#004950-transition-points-tool-skipping-maximum-amplitude)

### Older Adult Skipping Protocol
- **What:** Gentle, lower-amplitude skipping prioritizing eccentric deceleration quality over height or distance
- **How:** 20-30 second bouts with walking recovery; focus on controlled single-leg landing; build gradually over weeks
- **When:** 2-3 times per week ongoing; builds fall-prevention capacity and movement confidence for adults 60+
- **Ref:** [00:03:11](transcript.md#000311-older-adults-eccentric-control-tool-skipping)

### Run-Specific Isometric Training (Alex Terca Protocol)
- **What:** Maximal isometric push against immovable bar in the exact mid-stance sprint position: foot flat, 15-degree knee bend, 5-10 degree hip bend, foot directly under center of mass
- **How:** Push as hard as possible for 3-5 seconds; 3-4 reps per set; 3 sets; single leg only
- **When:** Weight room sessions for athletes who have plateaued on maximal strength; more valuable than adding weight at advanced levels
- **Ref:** [02:22:12](transcript.md#002212-resistance-training-to-improve-movement-sprinting-kinetics-individualization)

### Staggered Stance Lifting
- **What:** All weight room exercises in staggered foot position (heel-toe, kickstand, split, or single-leg) rather than parallel bilateral
- **How:** Alternate leading foot across sets or within sets; exception: trap bar deadlifts for neural drive; non-applicable to pull-ups/dips
- **When:** Every weight room session; applies to presses, rows, curls, extensions, any ground-contact lift
- **Ref:** [02:32:29](transcript.md#003229-transferring-weight-room-to-track-staggered-stance-stretching)

### Contralateral Fascial Sling Stretch (Yoga Lunge Variant)
- **What:** Lunge with front foot flat, rear foot elevated on big toe, rear knee behind glutes, opposite arm extended overhead palm to ceiling — stretches the diagonal fascial sling from rear big toe through pelvis to opposite fingertips; mirrors the kinetic chain active during single-leg push-off
- **How:** Hold position; explore rotation, side-bend, and posterior pelvic tilt variations; 30-60 seconds each side
- **When:** Movement preparation or cool-down before running/sprinting sessions
- **Ref:** [02:32:29](transcript.md#003229-transferring-weight-room-to-track-staggered-stance-stretching)
