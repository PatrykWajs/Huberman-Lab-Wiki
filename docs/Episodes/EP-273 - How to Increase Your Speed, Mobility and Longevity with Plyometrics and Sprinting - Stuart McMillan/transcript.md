## [00:12:13] Natural Sprinters, Kids, Sports Specialization

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about who becomes a sprinter. Is it purely genetic? Is it upbringing? I know you work with elite athletes, but I'm curious — when you see a kid at, say, seven years old — can you tell if they're going to be fast?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah. You know this is interesting because I think at that age you can get a pretty good sense of somebody's potential. But potential and actual performance are two very different things. There's a lot of factors that will intervene between seven years old and twenty-two years old when an athlete might actually reach their potential. So the way I think about it is — there are definitely some kids that just look different. They move differently. They have a certain quality to their movement that indicates real potential. But there are also many kids that look ordinary at seven and eight and then something clicks and they start to move at a very high level. And vice versa — you'll see kids that look spectacular at twelve and thirteen but then everybody catches up and they end up being relatively ordinary. So I'm very cautious about making early predictions.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the argument for or against sports specialization in kids early on?

**Stuart McMillan:** I think the argument for early sports specialization is very weak. I think the evidence strongly points to the fact that early sport specialization leads to early burnout, leads to overuse injuries, and leads to a much lower ceiling in terms of ultimate performance. Because the adaptations that you want your body to make are diverse. You want to explore a wide range of movement patterns. You want to develop coordination in many different planes and in many different ways. And if you focus too early on one specific sport you start to over-train some muscle groups and under-train others, you start to develop compensatory patterns that are actually going to limit you later on. The data is pretty clear — most elite athletes were multi-sport athletes when they were young.

## [00:17:00] Athletes, Identity, Race Selection

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to talk about something that you mentioned earlier off camera which is the relationship between athlete identity and race selection. This is something I've never thought about before. Can you explain what you mean by that?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah. So one of the most interesting things in coaching is watching athletes develop their identity as athletes. And I think one of the most critical decisions that an athlete makes — not just at the elite level but at any level — is deciding which event they're going to specialize in. And this is driven partly by performance and partly by identity. There are athletes who run the 100 who should be running the 200 or the 400. And there are athletes who run the 400 who should be running the 200. And the reason they're in the wrong event often has more to do with identity than with performance. People identify with certain events and certain characters. Like the 100 meter runner is seen as a certain type of person — explosive, powerful. And the 400 meter runner is seen as a different type of person. And athletes will sometimes choose the event that best matches the image they want to project rather than the event that best matches their physical gifts.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you have an example?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah — so there was an athlete who I won't name, but she was a very talented 100/200 meter runner, and on the basis of physiology, on the basis of everything we could see, she was a 400 meter runner. She had the body, she had the aerobic capacity, she had the ability to distribute effort over time in a way that was perfectly suited for the 400. But she would not run the 400. She identified as a 100/200 runner. She wanted to be seen as a pure sprinter. She wanted to be fast. And the 400 in her mind was the middle distance event. So she ran the 100 and the 200 for years with decent results but never reached the heights that she could have. And eventually, when she was older, she did transition to the 400 and had some great results — but by then it was too late in her career.

**Andrew Huberman:** That reminds me of a quote I've heard attributed to Bill Bowerman — the great Oregon coach — where he talked about how he advised a young Steve Prefontaine on his event. Do you know that story?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah. And the argument there was whether Pre should run the mile or the 5K. And I think Bowerman understood that Prefontaine's personality was suited for a certain event. Pre was a guy who wanted to be out front. He wanted to lead from the gun. He wasn't a guy who was going to sit in the pack and kick at the end. And the event had to match the person, not just the physiology. I think that's a really important principle — the event should express who you are, not just what your body is capable of.

## [00:23:38] Walking to Sprinting, Gait Patterns, Tool: Flat-Foot Contact

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the continuum from walking to sprinting. So everyone knows how to walk, most people know how to jog, but what are the actual stages and what makes them different mechanically?

**Stuart McMillan:** So the way I think about it there are really five stages. You've got walking, you've got jogging, running, striding, and sprinting. And they are fundamentally different movement patterns. They're not just more or less of the same thing. And I think that's important. When you go from walking to jogging you don't just move your legs faster. The mechanics actually change. And the way I would describe the change from one to the next — it comes down primarily to speed. As speed increases, the mechanics need to change to accommodate that speed. And one of the most important variables in that is foot contact. How your foot strikes the ground changes as you move through these stages. At a walk you have a heel strike. That's normal, that's fine. As you jog and run you start to see more midfoot contact. And as you get to striding and sprinting you're landing on the forefoot or the ball of the foot.

**Andrew Huberman:** The cue I've heard from elite coaches is that speed dictates foot strike. Is that how you think about it?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes exactly. Speed dictates foot strike. And that's one of the most important cues we give. Because if you try to force a forefoot strike when you're jogging slowly you're actually going to create problems. You're working against the natural mechanics. But as speed increases, foot strike naturally moves toward the forefoot. So rather than thinking about where on your foot you're landing, think about moving faster. The foot strike will take care of itself. That said, there's a specific drill cue we use around what we call flat-foot contact, which is when the foot is flat on the ground directly underneath the center of mass. That moment — when the foot is flat, the knee has about a 15-degree bend, the hip has about a 5 to 10-degree bend — that's the moment of maximum force application. And we want athletes to spend more time in that position, in that quality of contact, because that's where speed comes from.

**Andrew Huberman:** And at that moment you're applying force straight down into the ground?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. And the direction of that force is critical. At top speed you want the force to be as vertical as possible, because that's what propels you. During acceleration you want more horizontal force. That's why the body is leaned forward during the drive phase out of the blocks. But at top speed you're much more upright, and the force is going straight down.

## [00:30:35] Visual Focus, Body Position, Running, Lifting Weights

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about visual focus when running. Where should the eyes be looking?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah this is something I find really fascinating. At the highest speeds — when you're in a full sprint — the head should be basically neutral, looking slightly down the track. Not chin to chest but not dramatically tilted up either. The thing that's really important is that the torso leads and the head follows. So the head doesn't drive the movement. The torso drives the movement. And when athletes get fatigued or when they're trying too hard, the head starts to come up, the chin juts out, the neck extends — and as soon as that happens you get a cascade of tension through the body that disrupts the entire kinetic chain. So one of the cues we use is to keep the face soft. Eyes forward, jaw relaxed, face relaxed. Because that relaxation cascades down through the body.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about body position more broadly when lifting weights? You seem to think about weight training very much in the context of movement and sprinting.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah absolutely. And I think one of the biggest mistakes people make in the weight room is that they're training in positions that have very little transfer to how they actually move. When you stand with both feet parallel — in a squat position — that's a pattern we almost never use in locomotion. When was the last time you had both feet parallel and bent your knees while moving? Never. All locomotion is done in a staggered stance, with one foot in front of the other. So one of the things I advocate very strongly is doing most of your weight room work in a staggered stance. Because that more closely mirrors the positions you're actually going to be in when you're running.

## [00:36:00] Tool: Skipping & Benefits

**Andrew Huberman:** So let's talk about skipping. You've described skipping — or what you call plyrics — as potentially the single most important exercise people can do. That's a pretty bold claim. Can you back it up?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah I think I can. So let me first say what skipping is mechanically. Skipping is essentially a bounding movement. It's a single-leg jump repeated alternately — left leg, right leg, left leg, right leg. When done properly it involves the full kinetic chain. It involves the ankles, the knees, the hips, the core, the shoulders, the arms. It's a cross-body, coordinated, rotational, rhythmic movement. And I would argue that it checks more boxes in terms of physical development than almost any other single exercise.

**Andrew Huberman:** Walk me through the boxes.

**Stuart McMillan:** Okay so first — joint health. Skipping loads the joints of the lower body in a way that is functional and progressive. The impact forces in skipping are lower than in running but higher than in walking, so it's a great middle ground for people who want to load the joints without the full impact of running. Second — eccentric loading. Skipping has a significant eccentric component. When you land on one leg, your muscles are absorbing force while lengthening. And eccentric strength is one of the most important qualities for injury prevention and for longevity. It's also one of the most undertrained qualities in most people's programs. Third — coordination. Skipping requires cross-body coordination — the left arm swings with the right leg and vice versa. And that cross-body pattern is the fundamental pattern of human locomotion. When we walk, run, sprint — it's always the contralateral arm and leg working together. Skipping reinforces that pattern. Fourth — plyometric loading. Skipping is a form of plyometric exercise. It involves a rapid loading and reloading of the elastic energy in the tendons and muscles. This is the quality that makes sprinters fast. And it's a quality that you can train with skipping. And fifth — it's accessible. Anyone can skip. You don't need equipment. You don't need a gym. You can do it in your driveway, in a park, anywhere. And it scales very well — from a gentle beginner skip all the way up to a maximal amplitude sprint-pace skip that will challenge the most elite athlete.

**Andrew Huberman:** And why plyrics? Where does that term come from?

**Stuart McMillan:** So plyrics is a word that I coined — it's a combination of plyometrics and the word for the act of skipping. I wanted a term that captured what skipping really is, which is a plyometric exercise. Because the word "skipping" conjures images of children on a playground and people don't take it seriously. And that's one of the biggest barriers. The name makes people think it's too easy, too childlike. But when you see elite athletes doing maximal amplitude skipping it's anything but childlike. It's incredibly demanding.

## [00:45:01] Tools: Skipping, Beginners, Jogging Incorporation

**Andrew Huberman:** So give me a practical protocol for skipping. How do you introduce it to someone who hasn't skipped since childhood?

**Stuart McMillan:** Right so the entry level for skipping — and I'll make this very practical — is to simply skip. Just start skipping. Don't think too much about it. Most adults, when they start skipping, are very self-conscious. They feel awkward. They feel exposed. Because skipping is associated with childhood and they feel they look silly. And that's the first barrier to overcome. You've just got to get over that and start moving. For a true beginner I'd say start with 30 seconds of skipping alternated with 30 seconds of walking. Do that for 5 to 10 repetitions. So you're looking at maybe 5 to 10 minutes total. And then over a few weeks you can progress that. You can increase the duration of the skipping intervals and decrease the walking intervals. And then when you get comfortable with skipping for 2 to 3 minutes continuously, you can start to incorporate it into a more formal warm-up or workout.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what does a more advanced protocol look like?

**Stuart McMillan:** For an intermediate to advanced person who's comfortable with skipping, I think incorporating it as a primary warm-up tool is incredibly valuable. I'd use 10 to 15 minutes of skipping as a warm-up before any athletic activity — before lifting, before running, before anything. And within that you'd do different variations. You'd do a gentle warm-up skip for the first few minutes. And then you'd progress to a higher amplitude skip — higher knee lift, more vigorous arm drive — for the latter part of the warm-up. For people who want to use skipping as the primary workout — and I think this is underrated — you can do maximal amplitude skipping as the primary training stimulus. In that case I'd recommend doing 50 meter maximal amplitude skips with full recovery between reps. So you go as high and as hard as you can for 50 meters, walk back, and then do it again. 10 to 15 reps of that is a legitimate workout.

## [00:49:50] Transition Points, Tool: Skipping, Maximum Amplitude

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about what maximum amplitude skipping actually looks like. Because I think most people have a picture of low-level skipping in their head.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so maximum amplitude skipping is essentially jumping as high as you can with each bound while simultaneously covering as much ground forward as you can. So each bound has two qualities — vertical and horizontal. You want to go high and you want to go far. And the arm drive is critical. The arm opposite to the driving leg drives up — elbow bent at about 90 degrees, hand drives up to about eye level. And simultaneously the driving knee comes up to about hip height or above. The free leg — the landing leg — is stretched out to reach the ground. It looks very different from the childhood version of skipping. It looks much more athletic, much more powerful. When you see elite sprinters doing maximum amplitude skipping you can see immediately why it transfers to sprinting mechanics.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a connection between the transition points in a skip and what happens at transition points in a sprint?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes absolutely. The transition point in skipping — the moment between the flight phase and the ground contact — is analogous to what happens in sprinting. The key is what we call the switch. The moment where the driving leg is at its highest point and the opposite leg is beginning to drive down to the ground. Getting that switch to be fast and crisp is one of the key technical qualities of both skipping and sprinting. And skipping is a great way to train that switch because the movement is slower and more controllable than full sprinting, so athletes can really focus on the quality of that transition.

## [00:53:03] Concentric & Eccentric Phases, Running

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned eccentrics earlier. Can you go deeper on the role of eccentric strength in running and why it's so important?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so this is something I feel very strongly about. There's a general bias in strength training toward concentric work — lifting, pushing, pressing. The muscles are shortening while generating force. And that's because concentric work is more intuitive and in many ways more comfortable. Eccentric work — where the muscle lengthens while generating force — is less comfortable. It creates more soreness. It requires greater control. But it's incredibly important for performance and for injury prevention. In running, every single time you land on one leg, you are doing an eccentric contraction in the hip extensors, the knee extensors, the ankle plantarflexors. The muscle has to absorb the impact. And if you don't have strength in that eccentric phase — if you can't control the landing — then one of two things happens. Either you collapse — you lose posture and form — or you compensate somewhere and create an injury. I actually did some work in the 1990s at a sports performance center in Calgary where we tested eccentric strength in athletes. And what we found was that the thing that most differentiated elite sprinters from sub-elite sprinters was not the concentric force they could produce but the eccentric force they could absorb. Elite sprinters can absorb more force per unit of body mass than sub-elites. That's the real differentiator.

## [00:55:32] Transitioning to Striding, Posture, Center of Mass

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the transition from running to striding to sprinting. What needs to change in terms of posture and mechanics?

**Stuart McMillan:** So as you move through the gait spectrum from jogging to running to striding to sprinting, the key variable that changes is the relationship between your center of mass and your base of support — your feet. At a walk and jog, your center of mass is relatively far back relative to where your foot contacts the ground. As you move faster, your center of mass moves forward. And at top speed — at a full sprint — your foot is contacting the ground almost directly underneath or even slightly behind your center of mass. That's what generates propulsion. If your foot is in front of your center of mass — which is what happens in overstriding — you're actually braking with every step. You're slowing yourself down. So one of the key technical focuses in improving speed is to reduce overstriding and to get foot contact closer to underneath the center of mass. And posture is critical for this. You have to be tall. You have to have your pelvis in a neutral or slightly anterior tilt position. You can't be running with a posterior pelvic tilt — butt tucked under — because that moves your center of mass backward and promotes overstriding.

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some practical cues for improving posture while running?

**Stuart McMillan:** I like a few things. First — imagine a string attached to the top of your head pulling you toward the ceiling. That cue promotes height, promotes a neutral head position, and promotes a tall spine. Second — think about pushing the ground away from you, rather than pulling yourself forward. The feeling should be that you're applying force into the ground, not that you're reaching forward with your legs. Third — and this is something I say a lot to athletes — run with your hips high. Think about getting your hips up. Because when the hips sag — which happens when people get tired — the whole system collapses.

## [00:03:11] Older Adults, Eccentric Control, Tool: Skipping

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about older adults specifically. You've mentioned that skipping is particularly important for aging. Can you elaborate on that?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. So the thing that most threatens older adults in terms of physical function and independence is the ability to decelerate — to slow down, to stop, to catch themselves when they stumble. That's an eccentric capacity. Falls are the number one cause of injury-related death in older adults. And falls happen not because people can't generate force — they can generally generate enough force to stand up and walk — but because they can't absorb force quickly enough when they lose balance. They can't eccentrically control the descent quickly enough to prevent a fall. And skipping — particularly landing on one leg repeatedly and absorbing that impact — is one of the best ways to train that eccentric control. I actually have my father doing this. He's in his late 70s. He skips. He does a gentle form of skipping — not the maximal amplitude version — and it has transformed his movement quality. He's more confident moving. His balance has improved. His gait has improved. And his confidence has improved. Because when you know that you can handle the impact of a single-leg landing, you trust your body more. And that trust changes how you move through the world.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's remarkable. How long did it take for him to see those improvements?

**Stuart McMillan:** A few months. And it wasn't daily, high-intensity skipping. It was a few sessions a week of very gentle skipping — just enough to load the system, just enough to challenge the balance and the eccentric control. And the benefits accumulated over time.

## [00:08:00] Naming Importance & Public Health; Skipping, Plyometrics

**Andrew Huberman:** You've made the point that naming matters — that calling it "plyrics" or "plyometrics" rather than "skipping" changes how people receive it. Can you say more about that?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah. Names carry enormous cultural weight. When I say "skipping" to a 45-year-old man who's been lifting weights for 20 years, he's thinking of his little sister skipping rope in the driveway. He's not taking it seriously. But if I say "single-leg plyometrics" or "bounding" or "plyrics" — the same movement suddenly becomes something worth paying attention to. And this is not just a marketing issue. This is a genuine public health barrier. There is a huge population of people who would benefit enormously from skipping but who would never do it because they think it's beneath them. Because of the name. And if we could rebrand this movement — give it a name that signals its real value — I think we'd see a lot more people doing it and a lot of improvement in population health as a result.

**Andrew Huberman:** This is true in science too. The name really does matter. The way something is labeled shapes how people think about it.

**Stuart McMillan:** Completely. And the other piece is the context in which we present it. If you put it in a warm-up as a throwaway — "oh we'll do some skipping before we get to the real work" — people won't take it seriously. But if you structure it as a primary training tool and give it appropriate time and attention in the program, people start to appreciate what it actually does.

## [00:14:06] Cross-Body Coordination, Rotation, Gaits; Phones & Posture

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about cross-body coordination. You've emphasized this as fundamental to human movement. Can you explain what it is and why it matters?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so cross-body coordination is the pattern where opposite limbs work together. The right arm and left leg move together, and the left arm and right leg move together. This is the fundamental pattern of all bipedal locomotion. When you walk, you naturally swing your right arm when your left leg steps forward. When you run, that cross-body pattern becomes even more pronounced. And when you sprint, the arms are driving hard and the coordination of arms and legs is critical for generating speed. The reason this pattern evolved is because it allows you to counterbalance the rotational forces generated by the legs. When you step forward with your left leg, your pelvis rotates to the left. The right arm swinging forward counteracts that rotation and keeps the body balanced and efficient. If you suppress arm swing — if you run with your arms held still or crossed in front of your body — you actually have to work much harder to stabilize the pelvis. And you lose energy in the process.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the idea of training anti-rotation? A lot of core training is focused on resisting rotation.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah and I have some issues with the anti-rotation focus. I think it has a place. There are certainly movements and sports where you want the ability to resist rotation. But I think in the context of human locomotion — walking, running, sprinting — rotation is not the enemy. Rotation is what makes locomotion efficient. We're a rotational species. Our pelvis rotates, our thorax rotates, our shoulders rotate. That rotation is what allows energy to flow through the body from the ground up. So training exclusively to resist rotation can actually be counterproductive for running mechanics if it teaches you to be too stiff in the trunk.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about phones? You've mentioned the phone posture issue.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. So this is something I feel strongly about as well. When you're looking at your phone with your head down and your chin on your chest, you're training that posture. You're spending hours every day with your head in that forward flexed position. And that posture is the opposite of what you want for good running mechanics. Running requires a tall, neutral spine, head in line with the torso, eyes forward or slightly down the track. The phone posture is the exact opposite — head forward, spine flexed, shoulders rounded. And the brain's postural control system is highly adaptive. Whatever position you spend the most time in becomes your default. So if you spend hours every day with your head down looking at your phone, that becomes your default running posture as well.

## [00:22:27] Expression Through Movement, Playfulness, Confidence

**Andrew Huberman:** You've spoken about movement as expression. Can you elaborate on that concept? Because I think most people think of movement as purely mechanical — you're either fast or slow, strong or weak.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so this is something that I think is deeply important and deeply underappreciated. Every person moves in a way that is unique to them. And that uniqueness reflects who they are. It reflects their personality, their history, their emotional state, their relationship to their body. When I look at an athlete run, I see who that person is. I really do. And it's not just mechanical efficiency I'm looking at. I'm looking at the expressive quality of the movement. Is this person open or closed? Are they expansive or contracted? Are they confident or fearful? Are they joyful or dutiful? And all of those qualities show up in how people move. Usain Bolt is the perfect example. Bolt doesn't just run fast. Bolt expresses joy and confidence and playfulness through his running. You feel it when you watch him. That openness, that expansion — that's not separate from his speed. It's part of why he's as fast as he is. A contracted, fearful movement pattern generates less force. An open, expansive, confident movement pattern generates more force. The mechanics and the expression are not separate things.

**Andrew Huberman:** And how do you develop that expressive quality in athletes who are more closed or more contracted?

**Stuart McMillan:** It's interesting because you often can't develop it directly. If I say to an athlete "be more expressive" they don't know what to do with that. So I use a lot of indirect tools. One of the things I do is playfulness. I build in play to the training session. Because when people play, they drop their self-consciousness. They drop the performance pressure. And when they do that, the movement opens up. You'll see something in their movement that you never see when they're performing — some quality that is authentically theirs. And my job is to identify that quality and build on it.

## [00:28:53] Being Yourself, Expression, Essence & Movement

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to stay on this idea of self-expression through movement because it connects to something deeper. It's not just about running mechanics.

**Stuart McMillan:** Right. And I think this is what distinguishes great coaches from good coaches. Good coaches can teach mechanics. Great coaches can teach the person to be themselves within those mechanics. Because mechanics without expression is mechanical. It's robotic. It's what you see when you try to impose a template on an athlete — "do this, do that, move this way" — and what you get is an athlete who looks coached. Who looks like they're doing an impression of somebody else. Great athletes have a signature quality to their movement. Bolt looks like Bolt. Maurice Greene looked like Maurice Greene. Michael Johnson looked like Michael Johnson. And none of them look like each other. They all achieved extraordinary results through very different movement expressions. And the coach's job is to help each athlete find their own movement signature and then develop it. Not to erase it and replace it with someone else's.

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you find that signature in a new athlete?

**Stuart McMillan:** I watch them. I watch them a lot in unstructured settings. Not when they're performing for me, not when they know they're being observed, but when they're just moving. How do they walk across the parking lot? How do they move when they're warming up on their own? How do they move when they're laughing and joking with their teammates? Because in those unstructured moments the authentic movement comes out. And then when we start formal sessions I try to create environments where that authentic movement is more likely to emerge. Less structure, more play, more variation, more surprise. Because the nervous system responds to surprise and novelty in a way that can unlock movement patterns that rigidly structured practice suppresses.

## [00:36:39] Connecting with Movement, Building Cues, Mood Words

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you give me some examples of the cues you use? Not mechanical cues like "bring your knee up" but the kind of expressive cues you're talking about?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so I use what I call mood words or feeling words. Rather than "drive your arm" I might say "be powerful." Rather than "extend your hip" I might say "be explosive." Or I might use color. I might say "run as if you're running in gold." And what happens is the athlete's nervous system interprets those words and creates a movement response that is authentic to them. Because "powerful" means something specific to each person. "Gold" means something specific. And the movement that comes out in response to those words is theirs. It's not mine. I haven't told them to do anything specific — I've given them a feeling to aim for and they figure out how to get there. This is why some of the best sprinters in history worked with coaches who gave almost no technical instruction. Charlie Francis, who coached Ben Johnson, was not known for giving a lot of technical cues. He created environments and conditions and then let the athletes figure out how to be fast.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a very different model from what most people think of as coaching.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. And it's more art than science in some ways. But it's grounded in a deep understanding of the science. You have to know the mechanics. You have to know what efficiency looks like. But then you have to be able to translate that knowledge into something that the athlete can use. And often the most efficient translation is not a mechanical cue but an experiential one.

## [00:45:05] Pressure & Peace; Exercise, Movement & Age

**Andrew Huberman:** You have a concept that keeps coming up in your writing and I've heard you mention it before — the concept of pressure and peace. Can you explain that?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah. So in sprinting — and this came out of a conversation with one of my athletes — the best performances happen when an athlete can simultaneously hold two qualities: pressure and peace. Pressure in the sense that they're applying maximum effort, maximum force, maximum intent. And peace in the sense that they're not fighting themselves. They're not tense. They're not anxious. They're not overly controlling. They're in a state of effortless effort — which sounds paradoxical but is a real state that athletes achieve when they're performing at their best. And what I've come to believe is that this balance of pressure and peace is not just a performance concept. It's a life concept. The people who are most effective — not just in sport but in any domain — can hold both simultaneously. They can be fully committed, fully engaged, under pressure, and simultaneously at peace with what they're doing.

**Andrew Huberman:** And how does age interact with this?

**Stuart McMillan:** I think as we age the relationship to pressure changes. Young athletes often have too much pressure and not enough peace. They're trying too hard. They're anxious. They're performing for approval. As athletes mature — and I see this in the athletes I've worked with for many years — the pressure becomes more self-directed and the peace becomes more accessible. They've been through enough that they know they can handle whatever comes. And that knowing creates peace. And the best older athletes I know — the ones who are still performing or training at a high level in their 40s and 50s and beyond — they've found that balance. They're not trying to impress anyone. They're not performing for approval. They're moving for the love of movement. And that love of movement is the thing that keeps them going.

## [00:51:39] Music, Art, Rhythm, Coaching; Soccer, Greatest Players & Countries

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked about rhythm as fundamental to movement. And I know you have an interest in music. How do those connect?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah music and movement are deeply connected and I think most people feel this intuitively — when you put on music and it has a great beat, you want to move. Your body responds to rhythm. And I think that's because rhythm is fundamental to all movement. All locomotion is rhythmic. Walking has a rhythm. Running has a rhythm. Sprinting has a rhythm. And when your movement is rhythmically organized — when all the parts are moving in coordinated timing — you're efficient and you're fast. When your movement is arrhythmic — when the parts are out of sync — you lose energy and you slow down. So I think of coaching as partly a process of helping athletes find the right rhythm. And music can be a tool for that. Not just as motivation but as an actual technical tool. You can use music to teach rhythm. You can use the beat to cue foot strikes. You can use it to help athletes find the flow state that corresponds to their best performances.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's your view on soccer players as sprinters? Because I know you've done a lot of work across different sports.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so soccer players are interesting because they have to sprint, but the demands of soccer sprinting are very different from track sprinting. Soccer sprinters start and stop. They sprint at angles. They sprint with a ball at their feet. So the pure sprinting mechanics are altered by the sport-specific demands. But there are players — and Brazil and some other countries have produced them — who have this extraordinary quality of movement that transcends the sport-specific demands. Players like Pelé, Ronaldo — the original Ronaldo — Messi in his own way. These players move with a quality that is impossible to teach. It's the same quality you see in Bolt or in the greatest track sprinters. There's an efficiency and a beauty and an expressiveness that goes beyond technical training.

## [00:00:25] White & Black Athletes, Genetics, Environment

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to ask about something that's often talked about but not always very well — the question of genetics and race in sprinting. Why do athletes of West African descent tend to dominate the short sprints? Is it genetics? Is it environment? Is it culture?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah so this is a conversation that makes a lot of people uncomfortable but I think it's worth having clearly and honestly. The evidence strongly suggests that there are genetic differences between populations that have implications for athletic performance. That doesn't mean that every individual from a particular population will be fast or that every individual from another population will be slow. But at the population level there are real differences. West African populations tend to have a higher proportion of fast-twitch muscle fibers. They tend to have body proportions — longer limbs relative to torso — that are biomechanically advantageous for sprinting. These are real biological differences. But environment is equally important. The reason Jamaican sprinters are so dominant right now has a lot to do with the culture of sprinting in Jamaica. The national championships in Jamaica draw crowds that rival the Olympics. Being a great sprinter in Jamaica is the highest-status thing a young person can aspire to. That cultural investment — the coaching infrastructure, the competitive structure, the national attention — produces elite sprinters in a way that the genetics alone could not produce. So it's both. It's genetics and environment interacting. And separating them is not only scientifically difficult but practically less useful than asking: given the genetics you have, what environment can best develop what you have?

## [00:08:27] Running Form, Tools: High Knees, Stiff Springs, Hip Extension

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to drill down into some specific running form cues and drills. Let's start with high knees. What's the purpose of high knee drills and how do they connect to actual sprinting?

**Stuart McMillan:** So high knees — and I should say that we do use high knees but with some caveats — are primarily a drill for teaching athletes to lift the driving knee. In sprinting, the recovery leg — the leg that's coming forward after push-off — needs to have a high knee to shorten the swing radius, which makes the recovery faster. And a faster recovery means you can get the foot back to the ground sooner, which means you can apply force again sooner. So the high knee is important. But the issue with high knee drills as they're often taught is that they focus too much on the concentric — the lifting — and not enough on the ground contact. The elite sprinter isn't lifting their knee so much as they're putting their foot into the ground and the knee lift is a consequence of that ground action. It's a bottom-up movement, not a top-down movement. So the cue I prefer is not "lift your knee" but "push the ground away." Push the ground away hard and the knee will come up as a consequence.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the stiff springs concept?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. So at top speed sprinting — when you're fully accelerated — the leg acts as a stiff spring. When the foot contacts the ground, you want very little collapse at the ankle and knee. You want the leg to be stiff so that the elastic energy stored in the tendon — the Achilles, the calf complex — can be released quickly to propel you forward. This is what we call stiffness. And it's actually one of the qualities that distinguishes elite sprinters — they have high leg stiffness at top speed. The leg doesn't collapse. It's springy. And stiffness is developed through plyometric training — jumping, hopping, bounding, skipping. The tendons adapt to become better at storing and releasing elastic energy.

**Andrew Huberman:** And hip extension — you've mentioned this several times. What's its role?

**Stuart McMillan:** Hip extension is the primary power generator in sprinting. When you push off the ground, what's happening is the hip extensor muscles — the glutes primarily — are contracting hard and extending the hip. And the range of that hip extension — how far behind the body the leg gets — is a key determinant of stride length and power. Many recreational runners have limited hip extension. Their hip never gets behind their center of mass properly. And this is partly a flexibility issue — hip flexors that are tight from sitting all day limit hip extension. But it's also a strength issue — the glutes aren't strong enough to push the leg back fully. And this is where the weight room can really help. Exercises that build hip extension strength in functional positions — staggered stance hip hinges, single-leg deadlifts, split squats with good range of motion — can make a real difference to running mechanics.

## [00:17:21] Skipping Rope, Aging; Protocols & Rigidity, Principles Alignment

**Andrew Huberman:** What about skipping rope as opposed to regular skipping? Is it different enough to be considered a separate tool?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes, quite different. Rope jumping is a more constrained movement. You're jumping mostly vertically in place. And it's a great tool for certain things — cardiovascular conditioning, coordination, rhythm, lower leg conditioning. It's a legitimate tool and boxers have used it for a very long time for good reasons. But it doesn't give you the locomotor pattern, the cross-body coordination, the horizontal force production that you get from skipping without a rope. So I see them as complementary tools, not substitutes. Both have value. But for the specific benefits I'm describing — the locomotor pattern, the hip extension, the cross-body coordination — you need the free-form skip without the rope.

**Andrew Huberman:** You seem to be someone who values principles over rigid protocols. Can you say more about that?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yeah. This is something I feel strongly about. We live in an era of protocol worship. People want to know exactly what exercises to do, in what order, with what weight, for how many sets and reps, on which days. And I understand the appeal of that. It removes uncertainty. It gives people a clear path to follow. But the problem with rigid protocol adherence is that it ignores individuality. Every person is different. Every day is different. The right training for me on this particular day depends on how I slept, what I ate, what's happening in my life emotionally and psychologically, what my body feels like, what my athletic history is. A rigid protocol cannot account for all of that. So I prefer to teach principles. And the principles are: apply forces to the ground, do it quickly, do it in the right direction, manage your body mass, recover fully between efforts, and progress gradually over time. Within those principles there's enormous room for individual variation.

## [00:22:12] Resistance Training to Improve Movement, Sprinting Kinetics, Individualization

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about weight training for improving sprinting and general movement. You mentioned the four factors of sprinting kinetics — force magnitude, rate of force development, direction of force, and body mass. How does weight training address those?

**Stuart McMillan:** So the way I think about it — for most recreational athletes who are not yet strong, increasing maximum strength will help. There's a threshold of strength below which you're not generating enough force to be fast. And getting stronger will move you toward and past that threshold. For elite athletes who have already achieved a high level of strength, the marginal return on more strength is very low. At that point what matters more is force transmission — how quickly and efficiently you can apply the force you have. And that's where plyometrics, skipping, bounding, and specific isometric training become more important than adding more weight to the bar. The specific isometric work I'm referring to — and this comes from the research of Alex Terca — is isometric contractions in the exact position of ground contact. So: foot flat on the ground, 15-degree knee bend, 5-10 degree hip bend, foot directly underneath the center of mass. You push as hard as you can against an immovable bar or hold a very heavy load in that position. Three to five seconds per contraction, three to four repetitions, three sets. This builds strength in the specific position where you need it most in sprinting.

**Andrew Huberman:** And you said you do almost everything in a staggered stance in the weight room?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. Everything except occasionally trap bar deadlifts for neural drive. Every single lift — overhead press, row, curl, extension — everything is done with one foot in front of the other. Because that's the position of all locomotion. That's where the transfer to movement happens. And within the staggered stance I vary which foot is forward, so I'm developing symmetrical capacity on both sides. Andrew also mentioned the yoga lunge position — and this is actually one of my favorites as a stretching and movement preparation tool. Front foot flat, rear foot up on the big toe, rear knee behind the butt, opposite arm raised with palm facing the ceiling. This stretches the entire fascial sling that runs from the big toe of the rear foot across the pelvis and up through the body to the opposite fingertips. And that fascial sling is exactly the kinetic chain that's active during single-leg push-off in sprinting. So it's one of the most functional stretches you can do.

**Andrew Huberman:** The cross-body nature of that is what makes it functional.

**Stuart McMillan:** Exactly. Left foot planted, right arm extended — you're loading the contralateral fascial chain. And that chain is the one that does the work in every single running stride.

## [00:32:29] Transferring Weight Room to Track, Staggered Stance, Stretching

**Andrew Huberman:** I've been doing staggered stance lifts for years actually. I vary the stance within sets. Does that matter?

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes, that's great. Varying which foot is forward within a set or between sets ensures you're developing bilateral symmetry in the staggered position. And it also adds a small element of proprioceptive challenge because your body has to recalibrate when you switch. I think that's actually an underrated benefit. Your nervous system gets practice adapting to slight variations in base of support, which transfers to better balance and stability in all dynamic movements.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the big toe specifically? You mentioned it in the context of the rear foot in that lunge.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes. The first ray — the big toe — is critical to efficient locomotion. Push-off happens through the big toe. When you watch an elite sprinter in slow motion, you can see them rolling over the big toe at the end of each ground contact. The metatarsophalangeal joint of the big toe extends fully, loading the plantar fascia like a bowstring, and that elastic energy is released to propel the body forward. This is called the windlass mechanism and it's a huge source of free energy in running. But if the big toe is stiff — if you can't extend it fully — you lose that mechanism. So anytime I have the opportunity to train with the rear foot elevated on the big toe, I take it. Because it loads that joint through its full range of motion. And that improves its function over time.

## [00:36:52] Performance-Enhancement, Elite Athletes, Androgen, Reputation

**Andrew Huberman:** There's been a new discussion about performance-enhancing drugs with something called the Enhanced Games being proposed — a kind of PED-allowed sports league. Regardless of what happens with that, I want to ask you directly: how common is androgen use — testosterone derivatives — in elite track and field now compared to, say, the 1980s?

**Stuart McMillan:** I'll be direct. In my experience in this sport right now — I don't see it. I genuinely don't know of an elite sprinter that I could point to and say with confidence that person is dirty. And that's very different from the situation in the 1960s, 70s, and 80s when it was rampant. The 80s in particular — Ben Johnson, Florence Griffith Joyner, and many others — the drug use was widespread and barely concealed. In the 90s when testing started becoming more sophisticated it got much less common. And now the testing is very rigorous. Athletes are tested out of competition, not just at meets. They have to declare their whereabouts at all times. It's very difficult to cheat. So my honest assessment is that elite sprinting is largely clean now. There are pockets — Russia with the state-sponsored doping program that's now well documented, some issues in Kenyan distance running — but I think the top sprinters in the world right now are competing clean. And actually one of the strongest pieces of evidence for that is how young the fast times are appearing. Trayvon Bromell ran 9.97 as a high school junior. He was 5'7", 135 pounds. That kid was not taking drugs. Bolt ran sub-10 when he was 19 years old, before he had any of the professional support structure that would make doping possible.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the reputational damage you mentioned — that's a real concern for you.

**Stuart McMillan:** Absolutely. I coached one athlete in my career who tested positive — a bobsled athlete named Pav Jovanovic, who was at the time one of the best bobsled drivers in the world. He tested positive for nandrolone in 2001. And it was later shown that the positive came from a contaminated supplement. Remember at that time you could buy things at GNC that turned out to be contaminated with nandrolone because some supplement companies were literally lacing their products with steroids to improve results. And here's an athlete who was not intentionally cheating, who tested positive, who was banned from his sport, whose career was ruined, whose reputation was destroyed. And he subsequently took his own life. That's the human cost of this. And the other side of the cost is the innocent athletes who are tarred by the assumption that everyone in the sport is dirty. They are not. The vast majority of athletes in track and field are working incredibly hard, making very little money, doing it cleanly. And they deserve better.

## [00:46:45] Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT), Age; Pharmacology vs. Training

**Andrew Huberman:** You've brought up TRT — I've been open that I use a micro-dose of testosterone, 25 milligrams every other day, since I was 45, combined with 600 IU of HCG every other day to maintain fertility. My testosterone was in the mid-700s before, but I was experiencing a lot of fatigue. And for me it's helped with recovery substantially. What's your take on TRT more broadly — and the distinction between TRT for health reasons versus supraphysiological use?

**Stuart McMillan:** I think the distinction is really important. TRT in the sense of maintaining physiologically normal testosterone levels in someone whose natural production has declined — I have no issue with that. That's health maintenance. It's not performance enhancement in a meaningful sense. The problem is people in their teens and 20s taking testosterone when they don't need it — when their natural production is perfectly fine — because they want to look a certain way or perform a certain way. And the downstream costs of that can be significant. It shuts down endogenous testosterone production. It can suppress sperm production, sometimes permanently. And for young men who think they don't want children now but might want them later — that's a real risk. The Instagram-driven culture of body image has created a huge market for testosterone misuse in young men that concerns me. For the competitive athlete in a tested sport, of course any exogenous testosterone is a violation and the testing will catch it. But for the general population adult who's aging and experiencing the natural decline in testosterone — I think it's a legitimate tool used responsibly under medical supervision.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the broader question of pharmacology versus training? There's a perception that drugs provide a massive shortcut. Does the evidence support that?

**Stuart McMillan:** I think people overestimate how much performance-enhancing drugs contribute to elite performance. The margins in elite sport are incredibly small. To go from a 10.2 to a 9.9 in the 100 meters is not a drug achievement. That's thousands and thousands of hours of work. Eating right. Sleeping right. Having a good life. Drugs don't give you that. What drugs do — and this was true even in the drug era — is they let you recover faster. They let you train harder and more frequently. But you still have to do the training. You got fast first, and then drugs let you get a little faster. You don't take drugs to go from slow to fast. Andre DeGrasse barely squatted his bodyweight when I started coaching him and won three Olympic medals eighteen months later. Without drugs. That tells you something very important about what's actually necessary to perform at the highest level.

## [00:52:14] Single Physical Metric & Sprinting; Pressure & Peace

**Andrew Huberman:** You asked me earlier in our conversation what I think is the single best physical metric of overall health and longevity. And I want to come back to that now because I have a different answer than I would have had before this conversation. What's your answer?

**Stuart McMillan:** I asked you that earlier and I'll give you my honest answer. I think the ability to maximally sprint — not to be a great sprinter, but to have the ability to go out and sprint flat-out without injuring yourself or blowing a gasket — is the single best proxy for overall physical health and longevity. Think about everything that has to be in place for that to be possible. You need joint health — healthy ankles, knees, hips. You need muscular strength and power. You need neuromuscular coordination. You need cardiovascular fitness. You need eccentric control. You need adequate mobility. You need a functional gait pattern. If you can sprint — not necessarily fast, just sprint at maximal effort for you — all of those things are working. None of them can be absent. And VO2 max, grip strength, walking speed — these are all reasonable proxies. But I would argue that the ability to sprint subsumes all of them. Can you run fast for you? That's the question.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think that's right. And it connects to what you said about pressure and peace — applying maximum effort without fighting yourself. That's the standard for a great sprint and maybe the standard for a great life.

**Stuart McMillan:** Yes exactly. And it applies to everything we do. The ability to generate pressure — real effort, full commitment — and simultaneously to be at peace with what you're doing, not anxious, not tense, not fighting. The first two-thirds of the day is pressure. You do the hard work. You train, you study, you build. And the last third is peace. You rest, you recover, you enjoy. And you can be at peace with the pressure because you know the peace is coming. And vice versa — you can be at peace knowing that the pressure of tomorrow's work is part of what creates the meaning. That balance is what I see in every great athlete I've ever coached. And it's what I aspire to in my own life.

**Andrew Huberman:** Stuart, this has been one of the most remarkable conversations I've had on this podcast. I've learned things today that I genuinely did not know before. And I think the listeners and viewers are going to find this both practical and philosophically rich in a way that I did not entirely anticipate. Thank you so much.

**Stuart McMillan:** Thanks Andrew. It's been a real pleasure. I appreciate you showing up to track meets and talking about the sport. It's important for what I fear is a dying sport. We need more people out there supporting track and field — the foundation of human movement.
