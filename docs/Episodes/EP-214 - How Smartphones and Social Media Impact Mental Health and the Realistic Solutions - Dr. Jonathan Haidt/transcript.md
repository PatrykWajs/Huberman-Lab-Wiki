## [00:16:26] Smartphone Usage, Play-Based to Phone-Based Childhood

**Jonathan Haidt:** What I found in writing "The Anxious Generation" is that there were actually two transitions happening simultaneously. The first is the phone-based childhood coming in — the loss of time for real-world socializing, for sleep, for free time. But the second is the loss of the play-based childhood. Those two are deeply connected. Because as we take away the real world, we push kids onto the phone. So it's not just that we gave them something bad; we took away something good.

**Andrew Huberman:** What defines a play-based childhood?

**Jonathan Haidt:** A play-based childhood is one in which kids from early childhood — age three or four — through at least age twelve, or ideally through adolescence, have a lot of time to play, especially with other kids, and especially outdoors, without direct adult supervision. That's the key: without adults managing everything. Kids need to be out exploring, fighting, making up games, resolving conflicts. Because all of those involve the exquisite development of social skills.

## [00:20:40] The Tragedy of Losing Play-Based Childhood

**Jonathan Haidt:** There's a three-act tragedy here. Act one is the loss of community, the loss of trust in each other. If we go back to the 1950s and 60s — my parents grew up in New York City in the 30s and 40s — people spontaneously organized themselves into villages. Village life seems to be sort of the default way of living that humans have preferred for thousands of years. The kids run around, all the adults take part in supervising all the kids, but nobody has to helicopter because the kids are playing; they're doing their thing. Kids need to be out playing with each other, especially outdoors. We evolved in savannas; we're attracted to nature. Act two of the tragedy is the loss of the play-based childhood, which happens mainly in the 1990s. In America and Britain we freaked out about child abduction and child sexual abuse. There were real scandals — the Boy Scouts, the Catholic Church. And I recall growing up thinking you could get kidnapped. But when I was doing research for "The Coddling of the American Mind" I found, according to FBI statistics, there are only about 100 to 150 true kidnappings a year in our whole country. And yet overprotecting our kids, keeping them inside, has spiked the suicide rate so much that the death toll is vastly higher from the extra suicides than it would be if we could completely wipe out kidnapping. In the 1970s there was a real crime wave that went through the 1980s; crime was actually high. Even still you were kicked out of the house to go play. But we began to lose trust in each other for a lot of reasons. Robert Putnam wrote about this in "Bowling Alone" — the loss of social capital. Many reasons for that: the changing media environment, air conditioning and television pulled people off their porches, family sizes are shrinking. By the 1990s we stop letting our kids out. We think they must always be supervised. And as that act is happening — we're keeping kids inside — guess what, these computer things that we started getting in the 1980s are getting interesting. Act three is the Great Rewiring — the arrival of the phone-based childhood between 2010 and 2015.

## [00:30:02] Girls vs. Boys, Interests & Trapping Kids

**Jonathan Haidt:** Boys are more interested in things, in mechanics, in systems. Girls have a more evolved and elaborate mental map of social space — they're more interested in social relationships. The earlier internet was much more appealing to boys; boys would take computers apart, build computers, learn to program. The trapping dynamic is crucial. Facebook and then Instagram discovered that what keeps people on the platform is social reciprocity and social threat. ## [00:37:31] "Effectance," Systems & Relationships, Animals

**Jonathan Haidt:** There's a concept from developmental psychology called effectance — it's basically the pleasure of having an effect on the world, of making things happen. Babies experience this when they discover they can move their hands and make a mobile spin. It's intrinsically motivating. The reason video games are so compelling for boys is that they are massive effectance machines. You have complete agency, you level up, you're competent, the world responds to you. Now compare that to school. In school, you largely sit and follow instructions. The environment has a great deal of control over you. It's the opposite of effectance. So boys are checking out of school and going to video games because video games give them the sense of effectance that their brains are hungry for. The tragedy is that they're getting effectance in the virtual world while their real-world competence, real-world social skills, and real-world agency are not developing.

## [00:55:24] Play & Low-Stakes Mistakes, Video Games & Social Media, Conflict Resolution

**Jonathan Haidt:** Play is how children develop the skills they need for adult life — and the key feature of play is that it involves low-stakes mistakes. When you're playing tag and you do something wrong, you get tagged, and then you play again. The consequence is minor and immediate. You learn from it and move on. Social media and video games also involve mistakes, but the consequences are very different. A video game mistake means you restart a level. A social media mistake — posting something that gets you mocked by a thousand people — can be catastrophic and lasting. Real play, especially rough-and-tumble play for boys and social drama play for girls, requires reading social signals in real time, adjusting behavior, negotiating, managing conflict. None of that is learned through a screen. And conflict resolution in particular is completely different. In real life, if you have a conflict with someone, you have to work it out eventually because you're going to see that person again. On social media, you can just block them, or worse, pile on with others. The art of reconciliation, of repair, is not being learned.

## [01:01:23] Social Media, Trolls, Performance

**Jonathan Haidt:** Social media created an entirely new social universe in which performance metrics — likes, followers, shares — became the currency of social status. This is toxic for adolescents for at least three reasons. First, it makes social life performative. Instead of just being yourself with friends, you're managing a brand. Second, it exposes you to massive audiences, including anonymous trolls. In real life, if someone says something mean to you, usually only a few people hear it. On social media, thousands can pile on. Third, the algorithms amplify the most extreme, most outrage-inducing content, which means the most extreme trolls get the most engagement. So teenagers are not just experiencing normal social conflict; they're experiencing an environment engineered to maximize social threat and social reward at the same time.

## [01:06:47] Dynamic Subordination, Hierarchy, Boys

**Jonathan Haidt:** There's a concept from primatology called dynamic subordination — the idea that in a healthy social hierarchy, individuals move up and down based on their behaviors and contributions. A young male chimpanzee starts near the bottom, but if he's got talent and makes the right alliances, he works his way up. This is healthy. This is what boys need. They need to be in real hierarchies where they can demonstrate competence and earn status through real deeds. Online, the hierarchy is determined by follower counts and viral moments. There's no real-world competence involved. A boy can be at the bottom of every real-world hierarchy — academically, athletically, socially — but can accumulate followers by being outrageous or offensive online. That's a very dysfunctional substitute for real status hierarchies.

## [01:10:15] Girls & Perfectionism, Social Media & Performance

**Jonathan Haidt:** For girls the dominant mechanism of harm is different. Girls' social lives are organized around relationships, around being accepted, around fitting in. Girls are much more attuned to social signals — what does she think of me, am I included, am I excluded. Social media takes all of those already-powerful drives and puts them into a high-speed, high-stakes, always-on environment. Now add visual social comparison — Instagram in particular — where girls are constantly comparing their appearance, their lives, their bodies, to idealized images of other girls, including influencers and celebrities. The research is clear that this drives body image problems, eating disorders, and a kind of perfectionism that is deeply maladaptive. The British have a saying: "Don't put your daughter on the stage, Mrs. Worthington" — which acknowledges that for girls to grow up being looked at and commented on about their looks is really bad for them. Social media puts every girl on stage, all the time.

## [01:21:15] Puberty & Sensitive Periods, Culture & Identity

**Jonathan Haidt:** Puberty is also a sensitive period for culture and identity. There's a concept in anthropology and developmental psychology called the cultural brain hypothesis — the idea that humans have evolved to be very sensitive to culture, especially during childhood and adolescence. We're born radically unfinished, which is costly — we need years of parental care — but it means we can be wired up for any culture on Earth. This is our great advantage as a species. But the flip side is that the culture we're immersed in during development shapes us profoundly. If adolescents are spending most of their waking hours immersed in the culture of TikTok and Instagram — a culture created by algorithms optimizing for engagement, not for human flourishing — then that's the culture their brains are wiring up for.

## [01:23:55] Brain Development & Puberty; Identity; Social Media, Learning & Reward

**Jonathan Haidt:** One of the things I find most alarming is what's happening with identity development. In the 1960s, the developmental psychologist Erik Erikson said that the main task of adolescence is identity formation — answering the question: who am I? This requires experimentation, exploration, some risk-taking, some rebellion, some failure. In a healthy adolescence, kids try out different identities, different social groups, different behaviors. They find what fits. Social media has radically disrupted this. Now instead of private exploration, every identity move is public and permanent. Kids are terrified to experiment because experimentation might get them mocked or cancelled. So they lock into identities — often extreme ones — very early. The algorithms also nudge them toward more extreme content, because extreme content is more engaging. So a kid who is feeling a little sad might go looking for relatable content on Instagram and end up in an eating disorder community or a self-harm community, because that's the direction the algorithm pushes.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the social media platform is like the casino analogy — a slot machine — because you never know when you're going to get the reinforcement and that drives even more engagement. But here we're talking about the content too, which is that children through puberty who are spending enormous amounts of time on these platforms — they are learning but what they're learning are these rapidly reinforced dopamine loops that lead to diminished amounts of dopamine not just in short periods of time but very quickly, and that inhibit other forms of learning.

**Jonathan Haidt:** That's right. The ability to pay attention to a teacher which is not as stimulating as what you're seeing on TikTok — there's tons of learning, but it's learning of all the wrong things. Junk food is actually a very good analogy here. If you raise your kids with junk food you're going to have all kinds of metabolic problems, developmental problems, obesity, diabetes. But if you have a normal development and then as an adult you indulge in more junk food, it's not good for you but it's very different than being raised on it. The developing brain and developing body are much more sensitive. The phone-based childhood is essentially saying: hey kid, you want the end without the journey? You want the dopamine without the effort? Here's a superstimulus for every evolved motive you have. Take it. And that cuts off development.

## [01:33:37] Tool: 4 Recommendations for Smartphone Use in Kids

**Jonathan Haidt:** My four recommendations are four ways of breaking out of this trap. The first two are really about delaying and getting past this period of plasticity. Here are the four norms that I think can break us out of this trap.

Norm number one: no smartphone before high school. You can give your kid a flip phone — Millennials had flip phones, they came out fine. No smartphone of their own, no personal pocket internet access. You can have a computer in your living room or kitchen when you have young kids and they can do things on the computer, but you do not want to give your child the entire internet in his or her pocket. You do not want the entire world to be able to reach your child whenever they want to. It's just insane that we're giving children even before puberty a smartphone. Let them have a flip phone or a simple phone watch — something like that.

Norm number two: no social media until 16. I believe there is no way to make social media safe for children. If they're going to be entering a domain in which prestige is gained by having posts that get the most likes and followers, you're making them brand managers. You're making them performative. They're going to be exposed to horrible things. I now ask young people: is there something that you saw when you were young that you really regret seeing? And a lot of them have an answer. The last thing we should be doing is exposing our children in this sensitive period to socializing information from random weirdos on the internet who were selected by an algorithm for the extremity of their behavior and the degree to which that extremity earned them likes, which makes them extra prestigious. Laws that raise the age to 16 or 18 — I think that's what we need.

Norm number three: phone-free schools. If we expect kids to learn, and learning is brain change, you have to lock up the phone in the morning — either in a phone locker or a Yonder pouch. If the policy is "keep it in your pocket," that's not a policy — that's just a recipe for constant conflict with the kids because they can't help it. They have to text if anyone's texting.

Norm number four is the hardest: far more independence, free play, and responsibility in the real world. We have to not just roll back the phone-based childhood and make them sit and do nothing — we have to restore a really fun, adventurous childhood. You go out on your bicycle, you're hanging out with your friends, sometimes something happens and you have memories, exciting things and scary things happen. The virtual adventures are just not going to cut it. Our kids need adventure, they need independence, where they work out the conflicts themselves. If we do those four things I think we can restore childhood in the real world: no smartphone before high school, no social media till 16, phone-free schools, more independence and free play.

## [01:41:48] Changing Childhood Norms, Policies & Legislature

**Jonathan Haidt:** The good news is that there are policy levers. Eight states have now passed "free range" laws clarifying that it is not child neglect for a child to be outside unsupervised. Law could help to stop incentivizing helicopter parenting, provide more spaces that are safe for kids to play in, not car-zones. On the tech side, the KOSA Bill — the Kids Online Safety Act — would require platforms to take reasonable steps to prevent harm to minors. Laws that raise the minimum age for social media to 16 or 18. The book has a whole fourth part which is suggestions for governments, tech companies, schools, and parents.

## [01:49:13] Summer Camp, Team Sports, Religion, Music

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a protective effect of summer camp? Or even just after-school sport where both kids and parents agree — no phones on the field?

**Jonathan Haidt:** First, summer camps are magic. Never send your child to a summer camp that doesn't ban phones, because this is the best chance you have for a detox. I hear the story over and over: "My girl got an iPhone when she was 11, she suddenly became surly — no longer the sweet funny child she was. I sent her to summer camp the next summer and the girl who came back was my wonderful sweet child. Her personality was back. And then she got back on her phone and she became surly again." Summer camps are the most powerful technique known for a detox because the kid isn't being deprived — they are in a bunk with other kids with no phones, joking and talking and laughing and fighting and doing all those healthy things.

The other thing that the evidence shows is that team sports and religion — those two things are very, very protective. I would strongly urge people to encourage their kids to play team sports. My kids run track, which is great, but team sports force more cooperation. There's some evidence that team sports are even better for mental health than individual sports. You don't want super over-supervised, high-pressure sports leagues — that's better than nothing, but ideally the more they play games that are more intramural, where the kids are enforcing the rules themselves, the better.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about music? **Jonathan Haidt:** Playing in a band or in a choir I think definitely should be on the list. I was really interested in synchrony for a while. ## [01:54:36] Boredom, Addiction & Smartphones; Tool: "Awe Walks"

**Andrew Huberman:** Kids and probably adults nowadays are not familiar with what it is to be bored. There's always an input. There's this concept from the world of mindfulness — wordlessness — the importance of being in states where we're not reading, not thinking in complete sentences, not taking in sensory information. Under those conditions we're able to actually register how we feel about things, which is a prerequisite for agency.

**Jonathan Haidt:** That's exactly right. And for that I would commend you to the work of Dacher Keltner, who is at Berkeley. He's done fascinating work on awe. Awe is the emotion that you get when you encounter something vast that you can't fully take in — a mountain, an ocean, a piece of great art, music that brings you to tears. Awe is one of the most powerful of all the emotions and it has major health benefits. And what's wonderful about awe is that it requires you to be present, in your body, in a real environment. You can't really experience awe through a screen in the same way. And one of the great tools from Dacher Keltner's research is what he calls "awe walks" — just going out for a walk and intentionally looking for things that inspire awe. It doesn't have to be the Grand Canyon. It can be a tree, a sky, a face. And these awe walks have been shown to reduce anxiety, reduce rumination, increase feelings of connection and meaning.

## [02:03:14] Casino Analogy & Ceding Childhood; Social Media Content

**Jonathan Haidt:** The casino analogy is really apt. The slot machine is the perfect symbol of social media. Variable reward schedules are the most powerful behavioral conditioning mechanism known. Every time you pull the lever — every time you scroll — there might be something amazing or there might be nothing. That uncertainty is incredibly compelling. The companies know this. They designed these products to be maximally engaging, not to be maximally beneficial to users. And the result is that we have essentially handed our children to a casino. Now, Marshall McLuhan said "the medium is the message" — don't focus so much on the content of television, focus on the transformation of human life when the television becomes the family hearth. In the same way: yes, it would be nice if we could clean up the content on social media — less hardcore porn, less violent content. But if we could make Instagram just be happy girls living beautiful lives and our daughters were to watch eight hours a day of this, is that good for them? Hell no. So the content matters but it's not the core issue. The core issue is the medium itself and what it does to developing brains.

## [02:11:45] Regaining Childhood Independence; Tool: Family Groups & Phones

**Jonathan Haidt:** If you're listening to this podcast and you have kids that are in elementary or middle school, be sure to talk with the parents of your kids' friends. I think the way out isn't "hey, these people are exploiting you" — that might be helpful. I think the way to break out is: look, you can see what this is doing to you. You mostly agree that this is wasting your time, it's garbage. You want a way out but you just feel like you can't. There's such resignation. But look — the cool kids over there, they have flip phones, and they're out every day after school doing stuff, getting pizza, building a fort. So I think the way out is to give kids an exciting childhood. Kids are so lonely now. The way to do it is not: "We're going to take away all this stuff from you and now you have nothing to do." Make it more like: "I'm not trying to hurt you here. I want you to have fun the way I did. We all had human childhoods full of adventure. I want that for you."

## [02:16:09] Screens & Future Optimism, Collective Action, KOSA Bill
