# Episode 116: How to Stop Headaches Using Science-Based Approaches

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman provides a comprehensive breakdown of headache biology, types, and treatments — from prescription drugs to nutrition and behavioral interventions. The episode covers the four main tissue sources of headache pain (muscle tension, blood vessel/meningeal pressure, neural/trigeminal, and inflammatory/sinus), then maps each source to the major headache types: tension, migraine, cluster, hormonal, sinus, and TBI-related, and closes with a discussion of thunderclap headache caused by extreme cerebral vasoconstriction. The second half focuses entirely on evidence-based treatments including creatine, omega-3 fatty acids, peppermint oil, red light therapy, Botox, acupuncture, curcumin, caffeine timing, and herbal compounds — all grounded in peer-reviewed research with mechanisms explained.

## Key Takeaways

1. **There are four tissue sources of headache pain** — muscle tension (scalp, jaw, neck), blood flow and meningeal pressure, neural activation (especially the trigeminal nerve), and inflammation (sinus/immune). Identifying the source is the key to selecting the correct treatment. [00:07:47](transcript.md#000747-headache-sources-muscle-tension-blood-flow--meninges)

2. **Nociceptors are the critical cell type** — Nociceptors are neurons specialized only for signaling pain (unlike neurons that respond to touch, temperature, or pressure). Headache treatments largely work by modulating nociceptor activation thresholds. [00:23:21](transcript.md#002321-3-neuron-types-pain-tension-headaches)

3. **Tension headaches are muscular in origin** — They involve contraction of muscles on the skull, jaw, and neck, which compress nociceptors and trigger pain signals. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) are standard acute relief; Botox injections are the most effective long-term treatment. Tolerance to NSAIDs builds quickly and they carry liver and exercise-adaptation costs. [00:31:05](transcript.md#003105-tension-headaches)

4. **Migraines are vascular and neural in origin, affect women 3x more than men** — Migraines involve dilation of blood vessels and activation of the trigeminal nerve via CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide). Despite the sex disparity, the cause does not appear to be purely hormonal. Aura (visual/auditory/sensory premonition) and photophobia are hallmarks. [00:34:29](transcript.md#003429-migraine-headaches-aura-photophobia)

5. **Cluster headaches originate from the trigeminal nerve and are linked to herpes-1 virus** — They are unilateral (one-sided), feel like they come from behind the eye or nose, and can last 15 minutes to 3 hours. The herpes-1 virus (which causes cold sores) lives on the trigeminal nerve and can inflame it — explaining why cluster headaches track the same three nerve branches (ophthalmic, mandibular, nasal). They are among the most severe headache types known. [00:43:10](transcript.md#004310-cluster-headaches)

6. **Hormonal headaches are driven by estrogen and progesterone fluctuations** — Drops in estrogen and progesterone during specific phases of the menstrual cycle trigger headaches. Estrogen binds to blood vessel surfaces (not just altering gene expression) to regulate vascular tone; when it drops, vessels become more prone to pain-generating dilation. Omega-3 supplementation has been shown to reduce both frequency and severity of PMS-related headaches. [00:47:47](transcript.md#004747-hormone-based-headaches-menstrual-cycle--menopause)

7. **Creatine monohydrate significantly reduces TBI-related headaches and may help all headache types** — A pilot study found that high-dose creatine (0.4 g/kg body weight for 6 months) dramatically reduced headache frequency, dizziness, and fatigue after traumatic brain injury. Creatine buffers brain energy reserves (phosphocreatine/ATP), allowing neurons to recover faster from the calcium dysregulation and metabolic stress caused by head impacts. [01:02:08](transcript.md#010208-tool-headache-treatment-creatine-monohydrate--tbi)

8. **Increasing omega-3 (EPA/DHA) while reducing omega-6 (linoleic acid) reduces migraine and tension headache frequency** — Two RCTs showed that raising omega-3 intake to 1.5–2 g/day EPA+DHA while reducing omega-6 from seed oils produced significant reductions in headache frequency and intensity. Getting above 1 g EPA/day benefits tension, migraine, and hormone-related headaches — covering over 70% of all headache types. Omega-3s are also available in prescription form, signaling their clinical-grade potency. [01:13:55](transcript.md#011355-headache-treatment-omega-3--omega-6-fatty-acids-linolic-acid)

9. **Red light exposure can offset photophobia and may reduce migraine severity** — Aura involves a spreading depression of neuronal excitability originating in the visual cortex and sweeping forward. During or before a migraine, viewing red light (620–750 nm wavelength) activates a specific subset of retinal neurons in a way that reduces the pain-amplifying signals that blue/white light trigger. 10–15 minutes of red light exposure may reduce photophobia intensity. [01:31:24](transcript.md#013124-tool-aura-photophobia--offsetting-with-red-light)

10. **Peppermint oil applied topically is a validated tension headache treatment — and also effective for migraines** — A double-blind RCT found that 10% peppermint oil in ethanol solution applied to the forehead and temples reduced tension headache pain equivalently to 1000 mg acetaminophen. The active compound menthol acts on TRPM8 cold-sensing receptors and reduces substance P. A 2020 systematic review of herbal treatments confirmed peppermint/menthol is also effective for migraine headaches. [01:49:43](transcript.md#014943-tool-alternative-headache-treatments-peppermint-oil-menthol)

11. **Caffeine timing relative to headaches matters — it can treat or cause headaches** — Caffeine is both a vasoconstrictor and a vasodilator depending on context. It can reduce migraine intensity when taken at onset. However, regular caffeine drinkers who skip their daily dose experience caffeine-withdrawal headaches due to vasodilation. Strategic caffeine use or gradual tapering can prevent rebound headaches. CGRP activation is one of the key pathways caffeine interacts with. [02:05:41](transcript.md#020541-tool-migraine--herbal-treatment-caffeine-timing)

12. **Curcumin (turmeric) has potent anti-inflammatory effects that may reduce headache frequency — but caution at high doses** — Curcumin is among the most effective natural anti-inflammatory compounds known; it reduces headache partly by inhibiting nitric oxide (NO)-driven vasodilation. It should not be taken before exercise (blocks beneficial adaptation inflammation). At high doses, curcumin inhibits DHT (dihydrotestosterone) synthesis, which can reduce libido and impact androgen-dependent tissues. Effective dose: approximately 80 mg/day combined with 2.5 g omega-3/day. [02:13:26](transcript.md#021326-tool-migraine-treatments--curcumin-turmeric)

13. **Extremely spicy peppers (Carolina Reaper) can trigger thunderclap headache and permanent brain damage via cerebral vasoconstriction** — Thunderclap headache is a distinct type not caused by muscle tension or trigeminal inflammation, but by extreme cerebral vasoconstriction. Eating ultra-spicy peppers simultaneously activates heat and spice neural pathways that constrict cerebral vasculature and cut off blood supply to neural tissue. In a documented case, a man eating a Carolina Reaper in a competition suffered thunderclap headache and permanent brain damage (neuronal death from ischemia — effectively a stroke). [02:18:00](transcript.md#021800-carolina-reaper-pepper--thunderclap-headache)

## Tools & Protocols

### Creatine Monohydrate for TBI-Related and General Headaches

- **What:** Creatine monohydrate oral supplementation to buffer brain energy stores (phosphocreatine/ATP), improving neuronal recovery and reducing headache frequency after head injury or in general recurring headache conditions.
- **How:** The pilot study used 0.4 g/kg body weight per day. For a 75 kg person that is approximately 30 g/day — significantly higher than the standard athletic dose of 3–5 g/day. Huberman notes that anyone considering higher doses for headache prevention should work with a physician. Standard dose of 5 g/day is a reasonable starting point for general headache reduction.
- **When:** Daily supplementation over weeks to months, not acute. Most beneficial for people with TBI history or recurring headaches.
- **Ref:** [01:02:08](transcript.md#010208-tool-headache-treatment-creatine-monohydrate--tbi) | Study: https://bit.ly/3Y8lKLU

---

### Omega-3 Supplementation + Omega-6 Reduction

- **What:** Increase dietary omega-3 EPA+DHA while simultaneously reducing omega-6 linoleic acid consumption to shift inflammatory balance and reduce headache frequency and intensity across tension, migraine, and hormonal headache types.
- **How:** Target 1.5–2 g EPA+DHA per day from fish oil or algae-based supplements. Simultaneously reduce omega-6 intake by minimizing seed oils (corn, soybean, sunflower, safflower, canola). Getting above 1 g EPA/day is the threshold shown to have meaningful effect; some research supports up to 3 g/day total omega-3 (per Dr. Rhonda Patrick). Omega-3s can be obtained from fatty ocean fish (salmon, sardines, anchovies) but supplementation is typically needed to reach therapeutic doses.
- **When:** Ongoing dietary protocol — effects appear over weeks to months, not acutely.
- **Ref:** [01:22:14](transcript.md#012214-tool-omega-3-supplementation-omega-6-fatty-acids--inflammation) | Studies: https://bit.ly/3X5lRXw | https://bit.ly/3Y4SXaZ

---

### Omega-3s for Hormonal (PMS-Related) Headaches

- **What:** Omega-3 supplementation specifically targeting pain and headache symptoms associated with premenstrual syndrome and menstrual-cycle-related hormonal fluctuations.
- **How:** Same protocol as general omega-3 supplementation — aim for above 1 g EPA/day. Omega-3s have been classified as NSAIDs in some research contexts due to their analgesic and anti-inflammatory potency, and a prescription form exists.
- **When:** Ongoing daily supplementation; not acute relief.
- **Ref:** [01:28:11](transcript.md#012811-hormone-headache-treatment--omega-3s) | Study: https://bit.ly/40uX5Tu

---

### Red Light Therapy for Photophobia and Migraine

- **What:** Exposure to long-wavelength red light (620–750 nm) to reduce photophobia and attenuate pain-amplifying visual signals during migraines and aura.
- **How:** Use a red light panel or red light bulb and remain in its presence (avoid staring directly) for 10–15 minutes when photophobia or aura begins. Avoid bright white or blue light during this period.
- **When:** At onset of aura or photophobia, or prophylactically during known migraine-prone periods.
- **Ref:** [01:31:24](transcript.md#013124-tool-aura-photophobia--offsetting-with-red-light)

---

### Botox Injections for Chronic Tension Headaches

- **What:** Botulinum toxin injected into specific scalp, forehead, and neck muscles to block acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction, preventing the muscle contractions that compress nociceptors and generate tension headache pain.
- **How:** Administered by a licensed physician or neurologist. FDA-approved for chronic migraine (15+ headache days per month). Multiple injection sites across scalp, temples, and back of neck. Effects last approximately 3 months per treatment cycle.
- **When:** Appropriate for chronic tension headache sufferers who have not responded adequately to NSAIDs or lifestyle modifications.
- **Ref:** [01:44:15](transcript.md#014415-tool-tension-headache--botox-treatment)

---

### Peppermint Oil (Topical) for Tension and Migraine Headaches

- **What:** Topical application of 10% peppermint oil in ethanol to forehead and temples to reduce headache pain via TRPM8 cold-receptor activation and substance P reduction. Effective for both tension and migraine headaches.
- **How:** Use a 10% peppermint oil solution applied to forehead and temples at headache onset. Avoid contact with eyes. Reapply as needed.
- **When:** At onset of tension or migraine headache as a first-line intervention. Clinically shown equivalent to 1000 mg acetaminophen for tension headaches.
- **Ref:** [01:49:43](transcript.md#014943-tool-alternative-headache-treatments-peppermint-oil-menthol) | Study: https://bit.ly/3wZegiu

---

### Acupuncture for Tension Headaches

- **What:** Traditional acupuncture targeting specific insertion sites on scalp, face, and neck to activate sensory neurons and deactivate pain/motor pathways, reducing headache frequency and intensity. Research by Dr. Qiufu Ma's lab at Harvard Medical School has mechanistically confirmed how precise needle sites reduce inflammation and dampen muscle activity in the forehead and temples.
- **How:** Performed by a licensed acupuncturist. Multiple studies show efficacy for tension-type headaches comparable to conventional drug treatments. Many insurance companies now cover acupuncture for this indication.
- **When:** Recurring sessions (weekly or bi-weekly) for people with chronic tension headaches who prefer non-pharmacological approaches.
- **Ref:** [02:01:35](transcript.md#020135-tool-tension-headaches-treatment--acupuncture)

---

### Caffeine Timing for Migraine Management

- **What:** Strategic use of caffeine at migraine onset as a vasoconstrictor to counteract the vascular dilation driving migraine pain; or gradual tapering to avoid withdrawal-induced headaches from missed doses.
- **How:** For migraine relief — consume caffeine (coffee, tea, or caffeine-containing OTC migraine medications like Excedrin) at the very onset of migraine symptoms. For withdrawal prevention — do not skip regular caffeine abruptly; taper slowly over 1–2 weeks.
- **When:** Acute use at migraine onset. Prevention strategy for regular caffeine consumers who experience headaches when skipping their usual dose.
- **Ref:** [02:05:41](transcript.md#020541-tool-migraine--herbal-treatment-caffeine-timing)

---

### Curcumin (Turmeric) for Headache Prevention

- **What:** Curcumin supplementation as an anti-inflammatory intervention to reduce headache frequency, partly by inhibiting nitric oxide (NO)-mediated vasodilation. Potent enough to have been studied at doses generally safe up to 8000 mg/day, though that is not recommended. High doses inhibit DHT synthesis and can reduce libido.
- **How:** Approximately 80 mg curcumin per day (sensitive individuals: start at 25–50 mg), taken alongside 2.5 g omega-3 fatty acids per day. Use enhanced bioavailability formulation (with piperine/black pepper extract, or as phytosome formulation). Do not take within 2–4 hours of exercise. Take on rest days or in evenings.
- **When:** Daily supplement for chronic headache sufferers. Not for acute headache relief. Avoid peri-workout.
- **Ref:** [02:13:26](transcript.md#021326-tool-migraine-treatments--curcumin-turmeric) | Review: https://bit.ly/40uXbuk
