# The Science of How to Optimize Testosterone & Estrogen

## [00:00:00] Introduction

Welcome to the Huberman Lab Podcast, where we discuss science and science-based tools for everyday life. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. This podcast is separate from my teaching and research roles at Stanford; it is, however, part of my desire and effort to bring zero-cost-to-consumer information about science and science-related tools to the general public.

This month on the Huberman Lab Podcast, we're talking all about hormones — these incredible chemicals that can impact our mood, our behavior, our feelings of optimism or pessimism. The amazing thing about hormones is that hormones impact all those things, but all those things — how we feel and what we do and what we think — also can impact our hormones. It's a really fascinating area of biology that impacts every single one of us every day, both in wakefulness and in sleep, and throughout the lifespan.

Today, we're going to be talking about hormone optimization, and we're mainly going to be focusing on estrogen and testosterone and their derivatives. Last episode, we talked about sexual development — how chromosomes, gonads, and hormones impact what we call sexual development, leading all the way up to puberty. Today, we're mainly going to talk about processes that happen from puberty onward, and we're going to weave in a lot of practical tools along the way for how to optimize these incredibly powerful things that we call hormones.

## [00:05:50] Hormone Optimization

Today's discussion will certainly be relevant to physical performance. We're going to talk about how specific types of exercise, particular patterns of cold exposure, and particular patterns of breathing can impact sex steroid hormones — both estrogen and testosterone. The discussion isn't really geared towards performance enhancement in sport, although we will do an entire episode related to performance enhancement in physical enterprises.

One of the first things to understand if you want to optimize your hormones is where they come from. There are a lot of different glands in the body that produce hormones — the pineal gland, the hypothalamus, the gonads (ovaries or testes), the thyroid gland — but when we're talking about the sex steroid hormones, estrogen and testosterone, the major sources are ovaries for estrogen and the testes for testosterone, although the adrenals can also make testosterone.

There are also some enzymes called aromatases that can convert testosterone into estrogen. In a male that has very high testosterone, some of that is going to be converted into estrogen by aromatase. Aromatase is made by body fat; it's also made in the testes themselves — a lot of people don't realize this, but the testes actually have the capacity to manufacture estrogen and aromatase, albeit at low levels.

It's important to note that there's a huge range in terms of the levels of hormones — testosterone and estrogen — between individuals, and it actually occurs within individuals across the lifespan. If you want to examine your hormones, you should do that in conjunction with a medical doctor, ideally an endocrinologist.

Prepubescent females make very little estrogen. During puberty, levels of estrogen (mainly estradiol, the most active form in both males and females) basically skyrocket, and then across the lifespan, estrogen varies depending on the stage of the menstrual cycle. As one heads into menopause — which typically takes place somewhere between age 45 and 60 — levels of estrogen drop. Testosterone will fluctuate across the lifespan as well, dropping off at about a rate of 1% per year in males, although there's tremendous variation — there are examples of men in their 90s who still have testosterone levels that mimic pubertal levels.

## [00:07:00] Salutogenesis: A Powerful Way to Conceptualize Health

Before we dive into hormone optimization, I want to raise what I think is a very important point that I hadn't heard of until recently: the concept of salutogenesis. Many of us are familiar with the concept of pathogenesis — the idea that there are all these scary diseases like dementia and heart disease and stroke that await us if we don't take good care of ourselves. Salutogenesis is a different orientation toward health and well-being where you're taking on particular behaviors in order to promote well-being above where you would be if you were not doing those behaviors.

These two things, salutogenesis and the pathogenic model, are really two sides of the same coin. If you like exercise because it feels good, great, but many people exercise or eat well in order to avoid heart disease or dementia. The salutogenic model differs in that it involves a mindset toward doing those things in order to feel good, in order to enhance your level of energy, in order to improve endocrine function and metabolic function. And really, the most powerful mindset is going to be one where you are thinking about both models — doing things to move away from sickness, as well as doing things to move toward health and well-being.

This is especially relevant because of some of the mindset effects discussed in previous episodes. There are incredible studies by Alia Crum's group at Stanford and others showing that if you tell people that the behavior they're about to do is good for them, you see much greater positive health effects than if they aren't aware of that information. So we should really be thinking about not just moving away from disease and negative things, but also why certain things are good for us — it's well-established from really good scientific studies that keeping in mind the positive effects of things can have an outsized effect on well-being, right down to the level of our physiology.

## [00:10:03] Estrogen and Testosterone: Sources, Levels & Ratios

Estrogen and testosterone and their derivatives are what we call sex steroids. The sex steroids immediately call to mind sex and anabolic steroids, but I want to emphasize that estrogen and testosterone are present in everybody — it's their ratios that determine their effects. The ratio of estrogen and testosterone in every individual has profound influence on feelings of well-being, feelings of optimism, feelings of anxiety or lack of anxiety, on reproduction, and on sexual behavior independent of reproduction. They are profoundly powerful molecules, and we all make these molecules to some degree.

There are also important behavioral tools, supplementation tools, as well as prescription drugs that can impact the ratios of testosterone and estrogen in really powerful ways.

The other glands and tissues in the body that make these hormones are the adrenals — right on top of the kidneys. The release of these steroid hormones from the adrenals, in particular testosterone and some of its related derivatives, are mainly activated by competition.

## [00:15:46] The Power of Competition, Plus: Anxiety, Persistence & Dopamine

Most males of a given mammalian species never get to reproduce — they never even get to have sex at all. Testosterone plays a powerful role in determining which members of a given species will get to reproduce. It's well known in species like elephant seals and antlered animals that higher levels of testosterone correlate with access to females. The males with higher testosterone forage further and will fight harder for the females.

There is very good evidence now that testosterone can reduce anxiety, promote novelty-seeking, and promote competitive interactions. We have a brain region called the amygdala, most famous for its role in fear — it really is involved in threat detection, it sets our thresholds for anxiety and what we consider scary or too much. Testosterone secreted from the gonads and elsewhere in the body binds to the amygdala and changes the threshold for stress, essentially lowering stress and anxiety, selecting individuals of a given species to push further, being willing to suffer more — and also reducing pain — in pursuit of reproduction.

It's well known in humans that both males and females who have elevated levels of testosterone will engage in more novelty-seeking. Testosterone increases generally lead to more foraging, more novelty-seeking, increases in libido, and increases in desire to mate.

It's also true that competition itself can increase androgens such as testosterone. Winning promotes more dopamine, and later, more testosterone. However, in the short term, just competing increases testosterone independent of whether or not you win or lose, so the short version is that competition increases testosterone. This may be an ancient mechanism whereby the androgens are feeding back to encourage more competitive-type behaviors.

Increases in testosterone in females are also going to lead to increased seeking of reproductive behavior and increase libido. In fact, there's a particular phase of the menstrual cycle where testosterone peaks just before ovulation that leads female humans to seek out sex more than they would otherwise during their cycle.

## [00:20:58] Testosterone & Libido Pre-Ovulation

Increases in testosterone, regardless of chromosomal or gonadal background, are increasing seeking of mates, increasing desire to compete, and lowering the threshold for stress and anxiety. A single molecule — testosterone — does all of this regardless of whether someone has ovaries or testes.

## [00:21:48] Estrogen & Sexual Receptivity; Libido In Males

While increases in testosterone promote seeking of mates and reproduction in both males and females, in females, it's actually increases in estrogen that promote receptivity to mating. So testosterone is driving the seeking of sex, and estrogen is promoting the actual act of sex from females — so-called receptivity.

In males, testosterone is promoting seeking of sex, but it's also estrogen in males that's important for libido. If estrogen levels are brought too low, then men will completely lose their libido. This is often overlooked in the discussion about testosterone therapy and performance-enhancing drugs — people think that hyperandrogenized individuals will have very high levels of libido, and they will, provided estrogen is available in sufficient ratios to match that testosterone. You actually need both in both males and females. In females, testosterone levels are always going to be lower than estrogen levels; in males, estrogen levels are always going to be lower than testosterone levels.

## [00:23:10] How Sex Behavior Impacts Testosterone: Observing vs. Actual vs. Abstinence

There are studies showing that sexual behavior itself can increase testosterone. A study published in 2011 from Escasa et al. showed that men who observe sex — watching pornography — will have slight increases in testosterone during the observation, of about 10%. Whereas when people participated in sex, they had 70% increases in testosterone. So there are quite significant increases in testosterone during the physical act of sex, and far less so during observing sex.

The question of whether ejaculation adjusts testosterone levels is somewhat nuanced. Sex and ejaculation itself does not reduce testosterone levels, although it will increase prolactin levels. However, abstinence or sex without ejaculation for a week or more will increase testosterone levels up to 400%. So sex itself increases testosterone; however, abstinence also increases testosterone even further.

In both men and women, sex increases prolactin post-sex. Testosterone and dopamine increase in the seeking-out and the behavior of sex, and then after sex, prolactin levels go up, there's a kind of quiescence — the whole nervous system is promoted towards calm. This may actually have something to do with pair-bonding and the encouragement of individuals to spend more time together to exchange different smells and hormones, and maybe even pheromones.

## [00:26:46] Testosterone & Prolactin: Sex Seeking vs Pair Bonding

Prolactin actually sets the refractory period in males during which he can't have sex again, and the duration of the refractory period will vary tremendously depending on how much and how long that prolactin release occurs. Some people take vitamin B6 in order to reduce prolactin levels and thereby reduce the duration of the refractory period.

Peaks in testosterone in males and females cause individuals to seek sex, not promote parenting, whereas reductions in testosterone, increases in prolactin, and decreases in cortisol move individuals of both sexes toward parenting behavior and less toward reproductive behavior.

## [00:27:30] DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone): Effects on Levels/Ratios

DHEA, which is mainly made by the adrenals, has been promoted as a catch-all for increasing testosterone and estrogen in males and females, and indeed, DHEA will increase both testosterone and estrogen. This is something to be mindful of if you're thinking about taking DHEA or you're taking DHEA already.

The extent to which DHEA increases one hormone or the other will depend on whether you're starting off with more estrogen than testosterone, or more testosterone than estrogen, and whether or not you have a lot of aromatase. For individuals that have a lot of aromatase being made by the testes or by body fat, if you take DHEA, there's a good chance that a fair portion of that is going to be shuttled towards estrogen production and not towards testosterone production. So the way to think about DHEA is that it's a kind of global promoter of the sex steroid hormones, and its specific effects are going to depend on where you started and whether or not you have ovaries or testes.

## [00:28:45] Behaviors That Decrease Testosterone (& Cortisol): Parenting & Prolactin

Just as there are behaviors that can increase testosterone, there are behaviors that can decrease testosterone, and one of the most well-characterized ones in humans is becoming a parent. Expecting fathers have an almost 50% decrease in testosterone levels (both free and bound testosterone); their cortisol levels, a stress hormone, drop by almost three-fold; and their estradiol levels double.

These effects of reduced testosterone, increased estradiol, and reduced cortisol can all be explained by an increase in prolactin. Not just in humans, but in other species as well, when the male and female of that species are expecting young, they lay down more body fat — the assumption is that this is to prepare for long nights of no sleep. So the "dad bod" can be partly explained hormonally.

How long these hormonal changes last has to do with how much contact and how much smell of the baby the father happens to have, so how available or unavailable he is will actually impact his level of hormones.

## [00:31:24] How Illness Impacts Testosterone & Estrogen: Cytokines, e.g., IL-6

The other behavior that markedly reduces testosterone in both males and females and markedly reduces the desire for seeking sex is illness. When people feel sick, they don't feel like seeking out mates — and this can be explained by the release of what are called inflammatory cytokines. Cytokines travel in the lymph and in the blood and attack invader cells like bacteria and viruses. The best-known example of a pro-inflammatory cytokine is IL-6, and it's known that IL-6 injected into individuals will decrease the desire for sex and eventually will reduce levels of testosterone and estrogen independent of feeling lousy.

Inflammatory cytokines like IL-6 don't just travel to the gonads and shut them down — they actually have ways to interact with some of the receptors that the steroid hormones bind to and prevent those hormones from having their effect. So keeping levels of IL-6 low is going to be important for them to exert their effects. We're going to talk about how to modulate IL-6 and how to increase another cytokine called IL-10, which is anti-inflammatory, in ways that can help promote the sex steroid hormones.

## [00:33:20] How Exactly Do Behaviors Change Hormones?

You should be asking yourself: how is it, at a mechanistic level, that behaviors can modulate hormones at all? It's kind of strange that just the mere act of being a parent can change testosterone levels so dramatically, or estradiol levels so dramatically. Is it the sweat of the baby? The sight of the baby? It turns out that many of those effects are because of smell, or in some cases, possibly pheromones.

A hormone is a chemical that travels in the body and impacts tissues and cells elsewhere in the body. A pheromone is a chemical that's released by one member of a species that goes and impacts members of the same species, or even of other species.

## [00:34:18] Pheromones: Miscarriage, Menstrual Cycles, Puberty Onset, & Mate Recognition

Pheromone effects are absolutely well-established in lots of animal species, but they are very controversial in humans. The main pheromone effects discussed in animals are:

The **Lee-Boot effect**: when you house females of a given species together with no males, they start displaying longer estrus cycles. The presence of the male itself is changing the ovulation cycle.

The **Bruce effect**: a pregnant animal will abort or reabsorb her fetus if the father of those animals is removed and a novel male is placed in her vicinity for about 48 hours. A pheromone from male urine activates the gonadotropin-releasing hormone system and causes a reintroduction of the estrus cycle and a spontaneous abortion of the fetus. Whether miscarriages in humans are caused by detecting the pheromones or odors of novel males is still an open question.

The **Vandenbergh effect**: puberty in females can be accelerated by placing a novel sexually competent male with a young female who has not undergone puberty. There's also a version where placing juvenile females with more mature females of the same species causes a significant delay in the onset of puberty.

These are all pheromone effects because they're not conscious, they don't require actual contact — they can be mediated by the urine or sweat of a given species.

Regarding pheromones in humans, a paper published in 1998 by Stern and McClintock showed that sweat from females — collected from pads worn in armpits and then applied beneath the nose of other women (diluted in alcohol so they couldn't detect the odor) — can modulate the menstrual cycle pattern of other adult females. The sweat and pheromones of females can modulate the menstrual cycle patterns of other adult females, changing the duration and pattern of ovulation relative to the follicular phase.

People are so skeptical about the presence of pheromone effects in humans because there's no well-identified pheromone organ. There's something called Jacobson's organ (the equivalent of the vomeronasal organ), thought to be a combination of smell and taste in the back of the nose. And there's a well-established phenomenon with t-shirts: a girlfriend can pick out her boyfriend's t-shirt among hundreds of others, not because it smells different in a detectable way, but because something about it seems different — at a level of accuracy way above statistical significance.

Perfume manufacturers have picked up on the idea of pheromones and have entire laboratories set up to build chemical compounds into perfumes designed to attract mates. During mating seasons for different animals, you can even see the flehmen response — where animals open their lips and expose their gums to capture pheromones floating in the wind.

## [00:43:33] Apnea: A Powerful Bi-Directional Influence On Estrogen & Testosterone

One of the main behaviors shown to be associated with poor levels of estrogen relative to age-matched controls (for people with ovaries) or lower levels of testosterone compared to age-matched controls (for people with testes) is apnea. Apnea has everything to do with under-breathing and the buildup of too much carbon dioxide in the body.

I went deep into the literature on advanced menopause and on andropause, and in every case, you could find multiple papers showing that apnea — or poor efficiency of breathing and buildup of too much carbon dioxide in the body — was a problem. Mostly sleep apnea, although apnea in general was shown to be an issue negatively impacting hormones.

The directionality of this effect isn't entirely clear. It could be that reductions in estrogen cause apnea — I found at least one paper showing that there are estrogen receptors on some of the neurons that innervate the lungs, meaning reductions in estrogen may adjust breathing by changing our sensitivity to our own lungs. As well, testosterone reductions were associated with apnea, and testosterone receptors are also found on a lot of cells in the viscera, including the lungs.

There are at least four quality studies showing that when apnea is reduced or eliminated in sleep, there are significant increases in testosterone in males and in proper estrogen-to-testosterone ratios in females. The mechanism is through reductions in cortisol. Cortisol is released early in the day as we wake up and serves healthy roles, but you don't want cortisol to be too high. The molecule cholesterol can be converted into testosterone or estrogen, but there's a competition whereby cholesterol will turn into cortisol and not testosterone — or cortisol and not estrogen — if stress levels are too high.

## [00:47:44] Mouth vs. Nose Breathing & Hormone Levels: Effects Via Sleep and Direct Effects

There's now a lot of literature showing that breathing through the nose — not through the mouth — is powerful for improving lots of things. First, it improves cosmetic features of the jaw and face; this was well-established by Sandra Kahn and Paul Ehrlich at Stanford in their book "Jaws: The Story of a Hidden Epidemic." Mouth-breathers show changes in the cosmetics of their face and jaw in twin studies — the chin drops back behind the upper mandible, there's a lengthening in the face, a drooping of the eyes.

Nose-breathing in wakefulness and in sleep promotes positive things related to gas exchange of carbon dioxide and oxygen, and it can modify levels of different neurotransmitters and neuromodulators in ways that positively can impact hormones. Being a nasal-breather and avoiding mouth-breathing can actually positively impact the hormones testosterone and estrogen — the way it does that is by making you a better sleeper, which allows you to produce more testosterone and the appropriate amounts of testosterone and estrogen. Deep sleep supports the gonads, the ovaries and the testicles and their turnover of cells and the production of cells.

For people with severe sleep apnea, they are going to need a CPAP machine. Many people, however, are starting to tape their mouths shut — as described by James Nestor in his book "Breath: The New Science of a Lost Art" — simply taping shut the mouth with tape that will allow you to open your mouth if you really need to during sleep can allow people to shift from being mouth-breathers and snorers to nose-breathers.

In the daytime, the best way to get good at nasal-breathing is to breathe through your nose more — particularly during cardiovascular exercise, most of the time, provided you're not in maximum effort. Nasal-breathing during exercise is hard at first, but as you do it, because the sinuses have a capacity to dilate over time, you'll get better at it. The nasal passages will start to dilate more. This has positive cosmetic effects, reduces apnea, offloads more carbon dioxide, increases lung capacity, and prevents apnea in sleep.

## [00:51:11] How Sleep Adjusts Cortisol/Testosterone and Cortisol/Estrogen Ratios

Getting proper sleep can really offset all the reductions in testosterone and estrogen and reductions in fertility that occur if we don't get enough sleep. Sleep actually adjusts things like testosterone and estrogen by modifying cortisol. The molecule cholesterol can be converted into testosterone or estrogen, but there's a competition whereby cholesterol will turn into cortisol and not testosterone if stress levels are too high. So the simple version: get your breathing right during the waking hours — you should be a nose-breather — and in sleep, you also want to be a nose-breather, because that's going to increase the amount of oxygen you're bringing into your system and the amount of carbon dioxide you're offloading, reducing apnea. Breath-holding in sleep leads to buildup of carbon dioxide and leads to increases in cortisol, which then decrease testosterone and estrogen in negative ways across all sexes.

## [00:53:49] O2:CO2 Ratios, Nasal Breathing During Exercise

My advice: breathe through your nose while exercising unless you're in maximum effort. Pretty soon, you'll find you can actually create more output than you would if you were breathing through your mouth. For swimmers and fighters and martial artists, there are sport-specific protocols, but for most recreational athletes or exercisers, learn to be a nasal-breather. It has positive cosmetic effects, reduces apnea, offloads more carbon dioxide, increases lung capacity, dilates the sinuses, and prevents apnea in sleep.

Becoming a nasal-breather can have all sorts of positive effects by reducing cortisol, reducing apnea, and indirectly raising testosterone and estrogen in the proper ratios. When you go into the scientific literature, it comes through as one of the most powerful things you can do that is zero cost.

## [00:56:30] Light Viewing Patterns & Hormones: Dopamine, GnRH

The scientific literature on light and hormones is robust and extends back several decades. Viewing light with your eyes can have a direct effect on hormone levels and fertility, on hormone levels and libido, and on hormone levels and your ability to heal quickly.

In order to understand the powerful effects that light can have on the sex steroid hormones, we need to understand seasonal breeding animals. In order to understand how light impacts various neurotransmitters and hormones, you need to understand the biology of species that change their coat color across the seasons — species like rabbits, fox, and various mustelids (ferrets, ermines). Those same animals breed in the spring and shut down breeding, shut down ovulation, and often shut down testosterone production in the winter months.

## [00:57:44] Spring Fever: Tyrosinase, Hair Color, Mating Frequency

Dopamine is the link between light and hormone production. Dopamine has a precursor — tyrosine — which is an amino acid that comes from food. When dopamine levels are high, there's a tendency for more gonadotropin-releasing hormone, luteinizing hormone, follicle-stimulating hormone — all the hormones that come from the hypothalamic-pituitary axis and stimulate estrogen and testosterone release from the ovary and testes. Dopamine basically increases all of that.

The same amino acid-based pathway and many of the same enzymes that are devoted to dopamine and dopamine increasing the sex steroid hormones are also devoted to giving pigmentation to the hair and skin (via tyrosine and tyrosinase). This is why in the summer months, when days are longer, animals breed more — and why in the winter months when days are shorter, animals breed less. This is also why many people feel an elevation in mood in the spring and summer months because of the amount of sunlight they're getting.

Increased viewing of sunlight — and it has to be to the eyes, not the skin — increases dopamine levels in the brain. Increased dopamine levels in animals and humans increases the activity of melanin-producing cells, which give pigmentation to the skin and hair, and indirectly increase the amount of testosterone and estrogen, and thereby reproductive behavior, feelings of well-being, social interactions, and reductions in anxiety.

The protocol: get anywhere from 2 to 10 minutes of bright light exposure in your eyes early in the day. It is not sufficient to do this with sunglasses unless you have to for safety reasons; it's fine to wear prescription lenses and contacts. If you can't get sunlight for whatever reason, use bright artificial light. This is absolutely critical for timing the cortisol release properly, limiting cortisol release to the early part of the day, and getting increases in dopamine that are going to promote the production of testosterone and estrogen to healthy levels.

The other critical aspect of light-viewing behavior is to avoid bright light exposure to your eyes in the middle of the night. Viewing bright light in the middle of the night suppresses dopamine release, and if you're suppressing dopamine release, you are suppressing testosterone levels. There are two studies published in Cell and Neuron showing that viewing bright light with the eyes in the middle of the circadian night has a detrimental effect on dopamine, and therefore has a detrimental effect on things like testosterone and estrogen.

## [01:04:39] Specificity of Hormone Effects

Everything described here is for people regardless of chromosomal or gonadal background. If you're somebody who naturally has ovaries and has higher levels of estrogen than testosterone, then viewing bright light early in the day — because of dopamine's effects — is going to promote more estrogen and subtle increases in testosterone. If you're somebody who starts off with more testosterone and lower estrogen, bright-light viewing is going to increase testosterone and estrogen in parallel, but you're still going to maintain the ratio of testosterone to estrogen. You don't have to worry that you're going to increase the wrong hormone — this is all about optimizing the ratios of hormones you already have.

## [01:06:03] Temperature: Cold & Hot Gonads

Temperature and day length are linked. In summer, when days are longer, it tends to be warmer out; in winter, when nights are longer, it tends to be colder out. In the winter months, testosterone and estrogen tend to be lower in many animals and in humans; in the summer months, because of dopamine's role in promoting the sex steroid hormones, when days are longer and it's warmer, humans tend to make more estrogen and testosterone relative to other months.

There's a recent movement where people use cold exposure — including, remarkably, underwear with ice packs — to try and increase testosterone and libido. Things like ice baths and cold showers can have positive effects on the sex steroid hormones. It's not actually the cold that's having these effects — what happens is there's a rebound in vasodilation after cooling. Cooling causes vasoconstriction, and then after the cooling, there's a rebound vasodilation and more infusion of blood into the gonads.

The most plausible explanation is that you're cooling the neurons that control vasoconstriction and vasodilation and shutting down the entry of blood and hormones into the gonad, and then when the gonad and the surrounding area heats up again, you're getting a rebound hyper-vasodilation that delivers increased levels of GnRH and other hormones and carrier proteins that would then stimulate the gonad to release more testosterone or estrogen.

Conversely, excessively high heat is not good for the testes, sperm production, or sperm health. Sperm have proteins in the cap that cause them to swim faster when expressed properly, and heat actually alters the location and function of a lot of those proteins — they're very heat sensitive.

The GnRH pathway is important to understand here. GnRH, gonadotropin-releasing hormone, comes from neurons that — remarkably — start off in your nose early in development and migrate into the hypothalamus. Those neurons extend processes (axons) into the pituitary and release GnRH into the pituitary. From there, GnRH stimulates follicle-stimulating hormone and luteinizing hormone, which then travel into the bloodstream and reach the ovary or testes through the vascular system — which is controlled by neurons.

## [01:14:10] How To Exercise: Types, Effort Level, Sequencing

If you want to optimize your hormones and in particular testosterone, we need to look at what exercise does. Heavy weight training — where sets are done in the one-to-eight rep range, working at anywhere from 70% to 95% of maximum — leads to the greatest increases in testosterone. Not weight training to failure where completion of a repetition is impossible, but weight training with heavy loads but not to failure.

The mechanism relates to the engagement of neurons that recruit high-threshold motor units in muscle when moving heavy loads. There's a Henneman size principle in muscle physiology: as loads increase, you have to recruit more and more neurons to activate muscle. This neural activity of recruiting high-threshold motor units provides some sort of feedback signal to the gonad to produce more testosterone, or is increasing the activity of receptors in the body.

The paper by Ratamess et al. published in 2005 on modulations in androgen receptor content after heavy resistance exercise, and the work of Izquierdo et al. published in 2006 on "Differential Effects of Strength Training Leading to Failure Versus Not Failure on Hormonal Responses, Strength, and Power Gains" — both point to the fact that there's something about the engagement of neurons recruiting high-threshold motor units when moving heavy loads, but not to failure, that provides feedback that increases testosterone. In males or females, weight training with heavy loads in the one-rep-maximum to six-to-eight rep range increases testosterone significantly, for about a day, sometimes up to 48 hours.

## [01:21:42] Cardio/Endurance vs. Resistance Training (First or Last?) Yes, It Matters

If you want to optimize testosterone levels, it seems that weight training first and doing cardio-type endurance activity afterward is the right order of business. Endurance activity performed first leads to decreases in testosterone during the weight training session as compared to the same weight training session done first followed by endurance activity. When these are done on separate days, it doesn't seem to have an effect.

High-intensity interval training — sprinting, etc. — which somewhat mimics the neural activity that occurs while moving heavy weight loads, is going to increase testosterone, and there's ample evidence for that in the literature. Endurance exercise that extends beyond 75 minutes is going to start to lead to reductions in testosterone, presumably by increases in cortisol. Limiting endurance exercise to 75 minutes or less and not making it too intense is one way to keep cortisol from going through the roof.

This is one of the reasons why various endurance athletes — professional cyclists, for instance — will sometimes rely on testosterone therapy, not just for muscle hypertrophy but to prevent cortisol-induced reductions in testosterone during extended exercise.

## [01:24:41] Estrogen & Menopause: Compounds That May Ameliorate/Reverse Symptoms

Menopause is this fairly massive reduction in the amount of estrogen circulating in the blood, mainly because the ovary is now depleted of some estrogen production of its own — the eggs have been depleted. Menopause is characterized by hot flashes, mood swings, headaches (in particular migraine headaches), brain fog, and can be very disruptive.

Andropause, thought to be the analog to menopause in males, has certain differences: menopause is a very robust phenomenon for most women, whereas for men, andropause is going to sometimes happen and sometimes won't. Some men maintain levels of circulating androgens quite high, even as similar as they were in puberty, well into their 60s, 70s, and 80s if they're optimizing other things — and probably genetics plays a role as well.

Physicians will often prescribe supplemental estrogen for menopause — oral estrogen, a patch, a pellet — with varying success and side effects for some people. There's also always a concern about supplementing estrogen when there's a breast cancer background in the family, because a lot of those cancers are estrogen-dependent. This is why drugs like tamoxifen and anastrozole — which block either aromatase or estrogen receptors directly — were initially developed as cancer treatments, not performance enhancement.

Looking at the literature on menopause outside of standard estrogen therapy, there are some interesting compounds supported by quality peer-reviewed studies. All of these can be explored on examine.com:

**Black cohosh**: There are 13 peer-reviewed studies showing modest but real effects — significant over placebo — with consistent but minor increases in estrogen and modest decreases in menopause-related symptoms. Studies generally carried out over six-plus months in women ages 45–64.

**Panax ginseng**: Has been shown to decrease some symptoms associated with menopause, mostly related to libido.

**Maca**: Minor effects.

**Valeriana officinalis**: Can provide some improvement in hot flash symptoms and insomnia associated with menopause in 100-subject-plus, age 45–64, double-blind placebo-controlled studies.

**Pueraria mirifica** (also called Kwao Krua Kao): Four studies showing it to be comparable to estrogen replacement therapy in reducing symptoms of menopause. In every single study, it was comparable to estrogen replacement therapy. This compound is a serious consideration and should be discussed with a doctor; any hormone-related compound requires caution because of the relationship to breast cancers.

## [01:33:18] Nutrients That Optimize The Foundation For Hormones

Certain collections of nutrients are useful for promoting testosterone and estrogen production in their proper ratios. These are the usual suspects: vitamin D, which is important for so many biological functions including endocrine functions; zinc; magnesium. A paper by Wrzosek et al. 2020 in Endocrinology and Metabolism Review talks about how the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and the sex steroid hormones are negatively impacted by deficiencies in magnesium, deficiencies in vitamin D, and deficiencies in zinc.

Make sure you're getting adequate zinc, magnesium, and D3, unless you want these steroid hormone levels to be reduced. Getting blood work done is how you know whether or not your androgen levels, as well as things like vitamin D levels, are sufficiently high. The takeaway from these studies: make sure you're getting adequate zinc, magnesium, and D3 to maintain the foundation for sex steroid hormone production.

## [01:36:00] Opioids as Severe Hormone Disruptors

Opioids dramatically reduce levels of testosterone and estrogen. They do that mainly by disrupting the receptors on gonadotropin-releasing hormone neurons — those neurons within the hypothalamus that communicate to the pituitary. People who take large amounts of opioids, or even take low levels of opioids for long periods of time, will develop all sorts of endocrine syndromes: gynecomastia (male breast development in males), disruptions to the ovary in females. The major effect is actually way up in the hypothalamus — shutting down the production of GnRH, the very hormone that stimulates testosterone and estrogen release.

## [01:37:23] Testes, Antlers & Ovaries

The industry around testosterone and estrogen is vast and dates back hundreds of years. Things like the material off deer antlers (high in supposedly IGF-1, which is in the growth hormone pathway) and even the consumption of bull testes have been promoted. A lot of that is going to be broken down in the gut. The development of the birth control pill was only made possible by understanding the structure of estrogen and estradiol.

## [01:38:50] Creatine & Increasing DHT (Dihydrotestosterone)

Creatine has really good evidence for it. It's very clear that something about creatine — though the mechanism isn't exactly clear — either increases 5-alpha reductase or makes the testosterone molecule more susceptible to certain enzymatic reactions, leading to increases in DHT (dihydrotestosterone). DHT has a much higher affinity for the androgen receptor than does testosterone. It has powerful effects in creating balding, beard growth, and has a dramatic role in the masculinization of the brain prenatally.

Creatine can increase DHT, which means that if you take creatine and you're very DHT-susceptible, then you might experience some hair loss. Whether creatine causes baldness depends on how much 5-alpha reductase you have and how prone to hair loss you are.

## [01:40:14] Free and Bound Testosterone: SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin), Tongkat Ali

Most testosterone isn't just roaming around free in the blood — most of it is bound to either sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG) or to albumin. These are needed as transporters to get testosterone into cells so it can have its effects on gene expression and other pathways within the cells.

People talk about free testosterone as what's really important, and that's a little bit of a tricky statement. The binding globulins aren't there to soak up all your testosterone just to make it hard to free up — they're there to transport testosterone to specific tissues and to set the rate of bound and unbound testosterone so it's not all unbound at once. Sex hormone binding globulin can bind up too much testosterone to the point where it's having negative effects on libido, muscle growth, and fat loss — or it can be doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

Albumin is actually important for testosterone to be able to make it into the brain to have some of the pro-androgenic effects on cognition, because getting molecules across the blood-brain barrier (even if they're lipophilic sex steroid hormones) requires carriers, and those carriers often are bound to or include albumin.

**Tongkat ali** (also called Eurycoma longifolia Jack): Several studies on examine.com and PubMed support that it does seem to have some pro-fertility, pro-free-testosterone, and subtle aphrodisiac effects. It also seems to be a slight anti-estrogen. People take anywhere from 400 to 800 milligrams a day. It liberates some of the bound testosterone and allows more free testosterone to be available. Reported side effects include excessive alertness and insomnia if taken too late in the day.

## [01:43:23] Nettles, Prostate, Boron, & Blood Brain Barrier

**Stinging nettles**: Purported to free up testosterone in the blood, though the literature points to stinging nettle also having some fairly potent effects on the prostate and on the liver, so it might be a tricky molecule.

**Boron**: There's some scientific support for it freeing up more testosterone. The amounts people take are a couple grams per day. Not aware of specific side effects, but you always want to examine for the particular side effects.

The reason we're discussing these particular tools is that getting free testosterone into a range that works for your particular goals and needs is an attractive one — not freeing up all your testosterone, but optimizing the balance.

## [01:45:27] Hormone Related Cancers: Sometimes Reducing Estrogen and Testosterone Is Optimal

You have to be careful anytime you're modulating hormones, especially androgens and estrogens. The reason there's so much breast cancer and testicular cancer is that any tissue that undergoes rapid reproduction of cells — shedding of uterine lining, production of eggs in the ovary, production of Leydig and sertoli cells in the testes, ongoing production of sperm — is particularly vulnerable to the development of cancers. Many of those cancers are androgen-sensitive, which is why one of the major treatments for prostate overgrowth or prostate cancer is to give anti-androgenic drugs. It's about trying to prevent testosterone from encouraging growth of tumors.

More is definitely not better. It's not just because they can aromatize — it's because cancers, or any tissue that has a lot of turnover of cells, is going to thrive on androgens or estrogens, anything that promotes growth.

## [01:47:43] Ecdysteroids: Mimic Mammalian Hormones

Ecdysteroids are molecules that come from things like spinach and have a lot of similarity to the cholesterol molecule. The one being discussed now is turkesterone. Ecdysteroids are similar enough to cholesterol — the precursor to testosterone, cortisol, and estrogen — and it appears that some of them do have bioavailability, or their metabolites are bioavailable.

A study that came out in 2019, Isenmann et al. in the Archives of Toxicology, compared ecdysteroids given in conjunction with strength training or no strength training over a 10-week intervention. Their conclusion was that these ecdysteroids had fairly significant, above-placebo increases in muscle mass, strength, and hypertrophy effects. The authors stated: "Our results strongly suggest the inclusion of ecdysteroid in the list of prohibited substances." So they're saying these things are so powerful that they should be on the list of banned substances.

## [01:51:50] Optimizing Brain Hormones: Chorionic Gonadotropin, Fadogia Agrestis

Thus far in talking about optimizing hormones and supplementation, we haven't really talked about things that actually affect the brain directly — that increase pituitary output. Luteinizing hormone, coming from the pituitary, circulates and goes to the ovary to promote egg maturation and production of estrogen, and to the testes to promote testosterone and sperm production.

The prescription version of increasing luteinizing hormone is hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin), which is now available as a prescription drug. hCG was initially collected and synthesized from pregnant women's urine. It can increase sperm production, increase ovulation frequency, increase the number of eggs deployed in a given ovulation, and is pro-fertility, pro-testosterone, pro-estrogen. It's used to increase fertility, to restore testosterone when gonads have been shut down by excessive hormone therapies, or when people have lesions to the pituitary.

**Fadogia agrestis** (F-A-D-O-G-I-A A-G-R-E-S-T-I-S): According to the literature, this can increase levels of luteinizing hormone and thereby levels of testosterone or estrogen. Anyone of any chromosomal or gonadal background would expect both testosterone and estrogen to increase. The side effect profile of Fadogia agrestis hasn't really been documented, so it's a little unclear.

Anytime someone's going to start taking supplements or modifying sex steroid hormones, getting blood work done is extremely important for safety reasons and to know whether or not things are working. All of these things are subject to negative feedback — if testosterone goes too high, it can feed back and shut down luteinizing hormone, which will then shut down further testosterone production. Likewise, if estrogens are going too high, that can start to throw off various other hormones including FSH, progesterone, and LH.

The menstrual cycle is an absolutely exquisite balance of feedback: luteinizing hormone kept low and constant for the first 14 days, then a mid-cycle peak (typically when ovulation occurs), and FSH going up and then down across the first 14 days. Taking anything that modifies estrogens or testosterone on that background is really going to disrupt those interactions.

## [01:57:18] Additional Compounds, Liver Toxicity, Overall Milieu

The list of supplements and molecules that can adjust estrogen and testosterone is vast. I only touched on a few — really the ones for which there are human studies. There are other things out there with scientific data, like Bulbine natalensis, which has support for increasing testosterone but the studies were mainly in rats, and the human evidence mainly comes from sponsored (biased) research. Additionally, Bulbine natalensis seems to have liver toxicity similar to high levels of anabolic exogenous steroids.

There's an important distinction between taking something to create a backdrop of general support (magnesium, D3, zinc — which will create an overall milieu of opportunity to produce normal endogenous levels) versus taking something that's going to create a big inflection in the levels of a given hormone (like Fadogia agrestis or tongkat ali). These are very different.

The effects of supplementation, in some individuals, can be quite dramatic — but they're always, except in extreme cases, going to be far more subtle than just injecting testosterone or estrogen.
