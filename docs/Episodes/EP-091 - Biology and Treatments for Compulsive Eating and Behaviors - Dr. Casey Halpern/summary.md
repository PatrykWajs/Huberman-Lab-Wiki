# Episode 91: Biology & Treatments for Compulsive Eating & Behaviors | Dr. Casey Halpern

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Casey Halpern, Chief of Stereotactic and Functional Neurosurgery at the University of Pennsylvania, joins Andrew Huberman to discuss the neuroscience of compulsive behaviors — particularly binge eating disorder and OCD — and how deep brain stimulation (DBS) can interrupt pathological craving circuits in the nucleus accumbens. The conversation covers cutting-edge work identifying "craving cells" that fire before a binge episode, responsive closed-loop stimulation that predicts and prevents binge eating, and non-invasive approaches including transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) and MRI-guided focused ultrasound for treating tremor, epilepsy, and depression. The episode also explores the future of AI-driven biomarker detection for compulsive disorders and the role of lifestyle habits — sleep, exercise, and meditation — in stabilizing the same reward circuits targeted by DBS.

## Guest: Dr. Casey Halpern

**Dr. Casey Halpern, M.D.** is Chief of Stereotactic and Functional Neurosurgery and Professor of Neurosurgery at the Perelman School of Medicine at the University of Pennsylvania. His research and clinical practice focus on using deep brain stimulation (DBS) to treat compulsive and movement disorders including binge eating disorder, bulimia, OCD, Parkinson's disease, essential tremor, and dystonia. He is the principal investigator on the DBSLOC trial — a landmark responsive DBS study for loss-of-control eating — which resulted in a 2023 Nature Medicine publication demonstrating that closed-loop nucleus accumbens stimulation can interrupt binge eating episodes in real time.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The nucleus accumbens is the central hub for compulsive behavior.** This paired brain structure drives dopamine-mediated motivation for food, sex, and addictive behaviors. In binge eating and OCD, it becomes dysregulated, producing compulsive urges that override conscious intent. Repeated exposure to high-fat food in mice changes nucleus accumbens function within two weeks, creating hyperactive and hypoactive regions that predispose continued compulsive behavior. [00:39:28](transcript.md#003928-nucleus-accumbens-reward-circuits-eating-disorders--obesity)

2. **Deep brain stimulation (DBS) can treat compulsive eating by targeting the nucleus accumbens.** Electrodes placed in the nucleus accumbens detect abnormal "craving" neural firing patterns before a binge and deliver a 5–10 second stimulation burst that interrupts the urge — a paradigm called closed-loop or responsive DBS. The nucleus accumbens is almost a centimeter in size; sub-millimeter targeting precision matters because two to three millimeters deviation can mean the difference between therapeutic effect and no result. [01:02:07](transcript.md#010207-electrodes-in-nucleus-accumbens-identifying-craving-cells)

3. **"Craving cells" in the nucleus accumbens can be identified intraoperatively while patients view food images.** Halpern's team uses the same single-unit microelectrode recording technique used to identify Parkinson's tremor cells — listening to individual neurons in the nucleus accumbens while fasted, awake patients view pictures of personally high-craving foods. These cells fire in a predictable pattern preceding loss-of-control eating, making pre-emptive stimulation possible. [01:02:07](transcript.md#010207-electrodes-in-nucleus-accumbens-identifying-craving-cells)

4. **Closed-loop (responsive) DBS is superior to continuous stimulation for episodic disorders.** Continuous stimulation causes tolerance — OCD patients feel great when the device is first turned on but call back the next day with symptoms returned. A published mouse study in PNAS demonstrated that intermittent stimulation delivered only when a craving signal is detected produces more robust and durable blockade of binge eating than continuous stimulation. [00:47:18](transcript.md#004718-stimulation-of-nucleus-accumbens-continuous-vs-episodic-stimulation)

5. **OCD involves dysregulated orbitofrontal cortex and prefrontal cortex projections to the striatum.** The cortical control areas in OCD are not simply hyperactive or hypoactive — they are dysregulated. These areas project to the dorsal striatum (caudate/putamen) and to the ventral striatum including the nucleus accumbens, which gates compulsive reward-seeking behavior despite risk. DBS responder rate for OCD is approximately 50%, and even responders still have symptomatic OCD. [00:25:40](transcript.md#002540-brain-areas-in-ocd-risk-rewards--addiction)

6. **Binge eating disorder is the most common eating disorder, affecting 3–5% of the population and likely under-diagnosed.** Severe cases binge approximately once per day; loss-of-control eating (a broader category) can occur 20–30 times per week. Development follows a two-hit model: a genetic/neurological predisposition combined with a recurring environmental stressor, amplified by a society with cheap, highly palatable, heavily refined foods. [00:53:02](transcript.md#005302-developing-binge-eating-disorder-predisposition-environment-stress)

7. **Anorexia and compulsive overeating share the same core circuit dysfunction — compulsion despite risk.** Halpern argues that obese patients who compulsively overeat and anorexics who compulsively restrict are neurologically more similar than different. The nucleus accumbens has been studied in anorexia DBS trials in China and Europe; colleague Andres Lozano has targeted Area 25 (subgenual cingulate), monosynaptically connected to the nucleus accumbens, with some benefit. Anorexia has the highest mortality of all psychiatric conditions, including from metabolic complications. [01:16:46](transcript.md#011646-anorexia-obesity--compulsions-potential-treatments-for-anorexia)

8. **Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is FDA-cleared for depression, OCD, and nicotine addiction.** TMS modulates cortical circuits non-invasively; it cannot reach deep targets directly but may do so indirectly via highly connected superficial cortical areas. Halpern runs a parallel TMS trial for OCD funded by the Foundation for OCD Research — using TMS to define circuits and screen candidates for eventual invasive DBS study. [01:23:14](transcript.md#012314-non-invasive-brain-stimulation-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation)

9. **MRI-guided focused ultrasound is FDA-cleared for tremor and can ablate deep brain targets without surgery.** By converging ultrasound beams through the intact skull, focused ultrasound creates a precise thermal lesion with immediate tremor relief. Essential tremor — 10 times more common than Parkinson's and the most common neurologic condition in patients over 70 — is the primary cleared indication. Researchers are also exploring non-ablative modulatory use and blood-brain barrier opening for targeted drug delivery. [01:32:27](transcript.md#013227-mri-guided-focused-ultrasound-tremor-essential-tremor--parkinsons)

10. **Pre-binge states are preceded by negative affect, and even fully aware patients cannot stop themselves.** Patients in laboratory mood-provocation studies — under video surveillance with eye trackers, synchronized to implanted device recordings — still binge despite knowing they are being observed and studied. Awareness is valuable but insufficient for the most severe refractory patients; CBT benefits may not persist after treatment ends. [01:41:51](transcript.md#014151-pre-behavioral-states-in-compulsion--awareness-mood-provocation)

11. **AI and machine learning can anticipate compulsive episodes before the patient is consciously aware.** University of Washington labs are already using voice-pattern signatures to predict suicidal depressive episodes. Future wearable and implanted devices will train on body physiology (voice, breathing, sleep patterns, neural signals) to personalize stimulation parameters and adapt dynamically to a patient's changing brain state. [01:48:02](transcript.md#014802-machine-learningartificial-intelligence--compulsion-predictions)

12. **Daily habits — sleep, exercise, and meditation — remain foundational alongside DBS and behavioral interventions.** Halpern lost 20 lbs in three months simply by beginning to exercise and sleep properly during residency without intentional dieting. He now practices nightly meditation using an app, does resistance training including deadlifts under close supervision, and endorses 180–200 minutes of Zone 2 cardio per week plus at least six hard sets per muscle group per week as a minimum to offset deterioration. [02:09:53](transcript.md#020953-daily-habits-sleep-exercise-mediation)

## Tools & Protocols

### Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)

- **What:** Surgically implanted electrodes placed in deep brain targets (nucleus accumbens, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, thalamus) connected to a pacemaker-like implantable pulse generator (IPG) under the collarbone. The skull opening is approximately dime-sized; patients receive a scalp block and IV sedation during placement.
- **How:** Electrodes are placed stereotactically using MRI-guided coordinates. The IPG is programmed externally. In responsive/closed-loop DBS, onboard neural sensing detects biomarkers and triggers 5–10 seconds of stimulation automatically. Intraoperative CT confirms electrode accuracy to within 0.5 mm; tractography maps the nucleus accumbens–prefrontal cortex circuit to guide targeting.
- **When:** Indicated for OCD (FDA humanitarian device exemption), Parkinson's disease, essential tremor, dystonia, and — experimentally — binge eating disorder and anorexia nervosa. Used when medications and behavioral therapies have failed.
- **Ref:** [DBSLOC Clinical Trial — NCT03868670](https://www.clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/show/NCT03868670) | [Nature Medicine pilot study](https://go.nature.com/3Sc1ZA1) | [00:08:28](transcript.md#000828-neurosurgeons-view-of-the-brain-neurosurgery-specialization)

---

### Responsive/Closed-Loop DBS (for binge eating)

- **What:** A variant of DBS in which the device continuously records local field potentials from the nucleus accumbens, detects a craving-associated neural biomarker, and delivers a brief 5–10 second stimulation burst to interrupt the craving before the binge behavior begins. Stimulation may briefly elevate mood, disrupting the craving-to-binge cycle.
- **How:** Implanted sensing electrodes (~1 mm diameter) record local field potential activity from thousands of cells. Single-unit craving cells identified intraoperatively are correlated to population-level LFP signals analyzed offline. Machine learning algorithms trained on the patient's own data identify pre-binge firing patterns and trigger auto-stimulation.
- **When:** Experimental — NIH-funded, FDA-approved first-in-human trial at Penn (DBSLOC, 6 patients total). Being explored for OCD and addiction. Not yet FDA-approved for these indications.
- **Ref:** [Pilot study — Nature Medicine](https://go.nature.com/3Sc1ZA1) | [01:02:07](transcript.md#010207-electrodes-in-nucleus-accumbens-identifying-craving-cells)

---

### Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

- **What:** Non-invasive brain stimulation using a coil placed on the scalp that generates rapidly changing magnetic fields, inducing electric currents in the underlying cortex. Can excite or inhibit targeted brain regions depending on frequency and pattern of pulses.
- **How:** Patient sits in a chair; a coil is positioned over the target scalp location (over the frontal lobe for depression/OCD; different targets for addiction). Treatment sessions typically run 20–40 minutes, repeated daily for several weeks.
- **When:** FDA-cleared for major depression, OCD, and nicotine addiction. Being studied for eating disorders and other indications. Halpern's lab runs a parallel OCD TMS trial funded by the Foundation for OCD Research to identify circuits and screen DBS candidates.
- **Ref:** [FDA TMS clearances](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/neurological-devices/transcranial-magnetic-stimulation-tms-devices) | [01:23:14](transcript.md#012314-non-invasive-brain-stimulation-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation)

---

### MRI-Guided Focused Ultrasound (MRgFUS)

- **What:** Non-invasive ablative procedure that focuses ultrasound beams through the intact skull to converge on a precise deep brain target, generating heat that creates a small lesion — without surgery or implant. Researchers are also exploring modulatory (non-ablative) use and blood-brain barrier opening for targeted drug delivery.
- **How:** Patient lies in an MRI scanner wearing a transducer helmet. Ultrasound energy is focused on the VIM nucleus of the thalamus for tremor. Real-time MRI thermometry guides the procedure.
- **When:** FDA-cleared for essential tremor (unilateral thalamotomy) and Parkinson's disease tremor. Being investigated for OCD (capsulotomy target) and depression. Single-session procedure with immediate tremor relief in most patients.
- **Ref:** [Insightec Exablate Neuro — FDA clearance](https://www.insightec.com/clinical/essential-tremor/) | [01:32:27](transcript.md#013227-mri-guided-focused-ultrasound-tremor-essential-tremor--parkinsons)

---

### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) / Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)

- **What:** Structured psychotherapy targeting dysfunctional thought patterns and behaviors. For OCD: Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP), pioneered at Penn by Edna Foa. For eating disorders: structured behavioral management.
- **How:** Therapist-guided sessions expose patients to triggering stimuli while preventing compulsive responses, allowing the anxiety circuit to extinguish over time.
- **When:** First-line treatment for OCD and bulimia nervosa, often combined with SSRIs. DBS and TMS are considered after CBT + medication failure. Approximately 30% of OCD patients still suffer despite CBT and medication; CBT benefits may not persist after treatment ends.
- **Ref:** Standard psychiatric practice guidelines | [00:17:20](transcript.md#001720-obsessive-compulsive-disorder-ocd-prescriptions--cognitive-therapies)

---

### SSRIs and Tricyclics for OCD / Compulsive Behaviors

- **What:** SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, sertraline, fluvoxamine) and tricyclic antidepressants increase synaptic serotonin, dampening hyperactive orbitofrontal–striatal circuits driving OCD and compulsive eating. The serotonin system interacts with noradrenergic and dopaminergic systems, making prediction of response difficult.
- **How:** Daily oral medication, often at higher doses for OCD than for depression. Onset: several weeks. Response rate approximately 40–60% for OCD.
- **When:** First-line pharmacological treatment for OCD and bulimia nervosa. May be combined with CBT (synergistic). Neuromodulation is considered after inadequate response.
- **Ref:** Standard psychiatric practice | [00:17:20](transcript.md#001720-obsessive-compulsive-disorder-ocd-prescriptions--cognitive-therapies)
