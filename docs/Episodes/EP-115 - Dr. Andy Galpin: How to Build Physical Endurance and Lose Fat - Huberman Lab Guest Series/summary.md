# Episode 115: Dr. Andy Galpin: How to Build Physical Endurance & Lose Fat | Huberman Lab Guest Series

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Episode 3 of 6 in the Huberman Lab Guest Series on fitness, exercise, and performance with Dr. Andy Galpin. This 228-minute conversation covers the four categories of endurance — muscular endurance, anaerobic capacity, maximum aerobic output, and long-duration endurance — and provides specific training protocols for each. Galpin explains the biochemistry of fat loss, including the surprising fact that fat is primarily lost through exhalation as CO₂, and walks through the complete carbon-cycle framework unifying aerobic and anaerobic metabolism. The episode covers fuel source utilization across exercise durations (phosphocreatine → glycogen → fat), the true role of lactate as fuel and acid buffer rather than a fatigue agent, metabolic flexibility and how to assess and improve it, and the breathing "gear system" as a real-time training-zone calibration tool. A complete weekly training template integrating all four endurance categories is provided for both general fitness and goal-specific athletes such as half-marathon runners.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Endurance has two root factors: fatigue management and fueling** — All four categories of endurance (muscular endurance, anaerobic capacity, maximum aerobic output, long-duration) ultimately fail at either acid/waste buildup or energy depletion. Knowing which factor limits a given activity tells you exactly what to train. → [00:18:01](transcript.md#001801-endurance-categories)

2. **Fat is lost through exhalation, not sweat or urine** — Triglycerides are chains of carbon. When metabolized, carbon bonds are broken to release energy; the freed carbon is bound to oxygen and exhaled as CO₂. The only two ways to lose fat mass are to ingest less carbon or to expel more carbon. Exercise achieves the latter by dramatically increasing respiration rate. → [00:22:16](transcript.md#002216-fat-loss--respiration-carbon-cycles--storage-metabolism)

3. **"Carbon in, carbon out" is more precise than "calories in, calories out"** — A calorie is the energy released per broken carbon bond. Macro ratios (fat vs. carbohydrate) are largely irrelevant to net fat loss; total carbon intake and total carbon expiration are what matter. This is why high-carb, low-fat and high-fat, low-carb diets both produce fat loss when total intake is controlled. → [00:33:08](transcript.md#003308-exhalation-rates-exercise--fat-loss-calories)

4. **Exercising fasted does not enhance fat loss under normal conditions** — Muscle glycogen, liver glycogen, and blood glucose are sufficient for almost any typical training session even without breakfast. The body will not mobilize stored body fat simply because breakfast was skipped. Fasted training is a valid option but is not required for fat loss or fat adaptation. → [00:47:55](transcript.md#004755-excess-post-exercise-consumption-epoc-exercise-intensity--fat-vs-carbohydrate-energy-utilization)

5. **Burning fat during exercise does not equal losing body fat** — The highest percentage of fuel from fat occurs during sleep (~70%). Maximizing fat oxidation during a session does not maximize fat loss over time. High-intensity training burns mostly carbohydrates during the session but forces fat utilization during recovery as the body replenishes depleted glycogen stores in a caloric deficit. → [00:47:55](transcript.md#004755-excess-post-exercise-consumption-epoc-exercise-intensity--fat-vs-carbohydrate-energy-utilization)

6. **EPOC reflects carbon debt repayment, not a major fat-burning bonus** — High-intensity exercise drives the respiratory exchange ratio above 1.0, meaning CO₂ offload exceeds O₂ intake. After exercise ends, the body continues ventilating to repay that oxygen debt and clear accumulated waste. EPOC is real but smaller than once believed; its main value is confirming the session was genuinely high-intensity. → [00:47:55](transcript.md#004755-excess-post-exercise-consumption-epoc-exercise-intensity--fat-vs-carbohydrate-energy-utilization)

7. **Lactate is fuel and an acid buffer, not a cause of fatigue** — Lactate forms when pyruvate accepts a free hydrogen ion produced during anaerobic glycolysis, buffering pH. It is then shuttled to the heart, liver, non-working muscle fibers, and the brain where it is oxidized as fuel or converted back to glucose via the Cori cycle. Lactate also enhances cognitive performance — a primary mechanism behind pre-exam exercise improving test scores. → [01:50:45](transcript.md#015045-lactate-energy-production-buffer)

8. **Metabolic flexibility means excelling at both fat and carbohydrate utilization, not maximizing fat** — The gold standard is using the optimal fuel at the optimal time. Biasing too heavily toward fat adaptation actually downregulates enzymes for carbohydrate metabolism and limits anaerobic performance. Most people benefit from training across all intensity zones to develop both systems. → [01:08:01](transcript.md#010801-metabolic-flexibility-carbohydrates--fat-exercise--flexible-fuel-utilization)

9. **Protein contributes only 5–10% of fuel during exercise, and only during prolonged bouts** — Protein metabolism is entirely aerobic and energetically inefficient — Galpin's analogy: it is like burning metal to make a fire. The body strongly resists using protein as fuel because it is a high-value structural material with no meaningful storage form. → [02:12:49](transcript.md#021249-protein--fat-utilization-for-energy-exercise--fat-loss)

10. **Exercise snacks — 20-second all-out efforts — produce statistically significant VO₂ max improvements** — Running up a flight of stairs for ~20 seconds, repeated 2–3 times per workday, produces measurable gains in cardiorespiratory fitness after 3–6 weeks (18 total sessions). The same protocol acutely improves post-meal blood glucose control and insulin response. Mode of exercise is irrelevant; only the all-out exertion matters. → [00:07:30](transcript.md#000730-tool-exercise-snacks)

11. **Capillarization is the key muscular endurance adaptation** — Repeated near-failure contractions in a specific muscle group signal the body to grow more capillaries into that tissue, improving oxygen delivery and waste removal locally. This adaptation is entirely local — leg training does not capillarize arms. Exercise choice must precisely match the target muscle group. → [02:29:40](transcript.md#022940-muscular-endurance-fuel-sources-training--capillarization)

12. **The breathing gear system maps directly to training zones and guides interval recovery** — Gear 1 (nasal in / nasal out) = Zone 1–2; Gear 2 (nasal in / mouth out) = Zone 3–4; Gear 3–4 (mouth in / mouth out) = Zone 5+. For anaerobic intervals: recover to pure nasal breathing, then wait 30 more seconds before the next round. Zone distinctions (1–5) are largely arbitrary; gear transitions are tied directly to physiology. → [02:49:23](transcript.md#024923-tool-cardiac-output-heart-rate-zones--breathing-gear-system)

13. **Resting heart rate decreases with endurance training via stroke volume increase, not max HR increase** — Cardiac output (heart rate × stroke volume) stays constant to match energy demand. As stroke volume improves, fewer beats per minute are needed. Target resting HR is below 60 bpm. Maximum heart rate does not meaningfully increase with fitness and is not a useful fitness proxy. → [00:41:47](transcript.md#004147-cardiovascular-adaptations-cardiac-output--maximum-heart-rate)

14. **The Sugarcane protocol simultaneously tests and trains anaerobic capacity and pacing** — Round 1: all-out 2 minutes, note distance. Rest 2 minutes. Round 2: cover the same distance, note time elapsed. Rest 2 minutes. Round 3: sprint for the same duration as Round 2, try to beat Round 1 distance. The structure punishes both overconfidence and sandbagging. Attributed to trainer Kenny Kane. → [03:11:45](transcript.md#031145-tool-sugarcane-endurance-protocol)

15. **Low-carbohydrate diets impair anaerobic performance by downregulating glycolytic enzymes** — Fat-adapted individuals get better at using fat but worse at using carbohydrates. For any sport or activity with anaerobic demands, this is a meaningful performance penalty. Low-carb diets are well-suited for sedentary individuals or those doing exclusively low-intensity long-duration work. → [02:26:39](transcript.md#022639-low-carbohydrate-diet--performance)

16. **A complete weekly endurance program requires roughly 2 total hours across all four categories** — Muscular endurance (high-rep near-failure sets), anaerobic capacity (~5–6 min total all-out work per week), maximum aerobic output (5–15 min efforts, 1–2x/week), and long-duration Zone 2 (60–120 min/week). This combination covers fat loss, metabolic flexibility, VO₂ max, longevity, and functional fitness while remaining compatible with a full strength and hypertrophy program. → [03:29:10](transcript.md#032910-weekly-combination-training-metabolic-flexibility--longevity)

## Tools & Protocols

### Exercise Snacks
- **What:** Brief all-out efforts of ~20 seconds to spike heart rate, improve cardiorespiratory fitness, and acutely lower post-meal blood glucose
- **How:** Sprint up a flight of stairs (~60 steps), do burpees, sprint down a hallway, or use an assault bike for 20–30 seconds — any maximal exertion modality; 2–3 times per workday, 3+ days per week; no warm-up needed, no shower required
- **When:** Throughout the workday (morning arrival, midday, before leaving); particularly effective immediately after high-glycemic meals; also valid during breaks between video meetings
- **Ref:** [00:07:30](transcript.md#000730-tool-exercise-snacks)

### Training for Fat Loss (Glycogen Depletion Strategy)
- **What:** Sequence and manage training to force fat mobilization — the key driver is caloric deficit, not fat burning during the session itself
- **How:** Hypertrophy-range resistance training (6–30 reps, moderate rest) depletes muscle glycogen; any carbohydrate eaten afterward replenishes those stores; fat from the diet or stored fat covers the remaining caloric deficit; adding brief high-intensity intervals after weights further depletes glycogen; fasted training is optional, not required
- **When:** Any training session; fat loss occurs when total daily caloric intake is below expenditure regardless of session timing or fuel burned during the workout
- **Ref:** [00:59:35](transcript.md#005935-tool-training-for-fat-loss-carbohydrate-stores-liver-glycogen--fatigue)

### Muscular Endurance Training
- **What:** High-repetition, lower-load resistance training targeting local capillarization and acid buffering in a specific muscle group
- **How:** 3–4 sets per movement; 20–40+ reps to near-failure or failure; 60–90 seconds rest between sets; 2–3 sessions per week per muscle group; progress by adding 1–2 reps per week; when hitting a plateau, drop to ~80–85% of max reps and accumulate practice volume; exercise choice must precisely match the target muscle group
- **When:** After strength or hypertrophy work, not before intervals; low soreness allows 3–4 sessions/week; viable on rest days as active recovery
- **Ref:** [02:37:30](transcript.md#023730-tool-muscular-endurance--modifiable-variables-examples)

### Anaerobic Capacity Intervals
- **What:** Near-maximal efforts of 20–90 seconds targeting total anaerobic work capacity, lactate clearance, and acid buffering
- **How:** 4–8 rounds of 20–90 seconds at 90–100% max effort; rest ratio 1:1 to 1:3 (work:rest); shorter intervals (20s) benefit from longer rest (1:2 or 1:3) to ensure max quality; recover to nasal-only breathing before next round, then wait 30 additional seconds; total work target ~5–6 minutes per week; pick low-eccentric-load exercises (assault bike, rower, hill sprints, swimming); avoid box jumps and heavy barbell movements
- **When:** After full warm-up; not on same day as maximum aerobic output sessions; 1–2 sessions per week; more than 4 days/week risks endocrine and sleep disruption
- **Ref:** [02:58:10](transcript.md#025810-tool-anerobic-capacity--modifiable-variables-examples-nasal-recovery)

### "Sugarcane" Protocol
- **What:** A self-testing anaerobic capacity protocol that simultaneously trains pacing, maximal output, and repeat-effort capacity (attributed to trainer Kenny Kane)
- **How:** Round 1 — go all-out for 2 minutes, note distance covered. Rest exactly 2 minutes. Round 2 — cover the same distance, note time elapsed (will be slightly longer). Rest 2 minutes. Round 3 — sprint for the same duration as Round 2, attempt to exceed Round 1 distance. Can be performed on any cardio machine or running surface. The structure punishes overconfidence and sandbagging equally
- **When:** Use weekly or monthly as a benchmark; also functions as a full anaerobic capacity training session
- **Ref:** [03:11:45](transcript.md#031145-tool-sugarcane-endurance-protocol)

### Maximum Aerobic Output Training (VO₂ Max Intervals)
- **What:** Sustained high-intensity intervals targeting maximum oxygen utilization and aerobic capacity
- **How:** 1–3 efforts of 5–15 minutes (a one-mile time trial is a practical entry point); effort at ~90–95% max; rest equal to or slightly longer than work duration; supplement with 40% of total session time at 70–85% heart rate (below conversation pace, above Zone 2) to build oxygen transport without full system stress; 1–2 sessions per week
- **When:** Standalone day preferred; after power or speed work is acceptable; avoid after leg-dominant strength/hypertrophy sessions; do not combine with anaerobic capacity work in the same session
- **Ref:** [03:16:40](transcript.md#031640-tool-maximum-aerobic-output-training--modifiable-variables)

### Breathing "Gear System" for Training Zone Calibration
- **What:** A real-time breathing-based framework (credit: Brian McKenzie / SHIFT Adapt) to verify training zone and guide interval recovery
- **How:**
  - Gear 1 (Zone 1–2): Nasal in, nasal out at a set cadence (2–3 sec inhale / 2–3 sec exhale) — fully conversational; efficient fat-burning pace
  - Gear 2 (Zone 3–4): Nasal in, mouth out at whatever rate is needed — can speak in short phrases
  - Gear 3–4 (Zone 5+): Mouth in, mouth out — cannot speak; appropriate for competition and true max-effort intervals
  - Anaerobic interval recovery: return to nasal-only breathing; wait 30 additional seconds before next round
  - CO₂ tolerance can be tested at the SHIFT Adapt website to assess sensitivity to CO₂ elevation
- **When:** Apply during every training session; the gear you are in determines which energy system is being trained
- **Ref:** [02:49:23](transcript.md#024923-tool-cardiac-output-heart-rate-zones--breathing-gear-system)

### Long-Duration Zone 2 Training
- **What:** Sustained moderate aerobic exercise to build cardiac stroke volume, capillarization, fat-burning infrastructure, and respiratory muscle endurance
- **How:** 60–120 minutes per week at Zone 2 intensity (Gear 1: nasal in / nasal out; fully conversational); modes include running, cycling, hiking, swimming, rowing; weight-vest hiking (8–10 lbs) is an effective low-impact option; circuit alternatives (farmer's carries → plank → bodyweight squats → jump rope) count if rest is minimal; can be split into 2 × 30–45 min sessions; nasal-only breathing during Zone 2 deepens post-session down-regulation
- **When:** Standalone session, active recovery day, or after intervals as a structured cool-down; do before intervals only if Zone 2 is the primary goal that day
- **Ref:** [03:21:58](transcript.md#032158-tool-long-duration-endurance-training-circuits)

### Mixed Endurance Weekly Training Template (Half Marathon Example)
- **What:** A full weekly program integrating all four endurance categories for goal-oriented endurance athletes
- **How:**
  - 60–70% of total volume: moderate-intensity mileage in the target sport (Zone 2 to tempo pace) for tissue tolerance and motor skill development
  - ~10%: maximum speed / 20-second burst intervals
  - ~10–15%: maximum aerobic output intervals (e.g., 800m repeats with double-time rest)
  - ~10%: muscular endurance work (planks, wall sits, push-ups to failure) for posture and local fatigue resistance
  - Sample 4-day split: Day 1 — long Zone 2 run (45–60 min); Day 2 — tempo accumulation + muscular endurance; Day 3 — anaerobic capacity (20s bursts or 60s repeats); Day 4 — maximum aerobic output (mile or 800m repeats)
  - Common mistake: doing only long slow distance and linearly adding mileage — suboptimal without top-end and speed-end work
- **When:** Adjust volume based on current fitness; scale up one variable at a time (duration, intensity, or frequency)
- **Ref:** [03:37:23](transcript.md#033723-tool-mixed-endurance-training-half-marathon-example)
