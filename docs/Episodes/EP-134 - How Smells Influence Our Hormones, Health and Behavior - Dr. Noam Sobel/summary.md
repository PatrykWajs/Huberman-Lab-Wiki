# Episode 134: How Smells Influence Our Hormones, Health & Behavior | Dr. Noam Sobel

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Noam Sobel, professor of neurobiology in the Department of Brain Sciences at the Weizmann Institute of Science. Dr. Sobel's laboratory studies olfaction and chemosensation — the full range of chemical sensing beyond conscious smell. The episode covers how humans use smell to navigate the social world, communicate emotions and hormone states, synchronize biological rhythms, track odor trails, and how loss of smell signals neurodegeneration. Dr. Sobel also discusses his lab's work on digitizing smell and its implications for remote medical diagnostics. This is one of the most wide-ranging explorations of human olfaction in the podcast's history.

## Guest: Dr. Noam Sobel

Dr. Noam Sobel is Professor of Neurobiology at the Weizmann Institute of Science (Israel), where he leads the **Weizmann Olfaction Research Group (WORG)**. He was formerly a professor at UC Berkeley, where his lab conducted landmark experiments on human odor tracking. His research spans olfactory neuroscience, social chemosignaling, pheromone biology, olfactory diagnostics, and the computational digitization of smell. He is known for discovering that humans sniff their own hands after handshakes, that tears contain testosterone-suppressing chemosignals, and that hexadecanal modulates aggression in sexually dimorphic ways.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Humans are better at smell than commonly believed** — Humans can follow buried scent trails on hands and knees with high fidelity, comparable to dogs in some conditions. The claim that bloodhounds have "a billion" olfactory receptors was traced back to a fabricated number originating from an offhand lecture remark by geneticist Doron Lancet, with no scientific basis. Humans have ~7 million receptors of ~350 subtypes. Two nostrils confer a measurable advantage over one centralized nostril for odor tracking — analogous to stereo hearing for sound localization. ([00:06:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=406s)(transcript.md#000646-olfaction-circuits-smell))

2. **The nasal cycle reflects autonomic nervous system state** — Each nostril alternates between high- and low-airflow roughly every 2.5 hours. The high-flow nostril corresponds to a contralateral shift in autonomic tone. Right nostril dominance correlates with higher alertness/sympathetic drive; left nostril with calmer/parasympathetic state. Voluntary forced nasal breathing can shift this balance. The Sobel lab built a "nasal halter" wearable that logs 24-hour nostril airflow and can distinguish ADHD adults from non-ADHD adults, and detect whether they are on Ritalin — entirely from the nasal airflow record. ([00:39:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=2365s)(transcript.md#003925-the-alternating-nasal-cycle--autonomic-nervous-system))

3. **Breathing phase gates cognition** — Inhalation vs. exhalation phase-locks to neural oscillations across widespread brain regions. Memory encoding, fear processing, and attention are enhanced during the inhalation phase. The effect is stronger with nasal than oral breathing, though a weaker version exists with mouth breathing. A study by Off Perl from the Sobel lab showed significantly better visual-spatial performance on nasal inhalation versus exhalation, using a task with no olfactory component. ([00:48:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=2898s)(transcript.md#004818-cognitive-processing--breathing))

4. **Handshaking is covert chemosensory sampling** — After a handshake, people unconsciously bring their own hand to their nose at dramatically elevated frequency (~22% of time vs. ~8% baseline), filmed across multiple cultures with hidden cameras. Same-sex handshakes route the shaking hand to the nose; opposite-sex handshakes route the other hand. People actively sniff during the hand-to-nose gesture (confirmed by nasal airflow sensors). A watch device on the experimenter's hand that emitted pleasant or unpleasant odors drove the post-handshake self-sniffing up or down, confirming this is olfactory behavior. ([01:06:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=3979s)(transcript.md#010619-handshaking-sharing-chemicals--social-sensing))

5. **"Click" friendships are predicted by shared body odor** — Study by Inbal Ravreby in the Sobel lab showed that people who report instant friendship have significantly more similar body odor profiles than non-click pairs, matched for visual similarity and demographics. In a follow-up using total strangers and a round-robin non-verbal mirror game, body odor similarity measured by an electronic nose predicted post-interaction friendship ratings — suggesting odor similarity is causal in initiating bonds, not merely a result of them. ([01:15:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=4507s)(transcript.md#011507-smelling-ourselves--smelling-others))

6. **The Bruce Effect: male odor can trigger miscarriage in mice** — In mice, exposure to a novel male's urine after mating triggers spontaneous miscarriage in ~80% of cases, mediated by the vomeronasal organ (VNO). Humans likely lack a functional VNO, but Sobel's lab found that women experiencing unexplained repeated pregnancy loss can identify their spouse's body odor at more than double the accuracy of control women, and show altered hippocampal fMRI responses to stranger male body odor — suggesting an analogous chemosignal-mediated mechanism via the main olfactory epithelium. A causal blocking experiment in pregnant couples is currently underway. ([01:24:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=5098s)(transcript.md#012458-vomeronasal-organ-bruce-effect--miscarriage))

7. **Fear is chemically transmitted via sweat** — Sweat collected from people watching horror films or from first-time skydivers with Israeli paratroopers produces measurably different behavioral and neural responses in recipients compared to exercise sweat. Fear-sweat amplifies the perception of fear in ambiguous facial expressions, distinct from general arousal effects. The Sobel lab is building a "fear bank" of 1,000+ samples to identify the specific fear molecules and potentially develop nasal spray–based anxiety blockers targeting olfactory receptors at the source. ([01:40:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=6020s)(transcript.md#014020-social-chemo-signals-fear))

8. **Hexadecanal (HEX) modulates aggression sex-dimorphically** — HEX is the most abundant semivolatile in baby head odor. In men, HEX suppresses aggressive responses to provocation (Tyler Aggression Paradigm). In women, HEX increases aggressive responses. fMRI showed HEX activates the left angular gyrus (social appraisal) in both sexes, but functional connectivity from that region flows toward aggression-control circuits in men and aggression-release circuits in women. This sex-dimorphic pattern benefits infants: suppressing male aggression and enhancing maternal protectiveness simultaneously. ([01:50:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=6626s)(transcript.md#015026-chemo-signaling-aggression--offspring))

9. **Odor pleasantness is universal, not subjective** — Cross-person correlation in pleasantness ratings of odorants is approximately r = 0.8. Humans agree on ~90 of 100 odors tested. The impression of smell being "totally subjective" arises from a few genuine outliers (cilantro, guava) and from the absence of a shared odor vocabulary — unlike color or sound, children are never given language anchors for smells. When similarity matrices (not names) are compared across individuals, humans are no more variable in olfactory perception than in auditory perception, and significantly less variable than in color vision. ([02:38:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=9534s)(transcript.md#023854-human-perception--odorant-similarity))

10. **Menstrual synchrony is contested science** — The McClintock 1971 Nature paper on cycle synchronization among co-housed women has not replicated consistently. A 1998 Nature follow-up by Stern and McClintock applied donor sweat to recipient upper lips and found phase-dependent cycle-shortening and -lengthening effects. Sobel remains personally on the fence while noting most of the field is currently negative on the effect; statistics of cyclic events are notoriously complex. The Sobel lab intends to run a formal replication. ([02:03:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=7437s)(transcript.md#020357-menstrual-cycle-synchronization))

11. **Tears contain a chemosignal that lowers testosterone** — Emotional tears from women, placed covertly below the nose of men, lowered salivary free testosterone ~14% within 20–30 minutes, reduced self-reported sexual arousal, and reduced aggressive behavior in a social economic game. The testosterone effect was replicated independently by a South Korean group. Attempts to collect "tears of joy" from laughter failed — effective criers produce ~1 mL per 15 minutes from sad film stimuli; laughter produced no comparable volume. Separately, dog tears at reunion with owners were found to mediate oxytocin effects in human observers. ([02:12:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=7931s)(transcript.md#021211-sweat-tears-emotions--testosterone))

12. **Loss of smell is an early biomarker for neurodegeneration** — Parkinson's disease manifests olfactory loss approximately 10 years before any other symptom. Alzheimer's disease also shows early olfactory loss; one theory proposes the pathogen enters via the olfactory route. Congenital anosmia (0.5% of the population) goes undiagnosed until an average age of 14 and is associated with shorter lifespan, reduced social contacts, and hormonal deficits (Kallmann syndrome in men). Clinical smell tests (UPSIT scratch-and-sniff; Sniffin' Sticks) exist but are not yet routine at medical visits. ([00:54:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=3287s)(transcript.md#005447-neurodegenerative-diseases--olfaction))

13. **Smell can be digitized** — Sobel's lab built an algorithmic framework predicting the perceptual similarity of any two molecular mixtures from their chemistry, enabling the design of olfactory metamers — odor mixtures with zero molecular overlap that smell identical (including a Chanel No. 5 metamer). The first odor transmitted over IP was violets, recreated from atmospheric chemistry measurements sent from Germany. Osmo (a Google spinout, not a Sobel spinout) is the best-funded commercial effort. Near-term application: a validated COVID-19 olfactory self-screening test published in Nature Medicine. Long-term vision: a digital "electronic nose" in the bathroom capable of continuous disease diagnostics from metabolic products of illness. ([02:52:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=10332s)(transcript.md#025212-digitizing-smell-covid-19--smell))

## Tools & Protocols

### Olfactory Training After Smell Loss

- **What:** Daily structured exposure to multiple distinct odors to stimulate olfactory sensory neuron activity and promote neural regeneration.
- **How:** Select 4–6 distinct odors available at home (coffee, rose, lemon, eucalyptus, clove, vanilla, etc.). Sniff each intentionally and repeatedly, 2–3 times per day. No expensive kits required — items from kitchen, refrigerator, or medicine cabinet are sufficient.
- **When:** Begin as early as possible after smell loss (viral, traumatic, or other). Most effective in the first weeks to months. If no recovery within 12–18 months, prognosis for recovery diminishes significantly.
- **Ref:** [00:14:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=889s)(transcript.md#001449-loss--regeneration-of-smell-illness) — Sobel: "very strong evidence for success of training programs" over pharmacological approaches.

---

### Nasal Breathing Alternation to Shift Autonomic State

- **What:** Deliberate unilateral nostril breathing to shift autonomic nervous system balance.
- **How:** Close one nostril with a finger. Right-nostril breathing activates sympathetic tone (alertness). Left-nostril breathing activates parasympathetic tone (calm). Breathe for several minutes through the open nostril.
- **When:** Before tasks requiring high alertness (right nostril); before sleep, meditation, or calm focus (left nostril). Can also be used to identify current nasal cycle phase.
- **Ref:** [00:39:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=2365s)(transcript.md#003925-the-alternating-nasal-cycle--autonomic-nervous-system) — Sobel describes the nasal cycle and its autonomic correlates.

---

### Nasal Breathing During Memory Encoding

- **What:** Preferential nasal inhalation during learning or emotionally significant experiences to enhance neural processing.
- **How:** Maintain nasal breathing during study, exercise, or high-stakes situations. The inhalation moment is the point of sharpest cognitive processing. Effect is present but weaker with mouth breathing.
- **When:** Any situation requiring optimal attention or memory formation.
- **Ref:** [00:48:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=2898s)(transcript.md#004818-cognitive-processing--breathing) — Human non-olfactory cognition phase-locked to nasal inhalation; visual-spatial task performance significantly better on inhalation vs. exhalation.

---

### Olfactory Self-Test for COVID-19 / Viral Screening

- **What:** A brief smell test using commonly available odors to assess olfactory function as a rapid viral infection screen.
- **How:** Sobel's lab validated an online/app-based olfactory self-test. Users detect and identify a series of odors; significant impairment flags potential viral infection.
- **When:** At symptom onset or as a pre-symptomatic screen in high-exposure settings.
- **Ref:** [02:52:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=10332s)(transcript.md#025212-digitizing-smell-covid-19--smell) — Paper: "An olfactory self-test effectively screens for COVID-19": https://go.nature.com/3Vj6z1S

---

### Nasal Halter Monitoring (Research / Clinical Tool)

- **What:** A wearable device that logs 24-hour airflow from each nostril separately, serving as a continuous readout of autonomic nervous system balance.
- **How:** Device is affixed to the face and records each nostril independently. Data patterns differentiate ADHD from non-ADHD and detect Ritalin use from nasal airflow record alone.
- **When:** Research context currently; potential future diagnostic application at clinical visits or as a continuous wearable biomarker.
- **Ref:** [00:39:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EOstSv7SrRU&t=2365s)(transcript.md#003925-the-alternating-nasal-cycle--autonomic-nervous-system) — Sobel describes the nasal halter and ADHD discrimination data.

- **UPSIT (University of Pennsylvania Smell Identification Test)** — 40-item scratch-and-sniff forced-choice test developed by Richard Doty at Penn. Normed on large populations; reduced scores associated with Alzheimer's and Parkinson's.
- **Sniffin' Sticks** — European equivalent; pen-style odor delivery system developed by Thomas Hummel at University of Dresden.
