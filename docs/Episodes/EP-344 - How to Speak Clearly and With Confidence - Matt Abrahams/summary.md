# Episode 344: How to Speak Clearly & With Confidence | Matt Abrahams

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Matt Abrahams, lecturer at Stanford Graduate School of Business and author of *Think Faster, Talk Smarter*, joins Andrew Huberman to discuss how to become a better communicator across every context — public speaking, one-on-one conversations, spontaneous interactions, and planned presentations. The episode covers the evolutionary basis of communication fear, practical tools to eliminate filler words, frameworks for structuring information, how to recover when you blank out on stage, breathwork for managing nerves, and how to develop authentic speaking style. Abrahams draws on decades of teaching communication at Stanford and his background in improvisational theatre to provide immediately actionable protocols.

## Key Takeaways

- **Public speaking fear has an evolutionary basis.** In groups of roughly 150 people, status determined access to resources, food, shelter, and reproduction. Anything that puts status at risk — speaking in front of a crowd, talking to a boss about an important issue — triggers that deeply ingrained threat response. [00:03:21](transcript.md#000321-public-speaking-fear-status-speech-delivery)

- **Demonstrate credibility — do not declare it.** There are two types: resume credibility (titles, LinkedIn) and Costco credibility (free samples people can try right now — the questions you ask, the relevance you bring). Opening with credentials kills engagement; opening with a hook earns credibility through demonstration. [00:05:36](transcript.md#000536-speech-connection-credibility-authenticity)

- **Authenticity means understanding what you stand for, then articulating it clearly.** It requires introspection first. Many speakers are so worried about getting through their material that they never connect to a firm, clear, personal place from which to speak. [00:05:36](transcript.md#000536-speech-connection-credibility-authenticity)

- **Self-monitoring is the primary enemy of effective communication.** The more cognitive bandwidth spent judging your own performance in real time, the less available for actually connecting with the audience. Memorization compounds this by constantly creating a gap between "what I planned to say" and "what I am saying." [00:09:05](transcript.md#000905-monitoring-self-judgement-memorization-tool-object-relabeling-exercise)

- **Audience needs, not speaker intentions, define communication success.** Most people define success as "I got the information out." Real success is whether the audience understood it and can do something with it. The F-word of communication is fidelity — accuracy of transmission from speaker to listener. [00:23:19](transcript.md#002319-understanding-audiences-needs-tool-recon--reflection--research)

- **Structure beats lists every time.** Brains are wired for story — beginning, middle, end — not random bullet points. Problem-Solution-Benefit, What-So What-Now What, and Past-Present-Future are all story structures that dramatically improve comprehension and retention. [00:27:33](transcript.md#002733-questions-responding-to-the-audience-tool-structuring-information)

- **The tour guide mindset is the clearest model for any speaker.** Set expectations upfront (where are we going), signal transitions (we're moving from X to Y), and leave the audience with something to take away. Linear scaffolding is best for education; meanders are fine as long as the directional frame is clear. [00:50:32](transcript.md#005032-linear-vs-non-linear-speech-tool-tour-guide-expectations)

- **Violating audience expectations strategically captures attention.** Opening with something that breaks the expected pattern — an unusual prop, a counterintuitive statement, a question, or a physical demonstration — fires the orienting response and focuses attention immediately. Risk should be calculated; AB-test ideas with a few people first. [01:16:30](transcript.md#011630-calculating-risk-tool-violating-expectations--engaging-audience)

- **Physical movement during speaking dissipates autonomic arousal.** When energy is high, movement (pacing, gesturing) converts excess sympathetic activation into purposeful expression. Move during transitions and setup; stand still for the punchline. [00:39:09](transcript.md#003909-movement-stage-fright-content-expertise)

- **Filler words are not inherently bad — overuse is the problem.** Filler words hold conversational space and signal new ideas are coming. The mechanism to reduce distracting fillers is Jerry Weissman's landing phrase technique: exhale fully at the end of each phrase so you must inhale before speaking again — making "um" physically impossible during that pause. [02:13:19](transcript.md#021319-omit-filler-words-tool-landing-phrases-time--storytelling)

- **Improv is about being present and responsive, not being funny.** The "Yes, and..." principle and single-object improv exercises build spontaneity and agility. Anyone who takes improv — or at minimum understands its principles — becomes more comfortable with the discomfort of not knowing what comes next. [01:01:43](transcript.md#010143-tool-improv--agility-great-communication-examples-divided-attention)

- **Distancing via third-person self-reference reduces anxiety under pressure.** Mentally referring to yourself by name (rather than "I") creates psychological distance that lowers emotional reactivity and improves performance. Research by Ethan Kross at the University of Michigan supports this. [00:53:21](transcript.md#005321-develop-communication-skills-audience-size-tools-distancing-practicing)

- **Improving at communication requires repetition, reflection, and feedback.** Recording yourself and watching it three separate times — sound only, video only, then both together — reveals things you cannot perceive while performing. A nightly one-minute reflection journal accelerates improvement faster than practice alone. [00:31:34](transcript.md#003134-feedback--observation-tools-three-pass-speech-review-communication-reflection-journal)

- **Communication hygiene the night before is a training input, not a luxury.** Sleep, nutrition, and exercise before a major presentation should be treated the same as an athlete preparing for competition. Caffeine should not deviate from your usual protocol on presentation day. [01:34:29](transcript.md#013429-communication-hygiene-caffeine-tools-nsdryoga-nidra-vestibular-system--sleep)

- **Pre-talk anxiety has both symptom management and source management.** Symptoms (heart rate, sweating, blushing) can be modulated with breathwork and cooling the palms. Sources (fear of negative outcomes) require becoming present-oriented — through deep breathing, conversation, tongue twisters, or counting backwards from 100 by 17s. [02:19:49](transcript.md#021949-pre-talk-anxiety-management-acknowledgements)

- **Context affects how requests are received.** Asking for a raise requires reading situational context — time of day, the other person's stress load, recent events — as much as crafting the right words. Research on parole board decisions shows judges rule differently before versus after lunch on identical cases. [02:16:52](transcript.md#021652-asking-for-a-raise-poor-communicators--curiosity-memorization)

## Tools & Protocols

### Tool: Object Relabeling Exercise
- **What:** Point at objects in the room and call each one something it is not, for 15 seconds, without pausing to judge yourself.
- **How:** Stand up, point at any object (ceiling, floor, mug), and immediately say a different name. Move quickly from object to object. Children are naturally good at this; adults must overcome the inner critic.
- **When:** Daily or before any high-stakes interaction to disrupt self-judgment and build spontaneity.
- **Ref:** [00:09:05](transcript.md#000905-monitoring-self-judgement-memorization-tool-object-relabeling-exercise)

### Tool: Recon – Reflection – Research (3 Rs for Audience Analysis)
- **What:** A pre-communication framework to shift orientation from "what I want to say" to "what they need to hear."
- **How:** (1) **Recon** — what do you already know about this audience? (2) **Reflection** — what do you think they need from this interaction? (3) **Research** — what can you find out about them beforehand?
- **When:** Before any significant communication event — a talk, pitch, meeting, or difficult conversation.
- **Ref:** [00:23:19](transcript.md#002319-understanding-audiences-needs-tool-recon--reflection--research)

### Tool: Structuring Information (Answer – Detail – Example)
- **What:** A three-part structure for responding to questions or making points: give the headline first, then detail, then a concrete example.
- **How:** State your main point immediately. Elaborate with supporting information. Ground it in a specific instance. Variations: Problem-Solution-Benefit; What-So What-Now What; Past-Present-Future.
- **When:** Any time a response risks becoming an unstructured list.
- **Ref:** [00:27:33](transcript.md#002733-questions-responding-to-the-audience-tool-structuring-information)

### Tool: Self-Recording with Three Sensory Passes
- **What:** Record yourself on video and watch it three separate times, each time with a different sensory focus.
- **How:** (1) Sound only, no video — listen to your words, pacing, filler words. (2) Video only, no sound — observe body language, gestures, eye contact. (3) Both together — integrate what you see and hear. Note positives and areas to improve each time.
- **When:** After any presentation, meeting simulation, or practice session.
- **Ref:** [00:31:34](transcript.md#003134-feedback--observation-tools-three-pass-speech-review-communication-reflection-journal)

### Tool: Communication Reflection Journal
- **What:** A nightly micro-journal to convert communication experience into deliberate practice.
- **How:** Each night, spend one minute writing what went well and what did not go well in your communication that day. Every Sunday, spend five minutes reviewing the week's entries and make one concrete plan for the following week.
- **When:** Daily. Abrahams has done this for over 15 years.
- **Ref:** [00:31:34](transcript.md#003134-feedback--observation-tools-three-pass-speech-review-communication-reflection-journal)

### Tool: Tour Guide Expectations (Signposting)
- **What:** Adopt the mindset of a tour guide — setting expectations, narrating transitions, and gifting the audience a takeaway.
- **How:** At the start, tell people where you are going. At each transition, signal the move ("Now that we've covered X, let's turn to Y"). At the end, give the audience something concrete to take away. Allow detours along the way as long as the directional frame is clear.
- **When:** Any structured presentation, meeting, or educational conversation.
- **Ref:** [00:50:32](transcript.md#005032-linear-vs-non-linear-speech-tool-tour-guide-expectations)

### Tool: Distancing (Third-Person Self-Reference)
- **What:** When anxious before or during a talk, mentally refer to yourself by name rather than "I."
- **How:** Ask: "What would [your name] do here?" Research by Ethan Kross shows this reduces emotional reactivity and improves performance under pressure.
- **When:** Pre-talk nerves, moments of self-doubt during a talk, or high-stakes interpersonal situations.
- **Ref:** [00:53:21](transcript.md#005321-develop-communication-skills-audience-size-tools-distancing-practicing)

### Tool: Practicing with Progressive Audience Sizes
- **What:** Build speaking comfort by systematically scaling the audience.
- **How:** Practice solo (mirror or recording) → one trusted person → small group (3–5) → room-size (10–20) → large audience. Each step desensitizes the anxiety response before the next escalation.
- **When:** When preparing for any high-stakes presentation or when building speaking skill from scratch.
- **Ref:** [00:53:21](transcript.md#005321-develop-communication-skills-audience-size-tools-distancing-practicing)

### Tool: Improv for Agility
- **What:** Single-object improv exercise to build spontaneous speaking confidence.
- **How:** Look at any random object and speak about it for 60–90 seconds without preparation. Rotate through: describe it, tell a story about it, explain what it represents. Alternatively, open a book, pick a random word, and speak about what it means.
- **When:** Daily or weekly as a communication drill. Also useful immediately before any high-stakes event as a vocal warm-up.
- **Ref:** [01:01:43](transcript.md#010143-tool-improv--agility-great-communication-examples-divided-attention)

### Tool: Violating Expectations to Engage Audiences
- **What:** Open a talk with something that breaks the expected pattern to fire the orienting response.
- **How:** Use an unusual prop, a counterintuitive statement, a question that reveals a gap in the audience's knowledge, or a physical or sensory demonstration. AB-test the idea with a few people before the talk. Make sure the violation supports — not undermines — the goal.
- **When:** At the start of any presentation where engagement from the first moment is critical.
- **Ref:** [01:16:30](transcript.md#011630-calculating-risk-tool-violating-expectations--engaging-audience)

### Tool: Introduce Yourself Strategically
- **What:** A non-standard self-introduction that leads with passion rather than name.
- **How:** Do not open with your name. Start with something you care about, are passionate about, or something observed in the shared environment. Then say your name. Benefits: you stand out, you can use vocal emotion naturally, and non-native speakers prime the room to understand their speech pattern before hearing their name.
- **When:** Any round-table introduction, networking event, or meeting where introductions are expected.
- **Ref:** [01:44:43](transcript.md#014443-anticipation-tool-introduce-yourself-connect-to-environment-phones)

### Tool: Role Model Communication
- **What:** Deliberately study 2–3 communicators you admire and systematically adopt one element at a time.
- **How:** Identify what specifically they do — pacing, pauses, handling questions, posture, transitions. Adopt one behavior consciously over the course of weeks. Sources can include people in completely different fields — kindergarten teachers, stand-up comedians, athletes.
- **When:** Ongoing. Incorporate into post-event reflection practice.
- **Ref:** [01:51:30](transcript.md#015130-customer-service--kids-jobs-tool-role-model-communication-covid-pandemic)

### Tool: Contingency Planning (Avoid Blanking Out)
- **What:** A pre-planned recovery system for the most common on-stage failures.
- **How:** (1) Do not memorize — use a structure/road map instead. (2) Rationalize: blank-out probability is around 20%, not 100%. (3) If you do blank out, retrace your steps — repeat the last thing you said. (4) If that fails, ask the audience a question to buy 10–15 seconds while your brain retrieves. Prepare bridge phrases in advance such as "I get so passionate sometimes I get a little ahead of myself." Practice them before the talk.
- **When:** In preparation for any high-stakes talk; use the recovery steps in real time as needed.
- **Ref:** [01:23:23](transcript.md#012323-damage-control-tools-avoid-blanking-out-contingency-planning-silence)

### Tool: Landing Phrases (Eliminating Filler Words)
- **What:** A breath-based technique developed by Jerry Weissman to eliminate filler words in the silences between phrases.
- **How:** At the end of each sentence or phrase, exhale completely — be fully out of breath. You must then inhale before speaking, which makes "um" physically impossible during that pause. Practice by reading your daily calendar aloud once per day, landing each entry fully before moving to the next.
- **When:** Practice daily. Apply in any speaking context where filler words are a problem.
- **Ref:** [02:13:19](transcript.md#021319-omit-filler-words-tool-landing-phrases-time--storytelling)

### Tool: Breathwork for Pre-Talk Nerves
- **What:** Exhale-emphasized breathing to activate the parasympathetic nervous system and reduce heart rate.
- **How:** The physiological sigh (double inhale through the nose followed by a long, slow exhale) is the fastest way to calm down acutely. For ongoing management: emphasize any exhale — make it longer and slightly more vigorous than the inhale. The vagus nerve pathway that slows heart rate activates specifically on exhale. Do this covertly by walking back to the podium with a long exhale.
- **When:** In the minutes before going on stage; covertly during a talk when nerves spike.
- **Ref:** [01:30:32](transcript.md#013032-nerves-tool-breathwork-spontaneous-communication-beta-blockers)

### Tool: NSDR / Yoga Nidra for Pre-Talk Recovery
- **What:** A 10–30 minute non-sleep deep rest practice to partially restore cognitive function after poor sleep.
- **How:** Use a zero-cost yoga nidra or NSDR script (audio guided). The goal is to stay awake while the body is relaxed; the practice trains simultaneous alertness and physical ease — directly useful for public speaking. Do it 60–90 minutes before the event or in the morning.
- **When:** The night before or morning of a major talk when sleep was inadequate.
- **Ref:** [01:34:29](transcript.md#013429-communication-hygiene-caffeine-tools-nsdryoga-nidra-vestibular-system--sleep)

### Tool: Conversation Before Speaking (Warm-Up)
- **What:** Having any low-stakes conversation before a high-stakes speaking event to activate the speech production system.
- **How:** Find someone to talk to for 3–5 minutes on any mundane topic before going on stage. If backstage, seek someone out. The act of turn-taking — speaking and listening — shifts the brain from anticipation mode into present-moment connection mode.
- **When:** Immediately before any keynote, meeting, interview, or difficult conversation.
- **Ref:** [01:40:08](transcript.md#014008-conversation-before-speaking-delivering-engaging-speeches)

### Tool: Tongue Twister Warm-Up
- **What:** Saying tongue twisters aloud to force present-moment orientation and warm up the voice.
- **How:** Say any tongue twister out loud three times fast immediately before a podcast interview, presentation, or talk. It is impossible to say a tongue twister and be mentally elsewhere — it guarantees presence and warms the voice.
- **When:** As part of a pre-talk routine, immediately before speaking.
- **Ref:** [02:19:49](transcript.md#021949-pre-talk-anxiety-management-acknowledgements)

### Tool: Cooling for Physical Anxiety Symptoms
- **What:** Holding something cold before speaking to reduce blushing and perspiring caused by elevated heart rate.
- **How:** Hold a cold glass, bottle, or ice pack in the palms (which are thermoregulators for the body) for 1–2 minutes before going on stage.
- **When:** When physical symptoms of anxiety (perspiring, blushing, flushing) are the primary concern.
- **Ref:** [02:19:49](transcript.md#021949-pre-talk-anxiety-management-acknowledgements)

### Tool: Envisioning a Positive Outcome
- **What:** Deliberately rehearsing a realistic positive outcome before a talk rather than catastrophizing.
- **How:** Instead of imagining freezing, forgetting, or being judged, picture the audience nodding, asking good questions, and feeling engaged. The goal is not "this will be the best talk ever" — simply "I will add value and people will learn something."
- **When:** In the 30–60 minutes before any high-stakes communication.
- **Ref:** [02:10:57](transcript.md#021057-public-speaking-fear-tool-envision-positive-outcome-arguments--mediation)

### Tool: Paraphrasing to Handle Interruptions
- **What:** Synthesizing and reflecting back what an interrupter said to politely re-take the conversational floor.
- **How:** When interrupted, briefly paraphrase what was said ("Cost is really important — and that's actually what I was about to cover") and use it as a bridge back to your point. Also effective for stopping someone who is talking too long.
- **When:** In meetings, presentations with Q&A, or any conversation where interruptions or overtalking occur.
- **Ref:** [02:07:36](transcript.md#020736-interruptions-tools-paraphrasing-speech-preparation)

### Tool: Setting Expectations to Manage Interruptions
- **What:** Stating the format upfront so any interruption visibly violates an agreed frame.
- **How:** Before a presentation or important conversation, say: "I'd like to spend the first five minutes laying the foundation, then I'd love your questions." If someone interrupts, they are now visibly violating the agreed structure, making it socially easy to redirect them.
- **When:** Any meeting or presentation where interruptions are likely.
- **Ref:** [02:07:36](transcript.md#020736-interruptions-tools-paraphrasing-speech-preparation)
