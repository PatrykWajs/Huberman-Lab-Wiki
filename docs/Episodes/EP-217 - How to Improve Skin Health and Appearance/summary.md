# Episode 217: How to Improve Skin Health & Appearance

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman delivers a comprehensive solo episode on skin biology, the science of sun exposure and sunscreens, and evidence-based interventions for achieving youthful, healthy skin. He covers the bidirectional relationship between skin, the immune system, and the gut microbiome, then systematically reviews what the peer-reviewed literature and board-certified dermatologists recommend for collagen support, anti-aging compounds, phototherapy, peptides, nutrition, and the treatment of acne, rosacea, and psoriasis. The episode is grounded in consultation with multiple dermatologists — including an oncology-focused skin-cancer specialist — and is oriented toward helping listeners make informed, personalised decisions at any age or goal level.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Skin is a systemic health mirror.** The appearance of skin — colour, tone, and texture — directly reflects the status of the immune system, gut microbiome, hormones, and overall organ health. Poor sleep, chronic stress, or illness is visible in the skin within hours. [00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-skin-health)

2. **Sun exposure is a double-edged tool.** Some daily sun is beneficial — it drives vitamin D synthesis, testosterone, and estrogen production via the skin's role as an endocrine organ. Excessive UV exposure irreversibly damages collagen and elastin in the dermis (photoaging) and mutates DNA in keratinocytes and melanocytes, increasing skin-cancer risk. [00:19:51](transcript.md#001951-aging-sun-exposure-skin-cancers-physical-barriers)

3. **Sunburn alone does not predict skin cancer risk; cumulative UV does.** Avoiding sunburn is insufficient — cumulative UV exposure without burning still drives most non-melanoma cancers and a substantial proportion of melanomas. Around 80–90% of melanomas arise in de novo (non-mole) skin, not in existing moles. [00:27:24](transcript.md#002724-sunburn--skin-cancers)

4. **Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide / titanium dioxide) are the safest option.** Organic/chemical sunscreens (oxybenzone, avobenzone) are potential endocrine disruptors. Mineral-based sunscreens at ≤25% concentration are considered safe for all ages; chemical-based sunscreens should not be used on children under 6 months. Wearing sunscreen does not fully eliminate vitamin D synthesis because long-wavelength light still penetrates the skin. [00:36:50](transcript.md#003650-organic-chemical-sunscreen--inorganic-mineral-based-sunscreen)

5. **Avoiding all sun exposure carries its own mortality cost.** A Swedish melanoma cohort study found non-smokers who avoided sun exposure had a life expectancy reduced by 0.6–2.1 years compared to the highest sun-exposure group — comparable to smokers — underscoring that sun avoidance is not a risk-free default. [00:55:34](transcript.md#005534-sun-exposure-melanoma--life-expectancy)

6. **Hydrolysed collagen + vitamin C is the best-supported oral supplement for skin youthfulness.** Meta-analyses of RCTs show 15–30 g/day of hydrolysed collagen peptides combined with 500–1,000 mg vitamin C produce statistically significant improvements in skin elasticity, wrinkle reduction, and moisture. Bone broth is a food-based alternative. [01:03:13](transcript.md#010313-tool-youthful-skin-collagen--vitamin-c)

7. **Niacinamide (500 mg x2/day for ≥3 months) addresses ceramide production, pore size, dark spots, and oil balance.** It also reduces skin inflammation, making it useful for acne and rosacea. Topical formulations require ≥2% concentration; many commercial serums already contain it. Hyaluronic acid, used alongside niacinamide, acts as a humectant that retains skin moisture. [01:20:58](transcript.md#012058-tool-niacinamide-nicotinamide-youthful-skin-dark-spots-hyaluronic-acid)

8. **Retinoids are the gold-standard dermatologist recommendation for youthful skin.** Tretinoin (Retin-A) and its OTC relatives (retinol, retinyl esters) convert to retinoic acid inside skin cells, activating transcription factors that upregulate collagen synthesis and cellular turnover. Pregnant or breastfeeding women must avoid all retinoids due to teratogenicity. Dermatologists suggest starting in one's 20s for those not pregnant or planning pregnancy. [01:26:25](transcript.md#012625-tool-retinol-retin-a-tretinoin-retinyl-esters-youthful-skin)

9. **BPC-157 and copper peptides are popular for skin rejuvenation but lack human RCT evidence.** BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound 157) increases angiogenesis and fibroblast motility in animal models, potentially improving skin vascularisation. However, increased vascularisation also carries a theoretical risk of accelerating tumour growth. Copper supports collagen synthesis and DNA repair, but excess copper is pro-inflammatory — dietary or multivitamin levels are sufficient; additional supplementation is not recommended. [01:12:55](transcript.md#011255-peptides-bpc-157-copper)

10. **Red and near-infrared phototherapy (10–15 min, 5–7 days/week) yields mild-to-moderate skin improvements.** Mechanisms include reduced reactive oxygen species, improved mitochondrial function, lowered inflammation, and increased local blood flow. Evidence supports benefits for anti-aging, acne, and psoriasis. A Nobel Prize was awarded in the early 1900s for phototherapy's use in treating lupus. [01:33:07](transcript.md#013307-tool-phototherapy-youthful-skin-treating-skin-conditions)

11. **Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) in highly processed foods accelerate skin aging and worsen inflammatory skin conditions.** AGEs form when sugars, proteins, and fats interact at the high heat used in food manufacturing. Minimising processed food intake is foundational for skin and overall health. [01:47:54](transcript.md#014754-highly-processed-foods-advanced-glycation-end-products--skin-health)

12. **Acne is driven by hormones and insulin; low-glycaemic diet and gentle cleansing are the primary interventions.** High insulin and IGF-1 stimulate sebum overproduction and keratinocyte proliferation inside hair follicles. Over-cleansing strips the skin microbiome; 2–3 times/day with a fragrance-free cleanser and lukewarm water is recommended. Salicylic acid (aspirin-class) reduces keratinocyte stickiness and pore clogging. Popping pimples triggers matrix metalloproteinase activity that causes permanent indentation scarring. [01:58:58](transcript.md#015858-acne-hormones--insulin-tool-low-glycaemic-diet-dairy)

13. **Psoriasis is now understood as an immune-mediated disease, not simply a skin-cell overproduction disorder.** For more than 80 years dermatologists incorrectly attributed it to excess skin-cell production; it is now known to involve immune system overactivation. Modern biologics targeting interleukin-17 (IL-17) and interleukin-23 (IL-23) are highly effective prescription treatments. Anti-inflammatory lifestyle measures remain important adjuncts. [02:21:04](transcript.md#022104-psoriasis-treatment-immune-system--prescriptions)

## Tools & Protocols

### Mineral Sunscreen

- **What:** Application of sunscreen containing zinc oxide and/or titanium dioxide (≤25% concentration) to protect against UV-induced photoaging and skin cancer.
- **How:** Choose products with zinc oxide and/or titanium dioxide as the sole active ingredient. Avoid chemical/organic sunscreens (oxybenzone, avobenzone) — especially for children under 6 months. Physical barriers (UPF clothing, hats) are always safe alternatives and are preferred by dermatologists for high-exposure situations. Note: wearing sunscreen does not fully block vitamin D synthesis as long-wavelength light still penetrates skin.
- **When:** Before sustained outdoor UV exposure; not necessarily required daily for those with naturally higher melanin and limited outdoor activity.
- **Ref:** [00:36:50](transcript.md#003650-organic-chemical-sunscreen--inorganic-mineral-based-sunscreen)

---

### Vitamin D Supplementation

- **What:** Supplementing vitamin D to support immune function, hormone production, and overall health when dietary or solar sources are insufficient.
- **How:** Most people obtain vitamin D from fortified dairy and diet. If supplementing, start at 1,000–3,000 IU/day; up to 5,000 IU/day is common. 10,000 IU/day is considered high for most people. Measure blood levels before supplementing to confirm actual deficiency — many people are surprised to find they are still low even with regular sun exposure and fortified dairy intake.
- **When:** Especially important in regions or seasons with limited sun exposure. Test levels periodically.
- **Ref:** [00:31:58](transcript.md#003158-vitamin-d-sun-exposure--sunscreen)

---

### Hydrolysed Collagen + Vitamin C

- **What:** Oral supplementation to improve skin elasticity, reduce wrinkle depth, and support collagen synthesis in the dermis.
- **How:** 15–30 g/day of hydrolysed collagen peptides (fish, bovine tendon, bone broth) combined with 500–1,000 mg vitamin C. Many collagen supplements already include vitamin C — check labels. Bone broth daily is a food-based alternative.
- **When:** Daily, long-term. Measurable effects in studies appear after several weeks to months of consistent intake.
- **Ref:** [01:03:13](transcript.md#010313-tool-youthful-skin-collagen--vitamin-c)

---

### Niacinamide (Nicotinamide)

- **What:** Vitamin B3 (oral or topical) to improve ceramide production, skin moisture, oil balance, pore appearance, and hyperpigmentation; also reduces skin inflammation relevant to acne and rosacea.
- **How:** Oral: 500 mg twice per day (1,000 mg/day total). Topical: serums or ointments at ≥2% (up to 10%) concentration. Many commercial skincare products already contain niacinamide — check labels.
- **When:** Oral — consistent daily use for at least 3–6 months before visible results. Topical — daily application per product instructions.
- **Ref:** [01:20:58](transcript.md#012058-tool-niacinamide-nicotinamide-youthful-skin-dark-spots-hyaluronic-acid)

---

### Hyaluronic Acid (Topical)

- **What:** A naturally occurring extracellular matrix component applied topically as a humectant to retain moisture and support a plump, youthful skin appearance.
- **How:** Apply serums or ointments containing hyaluronic acid, frequently combined with niacinamide in a single product.
- **When:** Daily topical application after cleansing.
- **Ref:** [01:20:58](transcript.md#012058-tool-niacinamide-nicotinamide-youthful-skin-dark-spots-hyaluronic-acid)

---

### Retinol / Tretinoin (Retinoids)

- **What:** Vitamin A derivatives that convert to retinoic acid inside skin cells, activating transcription factors that stimulate collagen synthesis, accelerate cellular turnover, and reduce wrinkles. Tretinoin (Retin-A) is prescription-strength; retinol and retinyl esters are available OTC at lower potency.
- **How:** Apply topically per dermatologist prescription or OTC product directions. Start at low concentration to minimise irritation and redness. Pregnant or breastfeeding women must not use retinoids under any circumstances.
- **When:** Typically applied at night (photosensitising). Effects accumulate over weeks to months. Dermatologists suggest starting as early as one's 20s for those not pregnant or planning pregnancy.
- **Ref:** [01:26:25](transcript.md#012625-tool-retinol-retin-a-tretinoin-retinyl-esters-youthful-skin)

---

### BPC-157 and Copper Peptides (Experimental)

- **What:** Peptide compounds added to topical creams, oral capsules, or injections, promoted for skin rejuvenation via increased angiogenesis and collagen pathway support. Human RCT evidence is essentially absent.
- **How:** Products may contain BPC-157, copper, or collagen precursors. Transdermal penetration of topical BPC-157 is unconfirmed. If evaluating any peptide-based product, verify the specific peptides listed. Do not supplement copper beyond levels found in a standard multivitamin/mineral — excess copper is pro-inflammatory.
- **When:** Only for those who choose to experiment after understanding the evidence gap and the theoretical tumour-vascularisation risk associated with BPC-157.
- **Ref:** [01:12:55](transcript.md#011255-peptides-bpc-157-copper)

---

### Red and Near-Infrared Phototherapy

- **What:** Exposure to long-wavelength red (~630–700 nm) and near-infrared (~800–1,000 nm) light to reduce reactive oxygen species, improve mitochondrial function, lower inflammation, and increase local blood flow — supporting skin health, youthfulness, acne reduction, and psoriasis management.
- **How:** Position a medical-grade red/NIR light panel approximately 1–2 feet from skin. Sessions of 10–15 minutes. Joovv devices are referenced in the episode. Sunlight also contains these wavelengths but is non-specific.
- **When:** 5–7 days per week consistently for mild-to-moderate improvements.
- **Ref:** [01:33:07](transcript.md#013307-tool-phototherapy-youthful-skin-treating-skin-conditions)

---

### Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition for Skin

- **What:** A whole-food, low-glycaemic, anti-inflammatory dietary pattern to reduce systemic inflammation that manifests as accelerated skin aging, acne, rosacea, and psoriasis.
- **How:** Emphasise vegetables, fruits, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats. Include prebiotic fibre (vegetables, fruit, legumes) and low-sugar fermented foods (refrigerated kimchi, sauerkraut, pickles — must be refrigerated, not shelf-stable). Minimise highly processed foods, refined sugars, AGE-rich foods, and high-glycaemic dairy. Limit alcohol. Ensure adequate vitamin C. Avoid excess vitamin A supplementation.
- **When:** Ongoing dietary baseline.
- **Ref:** [01:41:10](transcript.md#014110-tool-nutrition-for-skin-health-anti-inflammatory-diets)

---

### Gut Microbiome Support

- **What:** Maintaining gut microbiome diversity to reduce systemic inflammation that manifests in skin conditions.
- **How:** Daily intake of prebiotic fibre and low-sugar fermented foods (refrigerated kimchi, sauerkraut, brine-containing pickles — must be refrigerated, not shelf-stable). Supplement with additional fibre if dietary sources are insufficient. Chronic high-dose pill probiotics are expensive and may be associated with brain fog; food-based sources are preferred.
- **When:** Daily, as an ongoing lifestyle practice.
- **Ref:** [01:52:08](transcript.md#015208-tools-reduce-inflammation-gut-microbiome-sleep-alcohol-smoking-stress)

---

### Acne: Low-Glycaemic Diet, Gentle Cleansing & Topical Protocol

- **What:** Dietary, hygiene, and topical adjustments to reduce sebum overproduction, keratinocyte proliferation, and skin microbiome disruption driving acne.
- **How:** Low-glycaemic diet; reduce or eliminate high-glycaemic dairy. Cleanse face 2–3 times per day maximum with a gentle, unscented, unfragranced cleanser (e.g. Dove bar soap, Cetaphil) using lukewarm — not hot — water. Do not pop pimples: mechanical disruption triggers matrix metalloproteinases that destroy the extracellular matrix and cause permanent indentation scarring. Apply a small amount of topical corticosteroid cream to reduce pimple redness without popping. Salicylic acid (applied only to clean skin, if dermatologist-recommended) reduces keratinocyte stickiness and pore clogging. Shower promptly after gym workouts. Consult a dermatologist for prescription options (topical/oral antibiotics, benzoyl peroxide, isotretinoin) if lifestyle changes are insufficient.
- **When:** Ongoing dietary and hygiene practice. Prescriptions as directed.
- **Ref:** [01:58:58](transcript.md#015858-acne-hormones--insulin-tool-low-glycaemic-diet-dairy), [02:07:26](transcript.md#020726-tools-face-cleansing--acne-scarring--popping-pimples)

---

### Rosacea Management

- **What:** Lifestyle, dietary, and clinical interventions to reduce rosacea-associated flushing and skin redness. There are four major types of rosacea; most respond to the measures below, some require additional prescription treatment.
- **How:** Reduce or eliminate alcohol (vasodilator — often the primary trigger). Avoid very hot foods and beverages (both temperature and spice). Follow anti-inflammatory, low-glycaemic diet. Avoid over-cleansing; use lukewarm water and fragrance-free cleanser. Use sunscreen regularly (sun-induced inflammation exacerbates rosacea). Reduce stress. Niacinamide supplementation may help. For acne-type rosacea: acne treatments can be incorporated. For stubborn rosacea with broken blood vessels near the skin surface: pulsed dye laser (PDL) is an in-office dermatological procedure that uses a targeted laser wavelength to destroy dilated sub-surface blood vessels.
- **When:** Lifestyle changes ongoing. PDL is an in-office dermatological procedure.
- **Ref:** [02:13:29](transcript.md#021329-tool-treating-rosacea-alcohol-skin-care-nutrition), [02:18:31](transcript.md#021831-stubborn-rosacea-over-cleansing-pulsed-dye-laser)

---

### Psoriasis: Immune Modulation

- **What:** Reducing immune system overactivation that drives psoriasis-related skin cell overgrowth and inflammation. Psoriasis is now understood as an immune-mediated disease, not a simple skin-cell overproduction disorder.
- **How:** Anti-inflammatory lifestyle (diet, sleep, stress reduction, gut microbiome support). For moderate-to-severe cases: prescription biologics targeting interleukin-17 (IL-17) and interleukin-23 (IL-23) are the most effective treatments. Managed by a dermatologist.
- **When:** Lifestyle interventions ongoing. Biologic prescriptions per dermatologist direction.
- **Ref:** [02:21:04](transcript.md#022104-psoriasis-treatment-immune-system--prescriptions)

---

### Annual Full-Body Skin Check & Laser Resurfacing

- **What:** Annual professional dermatological examination of the entire body surface — not just moles — to detect pre-cancerous and cancerous lesions early. Laser resurfacing is an emerging preventive dermatological procedure that disrupts and regenerates the epidermal layer to reduce UV-induced mutations and improve skin youthfulness.
- **How:** Schedule a full-body skin check with a board-certified dermatologist annually. Note that 80–90% of melanomas arise in de novo (non-mole) skin, so self-monitoring moles alone is insufficient. Any pimple-like lesion or area seeping plasma, pus, or blood that persists more than one month should be evaluated immediately. Laser resurfacing is performed in a dermatologist's office and encourages skin-cell turnover, reducing the incidence of certain UV-induced skin cancers.
- **When:** Full-body check once per year minimum; more frequently with a family history of skin cancer or significant cumulative sun exposure.
- **Ref:** [00:49:20](transcript.md#004920-skin-cancers-moles-laser-resurfacing)
