## Episode Overview

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

Dr. Keith Humphreys, professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at Stanford School of Medicine and former Senior Policy Advisor at the White House Office of National Drug Control Policy, joins Andrew Huberman for a nearly three-and-a-half hour deep dive into addiction science, policy, and recovery. They cover the neurobiology and genetics of addiction across all major substances — alcohol, cannabis, opioids, stimulants, nicotine — and behavioral addictions including gambling and social media, examining how for-profit industries deliberately engineer compulsive use. The conversation addresses evidence-based clinical approaches to recovery including motivational interviewing, 12-step fellowships, contingency management for stimulant addiction, and pharmacological frontiers such as GLP-1 agonists and ibogaine. They also explore how brain plasticity makes adolescent exposure especially dangerous, why cue-triggered dopamine activation in the nucleus accumbens predicts relapse better than self-report, and how policy tools like insurance parity laws and Medicaid expansion have reshaped addiction treatment access. Humphreys also shares a decade of hospice counseling experience and discusses the philosophical link between addiction, the desire for oblivion, and the fear of death.

## Key Takeaways

- **Addiction is progressive narrowing of pleasure, not just compulsive behavior.** Huberman's definition — a progressive narrowing of the things that bring one pleasure — is confirmed by Humphreys: natural rewards fall away until the substance or behavior is the one remaining source of reward, which explains why people persist even when losing everything. [00:03:22](transcript.md#000322-addiction-genetic-risk)

- **Genetic risk is real but not destiny.** Heritability for most substance addictions is large (risk ratios of 3–4.5x for children of alcoholics raised by non-drinking adoptive parents). The strongest single predictor of alcohol use disorder risk remains a family history question, outperforming any genetic panel. Father-to-son transmission is the strongest link. [00:09:14](transcript.md#000914-alcohol-use-disorder--alcoholism-genetic-predisposition--addiction-risk)

- **A subset (~8–10%) experience alcohol as highly euphoric with blunted negative feedback.** Psychiatrist Mark Schuckit showed that sons of alcoholic fathers exhibit less body sway and fewer hangovers from equivalent doses — they miss the "stop drinking" signal, making them dramatically more vulnerable to alcoholism. This pattern of differential drug reward is largely genetic and applies across substances. [00:09:14](transcript.md#000914-alcohol-use-disorder--alcoholism-genetic-predisposition--addiction-risk)

- **Alcohol's purported health benefits do not outweigh cancer risk.** The J-shaped curve showing non-drinkers at higher mortality is confounded because many non-drinkers are former heavy drinkers with pre-existing damage. Any marginal cardiac benefit from moderate drinking is smaller than the increased cancer risk. The net mortality math does not favor drinking. [00:23:36](transcript.md#002336-health-benefit-to-alcohol-red-wine-cancer-risk-social-pressure)

- **Modern cannabis is ~65x more brain-exposure-potent than 1980s cannabis.** THC content has risen from ~3–5% to ~20% average in legal markets, and 42% of users now consume daily (vs. once or twice a week historically). The combined effect equals the potency gap between a coca leaf and cocaine. Cannabis is a "performance-degrading drug" that undermines short-term memory, concentration, motivation, and adult-transition milestones — especially in adolescents. [00:37:41](transcript.md#003741-old-vs-new-cannabis--thc-levels-smoked-vs-edible-forms)

- **Cannabis psychosis risk is probably real and concentrated in adolescent users with a family psychiatric history.** Swedish military registry studies show elevated rates of psychotic disorders in male adolescent cannabis users. Humphreys now considers the evidence credible given the far greater potency of modern cannabis. He would not recommend cannabis to anyone with first-degree relatives with schizophrenia, schizoid personality, or bipolar disorder. [00:44:38](transcript.md#004438-cannabis--psychosis-risk-cardiac-health-youth-cannabis-use--transition-to-adulthood)

- **Gambling addiction is engineered around novelty, not winning.** Slot machines now represent 80%+ of casino revenue. "Losses disguised as wins" (LDWS) keep players engaged on novelty alone even as they lose money. The same novelty-reward architecture drives social media compulsion. Regulatory rollback on sports gambling advertising is causing real harm, especially to young men. [00:54:13](transcript.md#005413-industries-of-addiction-regulation-gambling-slot-machines-novelty-casinos)

- **Brain plasticity cuts both ways — early exposure is the primary addiction risk window.** Almost all addiction starts in adolescence because neural plasticity is highest. Starting smoking at 13 almost certainly leads to addiction; starting at 50 almost certainly does not. Addiction in this frame is deeply maladaptive learning, not a moral failure. [01:20:32](transcript.md#012032-brain-plasticity--age-ketamine-depression-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation-tms)

- **Nucleus accumbens dopamine activation predicts relapse better than self-report.** In Humphreys's fMRI study of meth-addicted patients in a residential program, cue-elicited nucleus accumbens activation predicted who would relapse — independent of what patients said about their own cravings. People may sincerely want to stop using and still relapse because they lack insight into their rewired reward circuitry. [02:09:53](transcript.md#020953-longterm-drug-use-dopamine-cues--relapse-social-media)

- **AA has the strongest evidence base for alcohol recovery, beating CBT by ~50% on abstinence outcomes.** A Cochrane collaboration review by Humphreys, John Kelly, and Mara Ferry found AA and 12-step facilitation counseling outperformed cognitive behavioral therapy and motivational enhancement therapy on abstinence rates. AA is free, available worldwide, 24/7, and growing. It works through immediate social reward, accountability, community, and the "one day at a time" structure. [02:29:08](transcript.md#022908-tool-12-step-programs-aa-accessibility--benefits)

- **GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide) show strong early signal for reducing alcohol use.** Multiple animal studies, small trials, and opportunistic epidemiological analyses show semaglutide reduces alcohol consumption. Humphreys is working with the VA and Novo Nordisk on a clinical trial for alcohol use disorder, noting alcohol consumption is the most "eating-like" drug behavior and therefore most likely to respond to satiety-pathway intervention. [02:44:38](transcript.md#024438-glp-1s-weight-loss-alcohol-addiction-pharmaceutical-advertisements)

- **Nicotine's perceived benefits are largely withdrawal relief masquerading as enhancement.** Smokers feel sharper after a morning cigarette because they are reversing overnight nicotine withdrawal — not because nicotine improves baseline function. The same mechanism applies to cannabis (sleep claims) and opioids (pain claims). Recognizing this reframe helps people understand dependency versus genuine therapeutic effect. [01:44:04](transcript.md#014404-nicotine-mistaking-withdrawal-for-benefit)

- **Stimulant addiction has no proven pharmacotherapy after 40 years.** Humphreys's biggest career disappointment: treatment options for cocaine and methamphetamine addiction in 2025 are essentially the same as in the late 1980s. The only evidence-based behavioral intervention is contingency management — providing escalating financial rewards for negative urine tests — now insurance-covered in most US plans. [01:36:10](transcript.md#013610-caffeine-addiction-stimulants--rehab-prescription-stimulants--adhd)

- **Ibogaine (administered with cardiac monitoring) produced dramatic PTSD and addiction remission in veterans.** The late Nolan Williams's open-label Stanford trial showed large reductions in PTSD, depression, and substance use after a single 22-hour ibogaine session. Brain imaging documented structural changes. A controlled trial is still needed to separate the chemical effect from ceremonial context. Ibogaine is illegal in the US; cardiac screening is essential due to QT-prolongation risk. [01:28:10](transcript.md#012810-ssris-mass-shootings-suicide-side-effects-drug-approval-ibogaine--ptsd)

- **91% of people entering alcohol treatment had external social pressure driving them.** Doug Polcin's research found that of people entering alcohol treatment voluntarily — alcohol is legal — 91% reported someone had leaned on them to quit (spouse threatening to leave, employer ultimatum, legal consequences). Ambivalence about surrendering a rewarding substance is normal; external contingency is often the mechanism of change. [02:16:21](transcript.md#021621-brain-stimulation-tms-homelessness-substance-use--rehab)

- **Genes are risk, not destiny; recovery can break multi-generational addiction chains.** As recovery progresses, people accumulate new reasons not to use — meaningful work, relationships, self-respect — that compound over time. The 24 million Americans currently in recovery look like everyone else; they are largely invisible because they blend back into ordinary life. [02:58:36](transcript.md#025836-failure-to-launch-youth-video-games-social-media-recovery-pathways)

## Tools & Protocols

### Protocol: Motivational Interviewing for Addiction Conversations

- **What:** A clinical communication method that elicits the patient's own reasons for change rather than imposing external pressure or moralizing.
- **How:** When someone expresses a desire to change, ask "Why would you want to do that? What would you get out of it?" — and help them elaborate in detail. Identify specific immediate rewards (e.g., "If you didn't smoke for a year, you'd save $2,000 — what would you spend it on?"). Acknowledge the genuine benefits the person currently gets from the substance — social connection, anxiety relief, perceived performance — and frame the decision as their own cost-benefit analysis. Do not lecture. Explore behavioral cues: where do you use? When do you never use? Remove friction (e.g., remove alcohol from the house so any relapse requires a deliberate trip to a liquor store).
- **When:** Any time someone raises interest in changing an addictive behavior, at any stage of addiction. Also useful for family members and clinicians wanting to support a loved one.
- **Ref:** [01:48:44](transcript.md#014844-tool-how-to-talk-to-someone-with-addiction)

### Protocol: 12-Step Programs (AA and Equivalents)

- **What:** Peer-based recovery fellowships (Alcoholics Anonymous, Narcotics Anonymous, Smart Recovery, Life Ring Recovery, Women for Sobriety) offering free, structured, 24/7 accessible community support.
- **How:** Attend meetings — aim to try multiple before concluding it is not a fit, because meetings vary dramatically by culture, demographics, and tone. Work through the 12 steps with a sponsor. Engage with the "one day at a time" framing: do not try to commit to lifelong abstinence, commit only to today. Accept social status earned from days/years sober as a real motivator. The "higher power" concept is flexible — it can be the group, nature, or anything other than the self.
- **When:** Suitable as primary or adjunct recovery support for anyone with alcohol use disorder. Strong causal evidence for alcohol; encouraging but weaker evidence for other substances. Not limited by insurance, schedule, or geography.
- **Ref:** [02:29:08](transcript.md#022908-tool-12-step-programs-aa-accessibility--benefits), [02:38:08](transcript.md#023808-aa-higher-power-cult-flexibility-tool-open-aa-meetings), [03:04:13](transcript.md#030413-aa-as-an-action-program-tool-try-different-aa-meetings)

### Protocol: Open AA Meetings as a Low-Barrier Entry Point

- **What:** AA meetings designated "open" allow anyone to attend — not just people with alcohol problems. Family members, curious observers, or people with other addictions can attend without commitment.
- **How:** Search aa.org or a local AA intergroup website for open meetings. Visitors can say "I'm here to learn" or say nothing when the room goes around. Going yourself first, before asking a loved one to go, can lower their resistance and demonstrates solidarity.
- **When:** For anyone wanting to understand AA before committing, for family members wanting to support a loved one's entry, or for people with other addictions wanting access to the most established mutual-aid infrastructure.
- **Ref:** [02:38:08](transcript.md#023808-aa-higher-power-cult-flexibility-tool-open-aa-meetings)

---

### Protocol: Try Multiple AA Meetings Before Deciding

- **What:** AA meetings differ widely in demographics, tone, formality, and culture. A bad first experience does not mean AA will not work.
- **How:** Approach it like dating — try different meetings at different times of day and in different locations. Look for meetings organized around shared identity (profession, age group, neighborhood). Go back to the one that felt like home. Highly intellectual people can find appropriate meetings; the program accommodates philosophical discussion as long as the core action (don't drink, go to meetings) is maintained.
- **When:** If initial AA experiences feel off-putting, alienating, or unrelatable.
- **Ref:** [03:04:13](transcript.md#030413-aa-as-an-action-program-tool-try-different-aa-meetings)

### Protocol: Structural Strategies for Social Media and Behavioral Overuse

- **What:** Environmental design changes that reduce compulsive digital behavior without relying on willpower alone.
- **How:** Use built-in app time-limit features on phones. Create phone-free zones (dinner table, bedroom). Put social media apps on a separate old phone kept in a time-locked physical safe (a ~$30 Supermax-style lock box that cannot be opened until the set time expires — requires physically sawing it open). Access social media only via browser to add friction. Turn off all platform notifications. Develop social norms around use (e.g., no social media at meals) analogous to existing norms around drinking before noon.
- **When:** For anyone who identifies compulsive social media, video game, or pornography use that is interfering with work, relationships, sleep, or life goals.
- **Ref:** [02:52:39](transcript.md#025239-social-media-addiction-tool-avoiding-social-media-strategies)

### Protocol: GLP-1 Agonists for Alcohol Use Disorder (Emerging)

- **What:** Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and related GLP-1 receptor agonists are showing early evidence of reducing alcohol craving and consumption, likely via reward-pathway satiety signaling.
- **How:** Prescribed by a physician; alcohol reduction is an off-label benefit under active investigation. Humphreys is conducting a clinical trial with the VA and Novo Nordisk. The "twofur" effect — simultaneous weight loss and drinking reduction — may motivate patients who are ambivalent about addiction treatment alone. People with alcohol use disorder are ~70% more likely to also be overweight.
- **When:** Emerging option for patients with co-occurring obesity and alcohol use disorder. Not yet a first-line addiction treatment; clinical trials ongoing.
- **Ref:** [02:44:38](transcript.md#024438-glp-1s-weight-loss-alcohol-addiction-pharmaceutical-advertisements)

---

### Protocol: Contingency Management for Stimulant Use Disorder

- **What:** The only evidence-based behavioral intervention for cocaine and methamphetamine addiction. Provides immediate, escalating financial rewards for negative urine drug screens.
- **How:** On each clinic visit with a negative urine analysis, the patient receives an escalating cash reward ($2 day 1, $4 day 2, $8 day 3, etc.). Coverage is now available through most insurance plans in the US. The intervention exploits the addict brain's extreme preference for immediate reward by making sobriety itself immediately rewarding.
- **When:** For individuals with stimulant use disorder (cocaine, methamphetamine). No pharmacotherapy currently exists; this is the primary evidence-based tool.
- **Ref:** [01:36:10](transcript.md#013610-caffeine-addiction-stimulants--rehab-prescription-stimulants--adhd)

---

### Protocol: Ibogaine for Opioid Use Disorder and PTSD (Research Setting Only)

- **What:** A 22-hour psychoactive compound derived from the iboga plant, showing dramatic results for opioid use disorder and PTSD in an open-label Stanford trial led by the late Nolan Williams.
- **How:** Currently only administered in controlled research settings outside the US (ibogaine is illegal in the US). Requires full cardiac screening and magnesium pre-treatment due to QT-prolongation risk. Heart rate monitoring throughout is mandatory. Sometimes followed by DMT. A proper randomized controlled trial is still needed to separate chemical from ceremonial effects.
- **When:** Being studied for treatment-resistant opioid addiction and PTSD, particularly in veterans. Not for recreational or unsupervised use under any circumstances.
- **Ref:** [01:28:10](transcript.md#012810-ssris-mass-shootings-suicide-side-effects-drug-approval-ibogaine--ptsd)
