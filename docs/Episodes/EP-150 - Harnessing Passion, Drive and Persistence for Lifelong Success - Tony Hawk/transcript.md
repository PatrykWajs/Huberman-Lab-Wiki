## [00:11:08] Early Skateboarding & Skateparks

**Andrew Huberman:** Did you immediately start skateboarding in the parks — on transition, as we say — or were you pushing around in the driveway like most kids?

**Tony Hawk:** It was transportation at first. Skating was kind of a fad — I started around '78, maybe '77 — and kids just had skateboards, they'd all cruise around. Then we started looking at magazines where people were skating in pools. That was the Dogtown and Z-Boys era. It was like these guys are flying — where do we do that? The skate park that opened in San Diego was Oasis. Actually Spring Valley was the first skate park I tried to go to. I was nine and you had to be 10. I remember sitting in the parking lot looking over the fence. My dad didn't realize there was an age limit. He said, "How old is he?" Nine. Sorry, he can't come. And then they closed not long after, so I never got to Spring Valley.

When I first went to Oasis, around '78, a friend was going. I had to get the authorization form notarized by the bank for my parents. And when I went — that was my epiphany. I first saw people flying around in person and I was like, "This is what I'm doing for as long as I can possibly do it." It looked like magic. It looked like they were flying on magic carpets. It spoke to me in the sense of being a daredevil, but also doing it individually — not relying on my team, not getting hassled by a coach. I can be part of the scene but do it my own way.

Then my parents moved to North County San Diego when I was in high school — mostly chasing real estate deals. I got lucky that Del Mar Skate Ranch was right there. Every other park closed, but Del Mar Skate Ranch remained open.

**Andrew Huberman:** Your dad's involvement is interesting. I got into skateboarding because my dad wasn't around much. A lot of kids get into skateboarding because it doesn't require parent involvement. Was it unusual to have parental involvement at that stage?

**Tony Hawk:** I remember Frank and Nancy with such fondness — not just because they took me in. They were at times the only point of stability in a landscape of like 200 people. Your dad had this way of moving about — he wasn't afraid to say what he thought. He'd impose some regulation at a contest, but he also understood this was a sport unlike other sports. He had his own way of doing it. Having his support was great. But the fact that he was always around and in charge of a lot of events — that sucked. Because it marked me as one being favored and spoiled. Most of my friends' parents didn't want them skating. They were stoked that my dad was giving that support, but they still were like, "Your dad's here — this is our thing, this is our getaway from our parents."

I did at some point tell him my frustrations with it. But he was very steadfast: "I've come this far, people are relying on me to organize these events." I had to suck it up for a while.

**Andrew Huberman:** Did it push you harder? Like, you could prove yourself with skateboarding — you can't fake that. You either can do it or you can't, and it's shown in real time.

**Tony Hawk:** Right. There's no deepfake version of skateboarding.

## [00:16:58] Adolescence, Skateboarding

**Andrew Huberman:** I recall you were quite skinny — or skinnier — back then.

**Tony Hawk:** Yeah, I had all kinds of things going against me at the time. Nowadays there are taller skateboarders, but I was not tall when I was growing up. By the time I was 16 I still looked like I was 13. I used to get pulled over — I had a Honda Civic 1977 CVCC that I bought with my earnings — and the cops would be like, "How old are you again?" Sixteen. "You look like you were 13 back there." Then I shot up around age 17.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's interesting. There are a lot of people who study longevity. Perhaps the fastest rate of aging we ever undergo is puberty. Kids who have a longer arc of puberty — a delayed growth spurt — that does seem to correlate with a longer life. That may have important and fortunate consequences for you. So at 17 you shot up. And as I recall — maybe you said it, maybe somebody else did — you were so skinny as a kid that you wore elbow pads as knee pads?

**Tony Hawk:** Yeah, that's a true story. I took inspiration from Steve Caballero because he was already an established pro when I started. He was wearing elbow pads on his knees in a full-page picture in Winchester doing a backside air. I was like, "I want to do that." He was small and I was like, that gives me hope — if he can do that, I can do it.

**Andrew Huberman:** As I recall, Steve also has a pretty severe scoliosis.

**Tony Hawk:** Right. But his size — I identified with that. And I didn't even know he was four years older than me. I just thought, oh, there are small guys doing that. But when I got tall, when I went through puberty — suddenly I had all these tricks and then I had the strength and the height that gave me confidence. All of a sudden it was like, I can go way higher now. I'm comfortable with these tricks, these intricate board maneuvers. That was a huge advantage. Being able to blast eight feet in the air as opposed to four feet in the air was a huge advantage. The bowls actually looked smaller when I stood on top. I was like — wait, this isn't that big.

**Andrew Huberman:** Isn't that wild — when the nervous system knows how to do something, and then your body changes, and you can do the same thing with so much more force. Did you have any concept of being a pro at that age — 14, 15?

**Tony Hawk:** There was none of that to be had, so we didn't have those aspirations. No one had really done it before — you could have some success, you could have maybe a signature model, but the top sales in skateboarding back then wasn't a career. The prize money was $150 for first place, $100 for second, $50 for third. I turned pro when I was 14. By the time I was 15 and a half, I had $600 in my bank account and I used that to buy a Honda Express moped. That was my earnings. So money wasn't the dopamine hit — the actual skateboarding was. Getting to the skate park on my own was amazing.

## [00:23:10] Turning Professional, The Bones Brigade

**Andrew Huberman:** What was it like in the Bones Brigade years — being young and famous, in some sense? You've always seemed to me very grounded, not caught up in it. We've never seen headlines about you blowing all your money or wrecking cars. You seem to have avoided a lot of the pitfalls of famous people, and yet you were famous from a very young age.

**Tony Hawk:** I think it was that that was never a goal. And then when I had a sense of it, I was very uncomfortable. I was happy to be successful, happy that people recognized me — that was amazing, just because I was good at skateboarding. But I was always walled up. Some people thought I was pompous or arrogant because I wasn't interacting, but I just didn't know what to do. Stacy broke me out of that. I remember one time there was a kid just staring at me, holding my signature model. Stacy said, "Go say hi to that guy." I was like, "Are you sure?" And I learned to break out of my comfort zone by doing that enough.

In the Bones Brigade years, we were so young that we thought this is forever, so we were definitely careless with our money. At some point my dad saw that — he didn't think it was going to be long-term, because no one had had a long-term career. He encouraged me to invest, get property, buy a house. That was my saving grace. Because I was definitely spending on cars and things beyond my means. We weren't paying taxes on anything — it was all 1099 income — and at the end of the year it would be like, "You owe this much." I'd be like, "Wait, what?"

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned Stacy. We should clarify for people — Tony's referring to the great Stacy Peralta.

**Tony Hawk:** He was the one who put me on the Bones Brigade when I was still considered sort of a circus act. What I was doing was largely made fun of because people thought it was just more like a free show.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you explain more? My first recollection of you is the finger flip era. People would ride up toward the top of a pool or ramp and do something on the coping, or go above it in the air. I recall seeing you do the finger flip aerial — I'd never seen anyone flip a board in the air. That was a jaw drop. If that was considered a circus act, I don't know what it was being compared to.

**Tony Hawk:** Before I had the height to take things up above coping, people weren't giving it much merit — it was just like, oh, he's doing a little board twist. When I started to get height and started doing those tricks visibly way up high, that's when the shift happened in terms of acceptance. But I was still labeled as a trick skater, robot skater. And then you had Christian Hosoi, who was all style — air higher than anyone, so flashy, such a rockstar personality. In that era it was very divided: no one liked us both. You'd be pitted against another skater and you're just trying to make your way through as a 15-year-old. It was hard. I got bullied.

Thrasher magazine would talk about my performance when I would win — I mean they'd report on it but in that Northern California way. Transworld skateboarding was the other rival. Some of those things that were said — it's amazing to me. But it brings up an important lesson: the kid who gets made fun of, if they're determined and they love what they're doing, that's going to be the kid that blows everyone away later.

I'll never forget — at the Back to the City contests in San Francisco, in the drain fountains in front of City Hall, there was this guy with kind of afro-like hair, pushing around, doing what are called daffies — he had two skateboards. I was thinking, this guy's going to get beat up. That guy was Mark Gonzales. One of the greatest — perhaps the greatest — street skateboarder. I remember thinking he was a kook, and then I realized who it was.

When you were away at night, laying on the pillow — did you talk to your dad about it?

**Tony Hawk:** I didn't really have a support group or any resource to voice those concerns. I just knew I wanted to keep getting better, that was it. If I was worried about those voices, I was going to go back to the skate park and learn more tricks. At some point I had so much of that as a foundation that it was undeniable: well, he can do all this stuff and he doesn't just do it at his home park.

I think that's when the tide turned for me — when I started doing well at other events, namely Upland Pipeline, which was for the most part the most frightening pool we could ride. The thing was big, and the hips — the way the transitions matched up — super tight, giant coping, super rough. If you fell in Upland you were getting chewed up. I would drive up there every weekend — drove Friday after school straight to Upland, skated at night, skated Saturday all day, skated Sunday early, then drove home. I lived in San Diego. I just made it my mission to figure that thing out because that was the proving ground for me.

## [00:35:27] Trick Development & Evolution

**Andrew Huberman:** It's clear you had enormous drive. Let's talk about the process of trying tricks and the anxiety associated with it. Was it systematic — I'm going to learn the basics first, then do a little aerial, then go bigger? Or did you just try what you wanted to try?

**Tony Hawk:** It comes in different forms, but for the most part I think about how I could combine existing tricks. Would this trick work going into this trick? Could your body position shift or would it all work in unison? When I approach a new trick — more in the last 20 years — my thought process is: I have all the pieces. I've done every bit of it in some other form. The landing is from whatever that is. If you can throw all those things together and make the timing work, it's going to work. I never went at something with a haphazard approach — throwing caution to the wind, hoping it works. It's always very much: I know I have all these things and I just have to put them together.

Now things are so technical that I'm doing the same approach hundreds of times and one of them just works because of some tiny fractional adjustment that I didn't even know happened. That is the curse of what tricks are now — there are plenty of moves I've done in the last 10 years that I only did once because it was too hard to get to. For instance, I did a 360 shove-it 5-0 fakie.

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's break that down. A 360 shove-it is pushing the board with your feet and letting it spin a full 360 rotation under your feet, then landing back on it. It's a trick people do on flat ground typically.

**Tony Hawk:** I've learned to do it on the vert walls — I can do 360 shove-its kind of in the air. But then I'm landing on my truck, the axle between the wheels, in a 5-0 position — basically a wheelie on the truck. So everything is so precise. I've got to do a 360 shove-it at exactly a certain spot on the wall. I've got to catch it so that my truck lands when my foot hits it. I have to land with my weight perfectly set back enough so I can come in backwards — fakie. Any time I try it, there's like a one-in-ten chance I'm even going to get into the position I need, and that's the one I have to commit to. Every time I do it, it's so intense. I did it once and I'd love to do it again, but I know what it's going to take.

## [00:40:33] Visualization, Dreaming

**Andrew Huberman:** Was that 360 shove-it 5-0 fakie something you thought of the night before? Do you ever use visualization? Have you ever had learning come to you in a dream — tried something, went to sleep, and the next day you made it?

**Tony Hawk:** Yes. Sometimes I'll wake up in the middle of the night and write something down because it was like, oh, there's this trick, I think I could do that. So you dream about skateboarding?

Yeah, that shifted a bit after I got hurt. I used to dream that I can't skate — I'm trying and the ramp is made of carpet, I can't get the speed, I can't get the timing. Then as I went through the traumatic injury, my dreams shifted to: wow, I can skate, I can do all my tricks again.

**Andrew Huberman:** A little piece of science around the "can't skate" piece — the feeling of being bolted down in a dream or not being able to run. There's a phase of sleep called REM sleep, where the brain is very active and dreams tend to be very vivid, but we are completely paralyzed. The body can't release adrenaline during REM, so it's almost like its own form of trauma therapy — experiencing an intense thing in your mind, but the body can't react. That's often why you feel like you want to move and you can't.

**Tony Hawk:** Actually a couple of my kids have struggled with that a couple of times.

**Andrew Huberman:** REM atonia interference. Not dangerous, and usually people can jolt themselves out, but it's terrifying. So your dreams before the injury had this "can't do it" quality — but in the Twilight moments between wakefulness and sleep, everything else falls away and you can focus on new moves?

**Tony Hawk:** Exactly. An example: I went to the X Games in Japan a few weeks ago. I was thinking I was going to go just to show support — there's not a lot of vert events anymore, so if there's a vert event, I want to show my support. They had a best trick event and I thought, maybe I could get in. Is there anything new, though? I'm still recovering from my leg. Then at some point I was falling asleep and I thought — oh, I could do that trick and come in 180. I know I could do that with my current state.

What I was going to do was a half-cab body rail to backside blunt. A half-cab is coming in backwards and rotating 180 as you approach the top of the ramp. Body rail means I jump around and land with my two trucks out and my tail on the coping — that's the blunt. I've done that before, coming in fakie. And I thought, what if I just keep coming around and come in backside direction? That keeps my body spinning. It might actually be easier. It wasn't, but I figured it out. I did it at X Games. It was my last run. It got seven plays, but for me it was a huge moment.

**Andrew Huberman:** That liminal state between wakefulness and sleep — if you're open to ideas showing up there, they almost always do. Do you have practices for pure relaxation?

**Tony Hawk:** I was never good at that — warming up, stretching, post-session, meditation, nothing. I just go skate and it's on. As I've gotten older I realize that's not the best technique. I do have more of an OCD warm-up run that I use to gauge how I'm feeling. Kind of like a surgeon checking off boxes before an operation — you're comfortable in your environment, then you do the work. My warm-up run is basic tricks, but they give me a sense of how stiff I am or what I need to adjust for the rest of the day.

## [00:47:09] "Feeling" While Skateboarding

**Andrew Huberman:** What feels the best? Making a new trick feels incredible. But what are two or three things that just feel so good?

**Tony Hawk:** Learning new tricks, for sure. Not even that it's something I created, but just doing something I've never done before. When I first learned backside varials — no one had done backside varials before, only frontside. A varial is where you reach down, grab your board, jump in the air, and turn 180 under your feet — like a shove-it but you're guiding with your hand. I learned that halfway up the pool at Oasis with no one around, and the feeling I got when I rode away was something I'd never experienced. That is the buzz I've been chasing ever since. That feeling was like no other.

And then just stripping everything else away — the most basic tricks. A backside Ollie is a no-handed aerial. It feels so good because even to this day, people say, "How does the board stay on your feet?" I can't even tell you how the board stays on my feet — I just know how to maneuver it and keep the pressure and the friction going. A clean backside Ollie, to me, feels as good as anything.

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm delighted to hear that it still feels that good. I don't think anyone can have the kind of lifelong progression you've had without a love of the feeling that it brings — even when no one's around. How often are you on your ramp with no one filming?

**Tony Hawk:** I mean, I'm trying to do it when I can. The skating I do by myself on the ramp is some of the most meaningful. You can really dig into what you're trying to work on. There's a young lady who's been skating at my ramp — Reese Nelson. She is so good. There are some amazing female skateboarders now. That's one of the big shifts since I started.

People assume that you go from zero to a hundred somehow, that there are people selected by genetics or luck to just get it. But it's clear you've spent a lot of time alone — driving somewhere to skate the next day, alone at the ramp. Do you ever reflect on that kind of drive?

## [00:51:15] Drive & Discipline; Injuries

**Tony Hawk:** I don't think about it. I just know I have to do it. When I first started skating, there was no way you'd think I was a natural or had any future in it. I was all gangly, falling all over the place. I've seen people that are naturals, and how they don't have that drive — they don't take advantage of what they have naturally. And I've seen other skaters who are driven and not really skilled — sloppy even — become the best. Andrew Reynolds. When we put him on our team he was super gangly, boards bouncing around, but trying every single trick. Then he became the boss. You have to give drive as much weight as natural talent, if not more.

I'll tell you my best example — I haven't talked about this publicly yet. I broke my leg doing a McTwist — something I've done thousands of times since Mike McGill created it in 1984. I've been doing McTwists for 40 years. I went around and did one with no speed last year, thinking I could do it like I was still 20, and got tangled up and broke my femur. I had a super long recovery, a false start, and a non-union fracture — my bone never connected back to itself and kept pushing itself further away. I had a second surgery in November.

And all along, in the back of my head: I have to get back to 540s. I can't explain why I have to. I hate that it means that much to me. It's not pride, it's not proving anything to anyone — I just have to do it.

Last week, I did it. It was so scary. I prepped for it — even down to my diet. I stopped drinking altogether. Every time I go to the ramp I'm just trying 540s — just to get the spin, to get the landing zone — with no intention to make one yet. And I had this heart-to-heart with my wife. She doesn't want to see me get hurt again. She was gracious and accepting — not like "yeah, you gotta go do it," but "okay, that's who you are." She was my only spectator.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've seen the video. My first response was: f yes. My second response was: that was really high — this isn't just above coping. This is head-high.

**Tony Hawk:** I wasn't going to make the same mistake I did last time, trying it low. Going high was more of a safety measure — which is ironic. The bigger the ramps, the safer it is for me, because I have a better landing zone and more time in the air to adjust. I can't skate some 8-foot pool. I'm too tall, I move too slowly now. That's why you don't see me in the park events — you'll see me on a 14-foot boat ramp because that's my happy place and that's where I'm safe.

Also, having my wife there — I just knew I wasn't going to get hurt in front of her.

**Andrew Huberman:** I recall you and I communicated not long after the first break. You texted me asking how long before you'd be skateboarding again. I said: skateboarding as in pushing, or what you do on vert? You said: what I do on vert. I said, well, it seems you're doing a lot of things right — deliberate cold, heat, pressure. You called it at two months. Then you came out at the Oscars about a week later, walking with no support, broken femur. Pretty soon I was seeing videos of you dropping in. I texted Mike Blabac: "Tony's back already — this is superhuman rates of healing."

## [00:58:46] Injury Recovery Practices

**Andrew Huberman:** Then you broke it again. Did you allow more rest the second time? What was driving you to get back so quickly the first time?

**Tony Hawk:** The first go-around, I just didn't listen to any professional advice. I've had a broken pelvis, broken elbow, knee surgeries — I've always pushed through and the timeline has always been shortened because I just get back out there and get healing started. In this instance I got back out right away. Not long after the Academy Awards — I was walking with a cane at that time, ditched the cane just to walk out on stage to present the award. As soon as I walked off stage they handed my cane back and I was hobbling backstage.

I was skating a mini ramp and already struggling because I couldn't put weight on my front foot — my bones still had not connected. There's a nail holding them in place, but I didn't realize how careful I needed to be. I decided to drop in on the mini ramp. It wasn't the drop-in itself — it was stepping off my board to get to the top. I stepped off like any other day without thinking, led with my front foot, and I felt the bone move in that moment. I felt it either twist or get out of place. I was in total denial for months — just saying, oh, it just hurts.

Seven, eight months into recovery, I was always in pain, my skating wasn't progressing. I went and got X-rays. They said: your bone never connected — you have a non-union fracture. And every time I skated, I was pushing it further away. The bone was laterally displaced — think of a pipe broken in the middle with one side offset to the other.

I found a specialist in non-union fractures. His approach was very pragmatic: I'm taking the nail out, taking the other hardware out, putting it together — and you cannot move for two months.

**Andrew Huberman:** Did you obey that?

**Tony Hawk:** I did, really. I was not going to risk that again.

**Andrew Huberman:** Did you prioritize sleep, nutrition, while recovering?

**Tony Hawk:** I was very disciplined in my diet, my schedule, and my sleep. Surprisingly I was very busy — my speaking engagements were getting booked left and right. I did a tour through Europe last summer of speaking engagements. That was a silver lining to my idle time — I leaned into it. Good money, and it's fun to interact. But all through that, in the back of my head: when can I skate? When can I skate? When I finally started skating again, it was night and day with my leg. I could lean forward suddenly. I was learning tricks every session, relearning tricks. I'm just lucky that I got to live in this time of modern medicine.

**Andrew Huberman:** Was those two months the longest you'd ever gone without skating vert?

**Tony Hawk:** Yeah. Without skating at all — not even just pushing around. Yes.

**Andrew Huberman:** Good for you for obeying doctor's orders. And good for you for deciding that your rate of recovery is going to be whatever it is for you. On one hand you listen to the medical professionals, on the other hand — it's like you decided two things at once. There are experts who have something to offer me, and I'll follow their advice. And I'm the expert at myself, and I'm going to make sure I come back 100%.

**Tony Hawk:** Yeah, or better. And I've come to terms with what I'm not going to be able to do anymore. There are some tricks now that are way more difficult — something changed in my body. I can't grab slob consistently. That used to be my go-to grab. I'd do it over 60-foot gaps. Now half the time I try to grab that way I don't reach it or I grab my foot instead. I can't make the adjustment to fix it. So I've come to terms with: that's not the go-to grab anymore, and that's okay. I had a good run.

## [01:06:52] Healthy Life Practices & Skateboarding

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there other things you can't wait to get back to?

## [01:15:03] Video Game Development

**Andrew Huberman:** The video game changed a lot of things for the general public's perception of skateboarding. It allowed kids who weren't going to bang up their shins to do incredible tricks. And seeing something — even in a video game — inspires kids to go outside and grab a real skateboard. Could you talk about the genesis of the video game? Were you into video games prior?

**Tony Hawk:** I've been into video games since the gecko. I was a kid playing Pong, Pac-Man, Missile Command, Qbert. Home systems: Super NES, Commodore 64, Sega. I always loved technology. When I started making money in the '80s, my first big purchase in electronics was a Commodore Amiga — considered one of the highest-end home computers, more graphic and game-oriented.

I got a call from a PC programmer who wanted to pitch a skate game. He had a crude engine — a skater cruising around, going in bowls, all keyboard controlled. Clunky, but something. The last thing we'd had as skating in video games was 720 in the arcade or Skate or Die for Commodore 64. So I was excited.

We went to Nintendo, Midway, all these different manufacturers — and were just told this was a bad idea. Someone at Midway said those exact words to me: "Skateboarding is not popular. Home video games are barely a thing. Why would anyone want to buy a video game about skateboarding?" So he got frustrated and needed to find a job.

Sure enough, about a year later, Activision called me. They were working on a skate game based on an engine from a game already released called Apocalypse, starring Bruce Willis. So the first version of my game was Bruce Willis on a skateboard with a gun strapped to his back, in a desert wasteland, doing kickflips. It was awesome. I picked it up, got past that visual, started playing — the motion felt right, the engine was right. I said, this is the baseline of something special.

The development process was about a year and a half. They'd FedEx me builds on CDs. I had a modified PlayStation and I'd play it, make notes. I thought skaters were going to dig this, and that was it.

Not long before release, they called and said: "We want to offer you a buyout of future royalties. Half a million dollars, and then you don't get royalties going forward." At that time in my life, hearing half a million dollars sounded like half a billion dollars. I was in a pretty good place in terms of my options — I'd just bought a new home. I thought I'm going to take a chance and see what happens. That was the best financial decision I ever made. I just let it ride.

As soon as the game was released, it was getting stellar reviews. The very next week they said: "Okay, we're working on number two — what do you want to do?" We ended up doing like 10.
