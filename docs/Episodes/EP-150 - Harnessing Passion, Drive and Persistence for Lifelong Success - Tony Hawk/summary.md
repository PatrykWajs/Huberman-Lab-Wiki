# Episode 150: Harnessing Passion, Drive & Persistence for Lifelong Success | Tony Hawk

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman sits down with Tony Hawk — legendary professional skateboarder, video game entrepreneur, and founder of the Skatepark Project — for a wide-ranging conversation about passion, drive, and lifelong progression. They cover Hawk's childhood and entry into skateboarding, how he turned professional at 14, his systematic approach to trick development and visualization, and the intrinsic motivation that has sustained him for over 40 years. The episode examines his recovery from a catastrophic femur fracture and non-union fracture, his philosophy on injury, discipline, and risk management, and the evolution of skateboarding culture — including its inclusion in the Olympics and the rise of female skateboarders. They also discuss the video game franchise, financial decision-making, philanthropy through the Skatepark Project, the autograph reseller economy, and the role of family and parenting in sustaining a long career.

## Guest: Tony Hawk

**Tony Hawk** (born 1968) is widely regarded as the greatest professional vert skateboarder of all time. Turning pro at age 14 and becoming the first skateboarder to land a 900 (two and a half rotations in the air) at the 1999 X Games, Hawk has been at the forefront of the sport for over four decades. He is the founder and namesake of the Tony Hawk's Pro Skater video game franchise (Activision, 1999), which has sold over 30 million copies and fundamentally changed public perception of skateboarding. He founded Birdhouse Skateboards in 1992 and the Skatepark Project (formerly Tony Hawk Foundation) in 2002, which has helped fund nearly 1,000 public skate parks in underserved communities. At the time of this recording (2023) he was 55 years old and had recently returned to performing McTwist 540s after recovering from a broken femur and non-union fracture.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Passion precedes purpose** — Hawk never set out to become a professional skateboarder or build a video game franchise. He simply found skateboarding felt unlike anything else at age 10 and pursued that feeling completely. He had no career goal; the career emerged from obsession. [00:05:55](transcript.md#000555-childhood-self-concept)

2. **Drive matters more than natural talent** — Hawk was not a natural athlete. He was small, gangly, and made fun of early on. Andrew Reynolds is cited as another example: sloppy technique as a teenager, became one of the best skateboarders alive through sheer relentlessness. Hawk explicitly states drive and discipline deserve more weight than innate ability. [00:51:15](transcript.md#005115-drive-discipline-injuries)

3. **Combining known elements is the template for innovation** — When developing new tricks, Hawk's mental model is: "I have all the pieces — I've done every bit of this in another form. I just have to put them together." He never approached a new trick without having already mastered its component parts. [00:35:27](transcript.md#003527-trick-development-evolution)

4. **The liminal state between sleep and wakefulness is a creative hotspot** — Hawk has formulated new trick ideas while falling asleep or in twilight wakefulness, when mental noise drops and spatial thinking opens up. The X Games Japan half-cab body rail to backside blunt came this way. [00:40:33](transcript.md#004033-visualization-dreaming)

5. **Intrinsic reward must be the primary driver** — The chemical fingerprint of making that first novel trick — Hawk's backside varial below coping at Oasis, alone with no audience — became the benchmark buzz he has chased for 40+ years. External recognition, money, and fame were never the target. [00:47:09](transcript.md#004709-feeling-while-skateboarding)

6. **Do not ignore expert medical advice when recovery stakes are catastrophic** — After his first femur break, Hawk ignored medical advice and re-fractured the bone while simply stepping off his board. The non-union fracture required a second, more invasive surgery. His second recovery he followed doctor's orders strictly for two months and came back stronger. [00:58:46](transcript.md#005846-injury-recovery-practices)

7. **Compulsive internal motivation is distinct from external pressure or ego** — Hawk could not explain why he had to land the 540 McTwist again after breaking his femur on it. It was not pride, not proving anything to anyone — it was simply a felt necessity he could not dismiss. [00:51:15](transcript.md#005115-drive-discipline-injuries)

8. **Financial shrewdness in early success requires structural guidance** — Hawk's father Frank urged real estate investment at 17. Hawk's decision not to take a $500,000 royalty buyout for Tony Hawk's Pro Skater was arguably the most consequential financial decision of his life. Both required someone to model or prompt the behavior. [01:23:00](transcript.md#012300-financial-investments-birdhouse)

9. **Protect your primary domain from adjacent activities that carry disproportionate injury risk** — Hawk deliberately avoided going deep into Motocross, car racing, and other extreme sports he enjoys because the risk to his skating career was too high. His last knee surgery came from overshooting a snowboard jump in Mammoth. [01:30:16](transcript.md#013016-professionalism-hobbies)

10. **Inclusivity accelerates a sport's evolution** — The most important catalyst for women's skateboarding was structural: the requirement for equal Olympic qualifying divisions forced equal prize money and equal promotion. That systemic change unlocked a wave of female participation far beyond what organic social change had produced. [01:49:28](transcript.md#014928-females-in-skateboarding)

11. **Cross-generational learning in skateboarding is a unique structural advantage** — A single session at a skate park includes a 55-year-old and a 10-year-old, both influencing each other. Reese Nelson (age 10) gave Hawk the technical cue that helped him land his comeback McTwist — pull out a little more. This two-way transmission of knowledge across decades is nearly impossible in most other sports. [01:56:04](transcript.md#015604-inspiration-kids-bones-brigade)

12. **Public success is not a substitute for intrinsic satisfaction** — Hawk notes that it was only in the last 5 to 10 years that he truly began to enjoy what skateboarding has provided him — the opportunities, what it means to his family, the culture he's embedded in. For decades prior, the goal was simply the next trick, the next contest, the next necessary business decision. [02:08:14](transcript.md#020814-future-goals-aspirations)

## Tools & Protocols

### Systematic Trick Development
- **What:** Break new tricks into component parts that have already been mastered in other contexts. Never attempt a new trick as a whole without having done each piece before.
- **How:** Map the desired trick against the existing toolkit. Identify the spin, the grab, the body position, the landing — all individually proven. Practice sub-components in isolation until timing aligns in a full attempt.
- **When:** Before attempting any genuinely novel maneuver or before returning to a high-risk trick after a long layoff.
- **Ref:** [00:35:27](transcript.md#003527-trick-development-evolution)

### Liminal State Ideation
- **What:** Use the twilight state between wakefulness and sleep to generate new ideas. Mental noise decreases and spatial/kinesthetic imagination opens.
- **How:** Keep a notepad or phone nearby when sleeping. When waking in the middle of the night with an idea, write it down immediately. Don't try to execute mentally in the moment — just capture the concept.
- **When:** Ongoing — Hawk describes this as his most fertile creative window outside of active skating.
- **Ref:** [00:40:33](transcript.md#004033-visualization-dreaming)

### OCD Warm-Up Run
- **What:** A fixed sequence of basic tricks performed at the start of every session to gauge physical state — stiffness, timing, how much adjustment is needed.
- **How:** Run through a predetermined set of familiar tricks before attempting new material. Assess feel — height, precision, comfort. Adjust session expectations accordingly. Hawk compares it to a surgeon's pre-op checklist.
- **When:** Before every skate session.
- **Ref:** [00:40:33](transcript.md#004033-visualization-dreaming)

### Music-as-Paired-State-Activation
- **What:** Build a playlist of songs deeply associated with high-performance states. Use specific songs as anchors for high-stakes attempts.
- **How:** Identify songs that were present during peak experiences in the relevant domain. Compile a ~10-song playlist. Let it run on random — if the right song plays, that is the moment to attempt the target action. Hawk's 10-song comeback McTwist playlist included New Order's "Ceremony," Nine Inch Nails' "Getting Smaller," Gang of Four's "I Found That Essence Rare," Rancid's "Sound System," and the Prodigy's "Clammylammy" (the song playing when he landed it).
- **When:** Before and during high-pressure or high-stakes attempts.
- **Ref:** [01:44:15](transcript.md#014415-music-high-school)

### Non-Union Fracture Recovery Protocol
- **What:** After a non-union fracture (bone fails to heal and segments displace further with use), strict immobilization under specialist supervision is required before any weight-bearing activity.
- **How:** Find a specialist in non-union fractures. Follow immobilization instructions exactly — Hawk obeyed two months of complete rest after his second surgery. Supplement with disciplined sleep, nutrition, and structured diet. Use idle recovery time for alternative productive activity (Hawk did a European speaking tour during this period).
- **When:** After any fracture that does not progress normally, especially if pain persists beyond expected recovery timelines.
- **Ref:** [00:58:46](transcript.md#005846-injury-recovery-practices)

### Physical Therapy for Returning to Technical Skill
- **What:** Work with a doctor of physical therapy to passively mobilize the body through ranges of motion required by the target skill — prior to attempting that skill.
- **How:** Identify the specific physical demands of the target movement. Have the PT contort and mobilize the body through those positions before the attempt. This primes tissue and the nervous system for the movement pattern.
- **When:** In the weeks before attempting a high-difficulty return-to-sport movement after injury. Hawk worked with BestCore (Dr. Best) before his comeback 540 McTwist.
- **Ref:** [01:06:52](transcript.md#010652-healthy-life-practices-skateboarding)

### Risk Domain Management
- **What:** Deliberately avoid deep investment in adjacent high-injury-risk activities that do not serve the primary domain.
- **How:** Identify which adjacent activities carry a risk-to-reward ratio incompatible with sustaining the primary skill. Enjoy them casually but do not train seriously or push limits. Hawk surfs and snowboards recreationally but will not attempt Motocross tricks or take serious car-racing training.
- **When:** Ongoing career management — reassess any time a new adjacent activity becomes appealing.
- **Ref:** [01:30:16](transcript.md#013016-professionalism-hobbies)
