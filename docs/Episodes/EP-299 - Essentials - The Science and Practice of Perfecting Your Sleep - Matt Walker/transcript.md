## 00:00:00 Matt Walker, Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. And now, my discussion with Dr. Matt Walker. Let's start off very basic — what is sleep?

**Matt Walker:** Sleep is probably the single most effective thing you can do to reset your brain and body health. Sleep as a process, though, is an incredibly complex physiological ballet. Sleep is broadly separated into two main types: non-rapid eye movement sleep on the one hand, and rapid eye movement sleep on the other.

---

## 00:00:25 Rapid Eye Movement (REM) & Non-REM Sleep, Paralysis

**Matt Walker:** When you go into REM sleep, you are completely paralyzed — locked into a physical incarceration of your own body. The brain paralyzes the body so that the mind can dream safely, because think about how quickly we would have all been popped out of the gene pool if, say, I thought I was one of the best skydivers who could simply fly and I leapt out of my apartment window — you're done. The involuntary muscles, thankfully, aren't paralyzed, so you keep breathing and your heart keeps beating. You also go through these bizarre what we call autonomic storms.

**Matt Walker:** There are only two voluntary muscle groups spared from the paralysis. The first is your extraocular muscles — because if they were paralyzed, you wouldn't be able to have rapid eye movements. The other, which we later discovered, is the inner ear muscle. Some people have argued that the reason the eyeballs are spared from the paralysis is because if they were left inactive for long periods, you may get oxygen-related issues in the aqueous or vitreous humor — the drain systems of the anterior eye require movement, which is also why people with glaucoma have deficits in drainage through the anterior chamber.

---

## 00:02:05 Sleep Cycles, Nighttime Sleep Structure, Hormones

**Matt Walker:** When I first fall asleep, I go into the light stages of non-REM sleep — stages one and two — and then I start to descend into the deeper stages. After about twenty minutes I'm heading into stage three and then stage four non-REM sleep. As I'm cast off from the murky waters of wakefulness, my heart rate starts to drop and my brain wave activity slows from perhaps 20–50 cycles per second while awake down to around 8 cycles per second.

**Matt Walker:** Then, as I move into stages three and four of non-REM sleep, hundreds of thousands of cells in my cortex all decide to fire together and then go silent together — a remarkable physiological coordination we don't see in any other brain state. I stay there for about 20 or 30 more minutes, so now I'm roughly 60 or 70 minutes into my first sleep cycle. Then I rise back up into stage two non-REM, and after about 80 minutes I pop up and have a short REM period, before going back down again — non-REM, then REM — cycling reliably every 90 minutes.

**Matt Walker:** In the first half of the night, the majority of those 90-minute cycles are comprised mostly of deep non-REM sleep — stages three and four. Once through to the second half of the night, that seesaw balance changes and the cycles are dominated by lighter stage two non-REM sleep and increasingly more REM sleep.

**Andrew Huberman:** Who suffers more — those who lack the early phase or those who lack the later phase of the night?

**Matt Walker:** It depends on what the outcome measure is. During deep non-REM sleep we get something almost like natural blood pressure medication — deprive you of it and you'll see autonomic dysfunction, heart rate and blood pressure abnormalities the next day. Deep non-REM also governs insulin regulation and blood sugar metabolism; lose it and you can look pre-diabetic. Growth hormone, interestingly, is more REM-dependent. Testosterone peaks during REM sleep in the second half of the night. So your profile of mental and physical dysfunction will differ depending on which phase you're missing. I would prefer neither. Sleep is just so profoundly important that it fought its way through every step along the evolutionary path — and every sleep stage survived with it. That tells you these stages are non-negotiable.

## 00:07:08 Nighttime Waking Up, Fragmented Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a scenario many people experience — they go to sleep fine, three or four hours in they wake up for whatever reason, maybe a noise or the temperature, go to the restroom, come back and fall asleep after 10 or 15 minutes. How detrimental is that?

**Matt Walker:** It is perfectly natural and normal, particularly as we progress with age. At the end of each REM period in the 90-minute cycle, almost everybody wakes up briefly and makes a postural movement — we turn over because we've been paralyzed for so long and the body wants to shift. For the most part, we can be relaxed about that. Where we need to be more attentive is if you're spending long periods — 20 to 25 minutes or more — unable to get back to sleep, or if you're consciously aware of waking up six, seven, or eight times throughout the night.

**Matt Walker:** The great science of sleep in the past 5 or 10 years has established that yes, quantity matters, but quality is just as important — and you can't have one without the other for a beneficial next day. You can't get 4 hours of brilliant-quality sleep and be unimpaired, nor can you get 8 hours of very poor-quality, fragmented sleep and be unimpaired the next day.

## 00:09:20 Sunlight Exposure & Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm a big proponent of people getting sunlight — ideally sunlight, but other forms of bright light — in their eyes early in the day when they want to be awake. Essentially, during the phase of the 24-hour circadian cycle when temperature is rising, and then getting less light in their eyes as temperature goes down later in the day and evening.

**Matt Walker:** That's exactly what we recommend — try to get at least 30 to 40 minutes of exposure to some kind of natural daylight. There was some great work recently in the occupational health domain where they moved workers from offices facing walls, with no natural daylight exposure, to offices with window access. Their sleep time and sleep efficiency increased quite dramatically — total sleep time went up by more than 30 minutes and sleep efficiency improved by 5 to 10%. If you're sitting at 80% sleep efficiency we're a bit concerned, but add 10% and you're in a great echelon of healthy sleepers. Light through the eyes is essentially the only portal by which the brain and body learn the time of day.

## 00:10:43 Caffeine & Sleep Effects, Tool: Timing Caffeine

**Andrew Huberman:** Does the timing of caffeine intake play an important role in whether it works for us or against us?

**Matt Walker:** The dose and timing make the poison. Caffeine has a half-life of somewhere between 5 to 6 hours for the average adult, which means the quarter-life is roughly 10 to 12 hours. So if at 8:00 p.m. you feel tired but have a cup of coffee, the adenosine you were feeling tired from doesn't disappear — it's just blocked. When the caffeine wears off, you're hit not only with those original levels of adenosine but with all the adenosine that has been building up during the hours the caffeine was in your system. It's a tsunami wave, which is what causes the caffeine crash.

**Matt Walker:** I would usually say take your typical bedtime and count back 8 to 10 hours — that's when you should really stop using caffeine. For those who keep drinking caffeine late into the evening, they may fall asleep and stay asleep, but the depth of their deep sleep is diminished — and it can be by as much as 30%. To drop your deep sleep by 30% through aging alone I'd have to age you by 10 to 12 years; you can just do it every night with a couple of espressos instead. You wake up unrestored and reach for more coffee the next morning, which begins a dependency cycle — uppers in the morning, and sometimes alcohol in the evening to come down.

## 00:13:42 Alcohol & Sleep Effects

**Andrew Huberman:** What happens when someone has a glass or two of wine in the evening?

**Matt Walker:** Alcohol is a sedative — it's sedating your cortex. And sedation is not sleep. When people have a couple of nightcaps and feel they fall asleep faster, what's actually happening is they're losing consciousness quicker, not necessarily falling naturalistically asleep any faster. That's the first thing to keep in mind.

**Matt Walker:** The second issue is that alcohol fragments your sleep. Through various mechanisms — including activation of the fight-or-flight branch of the autonomic nervous system — alcohol causes you to wake up many more times throughout the night. Some of those awakenings you'll remember, many you won't, but your sleep is littered with punctured awakenings. When you wake up in the morning you don't feel restored. The third consequence of alcohol is that it is quite potent at blocking REM sleep. REM is critical for learning and memory, and for emotional and mental health — we think of it as overnight therapy. We've discovered over the past 20 years that there is no major psychiatric disorder in which sleep is normal, which tells us there is a very intimate association between emotional mental health and sleep health.

**Matt Walker:** I don't want to be puritanical — I'm just a scientist trying to empower people with the scientific literature so they can make informed choices. My job is not to prescribe how people live.

## 00:16:23 Cannabis; THC, Alcohol, REM Sleep & Dreams

**Andrew Huberman:** Does marijuana disrupt sleep?

**Matt Walker:** THC can speed up the time it takes to fall asleep, but if you look at the electrical brain-wave signature of sleep with and without THC, it's not a natural fit. THC also, through different mechanisms, blocks REM sleep. That's why many users tell me they don't remember their dreams while using — and when they stop, they have crazy intense dreams. The reason is a rebound mechanism: REM sleep is clever, similar to alcohol in this sense. Your brain keeps track of how much REM it should have had versus how much it actually got, and when the substance clears your system it tries to recover that lost REM. In the early morning hours, the brain not only gets back the REM it would have had, it tries to claim back all of what it lost.

**Matt Walker:** Does it get back all the REM sleep? No — it never fully recovers what was lost. But it tries, and so you have these really intense periods of REM sleep with bizarre, vivid dreams. The same homeostatic mechanism applies to alcohol. You build up a pressure for REM sleep, a debt, and the brain will devour more REM once it's been starved of it for so long.

## 00:18:38 Melatonin, Supplementation?, Dose

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love to discuss melatonin — the OG of sleep supplementation. The only source of melatonin in the brain and body is the pineal gland. Is that still true?

**Matt Walker:** Yes, from best that we can tell. The brain has a central master 24-hour clock — the suprachiasmatic nucleus — that keeps internal time. It knows 24-hour time, but it also needs to communicate that to the rest of the brain and body. One of the ways it does this is by releasing the chemical signal melatonin. When melatonin levels are low or non-existent, it communicates "it's daytime" — for diurnal species like us, that means it's time to be awake. At night, as dusk approaches, melatonin is released and signals to the rest of the brain and body that it's nighttime and time to think about sleep.

**Matt Walker:** So melatonin tells the brain and body when it's day and night — and with that, when it's time to sleep or wake — but it doesn't really help with the generation of sleep itself. Melatonin is like the starting official at the 100-meter race in the Olympics: it calls the sleep racers to the line and begins the great sleep race, but it doesn't participate in the race itself. That's a whole different set of brain chemicals and brain regions.

**Matt Walker:** When it comes to supplementation, sadly the evidence in healthy adults who are not elderly suggests melatonin is not particularly helpful as a sleep aid. A recent meta-analysis found that melatonin will only increase total sleep by 3.9 minutes on average — minutes, not even a percentage point — and will only increase sleep efficiency by 2.2%. The force is not strong with this one for non-elderly healthy adults.

**Andrew Huberman:** Typical supplement doses range from 1 milligram to 12 or even 20 milligrams. My guess is that a normal night's melatonin release for someone in their 20s, 30s, and 40s would be far lower than that.

**Matt Walker:** Yes — many magnitudes lower. The optimal doses where you do see sleep benefits are somewhere between 0.1 and 0.3 milligrams. Typical commercial doses are 10 to 20 times, maybe more, what your body would naturally expect — what we call a supra-physiological dose. The only population where we typically see benefit, and where it's often prescribed, is adults 60 and older, because as we age the pineal gland can calcify and produce less melatonin. That's one reason older adults struggle with sleep onset and maintenance, and why melatonin supplementation in older adults with insomnia has some support. I prefer behavioral tools as the first line of entry, then nutrition, then supplementation, then prescription drugs. What concerns me is when people ask "what should I take?" without first thinking about their behavior and light-viewing habits.

---

## 00:24:44 Prescription Sleep Aids, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) & Sleep

**Matt Walker:** We know in clinical practice that there may be a time and a place for sleeping pills — they are a short-term solution to certain forms of insomnia, but they are not recommended for the long term. There are also many other ways to get sleep help that don't require medication. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is a non-drug psychological approach that is just as effective as sleeping pills in the short term and more effective in the long term. One recent study found that the benefits of working with a CBT-I therapist lasted almost a decade. In contrast, when you stop sleeping pills, you typically get rebound insomnia where your sleep goes back to being as bad or worse.

**Matt Walker:** The same principle applies to supplementation. There are so many easy-to-implement things when it comes to sleep that don't require putting exogenous molecules into your body and for which the margins of safety are quite wide. I'm not telling anyone whether they should venture into medications or not — that is completely their choice. All I'm saying is that if you want to optimize your sleep, there are plenty of ways to do it that don't require swallowing, injecting, or smoking anything.

## 00:27:02 Naps, Benefits, Insomnia, Tool: Nap Length

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about naps. What are the data on naps, and what are your thoughts on keeping naps short versus getting out past 90 minutes?

**Matt Walker:** Naps can have some really great benefits. We've found benefits for cardiovascular health and blood pressure, for cortisol levels, for learning and memory, and also for emotional regulation. The naps in those studies ranged anywhere from 20 minutes to 90 minutes — we sometimes use a 90-minute window so that participants can have a full sleep cycle and get both non-REM and REM. We've also found that naps of as little as 17 minutes can have quite potent effects on learning. NASA pioneered this back in the 1990s, finding that naps of just 26 minutes improved mission performance by 34% and daytime alertness by 50%, which birthed the NASA nap culture across all terrestrial staff during that period.

**Matt Walker:** However, naps can be a double-edged sword. When you nap, you release some of the sleep pressure that has been building — you open the valve on the pressure cooker. For people who are already struggling with sleep at night, napping during the day can make their sleep problems even worse. So for people with insomnia, we typically advise against napping. If you can nap regularly and don't struggle with sleep at night, naps are just fine. Try to limit naps to about 20 to 25 minutes and don't nap later than about 6 to 7 hours before your normal bedtime — that way you don't go into the deepest stages of sleep and wake up feeling worse than before. No one should feel guilty about getting the sleep they need. Society has stigmatized sleep with labels like slothful or lazy, but sleep is a right — a civil right — of all human beings.

## 00:30:34 Sleep Tips, Tools: "Do Nothing"; Winddown Routine; Worry Journal; Clocks

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any unconventional sleep tips?

**Matt Walker:** The first unconventional tip — and it may seem strange — is: if you've had a bad night of sleep, do nothing. What I mean is don't wake up any later the next day, don't sleep in to try to make up for it, don't nap, don't consume extra caffeine, and don't go to bed any earlier. If you wake up later you won't be sleepy until later in the evening, so when you get into bed at your normal time you won't be able to sleep — and you'll think your sleep is broken. By waking later you've reduced the window of adenosine accumulation before your normal bedtime. Resist the urge to go to bed at 9:00 if your body isn't ready until 10:30.

**Matt Walker:** The second tip is to have a winddown routine. Many of us treat sleep like a light switch — jump into bed, turn off the light, and expect sleep to arrive. But sleep is a physiological process; it's much more like landing a plane that takes time to gradually descend onto solid ground. Meditate for 10 or 15 minutes before bed, do light stretching, read. Try not to watch television in bed — too much light stimulating your brain, too activating. Think of it like downshifting gears as you pull into the garage rather than screeching to a halt from 60 miles per hour.

**Matt Walker:** The third tip is the worry journal. About an hour or two before bed — not right before — write down all of the concerns you have. It's like closing down all of the emotional tabs on your browser. If you shut your computer down with too many tabs open, you come back in the morning and the computer is red-hot because it never went to sleep. Studies from good researchers found that keeping one of those journals decreased the time it takes to fall asleep by 50% — well on par with any pharmaceutical agent.

**Matt Walker:** The fourth tip is to remove all clock faces from your bedroom, including your phone. If you're having a tough night, knowing that it's 3:22 in the morning or 4:48 in the morning does not help you in the slightest and will only make matters worse. Clock-watching creates anxiety that compounds the original problem.
