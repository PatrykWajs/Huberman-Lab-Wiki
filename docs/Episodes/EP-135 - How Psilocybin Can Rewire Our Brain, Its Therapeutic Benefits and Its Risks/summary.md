# Episode 135: How Psilocybin Can Rewire Our Brain, Its Therapeutic Benefits & Its Risks

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman delivers a comprehensive scientific exploration of psilocybin — what it is chemically, how it works at the cellular and neural circuit level, and how it triggers neuroplasticity. The episode covers the biology of psilocybin's conversion to psilocin, its highly selective action on the serotonin 2A receptor (and why that selectivity makes it fundamentally different from SSRIs), and the mechanics of a therapeutic psilocybin journey: set, setting, physical safety, eye mask use, the critical role of music playlists, and how the 4–6 hour session contour maps to specific subjective states. Huberman explains the key subjective phenomena — synesthesia, perceptual blending, ego dissolution, and oceanic boundlessness — and what research shows predicts positive versus negative therapeutic outcomes, including the role of anxiety management using the physiological sigh. The episode also covers the structural brain changes induced by psilocybin (dendritic spine growth in frontal cortex pyramidal neurons), the growing clinical trial data showing 60–75% depression remission rates at 25–30 mg doses combined with psychotherapy, conditions where evidence is strong versus weak (depression, cancer anxiety, addiction, OCD), and the contraindications, adverse events, and legal status as of May 2023.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Psilocybin is a tryptamine that closely mirrors serotonin structurally.** When ingested, it is converted to its active form psilocin in the gut and liver. Unlike serotonin (which binds broadly to many receptor types), psilocin selectively and potently activates the serotonin 2A receptor in the neocortex. ([00:12:00](transcript.md#001200-psilocybin-becomes-psilocin-in-the-gut-serotonin))

2. **The serotonin 2A receptor is the key driver of therapeutic outcomes.** It is densely expressed on pyramidal neurons in the prefrontal cortex. One or two psilocybin sessions can fully occupy these receptors in ways that produce lasting changes in mood, cognition, and behavior — something SSRIs, which flood serotonin broadly, cannot replicate. ([00:17:00](transcript.md#001700-the-serotonin-2a-receptor-therapeutic-outcomes-ssris-vs-psilocybin))

3. **Visual hallucinations occur even with eyes closed because the visual cortex has the highest serotonin 2A receptor density.** This is why clinical protocols require eye masks — not to suppress hallucinations, but to redirect attention inward so that spontaneous visual phenomena combine with thoughts and memories rather than with the external environment. Eyes-open sessions draw cognitive attention to distorted external perceptions and reduce therapeutic benefit. ([00:21:40](transcript.md#002140-serotonin-receptor-expression-visual-hallucinations--eyes-closed))

4. **Psilocybin dramatically expands functional connectivity across brain networks.** The default mode network, thalamo-cortical circuits, and other networks become far more interconnected during a session. Thalamic gating of sensory information broadens, pyramidal neuron apical dendrites communicate more laterally across brain areas, and the normal hierarchical flow from periphery to cortex is disrupted. This reduction in modularity underlies perceptual blending, synesthesia, and ego dissolution. ([00:59:48](transcript.md#005948-brain-networks-therapeutic-outcomes))

5. **Set, setting, and support are scientifically validated determinants of outcome.** Positive therapeutic results in clinical trials depend on: intention-setting before the session, a physically enclosed safe space (no accessible hazards), trained non-intoxicated guides, lying down with an eye mask, and curated music. Psilocybin use outside this framework carries significantly higher risk of adverse events and poor outcomes. ([00:36:21](transcript.md#003621-psychedelic-journey-set-setting--support))

6. **Music is a primary driver of the psilocybin journey, not a passive backdrop.** Clinical playlists follow the session contour: low-volume classical music at onset → high-energy percussion and drums during the peak (45–90 min) → softer choral or female-vocal music as intensity decreases → nature sounds during the comedown. Post-psilocybin neuroimaging shows increased low-frequency brain responses to music in reward and emotional areas; formerly anhedonic patients report being able to feel music again. ([00:43:43](transcript.md#004343-music--the-psilocybin-journey-duration-of-effects))

7. **Therapeutic dosing ranges are well-defined.** Microdosing is 1–3 mg/day. A moderate single session dose is ~10 mg (~1 g dried mushrooms). The therapeutic sweet spot for depression is 25–30 mg in one or two sessions spaced approximately one week apart. Mushroom psilocybin content varies 0.5%–2% by weight, making precise dosing difficult; clinical trials use synthetic psilocybin for consistent delivery. ([00:30:28](transcript.md#003028-psilocybin-magic-mushrooms-dosing-micro-dosing-heroic-doses))

8. **Oceanic boundlessness, unity, and bliss — not hallucinations — predict positive therapeutic outcomes.** The altered states of consciousness questionnaire data show feelings of unity, spirituality, blissfulness, and insight during the 25 mg session strongly correlate with lasting depression relief. Hallucinations (complex imagery, synesthesia) occurred in essentially all participants at 25 mg but did not differentiate responders from non-responders. ([01:16:53](transcript.md#011653-positive-psychedelic-journeys-unity-oceanic-boundlessness))

9. **Extreme anxiety is inversely correlated with therapeutic benefit, but moving through anxiety is essential.** Non-responders reported significantly higher subjective anxiety than responders. The guide's role is to encourage participants to let go and move through the peak anxiety rather than suppress or escalate it — ego dissolution and some anxiety are part of the process, but panic that overwhelms the session blocks positive neuroplastic outcomes. ([01:25:23](transcript.md#012523-bad-trips-anxiety--physiological-sighs))

10. **The physiological sigh is a validated real-time anxiety reduction tool for psilocybin sessions.** A double-inhale through the nose (full inhale + sharp second inhale on top) followed by a long slow exhale rapidly offloads CO2 and activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Matthew Johnson's lab at Johns Hopkins has begun teaching this technique to participants before sessions so they can self-regulate during the peak. Validated in a Cell Reports Medicine paper co-authored by Huberman and David Spiegel. ([01:25:23](transcript.md#012523-bad-trips-anxiety--physiological-sighs))

11. **Psilocybin induces rapid and persistent growth of dendritic spines in frontal cortex neurons.** In vivo mouse studies (published in Neuron) show new mushroom-shaped dendritic spines appear on pyramidal neurons during a session and persist long after the drug has cleared — these are the sites of new excitatory connections. Postmortem tissue from depressed humans shows reduced frontal cortex spine density; psilocybin appears to reverse this structural deficit. ([01:36:11](transcript.md#013611-neuroplasticity-structural-brain-changes--psilocybin))

12. **Psilocybin works as a neuroplasticity wedge, not as plasticity itself.** The journey initiates neuroplastic events (expanded connectivity, new dendritic spines) but adaptive changes in mood and behavior are largely consolidated in the weeks after the session. The session opens the window; post-session integration therapy and intentional behavior direct where the neuroplasticity lands. Neurogenesis is not the primary mechanism — structural changes to existing neurons (spine growth, dendritic branching) dominate. ([01:12:39](transcript.md#011239-depression--psychedelics-as-neuroplasticity-wedge))

13. **60–75% of people with major depressive disorder show substantial relief from one or two psilocybin sessions.** Multiple randomized clinical trials using 25–30 mg doses in proper therapeutic settings with combined psychotherapy report these rates — including patients who had not responded to SSRIs or CBT. Effect sizes are ~2.5x greater than psychotherapy alone and ~4x greater than standard pharmacological treatment. A single 25 mg session (NEJM, Nov 2022) showed significant relief out to 12 weeks post-session. ([01:48:08](transcript.md#014808-psychedelics-therapeutic-breakthroughs--depression))

14. **Evidence strength varies by condition.** Strongest evidence: cancer-related depression/anxiety and treatment-resistant depression. Moderate evidence: alcohol use disorder, tobacco addiction. Early-stage evidence: OCD, cluster headaches/migraines, demoralization from AIDS diagnosis. ([01:32:57](transcript.md#013257-therapeutic-use-of-psilocybin))

15. **Psilocybin is contraindicated for several populations.** Absolute contraindications: personal or first-degree family history of schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or psychotic episodes; current use of SSRIs or serotonergic antidepressants (do not stop without physician guidance); pregnancy or breastfeeding; age under 25. Violating these dramatically increases the risk of triggering lasting psychotic episodes, not just during the session. ([00:27:21](transcript.md#002721-safety--cautions-for-specific-patient-populations))

16. **Psilocybin has FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation and is being decriminalized.** As of 2018, FDA granted breakthrough therapy status for psilocybin-assisted therapy. As of May 2023, Oregon has approved regulated clinical therapeutic access; Oakland and a few other jurisdictions have decriminalized it. Psilocybin remains a Schedule I substance federally in the US. ([00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-psilocybin-legal-considerations))

## Tools & Protocols

### Therapeutic Psilocybin Session Framework

**What:** The structured protocol used in clinical trials to maximize safety and therapeutic benefit from a psilocybin session.

**How:**
- Conduct multiple preparatory sessions with trained guides to establish intention, rapport, and teach in-session tools (e.g., physiological sigh)
- Fast (no food) for at least 4 hours before the session; gut acidity drives psilocin conversion rate and onset timing
- Ensure setting is physically enclosed and safe — no accessible windows, traffic, or bodies of water
- Lie down (occasionally sit) in a comfortable, private, aesthetically calm space
- Wear an eye mask for the majority (ideally entirety) of the session to direct attention inward
- Play a curated music playlist: low-volume classical at onset → high-energy percussion during the peak (45–90 min) → soft choral/female vocals as intensity tapers → nature sounds during comedown
- Have two trained, non-intoxicated guides present throughout to ensure safety and support
- Use 25–30 mg synthetic psilocybin as the target therapeutic dose; one or two sessions spaced ~1 week apart
- Conduct integration sessions (talk therapy, journaling) in the 1–4 weeks following to translate the neuroplasticity window into lasting behavioral and emotional changes

**When:** For therapeutic use of psilocybin in clinical or research settings for major depression, treatment-resistant depression, cancer-related anxiety, or addiction. Not for unsupervised use.

### Physiological Sigh for Acute Anxiety Management

**What:** A breath-control technique used to reduce acute anxiety during the peak of a psilocybin journey.

**How:** Take a full inhale through the nose, then add a second sharp inhale on top (double inhale), followed by a long, slow, complete exhale through the mouth. Repeat as needed. This offloads CO2 rapidly and activates the parasympathetic nervous system.

**When:** During the anxious or disorienting peak phase of a psilocybin session when anxiety is escalating.

**Ref:** [01:25:23](transcript.md#012523-bad-trips-anxiety--physiological-sighs)

### Psilocybin Dosing Guide

**What:** Reference framework for understanding psilocybin dose categories used in research and practice.

**How:**
- **Microdose:** 1–3 mg synthetic psilocybin daily (approximately 0.1–0.5 g dried mushrooms)
- **Moderate single session:** ~10 mg (~1–1.5 g dried mushrooms)
- **Therapeutic dose:** 25–30 mg (~3.5 g dried mushrooms) — one or two sessions spaced ~1 week apart
- **Heroic dose:** >30 mg (typically outside clinical use)
- Note: mushroom psilocybin content varies widely (0.5%–2% by weight); synthetic psilocybin used in trials allows precise dosing

**When:** As a reference for understanding clinical literature; not a guide for self-administration.

**Ref:** [00:30:28](transcript.md#003028-psilocybin-magic-mushrooms-dosing-micro-dosing-heroic-doses)

---

### Post-Session Integration (Talk Therapy)

**What:** Combining psilocybin sessions with structured psychotherapy before and after to anchor insights and sustain therapeutic gains.

**How:** Preparatory CBT or psychoanalytic sessions before psilocybin to establish therapeutic alliance and intentions. Integration sessions in the 1–4 weeks after the journey to process experiences, reinforce behavioral changes, and translate the "neuroplasticity window" into lasting new patterns.

**When:** Used in all major clinical trials; appears to be a key amplifier of psilocybin's antidepressant effects.
