# Episode 263: How to Build Strength, Endurance & Flexibility at Any Age | Pavel Tsatsouline

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Pavel Tsatsouline — former Soviet special forces training instructor, founder of StrongFirst, and the person credited with bringing kettlebell training to the West — for a comprehensive conversation on building strength, endurance, and flexibility at any age. Pavel frames strength as "the mother quality" of all physical fitness, explaining that neurological adaptation — not muscle hypertrophy — is the primary driver of strength gains, and that treating strength as a practiced skill (via protocols like Greasing the Groove) leads to faster, safer, and more durable progress. The episode covers the contrasting Soviet and American powerlifting periodization systems, the concept of heterochronicity in recovery, and Pavel's "anti-glycolytic" approach to building endurance without accumulating damaging lactate. Practical topics include how to combine strength and endurance training without interference, why training to failure is counterproductive, abdominal pressurization for spine protection, the role of gaze direction in maximizing force output, and how flexibility is governed as much by the nervous system as by tissue length. Pavel also shares inspiring examples of his 87-year-old father performing 100+ pull-ups and squats weekly, demonstrating that consistent, sub-maximal practice remains highly effective across the full lifespan.

## Key Takeaways

- **Strength is the mother quality of all fitness.** Soviet research showed that placing endurance athletes — triathletes, cyclists, marathon runners — on heavy, low-rep strength programs made them race faster without adding muscle. Every sport and lifestyle activity requires a strength foundation. [00:02:29](transcript.md#000229-fitness-strength-model-athlete)

- **Strength is a skill, not a physical quality.** Soviet researcher Tanov found that as weightlifters got stronger over months, their EMG activity for the same weights dropped — the nervous system became more economical. Thomas Rasch showed no correlation between muscle growth and strength in the 1950s. Gains are primarily neural, not structural. [00:39:53](transcript.md#003953-greasing-the-groove-cramming-analogy-strength-is-a-skill)

- **Greasing the Groove (GTG) is the most efficient strength-acquisition method.** Perform a movement at ~40–50% of max reps, many times throughout the day, with at least 10-minute rests, stopping completely fresh every time. Spaced practice outperforms massed practice by a factor documented across 1,000+ papers back to the 19th century. Paul Anderson reinvented this independently, training across many hours with milk breaks between sets. [00:48:27](transcript.md#004827-tool-greasing-the-groove-protocol)

- **Heterochronicity governs how to program recovery.** Different systems — muscles, connective tissue, nervous system, endocrine system — recover at different rates. Soviet weightlifting managed this with high frequency and sub-maximal loads (overnight muscle recovery, minimal CNS stress). American powerlifting managed it by training hard only two weeks out of four. Both work because both respect the principle. [01:00:00](transcript.md#010000-frequency--recovery-heterochronicity-soviet-vs-american-training)

- **Never train to failure.** Each rep closer to failure exponentially increases recovery time. A Spanish study found that training to failure converted type 2X fast fibers to slower 2A — making it physiologically an endurance event. Failure also triggers long-term depression of neural pathways (the evil twin of long-term potentiation), and if emotionally charged, the adrenaline spike further cements the inhibitory memory. Ed Coan never missed a training lift in decades of world records. [03:03:42](transcript.md#030342-train-to-failure-recovery)

- **Anti-glycolytic training builds the most durable and specific endurance.** Verkhoshansky showed that the correct goal is to postpone fatigue — not to habituate athletes to it. Bikin's experiment had MMA fighters do 3 reps at 70% 1RM per exercise, rest 1 minute, repeat — never producing a burn. This group dramatically outperformed the traditional circuit-training group on the highest-correlating competitive performance metric: rate of heart rate recovery after a maximal deadlift set. [02:11:30](transcript.md#021130-not-seeking-the-pump-repeated-sprint-ability-tool-anti-glycolytic-endurance-training)

- **German interval training is the correct way to build cardiac stroke volume.** Sprint 60–90 seconds to ~85–90% max heart rate, then immediately jog — never stop — until HR drops to ~60–70%. Active recovery while HR is still high literally stretches the heart walls, increasing stroke volume. Starting with high-intensity work before building stroke volume leads to arrhythmias and poor performance. [01:46:38](transcript.md#014638-rest-periods-interval-training-tool-german-interval-training)

- **Grip strength is a top longevity biomarker and a full-body multiplier.** Making a maximally tight fist causes "irradiation" — neural tension overflows into adjacent muscles, instantly increasing strength in any exercise. Huberman adds that motor neuron degeneration with aging proceeds distal-to-proximal (outside-in); training peripheral muscles may offset this. Best tools: Iron Mind Captains of Crush grippers, rope climbing, heavy kettlebell snatches. [00:16:29](transcript.md#001629-dips-pull-ups-farmer-carry-tools-kettle-bell-mile-grip-strength--longevity)

- **Abdominal pressurization ("pressurizing the cylinder") is the correct spine-protection and force-amplification technique.** Inhale through pursed lips to engage the diaphragm, pull up the pelvic floor, then create intra-abdominal pressure by contracting the entire trunk circumference simultaneously. Soviet research identified a "pneumatic reflex": intra-abdominal pressure directly increases alpha motor neuron sensitivity — functioning as a volume control for muscular output. This same pressure controls flexibility: releasing it during stretching allows muscles to relax and reach new ranges. [03:51:21](transcript.md#035121-core-work-abdominals-tools-tension--attention-pressurize-abs)

- **Exhalation during maximal effort measurably increases strength.** Studies on fighters and lifters confirm this. Mechanism: pneumatic reflex + intra-abdominal pressure timed with contraction. Grunting and hissing in training are physiologically inevitable for maximal efforts — not performance. [04:03:34](transcript.md#040334-breathing-force-strength)

- **Gaze direction activates spinal reflexes that affect full-body strength.** Looking slightly upward activates tonic neck reflexes that increase extensor tone. Looking downward (chin toward chest) facilitates knee extension — used by some deadlifters. Neutral gaze (eyes follow the body's alignment, look at an appropriate floor spot) is the safest default, per Andy Bolton. Blindfolded lifting, pioneered by Soviet specialist Robert Roman, deepens proprioception further. [04:05:02](transcript.md#040502-directing-gaze-while-weightlifting)

- **Fragmentation allows more total training volume with less fatigue.** Dividing a session into 2–3 shorter bouts across the day mirrors the physiology of spaced practice. Soviet track coaches inserted 3×3 strength sets at 80% during practice sessions and observed a "strength after-effect" — tonic alertness and performance elevation lasting until the next day. Huberman notes adrenaline released via specific motor cortex → adrenal pathways (Peter Strick's research) explains this mechanism. [02:40:20](transcript.md#024020-post-exercise-fatigue-tools-fragmentation-feedback-volume)

- **Older adults maintain high strength capacity with consistency over intensity.** Pavel's father at 87 still does 50+ pull-ups and 100+ powerlifting-style bodyweight squats twice weekly. At 75 he deadlifted in the low 400s without a belt and held American Masters records. Pavel's father-in-law at 64 used GTG to achieve 20 pull-ups — something he could never do as an active U.S. Marine. Medium reps, medium effort, never to failure — works at every age. [03:17:46](transcript.md#031746-older-adults--strength-training-consistency-over-intensity)

- **Concurrent strength and endurance is achievable without interference if programmed correctly.** Neural-focused lifting (low reps, heavy): do it fresh, timing of endurance before does not matter much. Hypertrophy-oriented lifting: avoid endurance for 36–48 hours after. Keep sessions under ~60 minutes — longer sessions raise cortisol and shift adaptations toward endurance. Maintain any quality with just 1 session/week at 80% with 3×3. Shift priorities monthly. [02:23:05](transcript.md#022305-tool-planning-strength--endurance-training-individualization)

## Tools & Protocols

### Greasing the Groove (GTG)
- **What:** Spaced sub-maximal practice of a target movement throughout the day, never approaching fatigue.
- **How:** Do ~40–50% of your max reps per set (e.g., if max pull-ups = 10, do sets of 4–5). Rest at least 10 minutes between sets — the gap creates desirable difficulty analogous to spaced learning and allows initial memory consolidation. Can interleave 2–3 exercises (e.g., Zercher squat + bench press alternating every 5 minutes within a 10-minute window). Distributable through lifestyle: heavy grippers under desk, pull-up bar in doorway.
- **When:** Daily or near-daily; 2–3 days on, 1 day off as a rough guide. Avoid on days with planned near-maximal efforts. Valid beginner through advanced.
- **Ref:** [00:48:27](transcript.md#004827-tool-greasing-the-groove-protocol)

### Essential Training Movements
- **What:** A minimal battery of exercises covering the full body: posterior chain (narrow Sumo deadlift or kettlebell swing/snatch), squat pattern (Zercher squat or goblet squat), horizontal push (bench press), vertical pull (pull-up or weighted pull-up), and loaded carry (kettlebell mile). Dips added for those with healthy shoulders.
- **How:** Choose 2–5 exercises total from this menu. No reason to change them — there is no correlation between exercise variety and results in weightlifting research. Vary only on the margins (grip width, pause reps, block height, hang vs. floor) to address sticking points.
- **When:** Foundation of any program, beginner through elite.
- **Ref:** [00:07:19](transcript.md#000719-tool-essential-training-movements)

### Anti-Glycolytic Strength-Endurance Circuit
- **What:** Repeated sets of compound lifts at ~70% of 1RM for only 3 reps per set, with ~1-minute rest, repeated 10–15 rounds. Work stays in creatine phosphate / aerobic window — no burning, no heavy breathing. Based on Bikin's MMA research; also expressed as Pavel's "Iron Cardio" (1 clean + 1 press + 1 front squat per arm, put down, rest, repeat for 30 minutes).
- **How:** Select 1–3 compound exercises (e.g., Zercher squat, towel pull-ups, close-grip bench press). Do 3 reps of each. Rest 1 minute (walk around, shake limbs off). Repeat 10–15 rounds. Can also use a single exercise only.
- **When:** 2–4 times per week. Treat as light strength days — does not interfere with primary heavy strength sessions on intervening days. Builds repeat-sprint ability, improves heart rate recovery, and modestly increases strength.
- **Ref:** [02:11:30](transcript.md#021130-not-seeking-the-pump-repeated-sprint-ability-tool-anti-glycolytic-endurance-training)

### German Interval Training (Cardiac Stroke Volume)
- **What:** Effort intervals of 60–90 seconds at ~85–90% max heart rate, followed by active jogging recovery until HR returns to ~60–70%, repeated multiple rounds.
- **How:** Sprint, row, or cycle hard for 60–90 seconds. Do NOT stop — immediately transition to a jog or easy effort. The continued leg muscle contraction milks venous blood back to the heart while HR is still high, literally stretching the heart walls and increasing stroke volume. Repeat 4–8 rounds. This is not arbitrary HIIT — the physiology is specific and deliberately managed.
- **When:** 1–2 times per week for building aerobic base. Do not combine with heavy strength training in the same session.
- **Ref:** [01:46:38](transcript.md#014638-rest-periods-interval-training-tool-german-interval-training)

### Glycolytic Power Repeats (Concurrent Cardio + Muscle Growth)
- **What:** 30-second maximal efforts (Wingate-style: cycling, kettlebell snatches, uphill sprints) followed by ~5 minutes of full rest, repeated 5+ times.
- **How:** Go all-out for 30 seconds. Walk or sit for 5 minutes. Repeat. Based on Dybala and Burgomaster research showing this protocol simultaneously improves cardiovascular fitness, peripheral mitochondrial density, and builds muscle — because the brief high-acidosis window makes cell membranes permeable to anabolic hormones (Vassiliev's theory: free creatine from creatine phosphate burndown is also anabolic).
- **When:** 1–2 times per week. Can substitute for steady-state cardio when time is limited.
- **Ref:** [01:51:34](transcript.md#015134-tool-cardiovascular-training-glycolytic-power-repeats-muscle-growth)

### Pause Reps (Isometric at the Sticking Point)
- **What:** Add a 3–5 second pause at the hardest point of a movement (bottom of squat, bar on chest in bench press) while maintaining full muscular tension.
- **How:** Lower under control at the tempo of a near-maximal attempt — not exaggeratedly slow. Pause without bouncing or releasing tension. Explode through the concentric. Removes momentum, builds the sticking point, and teaches disinhibition (the ability to continue driving when bar velocity approaches zero). Also used as a positioning drill: load more weight than possible to lift, wedge into position, and apply pressure to find the optimal angle.
- **When:** Use on 1–2 sets per exercise for sticking-point work; not every set. Valuable as a warm-up pattern to rehearse correct position.
- **Ref:** [00:29:57](transcript.md#002957-concentric-vs-eccentric-only-movements-isometric-tool-pause-reps)

### Zercher Squat
- **What:** Squat with the barbell cradled in the crooks of the elbows at about waist height, in front of the body. Arms can be parallel, crossed, or interlocked depending on comfort.
- **How:** Walk the bar out of a rack (picking it up from the floor is an advanced skill — avoid as a beginner). Assume your natural squat stance. The load position triggers reflexive trunk stabilization automatically, teaching correct bracing without cueing. Goal for advanced athletes: double bodyweight.
- **When:** Primary substitute for back squat when axial spinal loading is contraindicated or shoulders/wrists are compromised. Also an excellent teaching tool for beginners to feel what correct abdominal tension feels like.
- **Ref:** [01:22:45](transcript.md#012245-bell-squat-non-spine-compressing-leg-work-tool-zercher-squat)

### Wave Loading & Step Loading (Periodization for Intermediates and Beyond)
- **What:** Two methods to structure load variation once linear progression stalls. Wave loading: increase weight across 3 sets, then start a new wave slightly heavier than the previous wave's opening weight. Step loading (Pavel's preferred method for self-coached trainees): start fairly heavy, stay with that weight until it feels light, then increase — reverse of the conventional light-to-heavy approach.
- **How:** Classic American powerlifting wave (4-week block): Week 1 light, Week 2 moderate, Week 3 near old PR, Week 4 new PR. Reset and climb again. Franco Colombo's variant: moderate → heavy → moderate → very heavy. For older/more experienced lifters: 6–8 week cycles are better than 12–16 week cycles.
- **When:** Intermediate through advanced trainees. Replace linear progression when beginner gains stall. Older lifters shift to shorter cycles.
- **Ref:** [01:28:41](transcript.md#012841-shorter-cycles-linear--wave-progression-step-loading-variable-overload)

### Wall Squat (Flexibility Protocol)
- **What:** Face a wall, stand close to it, and perform a deep squat without knees, chest, or head touching the wall.
- **How:** Start with feet ~12 inches from the wall, toes forward or slightly out. Squat slowly, keeping torso vertical. The wall provides immediate self-correcting feedback — any deviation causes contact. Progress by moving feet closer over weeks. Originated in shiko (sumo/martial arts) practice; applied at StrongFirst for teaching upright squat mechanics and hip mobility.
- **When:** Daily as a flexibility and motor-pattern practice. Use as warm-up or standalone session.
- **Ref:** [03:08:40](transcript.md#030840-flexibility-range-of-motion-training-kettle-bell-tool-wall-squat)

### Abdominal Pressurization Protocol
- **What:** Creating intra-abdominal pressure from below (pelvic floor), above (diaphragm), and all around (obliques + lower back) simultaneously — not flexing the rectus abdominis alone.
- **How:** (1) Inhale through pursed lips or a single nostril to engage the diaphragm and drive breath into the abdomen. (2) Pull up the pelvic floor as if preventing a bathroom emergency. (3) Place tongue between teeth and hiss in a ratcheting manner — direct all pressure below the neck into the abdominal cylinder. For lifting: hold this pressurized state (Valsalva that didn't happen). For long-duration work: "breathe behind the shield" — maintain trunk pressure while still breathing normally. Match pressurization timing with the moment of maximal force output.
- **When:** Every heavy compound rep. Practice as a standalone drill (contraindicated for those with high blood pressure or heart concerns without medical clearance).
- **Ref:** [03:51:21](transcript.md#035121-core-work-abdominals-tools-tension--attention-pressurize-abs)

### Fragmentation (Splitting Sessions Across the Day)
- **What:** Divide a planned training session into 2–3 shorter bouts separated by hours.
- **How:** Example: heavy deadlifts in the morning, pressing work in the evening. Or distribute GTG sets throughout the day instead of one gym block. Soviet track coaches inserted 3×3 at ~80% 1RM mid-practice (every 10 minutes or when athletes drooped) and observed tonic alertness increases lasting until the next day.
- **When:** When managing fatigue, when time is limited, or when total planned volume cannot be maintained with quality in a single session.
- **Ref:** [02:40:20](transcript.md#024020-post-exercise-fatigue-tools-fragmentation-feedback-volume)

### Concurrent Strength + Endurance Planning
- **What:** Programming both strength and endurance in the same week without interference.
- **How:** Neural-focused strength (low reps, heavy): do it fresh; what happens before matters little; endurance timing after is flexible. Hypertrophy-oriented lifting: avoid endurance for 36–48 hours after the session. Keep sessions under ~60 minutes (longer sessions elevate cortisol and shift adaptations toward endurance). Maintain any quality with just 1 session/week at 80% with 3×3. Shift priority monthly: dominant quality gets 3 days, secondary gets 2, tertiary gets 1.
- **When:** Year-round. Stuart McGill's "biblical week" (2 strength, 2 mobility, 2 endurance) cited as a solid balanced template.
- **Ref:** [02:23:05](transcript.md#022305-tool-planning-strength--endurance-training-individualization)

### Gaze Direction for Strength
- **What:** Intentional placement of gaze slightly upward during heavy lifts to activate tonic neck reflexes that increase extensor tone body-wide.
- **How:** Before initiating the lift, set gaze approximately 10–15 degrees above horizontal. Looking downward (chin toward chest) conversely facilitates knee extension — some deadlifters use this. Neutral gaze following the body's alignment is the safe default (per Andy Bolton). Blindfolded sets (Robert Roman's technique) eliminate visual dependence and deepen proprioceptive awareness.
- **When:** Apply on every heavy compound lift — squat, deadlift, press. Experiment with downward gaze on deadlifts only after mastering neutral.
- **Ref:** [04:05:02](transcript.md#040502-directing-gaze-while-weightlifting)

### Kettlebell Mile
- **What:** Run or walk one mile while holding a kettlebell at the side (suitcase carry), switching hands as often as needed.
- **How:** Load is ~30% of bodyweight (Pavel's example: ~60–70 lbs for a 210 lb person). Switch hands frequently — brief unilateral contractions followed by brief rest is the anti-glycolytic principle applied to grip and stabilizers. Do not carry on one side for the full mile. Start by walking; build to running over weeks. Developed by Dr. Mike Prevost for the U.S. Marine Corps.
- **When:** 1–2 times per week as loaded carry conditioning. Improves running posture, stabilizer endurance, and provides anti-glycolytic conditioning without the spinal loading of rucking.
- **Ref:** [00:16:29](transcript.md#001629-dips-pull-ups-farmer-carry-tools-kettle-bell-mile-grip-strength--longevity)

Soviet sports scientist, known in the West as "the father of plyometrics," but also the originator of the "anti-glycolytic revolution" in endurance training. ### David Rigert
Soviet Olympic weightlifting champion. Regarded by many as the greatest weightlifter of all time. ### Peter Strick
Neuroscientist at University of Pittsburgh. Mapped a two-synapse connection from specific motor cortex areas directly to the adrenal glands, providing the mechanism by which deliberate muscle engagement (especially core bracing) releases adrenaline via the vagus nerve, increasing noradrenaline in the brain and elevating alertness.

### Aleks Salkin
StrongFirst instructor based in France. Developed the "Iron Cardio" GTG-style protocol for law enforcement personnel on a stakeout — single clean + press + front squat per arm, metronomic rhythm, 30 minutes, no pump sought.
