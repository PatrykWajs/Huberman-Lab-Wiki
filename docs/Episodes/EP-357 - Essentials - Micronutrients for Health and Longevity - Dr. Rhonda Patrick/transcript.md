**Andrew Huberman:** Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. And now for my discussion with Dr. Rhonda Patrick. Rhonda, welcome. ## [00:00:20] Physical Challenges, Stress Response Pathways, Hormesis, Temperature

**Andrew Huberman:** Well, I have so many questions, but I want to start off with a kind of new but old theme that you're very familiar with. Temperature is a powerful stimulus as we know for biology. And you've covered a lot of material related to the utility of cold, but also the utility of heat. As I learn more and more from your content and from the various papers, it seems that cold can stimulate a number of things like increases in metabolism, brown fat, etc., but heat seems to be able to do a lot of the same things. I wonder whether or not the discomfort of cold, deliberate cold exposure, and the discomfort of heat might be anchoring to the same pathway. Would you mind sharing with us a little bit about what happens when we get into a cold environment on purpose and what happens when we get into a hot environment on purpose?

**Rhonda Patrick:** Let's take a step back. I think you brought up a really important point here. You know, we evolved to intermittently challenge ourselves. Before we had Instacart, where you could basically just get your food delivered to you, we were out hunting, gathering, we were moving, and we had to be physically fit. You couldn't catch your prey if you were a sedentary slob. Physical activity was a part of everyday life. And caloric restriction or intermittent fasting was also a part of it — food scarcity was something common, as was eating plants, getting these compounds that I mentioned. These are all types of intermittent challenges that activate genetic pathways in our bodies.

**Rhonda Patrick:** These are often referred to in science as stress response pathways because they respond to a little bit of stress. Physical activity is strenuous. Fasting is a little bit stressful. Heat, cold — these things are all types of little intermittent challenges. There is a lot of crosstalk between these stressors and the genetic pathways that they activate. And these genetic pathways that are activated help you deal with stress in a way that is not only beneficial for that little stressor, but stays active and helps you deal with the stress of normal metabolism, normal immune function, aging. This concept is referred to as hormesis.

**Rhonda Patrick:** Stress response pathways are activated by a variety of stressors. One pathway is called heat shock proteins. As their name implies, they are activated very robustly by heat. But you can eat a plant like broccoli sprouts, which is high in something called sulforaphane, and it activates heat shock proteins among other things. It also activates a very powerful detoxification pathway called NRF2, which helps you detoxify things like carcinogens. Cold also activates heat shock proteins. Now, you're going to more robustly activate heat shock proteins from heat versus cold, but there is some overlap. This concept of hormesis has a very profound antioxidant, anti-inflammatory response — or the production of more stem cells, or something like autophagy.

## [00:03:43] Tool: Sulforaphane & Detoxification, Cruciferous Vegetables, Moringa

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned plants as a route to creating intermittent challenge. There's a lot of debate — mostly online — about whether or not plants are our friends or plants are trying to kill us. The extreme version from pure carnivore types is that plants are trying to kill us. These generalizations are just not useful. I think a lot of people in the blogosphere gravitate towards them because it's just easier and a lot more sensational. But with respect to plants, there is evidence that sulforaphane is a very powerful activator of the NRF2 pathway.

**Rhonda Patrick:** This pathway regulates a lot of genes related to glutathione production — genes involved in detoxifying compounds we're exposed to from our food, like heterocyclic amines. There have been genome-wide association studies where people that don't have a certain gene version for detoxifying heterocyclic amines are very prone to colon cancer and increased cancer risk. But if they eat a lot of broccoli and cruciferous vegetables, it negates that risk because they're getting sulforaphane which activates glutathione transferase. Glutathione is a major antioxidant in our brain and in our vascular system. There is human evidence that compounds like sulforaphane — found in broccoli sprouts at up to 100 times the concentration of regular broccoli — are activating glutathione in the brain.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can we cook the broccoli and still get these nutrients, or do we have to eat it raw? I confess eating raw broccoli is really aversive to me.

**Rhonda Patrick:** You do somewhat lower the sulforaphane levels when you cook the broccoli. However, there was a study a few years back that showed adding 1 gram of mustard seed powder ground into your cooked broccoli increases the sulforaphane by four-fold. I personally shifted to supplementation with sulforaphane. There's also another compound called moringa — it's like a cousin — and it activates the NRF2 pathway similarly to sulforaphane. I've been buying this Kuli Moringa powder and I add it to my smoothies.

## [00:06:19] Tool: Marine Omega-3s Fatty Acids, Fish Oil Supplements

**Andrew Huberman:** So if you had to do your top three, your superstars of nutrients for the brain and body, it sounds like we've got one set. What would you put in alongside them?

**Rhonda Patrick:** Omega-3 — the marine omega-3 fatty acids. These are found in marine animals, cold-water fish, fatty fish. There are three fatty acids: ALA, EPA, and DHA. If you get a high-quality one, it's in a triglyceride form — you've got a glycerol backbone with three fatty acids attached, either DHA or EPA. If you have a lower-quality fish oil supplement, you have what's called ethyl ester form. Ethyl ester is not bad; it just means take it with food.

**Rhonda Patrick:** The International Fish Oil Standards (IFOS) has a website where they do third-party testing of a ton of different fish oil supplements from around the world. They measure the concentration of omega-3 fatty acids in the actual supplement — because nothing is ever what it says on the bottle — and also measure contaminants like mercury, PCBs, dioxins. They also measure oxidized fatty acids. These omega-3 fatty acids are polyunsaturated fatty acids which are extremely prone to oxidation, so please keep your fish oil in the refrigerator. They give you a total oxidation number, called TOTOX, and I like it to be under 10, ideally under six.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the dosage you recommend people get?

**Rhonda Patrick:** I think two grams is a good threshold. People can go to the IFOS website and browse through the products.

## [00:08:16] Benefits of Fish Oil Supplementation, Longevity, Tool: Omega-3 Index

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some things that getting 2 to 4 grams of EPA per day is going to help with in our brain and the rest of our body?

**Rhonda Patrick:** I personally think it is one of the most powerful anti-inflammatory dietary lifestyle things that we can get easily — that is going to powerfully modulate the way you think, the way you feel, and the way you age. There's been lots of work by Dr. Bill Harris and his collaborators looking at what's called the omega-3 index. This is the omega-3 level in red blood cells. Red blood cells turn over about every 120 days, so it's a long-term marker of omega-3 status.

**Rhonda Patrick:** He's done a variety of observational studies measuring omega-3 index in people and then looking at their mortality risk and cardiovascular disease risk. The standard American diet has an omega-3 index of about 5%. Japan, by contrast, has an omega-3 index of around 10 to 11% — a big difference — and they also have about a 5-year increased life expectancy compared to people in the US. What he found in his data was that people with an omega-3 index of 4% or lower had a 5-year decreased life expectancy compared to people with an 8% omega-3 index. To get from the 4% range to 8% required supplementing with at least 2 grams per day — a little bit less if it was triglyceride form, but 2 grams is a good safe number.

**Andrew Huberman:** Where and how can somebody measure their omega-3 index?

**Rhonda Patrick:** The omega-3 index is measured in red blood cells and takes 120 days to turn over. So if you're going to do a baseline test and want to know how much you went up after supplementing, you have to wait 120 days before doing the second test. That's how long it takes for your red blood cells to turn over.

## [00:10:34] Omega-3s & Inflammation

**Andrew Huberman:** How are omega-3s and some of these other related lipids having their positive effects? What are some of the purported, reported, and known mechanisms?

**Rhonda Patrick:** Some of the most well-known mechanisms have to do with omega-3 fatty acids being very powerful regulators of the inflammatory process. Resolvins are produced from metabolites of DHA, for example, and they play a role in resolving inflammation. You want your inflammatory response to be activated when it's supposed to be, but you want to resolve that inflammation in a timely manner — and resolvins help do that. Specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs) also help resolve inflammation.

**Rhonda Patrick:** When we talk about inflammation broadly, at the level of neurons we know that inflammatory molecules cross the blood-brain barrier. EPA specifically is able to help blunt inflammatory responses. Inflammation inhibits the release of serotonin, and EPA is able to reduce that inhibition, allowing more serotonin to be released. DHA does something similar through resolvins. DHA itself is also a very important fatty acid making up neuronal cell membranes. As you know, the structure and function of receptors and transporters — these membrane-bound proteins on the surface of neurons — are affected by membrane fluidity, and DHA plays a role in that.

**Rhonda Patrick:** In animal studies, if you make an animal deficient in DHA, their serotonin receptors and dopamine receptors are affected because the structure of those receptors is altered through membrane fluidity. There have also been studies in piglets and rodents showing that consuming phospholipid DHA during fetal brain development gets dramatically more DHA into the brain. If you're supplementing with 2 to 4 grams of fish oil, you're going to get some phospholipid form anyway because your body will make it.

## [00:13:14] Vitamin D; Health Benefits

**Andrew Huberman:** So we have these plant-based compounds, we have the omega-3s — EPA and DHA — and then you mentioned there's a third category. What would you place in your third category of foods or supplement-based nutrients that brain and/or body health can really benefit from?

**Rhonda Patrick:** I think the most obvious would be vitamin D. Seventy percent of the US population has inadequate vitamin D levels — 70% of the whole US population. Insufficient levels are defined as less than 30 nanograms per milliliter, as defined by the Endocrine Society. There have been a lot of meta-analyses of all-cause mortality studies where vitamin D levels really seem to be ideal between 40 to 60 nanograms per milliliter.

**Rhonda Patrick:** Vitamin D is a steroid hormone, meaning it actually binds to a receptor, which dimerizes with the retinoid receptor, and that complex goes into the nucleus of a cell — where your DNA is — and recognizes little sequences of DNA called vitamin D response elements (VDREs). There are specific sequences of DNA that this complex recognizes and turns on or turns off a whole host of genes. It's regulating more than 5% of the protein-encoded human genome.

**Rhonda Patrick:** One of the important things that I published on back in 2014 was that there are VDREs in tryptophan hydroxylase 2 — the enzyme that converts tryptophan into serotonin in the brain. Serotonin does not cross the blood-brain barrier, so tryptophan has to get into your brain and then you convert it to serotonin there. The enzyme that does that — tryptophan hydroxylase 2 — is activated by vitamin D. Vitamin D is also regulating our immune system, our blood pressure, water retention, bone homeostasis. More than 5% of the genome — it's enormous.

## [00:15:44] Tool: Vitamin D Supplementation, Bloodwork

**Andrew Huberman:** Where and what is a good starting range for people to think about D3 supplementation, and what foods can increase D3?

**Rhonda Patrick:** Vitamin D3 is a good way to supplement. Vitamin D2 is a plant source and is often found fortified in foods like milk. Vitamin D is naturally present to some degree in fatty fish, but you're not going to correct a deficiency with food alone — you're either going to correct it with sun exposure or with supplementation. As you age, you become very inefficient at making vitamin D3 in your skin.

**Rhonda Patrick:** There have been a lot of Mendelian randomization studies. These studies look at people that have common variations in genes involved in the conversion of vitamin D precursors into the active steroid hormone — 1,25-hydroxyvitamin D. If people have variants that prevent efficient conversion into the most stable form, 25-hydroxyvitamin D (which is what is typically measured in the blood), they have higher all-cause mortality, higher respiratory-related mortality, higher cancer-related mortality, and are more likely to get multiple sclerosis. This really hammers home the importance of measuring your vitamin D levels and being proactive about it.

**Rhonda Patrick:** Supplementation-wise, typically taking 1,000 IUs of vitamin D will raise blood levels by around 5 nanograms per milliliter. So if you're deficient at 20 nanograms per milliliter and want to get to 40, you're going to need at least 4,000 IUs.

**Andrew Huberman:** For people who are going to be stubborn and not get their D levels tested, and simply say "I'll just take some D3" — is that reasonable?

**Rhonda Patrick:** 1,000 to 5,000 IUs for most people will be reasonably safe. Looking at the scientific literature, it is extremely hard to get hypercalcemia — which would be the major concern with really high levels of vitamin D3 supplementation — at anything short of hundreds of thousands of IUs per day for a very long time. There have also been studies looking at people deficient in vitamin D — in one case African-Americans given 4,000 IUs per day — that reversed their epigenetic aging by about 3 years, because again it's a hormone regulating more than 5% of your protein-encoding genome. That said, taking vitamin D3 supplements does not replace the need to get out into the sun.

## [00:19:09] Tool: Magnesium, Dark Leafy Greens, Supplementation

**Andrew Huberman:** So we've talked about plant-based compounds, omega-3s, and vitamin D3. Is there anything else — supplement-based or food-based — that you think is especially useful for brain and/or body health?

**Rhonda Patrick:** I do think magnesium is important as well. About 40% of the US population doesn't get enough magnesium. It's an essential mineral we're supposed to be getting from our diet. Magnesium is involved in making ATP — the energetic currency of our cells — as well as in utilizing ATP, and also in DNA repair enzymes. I personally think that magnesium insufficiency causes an insidious type of damage daily that you can't look in the mirror and see.

**Rhonda Patrick:** When you're deficient in vitamin C, you can see scurvy. But you can't see DNA damage — it's happening right now, happening every day, during normal metabolism. We have DNA repair enzymes in our body that repair that damage, and those enzymes require magnesium as a cofactor. Importantly, magnesium is at the center of a chlorophyll molecule — chlorophyll is what gives plants their green color — so dark leafy greens are high in magnesium. Kale, spinach, Swiss chard, rainbow chard, romaine lettuce — all good sources. The 40% insufficiency in the US tells us people aren't eating their greens.

**Rhonda Patrick:** For supplementation, magnesium can cause GI distress at high doses. I personally like to take around 130 to 135 milligrams — that way it's not a huge bolus to my gut. Magnesium glycinate doesn't affect the gut as much. Magnesium malate is also excellent — malate has to do with short-chain fatty acids being good for the gut — and I always try to eat green apples and tart cherries, which are really high in malic acid.

## [00:21:23] Deliberate Cold Exposure, Mood & Dopamine

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked a lot about the use of deliberate cold exposure. What do you think is a reasonable and particularly potent stimulus to use in terms of cold?

**Rhonda Patrick:** Today I did 3 minutes at 49°F. I have a cold tub. I definitely do cold when I'm going to do a podcast, when I'm going to give a talk, or when I'm anxious. I feel good. I feel more focused, which is why I usually do it before any type of public speaking.

**Andrew Huberman:** The mood-enhancing effects that you report are almost certainly a consequence of having slowly elevating but significantly elevated dopamine that goes on for hours. That's almost a dreamlike profile for dopamine, because most things — an Adderall, a Ritalin, a cup of coffee, or a pre-workout drink — are going to give you a big spike in adrenaline and dopamine and a big crash. The advantage of cold is that sustained, gradual rise.

## [00:22:16] Cold Exposure to Enhance Mitochondria, Shivering, Browning Effect

**Rhonda Patrick:** Not doing cold too often is advantageous because you don't become cold-adapted. It's very hard for anyone to get truly cold-adapted. Some people start to look forward to the cold, and what I think they're looking forward to is the feeling afterward — that dopamine rush. But if you get cold-adapted, it blunts some of the effect. However, being cold-adapted means you have more mitochondria in your adipose tissue and perhaps even muscle — and that's been shown.

**Rhonda Patrick:** Shivering is a very inefficient way to produce heat. Your muscles are basically contracting and producing heat from that, but it's just not very efficient. The more elegant way to do it is to have your mitochondria produce tons of heat. Mitochondria are these little organelles inside of your cells responsible for producing energy, usually in the form of ATP. They have a double membrane with a negative charge on the inside and a positive charge on the inner membrane — an electrochemical gradient. You can uncouple that charge, and when you do, the mitochondria go into maximum respiration. They're trying to restore the gradient and in doing so, the energy they're making is actually heat, not ATP. You're burning glucose and lipids and making heat. As you become more adapted, you no longer shiver — you do this uncoupled thermogenesis instead.

**Rhonda Patrick:** Another type of adaptation that occurs is you actually produce more mitochondria in your adipose tissue. This is regulated by norepinephrine through a protein called PGC1-alpha, which signals for mitochondrial biogenesis. Per adipose cell, you're getting more mitochondria. This is often referred to as the "browning" of fat — if you look under a microscope at an adipocyte, it looks darker because there are more mitochondria inside. More mitochondria in muscle is associated with improved muscle mass and improved endurance.

**Rhonda Patrick:** We don't normally make more mitochondria automatically. You can stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis with high-intensity interval training, but most people aren't doing that consistently. What happens with mitochondria normally is they fuse with other mitochondria, exchange content including mitochondrial DNA, and then fission back apart — that's how they stay youngish. But as you age, you're mixing the same old pool of mitochondria together. Why wouldn't you want to bring new, healthy, young mitochondria into that pool? Cold exposure does exactly that.

## [00:26:40] Tool: High-Intensity Interval Training, Tabata Workout, Sauna, Memory

**Andrew Huberman:** What sort of cardiovascular or other types of training do you do? Do you do HIIT?

**Rhonda Patrick:** I do a lot of high-intensity interval Tabatas on a stationary cycle three times a week — 10 minutes because it's efficient, and I push myself really hard. The Tabata is 20 seconds on, 10 seconds off, and on means you're pedaling like your life depends on it, maxing it. Then I always have my sauna pre-heating up to about 189°F. I hop right in the sauna after my Peloton, down a bunch of water, and then get in. I'll either read a science paper, prepare for a presentation or podcast, or hash over things in my mind.

**Rhonda Patrick:** Interestingly, I use the sauna to memorize things. I think it has to do with the stress response — when you have an emotional trigger or are in a semi-stressful environment, you remember things better.

**Andrew Huberman:** The idea that being in a semi-stressful environment would aid learning and retention of information is really well-substantiated by the work of James McGaugh — he was at UC Irvine and I believe also the University of Arizona, both very strong in learning and memory. He really defined this inverted U-shaped function for the relationship between adrenaline and memory. Basically, if you're too relaxed, you won't remember information well. At peak — but not excessive — levels of stress, you are actually a memory machine, at least within the context of what you're trying to learn. That matches perfectly with what you're describing.

## [00:28:36] Sauna, Cardiovascular & Cognitive Health; Tool: Sauna Duration & Frequency

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to ask you about the sauna. Of course there's vasodilation and profusion of blood to the brain — is that a wonderful way to enhance cognition?

**Rhonda Patrick:** The vasodilation does occur, and there's a lot of overlap between moderate-intensity aerobic exercise and heat stress. When you're exercising you're elevating your core body temperature and sweating. When you're actually in the sauna, blood gets redistributed to the skin to facilitate sweating, but much like exercise, blood flow in general is improved to the brain, to the muscles, everywhere. There are studies showing that sauna use is associated with a much lower risk of dementia and Alzheimer's disease.

**Rhonda Patrick:** People that use the sauna four to seven times a week have greater than a 60% reduction in dementia risk and Alzheimer's disease risk compared to people that use it only once a week. People using it two to three times a week have something like a little greater than 20% reduction in risk — a dose-dependent effect. For cardiovascular mortality, men who use the sauna four to seven times a week have a 50% reduction in cardiovascular-related mortality compared to once a week. Two to three times a week gives something like a 24% lower death from cardiovascular disease. Sudden cardiac death — like a heart attack — is greater than 60% lower in men who use it four to seven times a week versus once. All of this is the work of Dr. Jari Laukkanen at the University of Eastern Finland, one of the world's experts on sauna use.

**Rhonda Patrick:** The more you do the sauna or any sort of heat stress, you become adapted — you start to sweat at a lower core body temperature. I stay in for about 30 minutes. That's a lot. You have to listen to your body. Most of the studies I just mentioned found that men who were in the sauna for only 11 minutes — even if they used it four to seven times a week — only had about an 8% reduction in cardiovascular disease-related death, instead of 50%. It had to be greater than 19 minutes. So about 20 minutes is the sweet spot at around 174°F.

**Rhonda Patrick:** To me, that's very strong data that this is more causal than a correlational thing — because that's always the problem with observational studies, and these corrected for a whole host of factors including cholesterol, exercise, and essentially everything under the sun. On top of that, you have the dose-dependent nature of both the duration and frequency. There have also been intervention studies directly comparing moderate-intensity aerobic exercise on a stationary cycle to 20 minutes in a sauna — physiologically the same things happen. Heart rate elevates during the activity, blood pressure increases, but then after: resting heart rate decreases below baseline, and blood pressure improves below baseline. This is happening equivalently in moderate-intensity cycling versus sauna.

**Rhonda Patrick:** The sauna really mimics moderate-intensity aerobic exercise, which is great for people who can't go for a run or even get on a bike — disabled people, for example, with appropriate safety caveats. There are some mild safety concerns: people who had a recent heart attack, those with certain rare cardiac conditions, those prone to low blood pressure — always talk to a physician before doing the sauna. Avoid alcohol in the sauna. Pregnant women should definitely avoid it. For healthy, fit people already exercising, adding a sauna provides a synergistic effect.

**Rhonda Patrick:** In addition to mimicking aerobic exercise, there are the heat shock proteins we talked about earlier. Many animal studies have been done looking at Alzheimer's disease in rodent models where heat shock proteins protect against it. Heat shock proteins are robustly activated in humans — 50% higher over baseline levels after just 30 minutes at 163°F in the sauna — and they stay activated in rodents for at least 48 hours. Having these heat shock proteins around, making sure they're properly taking care of our proteins so they're not aggregating into plaques in our brains, could be another potential way that sauna is protecting from Alzheimer's disease and improving cardiovascular health and longevity.

## [00:34:10] Tool: Hot Bath; Acknowledgements

**Andrew Huberman:** I know people are probably desperate to know — what if they don't have a sauna? I could imagine that a hot bath would work almost as well. Is that right?

**Rhonda Patrick:** Yeah. There have been some studies looking at activation of heat shock proteins and brain-derived neurotrophic factor increases with heat stress from a hot bath. The hot bath at around 104°F — which is typically what studies use for temperature — for 20 minutes from the shoulders down is a very robust activation of heat shock proteins and BDNF. Heat shock proteins also protect against muscle atrophy, having to do with protein structure and integrity of muscle tissue. This has been shown in animal data as well as some recent human data. In one study looking at muscle disuse, local heat treatment prevented something like almost 40% of the muscle atrophy from disuse.
