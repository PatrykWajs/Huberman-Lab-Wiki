## [00:00:00] Light

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are going to discuss light and the many powerful uses of light to optimize our health. One of the reasons why light has such powerful effects on so many different aspects of our biology is that it can be translated into electrical signals in our brain and body, into hormone signals in our brain and body, and indeed into what we call cascades of biological pathways. Meaning light can actually change the genes that the cells of your bodies express, and that is true throughout the lifespan.

## [00:00:47] Physics of Light

Light is electromagnetic energy. It can cause reactions in cells of your body — you see a piece of fruit and it's not ripe, but it gets a lot of sunlight and it ripens, because the electromagnetic energy of sunlight had an impact on that plant directly. The second thing to understand about the physics of light is that light has many different wavelengths. The simplest way to conceptualize this is to imagine that cover of the Pink Floyd album where there's a prism: a white beam of light goes in and the prism splits it into what looks like a rainbow — your reds, oranges, greens, blues, purples, etc. The third key point is that different wavelengths of light, because of the way their wave travels, can penetrate tissues to different depths. Every biological function of light has to do with the absorbance, the reflectance, or the passing-through of light at that particular cell or compartment within a cell.

## [00:01:57] Light & Body, Rods & Cones, Skin, Seasons & Melatonin

There are three primary examples of how you take light in your environment and convert it into biological events. First, we have photoreceptors in the back of our eyes — rods and cones — where the rods are very elongated and the cones look like little triangles. Second, light can impact our skin: in the top layer called the epidermis, melanocytes respond to light exposure by turning on genetic programs that lead to enhanced pigmentation, which we call tanning. Third, every cell of your body — bone tissue, heart, liver, spleen — can change how it functions if light can access it, either directly or through indirect pathways.

Light arriving on the eyes is absorbed by a particular cell type called the intrinsically photosensitive ganglion cell — also called the melanopsin cell — because it contains an opsin that absorbs short-wavelength light from sunlight. Those cells communicate to particular stations in the brain that connect to the pineal gland, a pea-sized gland in the middle of your brain that releases melatonin. Light activates these melanopsin cells, which in turn shuts down the production of melatonin from the pineal gland. Melatonin is therefore a transducer — a communicator of how much light on average is in your physical environment. For people living in the northern hemisphere, you get more melatonin release in the winter months than in the summer months, so you have a calendar system based in a hormone, and that hormone uses light to determine where you are in the journey around the sun. The environment around us is converted into a signal that changes the environment within us, and that signal is melatonin.

## [00:05:01] Melatonin Supplements?, Tool: Seasonal Sun Exposure; Reduce Night Light

Melatonin is well known for its role in making us sleepy each night and allowing us to fall asleep. The levels of melatonin in most supplements are far too high to be considered physiological — they are indeed super-physiological in most cases. Endogenous melatonin — the melatonin we make naturally — has two general categories of effects: regulatory and protective. Regulatory effects include positive impacts on bone mass and involvement in maturation of the gonads during puberty; high levels of melatonin tend to suppress testicle and ovarian function, which is why children don't go into puberty until the right time.

Because hormones in general are responsible for slow modulatory effects on our biology, melatonin serves as the primary example of how light throughout the year changes the way that cells, tissues, and organs work. To get the proper levels of melatonin according to the time of year, try to get outside as much as possible during the long days of summer and spring, and spend more time indoors in the winter months. Changes in melatonin duration across the year are normal and healthy.

The very firmly established fact is that light powerfully inhibits melatonin. If you wake up in the middle of the night, go to the bathroom, and flip on bright overhead fluorescent lights, your melatonin levels — which would ordinarily be quite high — will immediately plummet to near zero. If you do that night after night, you are really disrupting a fundamental signal that communicates information about where your brain and body should be in time.

## [00:09:15] Light, UVB, Skin Exposure, Testosterone & Estrogen, Fertility

In animals like mice, but also in humans, exposure to UV blue light — short wavelengths of light — can trigger increases in testosterone and estrogen and the desire to mate. Critically, it is not the exposure of light to the eyes but the exposure of skin to particular wavelengths of light that triggers these hormone increases. A paper published in the journal Cell Reports entitled "Skin exposure to UVB light induces a skin-brain-gonad axis and sexual behavior" explored these mechanisms. The basic finding was that when mice or humans were exposed to UVB light from sunshine or artificial sources, there were increases in testosterone and estrogen observed within a very brief period of time, with proper ratios maintained in both males and females.

Mice tended to seek out mating more, and there were also increases in gonadal weight — literally increases in testes size and ovarian size — when mice were exposed to UVB light past a certain threshold. UVB light exposure also changed various aspects of female biology related to fertility: follicle and egg maturation are well-known indices of fertility, and UVB exposure enhanced follicle maturation, meaning more healthy eggs were being produced. In terms of a protocol to increase testosterone, estrogen, mood, and feelings of passion, the idea is to get two to three exposures per week of at least 20 to 30 minutes of sunlight onto as much of your body as you can reasonably expose.

## [00:12:38] UVB Light & Improved Pain Tolerance

One of the impressive effects of UVB light — whether from sunlight or an artificial source — is its effect on our tolerance for pain. It turns out that pain tolerance varies across the year and is increased in longer-day conditions, occurring via UVB exposure to both the skin and the eyes. A study entitled "Skin exposure to ultraviolet B rapidly activates systemic neuroendocrine and immunosuppressive responses" found that even one exposure to UVB light changed the output of particular hormones and neurochemicals such as corticotropin hormone and beta-endorphins — endogenous opioids that counter pain and act as a psychological soother.

A second study published in the journal Neuron, entitled "A visual circuit related to the periaqueductal gray area for the antinociceptive effects of bright light treatment," showed that the periaqueductal gray — a region of the midbrain — contains neurons that release endogenous opioids such as beta-endorphins. Light landing on the eyes is captured by melanopsin cells, which translate that light into electrical signals handed off to areas of the brain to evoke the release of these endogenous opioids that soothe you and lead to less perception of pain — not blocking pain responses that protect you from harm, but acting as a painkiller from the inside.

## [00:14:54] Tool: Daily Sunlight Exposure Protocol; Blue Blockers

Try to get some UVB exposure, ideally from sunlight. The 20-to-30-minute protocol two or three times per week is an excellent one. Even on a cloud-covered day, you will get far more light energy — photons — through cloud cover than from an indoor artificial light source. Never look at any light, artificial or otherwise, that is so bright it is painful. It is fine to wear eyeglasses or contact lenses, as they will focus light onto the very cells you want it delivered to. However, sunglasses that are highly reflective, or trying to get sunlight through a car windshield or window, simply will not work — most windows are designed to filter out UVB light.

If you are keen on blue blockers, don't wear them outside, and you are probably doing yourself a disservice by wearing them in the morning and daytime. There is certainly a place for blue blockers in the evening and nighttime if you have issues falling and staying asleep. But blue blockers block those short-wavelength UVB wavelengths that you need arriving at your retina and skin for these powerful biological effects on hormones and pain reduction. Consider also the total amount of skin exposure you are getting: being completely cloaked in clothing versus being outside in shorts and a t-shirt will produce very different patterns of biological signaling.

## [00:17:08] Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD), Year-Round Sunlight Exposure

Whether to seek UVB exposure throughout the entire year or only in summer months depends on whether you experience depression in the winter months — so-called seasonal affective disorder. Some people have mild forms, some have severe forms, and some love the fall and winter; it must be considered on a case-by-case basis. Getting more UVB exposure from sunlight throughout the entire year is beneficial, provided you are not burning your skin or damaging your eyes. During the winter months, if you experience drops in energy or increases in depression, it can be very beneficial to access a SAD lamp or an inexpensive LED lighting panel positioned on your desk throughout the day.

People who are blind — provided they still have eyes — often maintain melanopsin cells, so even low-vision or no-vision individuals can benefit from getting UVB exposure to their eyes for mood, hormone pathways, and pain reduction. A cautionary note: people who have retinitis pigmentosa, macular degeneration, or glaucoma, as well as people especially prone to skin cancers, should definitely consult with an ophthalmologist and dermatologist before increasing total UVB exposure from any source.

## [00:19:16] Enhanced Immune Function & UVB Light, Tool: Winter Light Exposure

UVB light has interesting and powerful effects on immune function. All the organs of our body are inside our skin, so information about external conditions needs to be communicated to organs like the spleen, which is involved in the creation of molecules and cells that combat infection. Beautiful studies show that more UVB exposure from sunlight or appropriate artificial sources enhances spleen and immune function through a well-established circuit: UVB light arriving on the eyes triggers activation of neurons within the sympathetic nervous system — part of the autonomic nervous system that controls heartbeat, breathing, and immune activation. When sufficient UVB light reaches the eyes, a particular set of connections within the sympathetic nervous system is activated, and the spleen deploys immune cells and molecules that combat infection.

We often think that summer and spring have fewer infections floating around, but in fact there are not fewer infections — we are simply better at combating them. During winter, be especially conscious of accessing UVB light to enhance spleen function and keep the immune system deploying killer T-cells, B cells, and cytokines so that when infections are encountered, we can combat them well.

## [00:21:37] Light, Wound Healing, Hair & Nail Growth

It is well known that wound healing is faster when we are getting sufficient UVB exposure. The turnover of hair stem cells — which live in little niches in our skin — is also triggered by UVB exposure, and hair grows faster in longer days. A study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences showed that the exposure of melanopsin ganglion cells in the eyes is absolutely critical for triggering the turnover of stem cells in the skin, hair, and nails. Hair grows more, skin turns over more — looking more youthful as older cells are replaced with new ones — and all the renewing tissues of our body proliferate more when we get sufficient UVB light to both our eyes and our skin. Hair growing faster in longer days is not a coincidence or just perception — it is a biological reality driven by light.

## [00:23:03] Tool: Mood, Dopamine & Avoid Nighttime UVB Light

There is a particular neural circuit originating with melanopsin cells in our eye that bypasses all areas of the brain associated with circadian clocks, connecting instead specifically to pathways involving the release of dopamine, serotonin, and endogenous opioids. That pathway involves a brain structure called the perihabenular nucleus. As it turns out, if this pathway is activated at the wrong time of each 24-hour cycle — meaning at night — mood gets worse and dopamine output gets worse. Molecules specifically there to make us feel good are actually reduced in their output.

Avoiding UVB light at night is a way to prevent activation of this eye-to-perihabenular pathway that can turn on depression. Be very direct and succinct: avoid exposure to UVB light from artificial sources between 10 p.m. and 4 a.m. If you view UVB light during those hours, you activate those neurons very potently and will truncate or reduce the amount of dopamine you release. To keep your mood elevated, get a lot of UVB light throughout the day, and at night be cautious about UVB exposure from artificial sources. It also helps to put any artificial lights in the evening low in your physical environment, because the melanopsin cells reside in the lower half of our eyes and view the upper visual field — dim those lights as far down as you safely can.

## [00:25:38] Red Light Therapy, Infrared Light, Acne, Wound Healing & Scars; Mitochondria

Shifting to the other end of the light spectrum — red light and infrared light, which is long-wavelength light. Long-wavelength light such as red light and near-infrared light can pass through the skin into the deeper layers called the dermis, where it can change the metabolic function of particular cells. Taking acne as an example: within the dermis are sebaceous glands that make the oil present in skin. Those sebaceous glands are often nearby hair follicles. Red and infrared light reaching these deeper layers can activate stem cells, cause top layers of skin to be cleared out, and drive new healthy skin cells to replace lesions — this works in wound healing, getting scars to disappear, and removing certain patches of pigmentation.

Long-wavelength light can also get into individual cells and access the mitochondria, which are responsible for producing ATP. As cells age — especially very metabolically active cells — they accumulate reactive oxygen species (ROS). As ROS go up, ATP energy production in those cells tends to go down. Red light passes into the deeper layers of the skin, activates mitochondria, which increases ATP and directly or indirectly reduces these reactive oxygen species. ROS cause cellular damage, cellular death, and generally inhibit the way that our cells work. Red light and near-infrared light therapies for healing skin, improving skin quality, removing lesions, getting rid of scars, or unwanted pigmentation are not pseudoscience — they are grounded in the biology of how light interacts with mitochondria and reactive oxygen species.

## [00:29:19] Offset Age-Related Eyesight Decline, Red Light

In recent years there have been beautiful examples in neurobiology whereby red light and near-infrared light can be used to enhance the function of the cells that allow us to see better — and cells that allow us to think better. These data come from Dr. Glenn Jeffrey at University College London, a long-standing neuroscientist who over the last decade has emphasized the exploration of red light and near-infrared light for restoration of neuronal function as we age.

The Jeffrey Lab published two studies on humans that looked directly at how red light and near-infrared light can improve visual function. They had subjects — either younger (in their 20s) or 40 years old or older — view red light of about 670 nanometers at a safe distance of about a foot away, for 2 to 3 minutes per day. In one study they did that for about 12 weeks; in another, just a couple of weeks. The major finding was that in individuals 40 years old or older, but not in subjects younger than 40, there was an improvement in visual acuity — specifically a 22% improvement using the Tritan exam, which addresses the function of short-wavelength cones. In the landscape of visual testing, a 22% improvement is an extremely exciting result.

As we age, rods and cones — the most metabolically active cells in the entire body — accumulate a lot of reactive oxygen species. Red light used in these studies was able to reduce the amount of ROS in the rods and cones and rescue the function of short-wavelength and medium-wavelength cones. One of the additional things the Jeffrey Lab observed was a reduction in drusen — fatty, cholesterol deposits that accumulate in the eye as we age. Red light and near-infrared light therapies were able to reduce or reverse some of the accumulation of drusen, suggesting that red light may reduce cholesterol deposits and ROS to improve neuronal function.

## [00:33:35] Tool: Red Light Protocol, Frequency & Timing

Even though these studies are very exciting, they are fairly recent and more data are needed. The exposure to red light needed to happen early in the day — at least within the first 3 hours of waking. Nowadays there are a number of different red light panels and sources that fall within the range of red light and near-infrared light that one could use. Make sure that the red light source is not so bright that it damages your eyes — a good rule of thumb is that something isn't painful to look at. Never look at any light source, sunlight or otherwise, that is painful and makes you want to squint or close your eyes.

The wavelength of light matters: it is red light and near-infrared light that will be effective. The authors emphasized that red light of 670 nanometers and near-infrared light of 790 nanometers were effective and that those wavelengths can be complementary. Many commercially available red light panels combine both. However, most commercial panels are too bright to safely look at close up — that is why most are designed for skin illumination and arrive with eye protectors designed to shield out all red light. If exploring 670 and 790 nanometer light for enhancing neuronal function, set it at a distance that is comfortable to look at, does not force you to squint, and does not make you feel uncomfortable for the 2 to 3 minutes of illumination each day.

## [00:36:15] Red Light for Shift Workers

Red light has also been shown to be beneficial late in the day and even in the middle of the night — specifically for shift workers. A study entitled "Red light, a novel non-pharmacological intervention to promote alertness in shift workers" found that if you need to be awake late at night for shift work, studying, or taking care of children, red light is the best choice. If the red light is sufficiently dim, it will not inhibit melatonin production and will not increase cortisol at night. A late-shifted increase in cortisol — at 9 or 10 p.m. — is well known to be associated with depression and other aspects of mental illness. So if you need to be awake at night or even all night, red light is the preferred light source. Make it as dim as you can while still being able to perform the activities you need to perform.
