## [03:30] Q&A Session Begins: Napping and Sleep Quality

Does having an afternoon sleep affect your quality of sleep at night?

The business of naps is the following: keep them shorter than 90 minutes so you don't disrupt your nighttime sleep. Don't do them at all if napping disrupts nighttime sleep — even 10 minutes of napping can be disruptive for certain people. For those who wake from naps feeling groggy, that's sleep inertia. That phenomenon gave rise to the "nappuccino" — having coffee or espresso and then taking a nap.

Naps are wonderful if they're shorter than 90 minutes and don't interfere with nighttime sleep. But the practice of non-sleep deep rest — putting the body into a "body still, mind awake" state — is particularly valuable. Several studies from the University of Copenhagen show that NSDR replenishes levels of dopamine in key brain areas, restores mental and physical vigor, and does not disrupt nighttime sleep; it actually enhances one's ability to fall and stay asleep or to fall back asleep.

For anyone who is a napper, that's great. And for anyone who has challenges with sleep that might be related to napping, consider short 10- or 20-minute non-sleep deep rest protocols. They're completely zero cost. Huberman-narrated 10-, 20-, and 30-minute NSDR protocols are available on the Huberman Lab YouTube Clips channel, set against imagery of the Sydney sunrise.

## [06:34] The Power of the Placebo Effect

The placebo effect is real. Our belief about what we've taken or what is happening to us has a powerful effect on our physiology — it's not purely psychological. The whole notion of "psychosomatic" is starting to fall away as we begin to understand that beliefs have a powerful effect on what happens physiologically. Ali Crum, a tenured professor at Stanford's Department of Psychology who studies mindsets, has done beautiful experiments on stress showing that if you watch a short video about stress being harmful to cognition, sleep, and well-being, indeed those harmful effects occur. And if you watch a short video about how stress can be performance-enhancing — sharpening mental acuity and access to memory — that indeed that happens. These are called "belief effects." Placebo effects tend to be more general; belief effects tend to be around specific types of information.

The placebo effect has recently been shown to be dose-dependent. In a study covered in a Huberman Lab journal club episode with Peter Attia, participants took either zero, 0.25 milligrams, half a milligram, or a gram of nicotine — a known cognitive enhancer. People who were told they had received a higher dose of nicotine performed better on cognitive tasks even when they had consumed zero. And those told they had received a higher dose performed better than those who received the same dose but were told otherwise. What made this remarkable is that researchers recorded from brain centers of these individuals: levels of activity in areas relevant for cognition changed according to what people believed. The placebo effect is changing real neural activity.

There is an anecdote worth noting here. A Nobel laureate neuroscientist at Columbia University — his identity withheld — was observed chewing no fewer than six pieces of Nicorette during a visit. When asked, he explained he believed it would stave off Parkinson's and Alzheimer's by keeping levels of neuromodulators like dopamine and acetylcholine elevated, despite the blood pressure increases nicotine causes. He remains very sharp in his 80s.

The dose-dependent placebo effect raises important questions — and also reveals something powerful: that our belief system, including our understanding of the mechanisms driving effects of drugs or protocols, plays a powerful role in whether we get the outcome we want. That's the most important thing, provided the approach is safe.

## [11:31] Entering Rest and Digest State: Techniques and Tools

The fastest way to exit fight-or-flight is the physiological sigh — probably repeated two or three times in a row. Combining that with panoramic vision amplifies the effect.

There are certain things that if not done on a regular basis will cause the nervous system to idle at a higher "autonomic RPM." The key is to get enough sleep each night. Stress is not bad provided you sleep well. The challenge is that when stressed, sleep often doesn't come easily, or sleep is disrupted with middle-of-the-night waking.

Non-sleep deep rest is indeed a renaming — or partial renaming — of Yoga Nidra, which stands for "yoga sleep." Yoga Nidra was encountered in 2015 at a trauma and addiction treatment center in Florida where addicts who couldn't recover through other means were starting each day with 30 to 60 minutes of Yoga Nidra and achieving sobriety and recovery from trauma. The practice is immensely powerful: keeping the mind active while the body is still, as a self-directed practice. The decision to rename it NSDR was not to appropriate the practice but to make it more accessible — names like Yoga Nidra, or protocols named after people, often act as separators that deter people from trying them because they sound esoteric.

Anyone who enters a stressed state too frequently should learn self-directed relaxation first and foremost. NSDR three to five times per week, 10 minutes per session, is the recommended starting point as a zero-cost tool. If fight-or-flight persists, physiological sighs should be incorporated. Modern medicine does offer excellent pharmaceutical tools, and intermediate supplements like theanine and magnesium can be useful. But behavioral tools should always come first.

## [15:35] Muscle Growth, Learning & the Brain

Muscles get stronger and grow after a proper stimulus is applied — and the adaptation occurs in the time after the stimulus is provided. Resistance training gives a window into what the adaptation might look like because of the enhanced blood flow to working muscles. Endurance training is different: you sense the limit of your ability and then exceed it subsequently. In both cases, there is a delay between the stimulus and the adaptation.

In terms of cognitive learning, the same principle applies. The computational biology of learning suggests that setting task difficulty to approximately 85% correct trials and 15% error trials is optimal. If you're trying to learn a new piano piece, or teaching one to a child, introduce a small percentage — 10 to 15, maybe 20% — of novel material that is difficult. The exact percentage doesn't need to be precise.

The focused, deliberate attempt to learn something creates an underlying sense of agitation — and that agitation is the trigger, the stimulus, for neuroplasticity. If something could be completed easily, the nervous system would have no reason to change. The nervous system doesn't distinguish successful trials from failure trials. What it detects is the release of neuromodulators — adrenaline and norepinephrine among them — that accompany the agitation of struggling to learn. When those neurochemicals bathe surrounding neurons, those neurons signal that something needs to change. The stimulus for neuroplasticity has occurred.

The actual rewiring of neurons — the improvement or reduction in the strength of synaptic connections, and in rare instances the addition of new neurons — occurs during sleep and states of deep rest, or non-sleep deep rest, though there is less data to support NSDR specifically. The actual rewiring occurs away from the stimulus.

Two important principles: agitation and stress and their underlying neurochemicals are the stimulus for learning; and the rewiring — the actual plasticity — occurs during rest and sleep.

## [20:13] Hallucinogens: Personal Experiences & Clinical Insights

Taking LSD and psilocybin at too young an age produced bad experiences — some bad in the moment, some bad afterward. That is not a recommendation for adolescents. Being a child, adolescent, or teenager is already a psychedelic experience. The brain is wiring up in all sorts of interesting ways, and introducing massive amounts of neuromodulators haphazardly starts tampering with that wiring.

However, for adults who are not suffering from particular psychiatric challenges — namely forms of psychosis (one in 100 people experiences schizophrenic symptoms, a high number) or certain forms of bipolar depression — the clinical trials on psychedelics are very compelling. The psychiatric community is being forced to confront these data.

Two clinical trials were participated in — one on high-dose psilocybin, meaning more than two grams, taken twice, with the support of medically trained therapists. Most trials use psilocybin rather than LSD because psilocybin's effects last 3 to 7 hours — a manageable window for clinicians and technicians — whereas LSD's duration is far longer. The "Mount Everest" of psychedelics is ibogaine, or iboga, under investigation by Nolan Williams at Stanford School of Medicine. Iboga lasts 22 hours and has cardiac effects. It induces a state in which there are no hallucinations with eyes open, but closing the eyes produces high-resolution, accurate pictures of prior life events — with agency to change behavior and resculpt one's relationship to those experiences. The state of Kentucky recently directed its $40 million opioid settlement toward iboga trials.

Two medically supported psilocybin sessions have been shown to be more effective in treating major depression than other pharmaceutical treatments it has been compared against — though adverse outcomes do occur. Psilocybin's structure resembles serotonin; it appears to bind near-selectively to a particular serotonin receptor, producing enhanced or broader connectivity between brain areas that normally are not communicating. Different ways of thinking about the same problems. Depression is characterized partly by a lack of positive anticipation of the future, and macro-dose psilocybin trials appear helpful for that. Microdosing psilocybin has not been well-supported by existing trials, though there are not many trials of microdosing yet. Between micro and macro dosing, macro has more support — but caution, set, and setting matter enormously.

## [27:28] The Misunderstood Effects of MDMA

## [27:42] Exploring the Potential of MDMA in Clinical Settings

MDMA, ecstasy, comes with several important caveats. First, contamination risk — the fentanyl crisis in the US means purity is essential. Second, it is methylenedioxy-methamphetamine; the methamphetamine component raises concern, but the methylenedioxy component dramatically increases serotonin, and it now appears that the combination of increased serotonin and increased dopamine may provide a neuroprotective effect.

The early reports that MDMA was neurotoxic — "puts holes in your brain" — were the result of a laboratory error. The researchers, who conducted the study in earnest, discovered they had accidentally grabbed methamphetamine from the shelf rather than MDMA. The paper was retracted. The news agencies did not report the retraction.

The best current evidence is that MDMA taken in a clinically supported context can act as an empathogen — helping people develop empathy for themselves and relieve trauma. Clinical trials show up to 60% and as high as 67% remission of PTSD. This is with support: in an eye mask, going inward, communicating the experience to a qualified therapist, and returning to safety afterward. The clinical context matters entirely. The problem with having that much serotonin and dopamine in the system is that one can become empathic toward anything — which is why trials insist on the structured, inward-facing container.

The stance on psychedelics has changed significantly over the past five to ten years. A decade ago this discussion would not have been had with a microphone recording. But many laboratories at Stanford and elsewhere are now doing federally funded work on these compounds. These compounds are ultimately ways to adjust levels of neuromodulators in the brain — serotonin, dopamine, etc. — very potently, which is exactly why caution must be applied.

## [29:25] The Complex World of Psychedelics & Mental Health

Thinking about psychedelics by way of their mechanism is the right frame. They are potent neuromodulator adjusters. The goal is never plasticity in the abstract — the goal is plasticity directed toward a specific positive outcome. Any time there is plasticity, there is also the potential for maladaptive plasticity.

## [30:07] Ketamine: From Misconception to Medical Use

Ketamine and PCP (phencyclidine) are the same compound — something that is rarely communicated. PCP was culturally demonized; ketamine is now legally prescribed in the US. Ketamine is potentially addictive. The "k-hole" is a known phenomenon.

Mechanistically, ketamine is a potent NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate) receptor blocker, which blocks neuroplasticity in the short term and expands it in the long term. The way to think about these compounds is through their mechanisms. The expanded neuroplasticity they produce is not the goal in itself — the goal is to direct that plasticity toward a positive outcome, with full awareness that maladaptive plasticity is equally possible.

There are some studies on DMT and ayahuasca. It is harder to understand what is happening mechanistically in these very short, massive-neuromodulator-release scenarios compared to longer-duration psychedelics. Robin Carhart-Harris at the University of California San Francisco is doing extensive research on DMT.

## [34:48] The Gut-Brain Axis: A Key to Overall Health

The gut-brain axis is most definitely real, and one of the more exciting areas in science. Distinct microbiome niches exist throughout the body — in nasal passages, on the surface of the eyes and skin, in the urethra, in every orifice and mucus membrane. The gut microbiome, when well supported, produces certain fatty acids that serve as precursors or catalysts for the production of neurotransmitters in the brain. Enhancing the diversity of flora and microbiota in the gut and mouth is clearly beneficial for the nervous system.

Some of the most compelling data on gut-brain effects involves fecal microbiota transplants — taking the gut microbiota from one healthy individual and transferring it to another, with resulting relief from certain neuropsychiatric symptoms. It is not a comfortable concept, but it is compelling science.

The best, lowest-cost approach to supporting the gut-brain axis is one to four servings per day of low-sugar fermented foods — kimchi, sauerkraut, kefir, yogurt, natto, or the fermented food of any culture. Beer does not count. When access to fermented foods is limited — during travel or when on antibiotics — a pill probiotic is a reasonable supplement.

The challenge with studying the gut microbiome is that its effects are generalized and indirect. It is unlikely there will ever be a clean study showing that increasing a specific strain of lactobacillus directly improves cognition, because the microbiome is a moderating or modulating factor, not a direct mediating one. Like improving sleep, supporting the microbiome creates buoyancy across multiple systems rather than producing a single trackable effect. Diversity of the microbiome is the key variable. Antibiotics are not bad, but their overuse disrupts that diversity.

## [40:41] Sleep Patterns and Chronotypes: Personalizing Rest

Whether early sleep is better than late sleep for the same total duration depends on chronotype. Chronotype is real — it was not always believed to be, but newer data confirms it clearly. Some people feel spectacularly better going to sleep early and waking early; others feel much better staying up late and waking late. Both the total duration of sleep and the regularity of sleep timing matter.

If someone feels best sleeping from 11 p.m. to 7 a.m., the goal is to keep that bedtime within plus or minus one hour, at least five nights out of the week. That consistency is one of the more important and underappreciated variables in sleep health. Matt Walker's QQRT framework captures the four key dimensions: Quantity, Quality, Regularity, and Timing.

Life is not entirely about optimizing — it's good to stay up, watch a sunrise, and just live. The value of consistency is that it creates a buffer: regular routines make deviations — parties, events, travel — much less damaging.

## [42:50] Addressing ADHD & Focus in the Modern World

Many people struggle with focus. There are individuals who truly struggle to the extent that they have clinically diagnosable ADHD — and there are people who struggle for other reasons, including environmental distraction and unrealistic expectations about what sustained focus feels like.

Two episodes on ADHD and focus exist: one on behavioral tools, nutrition, and supplementation; another on prescription drugs. Both generated polar opposite reactions, with half the audience calling each episode irresponsible and the other half calling it validating. The role of a science communicator is to cover it all and give people the information to decide for themselves.

Drugs like Adderall and Vyvanse are amphetamines — that is true. In the young brain they can help enhance the neuromodulators that allow for elevated activity in areas like the prefrontal cortex, allowing for more focused attention and less impulsivity. The main function of the prefrontal cortex is to say to areas of the brain that want to cause impulsive action: "Not now."

There are also powerful behavioral tools. Research in China has explored training children to focus by having them fix their gaze on visual targets. The longer a person focuses on a visual target, the longer the neural circuits associated with sustained focus are activated. Even one to three minutes of staring at a fixed point one to three feet away can bring online focus circuits that allow for enhanced concentration in the following 10 to 20 minutes. This is vergent vision — the cuttlefish hunting, as opposed to the cuttlefish scanning. It does not require any equipment. Place a target on the wall, stare at it, then transition to work.

None of us — ADHD or otherwise — should expect focus to work like a square-wave function: sit down, open a book, and instantly be in deep concentration. Focus requires a warm-up, like physical performance. Children and adults with ADHD often have tremendous focusing capacity for things they genuinely enjoy, which demonstrates the capacity exists — the threshold to access it is simply higher. Building focus capacity gradually over time, removing distractions, narrowing the visual field with a hat or hoodie if needed, and setting realistic expectations about the ramp-up required — these are the behavioral foundations. Pharmaceutical tools, prescribed by a licensed physician, are also a legitimate option.
