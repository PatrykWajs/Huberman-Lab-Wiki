## Episode Overview

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

Dr. Lauren Colenso-Semple, PhD in integrative physiology and certified strength and conditioning specialist, dismantles the pervasive myth that women require sex-specific training programs, cycle syncing, or special nutritional timing — demonstrating with data that men and women respond to resistance training almost identically at the molecular level. She provides a complete, evidence-based framework for structuring resistance training at 2–6 days per week, covering rep ranges, rest intervals, set volume, rate of movement, and how to build to higher frequency via push/pull/legs splits. The conversation also addresses how menstrual cycle phase, hormonal contraception, perimenopause, and menopause do not meaningfully alter training requirements or adaptations, while clarifying what HRT can and cannot do. Nutrition topics include the fasted vs. fed training debate, the post-workout protein window, and the evidence on creatine monohydrate. The episode closes with rapid-fire coverage of cortisol misinformation, overtraining, ice bath recovery trade-offs, balance training, ab work, and Dr. Colenso-Semple's own push/pull/legs routine.

## Key Takeaways

- **Men and women respond to resistance training almost identically** at the molecular level — muscle protein synthesis, growth response, and relative strength gains are the same. The only meaningful sex difference is baseline muscle mass, set during puberty by the testosterone surge, not by training potential. [00:02:43](transcript.md#000243-muscle-in-men-vs-women-testosterone-individual-variation)

- **Testosterone within the normal range does not predict training response.** Whether a man's testosterone is 400 or 900 ng/dL, or a woman's is at the low end of her personal normal, there is no clear linear relationship to muscle gain. Only supraphysiological (pharmacological) levels change the calculus. [00:02:43](transcript.md#000243-muscle-in-men-vs-women-testosterone-individual-variation)

- **Resistance training is safe and beneficial starting at any age, including teenage girls.** Cultural stereotypes — not physiology — kept young women out of weight rooms. Starting earlier builds a "savings account" of muscle mass against age-related loss; you can also start at 70 and still gain muscle. [00:10:45](transcript.md#001045-testosterone--women-resistance-training-young-girls)

- **For beginners training 2–3x per week, full-body sessions are optimal.** Target all major muscle groups each session. Splits (upper/lower at 4 days; push/pull/legs at 5–6 days) become appropriate only as frequency increases. [00:17:46](transcript.md#001746-tool-beginner-resistance-training-for-women-frequency--goals)

- **Proximity to failure — not rep range — is the primary driver of hypertrophy.** Stopping 1–2 reps before true failure is the critical stimulus. Rep ranges from 3 to 30 all produce similar growth provided sets are taken close to failure, but lower-rep training requires significantly more sets to match the volume of moderate-rep training. [00:33:19](transcript.md#003319-tool-repetition-ranges-technique-vary-rep-ranges)

- **2–4 hard work sets per muscle group per session is sufficient.** Beyond four sets per exercise is likely overkill. Agonist-antagonist supersets (bench press paired immediately with a row; press with pulldown) maintain adaptation while cutting rest time and preventing phone distractions. [00:20:58](transcript.md#002058-tools-weekly-full-body-workouts-work-sets-rest-intervals-time-efficiency)

- **Move the weight as fast as possible during the hard (concentric) phase; control on the easier phase.** Intentionally slowing either direction is not advantageous. By reps 7–9 of a set of 10, natural slowing occurs because of load — this is not deliberate tempo work. [00:28:43](transcript.md#002843-forced-reps-drop-sets-rate-of-movement-partial-reps)

- **Menstrual cycle phase should not dictate training modifications.** The data do not support cycle syncing. Train consistently and progressively regardless of phase. Skipping 1–2 workouts for severe symptoms is acceptable but not physiologically required — survey data show ~75–80% of women report symptoms yet most do not change their training in response. [00:52:43](transcript.md#005243-menstrual-cycle-hormones--training-overcoming-internal-resistance)

- **Nutrition should not be changed based on cycle phase.** Observational data on cycle-phase eating are self-reported and insufficient to support changes in caloric, protein, or carbohydrate intake. The anabolic/catabolic phase hypothesis that spawned these recommendations is not supported by human outcome data. [01:14:57](transcript.md#011457-menstrual-cycle--physical-activity-nutrition)

- **Combined oral contraceptive pills do not blunt strength, hypertrophy, or power adaptations.** Massive endogenous hormone fluctuations across the menstrual cycle do not affect performance or adaptation — so synthetic hormones at similar magnitudes are equally unlikely to. Short-term side effects from a new pill may affect how training feels, but the adaptation itself is preserved. [01:03:59](transcript.md#010359-hormone-contraception--adaptations-perimenopause-menopause)

- **No training changes are needed during perimenopause or menopause.** Age-related muscle loss is primarily driven by physical inactivity and nervous system changes, not by estrogen decline. Lean mass does not accelerate its loss during the menopause transition itself. Training goals (muscle, bone density, fall/fracture risk reduction) remain identical. [01:03:59](transcript.md#010359-hormone-contraception--adaptations-perimenopause-menopause)

- **Pilates, yoga, and walking do not provide sufficient progressive resistance to offset age-related muscle loss.** Two 20-minute full-body resistance sessions per week is the minimum effective dose to attenuate sarcopenia. Muscle gain is possible at any age, including starting at 70. [01:17:50](transcript.md#011750-pilates-genes-tool-resistance-training-to-offset-age-related-muscle-loss)

- **Body somatotypes (ectomorph/mesomorph/endomorph) have no basis in exercise physiology research.** These categories originated in psychology. Genetic variability — bone structure, muscle insertions, fat distribution, height — is real and large, but is a starting point, not a fixed destiny. [01:26:25](transcript.md#012625-ectomorph-mesomorph-or-endomorph)

- **Training fasted vs. fed produces identical long-term muscle growth and fat loss outcomes.** Long-term studies in men and women confirm this. For most resistance training sessions, stored glycogen is sufficient fuel. Decide based entirely on personal preference and GI comfort. [01:30:42](transcript.md#013042-train-fasted-caffeine-preworkout--postworkout-nutrition)

- **The post-workout anabolic window is much wider than once believed.** Elevated muscle protein synthesis persists up to 24 hours post-training. Eating within 30 minutes vs. 3 hours post-workout does not meaningfully affect adaptation. Total daily protein intake, split across several meals consistently, matters far more than timing. [01:38:29](transcript.md#013829-protein-resistance-training--timing)

- **Creatine monohydrate (5 g/day) is safe, well-studied, and effective for training performance.** It yields an extra rep or shaves a second off a sprint. Gummy forms often contain negligible actual creatine — use powder. Creatine raises creatinine on bloodwork (tell your physician — it is benign). Brain health claims for healthy adults are premature; existing positive data come from clinical deficit populations (Alzheimer's, TBI, severe sleep deprivation). [01:40:12](transcript.md#014012-creatine-supplements-gummies-dose-brain-health-benefits)

- **Cortisol misinformation is rampant and harmful.** Acute cortisol spikes from exercise, stress, or cold exposure are normal and necessary for blood pressure and glucose regulation. They do not cause fat storage or Cushing-like changes in healthy people. Cortisol-reducing supplements are unnecessary and ineffective; pathologically elevated cortisol requires medical treatment. [01:54:52](transcript.md#015452-cortisol--women-stress--diet-cushing-syndrome)

- **HRT effectively targets menopausal symptoms but should not be oversold.** Current data support estrogen-based HRT for symptom management (hot flashes, poor sleep, night sweats). Data do not support claims that HRT prevents cognitive decline or cardiovascular disease. Testosterone therapy in women is evidence-supported only for low libido, at very low doses. [02:04:07](transcript.md#020407-menopause-symptoms--hormone-therapy-testosterone)

- **Ice baths and NSAIDs may attenuate soreness but blunt hypertrophic adaptation.** Reducing post-exercise inflammation shortens recovery time but compromises the stimulus needed for muscle growth. There is no proven method to accelerate adaptation without this trade-off. [02:23:06](transcript.md#022306-zone-2-cardio-weighted-vest-balance-training-ab-exercises-recovery)

## Tools & Protocols

### Beginner Full-Body Resistance Training Program
- **What:** Full-body program targeting all major muscle groups each session, designed for women new to structured resistance training at any age.
- **How:** 2–3 sessions per week (e.g., Mon/Wed/Fri). Train the entire body each session. 2–4 hard work sets per muscle group per exercise after warm-up sets. Select a load where near-failure is reached within your rep range. Progress load or reps over time. Vary movement selection per muscle group across sessions if desired (e.g., squat Mon, good morning Wed, hip thrust Fri for lower body). If training 4 days/week, shift to upper/lower split; 5–6 days/week, use push/pull/legs.
- **When:** Any time of day. Morning training tends to produce better energy throughout the day for many people.
- **Ref:** [00:17:46](transcript.md#001746-tool-beginner-resistance-training-for-women-frequency--goals); [00:20:58](transcript.md#002058-tools-weekly-full-body-workouts-work-sets-rest-intervals-time-efficiency)

### Warm-Up Protocol
- **What:** Progressive warm-up before work sets to rehearse mechanics and assess readiness.
- **How:** 1 light set at very easy effort through full ROM. 1 moderate set at roughly 50% of working load for 7–10 reps. Then proceed to work sets. A general dynamic warm-up and a specific lighter-load rehearsal are equally effective — no strict stretching routine required.
- **When:** Before every resistance training session.
- **Ref:** [00:40:28](transcript.md#004028-high-reps--injury-technique--warm-ups)

### Rep Range Selection
- **What:** Choose rep ranges based on goal; proximity to failure matters more than the specific range.
- **How:** Beginners: 8–12 reps to build movement proficiency before loading heavily. Experienced: 6–12 is a practical sweet spot for most exercises. Very high (15–30) or very low (3–5) rep ranges also drive hypertrophy if taken close to failure — but low-rep training requires significantly more sets to match volume. For variation within the week, do a lower-rep day and a moderate-rep day on the same compound lift.
- **When:** Throughout all training phases.
- **Ref:** [00:33:19](transcript.md#003319-tool-repetition-ranges-technique-vary-rep-ranges)

### Rest Intervals
- **What:** Autoregulate rest periods based on exercise type and goals.
- **How:** ~2 min for most exercises; ~3–5 min for heavy compound lifts (squat, deadlift, 1RM work). Use agonist-antagonist supersets (bench press → row; press → pulldown) to cut rest time without reducing adaptation — go from one exercise directly to the antagonist, rest the remainder of the interval, then repeat. Avoid getting lost in your phone between sets.
- **When:** Every training session.
- **Ref:** [00:20:58](transcript.md#002058-tools-weekly-full-body-workouts-work-sets-rest-intervals-time-efficiency)

### Rate of Movement
- **What:** Move the weight as fast as possible during the hard (concentric) phase; control on the easier (eccentric) phase.
- **How:** Do not intentionally slow either direction. Focus on "stand up fast" out of a squat even if load prevents moving quickly. By reps 7–9 of a set of 10, natural slowing occurs due to fatigue — this is not deliberate tempo training. Partial reps and isometric holds introduce variables that complicate progressive overload tracking and are not recommended as primary tools.
- **When:** Every set.
- **Ref:** [00:28:43](transcript.md#002843-forced-reps-drop-sets-rate-of-movement-partial-reps)

### Cardiovascular Training Integration
- **What:** Incorporate cardio without impairing resistance training adaptations.
- **How:** If training both in one session, do resistance training first. Avoid stacking high-intensity cardio (sprints) immediately before a leg workout. Separate by at least several hours, ideally a different day. True interference effect only occurs with very high concurrent volumes. For cardio goals, HIIT (e.g., 30 sec on / 30 sec off on a bike) matches longer moderate-intensity sessions for cardiovascular adaptation in less time. Enjoyable activities (tennis, hiking, cycling, pickleball) count toward weekly activity. Walking is beneficial for inactive people but is not sufficient as the sole exercise.
- **When:** Flexible scheduling around resistance sessions.
- **Ref:** [00:44:25](transcript.md#004425-cardiovascular-exercise-interference-effect-walking-high-intensity)

### Menstrual Cycle & Training
- **What:** No mandatory training modifications based on cycle phase.
- **How:** Train consistently and progressively across the entire cycle. If menstrual symptoms (cramps, low back pain, fatigue, low motivation) are severe on a given day, adjusting or skipping 1–2 workouts is acceptable — not required. Do not restructure the program around cycle phases or modify nutrition by phase.
- **When:** Throughout the cycle.
- **Ref:** [00:52:43](transcript.md#005243-menstrual-cycle-hormones--training-overcoming-internal-resistance); [01:14:57](transcript.md#011457-menstrual-cycle--physical-activity-nutrition)

### Resistance Training Through Perimenopause & Menopause
- **What:** Maintain or increase resistance training through and after menopause to preserve muscle, bone density, and reduce fall/fracture risk.
- **How:** Same principles as any other life stage — progressive overload, compound movements, sufficient volume. Estrogen decline does not independently accelerate muscle loss; physical inactivity is the primary driver. Machines and group fitness are good entry points for gym-averse beginners. Minimum effective dose: 2 x 20-min full-body sessions per week.
- **When:** Continuous — no break during menopause transition.
- **Ref:** [01:03:59](transcript.md#010359-hormone-contraception--adaptations-perimenopause-menopause); [01:09:01](transcript.md#010901-age-related-muscle-loss-nervous-system-tool-machines--group-fitness)

### Minimum Effective Dose for Countering Age-Related Muscle Loss
- **What:** Two 20-minute full-body resistance sessions per week as the minimum stimulus to attenuate sarcopenia.
- **How:** Target all major muscle groups with progressive overload. Pilates, yoga, and walking are insufficient as standalone stimuli for this purpose. Muscle gain is possible at any age — starting at 70 or later is still beneficial.
- **When:** Lifelong; start as early as possible.
- **Ref:** [01:17:50](transcript.md#011750-pilates-genes-tool-resistance-training-to-offset-age-related-muscle-loss)

### Training Fasted or Fed
- **What:** Base the decision entirely on personal preference.
- **How:** Long-term studies show identical muscle growth and fat loss outcomes for fasted vs. fed resistance training in both sexes. Stored glycogen is sufficient for most resistance sessions. For very long endurance sessions, pre-training nutrition is more relevant. Solid food eaten immediately before a session will not yet be absorbed as fuel for that session.
- **When:** Choose what makes you feel best and perform best.
- **Ref:** [01:30:42](transcript.md#013042-train-fasted-caffeine-preworkout--postworkout-nutrition)

### Protein Intake & Timing
- **What:** Prioritize consistent total daily protein over per-meal dosing or narrow timing windows.
- **How:** Muscle protein synthesis remains elevated for up to 24 hours post-training. Eating within 30 minutes vs. 3 hours post-workout does not meaningfully affect adaptation. Split daily protein across several meals for practicality. Do not obsess over an immediate post-workout protein shake.
- **When:** Distributed throughout the day; no strict post-workout window required.
- **Ref:** [01:38:29](transcript.md#013829-protein-resistance-training--timing)

### Creatine Supplementation
- **What:** Creatine monohydrate for training performance, confirmed safe with long-term use.
- **How:** 5 g/day, powder form. Gummy formats (even top Amazon sellers) often contain negligible actual creatine — avoid them. Timing is not critical. Creatine will raise creatinine on bloodwork — this is benign, but inform your physician. Only beneficial if actively training; no training stimulus means no muscle growth benefit.
- **When:** Daily, indefinitely while training continues.
- **Ref:** [01:40:12](transcript.md#014012-creatine-supplements-gummies-dose-brain-health-benefits)

### Balance Training Integration
- **What:** Include unilateral and multi-plane exercises for fall prevention and functional longevity.
- **How:** Single-leg Romanian deadlifts (can hold onto something for support), walking lunges, and other unilateral movements build the coordination needed to catch falls in real life. Train through full ranges of motion in multiple planes. Incorporate within regular resistance sessions — no separate balance session needed. Weighted vest walking is not a substitute for resistance training and does not improve muscle or bone.
- **When:** Integrated into regular resistance training sessions.
- **Ref:** [02:23:06](transcript.md#022306-zone-2-cardio-weighted-vest-balance-training-ab-exercises-recovery)

### Lauren's Personal Training Schedule
- **What:** Push/pull/legs rotation, 30–45 minutes per session, high frequency.
- **How:** Rotate push → pull → lower body → push → pull → lower body, with a rest day inserted when schedule requires. Sessions are shorter (30–45 min) to enable higher frequency and sustain the habit. Gets close to failure most sets; goes to true failure occasionally, especially on machines where failure is risk-free. Cardio is not structured — hiking with dogs and outdoor activity serves that purpose.
- **When:** Prefers mornings; avoids evening training to protect sleep.
- **Ref:** [02:16:19](transcript.md#021619-laurens-training-schedule-mobility-work)
