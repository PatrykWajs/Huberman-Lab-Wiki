# Episode 236: How to Safeguard Your Hormone Health & Fertility | Dr. Shanna Swan

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Shanna Swan, professor of environmental medicine and reproductive health at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai and the world's leading expert on endocrine disruptors, joins Andrew Huberman to explain how widespread chemical exposures — from food packaging, pesticides, cosmetics, and household products — are disrupting hormones and driving a global decline in fertility. She details the mechanisms by which phthalates, BPA, PFAS, and pesticides impair fetal development, alter reproductive anatomy, and suppress sperm counts and quality in males while elevating androgens in females. The episode closes with an extensive, practical toolkit of concrete steps anyone can take to minimize exposure and protect hormone health and fertility.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Environmental chemicals — especially phthalates — are measurably disrupting human reproductive health at the population level.** Phthalates are found in food packaging, personal care products, medical tubing, household plastics, and pesticide formulations. They are anti-androgenic: they lower testosterone and interfere with the masculinizing hormonal signals during fetal development. This is not a theoretical risk — it has been documented in multiple human birth cohort studies. [00:06:49](transcript.md#000649-environmental-chemicals-fertility-hormones-phthalates)

2. **Phthalate exposure during pregnancy produces "phthalate syndrome" in male offspring: reduced anogenital distance (AGD), smaller genitalia, and incompletely descended testes.** Swan's landmark work showed that male infants born to mothers with the highest urinary phthalate levels had significantly shorter AGD compared to those with the lowest levels. This was first observed in rodents at the National Toxicology Program (NTP) and later confirmed in human cohorts by Swan's team over a 10-year, $10 million research program. The three most anti-androgenic phthalates identified were DEHP, DBP, and BBzP. [00:19:11](transcript.md#001911-phthalate-syndrome-in-humans-pregnancy--babies)

3. **Anogenital distance (AGD) in males predicts adult sperm count.** Short AGD — indicative of reduced in-utero androgen exposure — correlates with lower sperm counts, lower testosterone, and reduced fertility in adult men. AGD is a permanent, measurable anatomical marker of the hormonal environment during the critical fetal masculinization window. In the animal literature, "AGD is forever" — adjusted for body size, it remains proportional throughout life. Mike Eisenberg at Stanford confirmed the link in infertility clinic patients: men who had fathered children had longer AGD than infertile men trying to conceive. [00:39:03](transcript.md#003903-anogenital-distance--sperm-count)

4. **Sperm counts in Western men have declined approximately 59% over the past 50 years, continuing to fall at roughly 1% per year.** Swan's 2017 meta-analysis of 185 studies confirmed this trend; she originally controlled for all known confounders and found the slope of decline was unchanged. Sperm counts below approximately 40–45 million per milliliter meaningfully impair fertility; above ~75–100 million per mL, additional quantity no longer helps. A Danish study found that if a male's mother smoked during pregnancy, he has a ~50% reduction in sperm count — a permanent developmental deficit, unlike the reversible reduction from his own adult smoking. [00:51:11](transcript.md#005111-sperm-count-decline)

5. **Pesticide exposure — particularly organophosphates and atrazine — significantly impairs sperm quality and male reproductive development.** Swan's four-city US study found men in agricultural Missouri had half the motile sperm of men in Minneapolis; within Missouri, five pesticides were significantly elevated in men with low sperm parameters. Atrazine, one of the most widely used herbicides in the US (banned in the EU), was shown by Tyrone Hayes to cause male amphibians to mount other males and to develop female reproductive tissue — evidence of neuroendocrine reorganization of the hypothalamic mating circuitry. [00:58:19](transcript.md#005819-sperm-quality--pesticides)

6. **Prenatal phthalate exposure alters sexually dimorphic play behavior in preschool-aged boys.** Using the Pre-School Activities Inventory (PSAI) in boys at age four, Swan's team found that higher prenatal phthalate exposure was associated with less rough-and-tumble play and more female-typical play. This effect was replicated in a Swedish cohort (collaborator Carl Bornehag), and controls were applied for sibling sex and parental attitudes. This indicates phthalates affect brain organization, not just genital anatomy, during the fetal programming window. [01:09:00](transcript.md#010900-preschoolers-phthalate-exposure-sexually-dimorphic-behaviors)

7. **Phthalate and androgen-disrupting chemical exposure during pregnancy is also linked to elevated androgen profiles in female offspring, increasing risk of PCOS.** Women with PCOS (elevated androgens) have daughters with a longer — more masculine — anogenital distance, confirming the measure is bidirectional. Environmental androgens in the maternal environment during pregnancy may be contributing to rising PCOS prevalence globally. [00:34:22](transcript.md#003422-polycystic-ovary-syndrome-pcos-mothers--female-offspring)

8. **BPA, BPS, and BPF in can linings and plastic containers are estrogenic endocrine disruptors.** The primary dietary BPA source is the inner lining of canned foods and beverages — every can unless the brand explicitly labels otherwise. BPS and BPF ("BPA's evil relatives") were introduced as "BPA-free" alternatives but carry similar hormonal activity. Heating food in plastic or leaving a plastic bottle in a hot car substantially increases chemical leaching because plasticizers are not chemically bound to the plastic matrix. [01:24:52](transcript.md#012452-tools-bpa-bps-bpf--can-linings-drinkware-plastics--microwave)

9. **Europe's chemical safety framework (REACH: precautionary principle) is far more protective than the US system.** In Europe, manufacturers must prove safety before a chemical enters the market. In the US, chemicals are assumed safe until proven harmful — and the documented harms are slow to result in restriction. Swan's 10-year, $10 million effort to confirm the phthalate-AGD link illustrates why post-market harm studies cannot keep pace with 80,000+ chemicals in commerce. One concrete US success: California banned DEHP from IV bags. [01:40:12](transcript.md#014012-europe-vs-us-chemical-safety-reach-program)

10. **Pregnancy is the highest-leverage window for reducing exposure — fetal tissue is more permeable and the reproductive programming window is irreversible.** Developmental exposures cannot be undone by later lifestyle changes; whatever occurred in the womb "stays in the womb." These effects pass to at least three generations: the germ cells for a child's children are already carried within the exposed fetus. Swan's book *Count Down* includes two chapters of practical steps for pregnancy. [01:46:20](transcript.md#014620-tool-pregnancy--fetal-health)

11. **PFAS ("forever chemicals") accumulate in the body and disrupt thyroid function, sperm quality, and immune response.** PFAS are found in non-stick cookware, waterproof clothing, food packaging, and drinking water. A Faroe Islands study showed that people with high PFAS body burden from eating local fish had measurably impaired immune responses — raising the question of whether PFAS compromises vaccine efficacy. Unlike phthalates and bisphenols (water-soluble, cleared in hours after stopping exposure), PFAS are fat-soluble and persist for years to decades. Distilling or reverse-osmosis filtering drinking water effectively removes PFAS. [02:09:39](transcript.md#020939-tool-receipts-thyroid-system-non-stick-pans)

12. **Wildlife across dozens of species show parallel fertility and reproductive decline, confirming that chemical pollution — not lifestyle choice — is the primary driver.** Multiple non-human species in chemically contaminated environments show reduced sperm counts, intersex development, and reproductive failure. This cross-species evidence is decisive: declining fertility in animals cannot be attributed to delayed childbearing, contraceptive use, or education levels. Global total fertility rates have declined ~50% in 50 years; South Korea is at 0.78 and Japan at 1.0, far below the 2.1 replacement threshold. [01:58:04](transcript.md#015804-other-animals--fertility-decline-ecosystems)

## Tools & Protocols

### Avoid Plastic Food Packaging and Containers
- **What:** Minimize dietary phthalate and BPA intake by switching away from plastic food and beverage containers.
- **How:** Replace plastic food storage with glass, stainless steel, or ceramic containers. Never microwave food in plastic — "microwave-safe" labeling only means the plastic will not melt, not that chemicals will not leach. Avoid canned foods, or choose brands that explicitly label BPA-free and BPS-free can linings. Use a stainless steel or glass water bottle. Do not leave plastic bottles in a hot car — heat accelerates chemical leaching.
- **When:** Permanent lifestyle change; highest priority during pregnancy.
- **Ref:** [01:24:52](transcript.md#012452-tools-bpa-bps-bpf--can-linings-drinkware-plastics--microwave)

### Buy Organic Produce (Dirty Dozen Priority)
- **What:** Reduce pesticide ingestion — a major source of reproductive toxins including phthalates added to pesticide formulations to increase absorption.
- **How:** Prioritize buying organic versions of the EWG "Dirty Dozen" (highest pesticide-load produce, updated annually). Consider buying unwrapped produce where possible — Swan noted that unwrapped heads of lettuce were cheaper and lower in chemical exposure than plastic-wrapped versions.
- **When:** Ongoing; priority during preconception and pregnancy.
- **Ref:** [01:30:07](transcript.md#013007-tools-buying-organic-skin-products-fragrance-sunscreens-consumer-guides)

### Eliminate Fragranced Personal Care Products
- **What:** Remove phthalates (which stabilize and carry scent) and parabens from skin care, hair care, and cosmetics.
- **How:** Choose fragrance-free or unscented products. The word "fragrance" or "parfum" on an ingredient label is a legal loophole hiding dozens of phthalate compounds without disclosure. Swan advises: if you can smell it, it is probably affecting your hormones. This covers perfume, scented soaps, roll-on and spray deodorants, shampoos, and laundry detergent. Use EWG's Skin Deep database or the Think Dirty app to screen products before purchasing.
- **When:** Ongoing; critical during pregnancy.
- **Ref:** [01:30:07](transcript.md#013007-tools-buying-organic-skin-products-fragrance-sunscreens-consumer-guides)

### Distill or Reverse-Osmosis Filter Drinking Water
- **What:** Remove PFAS, pesticide residues, phthalates, and other endocrine disruptors from tap water.
- **How:** Home water distillers or reverse osmosis systems with remineralization effectively remove PFAS and most chemical contaminants. Standard carbon filters alone are insufficient for PFAS removal. Swan personally uses a tabletop distiller, collects condensed water in a glass container, and stores it refrigerated. She describes the result as tasting like "melted snow."
- **When:** Ongoing.
- **Ref:** [01:34:31](transcript.md#013431-tools-distilling-water-shoes-clothing-food-sourcing-building-materials)

### Use Consumer Safety Guides Before Purchasing Products
- **What:** Identify and avoid endocrine-disrupting chemicals in household and personal care products before buying.
- **How:** Use EWG Skin Deep ([https://www.ewg.org/skindeep](https://www.ewg.org/skindeep)) for personal care and EWG's Guide to Healthy Cleaning for household cleaners. Think Dirty app enables quick barcode scanning. Consult EWG Dirty Dozen / Clean Fifteen annually for produce guidance. For personal chemical body-burden testing (urine-based), Swan recommends Million Marker ([https://www.millionmarker.com](https://www.millionmarker.com)), which tests for phthalates, bisphenols, and other endocrine disruptors.
- **When:** Ongoing purchasing decisions.
- **Ref:** [02:06:02](transcript.md#020602-tool-consumer-guides-personal--household-products)

### Decline Paper Receipts
- **What:** Avoid BPA/BPS skin absorption from thermal paper receipts.
- **How:** Request email or digital receipts whenever possible. If handling a paper receipt is unavoidable, wash hands promptly. BPA from thermal paper is measurably absorbed through skin.
- **When:** Ongoing.
- **Ref:** [02:09:39](transcript.md#020939-tool-receipts-thyroid-system-non-stick-pans)

### Replace Non-Stick Cookware
- **What:** Eliminate PFAS exposure from Teflon-coated and PTFE non-stick cookware.
- **How:** Switch to seasoned cast iron, stainless steel, ceramic-coated, or carbon steel cookware. Swan states explicitly there is no risk associated with seasoned iron pans. PTFE coatings release PFAS when scratched or overheated.
- **When:** Replace as current cookware becomes scratched or worn; sooner if concerned.
- **Ref:** [02:09:39](transcript.md#020939-tool-receipts-thyroid-system-non-stick-pans)

### Leave Shoes at the Door
- **What:** Prevent tracking outdoor pesticide residues, PFAS, and chemical pollution into the living space.
- **How:** Designate a shoe-free zone at the entrance. Dust tracked in on shoe soles is a significant source of PFAS body burden, particularly for children who play on floors.
- **When:** Ongoing.
- **Ref:** [01:34:31](transcript.md#013431-tools-distilling-water-shoes-clothing-food-sourcing-building-materials)

### Wash New Clothing Before First Wear
- **What:** Remove chemical treatments (flame retardants, PFAS fabric coatings, dyes) from new textiles before skin contact.
- **How:** Machine wash all new clothing, bedding, and towels before first use. Swan flags workout wear as particularly high-risk: sweat and heat increase dermal absorption of textile chemicals. Prefer cotton over synthetic fabrics.
- **When:** Once, for all new textile items.
- **Ref:** [01:34:31](transcript.md#013431-tools-distilling-water-shoes-clothing-food-sourcing-building-materials)

### Keep Cell Phones Away from Reproductive Organs
- **What:** Reduce heat exposure to testes during extended daily phone carry.
- **How:** Avoid keeping a phone in a front pants pocket directly against the body for hours at a time. Use a bag, a back pocket, or place the phone on a desk. Swan has not personally studied this; she does not claim electromagnetic effects are established but notes heat is a documented factor in sperm quality decline.
- **When:** Ongoing.
- **Ref:** [01:55:26](transcript.md#015526-sperm-quality-fertility-cell-phone-temperature)

### Pregnancy-Specific Maximum Exposure Reduction Protocol
- **What:** During the critical fetal programming window (especially early first trimester), combine all exposure-reduction measures simultaneously.
- **How:** Glass/stainless food storage; no plastic in the microwave; organic produce (Dirty Dozen priority); fragrance-free personal care; distilled or RO-filtered water; decline paper receipts; replace non-stick pans; shoes off at door; wash new clothes before wearing. Avoid home renovation or fresh paint during pregnancy. Avoid high-fat fish from potentially contaminated waters (PFAS bioaccumulate up the food chain). These effects pass to at least three generations — the germ cells for grandchildren are carried within the exposed fetus.
- **When:** Preconception through at least end of second trimester; ideally permanent.
- **Ref:** [01:46:20](transcript.md#014620-tool-pregnancy--fetal-health)
