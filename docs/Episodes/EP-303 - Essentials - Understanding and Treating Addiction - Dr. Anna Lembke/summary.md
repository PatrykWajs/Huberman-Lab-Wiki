# Episode 303: Essentials - Understanding & Treating Addiction - Dr. Anna Lembke

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

In this Huberman Lab Essentials episode, Andrew Huberman interviews Dr. Anna Lembke, Chief of the Stanford Addiction Medicine Dual Diagnosis Clinic, on the neuroscience of dopamine and addiction. Dr. Lembke explains the pleasure-pain balance model, how chronic overstimulation creates a dopamine deficit state, and what science-backed strategies — including 30-day abstinence, truth-telling, and intentional barriers — can support recovery. The episode also covers psychedelic-assisted therapy and the addictive design of social media.

## Key Takeaways

- **Dopamine operates relative to baseline, not in absolute spikes:** Dopamine releases constantly at a tonic baseline; pleasure is a rise above it, pain is a dip below. It is the deviation from baseline — not raw magnitude — that determines experience. [00:00:15](transcript.md#000015-dopamine-reward--movement)
- **Chronic overstimulation permanently lowers your dopamine baseline:** Repeated high-dopamine behaviors down-regulate receptors, eventually producing a dopamine deficit state — marked by anxiety, insomnia, dysphoria, and mental preoccupation — that persists even when not using. [00:01:54](transcript.md#000154-baseline-dopamine-genetics-temperament--addiction)
- **Impulsivity is the top temperamental risk factor for addiction:** It is not a moral failing — impulsive brains are ill-suited to a sensory-overloaded modern environment, not to life in general. [00:01:54](transcript.md#000154-baseline-dopamine-genetics-temperament--addiction)
- **The pleasure-pain seesaw governs every reward interaction:** Pleasure and pain are co-located in the brain; each pleasure tip is followed by an equal and opposite pain correction. Overindulgence can break the balance's resilience, leaving some people in a chronic deficit state even after years of abstinence. [00:07:18](transcript.md#000718-pleasure-pain-balance-dopamine-addiction)
- **30 days of total abstinence resets reward pathways:** The brain needs roughly 30 consecutive days without the substance or behavior for dopamine transmission to regenerate. Weeks one and two are worst; improvement typically begins in week three; most people feel significantly better by week four. [00:12:39](transcript.md#001239-resetting-dopamine-substance-or-behavior-recovery-tool-30-day-abstinence)
- **Triggers — including good news — release anticipatory dopamine followed immediately by a deficit, which is craving:** Even thinking about a drug spikes dopamine briefly then drops below baseline. Positive life events can trigger relapse by relaxing the hypervigilance required to stay sober. [00:18:39](transcript.md#001839-triggers-relapse-dopamine)
- **Truth-telling in all areas of life is an active neurological recovery tool:** Habitual honesty — not just about drug use — strengthens prefrontal cortical connections to the limbic and reward brain, re-engaging the circuits addiction disconnects. [00:21:37](transcript.md#002137-shame-truth-telling--recovery)
- **Psychedelic-assisted therapy (psilocybin, MDMA) shows promise for certain individuals but only inside rigorous clinical trials:** When effective, it provides a values-based spiritual lens shift. Recreational or unsupervised use is dangerous and almost never replicates clinical outcomes. [00:23:58](transcript.md#002358-addiction-psychedelic-assisted-therapy-psilocybin-mdma)
- **Social media is engineered as a drug; healthy use requires pre-planned intentionality:** Physical barriers, purpose-driven sessions, and maintaining offline human connection are necessary to avoid the addictive loop and the loss of sustained creative thought. [00:29:01](transcript.md#002901-social-media--addiction-tool-intentionality)

## Tools & Protocols

### 30-Day Abstinence Reset
- **What:** Complete abstinence from the addictive substance or behavior for 30 consecutive days.
- **How:** Zero interaction — no moderation, no tapering. Expect to feel significantly worse for the first two weeks (anxiety, sleep disruption, irritability, physical agitation); improvement begins around week three; most people feel better than pre-abstinence by week four.
- **When:** At any point when breaking an addictive pattern; applies to substances, behaviors (gambling, porn, social media), and even certain relationships.
- **Ref:** [00:12:39](transcript.md#001239-resetting-dopamine-substance-or-behavior-recovery-tool-30-day-abstinence)

### Intentional Barrier Strategy for Social Media & Behavioral Addictions
- **What:** Pre-planning physical and metacognitive barriers between yourself and the addictive behavior before entering tempting situations.
- **How:** Decide in advance — before sitting down, before picking up the phone — what you will and won't do. Use literal physical separation (phone in another room). Plan usage with a specific purpose rather than open-ended scrolling to preserve offline connection and sustained thought.
- **When:** Daily, and especially before situations where you know you're vulnerable (boredom, celebrations, stress).
- **Ref:** [00:29:01](transcript.md#002901-social-media--addiction-tool-intentionality)

### Radical Truth-Telling as a Recovery Practice
- **What:** Committing to honesty in all areas of life — not just about drug use — as an active neurological intervention.
- **How:** Stop all habitual small lies (excuses for being late, white lies, omissions). Practice transparency with a trusted person, therapist, or sponsor. This actively strengthens prefrontal-limbic connectivity and creates genuine interpersonal connection that itself generates dopamine.
- **When:** Daily, throughout recovery, and especially when urges arise.
- **Ref:** [00:21:37](transcript.md#002137-shame-truth-telling--recovery)

### Trigger Awareness Mapping
- **What:** Identifying personal relapse triggers — including positive events — to put protective structures in place before vulnerability windows open.
- **How:** Reflect on past relapse patterns. Note whether stress, loss, or success/reward triggers cravings. When a known trigger is approaching (promotion, celebration, vacation), proactively increase support (more meetings, check-ins, telling someone in advance).
- **When:** Ongoing, as a proactive maintenance practice in recovery.
- **Ref:** [00:18:39](transcript.md#001839-triggers-relapse-dopamine)
