# Episode 23: How to Build Endurance in Your Brain & Body

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Huberman covers the complete science of endurance across four distinct types — muscular endurance, long-duration aerobic effort, anaerobic HIIT, and aerobic HIIT — explaining the physiology behind quitting vs. persisting, including the locus coeruleus epinephrine system and the glial fatigue threshold. The episode details the five physiological systems that enable effort (neurons, muscle, blood, heart, lungs) and what each endurance type builds in those systems, from local mitochondrial respiration and motor neuron recruitment to capillary bed expansion and left-ventricle stroke volume increase. Practical tools cover breathing mechanics (nasal vs. mouth gearing, intercostal warm-up, physiological sighs for side cramps), the Galpin hydration equation, cold exposure timing for mitochondrial density, visual focus strategies for pacing and accessing the "extra gear," and the neuroscience of Steve Prefontaine-style reserve capacity. Supplementation for endurance — caffeine, beta-alanine, magnesium malate, and nitric oxide precursors — is discussed with dosing and caveats, and programming for concurrent strength and endurance training is addressed.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Quitting is neurological, not muscular.** Locus coeruleus neurons release epinephrine to sustain effort; glial cells monitor that release and shut it off when a threshold is crossed — that is when you quit. Training and mindset modulate this governor. [00:22:50](transcript.md#002250-why-you-quit-it-is-all-in-your-mind)

2. **It is 100% neural — the "90% mental" framing is meaningless.** Mental vs. physical is a false dichotomy. Everything, including willpower and motivation, is neurons, chemicals, and electrical signals. [00:27:19](transcript.md#002719-the-90-mental-myth)

3. **Neurons need glucose and electrolytes to keep firing.** Sodium drives the action potential, potassium resets it, magnesium and ATP power the sodium-potassium pump. Carbohydrates (or ketones if keto-adapted) fuel the whole system. Ruminating about whether to train burns the same neural energy as training itself. [00:28:10](transcript.md#002810-the-critical-need-for-carbohydrates-electrolytes-and-sometimes-ketones)

4. **Four endurance types require four distinct protocols.** Muscular endurance, long-duration (>12 min), anaerobic HIIT, and aerobic HIIT each stress different energy systems and demand different set/rep/rest structures. Everyone should train at least some of each. [00:37:15](transcript.md#003715-the-4-kinds-of-endurance)

5. **Muscular endurance: 3–5 sets x 12–100 reps, 30–180 s rest, no heavy eccentric.** Failure is local muscular, not cardiovascular. Builds mitochondrial respiration and motor neuron-muscle coupling. Avoid heavy or slow eccentric phases and avoid Olympic lifts at high rep counts due to form-degradation injury risk. [00:41:50](transcript.md#004150-protocol-for-building-muscular-endurance-no-major-eccentric-component)

6. **Long-duration endurance (>12 min, one "set") builds capillary beds in muscle and brain.** Key adaptation is mitochondrial density and microvascular expansion — more oxygen delivered per unit of effort. Each session makes the next feel easier because you are burning less fuel for the same output. [00:57:00](transcript.md#005700-why-everyone-should-train-long-duration-endurance-capillaries-in-muscle-and-brain)

7. **Anaerobic HIIT exceeds VO2 max; 3–12 sets, work:rest 3:1 or 1:5.** Trains mitochondrial oxygen utilization and nerve-to-muscle ATP access. Quality of reps is the limiting factor — use longer rest (1:5 ratio) when form matters (weights, kettlebells). [01:02:20](transcript.md#010220-anaerobic-hiit-3-12-sets-workrest-ratio-of-31-or-15-quality-of-repetitions-is-key)

8. **Aerobic HIIT at 1:1 work-to-rest builds stroke volume.** 3–12 sets of mile repeats (run 1 mile / rest same duration). Blood returning hard to the heart eccentrically loads the left ventricle, thickening it so it pumps more blood per beat — an adaptation unavailable from pure strength training. [01:10:59](transcript.md#011059-aerobic-hiit-11-workrest-ratio-tapping-into-all-energy-utilization-systems)

9. **Strength training alone does not improve brain oxygenation the same way endurance does.** Endurance work — especially high-intensity and long-duration — increases capillary beds inside the brain (hippocampus and beyond), delivering more glucose and oxygen to neurons and improving cognitive function. [01:20:10](transcript.md#012010-resistance-weight-training-useless-for-the-brain-what-is-good-for-the-brain)

10. **Nasal breathing = efficiency gear; mouth breathing = intensity gear.** Transition deliberately through gears as intensity rises. Exhale on the concentric (max effort) phase; inhale on the eccentric. Never hold breath during exertion. [01:25:45](transcript.md#012545-breathing-during-endurance-explosive-and-weight-training-nose-mouth-gears)

11. **Warm up the intercostals and diaphragm before training.** Three minutes of alternating chest-raising (intercostal) and belly-out (diaphragmatic) breaths improves oxygen delivery, warms the breathing muscles, and generates a mild adrenaline surge that raises motivation. [01:29:50](transcript.md#012950-intercostals-and-diaphragmatic-breathing-warming-up-intercostals-is-useful)

12. **Physiological sighs eliminate side cramps.** Side stitches are referred pain from the phrenic nerve innervating the liver — not a true muscle cramp. A double-inhale through the nose followed by a long exhale fully fires the phrenic nerve and clears the stitch. Repeat 1–3 times. [01:32:10](transcript.md#013210-eliminating-the-side-cramp-with-physiological-sighs)

13. **Accelerate through "the wall" — do not stop.** A slight speed increase when bonking shifts muscle fibers toward phosphocreatine or fat/ketone pathways. Exogenous ketones alongside carbohydrates serve as a fuel bridge. Stopping cements quitting; forward motion maintains the metabolic switch. [01:34:45](transcript.md#013445-accelerating-through-the-wall-accessing-alternative-fuel-sources-ketone-use)

14. **The Galpin Equation: body weight (lbs) / 30 = ounces of fluid per 15 min of exercise.** Losing 1–4% body weight in water causes 20–30% reductions in work capacity and mental performance. Above 70% VO2 max, gastric emptying slows — train the ability to drink during intense work by starting with small sips. [01:41:35](transcript.md#014135-the-galpin-equation-gastric-emptying-time-adapting-hydration-mid-training)

15. **Cold exposure boosts mitochondrial density but requires ≥6 h separation from training.** Cold within 6 hours suppresses the pro-inflammatory signal needed for both endurance and strength adaptations. Cold after endurance work with adequate separation can enhance mitochondrial density and respiration. [01:44:20](transcript.md#014420-boosting-mitochondrial-density-with-cold-wait-6-hours-before-coldbetween-training)

16. **5-minute parasympathetic down-shift post-training accelerates recovery.** Lying still or slow nasal-exhale breathing for 5–20 minutes immediately after training quiets the locus coeruleus, shifts from sympathetic to parasympathetic, and allows faster return to physical or mental work. [01:46:00](transcript.md#014600-accelerating-recovery-with-5-minute-parasympathetic-down-shift-after-training)

17. **Narrow visual focus activates the extra gear; panoramic vision conserves it.** Vergence (converging gaze to a point or milestone) activates thalamic alertness circuits and releases epinephrine — enabling higher output and the "kick" seen in elite runners. Panoramic (soft, wide) gaze reduces perceived exertion. Visual pacers are banned in competitive racing precisely because they are performance-enhancing. [01:48:00](transcript.md#014800-leveraging-the-visual-system-during-effort-milestones-dilation-and-contraction-pacing)

## Tools & Protocols

### Muscular Endurance Training
- **What:** Resistance-based endurance targeting local muscles to concentric fatigue, not cardiovascular failure.
- **How:** 3–5 sets x 12–100 reps (12–25 practical for most). Rest 30–180 s between sets. Use movements with minimal eccentric load: push-ups, planks, wall sits, kettlebell swings, pull-ups with fast (not controlled-slow) lowering. Do NOT slow the lowering phase deliberately or use Olympic lifts at high reps — form breaks down and injury risk rises. Train to near or actual failure each set.
- **When:** 2–3x per week; if done same day as strength work, do strength first.
- **Ref:** [00:41:50](transcript.md#004150-protocol-for-building-muscular-endurance-no-major-eccentric-component)

### Long-Duration Endurance (Zone 2 / Steady State)
- **What:** Continuous aerobic effort ≥12 minutes at conversational pace — one continuous "set." Builds capillary beds and mitochondrial density in muscle and brain.
- **How:** Run, cycle, swim, row, hike — any sustained movement below 100% VO2 max. Prioritize movement efficiency over speed. 30–60 min is a practical target. Build duration progressively.
- **When:** 1–3x per week; can be placed any day; best before high-intensity sessions in the weekly schedule.
- **Ref:** [00:51:31](transcript.md#005131-long-duration-endurance-12-minutes-or-more-one-set-efficiency-of-movement)

### Anaerobic HIIT
- **What:** Short maximal-effort intervals exceeding VO2 max. Trains mitochondrial O2 utilization and nerve-muscle ATP access.
- **How:** 3–12 sets. Work:rest = 3:1 (e.g., 30 s on / 10 s off) or 1:5 (e.g., 20 s on / 100 s off). Use 1:5 when form matters (weights, kettlebells). Stop a set when form degrades. Start with 3 sets and add 1–2 sets per week. Modalities: sprints, assault bike, kettlebell swings, rowing.
- **When:** 1–2x per week; allow 48–72 h recovery; not adjacent to aerobic HIIT.
- **Ref:** [01:02:20](transcript.md#010220-anaerobic-hiit-3-12-sets-workrest-ratio-of-31-or-15-quality-of-repetitions-is-key)

### Aerobic HIIT (Mile Repeats / Long Intervals)
- **What:** Longer intervals at or near VO2 max stressing all energy systems. Develops stroke volume, capillary beds, and VO2 max.
- **How:** 3–12 sets. Work:rest = 1:1 (e.g., run 1 mile / rest equivalent time) is most effective. Intensity = hard but sustainable per interval, not an all-out sprint. Start with 3–4 sets; build over weeks.
- **When:** 1–2x per week; ≥48 h separation from anaerobic HIIT and heavy strength work; allow 4–6 h minimum (ideally 24 h) between concurrent sessions.
- **Ref:** [01:10:59](transcript.md#011059-aerobic-hiit-11-workrest-ratio-tapping-into-all-energy-utilization-systems)

### Nose-Mouth Breathing Gearing
- **What:** Consciously shifting between nasal and oral breathing to manage metabolic gear and oxygen delivery.
- **How:** Nasal = lower gear, higher efficiency — use for long-duration work if possible. As intensity climbs, incorporate mouth. At max effort, breathe through whatever works. Exhale during the concentric (max effort) phase of any repetition; inhale on the eccentric. Never hold breath during exertion.
- **When:** Every workout; critical for pacing in long-duration and HIIT efforts.
- **Ref:** [01:25:45](transcript.md#012545-breathing-during-endurance-explosive-and-weight-training-nose-mouth-gears)

### Intercostal Warm-Up for Motivation and Oxygen Delivery
- **What:** Breathing exercise to warm intercostal muscles and diaphragm before training; generates pre-workout epinephrine.
- **How:** 3 minutes alternating: (1) chest-raising breaths — emphasize rib expansion upward (intercostal activation); (2) belly-out breaths — stomach expands outward on inhale (diaphragmatic activation). Produces mild adrenaline surge, raises motivation and oxygen delivery to muscles.
- **When:** Pre-workout, especially when motivation is low, or during the first few minutes of a session.
- **Ref:** [01:29:50](transcript.md#012950-intercostals-and-diaphragmatic-breathing-warming-up-intercostals-is-useful)

### Physiological Sigh for Side Cramps
- **What:** Double-inhale + long exhale to fully activate the phrenic nerve and eliminate referred-pain side stitches.
- **How:** Inhale through nose until nearly full → take a second sniff on top (fully inflate) → slow, long exhale through mouth. Repeat 1–3 times. The double inhale fires the phrenic nerve more completely, including its collateral branch to the liver, clearing the referred pain.
- **When:** Whenever a side stitch appears during exercise.
- **Ref:** [01:32:10](transcript.md#013210-eliminating-the-side-cramp-with-physiological-sighs)

### Accelerating Through the Wall
- **What:** Strategy to access alternative fuel when glycogen is depleted — do not stop.
- **How:** When you hit the "bonk," slightly increase speed rather than stopping. This shifts muscle fibers toward phosphocreatine (short burst) or fat/ketone oxidation (sustained). Exogenous ketones (ketone esters or salts) alongside carbohydrates can serve as a fuel bridge. The body is adapted to using multiple fuel sources simultaneously; the keto-vs-carbs dichotomy is an oversimplification.
- **When:** Long-duration efforts when glycogen depletion is felt.
- **Ref:** [01:34:45](transcript.md#013445-accelerating-through-the-wall-accessing-alternative-fuel-sources-ketone-use)

### Hydration Protocol — The Galpin Equation
- **What:** Body-weight-calibrated in-session fluid intake formula developed by Andy Galpin.
- **How:** Body weight in lbs ÷ 30 = ounces of water per 15 minutes of exercise. Pre-hydrate before training. Add sodium, potassium, and magnesium if sessions exceed 60 min or sweat rate is high. Avoid over-drinking (hyponatremia risk — excess water flushes electrolytes and can stop heart function). Above 70% VO2 max, gastric emptying slows — train the ability to drink during intense work by starting with small sips and building up.
- **When:** Every training session regardless of modality.
- **Ref:** [01:41:35](transcript.md#014135-the-galpin-equation-gastric-emptying-time-adapting-hydration-mid-training)

### Cold Exposure for Mitochondrial Density
- **What:** Timed cold immersion to boost mitochondrial biogenesis without suppressing training adaptations.
- **How:** Do NOT use cold within 6 hours of any training session — cold suppresses the pro-inflammatory signal required for endurance and strength adaptations. Cold used >6 h post-training or on rest days may increase mitochondrial density and improve mitochondrial respiration.
- **When:** Rest days or with a >6 h buffer from the nearest workout.
- **Ref:** [01:44:20](transcript.md#014420-boosting-mitochondrial-density-with-cold-wait-6-hours-before-coldbetween-training)

### Post-Training Parasympathetic Down-Shift
- **What:** 5-minute passive recovery immediately post-workout to quiet the sympathetic nervous system and accelerate physiological recovery.
- **How:** Lie still or sit quietly for 5–20 minutes (5 min minimum). Breathe slowly with long exhales through the nose. No phone, no driving. NSDR or slow diaphragmatic breathing with extended exhales is optimal.
- **When:** End of every training session, any modality.
- **Ref:** [01:46:00](transcript.md#014600-accelerating-recovery-with-5-minute-parasympathetic-down-shift-after-training)

### Visual Focus Modulation for Pacing
- **What:** Using gaze width to regulate perceived effort, energy expenditure, and access reserve capacity.
- **How:** Narrow focus (vergence — converge gaze to a specific point or milestone ahead) activates thalamic alertness circuits (zona incerta) and brainstem circuits (locus coeruleus), releasing epinephrine and enabling higher output. Panoramic (soft, wide, dilated) gaze reduces perceived exertion and conserves neural energy during sustained pacing. Alternate: focus on a milestone → run to it → dilate to recover → focus again. Visual pacers are banned in competitive racing because they are genuinely performance-enhancing.
- **When:** Long-duration bouts, races, and outdoor efforts. Especially powerful in the final push or when a competitor passes you.
- **Ref:** [01:48:00](transcript.md#014800-leveraging-the-visual-system-during-effort-milestones-dilation-and-contraction-pacing)

### Supplementation for Endurance
- **What:** Evidence-based compounds with meaningful endurance effects.
- **How:**
  - **Caffeine** (100–400 mg, 30–60 min pre-workout): improves power output and sustained effort; one study suggests it may inhibit the creatine system — separate intake if using both. See examine.com for the specific study.
  - **Beta-alanine** (2–5 g/day): buffers muscular acid accumulation; most effective for 1–4 min moderate-intensity efforts; causes harmless tingling/paresthesia (the niacin-flush sensation) in some users.
  - **Magnesium malate** (up to ~400 mg): reduces delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS); malate form is distinct from magnesium threonate or bisglycinate used for sleep.
  - **Nitric oxide precursors** (beet root juice, citrulline, arginine): vasodilation improves O2 delivery to muscles and neurons; modest but real effect; some users experience GI discomfort.
- **When:** Per compound notes above. Caffeine timing is most performance-critical.
- **Ref:** [01:57:57](transcript.md#015757-caffeine-magnesium-malate-to-reduce-soreness-nitric-oxide-beta-alanine)
