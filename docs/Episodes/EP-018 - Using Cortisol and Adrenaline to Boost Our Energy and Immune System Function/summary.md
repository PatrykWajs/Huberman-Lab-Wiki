# Episode 18: Using Cortisol & Adrenaline to Boost Our Energy & Immune System Function

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman presents a comprehensive framework for cortisol and epinephrine (adrenaline) — two hormones governing energy, immune function, and learning — distinguishing between short-term beneficial spikes and harmful chronic elevation. Both hormones are derived from cholesterol (cortisol) or dopamine (epinephrine), meaning chronic stress diverts cholesterol away from testosterone and estrogen production while chronically high brainstem epinephrine degrades mental performance. The central behavioral tool is timing the morning cortisol pulse with outdoor sunlight within 30–60 minutes of waking, which simultaneously entrains thyroid hormone release and prevents late-shifted cortisol that correlates with depression and insomnia. Deliberate short-term adrenaline spikes via cold exposure, cyclic hyperventilation (Wim Hof/tummo breathing), or HIIT prime the immune system to fight infection — confirmed by the Kox et al. 2014 PNAS E. coli injection study — and, when applied immediately after a learning session, consolidate memory via hippocampal epinephrine signaling. Chronic stress lasting more than four to seven days inverts the cortisol feedback loop from negative to positive, driving comfort-food cravings, abdominal fat deposition, hair graying through melanocyte stem cell depletion (Zhang et al., Nature 2020), and immune suppression. Huberman covers supplements for blunting chronic cortisol (ashwagandha: 14–28% reduction across six studies; apigenin: 50 mg evening) and warns that glycyrrhizin in licorice raises cortisol while suppressing sex hormones; the meta-skill throughout is training the nervous system to stay mentally calm while the body is physically stressed.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cortisol is a hormone of energy, not just stress.** Derived from cholesterol — the same precursor as testosterone and estrogen — chronically high cortisol diverts that substrate away from sex hormones, suppressing them. The brain releases CRH → pituitary releases ACTH → adrenals release cortisol. [00:15:48](transcript.md#001548-cortisol--cholesterol-competition-with-testosterone--estrogen)

2. **Time your cortisol peak to the morning with sunlight.** Get outside within 30–60 minutes of waking, no sunglasses. Sunny (100,000 lux): 5–10 min. Dense overcast (10,000 lux): ~30 min. Indoor artificial light (~100–200 lux) requires ~6 hours — effectively useless for entrainment. Late-shifted cortisol peaking at 8–9 PM is a signature of major depression, anxiety, and insomnia. [00:21:32](transcript.md#002132-tool-time-your-cortisol-peak-to-waking-using-specific-light-intensities)

3. **Morning cortisol also times thyroid hormone release for dual energy stacking.** The circadian clock — entrained by the morning cortisol pulse — triggers TSH and subsequently T3/T4 release. Getting sunlight early therefore stacks cortisol and thyroid energy benefits simultaneously. [00:55:11](transcript.md#005511-timing-thyroid-release-for-energy)

4. **Brief epinephrine spikes actively boost immune function.** Adrenal epinephrine signals immune organs (spleen, lymph nodes) to mobilize killer cells (B and T cells). McEwen's classic research and the Kox et al. 2014 PNAS E. coli injection study confirm one to four days of acute stress enhances immune resistance; beyond that, chronic stress suppresses it. [00:45:00](transcript.md#004500-using-stressors-to-enhance-our-immune-system-science--tools)

5. **Voluntary adrenaline release is achievable on demand.** Cyclic hyperventilation (25–30 deep inhale-exhale cycles + exhale hold), cold water immersion, and HIIT all produce equivalent adrenal epinephrine spikes — the same pathway responsible for immune priming and memory consolidation. Caffeine also spikes epinephrine but the effect habituates with daily use. [00:36:15](transcript.md#003615-protocols-adrenaline-breathing-described)

6. **The critical practice: mental calm during physical activation.** Epinephrine from the adrenals (body) and from the locus coeruleus (brainstem/brain) are separate systems. Training yourself to stay calm in mind while physically stressed separates the two — maximizing body-level immune and performance benefits while avoiding the psychological amplification of stress. [00:39:00](transcript.md#003900-practices-to-increase-energy-without-increasing-stress)

7. **Epinephrine after learning consolidates memory — before learning can impair it.** Past a threshold, pre-learning adrenaline degrades retention by overwhelming focus on somatic symptoms. Adrenaline applied immediately after a learning session primes hippocampal encoding, signaling that the preceding information is worth storing. This is the neurobiological reason emotionally charged events make preceding neutral events memorable. [00:57:02](transcript.md#005702-adrenalinestress-increase-performance--memory-if-they-are-after-learning)

8. **Optimal learning protocol: 90-min block → stressor → NSDR → sleep.** 90-minute focused session (ultradian cycle) → cold shower, intense breathing, or HIIT immediately after → NSDR/Yoga Nidra → good night's sleep. Caffeine taken toward the tail end of the learning block (not before) further enhances plasticity without disrupting encoding. [01:02:45](transcript.md#010245-an-optimal-learning-protocol)

9. **Habitual caffeine rewires brain toward anxiety circuits.** Magalhães et al. (Molecular Psychiatry, March 2021) found habitual coffee drinkers display distinct increases in functional connectivity biased toward anxiety-related brain networks — persistent even without active caffeine in the system. Huberman now delays caffeine 90+ minutes after waking and uses it toward the end of focus sessions, not before. [01:03:20](transcript.md#010320-coffee-changes-your-brain--increases-connectivity-of-anxiety-circuits)

10. **Epinephrine and cortisol are the real nootropics — "neural energy."** Racetams (piracetam, oxiracetam, aniracetam) work by raising blood glucose; block glucose and you block the effect. Endogenous epinephrine/cortisol constitute a more powerful neural energy system largely independent of blood glucose — making fasting and deliberate stress more potent cognitive enhancers than most commercial nootropics. [01:05:43](transcript.md#010543-nootropics-two-kinds---how--why-they-work-neural-energy)

11. **Chronic stress flips cortisol feedback from negative to positive.** Normally, high cortisol shuts off CRH/ACTH (negative feedback). After four to seven or more days of sustained stress, the system inverts — high cortisol triggers more cortisol (positive feedback loop). This cascade, documented by Mary Dallman's lab, drives comfort-food cravings (high-fat/high-sugar), abdominal fat accumulation, and further anxiety through body-fat neural innervation releasing additional stress hormones. [01:09:00](transcript.md#010900-biology-of-comfort-foods-from-negative-to-positive-feedback-loops)

12. **Chronic stress depletes melanocytes and causes hair graying.** Sympathetic nervous system activation drives melanocyte stem cell depletion in hair follicles; chronic stress also produces hydrogen peroxide in follicles, bleaching hair. Zhang et al. (Nature, 2020) confirmed this mechanism. Regular sunlight (stimulates MSH) and a consistent stress regulation practice (NSDR, meditation) slow the process. [01:15:00](transcript.md#011500-how-stress-makes-our-hair-gray--how-to-prevent-stress-induced-graying)

13. **Ashwagandha blunts chronic cortisol with broad downstream benefits.** Six independent peer-reviewed studies (64+ subjects, 1–6 months, both sexes) found 14.5–27.9% reductions in cortisol in otherwise healthy but stressed adults. Downstream: reduced CRP, fewer heart palpitations, improved T3/T4, decreased OCD symptoms, lower heart rate, modestly improved memory. Take in the evening to preserve the morning cortisol peak; use situationally, not indefinitely. [01:18:05](transcript.md#011805-blunting-chronic-cortisol-including-ashwagandha--science-of)

14. **Licorice (glycyrrhizin) raises cortisol and suppresses testosterone.** The 18-beta-hydroxy-cholenoic acid compound in black licorice significantly increases serum cortisol and blood pressure while decreasing testosterone and estrogen across multiple age-group studies (18–29, 30–64). Avoid during chronic stress periods or when optimizing sex hormone levels. [01:25:50](transcript.md#012550-licorice-increases-cortisol--blood-pressure--reduces-testosterone-by-glycyrrhizin)

15. **Apigenin (chamomile) reduces cortisol and anxiety before sleep.** 50 mg apigenin before bedtime calms GABA/chloride channels, carries mild anti-estrogen effects, and gently lowers cortisol. Pairs synergistically with ashwagandha for high-stress evening wind-down. [01:28:50](transcript.md#012850-apigenin-anti-cortisol)

16. **Fasting raises epinephrine and cortisol — a double-edged tool.** Any 4–6-hour food-free window raises epinephrine and cortisol, increasing neural energy and immune priming. However, combining aggressive chronic fasting with already-elevated life stress pushes the system toward sustained cortisol elevation and its negative cascade. Calibrate fasting depth to current stress load. [01:29:53](transcript.md#012953-protocols-for-optimizing-energy--immune-system-function--learning)

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight Viewing

- **What:** Bright natural outdoor light in the eyes within 30–60 minutes of waking to time the cortisol pulse to early morning.
- **How:** Go outside; no sunglasses. Sunny day (~100,000 lux): 5–10 min. Broken/light cloud cover: 10–20 min. Dense overcast (~10,000 lux): ~30 min. Indoor artificial light (~100–200 lux) takes ~6 hours — effectively useless for entrainment. Use the free Light Meter app if curious about actual lux. Never stare directly at the sun; if you need to blink continuously, it is too bright.
- **When:** Daily, within the first hour of waking. Non-negotiable for entraining cortisol and thyroid hormone timing.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:21:32](transcript.md#002132-tool-time-your-cortisol-peak-to-waking-using-specific-light-intensities)

### Cyclic Hyperventilation (Tummo / Wim Hof Breathing)

- **What:** 25–30 deep cyclic inhale-exhale cycles followed by an exhale hold; repeated for several rounds. Generates significant adrenal epinephrine release and primes the immune system.
- **How:** Inhale deeply through nose or mouth, exhale fully; repeat 25–30 times continuously. Brief exhale hold (15–30 sec) at the end of each set; optional inhale hold to close. Always on dry land — never near water, while driving, or operating machinery. Cleared by physician if pregnant, prone to seizures, or cardiovascular concerns.
- **When:** Deliberately to spike energy, prime immune function, or immediately after a 90-minute learning block to consolidate memory. Not for daily use as a chronic stress tool if already in a chronically stressed state.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:36:15](transcript.md#003615-protocols-adrenaline-breathing-described)

### Cold Water Exposure

- **What:** Cold shower, cold bath, or ice bath to produce a controlled adrenal epinephrine spike.
- **How:** Immerse or expose body to uncomfortably cold water. The critical practice is staying mentally calm while physically stressed — this trains separation of brainstem epinephrine from adrenal epinephrine. Do not try to suppress or ignore the stress; actively attempt calm while feeling it.
- **When:** Immediately after a learning session for memory consolidation. Also 1–3x per week for immune priming and stress-threshold building. Consult a physician before extreme cold exposure.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:30:04](transcript.md#003004-ways-to-increase-adrenaline-epinephrine--cortisol--why-that-is-good)

### High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)

- **What:** Brief bouts of maximal or near-maximal effort exercise that spike heart rate and adrenal epinephrine.
- **How:** Sprint intervals, intense resistance sets, or equivalent. Effective substitute when cold exposure is inaccessible. Keep sessions short — excessive volume when chronically stressed can worsen the stress load rather than prime immunity.
- **When:** Immediately after a 90-minute learning block for memory consolidation; or 1–3x per week for immune priming and stress threshold building.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:30:04](transcript.md#003004-ways-to-increase-adrenaline-epinephrine--cortisol--why-that-is-good)

### Optimal Learning Protocol (90-min Block + Stressor + NSDR + Sleep)

- **What:** Structured sequence exploiting the post-learning epinephrine window to maximize memory consolidation.
- **How:** 90-minute focused learning or work session (ultradian cycle) → immediately followed by cold shower, intense cyclic breathing, or HIIT → Non-Sleep Deep Rest / Yoga Nidra → good night's sleep. Optionally take caffeine toward the tail end of the learning block (not before) to further enhance plasticity around the session.
- **When:** Any time high-retention learning is the goal — language, motor skills, academic content, programming, or any skill acquisition.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:02:45](transcript.md#010245-an-optimal-learning-protocol)

### Ashwagandha Supplementation (Situational)

- **What:** Adaptogenic herb with 14.5–27.9% cortisol reduction confirmed across six independent peer-reviewed studies (1–6 months, 64+ subjects, both sexes).
- **How:** Standard extracts: KSM-66 or Sensoril. Verify current dosing ranges at examine.com human effect matrix. Huberman takes it periodically during high-stress periods, not as a permanent daily supplement.
- **When:** Evening, with the last meal or before sleep — taking in the morning risks blunting the desired morning cortisol peak. Use situationally.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:18:05](transcript.md#011805-blunting-chronic-cortisol-including-ashwagandha--science-of)

### Apigenin (Evening Wind-Down)

- **What:** Flavonoid from chamomile that calms GABA/chloride channels, has mild anti-estrogen effects, and gently suppresses cortisol.
- **How:** 50 mg before bedtime. Pairs synergistically with ashwagandha for stress-recovery nights.
- **When:** Evening only.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:28:50](transcript.md#012850-apigenin-anti-cortisol)

### Circadian-Aligned Eating (Cortisol Optimization via Meal Timing)

- **What:** Aligning food intake to daylight hours to modulate the epinephrine/cortisol rhythm without pharmacology.
- **How:** Eat when the sun is up; stop eating a couple of hours before sleep. Fasting windows of 4–6 hours naturally spike cortisol and epinephrine, increasing neural energy. Avoid eating immediately upon waking if leveraging fasted cortisol state. Do not combine aggressive prolonged fasting with already-chronic life stress.
- **When:** Daily. Meal timing is one of the highest-leverage non-supplemental regulators of cortisol rhythm.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:29:53](transcript.md#012953-protocols-for-optimizing-energy--immune-system-function--learning)

### Avoiding Licorice During Chronic Stress

- **What:** Precautionary avoidance of black licorice / glycyrrhizin-containing products, which raise cortisol and blood pressure while suppressing testosterone and estrogen.
- **How:** Omit black licorice and licorice-extract products from diet during elevated-stress periods.
- **When:** Especially important when already dealing with chronically elevated cortisol or when optimizing sex hormone levels.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:25:50](transcript.md#012550-licorice-increases-cortisol--blood-pressure--reduces-testosterone-by-glycyrrhizin)
