## 00:02:16 - What Are the Current Best Practices for Post-TBIs? Thoughts on Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy?

The question of best practices for traumatic brain injuries — TBIs — especially long-term, multiple ones, often brings up football, but most TBIs are not from football. Most head injuries involve construction workers, car accidents, and bicycle accidents. There is a lab at Stanford focused on solving this very problem.

The basic foundational rules apply: if you get a head injury, do not get a second head injury, though that is often not feasible for people continuing in demanding work. It helps to distinguish modulatory tools from things that directly change the brain. Modulatory means things like great sleep, quality nutrition, good social interactions, and avoiding chronic stress. All of those modulate brain function but don't directly mediate change — they set the foundation of what's possible. People with TBI should absolutely be doing these things.

Regarding hyperbaric chambers, the data are encouraging. Hyperbaric chambers essentially hyperoxygenate the brain for brief periods of time. They are hard to find and expensive, but the data on hyperbaric chamber and TBI are very interesting — they seem to improve brain function and, above all, improve the quality and duration of sleep, which allows the brain to repair itself, since brain change largely occurs during sleep.

For those without access to a hyperbaric chamber, EPA essential fatty acids at threshold levels between one and two grams per day have strong data behind them. There are now so many data on the valuable role of these essential EPA fatty acids that doctors are starting to prescribe EPA in prescription form for people with TBI. EPA also functions as an antidepressant — equally good in clinical trials to SSRIs once you get above approximately two grams per day. Dr. Rhonda Patrick deserves particular acknowledgment as a proponent of higher-dose EPA for TBI and cognitive function across all ages, and notably she was one of the first publicly trained scientists to put this information out to the world.

## 00:08:03 - Are There Effective Ways to Decrease Dopamine When You Get Too Much of It?

Dopamine is a slippery slope. Dr. Anna Lembke is the expert in this space. When dopamine is elevated — through excitement, pharmacology, or otherwise — it tends to narrow focus and create a drive to seek more of it in that same general theme. The definition of addiction, at its simplest, is a progressive narrowing of the things that bring you pleasure, whereas a good life is a progressive expansion of the things that bring pleasure.

Dopamine is not unique to any one pursuit. It is a molecule that puts you into a forward-seeking state with a kind of obsessive character. What can be done to counter it? The best approach is to not get into high dopamine states for too long, and if you do, to switch off the system by not pursuing more — not through pharmacology, but through behavioral restraint. The so-called postpartum depression that follows big events — the Monday blues, the post-party crash — is real neurochemistry. Dopamine drops around the four-month mark in relationships, which is predictable and worth understanding.

Random intermittent reward is the single best schedule of reward — the slot machine model — and one should apply this to oneself. Reward yourself not every time, but randomly. This applies to raising children as well: neither rewarding every behavior nor only rewarding winners produces healthy dopamine systems. Many high performers crash publicly and privately because their dopamine systems are dysregulated.

For severe cases of obsession and inability to sleep, psychiatrists use very low doses of dopamine receptor blockers such as Haloperidol — a one-time intervention with a very low dose, as blocking dopamine entirely feels extremely unpleasant. This is a far-end pharmacological option, not a first resort.

## 00:13:50 - How and When to Improve Brain Plasticity if You Have 10 Minutes a Day?

Given only 10 minutes a day to improve brain plasticity, the single most powerful anchor is morning sunlight viewing. Researchers Jay and Maureen Neitz at the University of Washington have discovered that the cells in the eye — the neurons that set the circadian clock — respond best to yellow-blue contrast and orange tones, not to the white bright light of overhead midday sun. This is why low solar angle sunlight in the morning and in the evening is particularly effective: those are the moments when yellow-blue and orange contrasts are present in the sky.

The deeper implication is wild: color vision likely evolved not for perception of colored objects — fruit, skin — but for extracting time-of-day information from the environment. The ability to detect color was first and foremost for synchronizing internal state with the external world. Our understanding of why color vision exists is essentially 180 degrees backwards from what most people assume.

A related insight from Erich Jarvis at Rockefeller University: elaborate speech and language are only found in species that also engage in dance and song, and the genomics point to song coming before language. Communication of emotional states is more foundational than communication about specific linguistic patterns. The colors in the sky were what the visual system evolved to extract — not a perception of color, but information about time.

## 00:17:51 - How to Use Supplements to Optimize Health When Career Prevents Consistent Routines?

For someone in a demanding career like Army infantry that prevents consistent routines, the most powerful anchor for the brain and body in time is viewing sunlight at consistent times of day — something established by research dating back to the 1930s. The second most powerful stimulus is movement and changes in body temperature. Increases in body temperature promote alertness; decreases promote sleep. The body drops one to three degrees in core temperature to enter sleep. Cold showers wake you up because they trigger adrenaline and a compensatory internal heating response. Hot baths and saunas promote sleepiness for the same reason — the body cools afterward.

When schedules are highly variable — such as switching from daytime to nighttime operations and back — the strategy is to combine as many zeitgebers simultaneously as possible: sunlight, cold or heat exposure, exercise, food, and social engagement. Bringing all of these cues together within a one-to-two-hour window for two to three consecutive days will cause the internal clocks to lock onto that new timing. The reason morning sunlight is emphasized for consistent schedules is that it sets all downstream processes — appetite, alertness rhythms, sleep — into motion correctly. When control over schedule is impossible, stacking cues replaces the regularity.

## 00:21:09 - How Is Social Media Changing Our Brains?

Social media and screen use change the brain in at least two well-documented ways: physically and cognitively. Physically, staring at something within two feet for extended hours each day causes the eyeball to literally elongate, shifting the focal plane in front of the neural retina rather than onto it — this is myopia. Clinical trials show that children who spend two hours a day outside, receiving UVB and blue light from sunlight, can reverse or reduce the incidence of myopia.

The recommendation from the data is to get at least 10 minutes of long-distance viewing — greater than 10 feet — for every 30 minutes of closeup screen viewing. Most people do not do this. Looking at a phone while walking to a car is a missed opportunity for distance viewing.

Cognitively, the narrow visual aperture of screens is making thinking myopic in a parallel sense — narrowing the bandwidth of thought and attention. This is separate from the content question. As for the content itself, it seems to upset everyone regardless of political orientation, which creates a kind of lose-lose dynamic. The format issue — the narrow visual window — is something that science understands mechanically, and it deserves more attention than it gets.

## 00:25:10 - What New Piece of Neurological Research Most Excites You?

The most exciting neurological research touches several converging fields. At Stanford, Tony Wyss-Coray's group is working on literally extracting proteins from young blood and young cerebrospinal fluid and infusing them into older organisms, which appear to become younger as a result. The fecal transplant literature is equally striking: transferring the gut microbiome from one organism to another causes the recipient to take on the physical characteristics of the donor.

Chris Gardner and Justin Sonnenburg at Stanford have shown that fermented foods — kimchi, sauerkraut, kombucha — four servings per day seem to have the biggest positive effect on the gut microbiome, more than fiber alone, and that people who eat this way show improved immune function and subjective wellbeing. High-dose probiotics, by contrast, can actually cause brain fog and issues in severe dysbiosis cases, making the food-first approach appealing.

On the dopamine front, the fundamental science of the dopamine system continues to be underappreciated. Understanding that every behavior you do or pharmacology you use spikes dopamine, and understanding the subsequent trough, allows you to approach effort with calibrated excitement — which paradoxically makes it possible to sustain behaviors more consistently. Approaching things with slightly less excitement can unlock more reliable long-term performance.

## 00:28:35 - Do You Believe in the Wim Hof Method? Does It Work? What's Happening in the Brain?

Wim Hof and his method are grounded in a real physiological mechanism: the deliberate induction of adrenaline through controlled means. The two primary Wim Hof tools are deliberate cyclic hyperventilation — also called Tummo breathing — and deliberate cold exposure. Both spike adrenaline.

Deliberate cyclic hyperventilation means breathing very deeply in and very deeply out approximately 25 times. This produces a strong adrenaline surge. Cold water, even at 60 degrees Fahrenheit, produces a 2.5x increase in dopamine above baseline that is long-lasting — on par with some prescription medications. These are not fringe effects; they are measurable neurochemical events.

The key insight from David Spiegel: there is a profound difference between going into a physiological state and choosing to go into that state. When you self-induce adrenaline via deliberate cold or deliberate breathing, you then have an opportunity to maintain prefrontal clarity while the body is in a state of high adrenaline. That is the training — learning that the body can be in a high-arousal state while the mind remains oriented and functional. This is useful for trauma survivors, for people who have been in chronic states of anxiety, and for anyone who wants to develop the capacity to remain clear under pressure.

Wim Hof discovered this method through personal grief — following the tragic death of his wife, he fell into a canal in Amsterdam in winter, and the shock to his system produced a kind of adrenaline-induced clarity that he was able to anchor his thinking to. He then realized that breathing alone could produce the same state.

A landmark study in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences demonstrated this effect: people who performed deliberate cyclic hyperventilation before injection with E. coli had far fewer symptoms than those who only meditated. Adrenaline suppresses many innate immune responses in the short term. This is why people can work through finals, crises, or caregiving roles, and then fall ill the moment they finally rest — stress holds the immune response at bay. The implication for stress recovery is to taper out of a high-stress period with some adrenaline-spiking activity rather than going immediately to full rest, to allow the system to come down more gradually.

## 00:37:08 - Can Red Light Therapy Help Treat Exercise Intolerance and Fatigue in Mitochondrial Disease?

Red light therapy has a stronger scientific foundation than its fringe reputation suggests — phototherapy won the Nobel Prize in 1908. Red light, being long-wavelength light, can penetrate skin and change mitochondrial function. Work from Glen Jeffery's lab at University College London shows that viewing red light at approximately two feet distance for a few minutes each morning can reverse some forms of age-related vision loss and macular degeneration. The mechanism is that red light reduces reactive oxygen species in photoreceptors, improving mitochondrial efficiency.

Two important constraints: the effect appears only in people over 40, and it only works when done within the first three hours of waking. The effect is durable — one to two minutes of exposure per week can produce benefits lasting up to three weeks. The specific improvement is in visual acuity for fine detail and particular wavelengths.

Regarding infrared saunas: typical infrared saunas do not reach the temperature needed to gain the full benefits of heat exposure — 80 to 100 degrees Celsius (176 to 210 degrees Fahrenheit). Protocols placing red light directly on skin are not clearly supported. The vision protocol using red light at distance in the morning is the best-established application to date.

## 00:40:39 - Is It Possible to Overdo Ice Baths?

Yes, it is possible to overdo ice baths, and the principle is the same as with any training stimulus. Any behavior that spikes adrenaline will lead to adaptation — you become cold adapted, you tolerate higher adrenaline states, and eventually the stimulus stops producing the same response. Like lifting weights, you must leave room for improvement rather than maximizing every session.

Rich Roll is cited as an example of someone whose history of addiction followed a dopamine trajectory — from alcohol to ultra-endurance running — illustrating how the dopamine pursuit system can attach to any behavior, including healthy ones, and still lead to excess. The question to ask is always: at the expense of what? If replacing cocaine with ice baths, the ice bath is a clear win. If neglecting recovery and balance for the ice bath, it becomes a problem of its own.

Three times per week is a reasonable frequency for ice baths — not every day. For sauna, a protocol of four 30-minute sessions on a single day produces a 16-fold increase in growth hormone, whereas daily sauna use produces diminishing returns. Doing very intense stimuli less frequently makes them more powerful. The same applies to neuroplasticity training: learning French or an instrument three times per week with focused practice will produce results. Daily practice is better if possible, but very few people can sustain that, and it should not discourage a rational, sustainable schedule.

## 00:46:10 - What Are Your Favorite Brain Hacks for Doing Hard Things?

The early approach to hard things — the method used before developing a more intentional practice — was using internal battles, imagined competition, and fear of shame to liberate adrenaline. It works, but it feels like a downward spiral, and it is not sustainable.

The alternative: before doing anything challenging, visualize the people who have supported you — those you love and those who believed in you. This is not a technique that comes from a meditation app; it is a personal evolution toward doing difficult things from a place of love rather than fear of failure or shame. For the speaker, this means thinking about a beloved bulldog and a beloved graduate advisor — both ornery, both beloved, both able to produce genuine emotion. The idea is to access the neurological state of love and care rather than the state of threat and competition, and to use that as the motivational fuel for hard effort.

## 00:48:25 - What Do You Fear? How Do You Manage Fear?

The greatest fear is letting down close friends. There is an intense relationship to friendship — one that supersedes even family ties in terms of the felt obligation and love involved.

On grief: it is essentially a remapping of space and time. The brain maintains maps of where loved ones are in physical space, when they are available in time, and the abstract dimension of closeness. Grief is the process of ripping oneself off those maps when someone is no longer present. What presents as sadness is often, on closer examination, the exact same feeling of love that existed when that person was alive. Grief is love — the same neural substrate, but now directed at an absence rather than a presence.
