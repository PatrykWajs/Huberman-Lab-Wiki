## [00:00:00] Duncan French

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and of ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. And now my conversation with Dr. Duncan French.

## [00:00:20] Resistance Training & Hormones, Testosterone, Men vs Women

**Andrew Huberman:** Duncan French, great to see you again. I found dozens of papers on how weight training impacts hormones and your name's on all of them. What is it about engaging motor neurons under heavy loads that sends a signal to the endocrine system to release testosterone? I've never actually been able to find that in a textbook.

**Duncan French:** I think it's the stress response — mechanical stress and metabolic stress. The downstream regulation of testosterone release at the gonads comes from many different areas. My work primarily looked at catecholamines and sympathetic arousal: epinephrine, noradrenaline, and how their signaling cascade — using the HPA axis to release cortisol — also influenced the adrenal medulla to release androgens and signal the gonads.

**Andrew Huberman:** Presumably in women, who don't have testes, weight training also increases testosterone — is that purely through the adrenals?

**Duncan French:** Absolutely. The adrenals are the only area of testosterone release for females, and yes, it's the same downstream cascade. The extent to which it happens is significantly less in females, but there's good data showing females can increase their internal anabolic milieu using resistance training and get consequent muscle tissue growth, tendon and ligament adaptations — all the beneficial consequences driven by anabolic stimuli.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is the same true in men? Do we know whether it's the adrenals or the testes — or both — that increase testosterone during weight training?

**Duncan French:** The field is divided. Understanding the acute adrenergic response is complicated — some of the anabolic response to exercise may come partly from the adrenal glands, partly from the gonads. But over longitudinal exposure to anabolic environments, the gonads clearly dominate. The field is split on whether the acute hormonal response meaningfully drives hypertrophy versus the elevated basal levels from habitual training.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned testosterone has enhancing effects on tendon and ligament too — people always think testosterone plus muscle, but it sounds like there's more to it.

**Duncan French:** Absolutely. There are androgen receptors on neural tissue, on neural axons — it's pretty much everywhere. Testosterone influences muscle tissue, ligaments, tendons, even bone to some extent — it can remove osteopenic characteristics. It's a magic hormone with many end-point impacts in terms of adaptation.

## [00:04:32] Increase Testosterone & Resistance Intensity, Tool: 6 x 10 Protocol

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there general principles of training that favor testosterone production that someone who's not an elite athlete could use — someone trying to build or maintain muscle, lose body fat, stay strong and healthy?

**Duncan French:** Testosterone is really stimulated by an intensity factor and also a volume factor — growth hormone is a bit different, driven largely by intensity alone. In many of the exercise interventions we used to investigate testosterone, we typically used a 6x10 protocol: six sets of ten repetitions at about 80% of a one-repetition max intensity, separated by two-minute rest periods.

**Andrew Huberman:** So 80% of one rep max, six sets of ten reps, two-minute rests — and that was the whole workout?

**Duncan French:** We would do that in a back squat — a multi-joint, challenging exercise engaging multiple muscles. We played around with classic German Volume 10x10 protocols, but they were unsustainable at 80%. The key to what we did was always adjusting loads to make sure ten repetitions were completed — if the load was too high, we would unload the bar and ensure the athlete completed the full ten reps. It comes back to the point that it's an intensity and volume derivative that is most advantageous for testosterone release.

**Andrew Huberman:** That hints at the possibility that the threshold between a workout that increases testosterone and one that diminishes it is actually a pretty narrow margin.

**Duncan French:** I think it does. What we saw with the 10x10 protocol was pretty significant drop-offs in load. We're trying to stimulate with mechanical strain through intensity and metabolic strain through volume — you need the actual weight on the bar for mechanical load, and the volume to drive the metabolic stimulus: lactate, glycogenolysis for that energy system. Athletes often couldn't sustain the intensity capabilities with 10x10, so we shortened the volume to maintain the intensity.

## [00:07:53] Rest Periods & Metabolic Stimulus

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there any evidence that training slowly can offset some of the negative effects of doing a lot of volume?

**Duncan French:** The rest is often the consideration that's overlooked in general population and many sporting environments. Rest is as important a programming variable as load, intensity, and volume. If you extend the duration of rest periods, you're influencing that metabolic stimulus — you're allowing the flushing of waste products, the removal of lactate from the body — and then the metabolic environment is reduced.

**Andrew Huberman:** So if I understand correctly, you want to create a metabolic stress. I could in theory do a 45 or 60-minute session, packing in more work per unit time — I won't be able to lift as much, but that's the way to go for hormonal adaptation?

**Duncan French:** Correct. In layman's terms, if the training goal is simply muscle tissue growth, athlete A doing six sets of ten with two-minute rest will likely see the highest muscle gains compared to athlete B doing the same volume with three-minute rests — because of the metabolic stimulus driven by the shorter rest periods.

## [00:09:26] Weekly Training Sessions, Varied Intensity & Volume, Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** How many days a week can the typical person do a protocol like that and sustain progress?

**Duncan French:** That comes back to your training age and history — there's resilience and robustness with an incremental training age. For a protocol that intensive, we'd look at two times a week, because you really need to be suffering a little bit through it, both in terms of the challenge of the load and tolerating the metabolic stress. I wouldn't promote doing that modality multiple times a week unless you're from the realm of bodybuilding and that's the sole purpose of what you're trying to achieve.

For a weekend warrior who wants to stay in shape and look good, I'd say two challenging workouts per week and then flex the other sessions: one with more volume emphasis and reduced intensity in larger rep ranges (12 to 15 to 20), and another with reduced volume but increased intensity. That gives you multiple end points of success.

## [00:10:53] Short-Term Stress, Testosterone & Performance, Mindset

**Andrew Huberman:** In the short term, a big increase in stress hormone can lead to an increase in testosterone — like a parachute jump. Stress can actually promote testosterone release in the short term?

**Duncan French:** Correct. We always hear about stress suppressing testosterone, suppressing the immune system — all these terrible things. But in the short term, yes, it can increase testosterone release.

**Andrew Huberman:** Does my cognitive interpretation of the stressor make a difference? If I voluntarily jump out of a plane, does it have a different effect on my testosterone than if you shove me out against my will?

**Duncan French:** All my PhD work looked at pre-arousal — how your body prepares for a stressor and then manages it throughout. We used a resistance training protocol these athletes knew would be very challenging — they knew there would be anxiety and physical distress, so their mindset approaching it was already set. What we saw was that fifteen minutes prior to the workout, epinephrine and noradrenaline were already starting to prepare the body sympathetically for what it knew would be a very challenging session.

**Andrew Huberman:** Was that stress good for performance or harmful?

**Duncan French:** From my data, the greater the arousal, the higher the performance from a physical exertion perspective. There's definitely individual biogenetics to these hormonal releases — the individuals with the highest adrenergic response, in terms of epinephrine and norepinephrine release, also sustained force output for a longer period of the workout. Those with lower sympathetic arousal simply didn't perform as well throughout.

## [00:13:24] Deliberate Cold Exposure, Mindset & Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to ask about cold — ice baths, cold showers. In theory, that's stress also — it's epinephrine. How should one think about the use of cold for recovery?

**Duncan French:** Throwing your body into a cold tub or ice bath will certainly have a physiological stress response, and people are using that for different end goals. If you're using the stress specifically to manage the mindset — to find something that disrupts the system, that's very uncomfortable — it's the same as doing 6x10 at 80%: you're finding a stressor and learning to manage the mindset around it.

But if you're using cold specifically from a physiological perspective to promote redistribution of blood flow to muscles that have gone through a workout, you have to understand what that stress mechanism actually does. Cold is going to clamp down every part of the vascular system. The data and literature around cryotherapy and cold baths is still evolving in terms of what they do at the level of muscle tissue, and we really need to understand how blood flow would be redistributed to areas of interest.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've heard cold can actually prevent some of the beneficial effects of training — that it can get in the way of muscle growth.

## [00:15:33] Tool: Cold Periodization, Recovery & Goals

**Duncan French:** There's pretty robust data now showing that cold definitely influences performance variables like strength and power, and absolutely in terms of muscle hypertrophy. There's a big theme in athletic performance right now around periodization of cold exposure as a recovery modality. When you're in a general preparatory phase actually pursuing muscle growth, that's usually when you're most sore and fatigued — but it's probably not the most beneficial approach to use an ice bath, because you're dampening the mTOR pathway and the hypertrophic signaling pathway.

Whereas in a competition phase, where quality of skill execution has to be maintained, you want to throw the kitchen sink of recovery capabilities in — because the muscle-building activity should already be in the bank, done during the general preparatory phase. Now you're focusing on technical execution.

**Andrew Huberman:** So if I understand correctly, the inflammation response and delayed onset muscle soreness — all the uncomfortable stuff — is actually the stimulus for adaptation, and using cold in that situation might short-circuit progress. But if I'm preparing for a competition, I've already built the power and strength I'm going to have, so reducing inflammation is good because it allows me to perform more work.

**Duncan French:** Absolutely. You have to be strategic about when you use these interventions. The appropriate time to drive recovery and optimize your body is when you're preparing for competition. When you're far from a competition date and trying to tear up the body a little to let its natural healing and adaptation processes take place — you don't want to negate that. You want the body to optimize its internal recovery, because that's how muscle growth happens.

At the UFC, we're trying to educate our athletes around appropriate timing — not just for cold, but for nutrition, lifting, and all interventions. The best professionals at any level listen, educate themselves, and build structure. The true elite aren't necessarily training harder — everybody in the UFC trains hard. The elite are the ones who can do it again and again on a daily basis and sustain technical output for skill development.

## [00:20:31] Sport, Skill Training & Quality Movement, Fatigue; Mental Fatigue

**Andrew Huberman:** For somebody who wants to get better at sport, do you recommend particularly long or short training sessions? Does intensity matter or is it just reps?

**Duncan French:** It's not a volume-driven exercise — it's quality-driven. It's about rehearsal of accurate movement mechanics. As soon as that becomes impacted by fatigue or inaccurate movement, you're losing the motor learning and the accuracy of the skill — what people call muscle memory. You're grooving neural axons to create movement patterns that are situational throughout sport. As soon as fatigue is influencing that repetition, it's time to stop.

The best coaches understand that shorter, very high-quality sessions are superior. A three-hour session versus a 90-minute session — we'll take the 90-minute session any day for skill acquisition, because that's driven by quality over quantity.

**Andrew Huberman:** Training and skill learning is incredibly mentally fatiguing. What leads to mental fatigue after physical performance?

**Duncan French:** If you have an amazing coach setting up challenging training, there's a strain to it — not physical strain, but figuring things out, figuring out the skill. That can be stressful. When an athlete hits the right technique, the reward center in the brain gets that dopamine shot — and there's only so many times we can get that before it becomes dampened. There's also an energetic piece: the fueling of the brain. The carbohydrate fueling strategy for learning and for physical training is actually pretty similar — it's glucose. It's sugar at the end of the day.

## [00:22:57] High-Intensity Training & Carbohydrates; Exogenous Ketones; Ketogenic Diet

**Andrew Huberman:** Is nutrition that doesn't include a lot of glucose — not a lot of carbohydrates — a problem for high-intensity training?

**Duncan French:** It comes down to metabolic efficiency. We rarely advocate a high-performance athlete in a high-intensity intermittent sport like MMA being totally ketogenic, because at the end of the day some of those high-intensity efforts require carbohydrate fueling for the energy produced at high intensities.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about exogenous ketones for people who are ingesting carbohydrates? Are there any known benefits of ketones even if one is not in a state of ketosis?

**Duncan French:** The use of ketones I'm primarily aware of in our sport is after the event — in terms of brain health, for athletes who potentially took trauma to the brain, looking to maintain the fueling and energy supply to the brain. Beyond that it's a bit out of my remit. To come back to the original question, for general population, I think there's a place to argue that cycling ketosis can be beneficial — it's going to lead to better metabolic management and metabolic efficiency at lower intensities where we should be fueling primarily with lipids and fats. The challenge is that we work with athletes who require high-intensity bouts of effort, so fueling appropriately is very important.

**Duncan French:** We use tactics here where we essentially have athletes on what you would call a largely ketogenic diet, but we fuel carbohydrates around training sessions — very timed exposure to carbohydrates immediately pre, during, and immediately post training. The rest of their diet — breakfast, lunch, dinner — looks like a ketogenic approach. We're being very tactical in the exposure to maximize intensity for training, then returning to a metabolically efficient, heavily reduced-carbohydrate diet because we've fueled the sessions that need it.

## [00:26:10] Metabolic Efficiency, Carbohydrates & Fat Stores, Tool: Nutrition Periodization

**Andrew Huberman:** So you teach the body to use fats through longer cardio bouts and lower carbohydrate periods, then you supply carbohydrates immediately around training to teach the body to utilize carbohydrates at that time — and you use each fuel source at the appropriate time rather than deciding one is good and the others are dispensable. Do I have that right?

**Duncan French:** Yes, absolutely. At low intensities of exercise or day-to-day living, we shouldn't be tapping into carbohydrate fuel sources extensively — that's for higher intensity work or fight-or-flight stress. If athletes or any individual has a high carbohydrate diet, they become predisposed to utilizing that fuel source preferentially. At low intensity, that's problematic — when exercise demand goes to higher intensity, they've already exhausted their fuel stores. They can't draw upon fat because oxidation is too slow, so they become fatigued.

**Duncan French:** What we tried to do through diet and exercise manipulation is teach the body to preferentially use a specific fuel source: fat at lower intensities and carbohydrate at high intensities. We look specifically at the crossover point between the two — it tells a lot about how an athlete's metabolism is working. For someone wanting to experiment with nutrition periodization: during phases of high-intensity interval training and weight training, eat more carbohydrate because you're depleting more glycogen. During phases of longer runs or less intense training, switch to lower carbohydrate. Before a competition, stack carbohydrates, ketones, and fats appropriately. You're consciously understanding the exposure to physical exertion and flexing your diet accordingly.

## [00:29:23] Heat Adaptation, Sauna, Sweating

**Andrew Huberman:** One of the reasons to deliberately expose oneself to heat is for things like growth hormone release. What are you doing with fighters to get them better at dealing with heat — and what's the right way to acclimate to heat?

**Duncan French:** We normally start with about 15 minutes of exposure. If someone's really lacking acclimation to heat, you can do that in three five-minute efforts, stepping out in between — in a hot sauna, around 200 degrees Fahrenheit. We try to work up to 30, 40, even 45 minutes continuous. For our athletes, the advantage of heat acclimation is ultimately their ability to sweat and lose body fluids, which is advantageous to the weight cut process and making weight.

**Duncan French:** We found that about 14 sauna exposures really starts to drive the adaptations we're looking for. So it's not a quick fix — a heat acclimation strategy has to happen long before fight week. This process has to begin 8 to 10 weeks before the fight so that we can get the adaptation and tolerance to heat. Heat adaptation is real — you can train the body to become better at cooling itself, which is what sweating is. The body is hugely adaptable and plastic, but the skill is understanding when, why, and how to change the overload and stimulus to drive specific adaptation. That's adaptation-led programming, and it applies not just to lifting or running — it fits into nutrition, sauna, cold bath, all of it, because we're driven by scientific insights.

## [00:32:22] Training, Nutrition & Adaptations, Tool: 12 Week Program

**Andrew Huberman:** If someone wanted to experiment with heat or cold adaptation, or change up their training regimen and diet for metabolic efficiency, is 12 weeks a good period to give something a thorough go?

**Duncan French:** For 99% of things that change within the body and physiologically adapt to a training stimulus, you're going to start to see either regression or progression — beneficial or detrimental effects — within three months. Absolutely 12 weeks. But individual interpretation always has to be considered. Be a thinking athlete or a thinking trainer: consciously understand where your body is at any moment in time. Create a journal, log your training, log your feelings, mood, sleep — elite athletes do that.

**Duncan French:** It might be 12 weeks for you, but someone else might get the same responses in eight weeks. We could put 15 guys on the mat, give them the same workout, and there would be 15 different responses — because the human organism is so complex that it adapts differently. Some people will tolerate it, some will be challenged by it, some have a metabolic makeup that promotes it, some are metabolically challenged by it. What we try to do here is understand on an individual level how to optimize athletic performance.
