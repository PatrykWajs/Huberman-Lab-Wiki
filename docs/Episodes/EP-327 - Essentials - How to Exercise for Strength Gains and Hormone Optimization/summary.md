# Episode 327: Essentials: How to Exercise for Strength Gains & Hormone Optimization | Dr. Duncan French

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Duncan French, VP of Performance at the UFC Performance Institute and a leading researcher in exercise endocrinology, explains how resistance training drives testosterone release through mechanical and metabolic stress — and outlines specific protocols to maximize that response. The conversation covers cold and heat periodization, matching carbohydrate vs. fat fueling to training intensity, skill-learning quality over quantity, and why 12 weeks is the standard window to assess any physiological intervention.

## Key Takeaways

- **Testosterone is triggered by both mechanical and metabolic stress** during resistance training. In women, release is exclusively through the adrenals; in men, both adrenals and gonads contribute — though the field is divided on which dominates the acute response. [00:00:20](transcript.md#resistance-training--hormones-testosterone-men-vs-women)
- **Androgen receptors are found throughout the body** — neural tissue, tendons, ligaments, and bone — not just muscle. Testosterone drives broad-spectrum adaptation including tendon remodeling and bone density improvements. [00:00:20](transcript.md#resistance-training--hormones-testosterone-men-vs-women)
- **The optimal testosterone-release protocol is 6 sets × 10 reps at 80% 1RM with 2-minute rests** (e.g., back squat). If load is too heavy to complete 10 reps, reduce weight mid-set — the intensity × volume combination is what drives hormone release. [00:04:32](transcript.md#increase-testosterone--resistance-intensity-tool-6-x-10-protocol)
- **Shorter rest periods (2 min vs. 3 min) produce greater metabolic stimulus** — more lactate accumulation and glycogen depletion — which is the primary driver of muscle hypertrophy gains over longer-rest protocols. [00:07:53](transcript.md#rest-periods--metabolic-stimulus)
- **Most people should do 2 intense testosterone-focused sessions per week.** The remaining sessions should vary: one with higher volume and lower intensity (12–20 rep ranges), one with lower volume and higher intensity. [00:09:26](transcript.md#weekly-training-sessions-varied-intensity--volume-recovery)
- **Pre-workout sympathetic arousal (epinephrine/norepinephrine) correlates directly with force output.** Athletes who mounted the highest adrenergic response 15 minutes before a known-challenging workout sustained force for longer throughout the session — greater arousal meant better physical performance. [00:10:53](transcript.md#short-term-stress-testosterone--performance-mindset)
- **Cold exposure blunts mTOR and hypertrophic signaling** — avoid ice baths and cold plunges during muscle-building phases. Reserve aggressive cold use for competition-prep phases when strength is already banked and recovery speed matters more than adaptation stimulus. [00:15:33](transcript.md#tool-cold-periodization-recovery--goals)
- **Skill training quality beats quantity** — fatigue corrupts movement mechanics and motor learning. A 90-minute high-quality session beats a 3-hour fatigued one. Mental fatigue depletes brain glucose just as physical training depletes muscle glycogen; both require carbohydrate fueling. [00:20:31](transcript.md#sport-skill-training--quality-movement-fatigue-mental-fatigue)
- **High-intensity intermittent sports require carbohydrate fueling.** A fully ketogenic diet limits performance at peak intensities where only glycolysis can supply energy fast enough. Exogenous ketones are used primarily post-event for brain recovery after head trauma, not as a general performance enhancer. [00:22:57](transcript.md#high-intensity-training--carbohydrates-exogenous-ketones-ketogenic-diet)
- **Nutrition periodization: match fuel to intensity.** Use fat during low-intensity and rest periods; supply carbohydrates immediately pre, during, and post high-intensity sessions only. This trains the body to tap each fuel source at the appropriate intensity and prevents premature glycogen depletion during competition. [00:26:10](transcript.md#metabolic-efficiency-carbohydrates--fat-stores-tool-nutrition-periodization)
- **Heat acclimation requires ~14 sauna sessions starting 8–10 weeks before a target event.** Begin at 15 minutes (~200°F), building to 30–45 minutes continuous. Adaptations compound only with sufficient total exposure — this is not a quick fix. [00:29:23](transcript.md#heat-adaptation-sauna-sweating)
- **12 weeks is the standard window to assess any physiological intervention.** Most adaptations show measurable progression or regression within 3 months. Individual variability is high — the same protocol can produce 15 different responses in 15 athletes. Keep a training journal to interpret your personal response. [00:32:22](transcript.md#training-nutrition--adaptations-tool-12-week-program)

## Tools & Protocols

### 6 × 10 Testosterone Protocol
- **What:** Resistance training protocol validated across multiple studies to maximize testosterone and anabolic hormone release.
- **How:** 6 sets of 10 reps at 80% of 1-rep max using a multi-joint compound movement (e.g., back squat). Rest exactly 2 minutes between sets. If load is too heavy to complete all 10 reps, reduce weight mid-set — never cut reps short. Complete all 10 reps every set.
- **When:** 2× per week as primary anabolic stimulus sessions. Other training days vary intensity and rep ranges (12–20 for volume days; heavier low-rep work for intensity days).
- **Ref:** [00:04:32](transcript.md#increase-testosterone--resistance-intensity-tool-6-x-10-protocol)

### Cold Periodization
- **What:** Strategic scheduling of cold exposure (ice bath, cold plunge) based on training phase rather than using it indiscriminately year-round.
- **How:** During general preparatory / muscle-building phases — avoid cold post-training (blunts mTOR and hypertrophic signaling). During competition-prep phases — use cold aggressively to accelerate recovery and maintain technical output; strength is already built.
- **When:** Building phase = no cold post-training. Competition prep = cold is appropriate and beneficial.
- **Ref:** [00:15:33](transcript.md#tool-cold-periodization-recovery--goals)

### Nutrition Periodization
- **What:** Dynamically matching macronutrient intake to training intensity rather than maintaining a static diet year-round.
- **How:** High-intensity training days — carbohydrates immediately pre, during, and post session; all other meals that day are low-carbohydrate/fat-based. Low-intensity or rest days — fat-based fueling throughout. Pre-competition — strategically stack carbohydrates, ketones, and fats. The body learns to preferentially tap each fuel source at the appropriate intensity level.
- **When:** Ongoing — diet fluctuates weekly with training demands.
- **Ref:** [00:26:10](transcript.md#metabolic-efficiency-carbohydrates--fat-stores-tool-nutrition-periodization)

### Heat Acclimation Protocol
- **What:** Progressive sauna exposure to increase sweat rate, improve thermoregulation, and support performance or weight management (e.g., weight cuts in combat sports).
- **How:** Start at ~15 minutes in a hot sauna (~200°F). Beginners can break this into 3 × 5-minute bouts with breaks. Add time incrementally across sessions until reaching 30–45 minutes continuous. Target ~14 total sauna exposures for meaningful physiological adaptation.
- **When:** Begin 8–10 weeks before a target event or competition.
- **Ref:** [00:29:23](transcript.md#heat-adaptation-sauna-sweating)

### 12-Week Adaptation Program
- **What:** Standard evaluation window for any new training, nutrition, or recovery protocol.
- **How:** Commit to the protocol for a full 12 weeks without major changes. Keep a training journal logging loads, reps, mood, sleep, and subjective feedback throughout. Assess results at 12 weeks before modifying. Individual responses vary — some may see results in 8 weeks, others need the full 12.
- **When:** Any time starting a new training, diet, or recovery intervention.
- **Ref:** [00:32:22](transcript.md#training-nutrition--adaptations-tool-12-week-program)
