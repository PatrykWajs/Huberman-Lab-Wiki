## [00:02:17] Dietary Protein & Protein Synthesis Limits?, Tool: Post-Resistance Training & Protein Intake (30-50g)

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's start off with something that many people have heard, which is that we can only assimilate 30 grams of protein per meal. And the simple question is, what constitutes a meal? If I eat 30 grams of protein, and an hour later, I eat 30 grams of protein, did I just eat two meals? Can I assimilate 30 grams in each? And can I assimilate more than 30 grams of protein under certain circumstances? What's the deal with this protein assimilation thing?

**Alan Aragon:** Okay. Well, you elucidated one of the issues right in the question. Are we talking about isolated, quickly digesting protein, or are we talking about a slower digesting protein, or are we talking about any one of those within the context of a mixed macronutrient meal with carbohydrate, fat, fiber? All of those conditions alter the behavior of what happens physiologically. And so, the origin of the whole 25 grams of protein max is the confusion of two separate concepts. There is digestion and utilization at the kind of entire body level, where protein has various metabolic fates for various systems, and there is the specific phenomenon of the muscle anabolic response, or muscle protein synthesis. So, we have to separate digestion and absorption in general, from muscle protein synthesis.

**Alan Aragon:** So, the 25, 30 gram cut off refers specifically to muscle protein synthesis, where there seems to be a plateau at 25-ish, 30-ish grams. And we thought this all the way from the late '90s, early 2000s, all the way up until 2016 or so. In 2016, McNaughton and colleagues compared 20 grams of protein versus 40 grams of protein, but instead of doing what previous researchers did with the training routes being very low volume, like 8 to 12 sets, they hit the subjects with a 24-set regimen, full body. When they ran this experiment and they compared 20 versus 40 grams of protein, the 40 grams of protein actually had a greater muscle protein synthesis response than the 20 grams. And it took us all the way to 2016 to figure that out.

**Andrew Huberman:** Up to 100 grams, as I recall.

**Alan Aragon:** That's exactly the study I was going to bring up. That's by Jorn Trommelen and colleagues, where they compared a 25-gram dose with a 100-gram dose, post-exercise. They used milk protein, which is 80% casein, which is a slow digesting protein, 20% whey, which is fast. And there was significantly greater muscle protein synthesis with the 100-gram dose, compared to the 25-gram dose. But my big issue with that study is they really needed to include an intermediate dose, to see whether there would have been a plateau in MPS with something like 40 or 50 grams. That's because there's a lot of other research seeing that plateau somewhere between 30 and 50 grams.

**Andrew Huberman:** If you can give an across-the-board recommendation of how much protein people should consume post-resistance training, post-resistance training, what would that number be? Would it be 20, 30, 50, or 100? Should it scale with body weight?

**Alan Aragon:** To maximize muscle protein synthesis, we really haven't seen doses beyond 50-ish grams, 30 or so to 50. My colleague Brad Schoenfeld and I, we scoured the literature, and we wrote this paper on what is the maximal anabolic dose of protein per meal for the goal of muscle building. And we boiled it down to somewhere between 0.4 to roughly 0.6 grams per kilogram of body weight. And so, in freedom units, we're talking 0.2 to 0.25 grams per pound, and that is what appears to max out muscle protein synthesis. So like about a quarter of your body weight in pounds, if you're looking at grams of protein, to maximize muscle protein synthesis, per meal.

## [00:09:16] Training Fasted, Post-Exercise Anabolic Window, Tool: Total Daily Protein

**Alan Aragon:** To maximize MPS, the answer is so weird. If we go all the way back to 2003, 2004, John Ivy and Robert Portman put out this book called 'Nutrient Timing', and they focused on this narrow post-exercise window of opportunity, they called it — the anabolic window. The concept was you needed to consume protein, and quickly digesting carbs within 30 to 60 minutes post-exercise, in order to maximize the anabolic response, maximize recovery, and maximize your muscle gain. This was all based on subjects who were training after an overnight fast. And so, what happens when you consume a meal pre-exercise — the anabolic/anticatabolic effect of that meal is going to last anywhere from three to six hours, depending on the size of the meal. So when you're somebody whose goal is to gain muscle at the quickest rate possible, you're almost never going to train fasted.

**Andrew Huberman:** You're going to have a pre-exercise meal at some point, at least a couple hours pre-exercise. And so when you're training, you actually still have these substrates in circulation through the exercise bout. The anabolic window is actually not hours, but days.

**Alan Aragon:** Exactly. A single resistance training bout causes this interesting cascade of things where muscle protein synthesis will peak 24 hours after the resistance training bout. And it will take as long as 48 to 72 hours to come down to baseline levels. So, the anabolic window is actually not hours, but days. So it's more a matter of making sure you are consuming — well, the first in the order of importance is total daily protein. If you get total daily protein right, then the timing of the constituent doses of the total are just a distant secondary concern. My colleague Brad Schoenfeld and I wrote a narrative review criticizing the post-exercise anabolic window in 2013. We did a meta-analysis of the existing literature, and we found that as long as total daily protein was about 1.66, 1.7 grams per kilogram of body weight — so about 0.7 grams per pound — then the timing relative to the training bout didn't make a difference.

**Andrew Huberman:** This is important for people to hear. What you're saying, basically, is that you have nutrients circulating in your body and stored in your glycogen, and so you're pulling from a reservoir. The thing that most people don't talk about is distinguishing between what's in circulation, versus when one ingests something. Total daily protein is the cake. The specific timing of protein relative to the training bout is the icing on the cake, and it's a very thin layer of icing on the cake.

## [00:15:53] Daily Protein Intake, Timing & Exercise, Muscle Strength/Size

**Andrew Huberman:** Let me ask it a little differently. Assuming caloric load is appropriate, is there anything fundamentally wrong or bad about distributing protein heavily towards the end of the day, from the perspective of body composition and health?

**Alan Aragon:** I would say no. We did a study testing out this anabolic window thing in 2014, where we tested immediate pre-exercise, 25 grams of whey protein versus immediate post-exercise, 25 grams of whey protein. We ran the experiment for 10 weeks. And there was no significant advantage of either condition. Fast forward to 2024, one of my colleagues, Yassin Lak, took our pre-post model and he compared an immediate pre and post 25 grams of protein sandwiching the resistance training bout, with a group that neglected all nutrients for three hours on both sides of the resistance training bout. Total daily protein was optimized close to a gram per pound. No significant difference in muscle size and strength gains. The bottom line: total daily protein is the most important factor. Individual meal timing is a secondary, minimal concern for the general population.

**Andrew Huberman:** So, what I'm hearing is that there's tremendous flexibility as to when you consume the protein that we all need, but the overall protein requirement seems to center somewhere around 0.7 to 1 gram per pound of body weight, total per day. If the amount in a given meal is a bit higher than 20 or 30 grams, you're fine. If it's a bit lower, you're probably fine.

**Alan Aragon:** Think about it this way. Total daily protein is the cake. The specific timing of protein relative to the training bout is the icing on the cake, and it's a very thin layer of icing on the cake.

## [00:26:46] Does Fasted Training Increase Body Fat Loss?, Cardio, Individual Flexibility

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about fasted training. And whether or not it indeed burns more body fat. Let's expand the conversation to include cardiovascular training. Let's be broad with what we're calling training — could be resistance training, circuit training, whole body, body part split, whatever. And let's define the fast as not having eaten either for four hours during the middle of the day, or for 8 hours to 12 hours, including sleep the night before. Assuming that one trains fasted, does one burn more stored body fat?

**Alan Aragon:** Picture two people eating the exact same diet, identical diet. By the end of the day, same macronutrition, same food selection, everything is identical. One group trains in a genuinely post-absorptive fasted state, eight to ten hours, eating nothing. They will burn more fat during the training bout — yes, body fat and intramuscular fat. Their net fat oxidation will be higher than the group that has a breakfast. But once again, we're looking at two groups who are consuming the exact same diet. So the group who consumed their breakfast is going to consume one less meal in the later part of the day, so their fat oxidation is going to be higher in the later part of the day. And so, it all comes out even by the end of the day.

**Andrew Huberman:** So, the principle with fasted training is yes, it does burn more fat during training, but you're looking at a snapshot of time within the course of the day — not the full day picture.

**Alan Aragon:** Exactly. Brad Schoenfeld and I tested this hypothesis — we took college-age women, and we compared fasted cardio with fed cardio. We ran a low-moderate intensity cardio study for about four weeks. We had one group do immediate pre-cardio meal, standardized, versus the same meal consumed post-cardio. Both groups lost a significant amount of body fat. No difference in body fat reduction between groups by the end of the study, whether they did their cardio fed or fasted, because we equated the total nutrition between the groups. The practical takeaway: if you prefer to train fasted and just feel better doing your cardio in a fasted state, great — do it fasted. If you can't stand doing fasted cardio and you'd rather have a breakfast beforehand, then go ahead and do that. Just know that that's not going to necessarily hinder your fat loss efforts, as long as you're net hypocaloric by the end of the day. There's also a later meta-analysis by Hagstrom and Hackett who found no significant differences in body composition improvement in fasted versus fed training conditions, as long as total nutrition is equated.

## [00:36:53] Dietary Protein & Body Composition

**Andrew Huberman:** Why is it that protein is so critical? Why is protein treated as the cornerstone of good nutrition, especially if one is attempting to maintain or perhaps even lose a little bit of body fat, simultaneously maintaining or gaining muscle?

**Alan Aragon:** It's mainly just a couple of things to keep in mind. The big thing about protein and body comp is, number one, protein directly supports lean body mass. It directly supports all the lean tissues in the body — skeletal muscle especially. And skeletal muscle is basically our metabolic engine that we can control. It manages our body's fuel use. So it's super critical to support skeletal muscle. Protein does that directly. And protein is more satiating than carbohydrate and fat — it's the most satiating macronutrient. Third, it's got the highest cost of metabolism, or cost of processing within the body. So, it's the most energetically or calorically expensive macronutrient to process — it has a higher thermic effect. Those are basically the three main reasons why protein is so critical to things like body composition improvement, high-quality weight loss, fat loss.

## [00:38:58] Animal vs Plant Proteins (Whey, Soy, Pea, Quorn), Muscle Size & Strength

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the hierarchy of protein quality? I feel like animal proteins are superior gram for gram as a function of calories ingested. But tell me what the data say.

**Alan Aragon:** Gram for gram, as a group, animal proteins are higher quality. They're more anabolic. They have a higher proportion of essential amino acids. They have a higher amount and proportion of the most anabolic-driving amino acids — the branched-chain amino acids, leucine specifically. And in the majority of the literature, when you compare animal versus plant proteins head-to-head, you see greater muscle protein synthesis. A couple of meta-analyses comparing whey and soy didn't find a staggering difference between the two anabolically — so we can call soy actually a high-quality protein. But when you look at individual studies, whey still has an edge. Interestingly, pea protein actually outperformed whey in one 2015 study for increasing muscle thickness, though that study has not been replicated.

**Alan Aragon:** The story gets interesting. We finally have studies where we're looking at completely vegan regimens versus omnivore groups. Lorraine and colleagues ran a 12-week study where both groups optimized protein at 1.6 grams per kilogram of body weight per day — but the vegan group boosted their intake with soy protein supplementation. No significant differences between groups in muscle size and strength gains. A couple years later, Montien and colleagues did the same thing, but used mycoprotein — commercially called Quorn — a fungus-based protein — for the vegan group. By the end of the 12-week study, no significant differences in muscle size and strength gains either.

**Andrew Huberman:** So, if you construct it properly, you can follow a vegan diet, as long as you get 0.7 grams per pound of body weight in protein?

**Alan Aragon:** Apparently, at a total daily protein dose of 1.6 grams per kilogram of body weight, the totally vegan group was able to hang in there against the omnivore group for muscle size and strength gains, at least within the conditions of that study. The resistance training stimulus is apparently robust enough to make protein source effects almost secondary. As long as total daily protein is where it needs to be, the animal-free group can hang with omnivores, at least for the conditions of those studies.

## [00:54:14] Body Re-Composition, Gain Muscle While Losing Fat?, Tool: Protein Intake & Exercise

**Andrew Huberman:** Is it possible to gain muscle while at the same time losing fat?

**Alan Aragon:** Yes.

**Andrew Huberman:** Does it require a caloric deficit?

**Alan Aragon:** This is the weird part. No. My friend and colleague Chris Barakat collected all the studies that witnessed this recomposition phenomenon — a simultaneous gain in lean mass and loss in fat mass. He collected 10 studies, and this review was put out five years ago. We can say at least a dozen studies have now shown this phenomenon, which we didn't necessarily think was possible 10 years ago. We thought you need a caloric surplus to gain muscle, and a caloric deficit to lose fat. But about 7 out of 10 studies showed lean mass gain dominant recomposition — more lean mass was gained than fat was lost.

**Andrew Huberman:** If you were going to suggest the best way to approach this for somebody who's willing to do resistance training three times per week, some cardio three times per week, and is willing to eat maintenance calories or slightly over — where would you set the calories?

**Alan Aragon:** I would say to keep the caloric surplus pretty judicious — about 10% above maintenance conditions, which could be somewhere between 200 to 300 calories above what you see as maintenance. The common thread amongst these recomposition studies was that protein was very high — somewhere between a gram to a gram and a half per pound of body weight. And there's a series of studies done by Joey Antonio and colleagues, where they fed the subjects four to 800 calories above and beyond their habitual intakes, just in protein — while resistance training — and either recomposition happened, or no significant change in body composition happened. Nobody gained fat and some people lost fat. When you consume very high amounts of protein, up to a gram to a gram and a half per pound of body weight, it drives down your intake of the other macros, it potentially increases your exercise and non-exercise energy expenditure, and it has a higher thermic effect.

## [01:02:55] Fiber; Starchy Carbohydrates & Fat Loss, Ketogenic Diet

**Andrew Huberman:** Fiber is good, right?

**Alan Aragon:** The short answer is it's good. Get it through fruits and vegetables. And if you're not doing that, get it through some supplement, but ideally through fruits, vegetables, legumes, and whole grains. Are starches inherently bad with respect to maintaining or losing body fat, if one is doing everything else equally?

**Alan Aragon:** That's a great question. I defer to the evidence. The broad literature comparing low-carb to moderate-carb to high-carb diets — when calories and protein are equated — shows no meaningful advantage to any particular carbohydrate level for fat loss. Starchy carbohydrates serve as a great fuel — they put glycogen in our liver and muscles, they fuel resistance training, and the brain fundamentally prefers glucose. Ketogenic diets do show advantages for some people, particularly for satiety and appetite management, but when studies properly equate protein and calories, the fat loss outcomes are broadly similar. The best diet is the one you can maintain long-term with appropriate protein intake.

## [01:10:36] Inflammation, Fat & Macronutrients, Hyper-Palatability; Fish Oil Supplementation

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about inflammation and its relationship to dietary fat and macronutrients. And also hyper-palatability — this issue of foods being engineered to be so delicious that we can't stop eating them.

**Alan Aragon:** The inflammation angle with respect to macronutrient intake is a very interesting angle. Hyper-palatable foods — foods engineered to hit the perfect combination of fat, sugar, and salt — are a real concern because they hijack normal appetite signaling. The data on ultra-processed foods consistently shows poorer health outcomes, not necessarily from any single macronutrient, but from the overall food environment they create. Fish oil supplementation is one of the more evidence-supported supplements for reducing systemic inflammation — particularly EPA and DHA from marine sources. The data on omega-3s for cardiovascular health, joint health, and inflammatory markers is fairly robust.

## [01:16:52] Added Dietary Sugars, Sugar Cravings, Tool: Protein Intake

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the deal with added sugars? Are they inherently problematic, or does it come back to total caloric load?

**Alan Aragon:** It primarily comes back to total caloric load and the crowding out of more nutritious foods. When you're eating adequate protein — and this is a key tool — high protein intake tends to naturally reduce sugar cravings and overall appetite because of its satiety effects. The evidence doesn't really support the idea that sugar is uniquely addictive or uniquely fattening beyond its caloric contribution. The issue is that added sugars are very easy to over-consume because they tend to be paired with fat in hyper-palatable combinations, and they add calories without contributing meaningfully to satiety. If your protein is high, your fiber is adequate, and your calories are in check, moderate amounts of added sugar are not going to derail your body composition goals.

## [01:24:03] Artificial Sweeteners (Aspartame, Sucralose, Saccharine, Stevia), Diet Soda, Weight Loss

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the evidence on artificial sweeteners — aspartame, sucralose, saccharin, stevia? Are they helpful or harmful for weight management?

**Alan Aragon:** The controlled intervention trials show positive effects of artificially sweetened or low-calorie sweetened beverages on weight loss and all of the metabolic consequences of what happens with weight loss, and they're all positive. There's some observational literature that casts some doubt on whether diet sodas are a good thing or not, but then we have the issue of reverse causality — where people who are in poor health are sort of seeking out these diet sodas rather than the diet sodas causing the poor health. So, on balance, artificially sweetened beverages tend to be a net positive for health. Sort of the vilifying of artificially sweetened beverages is just not necessarily founded in the controlled intervention literature.

## [01:32:04] Caffeine, Exercise & Fat Loss

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there any evidence that coffee or other caffeinated drinks have a thermic effect that allows you to burn more fat?

**Alan Aragon:** Yes. And yeah, it's a pretty consistent finding. The issue is whether the amount that occurs is something meaningful, that would be durable enough over time for us to be able to say we can use coffee and/or caffeine as an agent to enhance fat loss. I'm not sure if we're there yet, but the literature on balance shows a modest effect — a modest advantage on fat loss with caffeinated beverages like tea and coffee. A huge umbrella review on coffee and health effects showed it's net positive — cardiovascular health, mortality, almost everything that most people generally care about. Coffee either has a neutral or positive effect on it. But the threshold of benefit sort of cuts off at three to four cups a day — much more than that, we're looking at a potential detriment. Don't consume caffeine too close to bedtime, because even if you can fall asleep, it's going to disrupt the architecture of your sleep.

## [01:34:53] Alcohol, Red Wine, Sleep, Lifestyle; Quitting Drinking & Stress Resilience

**Andrew Huberman:** The debate around alcohol — is it good or bad for longevity? Where do you land on this?

**Alan Aragon:** Alcohol has real costs — it disrupts sleep quality, even if you can fall asleep, it disrupts the architecture of sleep. It contributes empty calories. It impairs recovery from training. The J-curve data suggesting light-to-moderate drinking is beneficial has significant methodological problems, particularly the issue of abstainer bias where former heavy drinkers are included in the non-drinking comparison group. I personally quit drinking almost seven years ago now. It taught me how to sit in my feelings and cope with stress and come up with solutions. My training sessions improved, my recovery improved, I was consuming almost a thousand calories less per day, my body composition improved, and my mental health improved. All departments of life just kicked up after I quit drinking. That said, many people can do alcohol in moderation — I was just one who was much better off quitting.

## [01:44:43] Seed Oils vs Animal Fats, Canola Oil, Olive Oil, Oil Production, Tool: Improve Diet Quality

**Andrew Huberman:** Should we fear seed oils or not fear them? Let's compare canola oil or soybean oil versus olive oil. And I want to make sure we talk about the processing — the high pressure, high temperature refinement process that most seed oils go through.

**Alan Aragon:** The big picture is that people over-vilify seed oils. One side over-vilifies seed oils, just like the other side over-vilifies standard land animal fats like beef tallow, butter, and lard. But when you compare the evidence base of those two things, you can find more evidence of adverse health outcomes from the land animal fats compared to the seed oils. It's really weird that seed oils are being vilified right now, because that's not the scientific consensus. When people ask about canola oil specifically, one of the hardest things to do is find dirt on canola oil amidst all the positive effects. There is even a meta-analysis comparing directly the effect of canola oil versus olive oil on blood lipid profile — and surprisingly, canola oil outperforms olive oil for improving blood lipids, in the sense of lowering LDL cholesterol. That's because canola oil has an extraordinarily high proportion of omega-3 fatty acids compared to olive oil. The practical tool for improving diet quality: prioritize whole, minimally processed foods overall, and don't demonize specific oils when the overall dietary pattern is the major driver of health outcomes.

## [01:55:50] Butter & Cardiovascular Risk, Saturated Fat, Mediterranean Keto Diet, Testosterone

**Andrew Huberman:** What about butter and cardiovascular risk? And what about the relationship between dietary fat — specifically saturated fat — and testosterone?

**Alan Aragon:** The relationship between dietary fat and testosterone is real but often overstated. Very low fat diets do tend to reduce testosterone, so there is a floor below which fat intake becomes problematic for hormone production. But we're talking about very low fat — like below 15-20% of calories from fat. Most people eating a reasonable diet are well above that threshold. Saturated fat and cardiovascular risk is an area of ongoing debate, but the totality of evidence still suggests that replacing saturated fat with unsaturated fat — particularly polyunsaturated fat — tends to improve cardiovascular risk markers. The Mediterranean approach remains one of the most evidence-backed dietary patterns for cardiovascular health. A Mediterranean-style ketogenic diet can work well for some people who prefer lower carbohydrates, as long as it maintains adequate protein and emphasizes unsaturated fat sources.

## [02:00:43] Menstrual Cycle, Tool: Diet Breaks; Menopause Transition & Body Composition

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the menstrual cycle and how it affects nutrition and training needs. And the concept of diet breaks.

**Alan Aragon:** The menstrual cycle does create meaningful variation in energy expenditure and appetite — the luteal phase tends to increase both. Women often feel more fatigued and have increased food cravings in the luteal phase. Diet breaks are an important tool — rather than prolonged, uninterrupted caloric restriction, taking periods of two weeks at maintenance calories can help preserve metabolic rate, hormonal health, and psychological wellbeing. This is particularly important for women. During the menopause transition, body composition tends to shift — there's often fat gain, particularly central fat gain, and some loss of lean mass. This makes resistance training and adequate protein even more critical during this period. The same principles apply — high protein intake, resistance training — but the dial may need to be turned up somewhat, particularly for protein.

## [02:07:04] Collagen Supplementation, Skin Appearance

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the evidence on collagen supplementation for skin appearance and joint health?

**Alan Aragon:** The collagen supplementation literature for skin is actually pretty promising — there are a number of reasonably well-designed randomized controlled trials showing improvements in skin hydration, elasticity, and appearance with hydrolyzed collagen supplementation. The proposed mechanism involves the collagen peptides being absorbed and signaling skin cells to increase their own collagen production. For joints, the evidence is more mixed but there are some positive signals. The main caveat is that collagen is a lower quality protein for muscle building — it's low in essential amino acids and very low in leucine. So if you're supplementing with collagen, it shouldn't come at the expense of your total quality protein intake. Think of it as an add-on for skin and joint benefits, not a replacement for your primary protein sources.

## [02:12:44] Supplements: Multivitamins, Vitamin D3, Fish Oil, Creatine, Vitamin C

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the evidence for common supplements — multivitamins, vitamin D3, fish oil, creatine, and vitamin C.

**Alan Aragon:** The supplement with the strongest evidence base for muscle performance and body composition is creatine monohydrate — it's been shown consistently to increase strength, power, and lean mass when combined with resistance training. The evidence is very robust across many studies and meta-analyses. Vitamin D3 is worth supplementing if you're deficient, which a large portion of the population is — get your levels tested. Fish oil / omega-3 supplementation has good evidence for cardiovascular health, anti-inflammatory effects, and potentially modest benefits for muscle protein synthesis. Multivitamins are useful as an insurance policy if you have gaps in your diet, but they're not a substitute for a whole food diet. Vitamin C is generally well-tolerated and supports immune function, but extremely high doses during training may actually blunt some of the adaptive responses to exercise, so reasonable doses — not megadosing — are advised.

## [02:20:03] Resistance & Cardio Training, Tool: Cluster Sets & Super Sets

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about resistance training and cardio. And specifically the tool of cluster sets and supersets.

**Alan Aragon:** For the general population seeking body composition improvement, a combination of resistance training and cardiovascular training is optimal. Resistance training is the primary driver of muscle gain, and it also contributes significantly to fat loss by increasing metabolic rate through increased muscle mass. Cardiovascular training provides cardiovascular health benefits and adds to caloric expenditure. The optimal approach is doing both. For resistance training efficiency, cluster sets involve doing your normal sets but breaking them up with brief intra-set rest periods — for example, doing four reps, resting 15-20 seconds, doing four more reps rather than eight continuous reps. This can allow you to maintain higher quality reps with heavier loads. Supersets involve pairing two exercises, often for opposing muscle groups, and doing them back-to-back with minimal rest. This increases training density — you do more work in less time. Both techniques can be valuable for improving workout efficiency while maintaining or improving stimulus for muscle growth.
