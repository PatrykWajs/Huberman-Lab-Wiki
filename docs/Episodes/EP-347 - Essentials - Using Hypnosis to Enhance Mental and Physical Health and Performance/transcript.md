## [00:00:00] David Spiegel

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. Andrew Huberman introduces the episode as a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. The segment transitions directly into the conversation with Dr. David Spiegel.

## [00:00:20] What is Hypnosis?; Clinical vs Stage Hypnosis

Andrew Huberman: Can you tell us what is hypnosis? David Spiegel describes hypnosis as a state of highly focused attention — like looking through the telephoto lens of a camera in consciousness, seeing with great detail but devoid of context. A common everyday analog is getting so absorbed in a good movie that you forget you are watching it; you are experiencing it rather than evaluating it. He confirms that being completely wrapped up in a sports game to the point where one's somatic reactions feel part of the event is a self-altering hypnotic experience.

Spiegel draws a sharp distinction between clinical and stage hypnosis. Stage hypnosis exploits cognitive flexibility to make people look foolish, and that misuse is what frightens people — the fear of losing control. In reality, self-hypnosis is a way of enhancing control over mind and body. It works precisely because it grants cognitive flexibility: the ability to shift mental sets, suspend critical judgment, and see a situation from a genuinely different point of view. That same capacity, if misused, can be dangerous, but understood correctly it is a powerful therapeutic tool.

## [00:02:33] Brain & Hypnosis, Cognitive Flexibility

Andrew Huberman asks what brain areas are active during hypnotic induction, deep hypnosis, and exit from hypnosis. Spiegel identifies three consistent neurological changes. First, activity in the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (DACC) — the conflict detector in the salience network — is turned down, making a person far less likely to be distracted and pulled out of whatever mental state they are in. Second, the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) develops higher functional connectivity with the insula, the mind-body control region also implicated in pain processing.

Spiegel illustrates the DLPFC-insula connection with a striking gastric acid study: highly hypnotizable subjects taken on an "imaginary culinary tour" increased gastric acid secretion by 87%, while subjects instructed to think of anything but food showed a 40% decrease. Even after injection of pentagastrin — a chemical that triggers acid release — the hypnosis condition produced a 19% reduction, demonstrating the brain's ability to override physiological signals most people assume are uncontrollable. Third, there is inverse functional connectivity between the DLPFC and the posterior cingulate cortex (part of the default mode network), producing a dissociative quality — acting without self-referential rumination — that adds to cognitive flexibility and explains why hypnotic subjects often show reduced self-monitoring.

## [00:06:14] ADHD, Self-Hypnosis & Focus

Andrew Huberman asks whether ADHD involves disruptions to these networks and whether hypnosis has been used to improve focus. Spiegel explains there are two angles. First, hypnosis can teach people to narrow attention and prepare the mind for focused engagement — the experience of being so absorbed in a task that an hour passes unnoticed is itself a hypnotic-like state. When that absorption does not arise naturally, self-hypnosis techniques can help induce it.

Second, for ADHD specifically, training in self-hypnosis might help, but hypnotizability would need to be assessed first. The capacity for hypnosis and the capacity for sustained focus share neural overlap, and guiding a person into that state deliberately could serve as a scaffold for the sustained attention that ADHD disrupts.

## [00:07:16] Stress Reduction, Mind-Brain Connection; Improve Sleep, Phobias

Spiegel describes hypnosis as highly effective for stress reduction because stress is partly perceptual — the sympathetic reaction (tension, sweating, elevated heart rate) feeds back into psychological distress, creating a snowball effect. Hypnosis can dissociate the somatic reaction from the psychological interpretation. He teaches clients to imagine the body floating somewhere safe and comfortable — a bath, a lake, or space — while picturing the stressor on an imaginary screen with the rule that no matter what appears on the screen, the body remains comfortable. This gives the person at least one controllable variable — their physical state — as an anchor for building broader control.

For sleep, Spiegel notes he receives emails from people who had not slept properly for fifteen years and now sleep through the night using the Reveri self-hypnosis app. For phobias — of flying, heights, or bridges — the problem is that avoidance ensures the only memories associated with the feared thing are fear-based ones. Hypnosis allows anxiety management sufficient to generate new, broader, and sometimes positive associations, gradually rewiring the network of memories attached to the feared stimulus.

## [00:10:35] Narrative & Hypnosis, Mental State Change; Reframing Trauma

Huberman asks how narrative and mental state interact in hypnosis, especially for trauma and phobia where simply discussing the fear can cause shutdown. Spiegel frames hypnosis as "systematic desensitization through mental state change." Mental state change itself has therapeutic value — evidenced by ketamine's effectiveness for depression, by the dissociative drug class broadly, and even by the simple morning reset when a problem that felt catastrophic at 11pm is manageable by 9am. The altered brain state provides a window in which the client can confront traumatic content that their ordinary waking state will not allow them to approach.

He illustrates with a case: a woman who survived an attempted rape and came to him wanting to improve her recall of the assailant's appearance. He floated her into a hypnotic state, had her picture the scene on an imaginary screen, and she recovered a detail she had not previously allowed herself to remember — that the attacker intended to kill her. Rather than worsening the trauma, the session allowed her to also recognize that her fierce resistance had likely saved her life. That restructuring of experience — seeing both the horror and her own agency — made the memory more tolerable and reduced its psychological grip. Spiegel also invokes Gordon Bower's concept of state-dependent memory: traumatic events are often encoded in dissociative states, so using hypnosis to approach the same altered state as during the trauma creates congruence that helps the brain reprocess what happened.

## [00:16:44] Naming Importance; Clinical Hypnotist, Durability of Hypnosis, Reveri App

Huberman reflects that the name "hypnosis" itself may be a barrier — if it were called something else it might be more readily embraced. Spiegel agrees: much of the public's fear stems from stage hypnosis associations rather than clinical reality. On durability, Spiegel notes that most clients he sees only need one or two sessions — not weekly appointments. After an initial session that assesses hypnotizability, walks through a self-hypnosis exercise, and evaluates response, most people can continue independently.

The Reveri app was developed to make this accessible at scale, with exercises addressing pain, stress, focus, insomnia, eating habits, and smoking cessation. Sessions range from about 15 minutes to 1-2 minute refreshers. Two-thirds of users report feeling better from even the one-minute refresher. The key clinical principle is that people will know very quickly — after a first or second attempt — whether hypnosis is likely to benefit them. Spiegel stresses the importance of working first with a licensed clinician who can rule out organic causes of symptoms before using hypnosis to manage them.

## [00:19:17] Obsessive Thoughts, OCD, Hypnosis

Huberman asks whether hypnosis can help with obsessive thoughts. Spiegel explains that people with OCD tend to fall on the low end of the hypnotizability spectrum. OCD is characterized by overcontrol of thought — excessive evaluation rather than experience — which is the opposite of the cognitive flexibility hypnosis requires. The typical OCD checking behavior (returning repeatedly to verify the stove is off) reflects an evaluative system overriding the experiential one. Some people with obsessive thoughts can gain modest benefit, but OCD as a group is not the population Spiegel would select as most likely to respond well to self-hypnotic approaches.

## [00:20:35] Hypnotizability, Spiegel Eye Roll Test, Eye-Brain Connection

Spiegel defines hypnotizability as an individual's stable capacity to experience hypnotic states. Using the Hypnotic Induction Profile, roughly one-third of adults are not hypnotizable at all, about two-thirds have some capacity, and approximately 15% are extremely hypnotizable. The score (0-10) guides treatment: low-to-moderate hypnotizables respond better when given clear explanations of the process before proceeding.

The Spiegel Eye Roll Test, developed by his father, is a rapid proxy measure. The subject tilts the head back, looks upward at the ceiling, and then closes the eyelids while keeping the eyes directed upward. If the irises roll back and the sclera (white) is visible as the lids close, hypnotizability is high to moderate. If the irises move downward so the colored part shows as the lids close, hypnotizability is lower. The test works because eye movements are deeply tied to levels of consciousness — from REM sleep to drug effects to the ancient Zen practice of directing gaze inward to a "third eye" between the brows. Closing the eyes during wakefulness while maintaining internal alertness is an unusual state that signals the brain to turn inward, away from external threat monitoring.

## [00:23:51] Trauma Recovery, Deliberate Self-Exposure to Pain or Trauma, Control

Huberman raises the principle that therapeutic benefit in trauma work comes not just from the state entered but from the fact that it was entered voluntarily. Spiegel affirms this: deliberate self-exposure is the hallmark of virtually all effective treatments. The difference between being overwhelmed by trauma and therapeutically confronting it is agency — choosing to face it, defining what happened on one's own terms, and finding a cognitive framework in which the event can be understood and integrated rather than avoided.

He frames the therapeutic goal as finding an algorithm for facing difficult things, putting them into perspective, and dealing with them such that one becomes stronger rather than more fragile. The issue is not whether one is exposed to stressors — life provides them regardless — but whether one has a method for processing them that preserves a sense of control. Hypnosis is particularly efficient at enabling that method because of the cognitive flexibility it affords.

## [00:25:57] Mind-Body Connection, Control; Reframing Pain, Tool: Opportunity for Action

Huberman asks how to reconcile the dual role of hypnosis: sometimes unifying the mind-body connection and sometimes deliberately uncoupling them. Spiegel explains that both modes serve control. The brain — including body signals — is a tool for understanding what is happening, what matters, and what needs attention. The goal is managed responsiveness, not total absorption or total suppression. Hypnosis is a limiting case for how far that regulation can be pushed.

On pain specifically: the brain is wired to treat all ongoing pain signals as if they were novel alarms requiring immediate attention. The therapeutic reframe is to categorize the pain — does it signal structural damage that requires action, or does it signal tissue healing in which the pain's presence means the body is recovering? Teaching that distinction modifies how the brain processes the sensation. For emotional pain and stress, Spiegel introduces the "opportunity for action" frame: interpret a threat or interpersonal problem not as something happening to you but as a prompt for something you can do about it. Blending receptive awareness with active response produces a deeper understanding and a sense of agency.

## [00:28:42] Children & Hypnosis; Group Hypnosis

Children can be hypnotized very effectively, though they typically need more external structure rather than self-directed protocols — the clinician effectively lends them prefrontal cortical scaffolding. A randomized trial Spiegel published in Pediatrics involved children undergoing voiding cystourethrograms (an anxiety-inducing radiological procedure). Meeting with children and mothers the week before, Spiegel encouraged the child to "play a trick on the doctors" — be there in body but somewhere else in mind, at Disneyland or visiting a friend. Procedures were 17 minutes shorter on average, and children showed measurably less anxiety and pain.

Group hypnosis is also effective. Spiegel conducted group hypnosis sessions with women who had metastatic breast cancer — groups of around ten meeting weekly. He found that the shared social experience of going into hypnosis together was, if anything, potentiating: participants reported stronger experiences than solo practice and valued the communal processing afterward.

## [00:30:29] Breathing in Hypnosis, Cyclic Sighing, Relaxation

Huberman notes that breathing is a bridge between conscious and unconscious physiological states and asks about its role in hypnotic induction. Spiegel explains that breathing patterns with longer exhales than inhales — cyclic sighing — appear to increase parasympathetic activity, likely because prolonged exhalation increases thoracic pressure and facilitates venous return to the heart, allowing heart rate to slow. He includes a slow, deliberate exhale as a standard part of his induction sequence.

He credits collaborative research with Huberman for leading him to emphasize the slow exhale more explicitly. Breathing sits at the exact edge of conscious and unconscious control — it runs automatically but can be voluntarily modified — making it an ideal demonstration to subjects that they can modulate internal physiological state deliberately. Whether approaching it through directed thought (as in hypnotic pain control) or through direct breath control, the result is the same: evidence to the nervous system that more internal regulation is possible than assumed.

## [00:32:06] Peak Performance & Hypnotic States

Athletic and artistic peak performance states share the same neural signature as hypnosis. A classical pianist who starts consciously monitoring finger movements immediately plays worse — peak performance requires exactly the dissociation from self-monitoring that characterizes hypnosis. The flow state experienced by athletes, musicians, or someone fully absorbed in creative work — moving without narrating, executing without evaluating — is a naturally occurring hypnotic-like state. Physical comfort and quietness are not prerequisites; some of the deepest hypnotic-like states occur during intense activity when full absorption in the task has been achieved.

He emphasizes looking for someone licensed and trained in a primary professional discipline — psychiatry, psychology, medicine, or dentistry — who has specific training and interest in hypnosis.
