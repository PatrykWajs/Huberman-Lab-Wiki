# Episode 347: Essentials: Using Hypnosis to Enhance Mental & Physical Health & Performance

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman speaks with Dr. David Spiegel — Associate Chair of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences and Director of the Center on Stress and Health at Stanford — about the neuroscience and clinical application of hypnosis. The conversation covers what hypnosis actually is (not stage hypnosis), the three brain-level changes it produces, which conditions it treats most effectively, how hypnotizability is measured, and how to access self-hypnosis tools. This is an Essentials repackaging of a prior full-length episode.

## Key Takeaways

- **Hypnosis is focused attention with cognitive flexibility.** It is like looking through a telephoto lens — great detail, devoid of context. Getting absorbed in a movie or a sports game to the point of somatic reaction is a mild, naturally occurring version. It is the opposite of losing control; it is a method of enhancing control over mind and body. [00:00:20](transcript.md#what-is-hypnosis-clinical-vs-stage-hypnosis)
- **Three consistent brain changes during hypnosis:** (1) reduced dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (DACC) activity — far less distraction; (2) higher DLPFC-insula functional connectivity — greater voluntary control over bodily processes including pain; (3) inverse DLPFC-posterior cingulate connectivity — the dissociative quality that enables action without self-referential rumination. [00:02:33](transcript.md#brain-hypnosis-cognitive-flexibility)
- **The DLPFC-insula link is powerful enough to override pharmacology.** Hypnotized subjects increased gastric acid secretion by 87% via imaginary food, reduced it 40% on command, and even reduced it 19% against an injected pharmacological trigger (pentagastrin). [00:02:33](transcript.md#brain-hypnosis-cognitive-flexibility)
- **Hypnotizability is a stable, measurable trait.** Scored 0-10 on the Hypnotic Induction Profile: roughly one-third of adults are not hypnotizable, about two-thirds have some capacity, and ~15% are highly hypnotizable. Low-to-moderate scorers benefit from more explicit explanation of the process before proceeding. [00:20:35](transcript.md#hypnotizability-spiegel-eye-roll-test-eye-brain-connection)
- **The Spiegel Eye Roll Test is a rapid proxy for hypnotizability.** Look up at the ceiling, then close eyelids while keeping eyes directed upward. Visible sclera (white) as lids close = moderate to high hypnotizability; visible iris (color) = lower. Eye movements are deeply tied to levels of consciousness — from REM sleep to drug effects to the ancient Zen practice of gazing inward at a "third eye." [00:20:35](transcript.md#hypnotizability-spiegel-eye-roll-test-eye-brain-connection)
- **Mental state change itself is therapeutic.** Evidenced by ketamine for depression, the dissociative drug class broadly, and the simple morning reset — a problem catastrophic at 11pm is manageable by 9am. Hypnosis leverages Gordon Bower's state-dependent memory: traumatic events are often encoded in dissociative states, so hypnosis creates congruence with that state and enables reprocessing. [00:10:35](transcript.md#narrative-hypnosis-mental-state-change-reframing-trauma)
- **Deliberate self-exposure is the hallmark of all effective trauma treatments.** The key variable is not exposure per se but voluntary, controlled engagement — choosing to face the trauma, defining what happened on one's own terms, and finding a cognitive frame in which the event is understood and integrated rather than avoided. [00:23:51](transcript.md#trauma-recovery-deliberate-self-exposure-to-pain-or-trauma-control)
- **OCD falls on the low end of hypnotizability.** OCD is characterized by overcontrol of thought — excessive evaluation rather than experience — the opposite of hypnotic cognitive flexibility. Stress, sleep, phobias, and PTSD are where hypnosis has the strongest evidence base. [00:19:17](transcript.md#obsessive-thoughts-ocd-hypnosis)
- **Peak performance flow states share the neural signature of hypnosis.** A classical pianist who begins consciously monitoring finger movements immediately plays worse; peak performance requires exactly the dissociation from self-monitoring that hypnosis produces. Physical comfort and quietness are not prerequisites — intense activity can produce the same state. [00:32:06](transcript.md#peak-performance-hypnotic-states)
- **Most people need only one or two clinical sessions.** After an initial assessment that scores hypnotizability, walks through a self-hypnosis exercise, and evaluates response, most clients continue independently. Two-thirds of Reveri users report feeling better after even the one-minute refresher. [00:16:44](transcript.md#naming-importance-clinical-hypnotist-durability-of-hypnosis-reveri-app)

## Tools & Protocols

### Hypnotic Stress Reduction Technique
- **What:** Dissociate the somatic stress reaction from psychological interpretation using a safe-body + imaginary-screen visualization.
- **How:** Imagine the body floating somewhere safe and comfortable (a bath, a lake, a hot tub, outer space). Picture the stressor on an imaginary screen with one rule: no matter what appears on the screen, the body stays comfortable. Once physical calm is anchored, use the screen to visualize one concrete action step toward resolving the stressor.
- **When:** During acute or chronic stress. Works standalone or as a foundation for clinical sessions.
- **Ref:** [00:07:16](transcript.md#stress-reduction-mind-brain-connection-improve-sleep-phobias) — described by Dr. David Spiegel; available as a guided exercise in the Reveri app.

### Self-Hypnosis for Sleep
- **What:** Hypnotic induction focused on sleep and insomnia.
- **How:** Use the Reveri app protocol (15-minute session or 1-2 minute refresher). Induce a hypnotic state and direct the mind toward sleep-conducive imagery and somatic relaxation.
- **When:** Before bed, or as a refresher during night waking. Consistent use builds long-term potentiation in the relevant networks.
- **Ref:** [00:07:16](transcript.md#stress-reduction-mind-brain-connection-improve-sleep-phobias) — Spiegel reports receiving emails from people who had not slept properly for fifteen years and now sleep through the night.

### Phobia Treatment via Hypnosis
- **What:** Generate new non-fear memories associated with the feared stimulus by using hypnotic anxiety management to permit gradual re-exposure.
- **How:** Assess hypnotizability. Induce hypnotic state. Guide the subject into controlled, anxiety-manageable contact with the feared situation (imaginal or graded real-world exposure). Avoidance ensures all memories of the feared thing are fear-based; hypnosis creates the mental safety needed to build broader, sometimes positive associations.
- **When:** Initially with a licensed clinical hypnotist; self-hypnosis can maintain gains.
- **Ref:** [00:07:16](transcript.md#stress-reduction-mind-brain-connection-improve-sleep-phobias) — Dr. David Spiegel's clinical practice.

### Pain Reframing via Hypnosis
- **What:** Modify the brain's interpretation of ongoing pain from "novel alarm" to "healing signal" or "manageable background process."
- **How:** In a hypnotic state, categorize the pain: does it signal structural damage requiring avoidance, or tissue healing in which pain's presence means the body is recovering? For chronic or post-injury pain, rehearse the interpretation that the body is healing. Apply the "opportunity for action" frame: treat the sensation as a prompt for active rehabilitation rather than passive suffering.
- **When:** Chronic pain, post-surgical recovery, procedural pain (especially pediatric). One or two sessions with a clinician, then self-hypnosis maintenance.
- **Ref:** [00:25:57](transcript.md#mind-body-connection-control-reframing-pain-tool-opportunity-for-action) — multiple clinical trials cited by Spiegel; Reveri app pain module.

### Slow Exhale Hypnotic Induction
- **What:** Use a slow, extended exhale at the start of a hypnotic induction to induce parasympathetic activation.
- **How:** Take a deep breath in; exhale slowly and deliberately (exhale duration longer than inhale). Prolonged exhalation increases thoracic pressure, facilitates venous return to the heart, and slows heart rate via parasympathetic activation — the cyclic sighing effect. Repeat as the gateway into a self-hypnosis session.
- **When:** Opening of any self-hypnosis protocol. Can also be used standalone for rapid stress regulation.
- **Ref:** [00:30:29](transcript.md#breathing-in-hypnosis-cyclic-sighing-relaxation) — Dr. Spiegel; informed partly by collaborative research with Huberman on cyclic sighing.

### Spiegel Eye Roll Test (Hypnotizability Self-Screen)
- **What:** A rapid proxy for hypnotizability, useful before deciding whether to pursue hypnosis-based treatment.
- **How:** Tilt head back. Look up at the ceiling (eyes directed upward). Close the eyelids while keeping eyes directed upward. Observe what is visible as lids close: visible sclera (white) = moderate to high hypnotizability; visible iris (colored part) = lower hypnotizability.
- **When:** Before beginning hypnosis-based treatment to calibrate expectations and tailor approach.
- **Ref:** [00:20:35](transcript.md#hypnotizability-spiegel-eye-roll-test-eye-brain-connection) — developed by Herbert Spiegel (David Spiegel's father); formalized in the Hypnotic Induction Profile.

### Finding a Clinical Hypnotist
- **What:** Locate a licensed professional with clinical hypnosis training.
- **How:** Use the referral services of: (1) Society for Clinical and Experimental Hypnosis (sceh.us); (2) American Society for Clinical Hypnosis (asch.net). Filter for someone licensed in a primary discipline (psychiatry, psychology, medicine, or dentistry) with specific hypnosis training and interest — not someone whose only credential is hypnosis.
- **When:** Before or alongside self-hypnosis app use, especially for serious conditions (pain, trauma, PTSD, phobias, OCD).
- **Ref:** [00:33:15](transcript.md#reveri-hypnosis-app-finding-clinical-hypnotist-acknowledgements) — Dr. David Spiegel's recommendation.

## Resources Mentioned

- **Reveri App** — Self-hypnosis app developed by Dr. David Spiegel. Available at reveri.com and via app stores. Covers pain, stress, focus, insomnia, eating habits, and smoking cessation. Sessions range from 1-2 minute refreshers to ~15 minutes. Two-thirds of users report feeling better after even the one-minute version.
- **Society for Clinical and Experimental Hypnosis** — Professional organization with clinician referral service. Website: sceh.us.
- **American Society for Clinical Hypnosis** — Professional organization with clinician referral service. Website: asch.net.
- **Gordon Bower's state-dependent memory research** — Cognitive psychology framework underlying hypnotic trauma processing; evidence that memories encoded in a particular mental state are most accessible when that state is re-entered.
- **Randomized trial on hypnosis + PTSD treatment (Israel)** — Shows that adding hypnosis to standard PTSD treatment improves outcomes.
- **Pediatric hypnosis RCT (Spiegel, published in Pediatrics)** — Children undergoing voiding cystourethrograms; Spiegel coached children to "play a trick on the doctors" — be there in body but mentally at Disneyland or a friend's house. Results: procedures were 17 minutes shorter on average; children showed measurably less anxiety and pain.
- **Gastric acid secretion hypnosis study** — Spiegel lab; highly hypnotizable subjects increased gastric acid secretion 87% via imaginary culinary tour, decreased it 40% on command, and reduced it 19% even against pharmacological pentagastrin trigger.
- **Group hypnosis study (metastatic breast cancer)** — Spiegel conducted weekly group hypnosis sessions (~10 women per group); shared social induction was potentiating — participants reported stronger experiences than solo practice and valued communal processing afterward.
