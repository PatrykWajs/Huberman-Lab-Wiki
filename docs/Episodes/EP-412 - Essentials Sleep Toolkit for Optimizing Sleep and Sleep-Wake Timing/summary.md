## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight Viewing Protocol
- **What:** Expose your eyes to bright sunlight early in the day to trigger cortisol release, enhance wakefulness, and set your circadian clock for optimal sleep 16 hours later.
- **How:** Step outside and look toward the sun (not directly into it, and avoid sunglasses) within 30-60 minutes of waking. On clear days, 5 minutes is sufficient; on cloudy days, 10 minutes; on densely overcast days, 20-30 minutes. Corrective lenses (glasses/contacts) are fine and even beneficial.
- **When:** Immediately upon waking, before checking your phone or consuming caffeine.
- **Ref:** [00:00:57](transcript.md#000057-slug)

### Cold Water Exposure for Morning Alertness
- **What:** Brief cold water exposure increases core body temperature through a thermostat response and releases adrenaline, enhancing wakefulness and setting circadian timing.
- **How:** Take a cold shower (1-3 minutes), use an ice bath, or immerse yourself in a dedicated cold tub. The external cold triggers your brain to heat your core.
- **When:** Early in the morning, ideally as part of your morning routine.
- **Ref:** [00:08:25](transcript.md#000825-slug)

### Caffeine Timing Strategy
- **What:** Delay caffeine consumption by 90-120 minutes after waking to achieve a longer, more stable energy arc and reduce afternoon caffeine cravings.
- **How:** Upon waking, engage in sunlight viewing, exercise, and other alerting activities first. Then consume caffeine. Limit afternoon consumption to under 100 mg after 4 p.m.
- **When:** 90-120 minutes after waking; avoid completely after 4 p.m. for optimal sleep.
- **Ref:** [00:10:44](transcript.md#001044-slug)

### Afternoon Sunlight Viewing for Evening Signaling
- **What:** Viewing sunlight in the late afternoon and early evening creates a second circadian anchor that signals your brain and body that sleep is approaching.
- **How:** Get 5-30 minutes of sunlight exposure (depending on cloud cover) in the late afternoon when the sun is at a low solar angle, about 2-4 hours before bedtime.
- **When:** Late afternoon to early evening, as the sun descends toward the horizon.
- **Ref:** [00:18:55](transcript.md#001855-slug)

### Evening Light Dimming Protocol
- **What:** Minimize artificial light exposure in evening hours to allow natural melatonin release and support sleep onset.
- **How:** Dim all indoor lights after sunset. If using screens or television, reduce brightness as much as possible while maintaining visibility. Avoid bright overhead lights entirely between 10 p.m. and 4 a.m.
- **When:** From sunset through bedtime; strictly enforce between 10 p.m. and 4 a.m.
- **Ref:** [00:20:51](transcript.md#002051-slug)

### Evening Hot Bath Protocol
- **What:** A hot bath or sauna session followed by cool showering triggers compensatory core body temperature drop, facilitating sleep onset.
- **How:** Soak in a hot bath, hot tub, or sauna for no longer than 20-30 minutes. Exit and take a coolish or warm (not hot) shower. The subsequent temperature drop of 1-3° promotes sleep.
- **When:** 1-2 hours before bedtime.
- **Ref:** [00:22:14](transcript.md#002214-slug)

### Cool Sleep Environment Setup
- **What:** Maintain a cool sleeping environment to facilitate and sustain sleep quality.
- **How:** Lower room temperature by at least 3° from baseline. Use a controllable mattress pad if available. Layer blankets for comfort, allowing you to extend hands or feet out to dissipate excess heat if needed.
- **When:** Before bedtime and throughout the night.
- **Ref:** [00:23:07](transcript.md#002307-slug)

### Consistent Sleep-Wake Timing
- **What:** Maintain a consistent wake time (±1 hour variation) to stabilize circadian rhythms and improve sleep quality.
- **How:** Wake at the same time daily, even on weekends. If you sleep in after staying out late, limit it to one hour past normal wake time; instead, take a short afternoon nap to offset the deficit.
- **When:** Every single day, including weekends.
- **Ref:** [00:29:15](transcript.md#002915-slug)

### Temperature Minimum Clock-Shifting Protocol
- **What:** Use knowledge of your temperature minimum (the lowest core body temperature in your 24-hour cycle) to phase-delay or phase-advance your sleep-wake schedule for jet lag and shift work.
- **How:** Calculate your temperature minimum as 2 hours before your typical wake time (e.g., if you wake at 7 a.m., your minimum is 5 a.m.). To delay your clock (sleep later), expose yourself to bright light, caffeine, or exercise 2-4 hours before this window. To advance your clock (sleep earlier), expose yourself after this window.
- **When:** Adjust 1-2 days before travel or schedule change.
- **Ref:** [00:30:19](transcript.md#003019-slug)

### Sleep Supplement Stack
- **What:** A combination of three supplements that can improve sleep quality when behavioral and environmental tools are insufficient.
- **How:** Take 30-60 minutes before bed: Magnesium threonate (145 mg), apigenin (50 mg), and theanine (100-400 mg). You can take all three or choose individual components based on tolerance. Start with individual supplements to assess response.
- **When:** 30-60 minutes before intended bedtime.
- **Ref:** [00:25:38](transcript.md#002538-slug)

### Red Light for Night Wakings
- **What:** Use red light instead of white/blue light during unavoidable middle-of-night wakings (feeding infants, shift work, early morning activities) to maintain circadian rhythm integrity.
- **How:** Install red light bulbs or use red light sources when you need to be awake and functional during normally sleep hours. Red light does not suppress cortisol or disrupt circadian clocks.
- **When:** During any wakings between 10 p.m. and 4 a.m. when sleep is intended.
- **Ref:** [00:33:51](transcript.md#003351-slug)

## Resources Mentioned

### Supplements / Compounds
- **Magnesium Threonate** — Recommended starting dose of 145 mg, taken 30-60 minutes before bed; approximately 5% of users experience gastrointestinal distress and should discontinue.
- **Apigenin** — Recommended dose of 50 mg, taken 30-60 minutes before bed; can be taken alone or in combination with magnesium threonate and theanine.
- **Theanine** — Recommended dose of 100-400 mg, taken 30-60 minutes before bed; some users report vivid or anxiety-inducing dreams at even low doses and should consider omitting from their stack.
- **Melatonin** — Noted as a hormone supplement at supraphysiological doses in commercial products; chronic use, especially in children, can interfere with testosterone, estrogen, and puberty systems; recommended only occasionally for jet lag adjustment.
- **Caffeine** — Adenosine antagonist that should be delayed 90-120 minutes after waking and limited to under 100 mg after 4 p.m. for optimal sleep.
