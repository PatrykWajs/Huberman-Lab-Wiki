# Episode 163: AMA #11: Improve Task Switching & Productivity and Reduce Brain Fog

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

This is a preview of Huberman Lab's 11th Ask Me Anything episode, part of the Premium subscriber channel. The public portion covers one full question: how to get better at task switching. The full Premium episode also addresses how to avoid brain fog.

Huberman covers the neuroscience behind task switching — including the role of the prefrontal cortex, push-pull neural circuit dynamics, and the coupling between visual focus and time-domain perception — and shares three practical protocols he personally uses: designated transition periods between tasks, a three-task daily list, and a two-to-three-minute daily perceptual exercise called space-time bridging.

## Key Takeaways

- **Task switching and cognitive flexibility are related but distinct.** The prefrontal cortex enables context-specific behavior, thinking, and action — it is central to both, but task switching specifically requires toggling between active neural circuit states. Task switching requires cognitive flexibility, but they are not the same thing. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **There is always a biological transition period when switching tasks.** The brain operates in push-pull: engaging new circuits requires simultaneously inhibiting or quieting old ones. This is a physical process that cannot be bypassed by willpower or intention. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **Expect five to ten minutes to fully drop into a new task.** This applies to reading, gym work, meetings, creative work — anything not hyper-familiar or hyper-motivating. Expecting instant focus is biologically unreasonable and leads people to falsely believe they have attention problems. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **Even a 15-second deliberate transition gap measurably improves task execution.** Designating a brief gap as "transition time" — not as a new task — activates top-down influences on neural circuit switching. Simply labeling the gap creates real performance gains on the subsequent task. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **Transition length should scale with prior focus depth.** Light task: one to two minutes. Deep focus task: two to ten minutes if available. Even 10 seconds is better than zero. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **Looking at your phone during a transition introduces a third task.** Photos, social media, and texts are high-stimulus, new-context injections that anchor attention and undermine engagement with the next task. The phone is task C inserted between task A and task B. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **Limit high-focus cognitive tasks to three per day.** A Berkeley professor taught Huberman this method: write down only three critical tasks per day — never more. Routine automaticity tasks (exercise, commute, standard meetings) are excluded. This reduces the total number of deep context switches required across a full day. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
- **The space-time bridging exercise trains the neural flexibility underlying task switching.** Visual focal distance and time-domain perception are tightly coupled — close focus fine-slices time (high frame rate); far focus coarse-slices it (low frame rate). Cycling deliberately through these focal distances trains the brain to shift fluidly between neural circuit states, the same type of shift required for any task switch. [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)

## Tools & Protocols

### Designated Transition Periods

- **What:** An explicit gap between any two tasks, labeled as "transition time" — not treated as a new task.
- **How:** Stop task A. Mentally designate the gap as transition. Do not look at your phone, social media, or any new information. Walking, passively looking around, or attending to breathing are acceptable. You are deliberately leaving task A behind without yet attaching to task B.
- **When:** Every time you switch tasks. Duration scales with prior focus depth — a few seconds minimum; one to two minutes after light work; two to ten minutes after deep focus work.
- **Ref:** [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)

### Three-Task Daily List

- **What:** Write down exactly three critical cognitive tasks for the day — never more.
- **How:** Each morning, identify the three things requiring genuine focused attention. Do not include automatic or routine activities (exercise, commute, standard meetings). These are excluded because they do not require deep context switching.
- **When:** Daily, before beginning work. Huberman learned this from an accomplished Berkeley professor and has used it ever since; he often writes just one or two tasks, capping at three.
- **Ref:** [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)

### Space-Time Bridging (Perceptual Exercise)

- **What:** A two-to-three-minute daily perceptual practice that trains the brain's ability to shift between neural circuit states by deliberately cycling visual attention from internal/close to far.
- **How:** Five stations, five to fifteen seconds each:
  1. Eyes closed — direct attention to bodily sensations or breathing.
  2. Eyes open — focus visual attention on the surface of one hand.
  3. Lower hand — shift gaze to a point roughly five to ten feet away.
  4. Shift gaze further — roughly fifty feet away.
  5. Look to the horizon or as far as possible.
  6. Close eyes — return attention to immediate environment and breathing.
  Distances do not need to be precise. Can be done indoors looking as far as the room allows.
- **When:** Ideally daily; effective even at one to three times per week. Huberman does it every morning, typically outdoors or from a balcony with a long sightline.
- **Ref:** [00:01:46](transcript.md#0001-46-is-there-a-way-to-get-better-at-task-switching)
