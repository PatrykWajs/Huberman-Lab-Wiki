# Episode 84: Sleep Toolkit: Tools for Optimizing Sleep & Sleep-Wake Timing

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman delivers a comprehensive, practical toolkit for optimizing sleep using behavioral, nutritional, and supplementation-based strategies. Building on the earlier "Master Your Sleep" episode and incorporating new science plus answers to thousands of listener questions, the episode is organized around seven levers — light/dark, temperature, food, exercise, caffeine, supplements, and digital tools — mapped onto three critical daily periods. The morning period (wake to ~3 hours post-waking) focuses on anchoring the circadian clock with sunlight, cold exposure, exercise, and delayed caffeine. The afternoon period covers strategic napping, NSDR, limiting late caffeine, and the protective value of late-afternoon sunlight viewing. The evening period (roughly 6 pm to sleep) emphasizes dimming artificial lights, hot-bath-driven temperature drops, a targeted supplement stack (magnesium threonate, apigenin, theanine, with optional glycine/GABA/myo-inositol), and tools for falling back asleep after nighttime waking. The episode closes with the temperature minimum concept as a master key for shifting circadian timing to beat jet lag or adjust shift-work schedules.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Seven levers govern sleep and wakefulness.** Light/dark, temperature, food, exercise, caffeine, supplements, and digital tools (NSDR, self-hypnosis) are the controllable inputs your nervous system uses to time sleep and wakefulness. ([00:08:24](transcript.md#000824-factors-to-control-circadian-rhythm--sleep))

2. **Morning sunlight is the single most powerful sleep tool.** Getting outdoor light in your eyes within the first 30–60 minutes of waking triggers a cortisol pulse, clears residual adenosine, suppresses residual melatonin, and sets a 16-hour countdown to sleep onset. Five minutes on clear days; 10–20 minutes on overcast days. No sunglasses; windows and windshields block the needed wavelengths. ([00:15:10](transcript.md#001510-morning-tool-morning-sunlight-viewing-cortisol))

3. **Artificial morning light is a backup, not a replacement.** Typical indoor lights (~200–500 lux) are insufficient to fire the cortisol/wake mechanism, yet are bright enough to disrupt sleep at night — a key asymmetry in retinal biology. Ring lights or SAD lamps at high lux are the best indoor fallback. ([00:20:44](transcript.md#002044-morning-sunlight-circadian-rhythm-artificial-lights-cloudy-days))

4. **Cold exposure and exercise in the morning rapidly elevate core temperature and alertness.** One to three minutes of cold water causes epinephrine release and compensatory core heating. Morning exercise does not shift the circadian clock, unlike afternoon or evening exercise. ([00:30:46](transcript.md#003046-morning-tools-temperature--deliberate-cold-exposure-exercise))

5. **Delay caffeine 90–120 minutes after waking; cut it off 8–10 hours before bedtime.** Delaying intake lets residual adenosine clear naturally, producing a longer, smoother energy arc and avoiding the afternoon crash. Late caffeine disrupts deep sleep architecture even if you fall asleep — documented by Matt Walker. ([00:34:58](transcript.md#003458-timing-caffeine-afternoon-crash-exercise))

6. **Food timing entrains a secondary circadian clock.** Eating early supports wakefulness via metabolism and temperature rise. Large meal volume causes drowsiness by diverting blood from the brain. Eating on the local schedule is one of the fastest jet lag interventions. ([00:40:08](transcript.md#004008-timing-eating-alertness--circadian-rhythm))

7. **Three critical daily periods each require distinct behavioral strategies.** Period 1 (wake to ~3 h): anchor wakefulness. Period 2 (midday/afternoon): limit caffeine, manage naps/NSDR, respect exercise's clock-delaying effect. Period 3 (evening to sleep): dim lights, cool the room, deploy supplements and relaxation tools. ([00:45:20](transcript.md#004520-3-daily-critical-periods))

8. **Naps are fine before ~3 pm; keep them under 90 minutes.** For people who dislike naps or wake grumpy, a 10–30 minute NSDR or Yoga Nidra session achieves similar recovery. Afternoon exercise delays the circadian clock and pushes natural sleep onset later. ([00:46:49](transcript.md#004649-afternoons-naps-deep-relaxation-nsdr-self-hypnosis-exercise--body-temperature-caffeine))

9. **Late-afternoon sunlight viewing inoculates against evening artificial light damage.** Viewing low-solar-angle sunlight around sunset signals to evening oscillators in the circadian clock and partially offsets melatonin suppression from subsequent indoor lights, confirmed in a Nature study by Samer Hattar's lab. ([00:51:59](transcript.md#005159-afternoon-tools-viewing-sunlight-in-late-afternoon-evening-light))

10. **Avoid bright overhead artificial lights between 10 pm and 4 am.** The retinal ganglion cells that entrain the circadian clock reside in the lower retina and are maximally triggered by overhead light. Use dim floor-level lights, candles, or moonlight. Blue-blocking glasses help but do not fully compensate for very bright light of any color. ([00:56:45](transcript.md#005645-eveningnight-tools-overhead-artificial-lights-light-sensitivity))

11. **Hot bath or sauna in the evening triggers compensatory core cooling that accelerates sleep onset.** 20–30 minutes in a hot bath/tub/sauna followed by getting out drops core temperature one to three degrees — the same drop required to enter deep sleep. Cool room + blankets is the complementary nightly strategy. ([01:04:40](transcript.md#010440-evening-tools-hot-bathsauna-temperature--sleeping-environment))

12. **Alcohol and THC impair sleep architecture even if they help onset.** Deep sleep stages are degraded. CBD may reduce anxiety-driven sleeplessness but evidence is not well standardized. The supplement stack is a superior alternative. ([01:09:40](transcript.md#010940-alcohol-thc--reduced-sleep-quality-cbd-anxiety--falling-asleep))

13. **Core supplement stack: magnesium threonate (145 mg), apigenin (50 mg), L-theanine (100–400 mg), 30–60 min before bed.** Synergistic effects on sleep onset and depth; each can be used individually. ~5% get GI distress from mag threonate; a subset gets vivid/disruptive dreams from theanine. ([01:11:45](transcript.md#011145-sleep-supplements-magnesium-threonate-apigenin--theanine))

14. **Melatonin supplementation is cautioned against for regular adult use.** OTC doses (1–10 mg) are pharmacologically supraphysiological; evidence for improving adult sleep is weak; chronic use — especially in children — may interfere with testosterone, estrogen, and puberty timing. ([01:23:14](transcript.md#012314-melatonin-supplementation-caution))

15. **Optional add-ons: glycine (2 g) + GABA (100 mg) every 3rd–4th night; myo-inositol (900 mg) every other night.** Glycine/GABA deepen sleep and reduce nighttime awakenings. Myo-inositol is especially useful for falling back asleep mid-night and carries a daytime anxiolytic tail. Huberman does not combine these two on the same night. ([01:24:24](transcript.md#012424-additional-sleep-supplements-gaba-glycine-myo-inositol--anxiety))

16. **Temperature minimum is the master key for jet lag and schedule shifting.** Body temperature is lowest ~2 hours before habitual wake time. Light, exercise, or caffeine in the 2–4 hours before your temperature minimum delays the clock; the same inputs immediately after it advances the clock. For shift workers: stay on the same shift for at least two weeks. Use red light when forced awake at night to preserve the cortisol rhythm. ([01:37:54](transcript.md#013754-tools-temperature-minimum--jet-lag-shift-work--red-lights))

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight Viewing
- **What:** Outdoor light exposure in the first hour of waking to trigger cortisol pulse, suppress residual melatonin, and set the circadian timer for the day.
- **How:** 5 min on clear days; 10 min with cloud cover; 20–30 min on dense overcast or rainy days. Look toward (not directly at) the sun; blink freely; never look at painfully bright light. No sunglasses. Corrective lenses and contacts are fine. Do not attempt through windows, windshields, or sunglasses. Use a lux meter app to verify brightness (typical indoor: ~200–1,000 lux; outdoor sunlight: 5,000–90,000 lux).
- **When:** Within the first 30–60 minutes of waking; ideally the first 5–15 minutes.
- **Ref:** [00:15:10](transcript.md#001510-morning-tool-morning-sunlight-viewing-cortisol)

### Artificial Morning Light (Backup)
- **What:** Ring lights, SAD lamps, or LED drawing tablets as fallback when outdoor sunlight is inaccessible (pre-sunrise waking, extreme winter darkness).
- **How:** High-lux light directed toward the eyes. Standard home ambient lighting is insufficient. Once the sun rises, still go outside.
- **When:** Only when outdoor sunlight is unavailable at wake time.
- **Ref:** [00:20:44](transcript.md#002044-morning-sunlight-circadian-rhythm-artificial-lights-cloudy-days)

### Deliberate Cold Exposure (Morning)
- **What:** Cold shower or ice bath to trigger epinephrine release, raise core body temperature, and boost dopamine for morning alertness.
- **How:** 1–3 minutes of cold water (cold shower, cold tub, or ice bath). Core body temperature rises paradoxically: the brain's medial preoptic area detects surface cold and compensates by heating the core.
- **When:** Early morning, within the first 1–3 hours of waking.
- **Ref:** [00:30:46](transcript.md#003046-morning-tools-temperature--deliberate-cold-exposure-exercise)

### Exercise Timing
- **What:** Strategic placement of physical exercise to reinforce alertness and avoid disrupting sleep timing.
- **How:** Morning exercise (0–4 hours after waking) maximizes alertness without delaying the clock. Afternoon exercise (~3–4 pm, near peak body temperature) optimizes physical performance but delays the circadian clock — expect a later natural sleep onset. Avoid vigorous exercise within 2–3 hours of target bedtime.
- **When:** Morning is best for sleep benefit; afternoon is acceptable but clock-delaying; late night is counterproductive for most.
- **Ref:** [00:30:46](transcript.md#003046-morning-tools-temperature--deliberate-cold-exposure-exercise)

### Caffeine Timing Protocol
- **What:** Delay and cutoff strategy to prevent afternoon energy crashes and protect nighttime sleep architecture.
- **How:** Wait 90–120 minutes after waking before first caffeine dose (allows residual adenosine to clear). Hard cutoff: no more than 100 mg caffeine after 4 pm; ideally cut off by 2–3 pm. Prefer non-smoked yerba mate, coffee, or espresso (smoked yerba mate carries carcinogen risk).
- **When:** Post-90-min morning window; hard cutoff in early afternoon.
- **Ref:** [00:34:58](transcript.md#003458-timing-caffeine-afternoon-crash-exercise)

### Meal Timing
- **What:** Eating earlier anchors a food-entrained circadian clock toward wakefulness; eating later shifts it toward sleep.
- **How:** Eating early triggers metabolism and temperature rise, compounding alertness signals. Avoid large meals: a full gut diverts blood from the brain and causes drowsiness regardless of meal quality. Avoid eating within 2–3 hours of bedtime. Intermittent fasting in the morning is compatible with alertness. For jet lag, eating on the local schedule is one of the fastest clock-shifting interventions.
- **When:** Earlier meals support daytime alertness; light or no food in the 2–3 hours before sleep.
- **Ref:** [00:40:08](transcript.md#004008-timing-eating-alertness--circadian-rhythm)

### NSDR (Non-Sleep Deep Rest) / Yoga Nidra
- **What:** 10–30 minute guided body-scan relaxation to restore mental and physical energy without full sleep; also used to fall back asleep after nighttime waking.
- **How:** Use the Madefor NSDR script on YouTube (free), the Reveri app, or any Yoga Nidra recording. Lie down, do not try to sleep — follow the guided scan with eyes closed. Dim your screen before starting late at night.
- **When:** Early-to-mid afternoon (before ~3 pm) for energy restoration; upon waking in the middle of the night to fall back asleep; as a substitute for extra caffeine on mornings after poor sleep.
- **Ref:** [00:46:49](transcript.md#004649-afternoons-naps-deep-relaxation-nsdr-self-hypnosis-exercise--body-temperature-caffeine)

### Afternoon Sunlight Viewing
- **What:** Outdoor light exposure in late afternoon to signal to evening oscillators in the circadian clock and partially inoculate against melatonin suppression from subsequent artificial light.
- **How:** 5–30 minutes outside as the sun descends (low solar angle / golden hour / sunset), depending on cloud cover. Blinking fine; direct staring at sun optional. The ratio of yellow, blue, and orange wavelengths at sunset is distinct from morning light and activates different clock compartments.
- **When:** Late afternoon to early evening, around or before sunset.
- **Ref:** [00:51:59](transcript.md#005159-afternoon-tools-viewing-sunlight-in-late-afternoon-evening-light)

### Evening Light Dimming
- **What:** Reducing overhead artificial light brightness in the evening to prevent melanopsin-containing retinal ganglion cells from suppressing melatonin.
- **How:** Dim all overhead lights after the sun goes down; switch to floor lamps, desk lamps, or candles (candlelight: ~3–10 lux vs. overhead/desk lamps: 100–1,000 lux). Use screen dimmer software. Blue-blocking glasses help but do not fully compensate for very bright light of any color. Avoid overhead fluorescent lights (gas stations, grocery stores) between 10 pm and 4 am.
- **When:** From sunset; especially strict between 10 pm and 4 am.
- **Ref:** [00:56:45](transcript.md#005645-eveningnight-tools-overhead-artificial-lights-light-sensitivity)

### Hot Bath / Sauna (Evening)
- **What:** Passive body heating to trigger compensatory core body cooling that accelerates sleep onset.
- **How:** 20–30 minutes in a hot bath, hot tub, or sauna; get out before overheated; follow with a cool or warm (not cold) shower. Core temperature then drops one to three degrees, passing through the thermal gate required for deep sleep entry.
- **When:** 1–2 hours before bedtime.
- **Ref:** [01:04:40](transcript.md#010440-evening-tools-hot-bathsauna-temperature--sleeping-environment)

### Cool Sleeping Environment
- **What:** Lowering bedroom ambient temperature to maintain the core body cooling required for deep sleep stages.
- **How:** Drop room temperature by at least 3°F (~1.5°C) below daytime comfort. Layer blankets for warmth — the goal is a cool environment, not feeling cold. Expose hands or feet from under blankets (glabrous skin portals on palms, soles, and upper face) to regulate heat. Avoid socks if prone to overheating — they block the foot heat-release portal. Controllable mattress covers (e.g., Eight Sleep) are effective but optional; a fan or open window also works.
- **When:** Maintained throughout the night.
- **Ref:** [01:04:40](transcript.md#010440-evening-tools-hot-bathsauna-temperature--sleeping-environment)

### Magnesium Threonate
- **What:** Brain-penetrating form of magnesium that supports GABA-mediated relaxation and improves sleep quality.
- **How:** 145 mg elemental magnesium as magnesium threonate (check elemental Mg on label, not total compound weight). Magnesium bisglycinate is a lower-cost alternative. Discontinue if GI distress occurs (~5% of users).
- **When:** 30–60 minutes before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:11:45](transcript.md#011145-sleep-supplements-magnesium-threonate-apigenin--theanine)

### Apigenin
- **What:** Flavonoid from chamomile; mild anxiolytic that promotes sleep onset.
- **How:** 50 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Note: mild estrogen-lowering activity — use with care if on hormone-sensitive medications or if low estrogen is a concern.
- **When:** 30–60 minutes before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:11:45](transcript.md#011145-sleep-supplements-magnesium-threonate-apigenin--theanine)

### L-Theanine
- **What:** Amino acid that promotes relaxation without sedation; reduces sleep-onset anxiety.
- **How:** 100–400 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Start at 100–200 mg. Reduce or discontinue if vivid or anxiety-ridden dreams occur (reported by a notable subset of users).
- **When:** 30–60 minutes before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:11:45](transcript.md#011145-sleep-supplements-magnesium-threonate-apigenin--theanine)

### Glycine + GABA
- **What:** Oral glycine and GABA to deepen sleep and reduce nighttime awakenings.
- **How:** 2 g glycine + 100 mg GABA taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Huberman takes every 3rd–4th night (not nightly) to preserve efficacy. Does not combine with myo-inositol on the same night.
- **When:** 30–60 minutes before sleep, on a rotating (not every-night) basis.
- **Ref:** [01:24:24](transcript.md#012424-additional-sleep-supplements-gaba-glycine-myo-inositol--anxiety)

### Myo-Inositol
- **What:** Supports GABA and serotonin signaling; particularly helpful for falling back asleep after mid-night waking and for daytime anxiety reduction.
- **How:** 900 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Huberman takes every other night, on nights not taking glycine/GABA. Also carries a noticeable daytime anxiolytic tail effect via serotonin interactions.
- **When:** 30–60 minutes before sleep, alternating nights (not combined with glycine/GABA nights).
- **Ref:** [01:24:24](transcript.md#012424-additional-sleep-supplements-gaba-glycine-myo-inositol--anxiety)

### Reveri App (Self-Hypnosis)
- **What:** Clinically-grounded guided self-hypnosis for falling asleep, falling back asleep after nighttime waking, or daytime NSDR. Developed by David Spiegel (associate chair of psychiatry, Stanford).
- **How:** Select a sleep or fall-back-asleep hypnosis protocol; lie down with eyes closed and follow the guided session. Available iOS only at time of episode; Android waitlist open. Pricing: $14.99/month, $99.99/year, or $249 lifetime (7-day free trial on all plans).
- **When:** Middle-of-the-night waking; also usable in the afternoon for NSDR.
- **Ref:** [01:26:48](transcript.md#012648-falling-back-asleep-reveri-app-nsdr-yoga-nidra)

### Eye Mask
- **What:** Blocks ambient light during sleep to prevent retinal activation of wakefulness signals.
- **How:** Wear a well-fitted, breathable mask. Critical: ensure the sleeping environment is cool enough — eye masks cover glabrous skin on the upper face and trap heat, causing early waking if the room is warm.
- **When:** During sleep, especially in rooms with light pollution.
- **Ref:** [01:29:35](transcript.md#012935-staying-asleep-eye-masks-ear-plugs-elevating-feet)

### Foot / Leg Elevation
- **What:** Elevating feet 3–5 degrees during sleep to improve glymphatic fluid circulation and increase sleep depth.
- **How:** Place a pillow under the ankles/feet, or prop up the foot of the bed. Exception: people with acid reflux should do the opposite — elevate the head side of the bed by 3–5 degrees.
- **When:** During sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:29:35](transcript.md#012935-staying-asleep-eye-masks-ear-plugs-elevating-feet)

### Nasal Breathing / Mouth Taping
- **What:** Training nasal breathing during sleep to reduce or eliminate sleep apnea and improve sleep quality.
- **How:** Apply a small piece of medical tape (e.g., 3M Micropore) vertically over the lips before sleep. During waking hours: perform low-intensity cardiovascular exercise exclusively through nose breathing — sinuses are plastic and dilate over weeks/months, making nasal breathing progressively easier. Alternatively, hold water in the mouth during exercise without swallowing, or use a mouthpiece.
- **When:** During sleep (tape); during daytime low-intensity cardio (training protocol).
- **Ref:** [01:31:38](transcript.md#013138-tool-sleep-apnea--nasal-breathing)

### Sleep Schedule Consistency
- **What:** Maintaining consistent sleep and wake times (within ±1 hour, including weekends) to stabilize circadian rhythm and prevent social jet lag.
- **How:** Fix a wake time and hold it. After late nights, allow at most 1 extra hour of sleep, not sleeping until noon. Use NSDR rather than extra caffeine to compensate for tiredness on disrupted mornings. Caffeine especially disrupts compensatory sleep when taken immediately upon waking after a poor night.
- **When:** Daily; especially critical on weekends and after late nights.
- **Ref:** [01:35:00](transcript.md#013500-sleep-schedule-consistency-weekends-compensatory-sleep--caffeine)

### Temperature Minimum Protocol (Jet Lag / Schedule Shifting)
- **What:** Using your body's temperature minimum (~2 hours before habitual wake time) as a precision lever to advance or delay the circadian clock for jet lag or shift work.
- **How:** Find your temperature minimum = habitual wake time minus 2 hours. To advance clock (earlier sleep/wake): get bright light, exercise, or caffeine immediately after your temp minimum. To delay clock (later sleep/wake): expose yourself to those same stimuli in the 2–4 hours before your temp minimum. Pre-travel: phase advance 1–2 days before departure by forcing early morning activity. For shift workers: stay on the same shift for at least 2 weeks at a time.
- **When:** When crossing time zones, adjusting to shift work, or voluntarily shifting sleep schedule.
- **Ref:** [01:37:54](transcript.md#013754-tools-temperature-minimum--jet-lag-shift-work--red-lights)

### Red Light for Nighttime Waking
- **What:** Using red-wavelength-only light when forced to be awake in the middle of the night to minimize circadian clock disruption and preserve the cortisol rhythm.
- **How:** Use red LED bulbs or strips (e.g., Joovv, KOZE) as the sole light source; keep brightness as low as practical. Not a solution for full-shift workers who need sustained nighttime wakefulness.
- **When:** Necessary nighttime waking (infant care, on-call, exam prep, airport departure); not for full overnight shift work.
- **Ref:** [01:37:54](transcript.md#013754-tools-temperature-minimum--jet-lag-shift-work--red-lights)
