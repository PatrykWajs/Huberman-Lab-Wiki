# Episode 25: How Smell, Taste & Pheromone-Like Chemicals Control You

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman delivers a comprehensive tour of chemical sensing — smell, taste, and pheromone-like signaling — and shows how these ancient systems continue to govern behavior, hormone levels, cognition, and longevity. The episode opens with a landmark *Science* paper demonstrating that human tears contain chemosignals that measurably lower testosterone and sexual arousal in men, establishing that chemical-chemical communication between people is real even if the word "pheromone" remains scientifically contested. Huberman then unpacks the neurobiology of the olfactory system in detail: how sniffing itself (independent of any odor) boosts alertness via the locus coeruleus and norepinephrine, why the olfactory bulb is one of the only brain regions undergoing adult neurogenesis driven by dopamine, and how smell loss predicts neurodegeneration and can track concussion recovery. The second half covers the five established tastes plus an emerging sixth (fat), explains why taste receptors appear in the gut, testes, and ovaries, and dissects how ultra-processed foods exploit these circuits to override satiety. Four practical protocols — from deliberate sniff training to miracle fruit taste inversion — make the episode unusually actionable.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Smell bypasses the thalamus — it is the most direct sense.** Olfactory neurons project straight into the olfactory cortex and amygdala, which is why smells trigger memories and fear responses faster and more viscerally than any other sense. [00:21:16](transcript.md#002116-smell-sniffing-a-piece-of-your-brain-in-your-nose-3-responses-to-smells)

2. **A literal piece of your brain sits inside your nose.** The olfactory epithelium contains neurons whose dendrites extend through the cribriform plate into nasal mucus — the only place where neurons are directly exposed to the external environment. [00:21:16](transcript.md#002116-smell-sniffing-a-piece-of-your-brain-in-your-nose-3-responses-to-smells)

3. **Human tears contain chemosignals that lower testosterone and reduce sexual arousal.** A Weizmann Institute study published in *Science* showed that men who sniffed women's sadness-evoked tears had significant reductions in testosterone and activity in brain areas linked to sexual desire — a concrete pheromone-like effect requiring no conscious awareness. [00:17:28](transcript.md#001728-the-chemicals-from-other-peoples-tears-lower-testosterone--libido)

4. **The act of sniffing itself (even of nothing) wakes up the brain.** Research from Noam Sobel's lab showed nasal inhalation phase-locks non-olfactory cognition: alertness rises on the inhale and dips on the exhale. Nasal breathing during learning produces measurably better memory consolidation than mouth breathing. [00:28:56](transcript.md#002856-sniffing-creates-alertness--if-done-properly-can-help-you-focus--learn-better)

5. **There are three distinct olfactory pathways: innate threat, innate appetite, and learned memory.** Smoke hits a hardwired amygdala-threat pathway; a delicious aroma hits an innate appetitive pathway; and grandmother's kitchen smell hits a learned cortical pathway. A fourth, still-contested accessory olfactory pathway (Jacobson's organ / vomeronasal organ) may underlie pheromone-like effects. [00:21:16](transcript.md#002116-smell-sniffing-a-piece-of-your-brain-in-your-nose-3-responses-to-smells)

6. **Smell loss predicts neurodegeneration and tracks concussion recovery.** Olfactory ability at age 57 is a stronger predictor of survival over the next decade than many clinical markers. Loss of smell is an early biomarker of Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and TBI — and recovery of smell after head injury reflects olfactory neurogenesis and can gauge healing progress. [00:43:45](transcript.md#004345-smell-as-a-readout-of-brain-health--longevity-regaining-lost-sense-of-smell)

7. **The olfactory bulb is one of the only adult brain regions with ongoing neurogenesis, driven by dopamine.** Olfactory sensory neurons turn over every three to four weeks; new neurons born in the subventricular zone migrate via the rostral migratory stream into the olfactory bulb. High-dopamine states — new relationships, novelty, medications like Wellbutrin — noticeably sharpen smell sensitivity within days. [00:48:30](transcript.md#004830-dopamine-sense-of-smell-new-neurons--new-relationships)

8. **Your olfactory gene profile determines which smells you can even detect.** Each olfactory sensory neuron expresses only one odorant receptor gene. People without a given gene simply cannot smell that compound — explaining why microwave popcorn smells pleasant to some and putrid to others, why cilantro tastes like soap to some, and why asparagus urine odor is detectable only by certain individuals. [00:59:48](transcript.md#005948-protocol-2-pleasant-or-putrid-the-microwave-popcorn-test-cilantro-asparagus-musk)

9. **The tongue map is a myth — all five (possibly six) taste types are intermixed everywhere.** Sweet (energy/sugar), salty (electrolytes), bitter (poison detection → gag reflex), umami (amino acids), and sour (fermentation/spoilage) receptors are distributed uniformly across the tongue. Emerging evidence supports a sixth receptor specifically for dietary fat. [01:04:32](transcript.md#010432-taste-sweet-salty-bitter-umami-sour-your-tongue-gustatory-nerve-nst-cortex)

10. **Taste receptors exist outside the mouth — including on the testes and ovaries.** Sweet (T1R1/T1R2) and umami receptors are expressed in gut mucosa, respiratory tract, and gonads, where they regulate hormone secretion and physiological responses. This may partly explain the visceral, whole-body pleasure some foods produce. [01:27:15](transcript.md#012715-food-that-makes-you-feel-good-or-bad-taste-receptors-on-our-testes-or-ovaries)

11. **Diet shapes taste receptor sensitivity over time — creating feedback loops for cravings.** Carnivores develop heightened umami sensitivity (up to 5,000× more than humans in tigers); herbivores develop heightened sweet sensitivity. Eating more meat over time increases desire for umami-rich foods; eating more plant-based foods increases desire for sweet foods. [01:25:05](transcript.md#012505-eating-more-plants-versus-eating-more-meat-cravings--desire)

12. **Processed foods exploit gut-brain dopamine loops to override satiety.** Ultra-processed foods activate neurons in the gut that release dopamine entirely independently of taste, driving continued eating even after conscious satisfaction. The engineered combination of fat, sugar, salt, and texture is specifically designed to suppress natural feedback loops. [01:39:00](transcript.md#013900-how-processed-food-make-you-crave-more-processed-foods)

13. **The Coolidge Effect occurs in both sexes and is mediated by odor alone.** Male rodents recover mating vigor — and female rodents recover receptivity — upon exposure to a novel partner's scent, even without any visual or physical contact. This shows airborne chemical cues can restore sexual motivation that has been fully exhausted. [01:43:03](transcript.md#014303-pheromones-desire-to-continue-mating-coolidge-effect-occurs-in-males--females)

14. **Humans unconsciously transfer strangers' skin chemicals onto their own mucous membranes.** A Weizmann Institute study found that after a handshake people subconsciously touch their own eyes within seconds — placing the contact's skin chemicals on a mucosal surface for chemical evaluation. This "bunting" behavior mirrors scent-marking in animals. [01:53:00](transcript.md#015300-we-rub-the-chemicals-of-others-on-our-eyes-and-skin-bunting-behavior)

## Tools & Protocols

### Protocol 1: Dry Sniffing to Prime Smell & Taste

- **What:** Perform 10–15 deliberate sniffs of nothing (no scent) to wake up the olfactory system before smelling or tasting a target.
- **How:** Sit quietly, close your eyes, and take brisk intentional nasal inhalations — not through the mouth — 10 to 15 times in succession. Then approach the target scent or food immediately. The richness and resolution of the subsequent perception will be significantly increased.
- **When:** Before meals, wine or spirits tasting, perfume testing, or any time heightened olfactory acuity is desired. Also effective as a general alertness primer before focused learning sessions.
- **Ref:** [00:34:00](transcript.md#003400-protocol-1-sniffing-nothing-10-15x-enhances-your-ability-to-smell--taste)

### Protocol 2: Know Your Smell Phenotype (Microwave Popcorn Test)

- **What:** Map your personal olfactory receptor gene profile to understand which odors you can detect and how you respond to them.
- **How:** Test your hedonic response to four categories: (1) microwave popcorn (2-acetyl-1-pyrroline), (2) cilantro, (3) asparagus metabolites in urine, (4) musky/skunky scents. Note which are pleasant, neutral, putrid, or undetectable. Patterns across these four reveal which odorant receptor genes you express and set realistic expectations for olfactory training.
- **When:** One-time self-assessment; revisit if smell acuity changes significantly due to illness, medication, or aging.
- **Ref:** [00:59:48](transcript.md#005948-protocol-2-pleasant-or-putrid-the-microwave-popcorn-test-cilantro-asparagus-musk)

### Protocol 3: Top-Down Super-Taster Training

- **What:** Expand taste discrimination by directing deliberate top-down attention to specific taste qualities during eating — rewiring cortical taste maps over weeks.
- **How:** While eating, consciously identify which of the five (or six) tastes is most prominent, then attempt to isolate secondary or background tastes. Spend 5–10 minutes per meal in this deliberate sensory analysis. The insular cortex taste maps expand with practice, increasing sensitivity across all taste categories. Varied foods, different cuisine styles, and drinks like wines or spirits accelerate the process.
- **When:** Daily, during any meal. Consistency across weeks produces lasting increases in taste discrimination.
- **Ref:** [01:19:30](transcript.md#011930-protocol-3-learn-to-be-a-super-taster-by-top-down-behavioral-plasticity)

### Protocol 4: Miracle Fruit — Taste Inversion Experiment

- **What:** Use the West African berry *Synsepalum dulcificum* (miracle fruit) to temporarily swap sour perception to sweet, revealing how profoundly taste experience is determined at the receptor level.
- **How:** Dissolve a miracle fruit tablet on the tongue for 60–90 seconds before eating sour foods (lemon wedges, vinegar, fermented items). The miraculin protein binds sour receptors and activates sweet receptors at low pH, making intensely sour foods taste like ripe peach or candy. Effect lasts 30–60 minutes. Can also be used with neutral foods (e.g., pizza) to reveal hidden sweet contributions.
- **When:** Occasional experiment; illustrates taste receptor plasticity and pharmacology. Tablets available online. Review package warnings before use.
- **Ref:** [01:39:44](transcript.md#013944-protocol-4-invert-your-sense-of-sweet--sour-miracle-fruit-swapping-bitter--sweet)

### Smell Training to Recover Lost Olfaction

- **What:** Structured daily exposure to distinct, vivid odors to drive olfactory neurogenesis and reconnect sheared or degenerated neural circuits.
- **How:** Choose 4–6 strongly distinct odors (e.g., rose, lemon, clove, eucalyptus). Sniff each deliberately for ~20 seconds twice per day, actively trying to recall and name the scent. Consistency over weeks to months is required — new neurons born in the subventricular zone take time to migrate, integrate, and form stable synapses in the olfactory bulb.
- **When:** After illness (COVID-19, upper respiratory infection), head injury, or any event causing smell loss. Also valuable for healthy individuals seeking to sharpen olfaction with age. Pair with regular exercise to further boost neurogenesis.
- **Ref:** [00:43:45](transcript.md#004345-smell-as-a-readout-of-brain-health--longevity-regaining-lost-sense-of-smell)

### Smelling Salts / Ammonia for Acute Alertness

- **What:** Brief exposure to ammonia-based smelling salts to trigger rapid, broad CNS arousal via the trigeminal nerve and systemic adrenaline release.
- **How:** A single brief inhalation activates the trigeminal nerve (not the standard olfactory pathway), causes systemic adrenaline release, and produces immediate intense alertness. Keep the inhalant 6–12 inches from the nose; do not inhale directly from the source as it can damage the olfactory epithelium and eyes.
- **When:** Acute, high-demand performance situations only (e.g., powerlifting, combat sports). Not for daily use due to mucosal irritation risk. See: Acute Effects of Ammonia Inhalants on Strength and Power Performance in Trained Men, *J Strength Cond Res* 2018 (PubMed: [28922211](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28922211/)).
- **Ref:** [00:35:50](transcript.md#003550-smelling-salts-ammonia--adrenaline)

### Peppermint / Mint Scent for Sustained Alertness

- **What:** Smell peppermint or other mint scents to produce sustained, moderate alertness without the adrenal intensity of smelling salts.
- **How:** Inhale peppermint essential oil or fresh mint; menthol activates TRPM8 cold-sensing channels and cholinergic arousal circuits, producing a broad wake-up effect. More moderate than ammonia; safe for repeated use.
- **When:** Studying, driving, afternoon slumps — any situation requiring moderate, repeatable alertness.
- **Ref:** [00:57:35](transcript.md#005735-mint-scents-create-alertness-by-activating-broad-wake-up-pathways)

**Diego Bohorquez** — Neuroscientist at Duke University; his lab demonstrated that gut mucosal neurons (neuropod cells) sense fatty acids, sugars, and amino acids and signal via the vagus nerve to trigger dopamine release in the brain. ([Bohorquez Lab](https://www.bohorquezlab.com))

**Charles Zuker** — Neuroscientist at Columbia University; performed the receptor-swap experiments in mice (swapping sweet and bitter receptors) that proved taste experience is determined at the tongue receptor level, not by downstream brain circuits. **Calvin Coolidge** — 30th U.S. President; the Coolidge Effect (renewed sexual motivation upon encountering a novel partner) is named after an apocryphal story about him. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge))

**James Nestor** — Author of *Breath*, referenced for data on benefits of nasal breathing for health and jaw structure. ([mrjamesnestor.com](https://www.mrjamesnestor.com/breath))
