# Episode 222: AMA #18: Cold Therapy Advice, Skin Health Tips, Motivation, Learning Strategies & More

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

This is the 18th Ask Me Anything episode of the Huberman Lab Premium subscription, originally recorded as a live AMA and subsequently released publicly. Andrew Huberman answers subscriber questions spanning skin health and aging, deliberate cold exposure protocols, self-motivation strategies, REM sleep optimization, school learning frameworks, shilajit and testosterone, his book writing process, and addiction recovery resources. The episode opens with announcements about expanded research philanthropy — three dollar-for-dollar donor matches (including the Tiny Foundation) now support human research at Stanford, Columbia, and the University of Oregon on deliberate heat for depression, goal setting, and immune-nervous-system interactions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Skin thinning with age is addressable through multiple interventions.** Collagen supplementation (15–30 g/day with vitamin C), red/near-infrared light (10–15 min/day, 5 days/week), prescription retinoids, and laser resurfacing all show moderate evidence for improving skin elasticity and appearance. Mineral-based sunscreens (zinc oxide up to 25%) are the safest choice for frequent use. Additional supports include regular non-fragranced moisturizer, an anti-inflammatory diet (limiting fried/processed foods, adding omega-3 fatty acids, fiber, fruits and vegetables), and adequate hydration. An upcoming episode with Dr. Teo Soleymani will cover oral vine-extract supplements that modify skin chemistry to provide internal sun protection. [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

2. **Cold therapy effectiveness is defined subjectively, not by exact temperature.** The optimal temperature is "cold enough that you want to get out but can safely stay in." Huberman's personal range is 45–50°F; beginners start at 55–60°F; high 30s–low 40s°F only with a spotter. Duration of 1–3 minutes per session is typical. Huberman structures sessions by "walls" rather than time — get in when reluctant but safe, stay until adaptation, then exit. [→ Cold Therapy Benefits & Guidelines](transcript.md#1446-cold-therapy-benefits--guidelines)

3. **Never do breathwork hyperventilation before cold water immersion.** Blowing off CO2 suppresses the gasp reflex, which has caused fatal blackouts. Deep breathing during immersion (not hyperventilation) is fine. Always have a spotter for very cold water. [→ Cold Therapy Benefits & Guidelines](transcript.md#1446-cold-therapy-benefits--guidelines)

4. **Cold therapy and morning exercise increase REM sleep that same night.** The epinephrine surge from cold exposure or exercise early in the day promotes more REM sleep later that night. A cold shower after resistance training does not impair hypertrophy or strength adaptation; only a cold plunge immediately after lifting may limit some adaptation. [→ Morning Routine: Exercise & Cold Exposure](transcript.md#2845-morning-routine-exercise--cold-exposure)

5. **NSDR may mimic REM sleep.** Huberman and Matt Walker's lab are preparing studies to test whether non-sleep deep rest (body still, mind active) mimics REM sleep's brain states. Free NSDR scripts are available by searching "NSDR Huberman" on YouTube, or via Kelly Boys on YouTube and the Waking Up app. [→ Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR) Protocols](transcript.md#2949-non-sleep-deep-rest-nsdr-protocols)

6. **REM sleep rebound is real and reliable.** Miss REM on one night, and the next night (without caffeine or alcohol interference) will compensate with extra REM. No current pharmacology reliably and specifically induces REM sleep. [→ REM Sleep Rebound & Compensation](transcript.md#3127-rem-sleep-rebound--compensation)

7. **Self-motivation is best done as a closed-loop internal system.** Announcing goals publicly provides premature reward and erodes motivation. A private written contract with yourself — signed after each completed session, with the work as the reward — is more effective. Supporting tactics: remove your phone to another room or a dedicated box, set a timer and do not leave your seat until it goes off, and optionally use Tim Ferriss-style fear setting (imagining worst-case outcomes of not doing the work). [→ Self-Motivation Strategies](transcript.md#2118-self-motivation-strategies)

8. **Three high-impact school learning interventions: sleep, meditation, micro gaps, and physical activity.** Brief focused meditation (3–5 min) before learning, random 10-second micro gaps during instruction (1–10 per hour), and cardiovascular exercise immediately before a learning session all meaningfully improve information encoding. [→ Meditation & Micro Gaps in Learning](transcript.md#3419-meditation--micro-gaps-in-learning)

9. **Micro gaps trigger the same rapid neural replay as REM sleep.** During a brief rest, the hippocampus and neocortex replay recent material at 20–30x speed in reverse. This occurs subconsciously and is equivalent to many repetitions of the material. [→ Meditation & Micro Gaps in Learning](transcript.md#3419-meditation--micro-gaps-in-learning)

10. **Shilajit has weak evidence for testosterone support.** One study of 60 participants shows a small but statistically significant FSH increase; one study shows a small testosterone increase; one study shows improved sperm quality. The perhaps most notable finding is a reduction in some LDL markers — yet still small. Tongkat ali and fadogia have more supporting data. Women with menstrual cycles should be cautious, as FSH stimulation can disrupt hormonal cycling. [→ Exploring Shilajit & Testosterone](transcript.md#4101-exploring-shilajit--testosterone)

11. **Sleep is the highest-leverage testosterone intervention.** An extra 20–30 minutes of REM sleep will do more for testosterone than shilajit. Optimal body fat (not too high, not too low), resistance and cardiovascular training, and proper nutrition are the foundation before any supplementation. [→ Exploring Shilajit & Testosterone](transcript.md#4101-exploring-shilajit--testosterone)

12. **Addiction is a neurochemical circuit disruption, not a willpower failure.** 12-step communities (free, widely available) have been scientifically validated in a Stanford psychiatry study. The episode with Dr. Anna Lembke is Huberman's top recommended resource for understanding and overcoming addiction. [→ Addiction & Recovery Resources](transcript.md#5132-addiction--recovery-resources)

## Tools & Protocols

### Mineral-Based Sunscreen
**What:** Zinc oxide (up to 25%) or titanium dioxide-based sunscreen for daily UV protection.
**How:** Apply to all exposed areas not covered by physical barriers (hat, clothing). Choose non-chemical formulas for frequent/daily use.
**When:** Any time of sun exposure, especially during peak UV hours.
**Ref:** [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

### Collagen Supplementation for Skin
**What:** Ingest collagen protein to support skin elasticity and plumpness.
**How:** 15–30 g/day (range 5–30 g seen in studies), taken with vitamin C to enhance absorption. Powder form (animal- or fish-derived) is most common.
**When:** Daily; results accumulate over weeks to months.
**Ref:** [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

### Red / Near-Infrared Light for Skin
**What:** Red and near-infrared light panel exposure to improve skin appearance.
**How:** 10–15 minutes per day, minimum 5 days/week, over several months.
**When:** Daily routine; can be combined with collagen supplementation for potential synergy.
**Ref:** [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

### Retinoid Therapy
**What:** Prescription retinoid (oral or topical) to improve collagen composition and skin appearance.
**How:** Must work with a qualified dermatologist or derm oncologist. Requires sun avoidance during treatment due to increased photosensitivity.
**When:** Ongoing; requires dermatologist supervision.
**Ref:** [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

### Laser Resurfacing
**What:** Dermatologist-performed procedure to remove the superficial epidermal layer, reduce skin cancer risk, and improve skin appearance.
**How:** Performed by a qualified derm oncologist (not purely cosmetic providers). Expect a peeling period and strict sun avoidance for days to a week post-procedure.
**When:** As recommended by a derm oncologist; primarily a cancer prevention tool with a cosmetic benefit.
**Ref:** [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

### Moisturizer for Skin Hydration
**What:** Regular application of a non-fragranced moisturizer to maintain skin hydration and plumpness.
**How:** Apply a high-quality, fragrance-free moisturizer to arms and other exposed areas regularly. Hyaluronic acid-containing formulas can enhance plumpness (discussed further in upcoming Dr. Teo Soleymani episode).
**When:** Daily.
**Ref:** [→ Skin Health & Appearance](transcript.md#0339-skin-health--appearance)

### Deliberate Cold Exposure Protocol
**What:** Cold shower or cold plunge for alertness, mood, and dopamine boost.
**How:** Beginners start at 55–60°F; intermediate at 45–50°F; advanced at high 30s–low 40s°F only with a spotter. Duration: 1–3 minutes. Measure readiness by "walls" — enter when reluctant but safe, stay until adaptation, then exit. Never do hyperventilation breathwork beforehand. Deep calming breaths during immersion are fine.
**When:** Preferably morning to boost epinephrine and improve REM sleep that night. Do not do immediately after resistance training if maximizing hypertrophy (though cold shower after lifting does not impair adaptation).
**Ref:** [→ Cold Therapy Benefits & Guidelines](transcript.md#1446-cold-therapy-benefits--guidelines)

### Contract-With-Self Motivation System
**What:** A private written contract for new routines that treats completion as its own reward.
**How:** Write the goal on paper; define the specific commitment (e.g., "3 x 30 min resistance training/week"); sign after each session; track weekly sign-offs. No public announcement. No external reward beyond the sign-off itself.
**When:** Starting any new habit or routine. Review at end of each week.
**Ref:** [→ Self-Motivation Strategies](transcript.md#2118-self-motivation-strategies)

### Phone Removal for Deep Work
**What:** Physically remove phone from workspace to eliminate distraction during focused work.
**How:** Place phone in another room or a dedicated box. Set up an emergency contact channel (office phone, family notification system) so you can disconnect without real risk. Set a timer; do not allow yourself to leave the seat until it goes off.
**When:** Any session requiring sustained focus: writing, studying, creative work.
**Ref:** [→ Writing Process & Overcoming Obstacles](transcript.md#4751-writing-process--overcoming-obstacles)

### Pre-Learning Focused Meditation
**What:** Brief focused meditation before a learning session to improve working memory, focus, and retention.
**How:** 3–13 minutes of attention directed to the spot just behind the forehead (interoception), with attention brought back whenever it drifts. No mystical intent required — purely a perceptual/attentional exercise.
**When:** At the start of any learning or study session, ideally daily.
**Ref:** [→ Meditation & Micro Gaps in Learning](transcript.md#3419-meditation--micro-gaps-in-learning)

### Micro Gaps During Learning
**What:** Deliberate brief pauses introduced at random intervals during a learning session to allow neural replay.
**How:** Pause information delivery for ~10 seconds, 1–10 times per hour, at unpredictable intervals. Students/learners should not use phones or review material during the gap.
**When:** During any lecture, study session, or podcast/audio learning. Can be self-applied by pausing a recording.
**Ref:** [→ Meditation & Micro Gaps in Learning](transcript.md#3419-meditation--micro-gaps-in-learning)

### Exercise Before Learning
**What:** Cardiovascular or any physical activity immediately before a focused learning session to elevate catecholamines and enhance memory encoding.
**How:** Any form — running, jogging, swimming, brisk walking. Begin the learning session immediately after finishing, while catecholamines (dopamine, epinephrine, norepinephrine) are elevated.
**When:** Before study sessions, lectures, or skill practice.
**Ref:** [→ Physical Activity & Learning](transcript.md#3913-physical-activity--learning)

### NSDR Protocol
**What:** Non-sleep deep rest (yoga nidra-style) to supplement REM sleep and restore mental energy.
**How:** 10–20 minutes of guided body-still/mind-active relaxation. Search "NSDR Huberman" on YouTube for free 10- and 20-minute versions. Also available via Kelly Boys on YouTube and the Waking Up app. Download to phone for offline access.
**When:** First thing in the morning if REM sleep was insufficient; or any time a mental reset is needed.
**Ref:** [→ Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR) Protocols](transcript.md#2949-non-sleep-deep-rest-nsdr-protocols)
