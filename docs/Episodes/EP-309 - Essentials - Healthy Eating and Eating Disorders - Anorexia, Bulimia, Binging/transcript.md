## [00:00:00] Eating Disorders

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are going to talk all about healthy and disordered eating, and indeed we are going to talk about clinical eating disorders such as anorexia, bulimia and binge eating disorder as well as some other related eating disorders.

Before we get into this material, I want to emphasize that today's discussion will include what it is to have a healthy relationship with food. We're going to talk about metabolism, how eating frequency and what one eats influences things like appetite and satiety, as well as whether or not we have a healthy psychological relationship to food and our body weight and so-called body composition — the ratio of muscle to fat to bone, etc.

## [00:01:05] Fasting, Intermittent Fasting, Healthy Eating

As we march into this conversation, I'd like to share some interesting and important findings in the realm of nutrition and human behavior. Many people are excited about or curious about so-called intermittent fasting. Intermittent fasting is, as the name implies, simply restricting one's feeding behavior to a particular phase of the 24-hour or so-called circadian cycle. Other forms of intermittent fasting involve not eating for extended periods of time — for entire days, or some people will extend to two or three days — typically doing what's called water fasting, ingesting fluids and hopefully electrolytes such as salt, potassium, and magnesium.

The reason electrolytes matter is that neurons of your brain and body that control your movements, your thoughts, and clarity of thinking are critically dependent on adequate levels of sodium, potassium, and magnesium. These ions are necessary for neurons to be electrically active; without them you can't think, you can't function, and it can be quite dangerous. A lot of the excitement about intermittent fasting relates to work done by Sachin Panda at the Salk Institute for Biological Studies in San Diego, whose lab identified important health benefits of restricting one's feeding window to particular times within the 24-hour cycle or even having extended fasts of one to three days.

What Sachin's lab and subsequently other labs showed was an improvement in liver enzymes, an improvement in insulin sensitivity, and various other health parameters when feeding windows were restricted to anywhere from 4 to 12 hours during each 24-hour cycle — demonstrated first in mice and then in some human studies. The fundamental truth about metabolism and weight is that regardless of whether you intermittent fast, eat small meals all day, or eat one meal in the evening, what matters is the calories you ingest filtered through the calories you burn via exercise and basal metabolic rate. Many people prefer intermittent fasting simply because it is easier to not eat at all than to limit portion size.

Nobody — not the government, no nutritionist, no individual — can define the best eating plan for any one person. We only know the measurements we can take: liver enzymes, blood lipid profiles, body weight, athletic performance, mental performance, mood. These things have strong cultural, familial, and social influences, so what seems normal eating in one community looks very abnormal in another.

## [00:06:10] Self-Diagnosis Caution

Now we are going to talk about eating disorders that fall into the category of clinically diagnosable eating disorders, for which there are serious health hazards and even the risk of death. There are clear criteria in the psychiatric and psychological communities to define things like anorexia, bulimia, and binge eating disorder. However, as we have that discussion I want to emphasize that self-diagnosis can be both a valuable but also a very precarious thing.

There is always a temptation as one learns about the symptomology of a given disorder to ask whether oneself or someone they know has that disorder. Diagnosis really needs to be carried out by people who are trained in that field and have deep expertise in recognizing the symptomology, including some of the more subtle symptomology of eating disorders. So if any of the symptoms resonate with you or someone you know, take that seriously, but take that information to a qualified health care professional who could diagnose or rule out any of these possible disorders.

## [00:07:20] Eating Disorders, Anorexia Nervosa

What is an eating disorder? Every society, culture, family, and individual has a different relationship to food. Eating disorders, however, have particular criteria that allow us to define them and to think about different modes of treatment as they relate to the psychological and biological symptoms of those disorders. Anorexia nervosa, most commonly referred to as anorexia, is perhaps the most prevalent and the most dangerous of all eating disorders — in fact, anorexia is the most dangerous psychiatric disorder of all, even more than depression.

The probability of death for untreated anorexia is very high. It is essentially a failure to eat enough to maintain a healthy weight. You can see all sorts of very troubling symptoms in someone who has been anorexic for some period of time: a general loss of muscle mass, low heart rate (the body's attempt to lower energy output), low blood pressure, sometimes fainting, loss of bone density and osteoporosis, loss of periods in girls and women, and all sorts of disrupted gut and immune functions.

One of the misconceptions about anorexia is that it stems from an overemphasis on perfectionism or from exposure to images in social media and advertising of extremely thin or muscular people. That turns out not to be the case. If you look at the prevalence of anorexia over the last 10 to 20 years and compare it to when anorexia was first identified — which was in the 1600s and perhaps even earlier — what you find is that rates of anorexia are not going up. Classically defined anorexia has existed at essentially the same prevalence for the last 100, 200, 300, and 400 years, which really speaks to the likelihood that there is a strong biological contribution to what we call anorexia nervosa.

Anorexia nervosa affects anywhere from 1 to 2% of women, with typical onset in adolescence close to puberty, though diagnosis often occurs in the early 20s when signs tracing back to the early teens become clinically apparent. Men can be anorexic as well, but anorexia nervosa does seem to occur at approximately 10 times the rate in women and young girls than it does in men and young boys.

## [00:11:07] Hunger & Satiety; Appetite, Body Fat & Brain

So what is hunger and what is satiety? The body communicates two types of information to the brain on a regular basis around feeding: mechanical information and chemical information. When your stomach is full, it sends signals to your brain that are purely based on mechanical fullness — nothing to do with nutrients — essentially saying "I'm full, don't be as hungry, don't motivate to find or ingest food." Whereas when our gut is empty, even if we have plenty of nutrients or plenty of body fat stores, we tend to focus on food more.

Chemical effects are equally important. When we ingest food, our blood glucose goes up; that information is signaled to the brain via neuronal pathways and hormonal pathways, and there are neurons within our gut that signal to areas of our brain stem involved in satiety. You have neurons in your hypothalamus that trigger eating and neurons that trigger cessation or stopping of eating. The hypothalamus contains the so-called POMC neurons (proopiomelanocortin neurons) that act as a brake on appetite via a hormone called melanocyte-stimulating hormone, and AGRP neurons that stimulate feeding and create a sort of anxiety or excitement about food.

If you eliminate these AGRP neurons — as has been done in experimental mouse models and as occurs in humans with lesions on these neurons — the result is complete loss of appetite for food whatsoever. Whereas if you stimulate these AGRP neurons, or in humans with a small tumor near them, they become hyperphagic and will eat to the point of bursting. Signals coming back from the body to inform the brain about nutrient levels come from three main sources: body fat, the gut, and blood chemistry. The more body fat we have, the more we secrete a hormone called leptin. Leptin goes to the brain and suppresses appetite — and leptin signaling is disrupted in people with bulimia, obesity, and certain forms of binge eating disorder.

When there are sufficient levels of body fat and leptin circulating in the blood, the hypothalamus and pituitary gland register that signal and in a completely subconscious way trigger the deployment of eggs in females and the production of sperm in males. So when body fat stores are very low, the reason why periods shut off or sperm production is reduced is because there is not enough leptin getting to the hypothalamus and pituitary, shutting off the hormones — things like gonadotropin-releasing hormone, luteinizing hormone, and follicle-stimulating hormone — that cause the ovaries and testes to ovulate or produce sperm.

## [00:15:48] Homeostasis & Reward Systems, Eating Disorders

You have two categories of neurons: one that acts as an accelerator — the AGRP neurons, saying eat and getting you excited to eat — and a category of neurons, the POMC neurons, that are suppressing hunger, acting like a brake. The body is constantly informing the brain about its status and whether it needs more food. From an evolutionary standpoint, it makes sense that we should eat as often as we can, as much as we can, and as fast as we can; food was scarce and seeking food was dangerous, so there are circuits in the brain to reward eating often, eating fast, and cramming as much food in as possible.

Every animal, including humans, has a hardwired circuit that pays attention to how much food is available, how much we are getting now, and how much we are likely to get in the future. You have a hypothalamic area called the arcuate nucleus that houses POMC neurons and AGRP neurons that regulate hunger and satiety. These neurons start getting active when we see food and think about food, driving hunger in a way that is responsive to what food looks like, what it smells like, our prior history of interactions with that food, and social context.

All of behavior — good decision-making and bad decision-making — can be framed in a simple model: we have knowledge of what we should do in one box, and what we actually do in another box. In between are two intervening forces that are critically important: homeostatic processes that regulate the balance of different systems in your body (hot and cold, awake or asleep, dopamine and the desire to pursue things, serotonin and the desire to relax) and reward systems. As we now move into discussion about anorexia and bulimia specifically, what you'll see is that these disorders are not a breaking of the mindset of what one should do — they are a disruption of these homeostatic and reward processes such that decision-making is completely disrupted and in many cases is not available to the anorexic or bulimic.

The person who starves themselves to the point where they might die — and in some cases sadly does die — can know perfectly well that their behavior is leading to bad outcomes and possibly even death, and yet they are not able to intervene without particular clinical help, because the homeostatic processes, the signals from the body and brain that say you need food, those aren't registering in the same way that they are for other individuals.

## [00:19:40] Anorexia, Puberty, Hyperacuity & Food

A careful analysis through medical epidemiology has shown that anorexia exists even in cultures and societies where food is scarce, which really speaks to a biological mechanism. Typically anorexia starts in adolescence right around puberty. Puberty is the most significant and dramatic developmental step anyone goes through in their lifespan — the body changes, the brain changes, perceptions change, one's own self-perception changes, and most of those changes are driven by changes in circuitry within the hypothalamus, where neurons controlling the production of sex steroid hormones such as testosterone, estrogen, and prolactin are all changing at very rapid rates.

Some experiments gave anorexic patients a gallery of pictures of different foods and asked them to arrange those foods according to preference, nutrient content, and caloric content. What they identified was something very unique to anorexics at the level of their perception of food: anorexics, rather than being anxious in the presence of food, have a hyperacuity — a hyper-awareness of the fat content of foods — almost to the point of being fat content savants. It's a well-known symptom of anorexia, especially in young anorexics, that they have a kind of obsession with food, caloric contents, and macronutrient ratios. They are actively avoiding high-fat content and calorie-rich foods, and defaulting towards very low calorie foods if they have to eat.

What that means is that we need to look at the areas of the brain that drive habit formation and habit execution. In the case of the anorexic, those habits are exactly the place where things start to go awry and that drive this very dysfunctional undereating behavior that sadly often leads to death or certainly bad medical outcomes. It turns out that the brain areas associated with habit formation and execution are the best point of intervention.

## [00:22:46] Decision-Making vs Reflexes/Habits, Anorexia

You have reflexes and you have neural processes that include what are called duration, path, and outcome (DPO) type processes. DPO is for all types of goal-related behaviors: duration (how long do I have?), path (which way am I going to navigate?), and outcome (was I able to achieve the goal?). It is a very conscious process — it requires decision-making and is reward-based, and relies on your forebrain, specifically the prefrontal cortex. The prefrontal cortex is what allows you to take information from memory, combine it with information about what's happening in the present context, and then direct your behavior toward particular outcomes.

Reflexes, on the other hand, don't involve the prefrontal cortex in the same way. Habits and reflexes, like walking once you've learned, are subconscious — you don't have to use the parts of the brain involved in duration, path, and outcome analysis. Anorexics have a brain area involved in evaluating and decision-making around food, and then another brain area involved in the reflexive consumption of particular foods and the reflexive avoidance of other foods. In the brain of the anorexic, it turns out that the reward systems have been attached to the execution of habits in a way that is unhealthy for body weight — the reward is now given for avoiding particular foods and only approaching very low calorie, low-fat foods.

There really does seem to be a flip in the switch in the anorexic brain that rewards them internally — they feel good when they avoid certain foods. It's not a deprivation-based model where they are masochistically avoiding food to punish themselves; rather, once this transitions into a set of habits, they are actually getting a sense of reward, feeling good presumably from the release of dopamine by approaching foods that are low fat, low calorie content. So their whole brain circuitry is skewed toward avoiding particular things, and they actually are rewarded for that — they feel better than if they were eating in a healthy, weight-supporting way.

## [00:26:20] Anorexia & Breaking Habits, Therapies & Family-Based Models

How do you break a habit? How do you rewire the brain circuitry that's literally causing a reflex and in this case causing a reflex that is killing the individual or at least leading to very bad health outcomes? The way you do that is through a cognitive mechanism where you teach the individual what is leading up to the habit. There are two main features of thinking that go into the sorts of habits anorexics execute.

The first is something called weak central coherence — essentially an inability to see the forest through the trees, a hyperacuity and focus on details within a given environment while missing the big picture. The other is a challenge in set-shifting: once they identify something that's of particular interest driving reward for the anorexic (such as identifying the high-fat foods or the one food on the table they can eat without anyone noticing), they home in on those low-fat, low-calorie foods and can't easily shift away. What's amazing is that once you teach anorexics what's happening to them, they are able to intervene — though they need support.

Another form of therapy that works well for anorexics, ideally combined with this habit rewiring, is a family-based model. Family-based models are basically where the entire family is made aware of the individual's challenges with a particular eating disorder, and in understanding some of the biology and psychology around it, they stop condemning the individual. They start to support that individual by cueing them toward their own habits as they observe them, giving them some autonomy, realizing that none of this changes overnight, and being taught about things like neuroplasticity and the ability to change one's brain in response to experience. All of these things fall under the umbrella of cognitive behavioral therapy, often done in conjunction with pharmacologic therapies.

## [00:28:59] Distorted Self-Image & Anorexia

Before moving on to talking about bulimia and some related disorders, there is an aspect of anorexia that is very interesting and quite troubling — and that's the distorted self-image. In the episode on depression, we talked about the anti-self confabulation that people who are depressed seem to genuinely believe — it's literally a lie they believe and their statements, feelings, and behaviors start to reflect that lie. Anorexics often will see themselves as overweight or imperfect in ways that are an obsession for them. The problem seems to be that they have a genuine distortion of their self-image — so much so that they don't actually see themselves accurately; their visual perceptions are off.

The reason we know this is because of some really important studies done in Jeremy Bailenson's lab at Stanford, where patients get to adjust an avatar of themselves to the point where they think it's as accurate as it could possibly be. Anorexics really distort this avatar — they create a serious mismatch between their perception of themselves and the reality. What's encouraging about the therapies discussed earlier — the family-based model, the cognitive behavioral treatments, the drug treatments, and the habit intervention model — is that as one starts to shift those things, the perception of self seems to follow. The perception of self shifts along with the change in habits.

Simply telling someone "you're so thin, you really need to eat" doesn't seem to work, because they just don't see themselves the same way that you see them. Fortunately, by changing habits, you rewire those circuits as well. Their brain, as it relates to perceptions — visual perceptions in particular — is completely off, but the habit change creates the neuroplastic correction.

## [00:31:53] Bulimia & Binge-Eating Disorder, Impulsivity & Prescription Treatments

Let's talk about bulimia, which is overeating and then purging — typically by self-induced vomiting or by ingestion of laxatives. Binge eating disorder has a lot of the same features as bulimia but typically no purging. The criteria for diagnosis is if somebody is doing this at least once a month over a period of anywhere from 2 to 3 months. They are not making the decision to overeat — they are driven from the inside to ingest far more food than they need, in some cases more than they would even want to eat. It's a lot like the habit described for anorexia — almost like it turns into a reflex once they get going, with all the homeostatic signals (leptin, insulin, glucose) being overridden.

There is a lot of shame associated with bulimia, often because people are vomiting and it's hard to hide. The hallmark feature of bulimia that distinguishes it from anorexia — aside from the overeating rather than undereating — is a lack of inhibitory control. The bulimic, unlike the anorexic, is hyper-impulsive and oftentimes has other types of impulse behaviors. For that reason, many of the treatments for bulimia and binge eating disorder are the sorts of treatments that work differently from those for anorexia.

The drugs that increase the neuromodulator serotonin — for instance, fluoxetine (also called Prozac), Paxil, etc. — can often be effective in bulimia. Some of the drugs used to treat attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, such as Adderall and Vyvanse, can also be used to treat bulimia and binge eating disorder. The reason: the prefrontal cortex is involved in the analysis of duration, path, and outcome — it's how we avoid impulsivity. For the anorexic, those circuits are overactive; for the bulimic, those circuits are essentially underactive. Drugs that increase serotonin, and sometimes drugs that increase dopamine and norepinephrine levels in the brain, allow for more top-down control. Behavioral interventions coupled with drug-based interventions are always more effective than either one alone.

With anorexia, the reward is tied to the execution of the habit — the anorexic feels great about restricting food intake, like they're winning some sort of game. With bulimia, they feel horrible about the fact that they're binging; there's immense shame. The reward is set up before the behavior, drawing them to food and making food look incredibly appetizing, and there's no impulse brake to stop that kind of behavior.

## [00:35:39] Recap & Key Takeaways

The major takeaways today are: we should all be asking the question of what healthy eating is for us — how do we develop a relationship to food that we can enjoy both socially and on our own, without being neurotic and compulsive about it. Today we focused on the extremes of food-related behaviors that qualify as genuine disorders: they are in psychiatric manuals, they are diagnosable, and they are serious health concerns — not just mentally troubling for the people suffering from them and the people around them, but genuine health concerns.

Anorexia nervosa is the most deadly psychiatric disorder by a huge margin, and if you look statistically at the number of people with eating disorders who die, it's not far off from the number of people who die from automobile accidents. The model worth taking away is one box for what you think, one box for what you do, and what is intervening between those. Why is it that you can know better and not do better? It's because you also have to cope with these subconscious homeostatic processes and reward processes, which often can be disrupted in ways that lead to behaviors not good for us or others.

Fortunately, there is the great gift that knowledge of knowledge can allow you to do better without question. And that knowledge of knowledge, allowing you to do better over time, leads to the incredible phenomenon called neuroplasticity — which essentially translates into doing better over time, such that even if difficult, eventually doing better becomes reflexive.
