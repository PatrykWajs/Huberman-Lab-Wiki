## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Emotions, Food & Nutrition

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. My name is Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. This podcast is separate from my teaching and research roles at Stanford.

Today, we're talking all about emotions. Emotions are central to our entire experience of life — whether or not we're happy, sad, depressed, or angry is our life experience. And yet, with all the importance we've placed on emotions, very few people actually understand how emotions arise in our brain and body. I mention both brain and body because, as you'll see today, emotions really capture the brain-body relationship. We cannot say that emotions arise just from what happens in our head.

The other thing about emotions is that there's no real agreement as to what's a good emotion or a bad emotion. Today, we're going to talk about the biology of the chemicals and pathways that give rise to emotions in the context of food and nutrition. The discussion around emotions has a long and rich history going back to Darwin, and even long before Darwin — philosophers and scientists have been having this conversation for hundreds, if not thousands, of years.

The idea that Darwin put forth, and that was really attractive for about the last 100 years, was that emotions are universal and that some of the facial expressions around emotion are universal. To some extent, it's true. I think the two most robust examples would be when we see, smell, or taste something that we like — there does tend to be a postural leaning in, we tend to inhale air at that time, and we tend to bring in more of whatever chemical substance is there.

## [00:02:30] Attraction & Aversion

So we tend to do a sort of "mmm" and lean in closer to things that are attractive to us. When we see and experience things that we don't like, sometimes it's a mild aversion — we just kind of lean back or look away. Other times, it's an intense aversion of disgust and we tend to cringe our face, tending to avoid inhaling any of the chemicals. This probably has roots in ancient biological mechanisms that are meant to prevent us from ingesting things that are bad for us — chemical compounds and tastes that might be poisonous.

So much of the foundation of any discussion about emotion has to center around this kind of push-pull of attraction to things or aversion from things. That's a very basic way of thinking about emotions, but if you think about it, it works for a lot of different circumstances. In the brain everywhere — from the deep circuits to the more higher-order evolved centers — we have this push-pull thing.

In a previous episode I talked about "go" — the circuits that allow you to emphasize action — and then "no-go" circuits, the circuits in the basal ganglia that allow you to de-emphasize and prevent action. At the core of attraction or aversion is an important theme: there is an action there. You're either moving forward or moving away from something. The brain has a body so that the organism can move, and the body has a brain so that the organism can move toward or away from things that are deemed to be good or bad.

## [00:03:38] Vagus Nerve, Sugar

There are circuits in the brain for aversion and for attraction toward things, and the body is governing a lot of that. So immediately in this conversation, I want to raise an important point about a nerve pathway that many people have heard of but that is one of the most oversold for the wrong reasons — and unfortunately undersold for its real power — and that is the vagus nerve. The vagus nerve is one — not the only, but one — way in which our brain and body are connected and regulate our emotional states.

Vagus is the 10th cranial nerve, which basically means the neurons and the control center of each of those neurons in the vagus live just near the neck. One branch of the vagus goes into the brain; the other branch goes into the periphery — not just the gut, but the stomach, the intestines, the heart, the lungs, and the immune system. The way to think about the vagus is the same way I would think about the eyes: the eyes are looking at colors, motion, brightness, and each one of those features is telling the brain something different. The vagus nerve is also analyzing many features within the body, informing the brain of how to feel about that and what to do.

A really good example that I think is an exciting one is as it relates to sugar. We all know that sweet things generally taste good. When you eat something sweet, within your stomach you have cells — neurons — that sense the presence of sugary foods independent of their taste and signal to the brain. Those sensors send information up the vagus to your brain, it goes through a series of stations, and then you release dopamine, the molecule that makes you want more of whatever it is that you just ingested.

In fact, this pathway is so powerful that they've done experiments where they completely numb all the taste and feeling in somebody's mouth, blindfold them so they don't know what they're eating, and give them a food that is either sugary or not sugary. What they find is that even though people can't taste the sugary food, they crave more of the food that contains sugar because of the sensors in the gut that sense sugar. What this tells us is that we have circuits in our body that are driving us toward certain behaviors and making us feel good, even though we can't consciously perceive them.

## [00:06:31] Gut "Feelings", Hidden Sugars, Amino Acids

For those who are interested in gut intuition and gut feelings — this is a gut feeling. Except this is a chemical gut feeling. It's a particular set of neurons detecting that something in your body has a particular feature — in this case the presence of sugars — and sending information to the brain to essentially control your behavior. This should completely reframe the way that we think about the so-called hidden sugars in foods.

What this means is that even if a food is very savory, like a piece of pizza, a piece of bread, or even a salad dressing — if there's sugar snuck in and you can't taste it, you will still crave more of that thing without knowing that you crave it because it has sugar. A lot of how we feel while we eat and after we eat is because of this vagal sensing of what's in our gut. It's sending information all the time: Is there sugar? Are there fats? Are there contaminants? All these parallel pathways are going up into our brain to regulate whether or not we want to eat more of something.

There are accelerators — things that make us want to eat more, like sugar and fats, because those are nutrient dense and help support the survival of animals. But also amino acids, and this is very important. Much of what comes from the data on what people eat and how much they eat is from a subconscious detection of how many amino acids — and what the array, meaning the constellation of amino acids — is in a given food. The sum total of these studies points in a direction where people will basically eat not until their stomach is full, but until the brain perceives that they have adequate intake of amino acids.

Amino acids are important because they are the building blocks of muscle and the other things in our body that need repair. But what most people don't realize is that amino acids are what the neurochemicals in the brain are made from. This is vitally important.

## [00:08:54] Dopamine, Craving, L-tyrosine

Dopamine is a molecule that makes you feel good. Dopamine release is caused by surprise, excitement, events that you're looking forward to and that turn out well. It is inhibited by events you're looking forward to that don't work out — this is called reward-prediction-error. Your expectation of something releases dopamine, and the actual event releases dopamine. If the event-related dopamine does not exceed the expectation, or at least match it, there's a much higher tendency that you won't pursue that thing again.

Dopamine is what's going to lead us to want to eat more of something, or to not want more of something, because dopamine really is about craving. It's about motivation and desire. The amino acid sensors in our gut are detecting how many amino acids, but they're also detecting which amino acids. There is a particular amino acid called L-tyrosine, which comes from food — it is in meats, it is in nuts, it is also in some plant-based foods. L-tyrosine is the precursor to a couple of other molecules like L-dopa that make dopamine.

However, the dopamine neurons that give rise to these feelings of good, wanting more, desire, and motivation reside in the brain. We want to respect and honor the power of the gut and this vagal pathway, but it's really neurons within your brain that drive the pursuit and decision-making. Some people make too little dopamine — some people make so little that they need prescription dopamine, like L-dopa. People with Parkinson's take L-dopa and other compounds to increase dopamine, because Parkinson's is associated with deficits in movement, a blunting of motivation and mood and affect, and a tremor.

Some people ask whether they should supplement L-tyrosine. It does increase mood, elevation, and alertness, and it is over-the-counter. However, you have to check with your doctor. People with pre-existing hyperdopaminergic conditions like mania should probably not take L-tyrosine. The other thing about taking L-tyrosine is there is a crash — not a massive crash if you take it at appropriate doses, but it can produce lethargy and a kind of brain fog the next day or so. L-tyrosine can be ingested through foods or through supplementation to increase dopamine levels, though taken chronically it can disrupt those dopamine pathways.

Foods rich in L-tyrosine generally give us an elevated mood and make us want to do more of whatever it is that we happen to be doing, as well as other things — motivation generalizes to other things. But foods that give us a big pulse of dopamine will make us crave more of that food and more of the activity that led to the ingestion of that food, and much of that is happening at a subconscious level that you're not even aware of.

## [00:12:57] Serotonin, Carbohydrates

The other neuromodulator that's really interesting in the context of the vagus is serotonin. Serotonin is a neuromodulator that creates a bias in which neural circuits are going to be active and makes it less likely that other ones will be. Serotonin, when it's elevated, tends to make us feel really comfortable and kind of blissed out wherever we are. That contrasts with dopamine and epinephrine, which mainly put us in pursuit of things — motivation is pursuit.

The conversation around the brain-body relationship and mood and serotonin for many years was: you eat a big meal, the gut is distended, you've got all the nutrients you need, you rest and digest, and serotonin is released. That's sort of true, but there's a lot more going on. Some of you have heard that more than 90% of the serotonin that we make is in our gut — and indeed, we do have a lot of serotonin in our gut. But here's the deal: most of the serotonin that impacts our mood and our mental state is not in our gut. Most of it is in the neurons of the brain in an area called the raphe nucleus.

You can't have a discussion about serotonin without having a discussion about antidepressants. During the late '80s and early '90s there was an explosion in the number of prescription drugs released. The first and most famous ones were Prozac, Zoloft, and Paxil — the so-called SSRIs, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors. Basically, those drugs work by preventing the reuptake of serotonin into neurons after it's been released, which leads to more serotonin overall — elevated serotonin. Those drugs were and can be very useful for certain people in cases of depression and some other clinical disorders. Not everyone responds well to them, and their side effect profile can include blunting affect, making people feel kind of flat.

Many people adjust their serotonin by just eating more food, and carbohydrate-rich foods will increase serotonin. I eat a relatively high protein, moderate fat, zero-carb or low-carb meal at lunch and in the afternoon to stay alert, because those foods tend to favor dopamine production, acetylcholine production, epinephrine production, and alertness. Then as evening comes around and I'm concerned about a good night's sleep, I will ingest foods that promote serotonin release because they contain a lot of tryptophan.

So as you're seeing, this isn't really a discussion about nutrition per se. This is a discussion about food which contains amino acids — amino acids being the precursors to neuromodulators — and neuromodulators having a profound effect on your overall state of alertness or calmness, happiness, sadness, and well-being.

## [00:16:12] Omega-3s, Depression, SSRIs

Now let's talk about things that we ingest in our body that allow our body to inform our brain to shift our mood. The omega-3 to omega-6 fatty acid ratio has a profound effect on depression. First, in an experiment done in animals, they found a model of learned helplessness where they put rats or mice in a jar and let them swim until they give up. Adjusting the omega-3 and omega-6 ratio so that the omega-3s are higher led to less learned helplessness — these animals would swim longer.

But that same study was essentially done in humans. They took people who were clinically depressed — major depression is a severe maladaptive state, meaning it inhibits job, relationships, appetite, and causes all sorts of negative health effects — and they did a comparison of 1,000 milligrams a day of EPA. EPA is one of the elements containing high levels of omega-3s found in things like fish oil — but it wasn't 1,000 milligrams of fish oil, it was 1,000 milligrams of EPA — compared to 20 milligrams of fluoxetine, which is Prozac. They found that they were equally effective in reducing depressive symptoms.

What was really interesting in addition to that is that the combination of 1,000 milligrams of EPA and fluoxetine had a synergistic effect in lowering depressive symptoms. Now there are lots of studies — if you go into PubMed and search for EPA or fish oil and depression, you would find a number of really impressive results showing that it's at least as effective as certain SSRIs at these dosages. And it can amplify or improve the effect of low dosages of some of these SSRIs.

No one compound or nutrient or supplement or drug — or behavior, for that matter — is going to be the be-all and end-all of shifting out of depression, or improving one's mood or improving sleep. You cannot expect to take a compound, regardless of source or potency, and have it completely shift your experience of life without continuing to engage in the proper behaviors: proper sleep, exercise, social connection, food, and so on.

## [00:19:12] Gut-Brain Axis, Gut Microbiome

Now let's turn to another aspect of the gut-brain relationship that will surprise you — in some cases might shock you — and that has some really cool and actionable biology. That's the gut microbiome, probiotics, and prebiotics. What's the deal with the gut microbiome and the gut-brain axis?

Today, we've actually been talking a lot about the gut-brain axis that has nothing to do with microbiomes. We've been talking about this vagus nerve that provides sensory information from the body to the brain, and then the brain also sends, in the same nerve, motor information to control the motility of the gut, the heart rate, how fast we breathe, and the deployment of immune components. But oftentimes when we hear about the gut-brain axis these days, it's a discussion about the gut microbiome.

I'm very happy there's so much discussion about the gut microbiome. I am somewhat dismayed and concerned that most of what I hear out there is either false or partially false. So we're going to clear up some of the misconceptions by understanding the biology. It is true that we have a lot of these little microorganisms living in our gut. They're not there because they want to help us — they don't have brains — but they are adaptive. They try to find and create environments that make it easier for them to proliferate. They don't care about you and me, but they are perfectly willing to exploit us in order to make more of themselves.

The microbiota that live in us vary along the length of our digestive tract. We are one long tube for digestion, and inside of that tube is a mucosal lining. The conditions of that mucosal lining set the rate and quality of our digestion, and they set our immune system. We're ingesting things all the time — air, bacteria, viruses — they're making their way into our gut, and some of those bacteria bias the mucosal lining to be more acidic or more basic so that they can make more of themselves.

## [00:22:35] Probiotics, Brain Fog, Tools: Fermented Foods, Saccharine Caution

Some of those mucosal lining conditions make us feel better, make us feel more alert, and bolster our immune system. Others make us feel worse. So the first rule: the microbiome isn't simply good or bad. Some of these little bugs that live in us do bad things to us — they make us feel worse, they lower our immunity, they affect us in negative ways. Some of them make us feel better, and they do that mainly by changing the conditions of our gut environment. In addition to that, they do impact the neurotransmitters and the neurons that live in the gut, and signal up to the brain to impact things like dopamine and serotonin.

Supporting a healthy gut microbiome is good for mood, great for digestion, and great for immune system function. However, that does not mean maxing out or taking the most probiotic and prebiotic that you can possibly manage. There are studies that show that if you take lots and lots of certain probiotics, like lactobacillus, it is not a case of more is better. There are things like brain fog that can come from that — brain fog is this inability to focus, people feel really not well generally.

The ingestion of fermented foods is one of the best ways to support healthy levels of gut microbiota without exceeding the threshold that would cause things like brain fog. Some people report that when they start eating small bits of fermented foods — because it doesn't require a lot — their overall mood is better.

There are also some things you can do to really damage your gut microbiome — and this is where there's a huge misconception I want to clear up. There was a study that showed that artificial sweeteners, but a particular artificial sweetener — saccharin — can disrupt the gut microbiome in ways that are detrimental to a number of different health markers, increasing inflammatory cytokines and all the other bad things that happen when the gut microbiome is thrown off kilter. Saccharin is not the most typical artificial sweetener used; more typical ones are aspartame, sucralose, and stevia. To my knowledge, the negative effects of artificial sweeteners on the gut microbiome were restricted to saccharin.

What happens is saccharin disrupts the microbiome and makes the environment within the gut — that mucosal lining — more favorable to bacteria that are not good for the organism. It's not just that it kills the microbiome — it shifts the microbiome. And shifts in the microbiome can be good or they can be bad.

## [00:25:39] Ketogenic Diet & Gut Microbiome, Tool: Individual Diet Variability

That takes us to another topic — nowadays, there are many examples where people have switched from a standard diet, or even a vegetarian or vegan diet, to a keto diet, and they experience positive effects for themselves. When one shifts to the ketogenic diet, there is a shift in the gut microbiome, and some people end up feeling better, some people end up feeling worse. Likewise, some people go from ingesting animal products to a vegan diet and experience positive shifts in mood and affect.

The point of all this is that when I say you have to find what's right for you, that's not a throwaway statement. Some people's microbiome and the mucosal lining of their throat, gut, and nose is improved by diets that are heavily meat-based and don't have many plants. Other people do much better on a plant-based diet without many animal products. It's highly individual, and this probably has roots in genetic makeup and what people were raised on.

The nervous system is set up by your genes, but your nervous system adapts early in life to your conditions — that's what it's for. The reason you have a nervous system is to move your body appropriately towards things that are good for you and away from things that are not. But also it was designed to adapt so that some people may like certain foods and react to certain foods better than others, because of the way that their nervous system was wired. This enteric nervous system that lines the gut communicates with the brain. Find the diet that's right for you and that works for you in the context of the other ethical and lifestyle choices that are important to you.

## [00:28:59] Tool: Belief Effects; Key Takeaways

As we round up, I want to share some results that, without question, will impact the way that you respond to food — mentally and even physically. I have a colleague at Stanford, Alia Crum, who has done some remarkable experiments on mindset. Two that are particularly interesting to me involve how our beliefs can really impact the way that our brain and body work together.

The most famous of these is an experiment where they had two groups of individuals who were each given a milkshake. They had factors measured from their blood by an IV while they ingested the milkshake and afterwards as well. One of the factors they were looking at was ghrelin — G-H-R-E-L-I-N — a peptide that increases with hunger; the longer you haven't eaten, the higher ghrelin goes. One group got a shake they were told was a low-calorie, healthy shake. The other group got a milkshake they were told was a very decadent high-calorie shake. What they found was that the "high-calorie shake" had a much more robust effect on blunting and reducing ghrelin — but the interesting thing you probably guessed already is that it was the exact same shake given to both groups.

This speaks to these so-called top-down mechanisms, or modulation of our physiology. This is not just the placebo effect. It's an incredible set of findings that illustrate the extent to which, whether or not we believe a food is going to be good for us or not, these belief effects are not about lying to yourself — in order for them to work, you have to be genuinely naive to the information. But also important is that the mind and body are in this fascinating interplay.

Today, we've talked mainly about how the body and things we put inside this tube running from our mouth to our rectum are impacting all these cells — neurons, microbiota in there, mucosal lining, heart, lungs — and how all that information is feeding up to the brain to impact how we feel. But also how we feel up here is impacting how our body reacts at levels of very core physiology. What you believe about certain substances, certain foods, certain nutrients does have a profound effect on the magnitude of their impact, and sometimes even the quality and direction of that impact. Thank you all for your time and attention today, and as always, thank you for your interest in science.
