# Episode 52: The Biology of Slowing & Reversing Aging | Dr. David Sinclair

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. David Sinclair, professor of genetics at Harvard Medical School and co-director of the Paul F. Glenn Center for the Biology of Aging, joins Andrew Huberman to explain why aging is a disease — not an inevitability — and what the science says we can do about it. The conversation covers the cellular and molecular mechanisms of aging (particularly the epigenome and sirtuins), and delivers specific, actionable protocols for fasting, supplementation (NMN, resveratrol, Metformin), exercise, cold exposure, and blood biomarker tracking. By the end, listeners have both a mechanistic understanding of aging biology and a complete framework for slowing or reversing the process. This episode also serves as a primer for the Lifespan podcast (launched January 5, 2022), which Sinclair announced during the recording.

**Dr. David Sinclair** is a tenured professor of genetics at Harvard Medical School and co-director of the Paul F. Glenn Center for the Biology of Aging. ## Key Takeaways

1. **Aging is a disease, not an inevitability.** Aging is the root cause of 80–90% of heart disease, Alzheimer's, and cancer. The definition of "disease" arbitrarily excludes conditions affecting more than half the population — aging fits every other criterion. Treating aging as a disease unlocks intervention strategies that go beyond band-aids, including reversing the age of tissues to make those diseases disappear. [00:07:45](transcript.md#000745-aging-as-a-disease)

2. **The epigenome, not the genome, is the primary driver of aging.** Aging is loss of epigenetic information — the "reader" that tells cells which genes to express. Sinclair's equation: aging = loss of information due to entropy. DNA stays mostly intact; the scratches accumulate on the control layer. 80% of longevity is epigenetic, not genetic. Cells that lose their epigenetic identity begin behaving incorrectly — this is also the root of most cancers. [00:10:23](transcript.md#001023-what-causes-aging-the-epigenome)

3. **Puberty rate may predict lifespan.** Studies show that slower development through puberty correlates with longer, healthier life — likely mediated by growth hormone, which is pro-aging. Animals engineered for low growth hormone are the longest-lived by far. Michael Bankowski's mouse "Yoda" lived five years (double normal lifespan) via caloric restriction combined with a low-growth-hormone dwarf mutation. Laron dwarfs in South America show near-absence of heart disease and cancer. [00:20:12](transcript.md#002012-puberty-rate-as-a-determinant-of-aging-rate)

4. **Hunger is a signal the body is designed to receive.** Persistent overfeeding shuts off longevity genes. Sirtuins respond to low sugar/insulin; mTOR responds to low amino acids (especially leucine, lysine, and valine). Together, "up sirtuin, down mTOR" during fasting activates all of the body's defenses: DNA repair, autophagy, improved insulin sensitivity, more energy, cleaner cells. Sinclair eats one meal per day in an ~2-hour evening window, keeping longevity genes on for most of the day. [00:23:00](transcript.md#002300-fasting-hunger-food-choices)

5. **Pulsing — cycling abundance and scarcity — maximizes both muscle and longevity benefits.** Chronically suppressing nutrients is not the goal. After fasting ends, longevity genes remain elevated for hours, just as exercise benefits persist after a session. By strategically timing eating, supplements, and exercise in cycles, you capture anabolic growth during abundance and repair/longevity activation during scarcity. This is why Sinclair's protocol took ~15 years to refine. [00:37:55](transcript.md#003755-amino-acids-leucine-pulsing)

6. **Metformin is a longevity drug, not just a diabetes drug.** Epidemiological data from tens of thousands of veterans shows diabetics on Metformin live longer than non-diabetics not on Metformin. It activates AMPK to mimic caloric restriction and appears protective against cancer, heart disease, and dementia. Sinclair skips it on exercise days (reduces stamina/endurance, not strength); ~20% of users have GI sensitivity. Berberine activates the same AMPK pathway and is available OTC as an alternative. [00:44:35](transcript.md#004435-metformin-berberine)

7. **Resveratrol is the accelerator pedal for sirtuins; NAD is the fuel.** 1 g/day resveratrol dissolved in olive oil (not water — fat increases bioavailability fivefold) is Sinclair's protocol. It must be light gray or white; brown indicates oxidation. Resveratrol without adequate NAD is like a revving engine with no gas. NMN (1 g/day) doubles blood NAD levels within two weeks (from unpublished clinical trials on dozens of humans). NMN outperforms NR and vitamin B3 because it delivers all three molecular components the body needs for NAD synthesis in one step. A head-to-head Cell paper showed NMN made old mice run 50% further; NR at the same dose had no effect. [00:50:29](transcript.md#005029-resveratrol-wine)

8. **NAD is circadian — NMN timing matters.** NAD controls the body's circadian clock (shown by Shin Imai in a Science paper). Taking NMN late at night can disrupt circadian rhythms. Taking it in the morning aligns with the body's natural NAD peak and can also reset the circadian clock when crossing time zones. [00:56:45](transcript.md#005645-resveratrol-nad-nmn-nr-dosage-timing)

9. **Excess iron accelerates aging via senescent cells.** Manuel Serrano's lab found excess iron increases the number of senescent (zombie) cells — inflammatory, cancer-promoting, aging-accelerating. Healthy people eating plant-leaning diets often run slightly low on iron yet have abundant energy. Do not let a physician reflexively prescribe iron supplementation based on below-average levels — what is optimal for a longevity-focused person differs from the population average. [01:12:04](transcript.md#011204-iron-load-and-aging)

10. **CRP and homocysteine are the most important underutilized blood markers.** High-sensitivity CRP (hsCRP) predicts cardiovascular inflammation and mortality independently of blood sugar — normal fasting glucose does not rule out dangerously high CRP. Homocysteine elevation signals disrupted methylation. Tracking biological age via methylation clocks (mouth swab) gives a real measure of whether interventions are working. Sinclair's own InsideTracker data showed his biological age moving from 58 to 31 over months of focused intervention. [01:15:05](transcript.md#011505-blood-work-analysis)

11. **Dietary cholesterol has almost zero impact on serum cholesterol.** A definitive study (flagged to Sinclair by Peter Attia) confirmed this. Most cholesterol is synthesized in the body; eating eggs and butter does not meaningfully raise blood cholesterol. The useful metric is the HDL:LDL ratio (target below 5). Sinclair went on a statin at age 29 due to family history of cardiac disease and now maintains LDL below 100 and a ratio of 2. [01:19:37](transcript.md#011937-c-reactive-protein-cholesterol)

12. **Plants contain xenohormetic molecules that activate human longevity defenses.** "Xenohormesis" (coined by Sinclair and Conrad): stressed plants produce resveratrol, quercetin, and other molecules that activate their own sirtuin defenses. Humans who ingest these molecules co-opt those same ancient defenses. Prefer organic, locally grown, visually imperfect produce. Resveratrol's lifespan-extending effect in yeast was confirmed to be via the sirtuin pathway — a single atom change to eliminate its antioxidant activity left the effect intact, while giving yeast pure antioxidants shortened their lives. [01:26:02](transcript.md#012602-amino-acids-plants-antioxidants)

13. **Epigenetic reprogramming can restore youthful gene expression in neurons.** Sinclair's Nature (2020) paper showed three transcription factors (OSK) injected into the retina reversed the biological age of neurons as measured by the methylation clock, and restored vision in blind mice. Human trials targeting blindness were planned for 2022–2023 (first in monkeys for safety). The long-term goal is an oral pill that rejuvenates the whole body by 20 years. [01:40:35](transcript.md#014035-neuroplasticity-and-neural-repair)

14. **The hypothalamus controls the body's aging program via GnRH.** Dongsheng Cai showed that inhibiting hypothalamic inflammation in mice maintains expression of GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone), reversing aspects of aging including bone density, muscle mass, and cognitive function. Activating SIRT1 specifically in the hypothalamus also extends lifespan. The hypothalamus is a master regulator — not just a reproductive hormone center. [01:48:07](transcript.md#014807-obesity-and-how-it-accelerates-aging-gnrh)

## Tools & Protocols

### Time-Restricted Eating / Intermittent Fasting

- **What:** Restricting food intake to a limited daily window. Sinclair typically eats one meal in the evening within a 1–2 hour window, fasting 18–22 hours per day.
- **How:** Skip breakfast; optionally skip lunch; eat a nutritious dinner. Start gradually — eliminate breakfast first, then reduce lunch. The body needs time to adapt; quitting cold turkey fails. Small amounts of olive oil or coffee with a splash of milk in the morning are unlikely to meaningfully break the fast (no protein or carbs = minimal mTOR/insulin activation).
- **When:** Daily. Most important step is to start; optimize timing later. Evidence suggests earlier eating windows may be slightly better for circadian alignment, but evening meals are practical for most people.
- **Ref:** [00:23:00](transcript.md#002300-fasting-hunger-food-choices)

### Extended (Multi-Day) Fasting

- **What:** Occasional 2–3 day fasts to trigger macroautophagy and chaperone-mediated autophagy (deep cellular cleansing). Chaperone-mediated autophagy (discovered by Ana Maria Cuervo) kicks in at day 2–3 and extended mouse lifespan by 35% when triggered.
- **How:** Water, tea, and coffee only; no calories.
- **When:** Once a month or less. The deeper autophagy pathway requires sustained fasting beyond 24 hours.
- **Ref:** [00:32:44](transcript.md#003244-fasting-schedules-long-fasts-macroautophagy)

### NMN Supplementation

- **What:** Nicotinamide mononucleotide — a direct NAD precursor that delivers all three molecular components needed for NAD synthesis in one step (NR lacks the phosphate group; vitamin B3 lacks the phosphate and sugar).
- **How:** 1 gram (1,000 mg) per day in a single morning dose. Look for white, crystalline, GMP-certified powder (tastes like burnt popcorn). Do not take at night.
- **When:** Every morning, alongside resveratrol. Aligns with the body's natural circadian NAD peak. Can also be used as a jet-lag reset upon arrival in a new time zone.
- **Ref:** [00:56:45](transcript.md#005645-resveratrol-nad-nmn-nr-dosage-timing)

### Resveratrol Supplementation

- **What:** Polyphenol that directly activates sirtuin enzymes — the "accelerator pedal" for the longevity pathway. Requires adequate NAD as fuel to be effective. Works via the sirtuin pathway, not antioxidant activity (confirmed by atom-change experiment in yeast).
- **How:** 1 gram (1,000 mg) per day dissolved in 2 teaspoons of olive oil (optionally add a splash of vinegar and a basil leaf — "like drinking salad dressing"). Can also be dissolved in Greek yogurt. Must be light gray or white powder; brown powder has oxidized (brown powder also causes diarrhea). Fat is required for ~5x bioavailability.
- **When:** Every morning alongside NMN.
- **Ref:** [00:50:29](transcript.md#005029-resveratrol-wine), [00:56:45](transcript.md#005645-resveratrol-nad-nmn-nr-dosage-timing)

### Metformin

- **What:** Prescription drug that activates AMPK to mimic caloric restriction. Associated with reduced cancer, heart disease, and dementia risk in large epidemiological datasets.
- **How:** Requires a physician prescription in the US (OTC in many other countries). Take in the morning. Skip on exercise days to preserve stamina (reduces endurance/reps, not strength or muscle quality — muscles built on Metformin have lower inflammation). Skip on days with GI discomfort (~20% of users are sensitive).
- **When:** Every morning except workout days.
- **Ref:** [00:44:35](transcript.md#004435-metformin-berberine)

### Berberine (Metformin Alternative)

- **What:** Plant-derived compound used in Chinese medicine for thousands of years. Activates AMPK similarly to Metformin, mimicking caloric restriction.
- **How:** Available OTC. Dosing varies — check label and consult a physician.
- **When:** Mornings, as an alternative if Metformin is not accessible.
- **Ref:** [00:44:35](transcript.md#004435-metformin-berberine)

### Quercetin Supplementation

- **What:** Flavonoid found in apples and onions that activates sirtuins AND helps clear senescent cells — a double benefit. Found to kill senescent cells in research ~20 years after Sinclair's original 2003 sirtuin activation finding.
- **How:** Supplement form. Can be dissolved alongside resveratrol in olive oil.
- **When:** Morning with other supplements.
- **Ref:** [01:26:02](transcript.md#012602-amino-acids-plants-antioxidants)

### Exercise (Strength + Cardiovascular)

- **What:** Aerobic exercise raises NAD levels and activates SIRT1 and SIRT3; resistance training maintains muscle mass, hormone levels (testosterone in men, hormonal balance in women), and insulin sensitivity.
- **How:** Pulse training — cycle exercise with fasting and rest periods to maximize benefits. Skip Metformin on workout days. Optimize for strength and longevity, not maximum muscle size. Consistent exercise can maintain testosterone at youthful levels even into older age.
- **When:** Regular. Time relative to eating and supplementation windows for maximum benefit.
- **Ref:** [01:33:45](transcript.md#013345-behaviors-that-extend-lifespan-testosterone-estrogen)

### Cold Exposure / Metabolic Winter

- **What:** Sleeping cool, wearing fewer layers in cold weather, and occasional cold showers or ice baths. Based on the Metabolic Winter Hypothesis (Cronise & Sinclair): ancestral periods of cold and hunger activated longevity pathways that modern comfort eliminates.
- **How:** Sleep at a cooler temperature; dress lightly in winter; use sauna and ice bath when accessible. Even mild chronic cold (sleeping slightly cool all night) burns extra energy and activates thermogenic/longevity pathways.
- **When:** Ongoing lifestyle choice.
- **Ref:** [01:46:19](transcript.md#014619-ice-baths-cold-showers-metabolic-winter)

### Blood Work Tracking (Key Biomarkers)

- **What:** Regular, longitudinal blood testing to track biological vs. chronological age and detect issues before they become symptomatic.
- **How:** Test every 6–12 months. Track trends over time rather than single readings. Key markers: hsCRP (high-sensitivity CRP), homocysteine, IGF-1, testosterone/estrogen, fasting glucose, insulin, lipid panel (HDL:LDL ratio target below 5), ferritin (watch for excess iron). Supplement with biological age (methylation clock swab test) to measure whether interventions are actually working.
- **When:** Every 6–12 months; use InsideTracker or equivalent for optimal (not just average) reference ranges and trend analysis.
- **Ref:** [01:15:05](transcript.md#011505-blood-work-analysis)

### Plant-Forward Diet with Stressed/Organic Produce

- **What:** Eating mostly plants, particularly those grown under stress (organic, local, imperfect), for high nutrient density and xenohormetic molecules. Sinclair does not take a multivitamin (to avoid excess iron), relying on plants for micronutrients instead.
- **How:** Dark leafy greens, colorful vegetables, "imperfect" produce that has been nibbled on or over-sunned. Some fish, chicken, or eggs acceptable (dietary cholesterol does not meaningfully raise serum cholesterol). Avoid excess meat to keep leucine/mTOR stimulation moderate.
- **When:** Every day.
- **Ref:** [01:26:02](transcript.md#012602-amino-acids-plants-antioxidants)

### Avoiding Unnecessary Radiation

- **What:** Minimizing cumulative radiation exposure from airport body scanners and dental x-rays. Each exposure is small but the effect is cumulative and accelerates epigenomic damage.
- **How:** At airports — request pat-down or use TSA Pre (metal detector only, no scanner). At the dentist — request x-rays only when clinically necessary, not on a routine annual schedule.
- **When:** Every time. Cumulative exposure adds up over a lifetime.
- **Ref:** [01:56:17](transcript.md#015617-x-rays)

### NMN Timing for Jet Lag Reset

- **What:** Using NMN's circadian clock properties to reset the body clock when traveling across time zones.
- **How:** Take a morning dose of NMN upon arrival in the destination time zone.
- **When:** Day of arrival when crossing significant time zones.
- **Ref:** [00:56:45](transcript.md#005645-resveratrol-nad-nmn-nr-dosage-timing)
