## [00:10:23] What Causes Aging? The Epigenome

**David Sinclair:** Yeah, well, that's contentious because scientists like to come up with new hypotheses — it's how they build their careers. But fortunately during the 2000s, we settled on eight or nine major causes of aging. We call them hallmarks because "causes" was a little bit too strong, but these eight or nine causes, at least for the first time, allowed us to come around and talk together. We put them on a pizza so everyone got an equal weighting, equal slices. But before that, by the way, we were trying to kill each other's careers in the field — that was horrible.

I like to think I was fairly generous, but I was one of the kids and the old guard really didn't like the new guard. We came along in the 1990s and said free radicals don't do much — there are actually genes called longevity genes — and that caused a whole ruckus. There was this competition for what never happened, which was a Nobel prize for this, and it just led to a lot of competition.

But then in the 2000s, we rallied around this new map of aging with these causes or hallmarks. I think, however, that there's one slice of the pizza that is way larger than the others, and that's the information in the cell that we call the epigenome.

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us a little bit more about the epigenome — frame it for us if you will, and then we'll get into ways that one can adjust the epigenome in positive ways.

**David Sinclair:** Yeah, so what I like to do as a reductionist is to boil it down. I actually ended up boiling aging down to an equation, which is the loss of information due to entropy. It's a hard thing to overcome — second law of thermodynamics. But this equation really represents the fact that I think aging is a loss of information in the same way that when you xerox something a thousand times you'll lose that information, or you try to copy a cassette tape. Even if you send information across the internet, some of it will get lost. That's what I think is aging.

There are two types of information in the body. There is the genetic information, which is digital — ATCG, the chemical letters of DNA. But there's this other part of the information in the body that's just as important, that's essential, and that's the systems that control which genes are switched on and off in what cell at what time in response to what we eat, et cetera. It turns out that 80% of our future longevity and health is controlled by the second part, the epigenetic information, the control systems.

I liken the DNA to the music that's on a DVD or a compact disc. The epigenome is the reader that says, okay, in this cell we need to play this particular song. And what I think is happening is that as you live longer and longer, the music is still there — your DNA doesn't change very much — but the reader gets scratched, and it starts to misread the music. And cells start to forget what type of cell they are — they don't know whether they should be a liver cell or a skin cell. This leads to chaos, because the cells will start behaving in the wrong way, and that ultimately leads to aging. Even cancer is a result of cells going through this identity crisis.

## [00:23:00] Fasting, Hunger & Food Choices

**David Sinclair:** Well, there is, but that doesn't mean that you're a slave to your early epigenome nor to your genome. The good news is that the epigenome can change. Those loops and structures can be modified by how you live your life. No matter what size you are, you can have a bigger impact on your life than anything your genes give you. 80% is epigenetic, not genetic.

**Andrew Huberman:** So let's talk about some of the things that people can do. The first one relates to food, blood sugar, insulin. I hear a lot about fasting being good for us, but rarely do I hear why it's good for us. One of the reasons I'm excited to talk to you today is because I want to drill into the details — I think understanding the mechanism will allow people to make better choices. So why is it that having elevated blood sugar and insulin ages us more quickly, and why is it that having periods of time each day without food — or perhaps longer periods — can extend our lifespan?

**David Sinclair:** Well, let's start with what I think was a big mistake — the idea that people should never be hungry. We live in a world now where there's at least three meals a day, and companies are selling bars and snacks in between. The feeling of hunger — some people never experience it in their whole lives. It was based, I believe, on the 20th century view that you don't want to stress out the pancreas and you try to keep insulin levels pretty steady without fluctuation.

What we actually found — my colleagues and I — across this field of longevity is that when you look at animals, whether it's a dog or a mouse or a monkey, the ones that live the longest — 30% longer — and stay healthy are the ones that don't eat all the time. This was actually first discovered back in the early 20th century, but people ignored it. Then it was rediscovered in the 1930s — Clive McCay did caloric restriction, he put cellulose in the food of rats so they couldn't get as many calories even though they ate, and those rats lived 30% longer. But then it went away, and came back in the 2000s in a big way, when a couple of things happened. One is that my lab and others showed that there were longevity genes in the body that come on and protect us from aging and disease. The group of genes that I work on are called sirtuins — there are seven of them — and we showed in a 2005 Science paper that if you have low levels of nutrients, the sirtuins get activated.

Now, why would evolution do that? I'll give you the evolutionary argument that was made — because it's really important and not enough people understand this. If you think back a few million years, our ancestors were essentially going through periods of feast and famine. When times are tough, if you're hungry, evolution says, you better stay alive, you better not get diseases. If you're plentiful, you're in a plentiful environment, well, stay alive now, reproduce as fast as you can. So if you feed your body well and you've got lots of nutrients and you're growing, the body doesn't need to protect itself as much. It's saying, hey, there's plenty of food, I don't need to waste energy on repairing things. And so the longevity genes actually switch off when you eat a lot and when you're growing. But when you're hungry, they come back on and say, protect this animal, repair its DNA, keep it clean. That's evolution's logic.

Now for humans, we don't want to be in the plentiful state all the time. I try to be hungry for some of my day. For many years, I've been eating only one meal a day usually. And that's not starvation — I'm plentiful. I live a plentiful life. But by going without food for 18 to 20 hours or more a day, I'm keeping those longevity genes on for most of the day. The good news is that most of the day, I'm not eating, so I'm not producing as much insulin, which is pro-aging, I'm not activating mTOR too much, which is pro-aging if it's too high, and I'm keeping the sirtuin levels up high.

**Andrew Huberman:** I find it fascinating also that people in very hot climates — where caloric expenditure is high — cultures where people are quite lean tend to live longer. I want to ask about the timing of eating. You mentioned you eat one meal a day — is it at any particular time of day? Or does it vary?

**David Sinclair:** I generally eat towards the end of the day. And I've talked about this publicly. I'll typically eat sometime around 6, 7, 8 PM depending on my schedule and with my kids. But there is some evidence for time-restricted eating being best done earlier in the day — the circadian alignment of that — but I think for quality of life, for social reasons, and practically, eating the big meal in the evening is the most practical for most people. The most important thing for most people is to start having periods of fasting rather than the exact timing. Get the behavior first, and then optimize.

## [00:35:56] Blood Glucose & the Sirtuins; mTOR

**Andrew Huberman:** You've told us that there's ample evidence that keeping your blood sugar low for a period of 24 hours can help trigger some of these pro-longevity, anti-aging mechanisms, and that extending them out two or three days can trigger yet additional mechanisms. How is it that blood glucose triggers these mechanisms? How are glucose and the sirtuins actually tethered to one another mechanistically?

**David Sinclair:** That's a really good question — it proves you're a scientist, a world-leading one. What we now know is that these longevity pathways, these longevity genes, talk to each other. We used to say, oh, my longevity gene is more important than yours — it was ridiculous, because they're all talking to each other. You pull one lever and the other one moves.

The way to think of it is that there are systems set up to detect what you're eating. The sirtuins will mainly respond to sugar and insulin. And then there's this other system called mTOR, which is sensing how much protein or amino acids are coming into your body. They talk to each other. But together, when you're fasting, you'll get sirtuin activation, which is good for you. And you'll also — through lack of amino acids, particularly three of them: leucine, lysine, and valine — the body will down-regulate mTOR. And it's that "up sirtuin, down mTOR" that is hugely beneficial — it turns on all of the body's defenses, the pro-chewing-up of old proteins, improved insulin sensitivity, more energy, repairing cells, all of that. These two pathways I think are the most important for longevity.
