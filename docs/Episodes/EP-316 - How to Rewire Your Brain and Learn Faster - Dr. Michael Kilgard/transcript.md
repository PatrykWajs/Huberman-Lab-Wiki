## [00:42:44] Learning & Plasticity Requirements; Focus, Friction, Rest & Reflection

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the requirements for plasticity in adults, because I want to make sure people listening have a clear framework. You've mentioned focus, you've mentioned friction. What are the absolute requirements for learning and neuroplasticity?

**Michael Kilgard:** Focus, friction, rest, and reflection. Those are the four essential elements. You need alertness — you have to be paying attention. If you're not attending to what you're experiencing, the neuromodulators don't get released. If the neuromodulators don't get released, the synaptic eligibility trace doesn't get locked in. So focus is absolutely essential.

Friction — the experience has to be difficult enough to require genuine effort. Easy tasks don't trigger the same neuromodulator release as challenging ones. This is why Gregg Recanzone's classic experiment with monkeys showed that the monkey that was tasked to attend to touch had massive rewiring in the somatosensory cortex, while the auditory cortex — which received the same sounds — didn't change, because the sounds weren't relevant or challenging for that monkey.

Rest and sleep — after learning, the brain needs to consolidate. Sleep is when the synaptic changes that were primed during learning get cemented. The hippocampus replays the day's events during sleep, and the cortex locks in the changes. Skipping sleep is skipping the consolidation phase.

Reflection — going over what you learned, mentally rehearsing it, testing yourself. This is where the synaptic trace that was laid down gets reactivated and strengthened. Post-learning reflection, even five to ten minutes of sitting quietly and thinking about what you just learned, is extraordinarily powerful. And self-testing is probably the most powerful learning tool we know of.

**Andrew Huberman:** So self-testing is the reflection mechanism that's most powerful. Not re-reading notes, not re-listening to a lecture, but actually trying to retrieve what you know.

**Michael Kilgard:** Exactly. The act of retrieval is the act of strengthening the pathway. When you struggle to remember something and then recall it, that struggle and retrieval is itself a plasticity event. It's not just a readout of what you know. It's actually reinforcing the connections that encode that memory.

## [01:23:25] Neuromodulators, Brain Complexity, Synaptic Eligibility Trace & Learning

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's go deep into the mechanics of neuromodulators and plasticity. You've mentioned the four — acetylcholine, norepinephrine, serotonin, dopamine. How do they actually work?

**Michael Kilgard:** So, the key concept is the synaptic eligibility trace. Alfredo Kirkwood at Johns Hopkins is the person who worked out the molecular details. Here's how it works: at any given synapse, if the pre-synaptic neuron fires just before the post-synaptic neuron, there's a molecular change at that synapse — a priming. That synapse is now eligible for either strengthening or weakening. This primed state lasts only milliseconds.

Then, seconds later, a neuromodulator — acetylcholine, norepinephrine, serotonin, or dopamine — arrives. If it arrives within that window, it confirms the synaptic change. The connection strengthens if the timing was in the right direction, or weakens if it was reversed. If no neuromodulator arrives, the eligibility trace expires and nothing changes. In one ear, out the other. You just forget it.

So, "fire together, wire together" — Hebb's rule from 1949 — was partly right, but the full picture requires the neuromodulator arriving as the confirming signal. The synapse itself only records the millisecond timing. The neuromodulator, arriving seconds later, is the gate that decides whether the change is permanent or not.

The four neuromodulators all do the same job in this framework. They each release from different brain regions — nucleus basalis releases acetylcholine, locus coeruleus releases norepinephrine, dorsal raphe releases serotonin, ventral tegmental area releases dopamine — but they all converge on the same mechanism at the synapse level.

**Andrew Huberman:** So focus is critical because focus triggers the release of acetylcholine from the nucleus basalis. That's the substrate for attention-dependent learning. You can't have learning without that neuromodulator signal.

**Michael Kilgard:** Right. And that's why the anticholinergics — scopolamine, atropine — are amnestic drugs. Block acetylcholine, block learning. Those are now date rape drugs because of exactly this property — you can experience something and never form any memory of it.

## [01:34:28] Synapses, Therapy for PTSD, Rewiring the Brain

**Andrew Huberman:** So in the case of PTSD, the synaptic connections encoding the traumatic memory are very strong — they were formed under conditions of extreme neuromodulator release. CBT can cure maybe 40% of civilian PTSD, and 20% of military PTSD. What about the 60-80% who don't respond?

**Michael Kilgard:** We used to think it was demons. Then we thought it was humors that are wrong, high or low. Now we think it's wires. And the answer is those wires are hard to change. It's hard to find the right one. There's 150 trillion of them. Which ones are that sexual assault? Which ones are that IED attack? I don't know. You don't know.

But can we go through and develop a process to help work through it? And that's what we've been doing, developing the tools where we use what we've learned in the lab, not with a deep brain stimulating electrode as I did before, but now switching and saying, 'Is there any other way we could trigger a brief burst, not of one neurotransmitter, but of a bunch of neurotransmitters?' And one way we came upon is this way of using an electrode placed on the vagus nerve to trick the brain into thinking you're having a heart attack.

My lungs expanded, my lungs expanded, my lungs expanded.' And we say, "Oh, they didn't do that." Turns out that sends a powerful subconscious, very transient, brief arousal signal.

## [01:39:01] Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS), Stroke, Spinal Cord Injury, Tinnitus, PTSD

**Michael Kilgard:** And when we trigger a brief burst, just half a second long, that makes three out of four of those neurotransmitters active.

**Andrew Huberman:** Which ones?

**Michael Kilgard:** Norepinephrine, acetylcholine, and serotonin are all released. Interestingly, dopamine is not even activated. Rats don't like it, or avoid it. Humans don't like it, or avoid it. It just doesn't matter, because it's all subconscious. Knowing what your guts are doing, your heart and lungs, who cares? So, that one is not connected to a rewarding pathway. We're not aware of it.

But the combination of releasing those three neurotransmitters activates the appropriate chemistry. It's the signal that the neuron is looking for. And no, it's not any one signal. It's a cocktail. There's not one factor for anything. There's 10 factors for everything.

And at first, it was just a dumb experiment repeating the one we had done with deep brain stimulation, showing we could quadruple the number of neurons that produced a response to a given tone. We've done it with hemorrhagic stroke, we did it with ischemic stroke. And so now, I put all this work into a company called MicroTransponder. **Andrew Huberman:** Whether you stimulate the release of acetylcholine, or dopamine, or serotonin, or norepinephrine, you get the same effect — which is essentially you get more plasticity from fewer effort trials. ## [01:47:33] Psychedelics, Neurostimulation, Importance of Timing

**Andrew Huberman:** There is this parallel universe to the one that you exist in with neurostimulation, which is that many, many people are interested in taking drugs, prescription or otherwise, that they can use to open the door to, and enhance neuroplasticity. I've been positively struck by the data on psychedelics. Traditionally, the psychedelics are LSD, psilocybin, MDMA. My read of the literature is that these are primarily serotonergic agonists, particularly at the 5-HT2A receptor. What's your read?

**Michael Kilgard:** Yeah. I mean, I spent five years trying drugs. That's not something I'm proud of, but I thought it was going to work. I tried amphetamine, and nicotine, and cocaine, and Adderall. All of them increased neuromodulator levels. None of them worked in the way I wanted them to work.

The problem is the timing. You elevate serotonin, or dopamine, or norepinephrine globally, for hours. But the synaptic eligibility trace is milliseconds, and the confirmation window is seconds. If you flood the entire brain with neuromodulators for hours, you're confirming everything indiscriminately. There's no specificity. The brain can't tell what it's supposed to learn from all that noise.

What you need is a brief, targeted burst at the right moment — when the right synapse is primed, when the right thing just happened. That's the difference between vagus nerve stimulation, which delivers a half-second pulse exactly when the therapist wants it, and a drug, which floods the system for hours. Both increase neuromodulators. Only the timed pulse creates targeted plasticity.

**Andrew Huberman:** So psychedelics might open a broader window of plasticity, might lower the threshold for synaptic change, but without the behavioral specificity — without doing the right thing during the elevated neuromodulator state — you're not getting the targeted rewiring.

**Michael Kilgard:** Right. And that's why the psychedelic-assisted therapy model requires the therapist. You need to be doing the right emotional and cognitive work during the window. The psychedelic creates a moment of elevated plasticity. The therapist helps direct what gets encoded. Without the therapeutic work, you might get a lot of plasticity, but it's not directed at the right target.

## [01:57:47] Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT) & Major Depression

**Andrew Huberman:** ECT — electroconvulsive therapy — has a terrible reputation, but what does the data actually say about it for severe depression?

**Michael Kilgard:** ECT is the most effective treatment we have for severe, treatment-resistant depression. Period. The data is clear on that. It's not a cure — patients typically need maintenance sessions. But for the ~30% of people with major depression who don't respond to any antidepressant, ECT often works when nothing else does.

The mechanism is not well understood, but my interpretation is that it's analogous to "whacking the stuck motor." You have a machine that's gotten into a stuck state — a bad attractor in the dynamical sense. All the usual small perturbations don't get it out of the rut. A large, non-specific perturbation — a seizure — resets the network to a more neutral state from which it can recover. It's not targeted. It's a global plasticity event. But sometimes global is what's needed to escape a local minimum.

The memory loss is real and is a significant side effect. Patients often lose memory of the day of each session. Multiple sessions are needed — typically around 20. But for severe depression, the tradeoff is often worth it. The risk of untreated severe depression is mortality by suicide. ECT's side effects are manageable compared to that.

## [02:01:51] Psychedelics/SSRIs as Plasticity Tools, SSRIs & Bone Strength, Failed Clinical Trials

**Andrew Huberman:** What about SSRIs? We've had a decade-long discussion about the serotonin theory of depression being wrong.

**Michael Kilgard:** The low serotonin theory of depression is wrong. That's fairly well established now. But SSRIs still work for many people, which initially seems paradoxical. My interpretation, and I think the field is moving toward this, is that SSRIs work as plasticity tools, not as serotonin replacement. By increasing serotonin availability, they lower the threshold for plasticity — they open a window. But what gets encoded during that window depends on what you're doing.

This is why SSRIs combined with therapy tend to work better than either alone. The SSRI opens the plasticity window. The therapy provides the right content to encode during that window. Without the therapy, you get a plasticity window with no direction. That may help some people — they may be doing the right things naturally, getting social interaction, exercise, positive experiences — but it's not reliable.

The bone density issue is important and underappreciated. Serotonin is critical for chondrocytes — the cells that maintain bone. When you reduce serotonin availability in bone tissue — which SSRIs do systemically — you get reduced bone density over time. This led to a serious problem in stroke trials where people were taking Prozac as part of a neuroplasticity intervention, and they were getting hip fractures. The clinical trial had to be redesigned.

It's a reminder that every intervention in a complex biological system has effects you didn't anticipate. Serotonin doesn't just do one thing. It's involved in bone, gut motility, platelet aggregation, and dozens of other processes. When you change its levels, all of those are affected.

## [02:13:18] Can VNS Accelerate Learning?

**Andrew Huberman:** The obvious question: if VNS paired with physical therapy can accelerate recovery from stroke — can it also accelerate healthy learning?

**Michael Kilgard:** In principle, yes. We've demonstrated this in animal models. ## [02:16:01] VNS Surgery, Patient Use & Specificity, Closed-Loop Vagus Nerve Stimulation

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell me about the device itself. How does it work practically?

**Michael Kilgard:** The device is smaller than your pinky nail. It's implanted about 3 centimeters deep in the neck — it wraps around the cervical vagus nerve. It's an outpatient procedure, done under local anesthesia. It communicates wirelessly to a controller that the therapist uses.

Closed-loop systems are the next frontier — devices that detect the right neural or behavioral event automatically and trigger the stimulation without human input. For PTSD, you might detect the biomarkers of a successful exposure therapy moment and automatically reinforce it. That removes the latency and variability of human decision-making from the loop.

## [02:18:18] Tinnitus Cause, Incidence & Self-Amplification; PTSD & Control

**Andrew Huberman:** Walk us through tinnitus. What is it, how does it develop, and why is it so persistent?

**Michael Kilgard:** Tinnitus is a phantom sound — a ringing, buzzing, or hissing that has no external source. It affects about 750 million people worldwide, roughly 15% of the global population. The underlying mechanism starts with hearing loss — usually from loud noise exposure, though aging also contributes.

When you lose hearing in a frequency range, the auditory neurons that used to process that range stop getting input. But the auditory cortex is plastic — it doesn't just go quiet. It compensates. It amplifies whatever signal remains. For most people, this compensation is silent — you just don't hear as well at those frequencies. But in some people, the compensation overshoots. The cortex amplifies the spontaneous neural activity in those deprived areas, and that background activity is perceived as a sound. That's the phantom.

Once the phantom is established, it becomes self-reinforcing. The attention system notices the sound. The limbic system attaches significance to it — often negative, because it's unexpected and persistent. That emotional loading triggers further norepinephrine and acetylcholine release, which drives further plasticity, which strengthens the tinnitus representation. The brain literally learns to hear the phantom sound more and more clearly.

The connection to PTSD is interesting. Both are cases of something being over-learned — a traumatic response pattern in PTSD, a phantom auditory representation in tinnitus — that the brain then treats as highly significant and continuously reinforces. Both share a loss-of-control element: the person cannot turn off the thing their brain is doing. That helplessness is itself stressful, which triggers more neuromodulator release, which drives more learning of the unwanted pattern.

## [02:28:12] VNS for Tinnitus; Disease Complexity & Treatments, Lazy Eye

**Andrew Huberman:** How does VNS treat tinnitus?

**Michael Kilgard:** The approach is to use VNS paired with tones that are not the tinnitus frequency to reverse the maladaptive plasticity. We play a sequence of tones that cover a broad frequency range except the tinnitus frequency. Each tone is paired with a VNS pulse. The neuromodulator release reinforces the representation of those non-tinnitus tones. Gradually, the auditory map shifts away from the over-represented tinnitus frequency.

We published this in Nature, and the results were really encouraging. Patients showed significant reduction in tinnitus loudness and the distress it causes. The key point is that we're not trying to suppress the tinnitus directly — we're retraining the underlying map. That's the correct level of attack. The tinnitus is a symptom of a mis-calibrated map. Fix the map, the symptom diminishes.

**Andrew Huberman:** This reminds me of amblyopia — lazy eye. I actually have amblyopia, so this is personal for me. The treatment counterintuitively requires patching the good eye, not the lazy eye. You're forcing the brain to use the pathway that's under-represented.

**Michael Kilgard:** Exactly. And that's Hubel and Wiesel's work — they won the Nobel Prize for discovering the critical period for visual development and how patching works. The lazy eye has a cortical representation that's been outcompeted by the strong eye. The standard treatment — patching the strong eye — forces the visual system to process input through the weak eye, and with sufficient time and stimulation, the cortical representation of the weak eye recovers.

More recently, atropine drops in the strong eye have been used as an alternative — pharmacologically blurring the strong eye to give the weak eye a competitive advantage. It's less disruptive to the child than wearing a patch all day.

Both tinnitus and amblyopia illustrate the same principle: maladaptive plasticity creates the condition; corrective plasticity — using the same mechanisms, but directed appropriately — is the treatment. The brain doesn't care why it's changing. It changes according to the rules of neuromodulator-gated synaptic modification. You just have to provide the right input and the right neuromodulator signal at the right time.

## [02:41:05] Complexity of Disease Treatments & Combination Therapies

**Andrew Huberman:** We've seen a lot of failed clinical trials in neurology and psychiatry. What do you take from that pattern?

**Michael Kilgard:** Failed trials teach us about the heterogeneity of disease. When a drug fails in a large randomized trial, the typical interpretation is "the drug doesn't work." But that may be wrong. It may be that the drug works for 30% of the population and not for the other 70%, and the average effect washes out to zero.

The lesson is that we need better ways to stratify patients before treatment. Which subgroup will respond? That requires identifying biomarkers — neural, genetic, behavioral — that predict response. Our stroke trials are moving in this direction. We're asking not just "does VNS help stroke patients" but "which stroke patients, at what stage of recovery, with what deficit pattern, benefit most?"

The combination therapy principle is also essential. Most diseases aren't caused by one thing. They won't be cured by one thing. Combining VNS with physical therapy works better than either alone. Adding a pharmacological plasticity window on top of that might work even better. The art of medicine — and the art of future clinical trial design — is figuring out the optimal combination and the optimal sequence.

## [02:48:50] Brain-Machine Interfaces, Information & Experiences; Closed-Loop Feedback; Resilience

**Andrew Huberman:** Where do brain-machine interfaces fit into this?

**Michael Kilgard:** Brain-machine interfaces are a beautiful demonstration of plasticity principles. When you attach an electrode to a neuron, give the brain a new input channel, and pair activity in that channel with something meaningful — the brain incorporates it. The neurons reorganize around the new information source. People with cochlear implants are learning to hear using a very coarse approximation of auditory input. The brain fills in the rest. It adapts.

The same principles apply to prosthetic limbs with sensory feedback. If you can give the brain closed-loop feedback from a prosthetic — touch the surface, signal goes to the motor cortex in real time — the brain can learn to use the prosthetic as an extension of the body.

The resilience implication is: diverse experience builds a richer, more redundant brain. More synaptic representations. More alternative pathways. When you lose some of them — to injury, age, disease — you have more to fall back on. The person who has done many things, learned many skills, had many types of experiences, has a more resilient brain than the person who has done only one thing well, even if that one thing was done brilliantly.

## [02:59:09] Evolving Clinical Trials, Combination Treatments & Disease Complexity

**Michael Kilgard:** The field is moving toward adaptive clinical trial designs. Instead of committing to a fixed protocol and running it to completion, you monitor outcomes in real time and adjust. Which subgroup is responding? Enrich the trial with those patients. Which component of the combination is working? Amplify it. This is personalized medicine applied to clinical trials.

The complexity of brain diseases demands this approach. Depression isn't one disease. Tinnitus isn't one disease. PTSD isn't one disease. They're categories we've applied to clusters of symptoms that probably reflect dozens of distinct pathophysiological mechanisms. The treatments that will work are the ones that match the treatment to the mechanism, not the symptom category.

VNS is a flexible platform. It can be timed differently, combined with different behavioral therapies, calibrated to different intensities. It's a tool, not a single treatment. The question is always: what are you combining it with, when are you doing it, and in which patient?

**Andrew Huberman:** And the closing message: the brain is extraordinarily plastic. The conditions for plasticity — focus, friction, rest, reflection — are accessible to everyone without any intervention. The frontier of VNS and pharmacological tools is about amplifying that innate capacity, not replacing it.

**Michael Kilgard:** Exactly. The fundamentals work. Get enough sleep. Engage with difficult things. Pay attention. Reflect on what you've learned. Test yourself. Those four things will produce neuroplasticity throughout your entire life. The new tools are for when those fundamentals aren't enough — when the injury or the disease has created a deficit too large for the normal process to overcome. That's when you want the screwdriver in the toolbox.
