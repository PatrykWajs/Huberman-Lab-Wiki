## [00:07:16] Cannabis, THC, Cannabidiol (CBD), Terpenes

**Matthew Hill:** Cannabis is a plant that has been around for some time with a very rich history of use around the world — for medicinal, spiritual, and recreational purposes — over several centuries. In the west it really wasn't mainstream until about the 1960s, and then it became more prevalent through the rise of the hippie counterculture. More recently, from the 1990s onward, it has become a very heavily used drug by a large swath of people ranging from teenagers on up.

In terms of what's inside the plant, cannabis has a lot of very complex chemistry and biology. There are many molecules within it called cannabinoids, and the main one that drives the intoxicating and psychoactive effects is Delta-9 tetrahydrocannabinol — what we call THC. The amount of THC in the plant will influence how high a person gets when they consume it. There are probably 70 to 100-some-odd other cannabinoids within cannabis; most of them are at pretty trace levels and vary across different cannabis types. The other one that's had a lot of attention is cannabidiol, or CBD. CBD is structurally similar to THC but doesn't behave anything like THC — it's not intoxicating at all, and arguably not psychoactive in the traditional sense, although some people claim it can affect anxiety state or mood.

There are also things called terpenes, which are highly volatile compounds not specific to cannabis — they're found in tons of other plants. These contribute to the smell and flavors of cannabis (limonene giving a citrusy odor, pinene giving an earthy tree smell, beta-caryophyllene, myrcene, etc.). Some terpenes do have known biological activity. There's emerging work playing with giving someone THC plus one other terpene or minor cannabinoid and seeing how it influences things. Doing this piecewise could take decades to really understand all the interactive components of cannabis. People tend to refer to this as the "entourage effect" — the idea that pure, isolated THC might be very different from THC in combination with these other molecules, and how they might influence how THC itself is working.

## [00:12:08] Psychoactive Effects, Cannabis "High"; Time Perception

**Andrew Huberman:** Some people listening have experienced the psychoactive effects, others perhaps have not. How would we describe what the "high" is?

**Matthew Hill:** The way people would usually describe the intoxicating effects of cannabis is there being some euphoria or positive mood — not on the same order as what people would describe with cocaine or other stimulants, but certainly some kind of positive aspect. Cannabis can also produce changes in feeding behavior, finding things funnier, changing the way you perceive environmental stimuli, and for some people creating a bit of a dissociative state where they might feel a little bit out of body. It's kind of a complicated intoxicating state to describe. With a stimulant, people feel like they're God, very happy and jacked up. With cannabis it's more like an introspective state — you might be more aware of your bodily feelings, but you also have a different perspective on external stimuli, processing information and focusing on things differently. In laboratory studies of intoxication, researchers typically use a visual analog scale (0 to 100) and ask: do you feel high? Euphoric? Is your mood elevated? Placebo cannabis studies have shown that even if you give someone a placebo cannabis if they think they're getting cannabis, a lot of people still report feeling a bit high.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the effects of cannabis on time perception? There's a reputation that cannabis disrupts time perception, that people will think a long period of time has passed when in fact very little time has passed.

**Matthew Hill:** There definitely seems to be some temporal dilation where people think things have passed longer than they actually have. There's some older work in animals — possibly pigeons and rodents — looking at temporal ordering where animals are given cannabinoids and respond differently to timed behavioral tasks. It does seem to produce some state where there's an altered perception of time passing. I'm not super familiar with modern work on this, though anything I can think of is pretty old.

## [00:16:55] Cannabis & Brain, CB1 Receptor, Endocannabinoids

**Matthew Hill:** THC as a molecule exerts almost all its effects by acting at one receptor widely expressed throughout the brain — the cannabinoid type 1 receptor, or CB1. People tend to use a lock-and-key analogy: the receptor is a protein that sits on a cell, and a molecule like THC is the key that fits in that lock; when it activates the receptor it triggers some biological process in the neuron that changes its activity. THC acts on CB1 receptors, which are very widely expressed — outside of ion channels, the CB1 is one of the most, if not the most, widely expressed receptor in the brain.

The CB1 receptor didn't evolve in humans in the hopes that one day humans would find cannabis. There are molecules our body produces called endocannabinoids, and they are interesting because they don't behave like a normal neurotransmitter. In the typical anterograde direction, neuron A releases a transmitter that crosses the synapse and acts on neuron B (either exciting or inhibiting it). Endocannabinoids are made in neuron B on the post-synaptic side and then go backwards to act on neuron A to regulate how much transmitter is released — this is called retrograde signaling. In many ways it's like a thermostat model: if neuron A is dumping out glutamate (excitatory) and neuron B gets too excited, it releases endocannabinoids to go back and tell neuron A to stop driving it.

The primary physiological role of endocannabinoids is to maintain homeostasis — they keep everything in its happy place. That's probably why the CB1 receptor is so widely distributed: neurons can excite or inhibit each other, and you don't want runaway excitation (like epilepsy) or runaway inhibition. Endocannabinoids act as circuit breakers that gate how much is coming in. There's also a CB2 receptor, mostly on immune cells (macrophages and similar), playing a role in regulating inflammation — it's not really expressed in neurons of the brain in the same way.

Interestingly, CB1 receptors are found on virtually every single kind of neuron in the brain — except dopamine neurons. Dopamine neurons are basically the only neurons in the brain that don't, as far as we've been able to characterize to date, express cannabinoid receptors. That said, cannabinoids don't leave dopamine unaffected — in the ventral tegmental area (VTA), dopamine neurons are regulated by a lot of inhibitory neurons that keep them quiet. Those inhibitory neurons that regulate the dopamine neurons are very rich in cannabinoid receptors (similar to how mu-opioid receptors work for morphine or heroin). When cannabinoid receptors are activated, they turn off that inhibitory control, allowing dopamine neurons to move into burst-firing states with big dumps of dopamine. Whether or not that relates to the positive affect or the euphoria hasn't been cleanly demonstrated, but cannabinoids do have an influence on dopamine transmission — just indirectly.

## [00:26:19] Endocannabinoid Types: Anandamide, 2-AG

**Matthew Hill:** Within the endocannabinoid system, there are two primary endocannabinoids. Unlike serotonin or dopamine — which are single molecules that diversify at the level of receptors — cannabinoids have one receptor regulated by two molecules, so diversification happens more at the molecular level.

The first is anandamide, a funny name because it comes from the Sanskrit word "ananda" for bliss. Raphael Mechoulam, who discovered the molecule about 30-odd years ago in Israel, named it to reflect inner bliss. Mechoulam is also the grandfather of the whole cannabinoid field — he has a landmark paper from 1964 (reportedly published April 20th, 1964, which may be where "420" came from) in which he isolated THC, and then in the 1990s his lab isolated anandamide as the first endogenous molecule to activate the CB1 receptor. Anandamide is a high-affinity but low-efficacy agonist: very low levels are required to bind the receptor, but once it binds, its ability to stimulate a biological response caps out fairly quickly — it doesn't have a sledgehammer effect.

The second endocannabinoid, discovered shortly after (also by Mechoulam's lab and a Japanese group), is 2-arachidonoylglycerol or 2-AG. It has lower affinity for the receptor but once it binds, it evokes a very robust intracellular signaling response. Most people who study neurophysiology and record activity in neurons deal almost entirely with 2-AG. The working model is that anandamide may be more tonic (a stage-setter that keeps circuits at a steady-state, like the thermostat of a house), while 2-AG is more phasic — a pinch-hitter brought in to do heavy lifting. During something like a seizure, neurons dumping out huge amounts of neural activity will flood the system with 2-AG to say "we need to turn off this circuit very quickly." Most forms of synaptic plasticity — where you need to strengthen or weaken a synapse in response to a change in the environment or a learning event — are driven by 2-AG signaling.

## [00:33:46] "Munchies," Cannabis & Appetite

**Andrew Huberman:** One of the prototypical responses to consuming cannabis is the munchies. Is the mechanism known?

**Matthew Hill:** Cannabinoid receptors are very highly expressed in the feeding circuits in the hypothalamus. One thing cannabinoids can do is disinhibit AgRP neurons (appetite-promoting neurons) so they become more active, which drives food-seeking behavior. But there's also a huge reward component. Work by Steve Mercer and Kent Berridge showed that you can put anandamide into the nucleus accumbens and that also stimulates palatable food intake. A Japanese group published a fascinating paper showing that under the influence of a cannabinoid, stimulating sweet taste buds produced an enhanced response in the gustatory cortex — but not for salty, bitter, or sour tastes. So there's the ability to jack up how the brain processes sweet-tasting foods, alongside engaging reward circuitry and regulating hypothalamic neurons.

Research in my lab has become interested in this at a behavioral level. My lab found that if you pre-satiate animals and then get them high on a cannabis extract (using vape chambers modeled to match blood THC levels seen in human smokers, around 100 ng/mL), they will reinitiate eating — even working for food via progressive ratio tasks. One female rat would do 300 lever presses to get one sugar pellet while high. You can also take animals that have had food paired with a nauseant (conditioned taste aversion), devaluing that food, and under cannabis they will re-engage with eating it anyway. The mechanism appears to involve locking in the reward value of food so it doesn't decay despite satiety, and possibly overriding the orbitofrontal cortex's assessment of how much an organism wants to work for a reward.

Additionally, there's evidence that endocannabinoids can override leptin signaling. Leptin is released from fat tissue and acts through arcuate nucleus neurons to tell the brain to stop eating. One mechanism by which leptin suppresses feeding is actually by activating metabolism of endocannabinoids, causing their levels to decline — and as endocannabinoid function drops, the animal becomes less interested in eating. THC essentially tricks the brain into thinking it's been fasting, activating receptors that are normally activated following a fasting state, pushing organisms into food-seeking behavior because food has high reward value. That's why when people get stoned they don't want lettuce — they want high-calorie, high-carb, high-fat food.

## [00:44:06] THC & Anandamide, Pharmacology

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a way to increase endocannabinoid activity without ingesting THC?

**Matthew Hill:** Yes. THC itself isn't exactly like an endocannabinoid — it behaves differently. Anandamide is rapidly metabolized by an enzyme called FAAH (fatty acid amide hydrolase). There are FAAH inhibitors that have been developed, which prevent anandamide breakdown and thereby elevate its levels. Exercise is known to elevate endocannabinoids — the "runner's high" was previously attributed entirely to endorphins (opioids), but the current thinking is that it's substantially driven by endocannabinoid release. Physical activity raises anandamide levels. CBD also has some FAAH-inhibitory properties, which may partially explain its anxiolytic effects.

THC's pharmacology differs from endocannabinoids in a critical way: it's a very high-efficacy, long-lasting, non-selective agonist of CB1 and CB2 across the entire brain simultaneously. Endocannabinoids are made on demand at specific synapses in specific circuits at specific times — very precisely tuned. THC hits the CB1 receptor everywhere at once, and because it's not metabolized quickly (it's highly lipophilic and sequesters into fat tissue), it persists for hours. This is why the intoxicating state is so diffuse and prolonged compared to what endocannabinoids would naturally produce.

## [00:52:37] THC & CB1 Receptors, Intoxication & Appetite

**Matthew Hill:** The widespread and persistent activation of CB1 receptors by THC is what produces the intoxicating state — not that THC is a super-duper version of an endocannabinoid or that it's boosting endocannabinoid function, but rather that it's essentially carpet-bombing a system very finely tuned to do very specific things at very specific times. For feeding, think of it as: endocannabinoids in hypothalamic feeding circuits normally go up following fasting, regulating AgRP neurons to promote food seeking. THC activates those same receptors, tricking the brain into a fasting-like state, driving food intake even when you're already full.

The lack of CB1 receptors in brainstem regions that regulate cardiac and respiratory function is one of the things that most differentiates cannabis from opiates. With opioids, activating receptors in those cardiorespiratory brainstem centers depresses neural activity, which is why opioid overdose can be fatal (cessation of breathing). Because cannabinoid receptors don't really exist in those regions, there is no known case of someone dying from a pure cannabis overdose in the same manner — though people can certainly do dangerous things while intoxicated.

## [00:58:57] Cannabis & Focus, Memory

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think some people use cannabis because it allows them to focus better?

**Matthew Hill:** Honestly, this is tricky to speak to because I don't think there's good evidence for it either way. A lot of the work done has been more about acute memory tasks like working memory or recall, rather than explicitly studying focus. Anecdotally, many people report using it for focus, but I can't speak to that with evidence. The only thing I can say confidently is that acutely, while someone is intoxicated on cannabis, there are definitely short-term effects on memory processing — primarily deficits in recall or consolidation. The animal evidence for this is very compelling. In humans, most work suggests there are some short-term memory deficits during the intoxicated state. I have not seen very much compelling evidence of long-term persistent memory effects in adult users beyond the intoxication window, though this remains an active research area and I'd be careful about making strong claims in either direction.

## [01:04:09] Routes of Administration, Concentration, Cannabis Research

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about routes of administration — smoking versus vaping versus edibles — and how that affects the experience and the data.

**Matthew Hill:** Inhalation (smoking or vaping) produces very rapid absorption — THC hits the bloodstream within seconds and peak levels occur within a few minutes. Edibles (oral consumption) are processed through the gut, converted in the liver from delta-9 THC to 11-hydroxy-THC, which is more potent and crosses the blood-brain barrier more efficiently. Edibles take anywhere from 30 minutes to 2 hours to kick in, with highly variable onset, and the high lasts much longer — often 4 to 8 hours versus 1 to 3 hours with inhalation.

Cannabis research has faced major challenges. In the US, researchers couldn't use dispensary products for legal reasons; they had to use standardized cannabis from NIDA (National Institute on Drug Abuse), which was relatively low potency (about 4% THC) and didn't reflect what people were actually using on the street (often 20-30% THC). A group in Colorado — Kent Hutchinson, Angela Bryan, and Cinnamon Bidwell — created a clever solution called the "CannABIS van" or "canavan": a mobile research lab that would park outside someone's house. The participant would use their own product in their own home, then come into the van for blood draws and cognitive testing. This allowed studying real-world cannabis use without bringing illegal products into a formal laboratory.

## [01:15:12] Self-Regulation, Inhalation & THC, Tolerance; THC Concentrates

**Matthew Hill:** What the Colorado group found was fascinating: despite people now using cannabis that's 20-30% THC, their blood levels were the same as those achieved with the old low-potency NIDA cannabis — around 100 ng/mL. People are remarkably good at self-titrating when inhaling, because inhalation gives fast feedback within a couple of minutes. Even rats in self-administration models titrate similarly — they prefer medium-potency products and actually take fewer vape hits off high-potency products.

Where things fall apart is with concentrates — dabs, distillate pens, and other products that can reach 90-98% THC. Cannabis plants biologically max out at about 25-30% THC, but concentrates can be far higher. These cannot be effectively self-titrated because the sheer volume of THC from a single hit is overwhelming. The Colorado group found blood levels of 200-300 ng/mL in concentrate users. Ryan McLaughlin at Washington State pioneered vape chamber models in rats for self-administration of cannabis vapor, which was difficult because unlike cocaine or heroin, rodents found IV cannabinoids somewhat aversive — but when allowed to titrate via vapor, they would work for it similarly to other reinforcing drugs.

## [01:23:36] Addiction & Cannabis, Cannabis Use Disorder

**Andrew Huberman:** Is cannabis addictive?

**Matthew Hill:** Cannabis use disorder is a real clinical diagnosis, and it's more prevalent than many people assume. About 9% of people who ever try cannabis will develop cannabis use disorder (sometimes called cannabis addiction); that number rises to about 17% in daily users. For comparison, alcohol dependence occurs in about 15% of users, and nicotine dependence in roughly 30%. So cannabis sits somewhere in the middle of addictive liability among common recreational substances.

Cannabis use disorder is characterized by tolerance, withdrawal upon cessation, inability to control use despite wanting to, continued use despite negative consequences, and time spent obtaining and recovering from cannabis use. Withdrawal symptoms include irritability, insomnia, anxiety, decreased appetite, and restlessness. These are real, measurable physiological phenomena — not just someone being cranky because they miss getting high. The mechanism involves CB1 receptor downregulation and desensitization with chronic exposure; when cannabis is removed, the system is in a hypoactive state. Importantly, cannabis withdrawal is not medically dangerous (unlike alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal), but it is genuinely uncomfortable.

## [01:31:30] Cannabis Legalization & Use, Edibles & ER Visits

**Matthew Hill:** Legalization in Canada (October 2018) has changed things, though not necessarily in all the ways people feared or hoped. Looking at Canada, the biggest proportional increase in use has actually been in elderly communities — 55+ years, especially women — going from about 1-2% to around 8%, a four-fold increase, though raw numbers aren't huge. Young adults (20-24) have also seen increased use. Interestingly, teenage use (14-18 year olds) has remained relatively unchanged; if anything, some US states that legalized saw slight dips in teenage cannabis use. The feared dramatic increase in underage use hasn't materialized.

Canada legalized flower for a full year before edibles came online, providing a natural before-and-after comparison. Once edibles became available, there was a notable increase in unintentional pediatric consumption resulting in ER visits — children eating gummies and candies that looked like regular candy. This has been a real public health concern. There's also been a question of legality versus safety: people conflate legal with safe (alcohol being the perfect example of a highly harmful legal substance), and the normalization of cannabis through legalization has led some people to underestimate its risks.

## [01:36:48] Oral Consumption, Edibles, Dosing & Time Course

**Matthew Hill:** Edibles present unique dosing challenges. Because of the delayed onset (30-120+ minutes) and the conversion to 11-hydroxy-THC in the liver (which is more potent and has slightly different pharmacokinetics than inhaled THC), people frequently over-consume. The typical story: someone takes an edible, doesn't feel anything after an hour, takes more, then both doses hit simultaneously and they're overwhelmingly intoxicated. 11-Hydroxy-THC may also produce a somewhat different subjective experience than delta-9 THC — some people report edibles feeling more "body-heavy" or producing more dissociation.

Recommended starting doses for edibles are typically 2.5-5 mg THC for naive users. In recreational markets, products often come in 10 mg doses, which can be quite overwhelming for inexperienced users. Wait at least 2 hours before redosing. For medical users (e.g., cancer patients using cannabis for nausea and appetite stimulation), the controlled delivery of oral THC as dronabinol (Marinol) allows for standardized dosing.

## [01:41:12] Drug Testing & Cannabis, Exercise

**Andrew Huberman:** What do we know about drug testing and cannabis? Cannabis is fat-soluble and presumably stays in the system for a long time.

**Matthew Hill:** Correct — THC and its metabolites are highly lipophilic and sequester into fat tissue. After a single use, THC metabolites (primarily THC-COOH) can be detected in urine for about 3-4 days. In chronic daily users, detection can extend to 30 days or even longer after cessation because metabolites slowly release from fat stores. Blood THC itself (reflecting actual intoxication) clears relatively quickly — within hours. So urine tests for cannabis metabolites are very poor proxies for actual impairment and create serious problems for employment drug testing. This is a major policy challenge in the era of legalization: there's currently no reliable breathalyzer or roadside test that accurately measures cannabis impairment the way a breathalyzer measures alcohol impairment.

Regarding exercise: physical exercise is one of the clearest non-pharmacological ways to boost endocannabinoids. Moderate aerobic exercise (running, cycling) in the range of 30-60 minutes raises plasma anandamide levels substantially. This endocannabinoid elevation is thought to contribute significantly to the "runner's high" — now understood to be more endocannabinoid-driven than purely opioid-driven.

## [01:46:04] Cannabis & Hormones, Gynecomastia, Sperm Quality

**Andrew Huberman:** Does cannabis impact hormones? There are claims about gynecomastia (breast tissue development in men) and effects on sperm quality.

**Matthew Hill:** The evidence on cannabis and testosterone is somewhat mixed — there are enough studies to suggest you might see transient drops in testosterone from cannabis, but it seems relatively short-lived. A lot of these studies also find that baseline testosterone is already within a normal dynamic range, and homeostatic regulation kicks in. Testosterone fluctuates across the day anyway, and as long as you're within normal ranges there really shouldn't be behavioral effects like changes in sex drive or physiological changes. Gynecomastia purportedly occurs on a backdrop of elevated androgens — such as during puberty (when transient gynecomastia is already fairly common due to aromatization of excess androgens) or in someone on anabolic steroids. It may exacerbate existing androgen-driven gynecomastia rather than causing it de novo.

Sperm quality (motility, morphology) may be affected. In vitro and some animal data suggest potential negative effects, and human data is mixed but concerning enough that if someone is struggling with fertility, eliminating cannabis use is a reasonable recommendation. Most men probably aren't significantly impacted, but some individuals may be more sensitive for reasons we can't currently biomarker.

## [01:54:37] Cannabis & Pregnancy; Selling Recreational Cannabis

**Andrew Huberman:** Up to 15% of pregnant women in the US have used cannabis during pregnancy — is that number accurate?

**Matthew Hill:** I've seen numbers ranging from 2% to 20%, depending on whether you're using self-report or verified blood levels. The higher numbers can come from blood samples where THC is detected but the woman reported not using cannabis. Self-report tends to be lower. The effects on the developing fetus are concerning and an area of active research. The endocannabinoid system plays critical roles in neurodevelopment — including cell migration, proliferation, and differentiation. THC exposure during pregnancy can perturb these processes. Animal models show effects on brain development, anxiety-like behavior, and altered stress response in offspring. The human data is harder to disentangle because of confounds (co-use of tobacco, alcohol, socioeconomic factors), but there's enough signal that the recommendation is clear: no cannabis use during pregnancy. Cannabis use for morning sickness — sometimes recommended anecdotally or even by dispensary employees — is a real concern because some of the first trimester is when critical neurodevelopmental events occur.

## [02:04:07] Vaping

**Andrew Huberman:** What about vaping specifically? Is it safer than smoking?

**Matthew Hill:** The pulmonary risks of smoking cannabis are real (combustion products, carbon monoxide, tar) but the evidence for cannabis smoking causing the degree of lung cancer risk seen with tobacco is actually less clear, partly because cannabis smokers typically smoke far fewer "cigarettes" per day than tobacco smokers. Vaping avoids combustion products but the 2019 EVALI (e-cigarette or vaping product use-associated lung injury) crisis was primarily linked to vitamin E acetate used as a cutting agent in illicit THC vape cartridges — not to properly manufactured cannabis vape products. Legal market vaping products are likely safer than smoking in terms of pulmonary irritants, but concerns remain about the long-term effects of inhaling heated cannabis oil vapor.

## [02:07:05] Psychosis, Anxiety & Cannabis

**Andrew Huberman:** This is one of the areas where you criticized my earlier episode. Let's talk about the relationship between cannabis and psychosis.

**Matthew Hill:** Cannabis use can precipitate psychotic episodes, and this is well established in the literature. However, the story is more nuanced than "cannabis causes psychosis." Acute cannabis intoxication can produce psychosis-like symptoms in anyone at high enough doses — paranoia, perceptual distortions, disorganized thinking. These are acute and generally resolve when the THC clears. The more important and contested question is whether cannabis causes lasting psychotic illness.

The data clearly shows that cannabis users have a higher rate of schizophrenia diagnoses compared to non-users, and the relationship is dose-dependent (more frequent use, higher potency → higher risk). What's contested is the directionality and the genetics underlying this. One important observation: there are people with a genetic predisposition to schizophrenia who also use cannabis at higher rates — potentially because the cannabis is self-medicating prodromal symptoms like anxiety. This creates confounding in epidemiological data. What I take issue with is presenting the cannabis-psychosis relationship as straightforwardly causal when the population attributable risk is actually quite modest in most analyses.

## [02:17:17] Cannabis, Psychosis, Schizophrenia & Genetics

**Matthew Hill:** The genetic angle is crucial. There are specific genetic variants (particularly in the AKT1 gene and variants near certain dopamine system genes) that appear to moderate whether cannabis use increases psychosis risk. People with certain genotypes who are heavy cannabis users may have a substantially elevated risk; in people without those variants, the risk increase may be much smaller. Studies using Mendelian randomization — a technique that uses genetic variants as proxies for exposure to test causality — have found evidence supporting a causal effect of cannabis use on psychosis risk, though the effect sizes are modest at the population level.

The endocannabinoid system's relationship to psychosis is mechanistically plausible: CB1 receptor activation in the prefrontal cortex can disrupt inhibitory control of glutamate release, potentially leading to patterns of neural activity associated with psychotic symptoms. High-potency THC products amplify this risk by producing much higher CB1 receptor occupancy. The adolescent brain is particularly vulnerable — the prefrontal cortex is still developing through the mid-20s, and adolescent cannabis exposure in animal models produces lasting changes in prefrontal function and anxiety circuitry.

## [02:30:45] Cannabis Use & Schizophrenia, Manic Bipolar, THC Potency, Nicotine

**Matthew Hill:** In terms of population-level risk, estimates suggest that if you eliminated all cannabis use, you might prevent roughly 8-12% of schizophrenia cases — that's meaningful but not the majority of cases. Cannabis is neither a necessary nor sufficient cause of schizophrenia; it's a risk factor that interacts with genetic vulnerability. The relationship with bipolar disorder and manic episodes is also real but similarly complex — cannabis use tends to worsen the course of existing bipolar disorder and may precipitate manic episodes in vulnerable individuals.

THC potency matters significantly. High-potency products (>15% THC) are associated with substantially higher odds of psychosis compared to low-potency products. This is one area where the shift in the market toward ever-higher potency is a genuine public health concern. Nicotine complicates the picture: many cannabis users co-use tobacco or nicotine, and nicotine affects the endocannabinoid system and dopamine system in ways that can interact with cannabis effects. Disentangling cannabis effects from nicotine co-use effects is an ongoing methodological challenge.

## [02:40:37] Schizophrenia, Cannabis Legalization

**Matthew Hill:** With legalization, did we see the expected surge in schizophrenia rates? The data is still coming in and is complicated by things like improved detection and diagnosis. Some studies from countries that legalized earlier (like Canada, Denmark, and certain US states) have suggested modest increases in first-episode psychosis presentations, but this remains contested. What's clearer is that high-potency products, particularly concentrates used by young adults, are associated with higher emergency department presentations for cannabis-related psychosis. Whether legalization per se increases schizophrenia prevalence versus cannabis use disorder or acute intoxication-related harms is an important distinction that the data hasn't fully resolved.

## [02:45:06] Cannabis Strains, Indica, Sativa, Subjective Effects & Expectancy Bias

**Andrew Huberman:** This was another area of disagreement. Do indica and sativa strains actually produce different biological and subjective effects?

**Matthew Hill:** No — not in any biologically meaningful or consistent way. The indica versus sativa classification is a botanical distinction based on plant morphology (plant height, leaf shape, grow patterns) and geographic origin. It does not reliably predict THC content, terpene profile, or the subjective experience a given person will have. The cannabis industry and popular culture have built an entire mythology around it — "indicas are relaxing, sativas are energizing" — but this is not supported by the science.

Studies that have given people blinded cannabis products and asked them to report effects find that expectancy (what they were told they were getting) has as large or larger an effect on reported experience than the actual product. This is a massive expectancy bias / placebo effect situation. The terpene profile varies across products and could theoretically contribute to differential effects, but the evidence for terpenes alone producing meaningfully different subjective experiences in humans is very thin. The THC concentration is the dominant driver of the intensity of intoxication. If you want to predict what experience someone will have, knowing the THC content and the person's tolerance level is far more predictive than knowing the strain name.

## [02:57:00] CBD, Pediatric Epilepsy, Adenosine

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about CBD. What are the established effects of CBD?

**Matthew Hill:** The clearest and most evidence-based clinical application of CBD is in pediatric epilepsy — specifically Dravet syndrome and Lennox-Gastaut syndrome. CBD (as Epidiolex, an FDA-approved pharmaceutical) significantly reduces seizure frequency in these conditions. The mechanism is likely multi-factorial; one proposed mechanism involves adenosine signaling. CBD appears to inhibit the reuptake of adenosine (similar to how caffeine works but in the opposite direction — caffeine blocks adenosine receptors while CBD may prolong adenosine's action by slowing its clearance). Adenosine has anticonvulsant and anti-inflammatory properties. CBD also has effects at TRPV1 receptors (pain, inflammation) and GPR55 (sometimes called the "third cannabinoid receptor"), and has some weak FAAH inhibitory activity raising anandamide levels.

## [03:07:22] Entourage Effect; Placebo Effect, CBD & Doses

**Matthew Hill:** The entourage effect — the idea that whole-plant cannabis or CBD-rich extracts work better than isolated CBD — is plausible but poorly demonstrated for most claimed benefits. For the most common use cases people have for CBD (sleep, anxiety, general wellness), the evidence is surprisingly thin given how widely CBD products are sold. Many commercially available CBD products contain much less CBD than labeled, or contain trace amounts of THC.

The placebo effect in CBD research is enormous. Studies that have used proper double-blind designs with CBD versus placebo for anxiety often find the gap between CBD and placebo is smaller than expected. This doesn't mean CBD has no effects, but it does mean that a lot of what people attribute to CBD may be expectation and placebo. Doses matter enormously: studies showing effects on anxiety or sleep often use doses of 300-600 mg CBD — an order of magnitude higher than what's in typical over-the-counter products (usually 10-25 mg). The disconnect between effective research doses and consumer product doses is significant.

## [03:19:12] Cannabis Health Risks, Cardiovascular Risk, Schizophrenia

**Matthew Hill:** Cannabis carries real health risks beyond psychosis. Cardiovascular risk is increasingly recognized: acute cannabis use causes elevated heart rate (tachycardia) through autonomic effects. There are case reports and epidemiological data linking heavy cannabis use — particularly smoking — to increased risk of acute cardiovascular events like myocardial infarction in otherwise young and healthy individuals. The mechanism may involve sympathetic activation, reduced oxygen-carrying capacity from carbon monoxide (in smoked cannabis), and direct cannabinoid effects on cardiac function. The risk is probably small in absolute terms for most users but is non-trivial.

Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (CHS) is a condition characterized by cyclic vomiting in heavy, long-term cannabis users. It's paradoxical because cannabis is generally antiemetic, but with chronic heavy use the system appears to become desensitized and dysregulated. CHS is associated with compulsive hot showering (the heat provides temporary relief). It resolves upon cannabis cessation.

## [03:27:08] Cyclic Vomiting Syndrome & Hot Shower

**Matthew Hill:** Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (CHS) is characterized by recurrent bouts of severe nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramping, typically in people who have been heavy daily cannabis users for many years. The paradox is that cannabis is antiemetic (anti-nausea), but with heavy chronic use the cannabinoid receptors in the gut and brainstem become chronically desensitized, and the normal antiemetic effect is lost or inverted. The hot shower behavior is a distinctive hallmark: hot water appears to activate TRPV1 receptors in the skin, which can provide temporary relief from the nausea — probably via a temperature-mediated modulation of the same circuits involved in nausea regulation. CHS resolves completely upon sustained cannabis abstinence. Emergency department physicians increasingly recognize this condition as cannabis use has risen.

## [03:31:30] Cannabis Benefits: Pain, Stress, Anxiety, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the genuine medical benefits of cannabis?

**Matthew Hill:** The most robustly supported medical applications are: (1) chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting — THC (dronabinol) and CBD are effective antiemetics; (2) neuropathic pain — cannabis appears to provide meaningful relief in conditions like multiple sclerosis-related pain and HIV-associated neuropathy, where conventional analgesics often fail; (3) muscle spasticity in multiple sclerosis — Sativex (nabiximols, a THC:CBD 1:1 ratio mouth spray) is approved in many countries; (4) pediatric epilepsy via CBD (Epidiolex).

For PTSD, the evidence is more nuanced but increasingly compelling. The endocannabinoid system is heavily involved in fear extinction — the process by which conditioned fear responses are dampened over time. Anandamide in the amygdala and prefrontal cortex plays a key role in allowing fear memories to be extinguished, and PTSD is characterized by impaired fear extinction. Cannabis and THC can facilitate fear extinction in animal models, and clinical data suggests that cannabinoids may help with specific PTSD symptoms — particularly nightmares (through effects on REM sleep) and hyperarousal. Several clinical trials are ongoing.

## [03:40:18] Cannabis & Anxiety, Anandamide & Stress Response

**Matthew Hill:** The relationship between cannabis and anxiety is U-shaped and highly context-dependent. At low doses, THC can be anxiolytic for many people — reducing anxiety and promoting relaxation. At higher doses, particularly in inexperienced users or in high-stress situations, cannabis can precipitate or intensify anxiety, panic attacks, and paranoia. The anxiolytic effects of low-dose THC likely involve endocannabinoid signaling in the amygdala — CB1 receptor activation there reduces excitatory input and dampens fear responses.

Anandamide specifically has been shown to be critically involved in stress buffering. When we encounter a stressor, the endocannabinoid system is engaged to help terminate the stress response — anandamide levels in the prefrontal cortex and limbic system go up following stress and help return the HPA axis (cortisol response) to baseline. People with high levels of anandamide breakdown (high FAAH activity) may be more anxiety-prone and stress-reactive. This is one rationale for why some people use cannabis medicinally for anxiety and stress — it may be partially replacing or augmenting a deficient endocannabinoid tone.
