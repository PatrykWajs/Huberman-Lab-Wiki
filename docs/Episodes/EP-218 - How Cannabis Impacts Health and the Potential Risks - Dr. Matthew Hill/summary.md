# Episode 218: How Cannabis Impacts Health & the Potential Risks | Dr. Matthew Hill

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman sits down with Dr. Matthew Hill — professor of cell biology and anatomy at the University of Calgary and an expert on cannabis biology — after Dr. Hill publicly critiqued claims from Huberman's earlier solo cannabis episode on X (Twitter). The conversation is a comprehensive, evidence-grounded review of cannabis science covering the mechanisms of THC and CBD, the endocannabinoid system's retrograde homeostatic circuitry, why indica/sativa classifications are a botanical myth, the pharmacology of edibles versus inhalation versus concentrates, addiction and withdrawal, drug testing limitations, cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome, cardiovascular risks, and cannabis effects on hormones, pregnancy, psychosis, PTSD, and anxiety. A recurring theme is the gap between what the science robustly supports and what popular culture or the cannabis industry has amplified beyond the evidence — including the nuanced (rather than simply causal) link between cannabis and psychotic illness, the U-shaped dose-response relationship for anxiety, and the striking disconnect between commercially available CBD doses and those used in effective studies. The episode also models how respectful public scientific disagreement, followed by genuine dialogue, produces more accurate public understanding than any single authoritative voice.

## Key Takeaways

1. **THC is the primary psychoactive compound; CBD is not intoxicating.** Cannabis contains ~70-100 cannabinoids, but delta-9 THC drives intoxication via CB1 receptors. CBD is structurally similar but pharmacologically distinct — not intoxicating, with genuine clinical evidence only for pediatric epilepsy (Epidiolex). Consumer CBD doses (10-25 mg) are an order of magnitude below the 300-600 mg doses used in studies that show effects on anxiety or sleep. [00:07:16](transcript.md#000716-cannabis-thc-cannabidiol-cbd-terpenes)

2. **The endocannabinoid system is a retrograde homeostatic circuit.** Endocannabinoids (anandamide and 2-AG) are made in post-synaptic neurons and travel backward to regulate pre-synaptic transmitter release — acting as circuit breakers that maintain neural homeostasis. THC carpet-bombs this finely-tuned system by binding CB1 everywhere simultaneously and persistently, producing diffuse intoxication that no endocannabinoid would naturally create. [00:16:55](transcript.md#001655-cannabis--brain-cb1-receptor-endocannabinoids)

3. **Anandamide and 2-AG have distinct functional roles.** Anandamide is high-affinity, low-efficacy — a tonic stage-setter keeping circuits at steady-state. 2-AG is low-affinity but high-efficacy — a phasic heavy-hitter deployed during seizures or synaptic plasticity events. Most forms of synaptic plasticity are driven by 2-AG. THC bypasses this specificity entirely. [00:26:19](transcript.md#002619-endocannabinoid-types-anandamide-2-ag)

4. **Cannabis-induced munchies work through multiple overlapping mechanisms.** THC disinhibits AgRP neurons in the hypothalamus (promoting food-seeking), engages nucleus accumbens reward circuitry, and selectively amplifies the gustatory cortex response to sweet tastes only. It locks in the reward value of food so satiety signals — including leptin — are overridden, tricking the brain into a fasting-like state that drives craving for high-calorie, high-carb, high-fat food. [00:33:46](transcript.md#003346-munchies-cannabis--appetite)

5. **People self-titrate THC effectively when inhaling, but concentrates break that ability.** With smoked or vaped flower, experienced users converge on ~100 ng/mL blood THC regardless of product potency (20-30% vs. older 4% products). Concentrates (dabs, distillates at 90%+ THC) overwhelm self-regulation, producing 200-300 ng/mL blood levels and substantially higher risk of acute psychosis and adverse events. [01:15:12](transcript.md#011512-self-regulation-inhalation--thc-tolerance-thc-concentrates)

6. **Cannabis use disorder is real, affecting ~9% of ever-users and ~17% of daily users.** Defined by tolerance, withdrawal (irritability, insomnia, anxiety, appetite loss, restlessness), inability to control use, and continued use despite consequences. CB1 receptor downregulation and desensitization are the underlying mechanisms. Withdrawal is genuinely uncomfortable but not medically dangerous, unlike alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal. [01:23:36](transcript.md#012336-addiction--cannabis-cannabis-use-disorder)

7. **Edibles are pharmacologically distinct from inhaled cannabis and far harder to dose.** Oral THC converts to 11-hydroxy-THC in the liver — more potent, slower onset (30-120+ min), longer duration (4-8 hours), more variable, and producing a somewhat different subjective experience (more body-heavy, more dissociation). The most common cause of cannabis-related ER visits. After Canada legalized edibles separately from flower, unintentional pediatric ingestions via candy-like products rose significantly. Start with 2.5-5 mg THC; wait at least 2 hours before redosing. [01:36:48](transcript.md#013648-oral-consumption-edibles-dosing--time-course)

8. **Drug testing for cannabis is a poor proxy for impairment.** THC metabolites (primarily THC-COOH) persist in urine 3-4 days after a single use and up to 30 days in chronic daily users — well beyond any window of actual impairment. Blood THC clears within hours. No reliable roadside test exists to measure cannabis impairment accurately, creating major employment and traffic safety policy challenges in the legalization era. [01:41:12](transcript.md#014112-drug-testing--cannabis-exercise)

9. **Cannabis has modest but real effects on hormones and notable effects on sperm quality in some men.** Transient drops in testosterone may occur but stay within normal physiological ranges. Sperm motility and morphology may be negatively affected in a subset of men — men struggling with fertility should stop cannabis use. Gynecomastia appears to occur only on a backdrop of elevated androgens (puberty, anabolic steroid use), not as an isolated effect of cannabis. [01:46:04](transcript.md#014604-cannabis--hormones-gynecomastia-sperm-quality)

10. **Cannabis during pregnancy is a serious risk with no safe threshold established.** Estimates of use during pregnancy range from 2% to 20% depending on self-report vs. blood verification. The endocannabinoid system plays critical roles in fetal neurodevelopment (cell migration, proliferation, differentiation). Animal models show offspring effects on brain development, anxiety, and stress response. The recommendation is unambiguous: no cannabis use during pregnancy, including for morning sickness. [01:54:37](transcript.md#015437-cannabis--pregnancy-selling-recreational-cannabis)

11. **Cannabis carries real cardiovascular risk that is underappreciated.** Acute use causes tachycardia via autonomic effects. Case reports and epidemiological data link heavy use to increased myocardial infarction risk in otherwise young, healthy individuals. Mechanisms include sympathetic activation, reduced oxygen-carrying capacity from combustion carbon monoxide, and direct cannabinoid effects on cardiac function. [03:19:12](transcript.md#031912-cannabis-health-risks-cardiovascular-risk-schizophrenia)

12. **Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (CHS) is a paradoxical consequence of heavy chronic use.** Despite cannabis being antiemetic, years of heavy daily use desensitize CB1 receptors in the gut and brainstem, inverting the normal response and producing cyclic severe nausea and vomiting. The hallmark is compulsive hot showering — likely via TRPV1 receptor activation providing temporary relief. CHS resolves completely upon cannabis abstinence. [03:27:08](transcript.md#032708-cyclic-vomiting-syndrome--hot-shower)

13. **Indica vs. sativa is a botanical myth with no reliable biological basis.** The classification reflects plant morphology and geographic origin — it does not predict THC content, terpene profile, or subjective experience. Studies show expectancy (what users are told they're getting) has as large or larger an effect on reported experience as the actual product. THC concentration and individual tolerance are the dominant predictors of intoxication. [02:45:06](transcript.md#024506-cannabis-strains-indica-sativa-subjective-effects--expectancy-bias)

14. **Cannabis increases psychosis risk but the relationship is nuanced, not simply causal.** Acute high-dose THC produces psychosis-like symptoms in anyone; the contested question is lasting psychotic illness. Risk is real, dose-dependent, and moderated by genetic variants (e.g., AKT1). People genetically predisposed to schizophrenia may self-medicate prodromal symptoms with cannabis, creating confounding in epidemiological data. Eliminating all cannabis use might prevent roughly 8-12% of schizophrenia cases. Adolescents and high-potency-product users carry the highest risk. [02:07:05](transcript.md#020705-psychosis-anxiety--cannabis)

15. **Genuine medical benefits are narrower than popular culture suggests.** Robustly supported: chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting (dronabinol), neuropathic pain (especially MS and HIV neuropathy), muscle spasticity in MS (Sativex), and pediatric epilepsy (Epidiolex/CBD). Emerging and compelling evidence supports cannabis for PTSD — particularly nightmares and hyperarousal — via endocannabinoid involvement in fear extinction. [03:31:30](transcript.md#033130-cannabis-benefits-pain-stress-anxiety-post-traumatic-stress-disorder-ptsd)

16. **The anxiety-cannabis relationship is dose-dependent and U-shaped.** Low-dose THC is anxiolytic for many users via CB1 activation in the amygdala, reducing fear circuit excitability. High-dose THC — especially in inexperienced users or novel settings — precipitates anxiety, panic, and paranoia. Chronic stress depletes anandamide; some people use cannabis to augment deficient endocannabinoid tone, partially explaining therapeutic self-medication patterns. [03:40:18](transcript.md#034018-cannabis--anxiety-anandamide--stress-response)

## Tools & Protocols

### Self-Titrating Cannabis Dose (Inhalation)
- **What:** Using inhalation's rapid pharmacokinetic feedback to control effective THC dose.
- **How:** Inhale one puff, wait 2-5 minutes to assess effects, then decide whether to inhale more. Experienced users naturally converge on ~100 ng/mL blood THC regardless of product potency.
- **When:** Any inhalation-based use. Self-regulation breaks down completely with high-potency concentrates (dabs, 90%+ distillates) — do not attempt to self-titrate these.
- **Ref:** [01:15:12](transcript.md#011512-self-regulation-inhalation--thc-tolerance-thc-concentrates)

### Edible Dosing Protocol
- **What:** Avoiding over-intoxication from oral cannabis.
- **How:** Start with 2.5-5 mg THC maximum. Wait a minimum of 2 hours before any additional dose. Never redose because you "don't feel anything yet" — both doses will hit simultaneously.
- **When:** Any oral cannabis consumption, especially for naive or infrequent users. Standard recreational products (10 mg doses) can be overwhelming for inexperienced users.
- **Ref:** [01:36:48](transcript.md#013648-oral-consumption-edibles-dosing--time-course)

### Exercise to Boost Endocannabinoids Naturally
- **What:** Non-pharmacological elevation of anandamide levels for stress buffering and mood.
- **How:** Moderate aerobic exercise (running, cycling) for 30-60+ minutes substantially elevates plasma anandamide. This is now understood to drive the "runner's high" more than endorphins/opioids.
- **When:** As a tool for stress buffering, mood elevation, and anxiety management without cannabis.
- **Ref:** [01:41:12](transcript.md#014112-drug-testing--cannabis-exercise)

### Cannabis Abstinence for Fertility Concerns
- **What:** Eliminating a potential contributor to reduced sperm quality.
- **How:** Completely stop cannabis use (both inhaled and edible forms) if trying to conceive and experiencing challenges. Allow at least 30-60 days for THC metabolites to clear from fat stores and for sperm parameters to normalize.
- **When:** When male factor infertility is suspected or confirmed, or proactively when fertility is a goal.
- **Ref:** [01:46:04](transcript.md#014604-cannabis--hormones-gynecomastia-sperm-quality)

### CHS Recognition & Cessation
- **What:** Identifying and resolving cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome.
- **How:** If a heavy long-term daily cannabis user presents with cyclic severe nausea/vomiting and compulsive hot showering, suspect CHS. The only effective treatment is sustained cannabis abstinence — the condition fully resolves upon cessation.
- **When:** Heavy daily cannabis use lasting years; symptoms are paradoxical given that cannabis normally reduces nausea.
- **Ref:** [03:27:08](transcript.md#032708-cyclic-vomiting-syndrome--hot-shower)
