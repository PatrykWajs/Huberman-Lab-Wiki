# Episode 37: ADHD & How Anyone Can Improve Their Focus

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman provides a comprehensive breakdown of ADHD — its genetics, neurobiology, and the dopamine-driven imbalance between the default mode network and task-related networks that underlies both the disorder and everyday focus failures. He covers the full diagnostic picture including hyper-focus, working memory deficits, time-perception problems, spatial disorganization, and the "pile system," then explains how low dopamine causes correlated (rather than anti-correlated) firing between default mode and task networks. The treatment landscape spans prescription stimulants (Adderall, Ritalin, Modafinil/Armodafinil), dietary interventions (elimination diets, omega-3s, phosphatidylserine), and over-the-counter nootropics (Alpha-GPC, L-Tyrosine, Racetams/Noopept, Ginkgo biloba). Crucially, Huberman details actionable behavioral protocols — including a 17-minute seated interoception exercise, panoramic vision/open monitoring, visual focus fixation training, and physical movement before focus tasks — that improve attention in both ADHD and non-ADHD individuals of any age. The episode closes with a warning that smartphone use is inducing ADHD-like attentional deficits in the general population and discusses emerging TMS-based treatments.

## Key Takeaways

1. **ADHD is strongly genetic but unrelated to IQ.** Concordance in identical twins reaches ~75%; fraternal twins 50–60%; parent-child ~10–25%. Having a close relative with ADHD greatly raises probability, but intelligence of any kind — standard IQ, emotional, spatial — has no relationship to ADHD. [00:07:56](transcript.md#000756-adhd-vs-add-genetics-iq-rates-in-kids--adults)

2. **ADHD is a dopamine-deficit disorder at the circuit level.** Low dopamine in prefrontal circuits causes the default mode network and task-directed networks to fire together (correlated) rather than in opposition (anti-correlated), producing mind-wandering instead of sustained focus. Brain imaging confirms that when ADHD improves — through treatment or aging — anti-correlation is restored. [00:26:40](transcript.md#002640-neural-circuits-in-adhd-default-mode-network--task-related-networks)

3. **Hyper-focus is the flip side of ADHD inattention.** People with ADHD can sustain intense focus on intrinsically rewarding activities because those activities spike dopamine enough to momentarily balance the circuit. This proves the capacity to focus is present — the deficit is in engaging it for non-preferred tasks. [00:14:57](transcript.md#001457-hyper-focus)

4. **ADHD phenotype includes time perception, working memory, and spatial organization deficits.** Low dopamine causes underestimation of elapsed time, explaining chronic lateness. Working memory — keeping information online for seconds to minutes — is severely impaired. Physical space is organized via a "pile system" that makes sense to the individual but is functionally ineffective. [00:16:45](transcript.md#001645-time-perception)

5. **Stimulants work by raising dopamine, not by sedating kids.** Adderall (amphetamine + dextroamphetamine) and Ritalin (methylphenidate) increase dopamine and norepinephrine, restoring anti-correlation between default mode and task networks. Because children's brains are highly plastic, drug-induced focus also teaches them what focus feels like, producing lasting circuit-level changes — which is why early treatment matters most. [00:49:18](transcript.md#004918-how-stimulants-teach-the-brains-of-adhd-children-to-focus)

6. **Attentional blinks explain ADHD distractibility.** When the brain locks onto one target it briefly "blinks" and misses immediately subsequent targets. People with ADHD have far more attentional blinks than neurotypical individuals — meaning they are over-focusing on certain elements and therefore missing others, not simply failing to focus at all. [01:10:50](transcript.md#011050-attentional-blinks)

7. **A single 17-minute interoception session can durably improve focus.** Sitting quietly with eyes closed, attending to breathing and bodily sensations for ~17 minutes, significantly and near-permanently reduces attentional blinks after just one session. The same practice may also offset age-related attentional decline. [01:16:56](transcript.md#011656-open-monitoring--17-minute-focus-enhancement)

8. **Panoramic vision (open monitoring) eliminates attentional blinks in real time.** Deliberately dilating the gaze to take in the full visual field activates a separate high-frame-rate neural stream, allowing the brain to detect multiple targets in sequence without attentional blinks. Works immediately and every time, with or without prior training. [01:16:56](transcript.md#011656-open-monitoring--17-minute-focus-enhancement)

9. **Blinking rate is a real-time proxy for dopamine and focus.** Dopamine suppresses spontaneous eye blinks; all stimulants (Adderall, Ritalin, caffeine) measurably reduce blink rate. Each blink resets time perception — low dopamine causes underestimation of elapsed time, directly explaining why ADHD individuals are chronically late and poor at scheduling. [01:22:50](transcript.md#012250-blinking-dopamine--time-perception--focus-training)

10. **Visual focus fixation training translates to cognitive focus.** Having students hold visual attention on near, mid-distance, and far objects for 30–60 seconds each (with minimal blinking), preceded by brief physical movement, significantly improved their ability to focus on other types of information. [01:22:50](transcript.md#012250-blinking-dopamine--time-perception--focus-training)

11. **Physical movement before focus tasks reduces reverberatory neural activity.** Kids and adults have ongoing premotor "reverberatory" commands to move; allowing movement (or a fidget outlet like a rubber band on a desk) channels this activity and enables sustained stillness and cognitive attention. Subtle foot-tapping or knee-bouncing during focus tasks also works, including for steady hands during surgery. [01:30:10](transcript.md#013010-reverberatory-neural--physical-activity)

12. **Omega-3 (≥1 g EPA/day; ≥300 mg DHA/day) plus phosphatidylserine synergistically support attention.** EPA modulates dopamine availability; 300 mg DHA/day is the attentional inflection point. Adding 200 mg/day phosphatidylserine (studied in children ages 1–12 across double-blind trials up to 147 subjects) produces additional synergistic improvement and may allow lower medication doses. [01:48:05](transcript.md#014805-dha-fatty-acids-phosphatidylserine)

13. **Elimination diets can dramatically reduce ADHD symptoms.** The landmark Pelsser et al. 2011 Lancet RCT (100 children, p < 0.0001) found removing foods to which children had mild antibodies produced highly significant improvements in focus, impulsivity, and motor control. All four neurologists/psychiatrists Huberman consulted agreed that eliminating simple sugars is the most important first dietary step. [00:56:35](transcript.md#005635-elimination-diets--allergies-in-adhd)

14. **Smartphones impose ADHD-like deficits on everyone.** A 2014 population study of 7,102 adolescents found that more than 60 minutes/day of smartphone use produced significant attentional deficits. For adults the estimated limit is ~2 hours/day. Constant context-switching trains the brain into short attentional bouts, degrading capacity for sustained focus. [02:09:14](transcript.md#020914-smart-phones--adhd--sub-clinical-focus-issues-in-adults--kids)

15. **TMS offers a drug-free path to rewiring prefrontal focus circuits.** Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation directed at prefrontal cortex circuits for task-directed behavior is being tested in active clinical trials alongside pharmacological treatments, combined with focused learning tasks to capitalize on neuroplasticity. [02:05:15](transcript.md#020515-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation-combining-technology--pharmacology)

16. **Cannabis reduces blinking and increases some aspects of focus, but impairs memory.** Chronic cannabis users show dramatically reduced spontaneous blink rates (correlating with years of use), suggesting dopamine-cannabinoid interactions. Cannabis increases dopamine while also activating serotonin and opioid systems, creating an alert-but-mellow state — but the well-known memory-impairing effects mean users focus on things they cannot recall. [01:35:00](transcript.md#013500-cannabis)

## Tools & Protocols

### 17-Minute Interoception Session (Attentional Blink Reduction)

- **What:** A seated, eyes-closed practice of attending to internal bodily sensations — breathing, heartbeat, skin contact — without directing the mind to any specific external task or formal meditation object. Distinct from open monitoring (panoramic vision) and from focused-attention meditation.
- **How:** Sit quietly for ~17 minutes. Close eyes. Attend to breathing and bodily sensations. When the mind drifts to external thoughts, gently return attention to internal sensations.
- **When:** Just one session produces near-permanent reduction in attentional blinks. Can be repeated daily or several times per week. Applicable across all ages, with or without ADHD.
- **Ref:** [01:16:56](transcript.md#011656-open-monitoring--17-minute-focus-enhancement)

### Panoramic Vision / Open Monitoring

- **What:** Deliberately dilating the visual field to take in the full surrounding environment — ceiling, floor, periphery — rather than fixating on a single point. Engages a separate high-frame-rate neural stream that eliminates attentional blinks in real time.
- **How:** Consciously widen your gaze without moving your eyes. Allow peripheral vision to fill in the entire scene. Practice transitioning between narrow soda-straw focus and wide panoramic mode.
- **When:** Use immediately before or during tasks requiring detection of multiple pieces of information in sequence. Works on first use, every use.
- **Ref:** [01:16:56](transcript.md#011656-open-monitoring--17-minute-focus-enhancement)

### Visual Focus Fixation Training

- **What:** Deliberate practice of holding visual gaze on targets at progressively greater distances with minimal blinking, strengthening the neural circuits shared with cognitive attention.
- **How:** First allow brief physical movement to discharge reverberatory motor activity. Then: hold gaze on a nearby object (e.g., hand) for ~30–60 seconds with minimal blinking; shift to a mid-distance target; then a far target. A few minutes daily.
- **When:** Before cognitively demanding work or study. Best preceded by physical movement.
- **Ref:** [01:22:50](transcript.md#012250-blinking-dopamine--time-perception--focus-training)

### Physical Movement Before Focus Tasks

- **What:** Brief physical activity prior to cognitive tasks to discharge reverberatory premotor activity, enabling both children and adults to sit still and attend. Fidget toys (e.g., rubber bands on desk) serve the same function for children in the classroom.
- **How:** Allow or encourage several minutes of free movement before sitting down to focus. During sustained focus tasks, subtle foot tapping or knee bouncing can channel ongoing premotor activity without disrupting mental attention.
- **When:** Before school or study sessions; before visual focus training; anytime inability to sit still is interfering with cognitive attention.
- **Ref:** [01:30:10](transcript.md#013010-reverberatory-neural--physical-activity)

### Elimination Diet (ADHD-focused)

- **What:** Removing simple sugars first, then foods to which the individual has mild allergic antibodies (oligoantigenic diet), to reduce interference with prefrontal dopamine signalling.
- **How:** Eliminate simple sugars as an immediate first step. Optionally proceed to allergy testing and remove personal allergens. Reintroduce one food at a time and monitor symptom changes. Work with a physician or dietitian.
- **When:** Most relevant for children with ADHD. Effects can be large enough to reduce or eliminate medication need in some individuals. Ongoing — diet effects change as allergies change over time.
- **Ref:** [00:56:35](transcript.md#005635-elimination-diets--allergies-in-adhd)

### Omega-3 Supplementation (EPA + DHA)

- **What:** Fish oil providing ≥1 g EPA/day and ≥300 mg DHA/day to modulate dopamine availability and reduce neuroinflammation. Modulatory (not mediating) effect on focus.
- **How:** Take with a fat-containing meal for absorption. Liquid fish oil is most cost-efficient; capsules are acceptable. Ensure DHA is above 300 mg/day for attentional effects.
- **When:** Daily, ongoing. Can be used alongside prescription medication and may allow lower medication doses.
- **Ref:** [01:04:46](transcript.md#010446-omega-3-fatty-acids-epas--dhas)

### Phosphatidylserine

- **What:** A phospholipid that supports neuronal membrane integrity and acetylcholine/dopamine signalling. Synergistic with omega-3s for ADHD.
- **How:** 200 mg/day. Take with omega-3s for enhanced effect. Studied over 1–6 months in children ages 1–12 in double-blind trials (up to 147 subjects).
- **When:** Daily use. Primarily studied in children with ADHD; adult data limited.
- **Ref:** [01:48:05](transcript.md#014805-dha-fatty-acids-phosphatidylserine)

### Alpha-GPC

- **What:** A choline precursor that raises brain acetylcholine via both the nucleus basalis (targeted) and pedunculopontine nucleus (diffuse) pathways, sharpening focus and cognition.
- **How:** 300–600 mg before demanding cognitive work (typical range); up to 1200 mg/day studied for age-related cognitive decline. Do not combine with anticholinergic medications.
- **When:** As needed for focus-demanding work. Consult a physician before regular high-dose use.
- **Ref:** [01:56:19](transcript.md#015619-acetylcholine-circuits-underlying-focus-alpha-gpc)

### L-Tyrosine

- **What:** An amino acid precursor to dopamine and norepinephrine. Modulates catecholamine levels; most useful when dopamine is depleted. Approach with caution if any mood disorder is present.
- **How:** 100–1200 mg (wide individual variation; titrate carefully). Take on an empty stomach before demanding work.
- **When:** Situationally — before high-demand cognitive sessions or during stress/sleep deprivation. Not a substitute for prescription agents in clinical ADHD.
- **Ref:** [01:59:04](transcript.md#015904-l-tyrosine-pea-phenylethylamine)

### Modafinil / Armodafinil

- **What:** Prescription wakefulness agents that weakly inhibit dopamine reuptake and activate the orexin/hypocretin system. Milder dopaminergic profile than Adderall/Ritalin. Armodafinil is substantially less expensive.
- **How:** Taken in the morning; prescription-only. Avoid afternoon dosing to prevent sleep disruption.
- **When:** For narcolepsy, shift work, or off-label cognitive enhancement under physician supervision.
- **Ref:** [01:51:45](transcript.md#015145-modafinil--armodafanil-dopamine-action--orexin)

### Racetams / Noopept

- **What:** Compounds that modulate the cholinergic (acetylcholine) system — and some AMPA receptors — improving learning and memory consolidation. Noopept (10 mg twice daily) is a more potent analogue. Legal status varies by country; sold OTC in the US.
- **How:** Take with a choline source (e.g., Alpha-GPC). Cycle use; monitor for side effects. Consult a physician, especially with ADHD.
- **When:** For cognitive enhancement, particularly in post-concussion or vascular brain injury contexts. Emerging evidence.
- **Ref:** [02:01:23](transcript.md#020123-racetams-noopept)

### Ginkgo Biloba

- **What:** A plant extract with minor evidence for reducing ADHD symptoms, but significant vasoconstriction/vasodilation variability and headache risk in some individuals (including Huberman).
- **How:** Use with caution; headaches are a known adverse effect. Consult a physician before use.
- **When:** Low evidence base compared to other options; consider only after other dietary and supplemental approaches.
- **Ref:** [01:50:54](transcript.md#015054-ginko-biloba)

### Smartphone / Technology Restriction

- **What:** Deliberate daily limits on smartphone use to protect attentional capacity.
- **How:** Adolescents: ≤60 minutes/day. Adults: ≤2 hours/day. Use phone-free blocks for deep work. Keep phone away from study space and bedroom.
- **When:** Continuously — especially during work, study, and family time. Critical for adolescents whose attentional circuits are still developing.
- **Ref:** [02:09:14](transcript.md#020914-smart-phones--adhd--sub-clinical-focus-issues-in-adults--kids)
