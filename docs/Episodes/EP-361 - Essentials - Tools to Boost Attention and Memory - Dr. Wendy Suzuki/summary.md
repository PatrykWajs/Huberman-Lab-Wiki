# Episode 361: Essentials: Tools to Boost Attention & Memory | Dr. Wendy Suzuki

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Wendy Suzuki, professor of neural science and psychology at New York University, joins Andrew Huberman to discuss evidence-based, daily habits that improve focus, memory, and overall cognitive performance. Dr. Suzuki explains the neuroscience of what makes events memorable, how the hippocampus functions as a hub for memory and imagination, and how aerobic exercise directly grows the hippocampus via BDNF. She presents her own peer-reviewed research on minimum effective doses of exercise for cognitive gains in adults aged 30-50, describes the dual-pathway mechanism by which exercise triggers BDNF release, and outlines three core tools — exercise, meditation, and sleep — that anyone can use immediately to upgrade their attentional capacity.

## Key Takeaways

- Four factors make memories stick: novelty, repetition, association, and emotional resonance. The amygdala and hippocampus interact to encode emotionally significant events particularly efficiently.
- The hippocampus is not only essential for forming new long-term memories — it is also the brain's primary associative engine for imagination and future planning. Damage to it impairs both recall and the ability to mentally simulate novel scenarios.
- One-trial learning evolved as a survival mechanism: a single high-emotion event can permanently stamp a memory, bypassing the usual repetition requirement.
- Every bout of aerobic exercise releases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which directly promotes the growth of new neurons and synaptic connections in the hippocampus — building a structurally larger, more resilient brain over time.
- BDNF is released via two pathways: (1) irisin — a protein secreted by contracting muscles that crosses the blood-brain barrier; (2) beta-hydroxybutyrate — a ketone released by the liver under the metabolic stress of exercise that also crosses the blood-brain barrier.
- A single 30-45 minute aerobic exercise session produces immediate cognitive benefits that last up to two hours: improved mood, better prefrontal function (Stroop task), faster reaction time, reduced anxiety, depression, and hostility.
- In low-fit adults aged 30-55, just two to three cardio sessions per week for three months significantly improved hippocampal memory (recognition and spatial episodic tasks) and prefrontal performance, and increased motivation to keep exercising.
- For mid-fit adults, every incremental increase in weekly exercise sessions — up to seven per week — produced proportionally better hippocampal memory and mood, demonstrating a dose-response relationship.
- A Swedish longitudinal study (2018) of 300 women tracked over 40 years found that high-fit women in their 40s gained an average of nine more years of good cognitive function in later life compared to low-fit peers.
- Just 10 minutes of walking shifts mood via dopamine, serotonin, and noradrenaline. This is the minimum threshold for mood effects; hippocampal and prefrontal benefits require sustained cardiovascular effort.
- Eight weeks of daily 12-minute body scan meditation significantly reduced stress reactivity (Trier Social Stress Test), improved mood, and boosted cognitive performance. Meditation trains the capacity to focus on the present moment — a skill with broad daily applicability.
- The top three tools to improve attention are: (1) aerobic exercise — direct effect on prefrontal cortex function; (2) meditation — trains present-moment focus; (3) sleep — foundational for all core cognitive functions.

## Tools & Protocols

### Aerobic Exercise (Primary Cognitive Tool)
**What:** Any cardiovascular activity that elevates heart rate — running, cycling, kickboxing, power walking, video workouts, spin class.
**How:** 30-45 minutes per session (including 5-minute warm-up and cool-down, so ~35 minutes at working intensity). Aim for two to three sessions per week minimum (low-fit baseline); up to daily for maximum hippocampal and mood benefits.
**When:** Morning is optimal — the cognitive boost (improved focus, memory, reduced anxiety) lasts up to two hours, so exercising before your most cognitively demanding work maximizes carry-over. However, any time of day provides benefits.
**Ref:** Dr. Suzuki's published lab research; Swedish Women Longitudinal Study (2018).

### 10-Minute Walk (Minimum Mood Tool)
**What:** A brisk 10-minute walk outdoors or indoors.
**How:** Walk continuously for at least 10 minutes. No special equipment or heart-rate target required.
**When:** Any time mood or mental energy needs a quick reset.
**Ref:** Multiple studies cited by Dr. Suzuki demonstrating mood-shifting neurochemical effects at this minimal dose.

### Morning Exercise Protocol
**What:** Perform your aerobic session first thing in the morning before work or study.
**How:** Complete 30-45 minutes of cardiovascular exercise, then move directly into demanding cognitive tasks within the two-hour benefit window.
**When:** Daily or as close to daily as schedule allows.
**Ref:** Dr. Suzuki's unpublished study on acute exercise effects across age groups 20-90s; published lab data on two-hour duration of acute cognitive benefit.

### Brief Daily Meditation (Body Scan)
**What:** A guided 10-12 minute body scan meditation — attending sequentially to physical sensations throughout the body without judgment.
**How:** Use any guided body-scan audio (app or recording). Practice daily for at least eight weeks to see statistically significant stress-reduction and cognitive gains. The key skill being trained is deliberate present-moment focus.
**When:** Any consistent daily slot; morning or pre-work recommended to prime attentional control for the day.
**Ref:** Dr. Suzuki's published study (8-week, 12-min/day body scan) showing reduced Trier stress test reactivity and improved mood and cognition.

### Positive Affirmations / IntenSati
**What:** Pairing intentional physical movement with out-loud spoken positive affirmations (e.g., "I am strong now," "I believe I will succeed"). Based on the IntenSati method developed by Patricia Moreno.
**How:** During exercise (or independently), vocalize positive self-directed statements timed to physical movements. Statements should be personally meaningful and future-oriented.
**When:** Integrated into exercise sessions, or as a standalone brief practice.
**Ref:** Published literature on affirmations improving mood; Dr. Suzuki's personal and clinical experience with IntenSati.

### Sleep (Foundational Cognitive Tool)
**What:** Consistent, sufficient nightly sleep.
**How:** Prioritize sleep quantity and quality as a non-negotiable cognitive performance lever; it underpins the benefits of exercise and meditation.
**When:** Every night.
**Ref:** Dr. Suzuki's summary of broad sleep-cognition literature; cited as the third essential tool for attention alongside exercise and meditation.

## Resources Mentioned

- **IntenSati** — Exercise method combining kickboxing, dance, yoga, and martial arts with spoken affirmations, developed by Patricia Moreno: https://www.intensati.com
- **Patient HM (Henry Molaison)** — Landmark 1957 neurological case demonstrating the hippocampus is essential for forming new declarative memories. Surgery in 1953-1954; foundational paper by Scoville & Milner (1957): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/13406589/
- **Rusty Gage / Salk Institute Neurogenesis Study** — Evidence for adult human neurogenesis in the hippocampus using BrdU labeling in terminally ill patients: https://www.salk.edu/scientist/fred-gage/
- **Swedish Women Longitudinal Study (2018)** — Tracked 300 women over 40 years; high-fit women in their 40s gained 9 more years of cognitive function: Hörder et al., *Neurology*, 2018: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29669688/
- **Trier Social Stress Test (TSST)** — Standardized laboratory stressor used to measure stress reactivity in Dr. Suzuki's meditation study: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5314087/
- **Stroop Task** — Classic cognitive test measuring prefrontal-dependent attentional control and interference suppression: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroop_effect
- **Eriksen Flanker Task** — Cognitive task measuring selective attention and response inhibition, dependent on prefrontal cortex: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eriksen_flanker_task
