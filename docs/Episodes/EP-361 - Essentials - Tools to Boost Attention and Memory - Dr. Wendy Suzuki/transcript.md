## [00:15:55] How Exercise Increases BDNF

**Dr. Wendy Suzuki:** two pathways that have been studied about how you go from moving your body to more BDNF — that neurotrophic factor that's increasing the growth of new hippocampal brain cells. The two pathways are the following.

One is irisin — a protein released by the muscles. In studies done with rats on running wheels, they showed that running rats had more of this irisin release. The irisin passed the blood-brain barrier — getting into the very protected bloodstream inside the brain — and that irisin stimulated the release of BDNF in the brain. That's pathway number one.

Pathway number two comes through the liver. Exercise is a stress generally — cortisol is released whenever we exercise. There is a ketone, beta-hydroxybutyrate, that has long been known to get released by the liver during exercise. We also know that that particular ketone passes the blood-brain barrier and it's another stimulant for BDNF. So the final common pathway seems to be BDNF stimulation in the hippocampus. It comes from all of our physiological systems — our muscles working, our liver responding to the stress of exercise — and it is giving more BDNF precursors to get into our brain to cause the upspike of BDNF, which is part of your bubble bath that you're getting every time you move.

## [00:20:03] Exercise Effects on Memory, Tool: Morning Exercise

**Dr. Wendy Suzuki:** Absolutely. Let me start with the immediate effects — acute effects — of exercise on the brain. This is asking what does a one-off exercise session do for your brain? There are three major effects that have been reproduced. I've seen it in my lab. Many labs have reproduced this. This is usually an aerobic exercise session, 30 to 45 minutes. ## [00:25:08] Minimum Exercise For Cognitive Benefits (Adults, 30s-50s)

**Dr. Wendy Suzuki:** When I jumped into the exercise work, everybody was studying people 65 or older because that's when cognitive decline begins. However, I thought, what happens in people in their 40s and their 50s, maybe even their 30s and their 20s? Because that's when we as humans are ready, willing, and able to increase our exercise and get set up to build our brains as we go into our 60s.

The first study that I did looked at low-fit participants from their 30s to mid-50s. We wanted to ask: how much exercise do you really need to start seeing benefits? No heart rate change, but they had to come into my lab and be in a group just like they were for the spin class.

What we found was that two to three times a week of cardio in these people — who were low fit, meaning exercising less than 30 minutes a week in the three months previous to the experiment — showed: baseline rates of their positive mood states went up relative to the video Scrabble group. Their body image got more positive. And really important, their motivation to exercise went up significantly. So the more you exercise, the more motivated you are to exercise.

What about cognition? Number one, we got improved performance on the Stroop task. But we were headed toward the hippocampus. What we found was improved performance on both a recognition memory task — can you differentiate similar items that we're asking you to remember — and a spatial episodic memory task where we had them navigate a doom-like virtual city. Their performance got better, which is very classically dependent on the hippocampus.

So I can now say in 30 to 50-year-olds that are low fit: two to three times a week. Is that doable? Absolutely. This is not like you have to become a marathon runner. You have to start moving your body on a regular basis, two to three times a week.
