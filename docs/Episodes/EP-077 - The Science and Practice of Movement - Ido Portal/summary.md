# Episode 77: The Science & Practice of Movement | Ido Portal

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman sits down with Ido Portal — widely regarded as the world's foremost expert on human movement — to explore the science and philosophy of movement practice. The conversation spans the neuroscience of motor systems (Sherrington's "final common path," Bernstein's degrees of freedom, the pre-motor humming system), the relationship between movement, emotion, and thought as three braided streams of experience, and the evolutionary connection between song, dance, and language uncovered by Erich Jarvis. Portal introduces his distinctions between lower-case "movements" (containers/techniques) and upper-case "Movement" (content/experience), and between big-frame and small-frame movement — the latter being where emotional residue and trauma are stored in tissue and where modern physical culture is almost entirely absent. Practical protocols covered include the 30-minute daily squat accumulation challenge, panoramic/open-gaze training, spinal wave practice, peripersonal space and reactivity training, and injecting variability into existing linear exercise routines. The episode closes on visualization, collective vs. self-knowledge, and Portal's philosophy that the ultimate practitioner is both wild and wise.

## Guest: Ido Portal

Ido Portal is an Israeli movement practitioner, teacher, and philosopher who has spent decades synthesizing capoeira, martial arts, gymnastics, dance, and other disciplines into a unified approach he calls the Ido Portal Method. He has trained world-class athletes including Conor McGregor and is considered an n-of-1 in the movement world — a true practitioner-intellectual who operates at the highest levels of both physical practice and conceptual understanding. ## Key Takeaways

1. **Movement is the final common path.** Sherrington's insight that so much of the nervous system is dedicated to movement applies uniquely to humans — we generate a greater variety of movement types and speeds than any other animal. [00:07:49](transcript.md#000749-what-is-movement)

2. **Three streams of movement braid together.** Action, emotion, and thought are all forms of movement that interlay into a braided experience. A genuine movement practice engages all three, not just physical execution. [00:10:56](transcript.md#001056-movement--the-body-mind-connection)

3. **Movements (containers) vs. Movement (content).** Lower-case "movements" are the containers — techniques and postures. Upper-case "Movement" is the content — the living, integrative experience. Most people chew on the cup instead of drinking the water. [00:14:47](transcript.md#001447-entry-points-to-movement)

4. **Movement education starts with awareness, play, and examination.** Entry into movement practice is through education — bringing awareness to the fact that one lives in a body, in motion, with emotions that are themselves movements. Playfulness and non-verbal experience are primary tools. [00:18:08](transcript.md#001808-early-education-in-movement-awareness-play--examination)

5. **The body, crowded streets, and a bag are all you need.** Portal spent two-hour sessions walking the dense streets of Hong Kong with a bag, avoiding touching anyone, as a full movement practice. The more refined the practitioner, the simpler the tools required. [00:21:19](transcript.md#002119-stillness-movement--the-environment-playfulness)

6. **Early specialization forecloses emotional and movement postures for life.** Unique postures — physical, emotional, intellectual — are formed early; later life is integration of those postures. Overspecialization narrows the alphabet of movement available to a person for their entire life. [00:31:34](transcript.md#003134-unique-postures-types-of-movement-contents-vs-containers)

7. **Discomfort is a marker, not a problem.** The frustration of a failed motor attempt is exactly what primes the forebrain for neuroplasticity. Leaning out of practice at the moment of failure is leaning out of learning. [00:40:50](transcript.md#004050-discomfort-marker-of-movement-failures--learning)

8. **The 30-minute squat accumulation challenge.** The squat is a fundamental resting position eliminated from modern life. Accumulating 30 minutes per day — not straight, but throughout the day — addresses digestive issues, lower back pain, hip pain, and the general loss of body foldability. [00:47:05](transcript.md#004705-movement-diversity-squat-challenge-injury-movement-evolution)

9. **Spinal waves unlock small-frame movement and discharge stored emotion.** Dorsal, ventral, side-to-side, rotational, and spiraling undulations of the spine mobilize what big-frame movement cannot reach. Emotional discharges, trauma releases, and defragging of overspecialized athletes are common results. [01:02:04](transcript.md#010204-core-movement-emotion--memory-spinal-waves-evolution)

10. **Song, dance, and complex language share an evolutionary root.** Erich Jarvis's research shows every species with elaborate song also has the capacity to dance. Singing likely preceded articulated speech. Movement — including the sub-vocal muscular movement that occurs when we read — is the substrate of language itself. [01:12:39](transcript.md#011239-song-dance--complex-language-movement-as-language-consilience)

11. **The pre-motor system is always humming.** Most movement is not instructed — it is permitted. The pre-motor system generates patterns constantly; the act of moving is the release of a gate. Skilled movement is not more control, it is better permission. [01:26:36](transcript.md#012636-potential-for-movement-humming)

12. **Degrees of freedom increase with mastery, not decrease.** Bernstein's finding: the accuracy of the sledgehammer's head increased even as variability of the arm joints increased. The system found a meta-pattern — a dynamic sleeve — not a fixed technique. Virtuosity is the invitation of variability back in. [01:32:18](transcript.md#013218-instructiveness-vs-permissiveness-degrees-of-freedom)

13. **Panoramic/open vision is four times faster than narrow focus.** The magnocellular visual pathway — activated in peripheral/panoramic awareness — transmits information at least four times faster than the narrowly focused parvocellular pathway. Modern life pushes almost everyone into near-constant narrow focus, contributing to myopia in children and slower reactivity. [01:38:06](transcript.md#013806-vision--movement-focus--awareness-panoramic-awareness)

14. **Walking gaits are an emotional and communicative practice.** Approaching with chin slightly down and linear sends one signal; approaching rounded and angled from the side sends another. Coordinating breathing with the gait is more efficient; pronation "errors" in elite distance runners show that practitioner exploration beats biomechanical orthodoxy. [01:52:43](transcript.md#015243-walking-gaits)

15. **Touch deprivation is a major undiscussed health crisis.** Adults need touch as much as children. Controlled re-exposure to uncomfortable physical proximity — through grappling, contact improvisation, partner dance, or everyday consensual touch — reduces reactivity and expands the richness of available responses. [02:03:05](transcript.md#020305-reactivity--personal-space-touch--proximity-to-others-play--discomfort)

16. **Visualization is only useful for the very experienced.** Without rich physical experience and external feedback, visualization produces fabrications and potentially stronger delusions. Only practitioners with already-developed sensory experience can use mental rehearsal productively. [02:18:13](transcript.md#021813-visualization--experience-feedback)

## Tools & Protocols

### 30-Minute Daily Squat Accumulation

- **What:** Accumulate 30 minutes per day in an unloaded resting squat position.
- **How:** Not 30 minutes straight — accumulated throughout the day. No specific technique required; the goal is to rest in the position, not to perform a "correct" squat. Use a gradual build-up period if pain is present.
- **When:** Throughout the day — while reading, watching video, or resting.
- **Ref:** [00:47:05](transcript.md#004705-movement-diversity-squat-challenge-injury-movement-evolution)

### Panoramic / Open-Gaze Practice

- **What:** Deliberately shift from narrow focus to soft, peripheral, panoramic vision during movement and daily life.
- **How:** Look at a vista or distant point; allow vision to become soft and non-focused; notice peripheral movement without tracking it. Practice the shift between narrow focus and open awareness intentionally throughout the day.
- **When:** Throughout the day; specifically at the start of a movement session. Take breaks from screens to look at distant vistas.
- **Ref:** [01:38:06](transcript.md#013806-vision--movement-focus--awareness-panoramic-awareness)

### Spinal Wave Practice

- **What:** Introduce segmental, small-frame movement into the torso — dorsal, ventral, side-to-side, rotational, and spiraling undulations of the spine.
- **How:** Best learned through structured instruction (Ido Portal Method); Portal uses dozens of systems of torso movement before letting students improvise. Start with barely-perceptible internal mobility before adding large-frame motion. Avoid settling into one pattern.
- **When:** As a dedicated practice segment; also useful to defragment highly specialized athletes.
- **Ref:** [01:02:04](transcript.md#010204-core-movement-emotion--memory-spinal-waves-evolution)

### Movement Investigation of Existing Exercise

- **What:** Inject variability and examination into linear exercises (weight training, running, yoga).
- **How:** Change foot position, head posture, eye state (open/closed), breathing pattern, and emotional state during standard exercises. Keep changing — do not add one variable and keep it. The examination itself is the practice. Try the same movement with a smile vs. a frown; with breath-holding; with eyes closed.
- **When:** During existing workout routines; start with one small change per session.
- **Ref:** [02:20:14](transcript.md#022014-linear-movement--movement-investigation-examination)

### Peripersonal Space & Reactivity Training

- **What:** Deliberately practice being in close physical proximity to others with different body dynamics.
- **How:** Engage in partner-based practices — contact improvisation, grappling, partner dance — where participants navigate each other's peripersonal space without a fixed rule set. The goal is reducing reactivity and expanding the richness of available responses.
- **When:** In structured practice contexts with consenting partners; communicate openly before and during.
- **Ref:** [02:03:05](transcript.md#020305-reactivity--personal-space-touch--proximity-to-others-play--discomfort)

### Walking as Movement Practice

- **What:** Use walking itself as an intentional, variable movement practice.
- **How:** Try different walks — chin slightly down and linear for efficiency; rounded and angled for openness. Vary gait, coordinate breathing with steps, vary level of pelvic involvement. Observe how different walks affect communication and emotional tone.
- **When:** Any walk; particularly valuable before social or professional encounters.
- **Ref:** [01:52:43](transcript.md#015243-walking-gaits)

### Crowded Environment Movement Practice

- **What:** Move through a crowded urban environment with a bag and the goal of avoiding touching anyone.
- **How:** Strap on a bag; walk dense urban streets for an extended period (Portal did 2-hour sessions in Hong Kong). Engage fully in body awareness — navigating around people, up and down escalators, through narrow spaces.
- **When:** Any time access to a gym or nature is unavailable; excellent travel practice.
- **Ref:** [00:21:19](transcript.md#002119-stillness-movement--the-environment-playfulness)
