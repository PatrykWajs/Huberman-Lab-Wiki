## [00:00:00] Introducing Fear & Trauma

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we're going to talk about the neuroscience of fear, trauma, and post-traumatic stress disorders.

## [00:00:17] What is Fear?

In the last 10 years, the field of neuroscience has shed light on not just the neural circuits that control the fear response, but also important ways to extinguish fears using behavioral therapies, drug therapies, and brain-machine interfaces. Today we are going to talk about all of those and you are going to come away with both an understanding of the biology of fear and trauma as well as many practical tools to confront fear and trauma. To give you a sense of where we are going, I'll just lay out the framework for today's podcast — first, the biology of fear and trauma.

## [00:01:03] Autonomic Arousal: "Alertness" vs. "Calmness"

Literally, the cells, circuits, connections, and chemicals in the body that give rise to the so-called fear response — and why sometimes, but not always, fear can turn into trauma. I will also describe the biology of how fear is unlearned, or what we call extinguished. You're going to learn that we can't just eliminate fears; we actually have to replace fears with a new positive event. Fear falls into a category of nervous system phenomenon that we can reliably call an emotion. Emotions include responses within our body — quickening of heart rate, changes in blood flow, a warming or cooling of our skin — but there's also a cognitive component involving thoughts, memories, and all sorts of stuff that together we call an emotion.

## [00:02:05] Fear vs. Stress & Anxiety

Most people are familiar with stress both as a concept and as an experience. Stress is a physiological response, and we cannot have fear without having several if not all of the elements of the stress response — however, we can have stress without having fear. Anxiety tends to be stress about some future event; we can't really have fear without observing some elements of anxiety, but we can have anxiety without fear. Fear is built up from basic elements that include stress and anxiety, and then there is trauma: when a fear somehow gets embedded in our nervous system and shows up at times when it's maladaptive.

The operational definition of trauma is that some fear — including stress and anxiety — gets reactivated at various times when it doesn't serve us well. To simplify: we can focus on two issues, fear and trauma, as they relate to specific biological and cognitive processes, and we can dissect how fears are formed, how fears are unformed, and how new memories can replace previously fearful experiences. This brings us to autonomic arousal and the autonomic nervous system, which has two branches: the sympathetic branch (alerting) and the parasympathetic branch (calming), acting as a seesaw to adjust overall alertness.

One of the main aspects of the autonomic nervous system is the HPA axis — the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. The hypothalamus is a collection of neurons deep in the brain that controls temperature, appetite, thirst, and sexual desire; it connects to the pituitary, which releases hormones into the bloodstream. The adrenal glands, sitting above the kidneys, release adrenaline (epinephrine) and cortisol — both so-called stress hormones that are also involved in waking up from sleep. This three-part HPA axis uses the brain to alert and prepare the body for action in both the short term (fast hormone release) and the long term (a slow tail that feeds back to gene expression and builds new circuits). One hallmark of fear and trauma is that fear responses are long-lasting, even when the triggering event is very brief.

## [00:07:51] "The Threat Reflex": Neural Circuits for Fear

We can't discuss fear without the famous amygdala — an almond-shaped structure on both sides of the brain that is the final common pathway through which the threat reflex flows. The amygdala is part of the amygdaloid complex with 12–14 areas that integrate information from memory systems like the hippocampus and sensory systems (vision, audition, taste, touch) into the lateral portion of the complex. There are two main output pathways: one to the hypothalamus and adrenals (triggering alertness and action), and another to dopamine systems including the nucleus accumbens and mesolimbic reward pathway.

This threat center can communicate with and activate the dopamine system — and later you'll realize why that's crucial for wiring in new memories to replace fearful ones. A fourth component involves the prefrontal cortex and its top-down processing: the ability to control or suppress a reflex, to attach narrative, meaning, and purpose to what is otherwise a generic response. There's no negotiating what fear feels like — there's only negotiating what it means, whether you persist, pause, or retreat.

Pavlovian conditioning is the mechanism by which memories get attached to the fear system. If you pair a conditioned stimulus (bell) with an unconditioned stimulus (food) enough times, the conditioned stimulus alone triggers the response. In fear circuits, unlike Pavlov's dogs, you don't need many pairings — you can get one-trial learning. The system is set up to create memories and anticipate problems: a single embarrassing piano recital can lead to avoidance of public performance, while one car break-in in San Francisco can create a lifelong aversion to the entire city.

The fear response can be learned through isolated incidents that generalize broadly, or through accumulated experiences that funnel into a very specific fear. The key insight is that much of the fear system is a memory system, designed to embed memories of previous experiences so the threat reflex is activated in anticipation of danger. Some memories are protective; others are maladaptive and limit healthy behavior.

## [00:17:49] Cognitive (Narrative) Therapies for Fear

Contrary to popular belief, it does not work to simply extinguish a fear — one needs to extinguish the fear and replace that fearful or traumatic memory or response with a positive response. The primary behavioral therapies that use language alone and have shown very strong positive impact are: prolonged exposure therapy, cognitive processing therapy (CPT), and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT). What's been measured is that the first detailed recounting of a trauma evokes an anxiety response sometimes as great or greater than the original event, but with each subsequent retelling the amplitude of the physiological response becomes progressively diminished.

Every clinician emphasized the same point: a detailed recounting of traumatic and fearful events in complete sentences, in full rich detail — including how one felt inside before, during, and after — is absolutely essential to obtaining positive effects from prolonged exposure, CPT, or CBT. Part one is diminishing the old experience; part two is the essential need to relearn a new narrative. That relearning taps the outputs of the fear circuits to dopamine areas — allowing the circuits underlying trauma to be mapped onto new experiences with positive associations, all through narrative and cognition.

Regular social connection — trusting social connection of any kind — is also very beneficial for processing fear and trauma. The prefrontal cortex's capacity to create meaning and attach purpose to reflexive responses is a powerful tool. For those working through fear and trauma without access to licensed clinicians, journaling and trusted conversation can serve as informal versions of narrative-based therapy.

## [00:23:34] PTSD Treatments: Ketamine, MDMA, Oxytocin

Ketamine is a dissociative anesthetic that appears to allow patients to recount their trauma while feeling none or a very different set of emotions than those experienced during the original event. In this way, ketamine-assisted psychotherapy brings together the two key elements: diminishing (extinguishing) the intensity of the original trauma through dissociation, and automatically building in a relearning of a new narrative. Many clinics across the US now offer it; it appears particularly beneficial for trauma coupled with depressive symptoms, where the data on ketamine and depression is quite strong.

MDMA — also known as ecstasy or molly — is a powerful synthetic drug that simultaneously produces very large increases in both dopamine and serotonin, a state not normally seen under any other conditions. Dopamine is associated with pursuing and seeking; serotonin with pleasure and contentment. People in the therapeutic setting report immense feelings of connection with people, music, and objects. MDMA-assisted psychotherapy seems to allow very fast relearning and new positive associations to be attached to the previously traumatic experience — again following the same model of extinction followed by relearning a new narrative.

The common thread across all these approaches is the same two-step model: first reduce the potency of the old traumatic experience, then replace it with a new narrative. Most conventional drug treatments have tried to simply suppress internal arousal — but a more promising direction is working with arousal rather than against it. When the threat system gets ramped up so that even a memory or unconscious association triggers the full fear response, recalibration requires actively engaging that system.

## [00:28:30] Deliberate Brief Stress Can Erase Fears & Trauma

Ongoing research has been having subjects perform breathing protocols called cyclic hyperventilation — five minutes a day of deliberate stress. The protocol involves deep inhale through the nose, full exhale through the mouth, repeated for every 25–30 breaths, followed by a full exhale and breath hold with lungs empty for 25–60 seconds, then resuming until five minutes is complete. Subjects report and data confirm a heightened level of autonomic arousal: heating up, perspiring, wide-eyed agitation — that's adrenaline being released into the system.

The hypothesis is that deliberately inducing a physiological state of stress, potentially in conjunction with recounting the traumatic or fearful circumstance, may help extinguish fear by training the system that stress can be consciously generated and survived. This approach is far different from ketamine- or MDMA-assisted psychotherapy or purely narrative-based therapy, but shares the underlying principle of engaging (rather than suppressing) the fear circuits. The key advantage is cost: it's low-cost or even zero-cost, and can be combined with journaling or re-exposure to the fearful narrative.

This remains an experimental approach, and people with anxiety or panic disorders should be very cautious. Ideally it should be done with clinician support. However, many people dealing with trauma are seeking self-directed approaches, and deliberate brief stress exposure is a promising direction for those who need it.

## [00:31:10] Nutrition, Sleep, & Other General Support Erasing Fear & Trauma

Several lifestyle and supplementation factors can support the process of overcoming fear and trauma. The foundational elements are quality nutrition and ample, regular quality sleep — these underpin all mental and physical health. Two supplements are worth highlighting specifically in the context of anxiety, stress, and fear. The first is saffron: 12 studies show that 30 mg of orally ingested saffron reliably reduces anxiety on standard inventories (such as the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale), with significant effects in both male and female subjects including double-blind trials and a meta-analysis of its anxiolytic effects.

The second is inositol: 18 grams taken daily for a full month has been shown to produce a notable decrease in anxiety symptoms on par in potency with many prescription antidepressants. It takes time for these effects to emerge. The important timing caveat: you would not want to take anxiolytic supplements during or before a session where you are deliberately amplifying the intensity of re-exposure in order to extinguish the experience — that would undermine the therapeutic mechanism. These supplements are better used outside of those sessions to help bring the system back to baseline.

## [00:33:38] Recap

Today we reviewed the biology of the pathways in the brain and body that underlie the fear response and give rise to chronic fear, trauma, and PTSD. We covered a large variety of approaches — behavioral therapies (prolonged exposure, CPT, CBT), drug-assisted psychotherapy (ketamine, MDMA), deliberate stress protocols, and nutritional supports. Most important is understanding the logical structure of the circuits underlying fear and PTSD, because that understanding allows each person to evaluate which treatments and approaches make the most sense for them.

The core principle: to extinguish a fear, you must diminish the old fear response through repeated detailed re-exposure, then replace it with a new positive narrative — and that second step is just as essential as the first. Whether through professional therapy, self-directed journaling, breathing protocols, or pharmacological assistance, the fear system is not fixed — it is plastic and can be recalibrated. There are great practitioners who can help, and there are also self-directed practices that are reasonable for those carrying fears and traumas that don't rise to the level of clinical intervention.
