# Episode 178: Protocols to Access Creative Energy and Process | Rick Rubin

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

This is a Q&A episode in which Rick Rubin — world-renowned music producer and author of *The Creative Act: A Way of Being* — answers questions submitted by Huberman Lab listeners after his first appearance on the podcast. The episode is structured around practical specifics: Rick's daily routine from waking to sleep, how he sources and captures ideas, his tools for raising HRV, meditation practices ranging from TM to metta, and the dietary and lifestyle choices that underpin his creative life. Topics include coherence breathing, morning sunlight, red-light exposure after dark, wired/air-tube headphones (and Rick's first-person account of Bluetooth-related lymph node swellings), writer's block, uncertainty and anxiety in the creative process, dream journaling, the unconscious mind, how Rick evaluates ideas that the industry told him were wrong (every single major collaboration), his sobriety from alcohol and curiosity about psychedelics, the philosophy of devotional creative work versus outcome-chasing, and the role relationships, organization, and chaos each play in sustaining long-term creativity. Huberman deliberately restrains himself to give Rick maximum airtime, and the episode ends with reflections on what makes podcasting and intimate conversation effective.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Coherence breathing raises HRV measurably.** Rick began coherence breathing specifically to raise his heart rate variability, which he said was relatively low. He counts cycles (six breaths per minute) to track time without a timer. At twenty minutes daily the improvement in HRV scores became clearly noticeable. [00:06:27](transcript.md#000627-tool-coherence-breathing-heart-rate-variability)

2. **TM is the baseline; sessions evolve organically.** Rick learned Transcendental Meditation at age fourteen and has used it for nearly forty-five years. Sessions often shift from TM into coherence breathing, a gratitude practice, or metta (loving-kindness) phrases. He once meditated the entire duration of a cross-country flight. [00:11:45](transcript.md#001145-tool-meditation-practices)

3. **Meditation's goal is to change how you are in the world, not just how you feel during the sit.** Rick compares it to physical exercise: the elevated state during practice produces lasting adaptive changes in daily life — better reactions, more openness, more presence. Every session is a deposit in a bank that is always there. [00:11:45](transcript.md#001145-tool-meditation-practices)

4. **Morning sunlight on exposed skin anchors circadian rhythm.** Rick goes outside "as naked as possible" immediately after waking and does not wear sunglasses. He described correcting a sunglasses-wearing, fully-covered dermatologist on the beach who suffered from insomnia; the dermatologist examined Rick's back and declared he had perfect skin. [00:15:43](transcript.md#001543-sunlight-skin-circadian-rhythm)

5. **Bluetooth headphones caused recurrent lymph node swellings behind Rick's ears; he switched to wired air-tube headphones.** The swellings would drain lymph when large enough. The correlation was direct — swellings appeared with Bluetooth use and stopped when he stopped. Air-tube designs have no electrical component near the head at all. [00:20:00](transcript.md#002000-headphones-natural-living-diet)

6. **Writer's block comes from judgment, not absence of ideas.** Rick identifies two roots: (1) "I'm not good enough" — a confidence issue, and (2) "Nobody will like what I make" — fear of external judgment. Both dissolve when you treat every piece as a diary entry made for yourself alone. A diary entry cannot be judged by anyone else because it is your experience. [00:28:34](transcript.md#002834-tool-writers-block-creativity-diary-entries-deadlines)

7. **Deadlines help at the end of a project, not the beginning.** Deadlines undermine the freedom necessary at the open, exploratory start of a creative project. Once the code is cracked and the piece is essentially complete, a deadline can be useful for the final details. [00:28:34](transcript.md#002834-tool-writers-block-creativity-diary-entries-deadlines)

8. **Uncertainty and anxiety coexist; comfort with uncertainty is structural acceptance, not emotional absence.** Rick is comfortable with uncertainty as the nature of creative work, but he still feels anxiety at the start of every new project because he cares about the outcome. He is comfortable because he accepts that the work unfolds on its own timeline and will not be released until he feels good about it. [00:35:54](transcript.md#003554-uncertainty-creativity-challenges-sensitivity-environment)

9. **Extreme sensory sensitivity is the source of both artistic greatness and painful overwhelm.** Rick wears red glasses indoors and lives a protected, monk-like life because overstimulation interferes with his access to his inner landscape. He connects this to his book's observation that many artists self-medicate because the sensitivity that makes them artists also makes ordinary existence painful. [00:35:54](transcript.md#003554-uncertainty-creativity-challenges-sensitivity-environment)

10. **Creative work is a devotional practice; outcome is completely outside the creator's control.** The only job of the creator is to make the best thing they can. Everything after completion — reception, commercial success, criticism — is out of their hands. Any thought about outcome during the making undermines the whole enterprise. [00:48:51](transcript.md#004851-creative-endeavors-outcome-surprise-in-oneself-experimentation)

11. **Ideas have their own calendar; Rick never abandons them.** Ideas that stall are simply not ready. He keeps many balls in the air simultaneously and works where momentum exists, following the universe's support rather than fighting against it. If something is impenetrable, he moves to another project. [01:01:39](transcript.md#010139-source-of-ideas-internet-information)

12. **Dream journals are read retrospectively, not interpreted in the moment.** Rick has kept dream journals and found that apparently surreal images, reviewed years later, clearly pointed at real emotional circumstances of that period. He does not force meaning on waking — he collects and reads the journal over long intervals. Huberman added that Paul Conti believes the unconscious controls more of the dialogue in dreams and is constantly trying to teach us things in the way we learn best. [01:08:31](transcript.md#010831-dreams-interpretation-unconscious-mind-motivations-art-outcome)

13. **Rick's handwritten diary goes back to high school; entries run one to eight pages and start all-caps, then shift to cursive.** He journals before engaging with the day. The morning of this recording, Rick journaled about the previous night's wrestling show, marveling at how the great experiences of his life all create the same feeling — "there are gems and I'm finding them." [01:14:07](transcript.md#011407-career-advice-book-writing-diary-entries-expressive-writing)

14. **Every major Rick Rubin collaboration was rejected by the industry before it succeeded.** LL Cool J, Beastie Boys, Slayer, Ghetto Boys, Johnny Cash, Adele, Eminem — every single one. His only filter is: if I like it, someone else might. [01:25:21](transcript.md#012521-audience-innovative-ideas)

15. **Rick has never consumed alcohol or cannabis and is curious about psychedelics.** He attributes his resistance to peer pressure partly to being an only child and to always knowing clearly what he wants. He also argues that for people who think substances help creativity, the real mechanism is often confidence and permission-to-be-weird. He expressed curiosity about psilocybin and ibogaine. [01:29:35](transcript.md#012935-alcohol-confidence-psychedelics)

16. **Morning routine: sunlight → slow waking → movement → focused work 11 AM–6 PM → red-light evenings → bed by 10 PM.** Rick wakes, goes into the sun as exposed as possible, eases slowly into wakefulness, then roughly an hour to an hour and a half later does a long beach walk or gym session, sometimes preceded by stretching with foam rollers or balls. Work begins no earlier than 11 AM (sometimes 1 PM). From sunset onward: red glasses, no screens, wind-down with family. In bed by 10 PM, asleep within fifteen minutes. [01:49:55](transcript.md#014955-daily-routine-red-light-circadian-rhythm-cheap-photons)

## Tools & Protocols

### Coherence Breathing for HRV

- **What:** Slow, rhythmic breathing at six breaths per minute — roughly a five-second inhale followed by a five-second exhale — practiced to measurably raise heart rate variability.
- **How:** Eyes closed, follow a guided audio track (see Ref). On the first cycle, match the sound pattern to learn which tone corresponds to inhale vs. exhale. Rick counts cycles (six per minute) to track duration without a separate timer — e.g. "1-1, 1-2, 1-3, 1-4, 1-5, 1-6, 2-1…" He has combined it with treading water (practicing after getting out of the pool, sitting in the sun).
- **When:** At least once daily; aim for ten minutes minimum. Twenty minutes a day produces noticeable HRV improvement. Rick sometimes practices immediately after waking and again later in the day.
- **Ref:** [TAKE A DEEP BREATH (coherence breathing audio)](https://youtu.be/i5apnLrzaT4?feature=shared&t=728) — [00:06:27](transcript.md#000627-tool-coherence-breathing-heart-rate-variability)

---

### Transcendental Meditation + Evolved Practice

- **What:** TM is a mantra-based silent meditation. Rick learned it at age fourteen and uses it as his daily baseline. Sessions frequently evolve into coherence breathing, a gratitude practice, or metta (loving-kindness) phrases.
- **How:** Start with TM. Allow the session to evolve naturally. For metta, repeat these four phrases silently on a loop: "May I be filled with loving-kindness. May I be well. May I be peaceful and at ease. May I be happy." After roughly one year of daily practice, shift "May I" to "May we" for your immediate family. After another year, expand to your community. After roughly five years, expand to the planet.
- **When:** Long flights, first thing in the morning, any time sustained inward attention is possible.
- **Ref:** [Jack Kornfield — Meditation on Lovingkindness](https://jackkornfield.com/meditation-on-lovingkindness/) — [00:11:45](transcript.md#001145-tool-meditation-practices)

---

### Morning Sunlight Exposure

- **What:** Deliberate early-morning sunlight on as much skin as possible immediately after waking, to anchor circadian rhythm and calibrate wake-sleep signaling.
- **How:** Go outside (ideally minimal clothing) within the first hour of waking. Rick describes going out "as naked as possible." Do not wear sunglasses during this exposure. Do not skip on overcast days — outdoor ambient light vastly exceeds indoor light.
- **When:** First thing after waking, daily.
- **Ref:** [00:15:43](transcript.md#001543-sunlight-skin-circadian-rhythm)

---

### Red-Lens Glasses / iPhone Red Screen After Dark

- **What:** Red-tinted glasses or an iPhone color filter worn from sunset onward to block blue-spectrum "cheap photons" that suppress melatonin and dysregulate circadian rhythm. Rick wears red glasses even indoors during the day when under bright artificial studio lights.
- **How:** Put on red glasses when the sun goes down. On iPhone: triple-click the side button (requires setup in Accessibility → Accessibility Shortcut → Color Filters). Or: Settings → Accessibility → Display & Text Size → Color Filters → Red/Green Filter. Avoid all unnecessary artificial light before sleep.
- **When:** From sunset until sleep, every evening.
- **Ref:** [iPhone red screen guide](https://ios.gadgethacks.com/how-to/keep-your-night-vision-sharp-with-iphones-hidden-red-screen-0173903) — [01:49:55](transcript.md#014955-daily-routine-red-light-circadian-rhythm-cheap-photons)

---

### Daily Movement: Beach Walk or Gym

- **What:** One to one-and-a-half hours of movement approximately one hour after waking. Beach walking is Rick's preference when available; gym sessions several days per week otherwise. Light stretching with foam rollers, balls, or yoga mats may precede the walk.
- **How:** No prescribed protocol — sustained, unhurried movement in natural light. Rick uses this time as mental open space, not focused exercise. He avoids work-related engagement during this period but will note any ideas that arise spontaneously.
- **When:** Roughly one to one-and-a-half hours after waking, daily. Work does not begin until this is complete.
- **Ref:** [01:49:55](transcript.md#014955-daily-routine-red-light-circadian-rhythm-cheap-photons)

---

### Diary Entries for Creative Blocks

- **What:** Free-form daily handwritten writing — not about the creative project itself, but about anything authentic. Rick has kept a handwritten diary since high school; entries run one to eight pages, starting all-capitals and shifting to cursive as speed increases.
- **How:** Write anything true. No format, no audience. Starting with "I don't know what to write" and continuing is valid. The goal is to get whatever is clogging you out so you can lean into the day. Huberman also referenced James Pennebaker's expressive writing protocol (15 min/day for four consecutive days about the most challenging experience of your life, supported by 200+ peer-reviewed studies) as a higher-intensity variant with strong physiological and psychological benefits.
- **When:** Daily, especially when experiencing any form of creative block or stagnation.
- **Ref:** [00:28:34](transcript.md#002834-tool-writers-block-creativity-diary-entries-deadlines) and [01:14:07](transcript.md#011407-career-advice-book-writing-diary-entries-expressive-writing)

---

### Dream Journaling (Retrospective Method)

- **What:** Recording dreams immediately upon waking, then reviewing the journal months or years later rather than interpreting each dream in real time.
- **How:** Keep a journal by the bed. Write whatever you remember upon waking without forcing meaning. Let meaning emerge on re-reading over long time intervals — Rick found apparently random and surreal dream imagery was coherently pointing at real emotional circumstances when re-read years later.
- **When:** Immediately upon waking, while memory is fresh. Rick does this in phases of his life rather than continuously.
- **Ref:** [01:08:31](transcript.md#010831-dreams-interpretation-unconscious-mind-motivations-art-outcome)

---

### Capturing Ideas (Low-Barrier Recording)

- **What:** Capture ideas immediately in whatever format has the least friction — phone notes, voice memo, scrap of paper — with no quality judgment at capture time. Rick uses phone notes (acknowledging some later become uninterpretable) and mentioned voice memos as an untried option worth experimenting with.
- **How:** Do not evaluate at capture time. Many best ideas arrive upon waking or in the shower — have a capture method ready in those moments. The point is to externalize before evaporation.
- **When:** Any time an idea surfaces, regardless of context.
- **Ref:** [01:19:25](transcript.md#011925-music-industry-capturing-ideas-money-ingenuity)

---

### Wired (Air-Tube) Headphones Over Bluetooth

- **What:** Rick switched from Bluetooth headphones after developing recurring lymph node swellings behind his ears that correlated directly with Bluetooth use. He uses wired headphones and prefers air-tube designs that eliminate all electrical components near the head. He also removes his phone from the bedroom for sleep.
- **How:** Replace wireless earbuds with wired alternatives. Air-tube headphones deliver sound through a hollow tube — no electrical component near the ear canal.
- **When:** Any time headphones are used.
- **Ref:** [00:20:00](transcript.md#002000-headphones-natural-living-diet)
