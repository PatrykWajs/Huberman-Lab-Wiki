## [00:13:52] Sound Localization

**Jennifer Groh:** It has to be learned, and it has to be continuously updated during the course of development until you reach your adult body size. Let me back up and talk about how we localize sound. The way we tell where a sound is coming from is by the physics of the world causing differential delays for the sound to arrive at one ear versus the other. A sound coming from this side will get to this ear before it gets to the other ear. It'll also be slightly louder in one ear because there's a kind of acoustic shadow cast by the head — the sound wave has to come around, and there's a little dip in sound intensity cast by the shadow of the head.

I like to think about the timing cues because they're really easy to calculate. If you know how far apart your ears are and the speed of sound, you can figure out the delay for sound to reach one ear after the other. Something like about half a millisecond is the largest delay you can experience — that's for a sound from your far right versus far left.

**Andrew Huberman:** We should tell the audience why our brows are furrowed — half a millisecond is less than the duration of a single action potential. And action potentials are the electrical signals that neurons use to communicate with one another, the fundamental medium of communication in the nervous system. So it seems totally weird for us to be able to process sensory information that is faster than the duration of that minimum increment of neural firing.

**Jennifer Groh:** There's research about how exactly this can be done — it involves things like lots of neurons firing together, really precise synapses that cause minimal delay, and very high temporal precision as signals go from one neuron to the next. It's an incredible feat of computational power by the brain.

## [00:21:56] Unfamiliarity of Hearing Your Own Voice; Tool: Bone Conduction Headphones

**Jennifer Groh:** Before I answer that, I want to loop back to the "do we have to learn this" question. ## [01:02:37] Ears, Visual & Auditory Integration, Sound Localization

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about brain structures. The ears separate different frequencies of sound. **Jennifer Groh:** I got hooked on that particular study I mentioned at the beginning — auditory signals in the superior colliculus being affected by the position of the eyes at the time the sound was presented. ## [01:09:48] Evolution of Visual & Auditory Systems, Music; Brain Controlling Vision

**Jennifer Groh:** When I think about a sensory system, I think about layers of sophistication and how they likely evolved. The visual system first evolved to detect light and dark on the order of 24 hours — the difference between nighttime and daytime, which means even with the total inability to see objects, you're safer if you know when to stay in and when to go out. Then motion detection probably came before the ability to see detail, because it's way more important to know if something big is coming at you. Then came additional layers of sophistication like fine detail and color vision. Color vision probably evolved last. In the auditory system, the same thing — you first need to know which direction a sound is coming from, whether it's low or high frequency.

In the motor system, the evolutionary history of the genes expressed in motor neurons that move the trunk are the same ones that undulating fish use. Then there are additional layers of motor neurons — the ones that flap the fins, and then the final addition are the motor neurons that control fine movement of the fingers. I have an obsession with this because if you look at historically primitive music, it's rich in bass tones relative to high frequency tones — and dance that we assign as historically primitive tends to involve a lot of movement of the trunk. As you go from low to high frequency, there's a body map — people making a very detailed point will often use a high frequency register of their voice and point with a finger.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the evolutionary layers of the motor system are mapped onto the frequency content of music and onto body movement patterns.

**Jennifer Groh:** That's the hypothesis, yes. I find it compelling.

## [01:27:01] Thinking & Sensory Simulations; Forming Thoughts

**Andrew Huberman:** The data, as I see them, point to silence being best for focus. I have a question about silence and the voice in our head. What are your thoughts on the use of sound as a way to change brain state?

**Jennifer Groh:** I want to back up on this question because I think you're asking a pretty deep question. One might wonder: why would sound environment matter? **Jennifer Groh:** Exactly. If your thought process is using auditory cortex to run a simulation, and you've got sound coming in, that's going to interfere. Silence removes that interference. **Jennifer Groh:** And norepinephrine for overall alertness, dopamine for reward and novelty, and acetylcholine for that focused spotlight — that's a kind of convergence of neuromodulatory systems that underlie our ability to do exactly what we're talking about: integrating multisensory information to form thoughts and direct action.

## [01:54:16] Chickens; Hypnotizing Chickens, Visual Attention & Focus

**Andrew Huberman:** Yes, you have chickens. What kind?

**Jennifer Groh:** I have bantam Mille Fleurs. Bantam means little guys. They're little guys. Mille Fleur means "a thousand flowers" — they have this beautiful speckled plumage.

**Andrew Huberman:** When did you start raising chickens?

**Jennifer Groh:** I had bantams when I was a kid. Around 2012, my town in Chapel Hill changed its zoning laws to allow chickens in neighborhoods like mine. **Andrew Huberman:** Well, I have a non-invasive experiment for you to try. Some years ago I got very interested in the relationship between vision and brain states. There's interesting literature about the fact that when we view horizons, especially from a vista, it relaxes our autonomic nervous system — we go into more parasympathetic mode. Our eyes naturally go into panoramic vision; we're not fixating on one particular point. Whereas when we do a vergence eye movement — bring our eyes together to focus on a particular point — there's a really interesting increase in the output of areas like locus coeruleus involved in attention and norepinephrine. I thought this was really wild. Maybe we're just staring into little boxes too much and that's why we feel so attentionally depleted. And then my graduate advisor told me some time ago that you can hypnotize a chicken by holding it down on a table and drawing a chalk line extending from its beak. The chicken will lie perfectly still, staring at that line. What's your interpretation of that?

**Jennifer Groh:** That's fascinating. From a visual processing standpoint, when you hold a chicken in that position and make it fixate on a narrow line, you're doing something very specific — you're capturing the visual system in a highly focused, vergence-like state. The chicken is perhaps in some kind of attractor state, visually speaking, where the brain's attention system has latched onto that single stimulus. I think this is related to what we know about attractor states in the brain — you can drive neural circuits into a stable attractor through sensory input, and once there, it takes a real perturbation to pull it out.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the panoramic vision relaxes the system, and the hyper-focused vision locks it in.

**Jennifer Groh:** Right. And if thoughts are essentially sensory simulations, then forcing a very particular visual input — a straight line, no complexity — could essentially freeze the simulation machinery. The chicken's brain may not be able to run any other simulation because its visual cortex is fully occupied with this one stimulus. That's speculation, but I think it's a plausible interpretation. Your chicken experiment would be very interesting to try — and you could do it very non-invasively.
