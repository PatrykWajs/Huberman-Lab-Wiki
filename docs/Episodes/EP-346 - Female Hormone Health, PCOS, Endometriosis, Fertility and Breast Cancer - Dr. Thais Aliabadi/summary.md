# Episode 346: Female Hormone Health, PCOS, Endometriosis, Fertility & Breast Cancer

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman speaks with Dr. Thaïs Aliabadi, board-certified OB/GYN and surgeon with 30 years of clinical experience, about the most common yet chronically undiagnosed conditions in women's reproductive health: PCOS and endometriosis. Dr. Aliabadi explains why over 90% of PCOS cases and a similarly large proportion of endometriosis cases go undiagnosed or are dismissed by physicians, and how this silence destroys fertility, quality of life, and — in the case of breast cancer — can cost lives. The conversation covers underlying mechanisms, the five pillars of PCOS, evidence-based treatment hierarchies from supplements through surgery, breast cancer lifetime risk assessment, fertility preservation, perimenopause, fibroids, adenomyosis, PMDD, and free tools women can use immediately.

## Key Takeaways

- **PCOS affects at least 15% of reproductive-age women** (over 20% in some populations) and more than 90% are never properly diagnosed or treated. The standard "just use birth control" response is insufficient — birth control addresses only one facet of a multi-pillar condition.
- **PCOS diagnosis requires 2 of 3 criteria**: (1) symptoms of elevated androgens (facial/body hair, acne, oily skin, male-pattern hair thinning); (2) ovulation dysfunction (irregular or absent periods); (3) polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. There are four phenotypes — not all patients look the same.
- **PCOS has three underlying pillars**: (1) dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian (HPA) axis, reducing LH/FSH signaling and suppressing ovulation; (2) insulin resistance driving androgen overproduction; (3) chronic systemic inflammation amplifying both. All three must be addressed.
- **Insulin resistance is the most actionable lever in PCOS.** Lowering visceral fat and improving insulin sensitivity through diet, exercise, and targeted supplements or medication can restore ovulation, lower androgens, and improve mood.
- **Endometriosis affects at least 10–20% of women** (likely higher due to systematic underdiagnosis). It is tissue similar to the uterine lining growing outside the uterus — on ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, and bladder — causing inflammation, scarring, chronic pelvic pain, and infertility.
- **Painful periods are not normal.** Period pain that disrupts daily life or is accompanied by GI symptoms, pain during sex, or pain during urination is a diagnostic red flag for endometriosis at any age, including teenagers.
- **Stage of endometriosis does not correlate with pain severity.** Stage 1 can be agonizing; stage 4 may be nearly painless. Diagnosis requires laparoscopic surgery for certainty; ultrasound can miss it.
- **Endometriosis impairs egg quality** by generating an inflammatory pelvic environment that damages follicles. AMH and antral follicle count should be checked even in young women with suspected endometriosis.
- **Egg count (AMH) should be checked at any age** when symptoms suggest PCOS or endometriosis — not deferred until infertility becomes apparent.
- **Breast cancer lifetime risk calculation is mandatory for all women.** The average American woman has a 12.5% lifetime risk. Mammograms starting at 40 applies only to very low-risk women. High-risk women need earlier and more intensive screening. Every woman should know her lifetime risk score.
- **PCOS and endometriosis frequently co-occur.** The chronic inflammation driving PCOS is Dr. Aliabadi's proposed mechanism for why PCOS patients have higher rates of endometriosis.
- **Separating obstetrics from gynecology** is Dr. Aliabadi's proposed structural solution to the crisis of undiagnosed gynecological disease — focused surgeons develop the depth of expertise needed.
- **50% of US counties have no OB/GYN.** Free online tools and AI platforms fill a critical access gap.
- **Progestins (synthetic) vs. progesterone (bioidentical) are not the same.** Progestins suppress the HPA axis; micronized bioidentical progesterone is used in perimenopausal/menopausal HRT with a better safety and side-effect profile.
- **"Unexplained infertility" is largely undiagnosed PCOS and endometriosis.** Both conditions should be ruled out before accepting an unexplained label.

## Tools & Protocols

**PCOS Self-Assessment**
- [ovi.com](https://ovi.com) — free PCOS risk calculator developed by Dr. Aliabadi from 25 years of clinical data. Zero cost, no physician needed.

**Lab Tests to Request (PCOS Workup)**
- AMH (anti-Müllerian hormone) — egg count proxy; age-appropriate reference ranges exist
- Fasting insulin and glucose (HOMA-IR)
- Total and free testosterone, DHEA-S
- LH and FSH (LH:FSH ratio elevated in PCOS)
- Prolactin
- Thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4)
- HbA1c
- Vitamin D level

**PCOS Lifestyle Protocol**
- Low-inflammation, low-glycemic diet; reduce processed foods and starchy carbohydrates
- Adequate protein intake
- High-intensity interval training and resistance training — both improve insulin sensitivity
- Prioritize sleep quality (poor sleep worsens insulin resistance and cortisol)
- Minimize chronic psychological stress (disrupts hypothalamic GnRH signaling)
- Limit or eliminate alcohol (estrogenic and pro-inflammatory)

**PCOS Supplement Stack**
- Myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol (combined, 40:1 ratio) — improve insulin sensitivity and restore ovulation
- Coenzyme Q10 — mitochondrial support and egg quality
- Vitamin D — correct deficiency
- Magnesium
- Chromium — insulin sensitizer
- Curcumin — anti-inflammatory
- Berberine — short-term pulse use; not recommended long-term due to limited safety data

**PCOS Prescription Options**
- Metformin (extended-release) — first-line insulin sensitizer; safe long-term; used even in non-diabetic perimenopausal women with borderline HbA1c
- GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) — effective for insulin resistance and visceral fat; also reduces food anxiety
- Spironolactone — androgen blocker for hair thinning and acne
- Birth control pills — suppress androgens and regulate cycle but do not treat underlying insulin resistance or inflammation
- GnRH antagonists (e.g., elagolix) — suppress estrogen/androgen production; used in endometriosis management

**Endometriosis Red Flags (Symptom Checklist)**
- Painful periods that disrupt daily life or require analgesics
- Pelvic pain outside of menstruation
- Pain during or after intercourse
- Pain during urination or bowel movements (especially around menstruation)
- Bloating and GI cramping tied to the cycle
- Infertility or difficulty conceiving

**Endometriosis Workup**
- Pelvic/transvaginal ultrasound (can miss superficial implants but detects endometriomas/chocolate cysts)
- AMH and antral follicle count
- Gold standard diagnosis: laparoscopic surgery with direct visualization and excision (same-day outpatient)

**Endometriosis Treatment Ladder**
1. Hormonal suppression: progesterone-based birth control or GnRH antagonists to slow growth
2. Laparoscopic excision surgery — preferred over ablation for thorough removal
3. Surgery followed by hormonal maintenance to prevent recurrence

**Egg Preservation Guidance**
- Check AMH and antral follicle count before pursuing egg freezing to gauge urgency
- Earlier freezing yields better egg quality
- AMH rough guideline: 20s ~2–4 ng/mL; 30s ~1–2 ng/mL; declines with age and with endometriosis

**Fertility Workup (Couples)**
- Female: AMH, hormones, STI screen, pelvic ultrasound (fibroids, uterine septum, anatomy)
- Male: semen analysis; assess cannabis, alcohol, heat exposure
- Rule out PCOS and endometriosis before labeling as "unexplained infertility"
- Autoimmune panel if indicated

**Breast Cancer Lifetime Risk Protocol**
- Use the Tyrer-Cuzick model to calculate personal lifetime risk (available at major hospital/cancer center websites)
- Average US woman: 12.5% lifetime risk
- High-risk threshold: ≥20% lifetime risk — qualifies for annual MRI in addition to mammography
- Key risk factors: BRCA1/2 mutations, family history, dense breasts, early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity
- Having children before age 40 and breastfeeding are protective
- 3D mammography (tomosynthesis) recommended over standard 2D

**Perimenopause / Menopause**
- Estrogen decline causes hair thinning, brain fog, mood changes, sleep disruption, vaginal atrophy
- Micronized (bioidentical) progesterone improves sleep, mood, and provides uterine protection with estrogen HRT
- Estradiol patch does not cause hair loss — the underlying estrogen decline does
- Low-carbohydrate and time-restricted eating help manage increased perimenopausal insulin resistance

**PMDD Protocol**
- Premenstrual dysphoric disorder: severe luteal-phase mood disruption (depression, irritability, anxiety)
- Treatment options: SSRIs taken only in the luteal phase, hormonal suppression, or bioidentical progesterone

**Naturally Increasing Progesterone**
- Restore ovulation via insulin sensitivity improvements (diet, metformin, inositol)
- Ovulation generates the corpus luteum, which produces progesterone
- For perimenopausal women not ovulating: micronized oral progesterone prescription
