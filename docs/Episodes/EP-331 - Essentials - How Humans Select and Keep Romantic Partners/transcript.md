## [00:00:00] Introduction

**Andrew Huberman:** Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. And now my conversation with Dr. David Buss.

## [00:00:21] Mate Selection, Preferences & Competition

**Andrew Huberman:** David, delighted to be here. I'm excited to ask you a number of questions about these super interesting topics about how people select mates. Just to start off, perhaps you could orient us a little bit about mate choice — some of the primary criteria that studies show men and women use in order to select mates, transient mates as well as lifetime mates.

**David Buss:** That's a critical distinction because what people look for in a long-term committed relationship like a marriage partner is different from what people look for in a hookup or casual sex. Let me back up and talk a little bit about the theoretical framework for understanding mate choice. It basically stems from Darwin's theory of sexual selection — Darwin noticed phenomena that couldn't be explained by survival selection alone, so he came up with sexual selection, which deals with characteristics that evolve due to their mating advantage rather than survival advantage.

**David Buss:** He identified two causal processes. The first is intrasexual competition: whatever qualities led to success in same-sex battles get passed on in greater numbers, producing evolution toward the characteristics associated with winning contest competition. In our species this now involves competing for position in status hierarchies. The second process, most relevant to mate choice, is preferential mate choice: if members of one sex agree about the qualities they desire, then those of the opposite sex who embody those qualities gain a mating advantage, while those lacking desired qualities get ignored or, in the modern environment, become incels.

**David Buss:** Whatever qualities are consensually desired — if there is a heritable basis to those qualities — they increase in frequency over time. In humans, these two causal processes are related: the preferences of one sex set the ground rules for competition in the opposite sex. If women prefer men who are able and willing to devote resources to them, that creates competition among men to acquire resources and display willingness to commit those resources to a particular woman.

## [00:03:26] Desirable Qualities of Men & Women, Universal Traits for Long-Term Mates

**David Buss:** What are the qualities men and women desire? Let's start with long-term mating. The most large-scale study done on this is one I conducted across 37 different cultures, now replicated by other researchers. We found three clusters: qualities both sexes wanted, qualities that were sex-differentiated, and attributes that varied highly across cultures.

**David Buss:** Universal desires — things men and women share — include intelligence, kindness, mutual attraction and love, good health, dependability, and emotional stability, though women prefer emotional stability somewhat more than men. You can go anywhere in the world and these are qualities people universally desire in long-term mates.

## [00:04:38] Women's Preferences; Men's Preferences; Age Differences

**David Buss:** Sex differences fell into two clusters. Women more than men prioritized good earning capacity, slightly older age, and qualities associated with resource acquisition: social status, drive, ambition, and a good long-term resource trajectory. Women don't necessarily look at resources a man possesses at the moment but at his trajectory. Women also attend to the attention structure — people high in status are those to whom the most other people pay the most attention.

**David Buss:** Women also use mate-choice copying. In studies where we photograph a man alone versus paired with an attractive woman or two women, women judge the exact same man to be significantly more attractive when he is paired with other women. From an evolutionary perspective this makes sense given the tremendous asymmetry in reproductive biology: fertilization occurs internally in women, who bear the burdens of pregnancy as well as opportunity costs in mobility. The costs of a bad mate choice and the benefits of a wise mate choice are both higher for women.

**David Buss:** Men, by contrast, value physical attractiveness more than women do. Physical appearance provides information about health status and, for men, information about a woman's fertility and reproductive value — not that men consciously think about fertility, but those cues are attractive nonetheless. Cues men find attractive — clear skin, symmetrical features, a low waist-to-hip ratio, full lips, lustrous hair — are all associated with youth and health. Age differences in mate preferences are among the largest sex differences in long-term mate selection: women prefer somewhat older men and men prefer somewhat younger women, with this pattern holding consistently across studies.

**Andrew Huberman:** Could you tell us about how men and women leverage deception in communicating around mate selection?

## [00:08:58] Mate Deception & Online Dating, Tool: Travel, Stress & Emotional Stability

**David Buss:** Both men and women deceive. Online dating is a modern cultural invention where people lie in predictable ways — they lie in ways that attempt to embody the mate preferences of the person they are trying to attract. Both sexes post photos that are not truly representative of their current appearance: older photos, or photos taken from angles designed to maximize attractiveness. In online dating, the photograph tends to overwhelm all other cues because we weren't evolved to process written statements, but we were evolved to respond to physical cues. Men attend to visual cues much more than women.

**David Buss:** Women also attend to olfactory, auditory, and other non-visual cues that simply cannot be assessed online. A man's vocal qualities matter, and his scent is a dealbreaker — if the guy doesn't smell right, that can eliminate him regardless of other qualities. I encourage people to stop with the hundred messages back and forth and meet in person for a cup of coffee.

**David Buss:** Even a brief in-person interaction can tell you a lot, but some qualities — like emotional stability, which is absolutely critical in long-term mating — require extended assessment. Taking a trip together places both people in unfamiliar environments where they must cope with unexpected challenges. How someone responds to stress is one of the hallmarks of emotional instability that you simply cannot detect on a coffee date.

**David Buss:** One important form of deception is misrepresenting interest in a long-term relationship versus a short-term hookup. Openly displaying interest in only casual sex is an ineffective tactic. Studies of deception show that men tend to exaggerate how similar they are to a woman in values, religious orientation, and political views — likely an evolutionarily recurrent form of deception that women have developed defenses against.

## [00:12:07] Short- vs Long-Term Mates, Men vs Women Preferences

**Andrew Huberman:** In terms of sexual partner choice, are there good studies exploring what people select for?

**David Buss:** We know something about how preferences for a sex partner differ from preferences for a long-term mate. Physical appearance becomes more important for women in short-term mating. Men's standards for physical appearance remain important in short-term mating, but with the footnote that men are willing to lower their standards when commitment and risk are low. Women in short-term mating are more likely to prioritize what I call "bad boy" qualities — men who are a bit arrogant or risk-taking — whereas in long-term mating they go more for "good dad" qualities like dependability.

**David Buss:** Women also use mate-choice copying more intensely in short-term mating. If thousands of other women find a man attractive, women find him attractive. This is the groupie phenomenon — the same man judged from a still photo versus shown with thousands of screaming fans is rated entirely differently. Women's attraction to a man depends heavily on context: his status, the number of women attracted to him, the attention structure, how he interacts with others. For men, context is comparatively irrelevant — they hone in on specific psychophysical cues the woman is displaying.

## [00:14:23] Jealousy, Mate Value Discrepancy, Vigilance to Violence

**Andrew Huberman:** What's known about jealousy in men versus women? A large fraction of homicides are reportedly the consequence of jealous lovers — what does evolutionary psychology say about jealousy?

**David Buss:** Jealousy is an evolved emotion that serves several adaptive functions. Once long-term mating exists, you need a defense to prevent or preserve the investment you have made in the relationship. Jealousy serves a mate-guarding or mate-retention function, getting activated when there are threats to the romantic relationship — cues of a partner's infidelity, emotional distance, or interested mate poachers.

**David Buss:** Mate value discrepancies are another trigger. Even without active threats, if a gap opens between partners' relative mate value — someone gets fired, or conversely a career takes off dramatically — jealousy can be activated. The higher mate value person has access to a larger pool of potential mates, and statistically is more likely to have an affair or trade up. People do things in response ranging from vigilance to violence: following, monitoring, hacking into devices, looking at eye contact between their partner and others. In America, approximately 28 to 30 percent of all married people will experience intimate partner violence — not a trivial percentage.

## [00:17:07] The Dark Triad, Sexual Harassment & Coercion

**David Buss:** We've been talking about sex differences on average, but there are critical within-sex individual differences. The dark triad is one of the most important. It consists of three personality characteristics: narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy. Those high in dark triad traits — predominantly men — tend to be sexual deceivers who are charming, skilled at seducing and then abandoning partners, and are serial sexual harassers and coercers.

**David Buss:** Combining dark triad traits with a dispositional pursuit of a short-term mating strategy is especially dangerous — that combination produces sexual harassment and coercion. A subset of men commits the vast majority of these acts of sexual violence.

## [00:19:14] Stalking, Motivations & Outcomes

**Andrew Huberman:** Could we talk about stalking? I once heard you mention that one of the scariest things about stalking is that sometimes it works.

**David Buss:** Stalking has multiple motivations, but one of the most frequent is a mating motivation: a breakup occurs, the man doesn't want to accept it, and he attempts to maintain or reestablish the relationship. About 80% of criminal stalkers are men, about 20% women. The motivation tends to be an attempt to get back together sexually or in a relationship, or to interfere with the woman's future mating prospects.

**David Buss:** It works in two senses. First, it does sometimes interfere with the woman's attempts to remate — the presence of a threatening ex scares off potential new partners. The circumstances often involve a mate value discrepancy where the man correctly realizes he will be unable to replace her with a partner of equivalent mate value. In a study of 2,500 victims of stalking conducted with Josh Duntley, we found that stalkers tend to be much lower in mate value than their victims — the woman realizes she can do better, and the man realizes he cannot replace her, leading to a last-ditch desperate measure to try to get her back.

## [00:21:53] Childhood Attachment Styles & Relationship Stability

**Andrew Huberman:** There is a lot of discussion about how early childhood attachment to parents influences mate choice later on. Is there anything interesting about that in terms of long-term partner stability?

**David Buss:** I can offer some informed speculation. A secure attachment style in both partners is conducive to long-term mating. Avoidant attachment styles are associated with more difficulty with intimacy and a higher probability of infidelity. Anxious attachment can create problems of its own — what I call high relationship load, the baggage someone brings to the relationship.

## [00:22:58] Self-Assessment for Mate Value, Self-Esteem

**Andrew Huberman:** How objective are people about assessing their own value in terms of finding, securing, and maintaining a relationship? And how should people assess themselves?

**David Buss:** People are generally pretty good at self-assessing mate value. Self-esteem has been hypothesized to be one internal monitoring device that tracks mate value — when we get a promotion or rise in status, self-esteem elevates; when we get fired or rejected, it plummets. So self-evaluation does track mate value to some extent. People high in narcissism tend to overestimate their mate value; others underestimate it.

**David Buss:** There is both consensual mate value — where a group of 100 people would largely agree on how attractive a person is — and individual differences in mate value preferences. If everyone pursued the same people with total consensus, there would be many mateless people and enormous dissatisfaction. Both the consensual components and individually differentiated components of mate value matter.

**David Buss:** Scientifically, there are no clean measures for accurate self-assessment because it is a complicated endeavor. But people develop a good intuitive sense of relative mate value, especially in groups where they can interact over time. One useful cue remains the attention structure: how many other people genuinely want to mate with this person?

## [00:26:35] Evolutionary Psychology & Neuroscience

**Andrew Huberman:** I feel like neuroscience and evolutionary psychology are nudging toward one another. Do you see that convergence happening?

**David Buss:** Absolutely. It's starting to happen and it will continue, because getting at the neuroscience means getting at the underlying mechanisms driving the process. An evolutionary perspective brings evolved function, ultimate explanation, the selective forces that created adaptations, and the functions of those adaptations. Neuroscience brings the underlying machinery — the mechanisms in which these adaptations are instantiated.
