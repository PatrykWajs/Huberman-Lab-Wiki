# Episode 152: Improving Sexual & Urological Health in Males and Females | Dr. Rena Malik

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Rena Malik, a board-certified urologist and pelvic surgeon, for a comprehensive discussion covering the full range of sexual and urological health for both males and females. The conversation spans pelvic floor function and dysfunction, the physiology of desire versus arousal, erectile dysfunction causes and treatments, female orgasm and lubrication, UTI prevention, kidney stones, the effects of oral contraceptives and SSRIs on libido, prostate health, anal sex safety, bicycling risks to pelvic nerve function, and evidence-based supplements for sexual function. Dr. Malik draws on clinical practice and research to separate myth from evidence, while both hosts emphasize open, shame-free communication about sexual health.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The pelvic floor is a bowl of muscles — and being too tight is as problematic as being too weak.** Most people hear only about Kegels (strengthening), but a hypertonic (overly tense) pelvic floor causes pain with sex, painful urination, incomplete bladder emptying, and constipation. Relaxation training is often more necessary than strengthening. [→ 00:07:45](transcript.md#000745-pelvic-floor-urination-sexual-function)

2. **Desire and arousal are distinct neurologically — and hormones explain only a small fraction of erectile dysfunction.** Desire is primarily testosterone-driven (in both sexes). Arousal (erection, lubrication, engorgement) is driven by nitric oxide, blood flow, and pelvic nerve signaling. Only 3–6% of erectile dysfunction is hormonal; the rest is vascular, neurogenic, or psychological. [→ 00:28:03](transcript.md#002803-desire-vs-arousal-erections-psychology-hormones-blood-flow-nerves)

3. **Kegels are beneficial but must be dosed correctly — and should be avoided if the pelvic floor is already too tight.** Dr. Malik recommends 10–15 reps twice daily, not hundreds per day. Over-training creates a short, tight muscle that worsens symptoms. Anyone with pelvic pain, painful erections, or difficulty emptying the bladder should see a pelvic floor physical therapist before doing Kegels. [→ 00:19:44](transcript.md#001944-kegels-benefits-risks-urinary-incontinence)

4. **PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis) preserve nitric oxide — and low-dose daily tadalafil also treats urinary symptoms from an enlarged prostate.** These drugs inhibit the enzyme that breaks down cGMP, keeping smooth muscle relaxed and blood flow elevated. Daily low-dose tadalafil (5 mg) is FDA-approved for both erectile dysfunction and benign prostatic hyperplasia; it has a 36-hour half-life making it well suited to daily use. [→ 00:43:13](transcript.md#004313-desire-arousal-issues-erectile-dysfunction-viagra-cialis-tadalafil)

5. **L-citrulline increases nitric oxide bioavailability more effectively than L-arginine, making it a reasonable supplement for erectile function.** L-arginine has poor oral bioavailability; L-citrulline converts to L-arginine in the body and lasts longer. It works upstream of PDE5 inhibitors by increasing nitric oxide availability. Useful as a supporting supplement, not a replacement for PDE5 inhibitors in moderate-to-severe ED. [→ 00:52:20](transcript.md#005220-l-citrulline-supplements)

6. **Problematic pornography use affects approximately 4% of users — but unrealistic expectations from pornography affect a much broader population.** Dr. Malik distinguishes between shame-inducing discourse and genuinely problematic use (compulsivity, relationship interference, desensitization). Arousal habituation — requiring more novel stimuli for the same response — also applies to masturbation technique: always using the same pressure or vibratory stimulation can reduce responsiveness to partner-based sex. [→ 01:01:37](transcript.md#010137-pornography-masturbation-ejaculation-healthy-sexual-behavior)

7. **There is a large orgasm gap: ~95% of men orgasm during a first sexual encounter versus ~45–50% of women in heterosexual first encounters — but ~90% in female homosexual first encounters.** Approximately 85% of women require clitoral stimulation to climax. The clitoris is wishbone-shaped and wraps around the vagina, meaning internal stimulation often engages clitoral tissue indirectly. Communication with partners is one of the highest-yield interventions for closing this gap. [→ 01:35:45](transcript.md#013545-female-orgasm-vaginal-penetration-stimulation-clitoris-g-spot)

8. **Vaginal estrogen is highly effective for reducing recurrent UTIs in post-menopausal women and is not associated with the risks of systemic estrogen.** UTIs affect up to 50% of women at least once; a third develop recurrent UTIs. Local vaginal estrogen restores mucosal thickness and the protective Lactobacillus-dominant microbiome without meaningful systemic absorption. It takes ~3 months to reach full effect. D-Mannose (2 g/day) and cranberry (36 mg soluble PACs) are also supported by AUA guidelines as preventive supplements. [→ 01:58:45](transcript.md#015845-urinary-tract-infections-utis-in-male-females-prevention-vaginal-estrogen)

9. **Oral contraceptives increase sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), which reduces free testosterone and can suppress libido for months even after stopping the pill.** SHBG levels can remain elevated for at least four months post-cessation, which may explain persistent low libido after discontinuing hormonal contraception. Non-hormonal copper IUDs avoid this effect entirely. [→ 02:19:14](transcript.md#021914-oral-contraception-libido-individuality-intrauterine-device-iuds)

10. **SSRIs delay or block orgasm by elevating serotonin, which is mechanistically antagonistic to sexual climax — but this effect is dose-dependent and manageable.** The same mechanism is used therapeutically off-label to treat premature ejaculation. Clinicians can switch patients to bupropion (Wellbutrin — dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor) to preserve antidepressant effects while reducing sexual side effects. PDE5 inhibitors can also be added as an adjunct. [→ 02:26:52](transcript.md#022652-anti-depressants-ssris-low-libido)

11. **Cardiovascular health is deeply linked to erectile function — 15% of men who develop erectile dysfunction will have a cardiovascular event within seven years.** Erectile dysfunction is frequently the first clinical sign of systemic vascular disease because penile arteries are smaller and more sensitive to endothelial damage. About 50% of men over 40 have ED; this rises to ~60% of 60-year-olds. Smoking and vaping cessation and management of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes are first-line interventions before pharmacology. [→ 02:44:14](transcript.md#024414-libido-behavioral-interventions-supplements)

12. **Testosterone supplementation in post-menopausal women has robust evidence for improving sexual desire and function.** Consensus statements support assessment of free and total testosterone, with supplementation when levels are low and desire is impaired. Women need approximately one-tenth the testosterone level of men. Transdermal testosterone (a tenth of one Androgel tube applied to a hairless area) is the recommended delivery method. Women have more testosterone than estrogen in their bodies throughout life. [→ 02:11:33](transcript.md#021133-testosterone-post-menopausal-women-libido)

## Tools & Protocols

### Kegel Exercises

- **What:** Rhythmic contractions of the pelvic floor muscles, classically described as the sensation of stopping urination mid-stream or lifting a blueberry with the vaginal muscles. For men: the sensation of trying to lift the penis off the floor without touching it.
- **How:** Squeeze for 5 seconds, relax for 5 seconds. Begin lying down (to isolate the muscles), then progress to sitting, then standing. Start with 10–15 repetitions per set. Do not train to exhaustion.
- **When:** Morning and evening, with an optional midday set. Beginners should start conservatively. Anyone with pelvic pain, painful erections, or trouble emptying the bladder should avoid Kegels until evaluated by a pelvic floor physical therapist.
- **Ref:** [→ 00:19:44](transcript.md#001944-kegels-benefits-risks-urinary-incontinence)

---

### Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

- **What:** Evaluation and manual treatment of pelvic floor dysfunction by a trained physical therapist, including internal palpation, biofeedback, and both relaxation and strengthening exercises tailored to the patient's presentation.
- **How:** Requires referral from a urologist or gynecologist. Evaluation involves pelvic exam (internal for women, rectal + perineal for men) to assess muscle tone, coordination, and endurance. Look for a therapist certified in pelvic floor PT who has treated your gender specifically.
- **When:** Indicated for chronic pelvic pain, pain with sex, painful erections/ejaculation, incomplete bladder emptying, urinary urgency/frequency, or incontinence.
- **Ref:** [→ 00:37:07](transcript.md#003707-pelvic-floor-medical-professionals-physical-therapy)

---

### Pelvic Floor Relaxation (Anti-Kegel)

- **What:** Techniques to down-train an overly tight (hypertonic) pelvic floor, including massage, vaginal dilators, muscle-relaxant suppositories (Valium, baclofen — prescription), and targeted stretching exercises such as happy baby pose.
- **How:** Work with a pelvic floor physical therapist for guided down-training. Happy baby pose stretches and elongates pelvic floor muscles. Proper diaphragmatic breathing during exercise (exhale on effort, inhale to relax) is essential to avoid chronic pelvic floor tension.
- **When:** For anyone with a hypertonic pelvic floor presenting as pelvic pain, painful penetration, or urinary symptoms. Avoid heavy abdominal work (crunches, especially with legs crossed) if pelvic floor symptoms are present.
- **Ref:** [→ 00:24:08](transcript.md#002408-pelvic-floor-relaxation-exercise-pelvic-floor)

---

### L-Citrulline Supplementation

- **What:** An amino acid precursor to L-arginine that increases endogenous nitric oxide production, supporting vascular smooth muscle relaxation and blood flow to the genitals.
- **How:** Typically 3–6 g/day. More bioavailable than L-arginine taken directly. Can be used standalone or alongside PDE5 inhibitors. Try one supplement at a time to isolate effects.
- **When:** For mild-to-moderate erectile dysfunction or general cardiovascular support. Less effective than PDE5 inhibitors for moderate-to-severe ED. Note: some individuals report herpes/cold sore flare-ups with L-citrulline; monitor for this.
- **Ref:** [→ 00:52:20](transcript.md#005220-l-citrulline-supplements)

---

### Low-Dose Daily Tadalafil

- **What:** A PDE5 inhibitor taken at 2.5–5 mg/day rather than 10–20 mg on demand. Provides steady-state effect that removes performance pressure and simultaneously improves prostate/bladder symptoms via smooth muscle relaxation of the prostate.
- **How:** Prescription required. Tadalafil has a 36-hour half-life, so daily dosing maintains continuous therapeutic levels without the need to time doses around sexual activity.
- **When:** FDA-approved for erectile dysfunction and benign prostatic hyperplasia. Particularly useful for men with psychogenic ED, urinary symptoms from BPH, or pelvic pain. A four-week trial is recommended before assessing benefit.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:29:32](transcript.md#022932-prostate-health-cialis-urination-difficulty)

---

### Vaginal Estrogen for Recurrent UTI Prevention

- **What:** Low-dose estrogen delivered locally (cream, suppository, or ring) to restore vaginal mucosal integrity and the protective Lactobacillus-dominant microbiome. Also addresses vaginal dryness and pain with intercourse (dyspareunia).
- **How:** Applied vaginally approximately twice to three times per week (cream/suppository); ring is inserted once and lasts three months. Not absorbed systemically in meaningful amounts — does not carry cardiovascular or breast cancer risks associated with systemic hormone therapy. GoodRx or Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs pharmacy can make it affordable if not covered by insurance.
- **When:** Post-menopausal women with two or more UTIs in six months, or three or more in one year. Takes approximately three months of consistent use to reach full effect.
- **Ref:** [→ 01:58:45](transcript.md#015845-urinary-tract-infections-utis-in-male-females-prevention-vaginal-estrogen)

---

### Cranberry (PAC-Standardized) for UTI Prevention

- **What:** A specific active ingredient in cranberry — proanthocyanidins (PACs) — prevents E. coli from adhering to uroepithelial cells. Supported in AUA guidelines for recurrent UTI prevention.
- **How:** 36 mg of PACs per day in soluble form (not whole-berry capsules, which use skin and stem and are poorly absorbed). Take as a once-daily capsule. For post-coital UTIs, two capsules on the day of sex and two the following day may also help (limited evidence).
- **When:** Daily for prevention of recurrent UTIs. Not a treatment for active UTIs.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:04:04](transcript.md#020404-male-urination-sitting-spermicide-cranberry-d-mannose-utis)

---

### D-Mannose for UTI Prevention

- **What:** A naturally occurring sugar that prevents E. coli from adhering to uroepithelial cells, causing bacteria to be flushed out during urination. Supported by AUA guidelines alongside cranberry.
- **How:** 2 g/day in powder or capsule form.
- **When:** Daily for prevention of recurrent UTIs. Not a treatment for active UTIs.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:04:04](transcript.md#020404-male-urination-sitting-spermicide-cranberry-d-mannose-utis)

---

### Male Urination Sitting

- **What:** Sitting to urinate allows gravity and relaxed pelvic floor positioning to facilitate more complete bladder emptying, potentially reducing symptoms of benign prostatic hyperplasia and reducing UTI risk from residual urine.
- **How:** Sit on the toilet rather than standing. Optionally lean forward and attempt a second void after standing then sitting back down.
- **When:** Particularly beneficial for men with BPH symptoms (weak stream, dribbling, incomplete emptying). An easy, zero-cost intervention. Not necessary for men with no emptying symptoms.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:04:04](transcript.md#020404-male-urination-sitting-spermicide-cranberry-d-mannose-utis)

---

### Avoiding Spermicide to Reduce UTI Risk

- **What:** Spermicide-containing condoms or standalone spermicide products disrupt the vaginal and urethral microbiome, significantly increasing UTI risk.
- **How:** Switch to non-spermicidal condoms or alternative contraceptive methods if recurrent UTIs are a concern.
- **When:** For any woman experiencing recurrent UTIs who uses spermicide. A modifiable risk factor in clinical UTI management.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:04:04](transcript.md#020404-male-urination-sitting-spermicide-cranberry-d-mannose-utis)

---

### Kidney Stone Prevention

- **What:** Dietary and hydration modifications to reduce kidney stone formation. Targeted approach depends on individual metabolic workup (24-hour urine analysis + bloodwork).
- **How:** Increase fluid intake to 2–3 liters per day; increase citrate intake (fruits, vegetables, Crystal Light — which has high citrate content); reduce oxalate-rich foods (especially spinach, rhubarb, excess nuts); reduce high-purine protein (red meat). Flomax (tamsulosin) or similar alpha-blockers can relax ureteral smooth muscle to help a stone pass.
- **When:** For anyone with a history of kidney stones or at metabolic risk. Seek urgent care if kidney stone is accompanied by fever, chills, or signs of infection (risk of rapid ICU-level deterioration).
- **Ref:** [→ 02:14:56](transcript.md#021456-kidney-stones-prevention-treatment)

---

### Behavioral Interventions for Sexual Dysfunction

- **What:** Lifestyle modifications targeting the root vascular and hormonal causes of sexual dysfunction: smoking cessation, vaping cessation, management of obesity, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes. The Mediterranean diet has the best evidence base in the sexual dysfunction literature.
- **How:** Work with a primary care physician or cardiologist to address comorbidities. These are first-line interventions before supplements or pharmacology.
- **When:** For anyone with erectile dysfunction or low libido; comorbidity management should precede all other interventions.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:44:14](transcript.md#024414-libido-behavioral-interventions-supplements)

---

### Tongkat Ali and Ashwagandha for Libido

- **What:** Herbal supplements with reasonable (though not large-scale) evidence supporting improvements in libido and sexual function. Tongkat Ali may increase free testosterone by reducing SHBG (and may partially override SSRI-related libido suppression); ashwagandha reduces cortisol, indirectly supporting sexual function.
- **How:** Standard doses per product labeling. Try one supplement at a time to isolate effects. Examine.com is recommended as an evidence-graded resource. Effects build over time — immediate effects suggest an undisclosed PDE5 inhibitor in the product.
- **When:** After addressing behavioral foundations (sleep, exercise, Mediterranean diet, comorbidities). Not a substitute for medical evaluation.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:49:15](transcript.md#024915-supplements-for-libido)

---

### Safe Anal Sex Practices

- **What:** Harm-reduction practices for anal sex to prevent STIs and tissue trauma. STI transmission rates are higher with anal sex than vaginal intercourse due to thin, friable anal tissue that can bleed with penetration.
- **How:** Always use a condom. Use adequate lubrication — the anus produces no endogenous lubrication. Choose iso-osmolar anal-specific lubricants; water-based are most accessible, silicone-based last longer, oil-based are incompatible with condoms. Start with smaller girth and progress gradually to avoid trauma.
- **When:** Applicable for any anal penetration regardless of partner sex or gender. Prioritize consent and a clear state of mind.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:37:48](transcript.md#023748-anal-sex-females-sexually-transmitted-infections-stis-lubricants)

---

### Cycling Modifications to Protect Pelvic Nerve and Blood Flow

- **What:** Adjustments to bicycle setup and riding posture to prevent pudendal nerve and artery compression, which can cause genital numbness (up to ~50% of high-volume cyclists) and erectile dysfunction in men.
- **How:** Use a wide, noseless bike seat (avoid narrow seats and seats with cutouts, which can concentrate pressure around the edges of the cutout). Sit upright — avoid aggressive aero/forward-leaning posture, which shifts weight onto the seat beak and compresses perineal structures.
- **When:** For any regular cyclist experiencing genital numbness, reduced sensation, or sexual dysfunction. Cardiovascular benefits of cycling are preserved with proper seat setup.
- **Ref:** [→ 02:34:22](transcript.md#023422-bicycling-genital-numbness-erectile-dysfunction)
