## [00:02:50] Strategies for Preventing Dementia

An audience member asks about protocols and research for preventing dementia, noting that many listeners are in or approaching their 50s.

Starting with ground truths: blood circulation is good for the brain — perhaps most important for the brain. Anything that is good for cardiovascular health is going to be good for brain health. Zone 2 cardio — cardiovascular exercise at an intensity where you can just barely maintain a conversation — is recommended at roughly 150 to 200 minutes per week. This seems to benefit the brain through the release of growth factors like brain-derived neurotrophic factor, through effects on inflammatory and anti-inflammatory cytokines, and through increasing blood flow in and through the brain.

Age-related decline in memory capacity, particularly working memory, can be related to reductions in dopamine transmission. Things that increase catecholamines — through zero-cost and very low-cost behavioral means — are known to improve working memory. The hubermanlab.com website lists out these approaches under dopamine regulation.

There is a growing conversation around selective pharmacology for offsetting age-related cognitive decline. Certain compounds like nicotine — despite increasing vasoconstriction — can offset some age-related reductions in dopaminergic and cholinergic transmission. A Nobel Prize-winning neuroscientist at Columbia University was observed chewing no fewer than five pieces of Nicorette during a half-hour conversation and explained that the nicotine was to offset age-related loss of dopaminergic and cholinergic neurons. Supplementation with choline donors to increase acetylcholine is another area of interest. Modafinil and compounds like Adderall, which are amphetamines, are being explored by some older individuals, though these come with significant caveats.

Protecting against head injury is important: the two-hit model of TBI suggests that repeated head impacts cause cumulative damage. Most head injuries occur not in sport but in construction accidents and car accidents. Hyperbaric chambers show some interesting early data for traumatic brain injury and neurodegeneration.

Drugs of abuse like methamphetamine can deplete dopamine neurons. MDMA, which is methylene dioxy methamphetamine, has a more complex picture — data from studies involving people from the LDS community who had only used MDMA suggested limited neurodegeneration at typical doses, though attention issues appeared at high cumulative doses of 80 milligrams or more. Contamination of drug batches with fentanyl is a critical concern.

Physical exercise that engages the neuromuscular system — not just cardiovascular exercise, but coordinated movement, learning new physical skills, dance — seems to especially offset age-related cognitive decline. The nervous system does not distinguish neatly between physical and cognitive challenge; they operate as integrated functional units.

## [00:15:07] Enhancing Willpower: Is It Comparable to Muscle Training?

An audience member asks whether willpower can be trained like a muscle, specifically referencing research on the anterior mid-cingulate cortex.

The anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC) is one of the most exciting areas of current neuroscience research. A neurosurgeon at Stanford named Joe Parvizi was stimulating in patients' brains and found that stimulating the aMCC produced a remarkable experience: one patient said "I feel like something really bad is going to happen — like a storm is coming" but then added "I'm going into the storm." Stimulating a nearby area produced a completely different experience. Another patient said "I feel like I'm going to get out of my chair and do something hard." This was prior to any theoretical understanding of what the aMCC was doing.

Research now shows that people who successfully overcome physical challenges, cognitive challenges, or who learn new skills — including successful dieters — show growth or increased activation in the aMCC. This brain region seems to be the one that puts us in a forward center of mass, both physically and cognitively and emotionally.

The aMCC is a hub with inputs and outputs to many areas of the brain. It can access circuits related to dopamine, norepinephrine, memory, and context. Evidence from human brain imaging and brain stimulation all point to the idea that whenever we do something truly challenging — in particular things we do not enjoy — the aMCC undergoes some form of plasticity. The aMCC is increasingly understood as the seat of so-called willpower, linked to tenacity and grit.

Crucially, plasticity goes in both directions. When we avoid challenges, the aMCC does not atrophy but undergoes a downshift in activation. The aMCC is especially active at baseline and available for plasticity in so-called superagers — people who seem to age extremely slowly by cognitive and physiological measures. Superagers maintain cognitive function later in life, and this appears related to their regular engagement in challenging activities.

The aMCC is also connected to the will to live. In terminal cancer patients, those who insist psychologically on fighting their illness often live longer — more months, more years, and in some cases achieve remission. The aMCC has connectivity to many areas of the brain and body and seems to be linked to this will to persist.

## [00:22:40] Minimizing Circadian Disruption for Shift Workers

An audience member asks how shift workers can minimize the effects of circadian rhythm disruption.

Most people are doing shift work by the technical definition — the criteria being at least a 2-hour variance in the sleep-wake cycle more than three nights per week. Evening phone and computer use pushes most people into this pattern.

The ideal pattern of cortisol release is elevated early in the day, then subsiding across the day. A cortisol peak that is shifted late is associated with depression and anxiety, as shown in research by David Spiegel and Robert Sapolsky at Stanford. Late cortisol peaks are a central feature of all forms of shift work.

Light can be thought of like food: we now live in a blue-light-rich world, much like a diet rich in highly processed foods — calorie dense but micronutrient poor. Blue and ultraviolet light activate intrinsically photosensitive melanopsin cells in the eye, activating alertness circuits. This is why SAD lamps for seasonal affective disorder use bright, blue-white light.

For shift workers, one of the best tools is to filter out blue light while working at night. Wearing blue-light-blocking glasses or being in red or orange light conditions has been shown to reduce cortisol levels at night during shift work. This can be done inexpensively with simple colored lights — no expensive red light panels required.

When returning to a non-work environment, shift workers need to be deliberate about when they sleep and how they access morning light. ## [00:29:24] Difference Between NSDR and Meditation

An audience member asks about the difference between non-sleep deep rest and meditation.

Non-sleep deep rest (NSDR) is rooted in Yoga Nidra, a thousand-year-old practice in which the body lies completely still while the mind remains awake. It is not structured thinking — more of a body scan and directed relaxation. The practice was encountered in 2015 at a trauma and addiction clinic in Florida, where it was used to help addicts in early recovery regulate impulses, deal with agitation, and improve sleep.

Research from a Scandinavian laboratory showed that Yoga Nidra can increase dopamine levels in the striatum and basal ganglia by up to 65 percent, measured by positron emission tomography imaging. The body-still, brain-active state is a very powerful way to increase dopamine through non-pharmacological means. The brain enters states during Yoga Nidra that are similar to sleep — body still, mind alert — and this appears to accelerate neuroplasticity and learning.

The term "non-sleep deep rest" was created because Yoga Nidra sounds esoteric to many people, and the traditional practice includes intentions and mystical components that can deter people from approaching it. NSDR is a more accessible framing that captures what the practice does. NSDR is very effective at restoring cognitive and physical vigor, can offset some degree of sleep loss, and improves the ability to fall and stay asleep. Sessions run 10 to 30 minutes. Free NSDR scripts are available on YouTube by searching "NSDR Huberman." Kelly Boys also offers sessions, including on the Waking Up app. Ten Percent Happier offers additional Yoga Nidra scripts.

Meditation — for instance, third-eye or focused-attention meditation — involves closing the eyes, concentrating on a point, and redirecting attention to the breath whenever it drifts. Work from Wendy Suzuki's laboratory at New York University and the University of Wisconsin shows meditation can improve memory, focus, and has some stress-offsetting effects. However, it functions more as a focus exercise than as an energy replenishment exercise.

Self-directed hypnosis, studied by David Spiegel at Stanford, is distinct from both. Hypnosis engages neuroplasticity in a specific way — the brain enters a state that is simultaneously focused and relaxed, allowing neuroplasticity to be accessed more quickly. It is used for specific problems such as smoking cessation, where success rates exceed those of most other protocols. The Spiegel eye-roll test — looking upward at the ceiling and then closing the eyelids to see if the whites of the eyes remain visible — is a clinical measure of hypnotizability, reflecting how easily a person enters the combined alert-and-relaxed state.

## [00:37:32] Combatting Mindless Phone Scrolling

An audience member asks how to stop mindlessly scrolling on phones.

On the way to Australia, the podcast producer Rob asked everyone on the trip to delete social media from their phones for the entire trip. Having one person retain the apps for posting purposes, while everyone else deletes them, is presented as a realistic approach.

For social media scrolling specifically, the recommendation is a delete-and-reinstall cycle: delete the app from your phone and reinstall it each time you want to use it. The behavioral barrier — the steps involved in reinstalling — dramatically reduces use. This approach acknowledges that simply telling yourself not to open an app does not work, especially for younger generations.

A 24-year-old at Santa Clara University articulated something important: when his phone powered down, he felt energy drain out of his body, and when it came back on, life energy returned. This is a different relationship with technology than older generations have, and it reflects the reality of how deeply integrated these platforms are into daily experience. The approach of trying to block or dam the system entirely is unlikely to succeed — a better framework is being a channel, not a dam, directing the flow rather than stopping it.

Practical tools include setting timers, grayscaling the screen to remove color, or limiting access. Deleting and reinstalling remains the most effective friction-based approach.

## [00:42:18] Dream Clinical Trials

An audience member asks: if resources and ethics were not an issue, what would your dream clinical trial be?

Rather than answer directly, the question was opened to the audience, inviting them to shout out what they thought science needed more of. Topics raised from the audience included:

Psychedelics — specifically psilocybin and MDMA — as tools for treating depression, PTSD, and for enhancing neuroplasticity. The MAPS trials using MDMA for PTSD have shown over 60 percent remission rates when conducted with licensed physicians. MDMA is a concern for populations with bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, or in young people, but the broader evidence is generating enthusiasm.

Hyperbaric chambers — understood mechanically as changing the chemistry of the brain and body through altered oxygen concentrations, interesting for TBI and neurodegeneration.

Childhood trauma — trauma defined as an adverse event that changes the nervous system in a way that causes maladaptive functioning going forward. Existing approaches include EMDR, breath work, somatic release, and psychedelic-assisted therapy. A structured, unified framework for treating trauma is lacking. All approaches point toward neuroplasticity — rewiring the nervous system.

Consciousness — there was a well-known bet in neuroscience that consciousness would be solved by a certain date; it was not. Better definitions and mechanistic frameworks for consciousness are needed.

ADHD — a better understanding of what attention deficit disorder really reflects beyond extreme clinical cases, given how much the diagnosis has expanded. Two episodes of the Huberman Lab podcast cover attention: one focused on behavioral and nutritional tools, one on pharmacological tools.

The science of negotiation — how people can resolve differences better. The nervous system perspective: we are a highly capable species in terms of communication and meta-cognition, able to think in past, present, and future terms. Change is more likely to come through collective groups and panels than through individual leaders.

Genetics — including whole-genome sequencing of embryos to screen for mutations, CRISPR editing, and adult genome modification. Gene editing has already occurred in China, producing babies with a modified HIV receptor gene, raising significant international ethics concerns.

The microbiome — the gut microbiome is only one of several microbiomes. There are microbiomes on the skin, in the nose, mouth, and genitals, each important and understudied. The gut microbiome receives most of the attention but the others represent major research gaps.

Female hormones — female hormone research has received far less attention than male hormone research. The NIH only began requiring female mice in research approximately eight years ago. This is both a resource and a sociological issue, and the field is slowly changing.
