# Episode 333: LIVE EVENT Q&A — Dr. Andrew Huberman Question & Answer in Melbourne, AU

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Recorded at the Plenary Theater in Melbourne, Australia as part of The Brain Body Contract live event series, this episode presents the full Q&A session from the Melbourne stop. Huberman takes audience questions spanning dementia prevention, willpower neuroscience, shift work circadian disruption, NSDR versus meditation versus hypnosis, phone addiction, and dream clinical trials. The event closes with a call for broader scientific curiosity, public engagement, and collective research priorities nominated by the audience.

## Key Takeaways

- **Zone 2 cardio is the strongest evidence-based tool for dementia prevention and brain health.** Roughly 150–200 minutes per week at an intensity where you can barely maintain a conversation supports blood flow to the brain, releases BDNF, and modulates inflammatory and anti-inflammatory cytokines. Most things good for cardiovascular health are good for the brain. [00:02:50](transcript.md#000250-strategies-for-preventing-dementia)

- **Age-related cognitive decline is partly driven by falling dopamine transmission, not just neurodegeneration.** Working memory loss in older adults correlates with reduced catecholamine signaling. Zero-cost behavioral protocols to support dopamine — listed at hubermanlab.com under dopamine regulation — can meaningfully offset this. Compounds like nicotine, choline donors, modafinil, and amphetamines are being explored by some, each with significant caveats. [00:02:50](transcript.md#000250-strategies-for-preventing-dementia)

- **Coordinated physical skill learning offsets cognitive decline beyond what cardio alone provides.** Dance, martial arts, and learning new movement patterns engage the neuromuscular system in ways especially beneficial for cognitive longevity. The nervous system does not fully distinguish between physical and cognitive challenge — they operate through overlapping circuits. [00:02:50](transcript.md#000250-strategies-for-preventing-dementia)

- **The anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC) is the neural seat of willpower and strengthens when you do things you dislike.** Brain stimulation research by Stanford neurosurgeon Joe Parvizi showed that activating the aMCC produced the subjective sense of bracing for a storm and wanting to do something hard. Doing consistently challenging things — especially things you do not enjoy — causes the aMCC to grow. Avoiding challenge causes it to downshift. Superagers show hyperactive aMCC function at baseline by regularly engaging in genuinely difficult activities. [00:15:07](transcript.md#001507-enhancing-willpower-is-it-comparable-to-muscle-training)

- **The aMCC is linked to the will to live and survival of serious illness.** Terminal cancer patients who psychologically insist on fighting their illness tend to live longer — more months, more years, and in some cases achieve remission. The aMCC has broad connectivity to brain and body systems and appears to underlie the resolve to persist under extreme adversity. [00:15:07](transcript.md#001507-enhancing-willpower-is-it-comparable-to-muscle-training)

- **Shift work is more widespread than most people realize — evening phone use qualifies most people.** Any variance of 2+ hours in sleep-wake timing more than three nights per week meets the clinical definition. Late cortisol peaks — a direct consequence — are associated with depression and anxiety per research by David Spiegel and Robert Sapolsky at Stanford. Filtering blue light at night with blue-blocking glasses or red/orange ambient lighting measurably reduces nighttime cortisol and can be done cheaply. [00:22:40](transcript.md#002240-minimizing-circadian-disruption-for-shift-workers)

- **NSDR restores energy and improves sleep; meditation trains focus; hypnosis enables specific targeted change.** NSDR, rooted in Yoga Nidra, increases dopamine in the striatum by up to 65% as measured by PET imaging in a Scandinavian lab. It restores mental and physical vigor, partially offsets sleep loss, and improves sleep onset. Meditation is primarily a focus-training tool. Self-directed hypnosis, studied by David Spiegel at Stanford, accesses neuroplasticity around specific problems — smoking cessation success rates exceed most other interventions. [00:29:24](transcript.md#002924-difference-between-nsdr-and-meditation)

- **The most effective way to curb mindless phone scrolling is delete-and-reinstall friction.** Removing social media apps and reinstalling each time adds enough behavioral steps to dramatically reduce impulsive use. Simply resolving not to open an app is insufficient, particularly for younger generations who experience the phone as an extension of their life energy. Grayscaling the screen and setting timers are secondary tools. [00:37:32](transcript.md#003732-combatting-mindless-phone-scrolling)

- **MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD is showing 60%+ remission rates in MAPS clinical trials.** These trials were conducted by licensed physicians and represent a major shift in trauma treatment. MDMA is contraindicated for people with bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, and for youth. The biggest safety concern in unregulated settings is fentanyl contamination. [00:42:18](transcript.md#004218-dream-clinical-trials)

- **Childhood trauma treatment lacks a unified framework despite many partially effective tools.** EMDR, breath work, somatic release, and psychedelic-assisted therapy all target the same mechanism: neuroplasticity and nervous system rewiring. A structured, integrated protocol that synthesizes these does not yet exist and represents a major research gap. [00:42:18](transcript.md#004218-dream-clinical-trials)

- **Female hormones and the non-gut microbiomes are critically understudied.** The NIH only began requiring female mice in research approximately eight years ago. Skin, nasal, oral, and genital microbiomes are each distinct communities that receive far less research attention than the gut microbiome — each represents a major understudied field. [00:42:18](transcript.md#004218-dream-clinical-trials)

- **Consciousness remains unsolved and genetics ethics lag behind capability.** A well-known neuroscience bet that consciousness would be mechanistically explained by a certain date was lost. CRISPR gene editing has already produced modified babies in China (modified HIV receptor gene), raising unresolved international ethics concerns. Whole-genome embryo screening is now possible, with ethical frameworks still catching up. [00:42:18](transcript.md#004218-dream-clinical-trials)
