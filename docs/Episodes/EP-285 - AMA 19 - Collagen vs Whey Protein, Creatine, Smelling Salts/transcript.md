## Protein Sources

The first question for today's AMA is: "Is there a distinct health advantage to using bone broth or collagen protein versus whey protein?" I get this question pretty often, and I touched on it in the episode I did with Dr. Lane Norton. The key thing to remember is that while protein is one of the three macronutrients — proteins, carbohydrates, and fats — not all proteins are created equal. What differentiates different protein sources has a lot to do with how easily those proteins are assimilated into our body and their amino acid content.

How bioavailable a protein is, as well as its quality or protein score, relates to a number of things — not the least of which is the amount of leucine, which is a particular essential amino acid that we need to get from food. Leucine is important because it's an essential amino acid you must get from food, and some protein sources such as whey protein, beef, chicken, and eggs have high levels of leucine compared to other types of protein — for instance, collagen protein and some bone broths. Whey protein's high leucine content makes it the superior form of protein if your goal is to grow muscle, get stronger, repair muscle damage caused by exercise, or simply engage protein synthesis. Even if you haven't been exercising intensely, dietary proteins that contain relatively high amounts of leucine — like whey protein — will help induce so-called muscle protein synthesis, which is generally beneficial.

## Comparing Whey Protein, Bone Broth, & Collagen

Through the lens of muscle building and recovery, a quality whey protein is the better choice compared to bone broth or collagen protein, which have relatively low amounts of leucine. Across the entire essential amino acid profile, whey protein is clearly superior — both based on its bioavailability and its amino acid composition.

That said, this does not mean bone broth and collagen protein are without value. This has also come up in the episode I did with Dr. Lane Norton and the episode I did about skin health. There are some data — not a ton, but statistically significant data — showing that people who regularly ingest collagen protein observe improvements in skin elasticity and appearance. Bone broth has high amounts of collagen, so bone broth and collagen protein can be considered together here. The amino acids in bone broth and collagen protein have been shown to support skin elasticity and appearance when ingested at levels of 15 grams per day over a period of about two weeks or more.

So through the lens of skin health, bone broth and collagen protein are superior to whey protein for that specific outcome. However, keep in mind that they contain calories — protein and sometimes fat — so check the packaging carefully. The question then becomes whether you should take both. Whey protein does not replace the skin-health benefits of collagen — there are no studies showing whey protein improves skin health and appearance. What whey protein does do is support muscle protein synthesis, overall health, immune health, and your general sense of vigor, creating a general milieu of health in the right context.

## Protein Intake Recommendations

When it comes to how much protein, I believe most people who are seeking muscle protein synthesis, recovery from exercise, and general health would do well to ingest approximately 1 gram of quality protein per pound of lean body weight or desired body weight. Some people simplify this to 1 gram per pound of total body weight per day — that works too, and you don't need to be neurotic about hitting that number every single day.

I'm about 210 pounds and get anywhere from 175 to 210 grams of protein per day, which I know from having tracked it recently. If you're in a limited-budget situation — which most people are — I'd suggest getting 60% to 70% of that total from whole food sources: quality lean meats, chicken, eggs, fish. If you're vegetarian, combinations like beans and rice can achieve the proper essential amino acid balance, and there are plant-based sources that meet all amino acid needs. The remaining 30% or so — and up to 50% if you're having trouble eating enough during the day — can come from a protein powder or protein bar, and whey protein is an excellent source in that case.

Whey proteins are available with minimal or zero carbohydrate, some with sweeteners like stevia, some without. They vary in cost, flavor, and mixability, so you have to find what works for you. Bone broth and collagen protein will count toward your daily protein total, but evaluate them with the understanding that they don't score as well on bioavailability and leucine content as whey protein.

## Whey Protein & Skin Health

One last important point about skin health as it relates to whey protein. In the episode I did about skin health, and when I had Dr. Teo Soleimani on the podcast — he's a derm onc, a dermatologist oncologist specializing in skin cancers, but also the cosmetic aspects of skin care including elasticity, rosacea, psoriasis, and acne — it was clear that one of the things shown to contribute to acne in some people is having a high leucine content in the diet.

Whey protein has been evaluated for its tendency to increase acne in some people, and it seems to be related to leucine's ability to increase certain components of the cell growth pathway involving mTOR — the mammalian Target of Rapamycin. Leucine and other components of whey protein that are effective in increasing insulin lead to increases in mTOR, which in turn lead to changes in the skin that sometimes show up as increased acne. I don't think you should be afraid of whey protein because of acne risk — what seems to happen is that the leucine-mTOR-insulin interaction in some individuals, not all, can exacerbate skin symptoms.

If you have issues with acne, try removing whey protein for a couple of weeks and observe whether symptoms improve. For women in particular, whose hormone cycles fluctuate across the month, whey protein and other high-leucine protein sources may exacerbate or create acne at particular phases of their cycle, due to the interaction between the mTOR pathway and hormones that fluctuate during the menstrual cycle. If you see that pattern, you might stop taking whey during that phase and take it only during others. For men with troubling acne, consider removing whey protein altogether and replacing it with casein protein or another high-quality protein to observe your reaction. The overarching principle: become a scientist of yourself — try something, observe the results, ask yourself why, and adjust accordingly.
