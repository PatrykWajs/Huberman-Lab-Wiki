# Episode 285: AMA 19 - Collagen vs Whey Protein, Creatine, Smelling Salts

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

> **Note:** This summary covers the publicly available portion (~18 min) of a Premium AMA. The full episode also covers creatine and water retention, creatine for brain health, creatine use in teenagers, smelling salts safety and effects, caffeine, nicotine, and prescription stimulants.

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman answers whether bone broth or collagen protein holds a distinct health advantage over whey protein — or vice versa. He explains that leucine content and bioavailability determine protein quality for muscle protein synthesis, and that the two protein types serve different goals: collagen for skin health, whey for muscle. He also covers how high-leucine diets can exacerbate acne in some individuals via the leucine–insulin–mTOR pathway, and provides a practical daily protein intake framework.

## Key Takeaways

- **Not all proteins are equal — leucine is the key differentiator:** Whey protein, beef, chicken, and eggs have relatively high leucine; collagen protein and bone broth have relatively low leucine. Leucine is an essential amino acid that must come from food. [00:05:13](transcript.md#protein-sources)
- **Whey protein is superior for muscle protein synthesis:** Its high leucine content and bioavailability make it the best protein powder for building muscle, exercise recovery, and stimulating protein synthesis even without intense training. [00:05:13](transcript.md#protein-sources)
- **Collagen and bone broth are superior for skin health:** 15 g/day of collagen protein over at least two weeks produces statistically significant improvements in skin elasticity and appearance. Bone broth is a whole-food source of collagen and can be used interchangeably. [00:07:30](transcript.md#comparing-whey-protein-bone-broth--collagen)
- **Whey does not replace collagen for skin:** There are no studies showing whey protein improves skin health. The two types address different goals and can be used together without conflict. [00:07:30](transcript.md#comparing-whey-protein-bone-broth--collagen)
- **Target ~1 g of quality protein per pound of body weight per day:** Huberman himself gets 175–210 g/day at ~210 lbs. Neurotic tracking is not necessary; hitting the range consistently is what matters. [00:09:00](transcript.md#protein-intake-recommendations)
- **Get 60–70% of daily protein from whole food sources:** Lean meats, chicken, eggs, and fish are preferred. Up to 50% can come from protein powder (e.g., whey) when whole food intake falls short. Vegetarian options like beans and rice can meet full essential amino acid needs when combined. [00:09:00](transcript.md#protein-intake-recommendations)
- **High leucine intake can exacerbate acne via the leucine–insulin–mTOR pathway:** Leucine raises insulin → activates mTOR (mammalian Target of Rapamycin) → drives skin changes associated with acne. This is not universal; individual susceptibility varies. [00:12:45](transcript.md#whey-protein--skin-health)
- **Women may experience cycle-phase-dependent acne from high-leucine proteins:** Fluctuating hormones during the menstrual cycle interact with mTOR pathway activation, making acne worse at specific phases. Consider removing whey only during those phases rather than entirely. [00:12:45](transcript.md#whey-protein--skin-health)
- **If whey causes acne, switch to casein:** Casein (milk protein) has a strong amino acid profile and does not appear to carry the same acne association as whey. Remove whey for two weeks first to confirm it is the cause. [00:12:45](transcript.md#whey-protein--skin-health)
- **Treat yourself as a scientist:** Run a controlled self-experiment — try a specific change for a defined period, observe the results, ask why, and adjust. This is the most reliable path to personal optimization. [00:12:45](transcript.md#whey-protein--skin-health)

## Tools & Protocols

### Daily Protein Intake Framework
- **What:** A practical approach to meeting daily protein targets for muscle synthesis and general health
- **How:** Aim for ~1 g protein per pound of body weight per day; 60–70% from whole foods (lean meat, chicken, fish, eggs); 30–40% (up to 50% if needed) from a high-quality protein powder such as whey; bone broth and collagen count toward the daily total but score lower on leucine and bioavailability
- **When:** Daily, ongoing
- **Ref:** [00:09:00](transcript.md#protein-intake-recommendations)

### Collagen / Bone Broth for Skin Health
- **What:** Daily collagen protein supplementation to improve skin elasticity and appearance
- **How:** 15 g/day of collagen protein or bone broth; check packaging for calorie content from fat; counts toward daily protein total
- **When:** Daily; minimum two weeks before assessing results; benefits accumulate over time
- **Ref:** [00:07:30](transcript.md#comparing-whey-protein-bone-broth--collagen)

### Whey Protein Acne Elimination Test
- **What:** Temporary removal of whey protein to determine whether it is contributing to acne
- **How:** Eliminate whey completely for two weeks; observe skin changes; if improvement is seen, switch to casein or another high-quality protein; women can also try removing whey only during acne-prone cycle phases
- **When:** Any time acne is a concern, particularly for women at specific phases of the menstrual cycle
- **Ref:** [00:12:45](transcript.md#whey-protein--skin-health)

### Evening Light Management
- **What:** Blocking short-wavelength (blue and green) light when exposed to bright artificial light at night
- **How:** Wear blue/green-blocking glasses (e.g., ROKA red lens) or dim all lights after dark; prevents short-wavelength light from suppressing melatonin and elevating cortisol
- **When:** Evening / after sunset, whenever in bright artificial light
- **Ref:** [00:00:30](transcript.md#introduction)
