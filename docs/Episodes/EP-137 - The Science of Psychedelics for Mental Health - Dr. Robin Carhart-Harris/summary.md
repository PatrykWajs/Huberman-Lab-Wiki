# Episode 137: The Science of Psychedelics for Mental Health | Dr. Robin Carhart-Harris

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Robin Carhart-Harris — distinguished professor of neurology and psychiatry at UCSF and one of the world's leading psychedelic researchers — joins Andrew Huberman to explain how classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT) alter brain networks, induce neuroplasticity, and produce lasting relief from major depression, anorexia, OCD, addiction, fibromyalgia, and chronic pain. The conversation covers the pharmacology of the serotonin 2A receptor, the structure of a clinical psychedelic session (preparation, journey, integration), the evidence (and limits) of microdosing, ego dissolution, combination psilocybin-MDMA therapy, and the road to FDA approval for both MDMA- and psilocybin-assisted therapies.

## Guest: Dr. Robin Carhart-Harris

Dr. Robin Carhart-Harris is a distinguished professor of neurology and psychiatry at the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF), where he leads the Neuroscape Psychedelics Division. He is one of the most cited researchers in psychedelic science, known for landmark brain-imaging studies on psilocybin, LSD, and DMT, the coining of the "entropic brain" hypothesis, and a landmark *New England Journal of Medicine* trial comparing psilocybin to the SSRI escitalopram for depression.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The word "psychedelic" means mind-manifesting — and it matters.** The term was coined by Humphry Osmond in 1956 correspondence with Aldous Huxley. It refers to compounds that act as direct agonists at the serotonin 2A receptor and reveal unconscious mental content. Many substances colloquially called "psychedelic" do not meet this pharmacological criterion. [00:06:31](transcript.md#000631-origin-of-the-word-psychedelics-pharmacology)

2. **Classic psychedelics primarily work through the serotonin 2A receptor, expressed in "recent brain."** Psilocybin, LSD, and DMT are all potent agonists at the 5-HT2A receptor. These receptors are densely expressed in the cortical regions that expanded most in human evolution — if you map cortical expansion from macaque to human, it matches the 2A receptor distribution. Their activation is responsible for the subjective psychedelic experience and the dramatic increase in global brain connectivity. [00:46:21](transcript.md#004621-global-functional-connectivity-serotonin-2a-receptor-subjective-experiences)

3. **Psilocybin dramatically increases global functional connectivity in the brain — during and after the session.** Under psilocybin, normally cliquish modular networks start communicating cross-modularly. This has been replicated with LSD and a forthcoming paper shows the same effect with DMT. Crucially, the decrease in modularity is also seen the next day and three weeks later in two independent depression cohorts, and its magnitude correlates with the degree of symptom improvement. [00:46:21](transcript.md#004621-global-functional-connectivity-serotonin-2a-receptor-subjective-experiences)

4. **A single 25 mg psilocybin session produces structural brain changes in first-time users.** In a completed (unpublished) study of healthy middle-aged volunteers (~age 40, never taken a psychedelic), diffusion tensor imaging showed changes in white matter tract integrity (prefrontal–thalamus and prefrontal–striatum) — a decrease in axial diffusivity in the same direction as normal neurodevelopment, opposite to aging pathology. Cognitive flexibility also improved. The EEG showed the "entropic brain effect": increased informational complexity of brain activity correlating with subjective intensity. [01:19:50](transcript.md#011950-first-time-psychedelic-use-entropic-brain-effect-neuroplasticity-cognition)

5. **Psilocybin outperformed escitalopram (Lexapro) for depression in a landmark NEJM trial, and it is not just a placebo effect.** The 2021 trial gave two 25 mg psilocybin sessions vs. six weeks of escitalopram. Psilocybin showed greater improvement on most measures including emotional blunting and well-being. Critically, pre-trial expectancy predicted response to escitalopram but not to psilocybin, ruling out expectancy as the driver of the psychedelic outcome. [00:58:45](transcript.md#005845-psilocybin--depression-long-term-effects-connectivity--neuroplasticity)

6. **The psychedelic experience itself is therapeutically necessary — not a side effect to eliminate.** Multiple independent groups converge: the magnitude of in-session subjective experience predicts clinical outcome. Efforts to develop non-hallucinogenic analogs may remove the very mechanism. Sub-perceptible microdosing is, by the etymology of the word, not a psychedelic (psyche-revealing) action — any benefit would come from the pharmacology alone, which SSRIs already provide via indirect 5-HT2A stimulation. [00:28:28](transcript.md#002828-psychedelic-therapy-music) [00:52:33](transcript.md#005233-pharmacology-therapeutics-without-psychedelic-effects-ssris)

7. **The mantra "trust, let go, be open" is the core clinical instruction — and it is measurable.** Attributed to Bill Richards at Johns Hopkins, this phrase is instilled during preparation sessions. Therapeutic rapport measured on the morning of dosing significantly predicts experience quality and outcomes weeks later. Readiness to surrender (letting go) is also formally measured and predicts response. Resistance commonly arises in the first one to two hours as normal waking consciousness breaks down. [00:36:26](transcript.md#003626-psychedelic-journey-trust-let-go-be-open)

8. **The psychedelic journey has a distinct early phase of struggle followed by resolution.** Research by Harry Brouwer in Carhart-Harris's lab characterizes the early phase as dominated by negative emotions — fear of dying, fear of going mad — as the drug dismantles the sense of a stable self. This is a basic, dose-dependent drug action. The latter half typically resolves into more open, positive states. Both phases are therapeutically relevant; the early resistance is the ego's defenses loosening. [00:43:01](transcript.md#004301-negative-emotions-fear--psychedelics)

9. **Integration is a lifelong practice, not a single debrief.** Post-session integration with a therapist consolidates insights into new patterns. Meditation is specifically synergistic: it promotes the ability to "sit with" rather than "sit on" difficult material. Carhart-Harris invokes Jack Kornfield's phrase: "After the ecstasy, the laundry." He frames psychedelic therapy as a "practice" analogous to meditation — something that must be maintained. The therapy team can support to a point, then it becomes the patient's own practice. [01:12:56](transcript.md#011256-integration-phase--psychedelic-therapy-meditation)

10. **Microdosing is scientifically unproven and likely driven by placebo.** Balazs Szigeti's self-blinding citizen science trial (LSD and psilocybin) found that positive expectancy fully accounted for perceived benefit. Those who thought they received a microdose but received placebo did as well as those who actually received one. [00:17:32](transcript.md#001732-microdosing)

11. **DMT and 5-MeO-DMT are rapid-onset, brief, and extremely intense classic psychedelics.** Both are direct 5-HT2A agonists — "rocket ships" compared to psilocybin's "ship leaving port." DMT is an order of magnitude less potent by receptor affinity but dose-matched produces comparably wild experiences. 5-MeO-DMT is described as producing more reliable and total ego dissolution — less visual, more complete immersion in a sense of interconnectedness. The Veterans Solutions Initiative uses ibogaine followed by 5-MeO-DMT for trauma in special operations veterans. [01:56:06](transcript.md#015606-dmt-rocketship--serotonin-2a-receptors-ibogaine)

12. **Ego dissolution is the pharmacological opposite of cocaine's ego inflation.** Carhart-Harris's lab directly compared cocaine and psychedelics on ego inflation vs. ego dissolution axes: cocaine inflates and does not dissolve; psychedelics dissolve and do not inflate. The "entropic brain" effect of 5-HT2A activation spreads and scrambles cortical activity, expanding the headspace and collapsing the start/stop boundaries of self. MDMA softens but does not fully dissolve the ego — it functions more as an empathogen, reducing the emotional barrier to re-engaging with trauma. [02:01:04](transcript.md#020104-ego-dissolution-cocaine-vs-psychedelics-relapses)

13. **MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD is years ahead of psilocybin in the regulatory pipeline.** MAPS completed two Phase III trials for PTSD (~67% remission rates) and filed data with the FDA, expecting a decision as early as 2023–2024 with rollout the following year. Psilocybin (Compass Pathways, treatment-resistant depression) only just entered Phase III as of 2023, with results expected around 2026. MDMA sessions involve more dialogue and less eye-mask use than classic psychedelic therapy; the MAPS model uses an optional redose (booster) mid-session. [02:17:54](transcript.md#021754-mdma-trauma--clinical-trials-future-regulatory-fda-approval)

14. **Relapse after psychedelic therapy is real, and Schedule I status blocks re-dosing.** In treatment-resistant depression, most participants relapse beyond the three-month mark. Re-dosing psilocybin would be the logical clinical response, but patients have no legal path to it outside a trial. Carhart-Harris calls this "a sick joke." Some participants from his first TRD trial are still well today; they are the exception, not the rule. [02:01:04](transcript.md#020104-ego-dissolution-cocaine-vs-psychedelics-relapses)

## Tools & Protocols

### Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Session Setup

- **What:** The standard clinical container for psilocybin therapy used across leading research sites (Imperial College, Johns Hopkins, NYU, UCSF). Remarkably consistent across all published trials.
- **How:** Patient lies on a sofa in a comfortable, non-clinical room. Eye mask worn throughout to encourage inward attention. A curated music playlist plays continuously — begins spacious and ambient, builds atmospheric and emotionally evocative mid-session (strings, tribal elements), resolves gently. Two trained therapists/guides present throughout; ideally mental health professionals (at minimum one psychiatrist, clinical psychologist, psychotherapist, or psychiatric nurse). Preparation sessions in the weeks before explore patient history, intentions, and fears. Dose typically 25 mg psilocybin. FDA requires patients remain on-site for at least 8 hours post-dose. Patient is not directed to think about symptoms — the psyche is trusted to surface what is relevant.
- **When:** One to two sessions spaced one to three weeks apart for most depression trials. Three sessions (two weeks apart) used in the UCSF anorexia trial.
- **Ref:** [00:28:28](transcript.md#002828-psychedelic-therapy-music); [00:36:26](transcript.md#003626-psychedelic-journey-trust-let-go-be-open)

---

### Integration Practice

- **What:** Deliberate post-session work to consolidate insights and translate them into lasting psychological change.
- **How:** Structured integration therapy sessions with a trained therapist in the days and weeks following the dose. Reflective practices include journaling and meditation. Meditation is particularly synergistic — both psilocybin and sustained meditation promote the ability to sit with difficult material rather than suppress it. The heightened plasticity window post-session is the optimal time to build new habits and process difficult material. Carhart-Harris frames it as a practice that must be maintained, like meditation; if it slips, things can slip.
- **When:** Begins the day after the dose session and continues as long as needed — weeks to months to indefinitely.
- **Ref:** [01:12:56](transcript.md#011256-integration-phase--psychedelic-therapy-meditation)

---

### "Trust, Let Go, Be Open" — Journey Mindset

- **What:** The core instruction given to patients before a psychedelic session, attributed to Bill Richards at Johns Hopkins, and used across all major research sites.
- **How:** Trust the process, the therapists, and the drug's action (therapeutic rapport measured pre-session predicts outcomes). Let go of attempts to control or steer the experience — readiness to surrender is formally measured and predicts therapeutic response. Be open to whatever content arises, including fear, grief, or disturbing imagery. When resistance arises in the first one to two hours, recognize it as the self's defenses loosening — breathe through it rather than fighting it.
- **When:** Internalized during preparation sessions; actively applied during the psychedelic journey itself.
- **Ref:** [00:36:26](transcript.md#003626-psychedelic-journey-trust-let-go-be-open); [00:43:01](transcript.md#004301-negative-emotions-fear--psychedelics)
