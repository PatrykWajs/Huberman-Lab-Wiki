## [00:06:33] Romantic Relationships, Change & Self

**Andrew Huberman:** And now for my discussion with Esther Perel. Esther Perel, welcome.

**Esther Perel:** Thank you. It's a pleasure to be here.

**Andrew Huberman:** There are so many questions and curiosities and puzzles and challenges around the topic of romantic relationships. What I really want to know is: to what extent is the decision to even think about being in a romantic relationship the result of wanting something that's external to us — like choosing a certain career, or a certain group of friends — versus something that reflects who we are as an individual?

**Esther Perel:** That's a very good starting question because it goes directly to the question of the relationship between self and other. When we enter a relationship, we are at the same time extending ourselves — an extension of who we are — and we are also hoping to gain access to parts of ourselves or parts of the world that we can't reach on our own. So there's both an element of "I am bringing myself" and "I am hoping to become something more or different."

People are often attracted to those who represent parts of themselves that they disavow — the qualities they wish they had, the qualities they were not allowed to have, the qualities that remind them of something important. At the same time, these are often the very qualities that become sources of conflict later on. What attracted you to someone can easily become the source of the problem. "You used to be so spontaneous" becomes "you never plan anything."

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a fascinating insight — that the very things that attract us become sources of conflict. Can you say more about that?

**Esther Perel:** We have two fundamental sets of needs that are in constant tension with each other. On the one hand, we need security, predictability, safety, dependability, reliability — and these come from togetherness, closeness, familiarity. On the other hand, we need freedom, novelty, mystery, risk, adventure, and the unknown — and these come from separateness, from difference.

These two needs don't go away. They live inside each of us and they live inside the relationship. And the person who is your anchor is often also the person you sometimes feel is pulling you down. The person who represents adventure is also the person who sometimes makes you feel unmoored.

"We want change, but up to a point and not on your terms." That's a sentence that captures so much of what happens in relationships. People want the relationship to evolve, but they want it to evolve in the direction they choose, at the speed they choose, in the manner they choose.

## [00:11:18] Cornerstone vs. Capstone Relationships, Age Differences

**Andrew Huberman:** I've heard you use the terms "cornerstone" and "capstone" in the context of relationships. Can you explain what those mean?

**Esther Perel:** These are sociological terms that describe when in life a couple comes together and what the relationship is meant to do in their lives at that time.

A cornerstone relationship is one where you meet early in life — typically in your early twenties or even younger — and you build your life together. The relationship is the foundation upon which you construct everything else: your career, your values, your identity, your sense of who you are. You grow up together, and the relationship shapes who you become as adults.

A capstone relationship is one where you meet later — perhaps in your thirties, forties, or beyond — when you have already built a life, already developed a strong sense of who you are, already established your career and your values. Here the relationship is more like the crowning piece of an already-constructed edifice. You are not building yourself through the relationship; you are bringing yourself to the relationship.

**Andrew Huberman:** And those two types of relationships face very different challenges?

**Esther Perel:** Completely different developmental mandates. The cornerstone couple faces the challenge of: can the relationship expand enough to accommodate the people you are becoming? Sometimes the couple grows together, sometimes one partner grows in a direction the relationship cannot follow. It's like a jacket — is the jacket too tight? You've grown but the jacket doesn't have room to grow with you.

The capstone couple faces a different challenge: you each arrive with a fully formed identity, fully established habits, preferences, a certain way of doing things, and suddenly you have to negotiate all of that with another person who has their own fully formed world. The flexibility required is enormous, and the willingness to adapt is much harder when you're older and more set in your ways.

And then there are the age-difference relationships — evolutionary biology, fertility, power dynamics, all come into play. And we're seeing something new: older women with younger men, which historically was much rarer. The modern landscape of relationships is much more plastic and varied.

## [00:16:53] Young vs. Older Couples, Dynamic Relationships

**Andrew Huberman:** When you're working with young couples versus older couples, what are the fundamental differences in what they're struggling with?

**Esther Perel:** Young couples often struggle with the tension between individuation and merging. They're in the process of still defining who they are, and the relationship is part of that definition. They're asking: who am I in this relationship? Who am I becoming? Does this person see me as who I am or as who I was?

Older couples often struggle with the accumulated weight of history — the patterns that have calcified, the resentments that have built up, the stories they've told about each other and about themselves that have become rigid. They have a longer history to carry.

But what both share is the need for the relationship to feel alive — not just functional, not just stable, but actually alive. A relationship can be "not dead" and still not be living. There's a big difference between absence of conflict and presence of vitality.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you elaborate on what "alive" means in the context of a relationship?

**Esther Perel:** A relationship that is alive is one where there is curiosity — where the partners are still interested in each other, still capable of surprising each other, still growing individually and together. It's a relationship where there is play, where there is imagination, where there is a sense of possibility. Where the future is not just a continuation of the present but contains something that hasn't happened yet.

A relationship that is "not dead" is one where people are coexisting reasonably peaceably, meeting their practical obligations, not fighting too much — but where the energy has gone flat. Where predictability has become deadness. Where familiarity has become invisibility. These are very different states.

## [00:20:13] Identity & Relationship Evolution

**Andrew Huberman:** How do people navigate the evolution of their identity within a long-term relationship?

**Esther Perel:** In the modern Western world, people have the experience of two to three relationships in adult life — and sometimes those relationships are with the same person. We live longer, we change more, and our expectations of what a relationship should provide are far more complex and demanding than they were for previous generations.

The key is what I would describe as the ability to redefine oneself and to redefine a relationship. This is more of a creative act than a problem-solving act. Most of us are trained to think of relationship difficulties as problems to be solved — as if there's a correct answer that, once found, will make everything work. But many of the fundamental tensions in a relationship are not problems; they are paradoxes. You don't solve a paradox; you learn to manage it, to live with it creatively.

Erik Erikson's work on developmental stages is relevant here. He described how at each stage of life we face new challenges — new developmental tasks. The same is true for relationships. A relationship has its own developmental stages, and what worked beautifully at one stage may no longer fit at the next.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the relationship itself has to evolve?

**Esther Perel:** The relationship has to be alive, not just not dead. And for it to be alive, the people in it have to be willing to allow it to change — which means being willing to lose the relationship as it was in order to have the relationship as it could become. That is an act of courage and of mourning simultaneously. You grieve what you're leaving behind even as you're excited about what might be ahead.

## [00:26:00] Curiosity, Reactivity

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked about curiosity being an antidote to reactivity. Can you unpack that?

**Esther Perel:** Curiosity is one of the top words for me when I think about what makes relationships work. And it stands in opposition to reactivity — not just in relationships but in life more generally.

Reactivity is a closed loop. When we are reactive, we are operating from the assumption that we already know — we already know what the other person meant, we already know what's happening, we already know how to interpret what just happened. And from that knowing, we respond — usually with defense, or with attack, or with blame, or with withdrawal. The response reinforces the original pattern, creates escalation, and locks both people into increasingly rigid positions.

Curiosity is an open loop. When we are curious, we are operating from the assumption that we don't fully know — that there might be something we haven't understood, something we haven't seen. Curiosity involves active engagement with the unknown without attachment to a particular outcome. It asks: what is happening here? What is this really about? What am I not seeing?

**Andrew Huberman:** But when we're in conflict with someone we love, curiosity seems very hard to access.

**Esther Perel:** That's exactly the challenge. When we feel threatened — and conflict in a close relationship is a form of threat, because it threatens the connection and safety we depend on — the nervous system activates fight, flight, or freeze. We become reactive. The physiology takes over.

That's why one of the things I work on with people is recognizing the physiological state they're in before they can do anything else. You can't access curiosity when you're flooded. You can't engage the prefrontal cortex when the amygdala has taken the wheel. So the first step is often just recognizing: I'm in a reactive state right now. That recognition itself — that meta-awareness — is the beginning of a shift.

And then the question becomes: what is my subjective experience here, and how does it differ from objective fact? We often experience our interpretations as facts. "You don't care about me" feels like a fact. But it's an interpretation — a story we're telling, based on something that happened. Separating the experience from the interpretation is a crucial skill.

## [00:31:59] Polarization, Conflict; Coherence & Narratives

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to ask about narrative — the stories couples tell. You've described this idea that people need coherence in their narratives. What do you mean by that?

**Esther Perel:** We are narrative creatures. We make sense of our experience through story. And we need the story to be coherent — we need it to hang together, to have a logic, a beginning, middle, and end. We need to be the protagonist of a story that makes sense.

When couples fight, they engage in what looks like a factual argument — "that's what happened," "no, that's what happened" — but is actually a battle of subjective experiences. Each person's account is internally coherent from their own perspective. The problem is that coherence requires completeness, and each person's account has gaps — things they haven't noticed, things they don't remember, things they've filtered out because they didn't fit the story.

I have a metaphor for this: the Swiss cheese metaphor. One person's story has holes in it. The other person's story has holes in a different place. What one person has left out is exactly where the other person starts. And they argue as if one of them has the truth, when in fact they each have a piece of it.

**Andrew Huberman:** That seems connected to what's happening in society more broadly — the polarization we see everywhere.

**Esther Perel:** Very much so. What I see in the therapy room with couples is a microcosm of what is happening in society. Polarization — the inability to hold complexity, the need to have a simple coherent narrative even at the cost of accuracy, the demonization of the other side — these are not just political phenomena. They are human phenomena.

And the work in couples therapy is, in a way, the same work that needs to happen at the societal level: creating space for multiple narratives to coexist without one needing to defeat the other. Acknowledging that the other person's experience is real even if it's different from yours. That "both/and" thinking rather than "either/or" thinking.

What troubles me about the current moment is that "pseudo-factual talk" — talk that presents subjective experience as objective fact — has become the dominant mode of discourse. "If I feel it, this is what happened." That conflation of feeling and fact is at the root of so much polarization, in couples and in society.

## [00:38:21] Apologies, Forgiveness, Shame, Self-Esteem

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about apologies. What makes a sincere apology?

**Esther Perel:** An apology has multiple components, and the most important one is often the one that people leave out. People apologize by saying "I'm sorry I did that" — which is an acknowledgment of the act. But a sincere apology must also acknowledge the impact on the other person. "I'm sorry I did that, and I can see how much it hurt you. It makes total sense that you'd feel that way."

That second part — the acknowledgment of the other person's experience — is what makes an apology genuinely healing. Without it, the apology is about the apologizer's guilt, not about the other person's pain.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about receiving an apology? You've talked about cases where people can't receive a sincere apology even when it's offered.

**Esther Perel:** This is equally important and equally difficult. Some people can't receive a sincere apology because accepting it feels like minimizing what happened to them. If I accept your apology, I'm saying it wasn't that bad — and it was that bad, and I need you to know it. So they hold onto the grievance as a way of maintaining the seriousness of the injury.

Others can't receive an apology because their identity has become organized around being the wronged party. If I forgive you, I lose my identity as someone who was wronged. And that identity carries with it certain rights and powers — the right to moral authority, the right to certain compensations.

In the Jewish tradition, there's a beautiful concept: if you apologize sincerely three times and the other person still won't accept it, the burden passes to them. The wrongdoer has done their part. At that point, the refusal to forgive becomes the other person's responsibility, their own wound to carry.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the relationship between accepting an apology and forgiving someone?

**Esther Perel:** They're not the same thing. Accepting an apology is about the interaction between two people. Forgiveness is something you do for yourself. Forgiveness is your freedom. It's the decision to release yourself from carrying this injury as a defining part of your identity — not because what happened didn't matter, but because continuing to carry it is harming you more than it's harming the person who hurt you.

You can forgive someone without accepting their apology. You can forgive someone who never apologized. And you can accept someone's apology without fully forgiving them — at least not yet.

**Andrew Huberman:** And shame — how does shame interfere with apology?

**Esther Perel:** Shame is one of the main obstacles to apology. Shame says: "I am bad." And if I am bad, then apologizing confirms my badness. It's unbearable. Guilt, by contrast, says: "I did something bad." Guilt can move toward repair because it's about the act, not the self.

Terrence Real has a wonderful definition of self-esteem that I love: self-esteem is the ability to see yourself as a flawed and limited person and still hold yourself in high regard. That's what allows people to apologize — the capacity to acknowledge their failure without being destroyed by it.

## [00:45:00] Relationship Conflict

**Andrew Huberman:** When you're working with a couple in conflict, what are you observing? What are you looking for?

**Esther Perel:** I'm watching the choreography — the dance between them. Not just what they're saying, but how they're moving in relation to each other. Are they moving toward each other or away from each other? Is one pursuing and the other fleeing? Are they both escalating? Are they both shutting down?

I'm also watching the moment when the conversation turns — when it shifts from problem-solving to something more primitive, more visceral. That shift usually signals that we're no longer dealing with the presenting issue but with something deeper.

One of the questions I often ask a couple at the beginning of a session is not "what happened?" but "what have you done this week to make things better?" That question shifts the frame from the narrative of grievance to the narrative of agency. It asks each person to take responsibility for the direction the relationship is moving.

**Andrew Huberman:** You describe three choreographies of conflict. What are those?

**Esther Perel:** Three basic patterns. The first is pursue-pursue: both partners moving toward each other aggressively — escalation, attack and counter-attack, two arrows pointing at each other. This is the volatile couple who fights loudly and often.

The second is flee-flee: both partners moving away — withdrawal, silence, avoidance, the stalemate of two people who won't engage. This is the couple who says "we never fight" but has no real intimacy either.

The third — and the most common — is pursue-distancer: one partner moves toward, the other moves away. The pursuer escalates, the distancer withdraws, which causes the pursuer to escalate more, which causes the distancer to withdraw more. A perfect vicious cycle.

**Andrew Huberman:** And how does the physical setting affect these dynamics?

**Esther Perel:** Enormously. Face-to-face conversation activates confrontation — it's the position of combat. Two people sitting side-by-side, looking at the same thing, often have a very different quality of conversation. Some couples can only have their most honest conversations in the car, because they're both looking forward, there's no eye contact, and the movement of the car creates a sense of going somewhere together.

And sometimes the most useful thing I can do in a session is stop the conversation. When people are hyperaroused — physiologically flooded — they cannot process what's being said. The prefrontal cortex is offline. Continuing the conversation at that point makes things worse. Sometimes silence is the right intervention.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned that love and hate coexist.

**Esther Perel:** Love and hate are not opposites in the way we think. They are both intense forms of connection to another person. Indifference is the true opposite of love. When someone is still furious at their partner, they are still deeply engaged. It's when people stop caring altogether — when they become indifferent — that the relationship is truly in danger.

## [00:55:35] Verb States of Conflict; Emotion, Narratives vs. Reality

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked about "verb states" of conflict. What do you mean by that?

**Esther Perel:** I use verbs rather than nouns because verbs describe what is happening dynamically — the actions and interactions — rather than static categories. The three verb states I describe are: pursuing and pursuing, fleeing and fleeing, and pursuing while the other is fleeing.

When I use the verb state, I'm pointing to the behavior in the moment — what are you doing right now in relation to the other person? This is more useful than saying "you are an anxious attacher" or "you are avoidant" because it doesn't label the person. It describes the dynamic.

And the dynamic can change. The same person who pursues in one relationship may distance in another. The same person who distances when feeling criticized may pursue when feeling abandoned. The verb state is not fixed — it's relational and contextual.

**Andrew Huberman:** You've also talked about the distinction between emotion, narrative, and reality. Can you say more?

**Esther Perel:** When people are in conflict, they experience a sequence: first there is a physiological response — a felt sense in the body. Then there is an emotion — a name we give to that felt sense. Then there is a narrative — a story we tell about the emotion, about what caused it, about what it means. And then we mistake the narrative for reality.

But the narrative is not reality. The narrative is an interpretation. It's constructed from our history, our fears, our hopes, our previous experiences. Two people can be in the same room having the same interaction and construct completely different narratives about what happened — and each narrative will feel absolutely real and completely accurate to the person who holds it.

One of the most important skills in relationships — and in life — is being able to hold your narrative loosely enough to ask: is this the only interpretation possible? What might I be missing? That's where curiosity comes in.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's the phenomenon of confabulation — people genuinely believe their interpretation?

**Esther Perel:** Exactly. Confabulation is when we fill in the gaps in our perception with plausible stories — and we believe those stories completely. We don't experience them as interpretations; we experience them as memories, as perceptions, as facts. The brain is a story-making machine, and it can't tolerate gaps. So it fills them in, seamlessly, and presents the result as reality.

This is why two people can have such wildly different accounts of the same event — not because one is lying, but because each has confabulated different stories to fill in their perceptual gaps.

## [01:00:10] Time Domains & Hurt; Caretaker & Romantic Relationships

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked about how hurt in relationships doesn't stay in the present — it reaches into the past and the future simultaneously.

**Esther Perel:** We don't live in a single time. The present moment of conflict is simultaneously the accumulation of all the previous times this happened, and an anticipation of all the future times it might happen. "You're never there for me" — the word "never" tells you that you're not in the present anymore. You've collapsed into a generalization across time.

When the past bleeds into the present in this way — when hurt accumulates — you're no longer responding to what just happened. You're responding to the entire history of similar hurts. And the person in front of you is simultaneously the person who is here now and a stand-in for everyone who has ever hurt you in this way.

This is why seemingly small conflicts can ignite such disproportionately large responses. It's not about what just happened. It's about what this activates.

**Andrew Huberman:** And you've connected this to the relationship between our romantic relationships and our earliest caretaking relationships.

**Esther Perel:** Yes. There are only two relationships in a person's life that use the same neural circuitry, the same attachment systems, the same fundamental dynamics: the relationship with your first caretakers — your parents, or whoever raised you — and your adult romantic relationships.

Allan Schore's work on right-brain development and attachment is relevant here. The brain essentially repurposes the neural architecture built in the infant-caretaker relationship for adult romantic attachment. The same circuits that regulated your earliest experiences of closeness, safety, soothing, and threat are activated in your intimate relationships as an adult.

This is why our romantic partners can trigger reactions that feel completely out of proportion to the current situation. Because the current situation is activating a much older and more primitive template.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the concept of repetition compulsion — the idea that we unconsciously recreate our earliest attachment dynamics in adult relationships?

**Esther Perel:** The repetition compulsion is the unconscious attempt to resolve the core attachment wound. We recreate familiar dynamics — not because we want to suffer, but because the familiarity itself is regulating, and because somewhere in us is the hope that this time it will turn out differently. That this time, someone will finally see me, finally understand me, finally give me what I needed.

I want to be clear: these frameworks are useful invitations, not iron laws. They are ways of making sense of patterns. If a framework resonates with a person — if it illuminates something they already sensed but couldn't articulate — then it's useful. If it doesn't fit, we don't force it. I always tell people: "If this resonates, this is what we'll go with. If it doesn't, let's find a different frame."

## [01:08:03] Couples Therapy; Language & Naming

**Andrew Huberman:** The language of psychology — the terminology — has become much more widely used in popular culture. How do you think about the role of naming and labeling in relationship work?

**Esther Perel:** Naming can be enormously useful or deeply harmful, depending on how it's used.

When a name expands understanding — when it helps someone recognize a pattern they were caught in, understand why they behave in certain ways, have compassion for themselves or for their partner — it's valuable. Terms like confirmation bias or fundamental attribution error illuminate real tendencies: we are complex and multi-determined in our own behavior, but we tend to see others as simple and consistent. Naming that asymmetry can open people up.

But when naming becomes pathologizing — when it reduces a person to a diagnostic category, when it locks identity — it becomes harmful. And I see this happening a great deal right now with terms like "narcissist," "gaslighting," and related vocabulary.

**Andrew Huberman:** Those terms are everywhere.

**Esther Perel:** They are everywhere, and they are doing damage. "My partner is a narcissist" — once that label is applied, the relationship is over in that person's mind. There is no complexity, no context, no possibility. The person has become their diagnosis, and you have become their victim.

And in couples, the weaponization of therapeutic language is particularly destructive. Instead of creating understanding between people, it creates walls. It gives each person a vocabulary for categorizing and dismissing the other. "You're gaslighting me." "You're being a narcissist." These terms shut down conversation rather than opening it up.

The destigmatization of mental health and therapy is a real achievement and I celebrate it. But the byproduct has been the weaponization of psychological language. We don't need more tools to pull people apart. We need more tools to help them understand each other.

**Andrew Huberman:** What do you think couples therapy gets wrong, or what do therapists sometimes miss?

**Esther Perel:** There are several things. One is that therapists sometimes focus so much on the individual psychology of each partner that they lose sight of the relationship itself as the unit of treatment. The relationship has its own life, its own patterns, its own history — and it can't be reduced to the sum of two individuals' psychologies.

Another is the over-emphasis on talking and processing at the expense of experience and action. Sometimes what a couple needs is not more conversation about the problem but a different kind of experience — something that shifts the energy, that introduces novelty, that breaks the deadness.

And the language piece we just discussed. Therapy is fundamentally about expanding people's narratives, helping them hold more complexity. When therapy gives people a vocabulary that narrows rather than expands — that sorts people into categories of victim and perpetrator, healthy and disordered — it works against its own purpose.

## [01:20:15] Sexuality in Relationships

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's move to sexuality in relationships. You have a distinctive view of the relationship between sex and the broader relationship.

**Esther Perel:** One of the most important things I've learned, both from research and from many years of clinical work, is that sexuality is not simply a barometer of the relationship. It is not the case that if you fix the relationship, the sex will naturally improve. And it's not the case that if you improve the sex, the relationship will naturally improve — though both of these things can happen.

Sexuality is a parallel narrative. It runs alongside the relationship narrative but has its own logic, its own history, its own language. And sometimes the two narratives are tightly intertwined — you can't separate them. And sometimes they're quite separate — someone can have a great relationship and a flat sexuality, or a troubled relationship and a vibrant sexuality.

**Andrew Huberman:** Why does fixing the relationship not automatically fix the sexuality?

**Esther Perel:** Because sexuality is organized differently than other aspects of intimacy. It has its own developmental history — it connects to early experiences of the body, of pleasure, of shame, of permission, of safety. These are often pre-verbal, pre-relational. They predate the relationship.

Also, traditional frameworks of sexuality have focused primarily on function — on performance and outcome. This created a context where sex became about achievement rather than experience. And achievement orientation kills desire, because desire is fundamentally about openness, uncertainty, and surrender — which are incompatible with the need to perform and succeed.

The shift I advocate for is: let's drop performance and outcome and think of sexuality as an experience rather than a goal. Sex isn't just something you do. Sex is a place you go.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a beautiful reframe.

**Esther Perel:** And it changes everything. If sex is a place you go — an experiential space with its own rules, its own language, its own time — then the question is not "did we have sex?" but "what kind of place did we go to?" What was the quality of that experience? Was it playful? Was it tender? Was it passionate? Was it awkward and funny? All of those can be good. None of them require performance.

## [01:26:20] Tool: Love & Desire, Sexuality

**Andrew Huberman:** You have a specific exercise for helping people understand their relationship to love and desire. Can you walk us through it?

**Esther Perel:** This is an exercise I often give to individuals and couples. Take a piece of paper and divide it into two columns. At the top of the left column, write "Love." At the top of the right column, write "Sexuality" — or "Desire" or "Eroticism," whichever word feels most accurate to you.

Then free-associate under each column. Whatever comes to mind when you think of love — words, images, memories, qualities. And whatever comes to mind when you think of sexuality or desire.

Then do it again with a different prompt. Left: "When I am loved, I feel..." Right: "When I am desired, I feel..." Again, free association.

Then look at the two columns. How much overlap is there? How much distance? What's in one column that's not in the other?

**Andrew Huberman:** What do people typically find?

**Esther Perel:** It varies enormously, and that's the point. For some people, the two columns are almost identical — love and desire are thoroughly intertwined, and they can't imagine one without the other. For others, the columns are almost completely separate — love is safety and warmth and comfort and knowing, while desire is mystery and risk and the unknown and transgression.

And there are people for whom these two things are in direct conflict. Love brings responsibility and worry and the weight of caring — and those feelings make desire impossible. Michael Bader's work is interesting here — he's written about how for some people, the more deeply they love someone, the harder it is to desire them, because love activates an early caretaking orientation that is incompatible with erotic engagement.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's striking — the idea that love itself can suppress desire.

**Esther Perel:** It's very common, and it's connected to attachment history. If your earliest experience of closeness involved being responsible for someone else's emotional state — if you were the caretaker in your family of origin — then closeness activates that caretaking mode. And it's very hard to be erotically present with someone you're busy taking care of.

"You can make someone have sex, but you can never make them want." Desire cannot be mandated. It has to be invited. And the conditions under which desire flourishes — freedom, novelty, uncertainty, the sense of the other as somewhat separate and unknown — are precisely the conditions that security and familiarity tend to erode.

## [01:31:28] Infidelity, "Aliveness"

**Andrew Huberman:** You've written extensively about infidelity. What do you see as the most misunderstood aspects of affairs?

**Esther Perel:** The most common understanding of infidelity is that it's about the failure of the relationship — that the person who cheated didn't get enough of something from their partner. Didn't feel seen, or loved, or desired. And sometimes that's true. There are affairs that are about loneliness, neglect, sexual rejection, indifference, chronic conflict.

But there is a whole other category of affairs that is not about the relationship at all. Or not primarily. These affairs are about the self — about the person's relationship to their own aliveness, their own sense of vitality and possibility. "I wanted to find another self, not another person."

**Andrew Huberman:** What do you mean by "aliveness"?

**Esther Perel:** The erotic is not the same as the sexual, though they overlap. The erotic is a quality of experience — a sense of liveness, vibrancy, vitality, hopefulness, curiosity, imagination, playfulness. It's the opposite of deadness. And sometimes in an affair, what people are seeking is not just another person but a different version of themselves — the version that is more alive, more present, more hopeful, less burdened.

There is often a profound grief in these affairs — not just the grief of the betrayed partner, which is enormous, but a grief on the part of the person who had the affair. A longing for something they felt they had lost and couldn't find in their daily life. The affair was not just a betrayal; it was also an existential statement about feeling dead inside.

This doesn't excuse the betrayal. The harm done is real and serious. But understanding the full landscape of what motivates infidelity is necessary if we want to actually address it.

## [01:35:17] Intimacy, Abandonment, Self-Preservation

**Andrew Huberman:** You've identified what you describe as the central tension of all romantic relationships. What is that?

**Esther Perel:** The central tension is this: How do I get close to you without losing myself? And how do I hold onto myself without losing you?

These are two fears that run in parallel. The fear of abandonment by the other — that if I need too much, push too hard, want more than I'm given, the other will leave. And the fear of abandonment of oneself — that if I give too much, adapt too much, lose myself in this relationship, I will disappear.

These are not mirror images. They feel different, they look different, they organize behavior differently. The person who fears abandonment by the other tends to pursue, to need reassurance, to monitor the relationship constantly for signs of withdrawal. The person who fears self-abandonment tends to create distance, to guard their independence fiercely, to feel suffocated by closeness.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can someone shift from one fear to the other?

**Esther Perel:** Within a single relationship, usually not — though there are exceptions. But people often play very different roles in different relationships across their lifetime. Someone who was the distancer in one relationship becomes the pursuer in the next. Because the dynamic is relational — it emerges between two people, not just within one.

**Andrew Huberman:** How can you recognize when someone is operating from the self-preservation fear?

**Esther Perel:** Look for rigidity. Inflexibility around seemingly minor things — the restaurant, the closet space, the thermostat. When someone fights disproportionately hard over small territory, it's often because the territory represents something existential: the sense that they still exist as a separate self, that they haven't been swallowed up. The restaurant isn't about the restaurant. It's about: do I still have the right to be me in this relationship?

## [01:41:26] Erotic Blueprints, Emotional Needs

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked about the idea that our sexual life is inscribed with our emotional history. Can you elaborate?

**Esther Perel:** One of my core beliefs is: tell me how you were loved and I will tell you how you make love. Our erotic life is not separate from our emotional history — it is its physical expression.

The questions that matter are not just about sexual preferences and techniques. They go much deeper: Growing up, were you protected? Were you taken care of, or did you take care of others? Was it okay to experience pleasure? Was it okay to cry, to laugh, to be vulnerable? Was there room for your needs, or were you always attending to someone else's? Was the body a source of delight or a source of shame?

These early experiences — many of them pre-verbal — shape the erotic blueprint. They determine what feels safe and what feels dangerous, what activates desire and what shuts it down, what needs to be present for full erotic engagement and what makes it impossible.

**Andrew Huberman:** And sexual fantasies connect to this?

**Esther Perel:** Deeply. Sexual fantasies are not primarily about sex. They are translations of our deepest emotional needs — often the needs that were frustrated or unmet in early life. A fantasy of control may be about the longing to have power in a life where one felt powerless. A fantasy of surrender may be about the longing to release responsibility in a life where one was always responsible. A fantasy of being watched may be about the longing to be fully seen and appreciated.

Sex is never just sex. It carries the full weight of our emotional lives — our fears, our longings, our wounds, our hopes.

**Andrew Huberman:** You have an episode of your podcast "Where Should We Begin?" where a fight about a closet reveals all of this.

**Esther Perel:** Yes — the couple came in ostensibly fighting about the organization of their shared closet and the question of who was responsible for cleaning up after the cat. These sounded like trivial domestic disputes. But as we worked through it, what emerged was something much more fundamental.

For him, the closet represented control and order — something he'd needed desperately growing up with a chaotic, militaristic father. The closet was his small zone of sovereignty. For her, the question of who cleaned up after the cat was about whether she was alone in this relationship — whether she was, again, responsible for everything while someone else's needs took priority. Her fear was abandonment; his was loss of self.

They weren't fighting about the closet. They were fighting about the fundamental questions of their early lives, playing out in the domestic arena.

## [01:49:42] Tool: Repair Work, Relationship Revival; Sincere Apologies

**Andrew Huberman:** When a relationship has been damaged — by betrayal, by accumulated hurt, by breach of trust — what does genuine repair look like?

**Esther Perel:** I work with three categories of relational ruptures. First, there are mistakes — things done carelessly, thoughtlessly, without malicious intent. Second, there are misunderstandings — failures of communication and perception that led to hurt even when harm wasn't intended. And third, there are betrayals — deliberate choices that violated the trust and care the relationship is based on.

Each requires different repair work, though there are elements that are common to all three.

The first element I call acknowledgment. The wrongdoer must acknowledge — must feel and express genuine remorse and guilt for the hurt and harm caused. Not just "I acknowledge that you feel hurt" — which is a way of keeping distance from responsibility — but a genuine taking-in of the impact of what happened. "I hurt you. I caused this. I understand why you feel the way you feel." And this acknowledgment must happen even if the person who caused the hurt believes their action was justified. You can believe you were right and still genuinely regret the harm you caused.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a subtle distinction.

**Esther Perel:** It's a crucial one. "I was right to do what I did but I'm sorry it hurt you" — that's not an acknowledgment, that's a defense. The acknowledgment has to stand on its own, without a "but."

The second element I call valuation. The person who caused the hurt has to demonstrate — through consistent action over time — that the relationship and the other person matter to them. They have to become, as I sometimes say, a "vigilante of the relationship" — actively checking in, asking how the other person is, making small gestures that show the other person is seen and valued. This is not a single grand gesture. It's a sustained campaign of attention.

The third element is erotic recovery — and by erotic I mean in the broader sense: revival of vitality, curiosity, play, adventure. New experiences, new risks taken together, imagination reactivated. Because repair alone is not enough. After the wound has been acknowledged and the valuation has been demonstrated, the relationship needs to be revived — brought back to life.

**Andrew Huberman:** Repair is not enough?

**Esther Perel:** Repair is not enough. Repair addresses the wound. Revival addresses the relationship's aliveness. And healing — true healing — is when you know the wound is there, you can feel it when you press on it, but it's no longer front and center, no longer defining everything. Healing doesn't mean forgetting. It means the wound no longer organizes your life.

Harriet Lerner has written a wonderful book on apology that I always recommend. She points out something interesting: it is sometimes easier to stand in front of someone's anger than in front of their hurt. Anger at least keeps a kind of distance. But when someone is simply hurt — when they show you the raw wound — that requires a deeper presence and a deeper capacity for guilt.

## [01:59:30] Tool: Relationship Readiness

**Andrew Huberman:** One of your most interesting questions is the one you ask new clients at or near the beginning of therapy. What is that question?

**Esther Perel:** I ask almost every new client, in various forms: "Knowing yourself as well as you do, what do you think makes it hard to live with you?"

It's a simple question, but it does a lot of work. It assumes that there is something — that nobody comes to a relationship as a blank slate without any difficulties. It puts the focus on self-knowledge rather than on the partner's failures. It opens a conversation about accountability. And it gives me immediate information about the person's capacity for self-reflection, their willingness to take responsibility, their degree of insight.

**Andrew Huberman:** What do you look for in the response?

**Esther Perel:** The quality and range of the answer. Can they name more than one thing? Can they name it with some specificity? Can they name it without immediately pivoting to what the partner does that makes them that way? Can they name it with some humor or lightness, or is it all delivered with heavy shame?

The person who can say, with some equanimity: "I know I'm difficult when I'm stressed, I know I can be controlling, I know I sometimes disappear emotionally" — that person has capacity for the relational work. The person who says "I can't think of anything, I'm actually pretty easy to live with" — that person is not ready yet.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what do you mean by "relationship readiness"?

**Esther Perel:** Nobody is ready in the sense of being free from all relationship difficulties. "Everyone has relationship issues — the only question is with whom." We are all works in progress. Readiness is not the absence of issues; it's the willingness to engage with them honestly.

And many of what we call relationship problems are not problems to be solved but paradoxes to be managed. You don't solve the tension between closeness and separateness — you learn to navigate it. You don't solve the tension between security and freedom — you find ways to honor both. The relationship is a living system, and living systems require ongoing tending, not one-time fixing.
