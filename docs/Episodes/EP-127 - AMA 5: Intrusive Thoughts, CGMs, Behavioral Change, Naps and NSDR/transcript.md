## [00:01:53] Strategies for Abstaining From Addictive Thoughts and Narratives

Our first question is about abstaining from thoughts. And in answering this question, we're going to skirt right up against a topic that I've covered before on the podcast, which is obsessive compulsive disorder — which is a very serious disorder. It can have a range of severity, but people with true OCD suffer a lot from obsessions — these are intrusive thoughts — and compulsions, which are the behaviors typically associated with those thoughts.

I think it's really important that we define OCD really clearly so that we can make sure that we're talking about intrusive thoughts and the desire to abstain from thoughts versus OCD and where there might be some overlap there. The functional definition of OCD that really pertains to the disorder — obsessive compulsive disorder — as opposed to when we call people neurotic, or say "I'm so OCD about this" — which has become a common use of the acronym — true OCD is a situation in which engaging in a particular compulsive behavior does not serve to reduce the intensity or the frequency of the obsessions. In fact, it makes it worse.

So the typical thing we hear these days is "oh, you're so OCD" — not the clinical disorder but OCD in the sense that someone is really clean, really fastidious, needs everything perfect. However, if that person can achieve some level of calm and comfort that's fairly long-lasting when everything is clean and perfect, well, then that's not obsessive compulsive disorder. This is really important to understand as we venture into answering the question about intrusive thoughts and the desire to abstain from certain thoughts.

A person with true OCD will keep cleaning and cleaning, or washing their hands, or arranging things at perfect right angles — but the more they do it, the more their anxiety goes up. A person who is merely extremely particular and very driven to resolve things so things are done a certain way — well, if that person can experience relief from engaging in exercise or from cleaning, and exercising for an hour or two in the morning makes them really calm the rest of the day, then they're not necessarily true OCD. However, if they're exercising for two hours in the morning and then find they can't concentrate on other things and the desire to exercise just increases and becomes intrusive and disrupts their quality of life throughout the day — well, then that's falling under the umbrella of what we would call a true disorder.

We want to highlight that because the question is about a desire to abstain from thoughts. The question is: in your discussion with Dr. Anna Lembke — the director of our Dual Diagnosis Addiction Clinic at Stanford University School of Medicine and the author of the incredible book *Dopamine Nation* — you were talking about abstaining from people's drug of choice for 30 days to reset the dopamine reward pathway. And indeed that is the prescriptive that Dr. Anna Lembke gives for most addictions and for people suffering from milder or even severe depression related to dopamine-seeking behaviors — social media, video games, sex, food, and so on.

However, in the instance of food or in some other addictions, you can't abstain for 30 days. Certain things like alcohol and drugs people can abstain for 30 days in efforts to never go back to them again. There are cases of severe alcohol or opiate dependence where people can't go cold turkey without risking dying — so there they need to really work with a physician. But the 30-day reset of the dopamine system is something that is very real and pertains to most people. It's going to be very effective for most people struggling with either behavioral addictions — sometimes called process addictions — or other types of addictions including substance abuse issues.

The question continues: "You said you were going to ask Ana how to abstain if your drug of choice is a thought or narrative as opposed to a substance or a behavior. As far as I can tell, your discussion with Ana moved on before you asked her the question. Would you be able to discuss strategies for abstaining from addictive thoughts and narratives?"

So let's talk about intrusive thoughts, and let's start off by asking: what is a thought? In the realm of neurobiology, we have sensations — the processes by which our neurons convert light, photons, sound waves, touch, mechanical pressure, and so on into electrical signals and chemical signals that head into our nervous system including our brain. And then our brain makes sense of them and helps us navigate the world. The sensation part is a pure transformation of mechanical information — or in some cases chemical information like smell. Literally, volatile chemicals floating around in the world are brought in through your nose and your neurons in your olfactory bulb convert those into electrical and chemical signals that your brain can understand.

Perceptions are your idea of what those signals out in the world are — either because you're paying attention to them or for some other reason. You decide that the stop sign in front of you is red and the sign behind it is white and the sky is blue or cloudy. Those are perceptions. So we have sensations and perceptions. Then we have emotions, which include the mind and body and are related to neuromodulators like dopamine, serotonin, and others — although those chemicals do other things as well. And then, of course, we have behaviors, actions — everything from moving a pen on a piece of paper to walking and running.

Thoughts are a fifth category of neural functioning that we really need to define, but that doesn't have as strict a definition as sensations, perceptions, feelings, or actions. But thoughts are real. One way we can define thoughts is that thoughts are perceptions that include data from the past, present, or future — or combinations of past and present, or present and future, or future and past. I'm not trying to give an overly complicated definition here, but if we are going to answer a question about how to abstain from thoughts in a really direct and actionable way, we really need to address what thoughts are.

Thoughts are perceptions that are generated internally. We don't need any external sensation in order to have a thought. We can close our eyes. We could be in sensory isolation for that matter. We could even be floating with no gravity, and we can have a thought. Thoughts tend to run pretty much automatically in the background all the time. Some people refer to this as chatter in the back of our minds in a very unstructured way. And then if we force our thoughts to be structured — because we decide they need to be, or if something in our environment captures our perception — then our thoughts tend to be structured.

Let me give you an example. If I'm just walking around, if there were a way in which we could broadcast my thoughts onto a screen — I hope we wouldn't do that — but those thoughts would be a mixture of semi-complete sentences and gibberish. However if somebody stops me and says, "Hi, good to see you. What are you doing today?" and I start answering, well, then my thoughts are suddenly being driven by an external stimulus — their question — and some internal ideas, memory of who I am and what I'm doing that day, so past and present, and so on. And so thoughts become structured.

So when we have a question about how to abstain from thoughts, we need to be additionally specific and really pinpoint that what we're trying to do is to abstain from thoughts we don't want — either because they are too repetitive and distracting, or because what's contained in those thoughts is disturbing. This is important because it gives us two answers to the question that are highly divergent.

One answer to how to abstain from intrusive thoughts — thoughts we're addicted to — is if those thoughts are merely on loop all the time and we can't stop them, but the thoughts themselves aren't particularly disturbing. Think about a song you can't get out of your head, or you keep recounting some event, but the event itself isn't very disturbing. It's just intrusive because it's there. Well, in that case, the data really point to trying to anchor your thoughts to some external stimulus. So getting into action, getting into activities that really draw your attention away from that thought. You may still hear it scrolling in the background — so you might be sitting in class still hearing that loop of thoughts — but that's something that over time ought to wane and disappear if we try and bring more and more attention to whatever it is in our environment, whatever it is that we happen to be learning or doing physically.

Things like mindful meditation — doing a 10 or even just 5-minute-a-day practice of sitting with eyes closed, or lying down with eyes closed, and really focusing on one's breath, focusing one's attention on what is sometimes called the third eye center but in science we'd say just the region right behind the forehead — has been shown to increase focus for singular topics and can improve memory and do a bunch of other things as well. Those are data from Dr. Wendy Suzuki's lab at New York University. She was a guest on the podcast as well. My laboratory has run studies on mindful meditation as well. So what you're really trying to do is learn how to focus better on one thing, and by focusing on that one thing, you focus off these repetitive thoughts.

Now, I have a feeling this question was asked — and that many people upvoted it — because the issue isn't just thoughts that are intrusive because they're there and on repeat, but because the thoughts themselves are actually troubling. This could be recounting a trauma, someone harmed you, you observed something that was disturbing, you felt wronged, you felt someone else was wronged, you can't seem to get your mind off of something, and your emotions tend to follow, so it's uncomfortable. I have a feeling this is the root of the question.

In that case, the approach is very different. What we know from essentially all of the quality scientific and clinical studies is that those sorts of intrusive thoughts are very much like a trauma. Now we have to be clear in defining what trauma is. I'll use the definition that Dr. Paul Conti — another incredible guest on our podcast, a Stanford-Harvard trained psychiatrist, and one of the world's foremost leaders on the issue of trauma and psychiatry and psychology generally — he defines trauma as an event or something that fundamentally changes the way that your nervous system works such that you function less adaptively going forward from that event.

So not every bad occurrence in your life is a trauma. That's good news. The bad news is many people have traumas, and traumas change the way that our nervous system works so that we don't function as well as we could. So in that sense, intrusive thoughts that are disturbing are in many ways traumas and are reinforcing that trauma.

Now, almost counterintuitively, in order to deal with trauma, you have to get very close to that trauma. You don't have to — and I would hope you would not — reexpose yourself to the very same traumatic event. But we know that one of the best ways to deal with traumas is to get very clear about the narrative around those traumas. This can be done with a therapist ideally, but not everyone has access to therapy or can afford therapy. There's also a range of quality among therapists, so we're always referring to the desire for people to do great therapy with excellently trained people.

But it turns out that if you want to extinguish an intrusive thought, one of the best ways to do that is to journal about that particular thought extensively. So rather than the earlier strategy for intrusive thoughts where they're just on loop and intrusive because they're present but their content isn't disturbing — when that thought is disturbing and intrusive, we know that it's very useful to script out as much detail about that particular thought and the things around it as possible. Ideally you would use complete sentences.

The reason for doing that is that thoughts, as I mentioned earlier, can often be fragmentary. They pop up in our mind seemingly spontaneously. They're inhibiting our ability to focus, or be present at work or with family or in other things, or to sleep. Writing things down in a lot of detail does seem to have this quality of both reducing the emotional load of whatever it is that thought is about, as well as diminishing the frequency of those intrusive thoughts over time.

So this is far and away different from the strategy I mentioned for the other types of intrusive thoughts. And really it's far and away different from the 30-day abstinence approach that Dr. Anna Lembke was talking about for substance or behavioral addictions.

Now, of course, this process of abstaining from thoughts or removing the addictive nature of certain thoughts can definitely take some time. A good example would be superstitions. I'll come clean here — when I was in college, I developed a knock-on-wood superstition. Any time I'd say something I didn't want to happen or did want to happen, I'd say "knock on wood" and knock on wood. And then I started suppressing the behavior, mostly because it was a little embarrassing. And then I started just telling myself in my head, knock on wood, knock on wood, knock on wood. And it was clearly a little bit of an OCD type thing — OCD in air quotes — in the sense that the more I did it, the more I wanted to do it.

So I needed to go cold turkey on the thinking, but how can you go cold turkey on a thought? You can't. What I was told to do, and what worked very well for me, was to just write down the worst possible outcome that I was concerned about. To really get close to the nature or the underlying basis of that intrusive thought. And I raise this because a lot of times the intrusive thought is not simply "I'm thinking about a car accident" or "I'm thinking about a breakup." It's some kind of nebulous, abstract set of words, ideas, or images around something that happened — that we saw or heard or experienced. And by putting a lot of clear structure to what the thought is exactly and putting some thought and structure onto paper about what that pattern of unhealthy thinking relates to, people often achieve tremendous relief in a fairly short amount of time. Sometimes just in one session of writing it down, sometimes they need to write it down multiple times.

What you're essentially trying to do with an intrusive thought or a trauma of any kind is turn a disturbing story — a story that evokes a lot of emotion and hijacks your nervous system — into what is essentially a known but repetitive and old, boring story where the emotional load has been depleted.

And there is one more thing I have to highlight: getting sufficient rapid eye movement sleep is also very important for removing the emotional load of traumatic experiences and intrusive thoughts. So you really want to strive to get the best possible sleep you can — that includes sufficient rapid eye movement sleep. We have multiple zero-cost resources for that at hubermanlab.com: the episode on mastery of sleep, the episode on perfecting your sleep, the toolkit for sleep, all of which are timestamped and can be accessed at completely zero cost.

So in order to remove intrusive and addictive thoughts, ask yourself: is this OCD in the classic sense? If it is, you should see a psychiatrist — they won't necessarily prescribe medication, but there are tools for true OCD that are very effective in many cases, and we did the episode on OCD which I invite you to listen to as well. You want to ask yourself, are the thoughts disturbing or merely intrusive and repetitive? If they're merely intrusive and repetitive, then learning to focus your attention on other things — and getting better at focusing on single things through an exercise like mindfulness meditation — can really help. Indeed, perhaps the best use of mindfulness meditation is to improve your level of focus. It does have other benefits as well, but that's going to be the major one that you'll experience even with very short 5 or 10-minute-a-day meditations. Great data on that from the scientific literature.

And then if those intrusive thoughts are not only intrusive but also disturbing, in that case you really want to put as much structure and thought — believe it or not — into what those thoughts are really about. Write them out on paper in complete sentences, and maybe do that multiple times until the underlying emotions related to those thoughts really start to diminish. By doing that, you're essentially doing your own form of trauma therapy. And again, the data really point to the fact that getting close to the specific details around those intrusive thoughts is going to be the best way to extinguish them.
