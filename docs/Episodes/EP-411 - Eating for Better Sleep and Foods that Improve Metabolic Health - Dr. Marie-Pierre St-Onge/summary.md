## Key Findings

- Higher fiber intake is associated with more deep sleep, while higher saturated fat intake reduces deep sleep and refined carbohydrates/simple sugars increase sleep arousals. — *[00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-slug)*

- Sleep deprivation increases hunger differently by sex: in men it elevates ghrelin (appetite-stimulating hormone), while in women it reduces GLP-1 (satiety hormone), causing a 300-calorie daily overeating increase. — *[00:07:15](transcript.md#000715-slug)*

- Chronic mild sleep restriction of 6 hours per night over 6 weeks (versus 7.5 hours) increases insulin resistance and blood pressure, with worse effects in post-menopausal women. — *[00:20:23](transcript.md#002023-slug)*

- Sleep restriction activates reward centers of the brain in response to food stimuli, creating heightened neuronal pleasure signals independent of actual metabolic need. — *[00:08:02](transcript.md#000802-slug)*

- A 2-week study showed that 5 hours of sleep per night versus 7.5 hours resulted in half a kilogram (approximately one pound) of weight gain with no dietary changes. — *[00:16:00](transcript.md#001600-slug)*

- During the first study on acute sleep restriction, cortisol levels and glucose/insulin profiles remained unchanged despite severe sleep deprivation, suggesting metabolic damage requires concurrent poor dietary choices. — *[00:17:09](transcript.md#001709-slug)*

- Mediterranean and DASH diets are associated with better sleep duration and reduced insomnia symptoms in large population studies of women. — *[00:30:08](transcript.md#003008-slug)*

- Eating a self-selected diet (versus controlled healthy diet) increased time to fall asleep by over 70% and reduced deep sleep by approximately 20%. — *[00:36:44](transcript.md#003644-slug)*

- Eating the last meal at least 3 hours before bedtime improves sleep quality; food's thermic effect (heat production) interferes with the body's cooling needed for sleep onset. — *[00:38:55](transcript.md#003855-slug)*

- Energy expenditure increases during sleep deprivation (approximately 90 additional calories), but reduced spontaneous physical activity and increased food intake create a net positive energy balance promoting weight gain. — *[00:23:41](transcript.md#002341-slug)*

- A meta-analysis of sleep restriction studies shows 250-400 calories of daily overeating, which compounds to significant weight gain over time in shift workers and chronically sleep-deprived populations. — *[00:15:31](transcript.md#001531-slug)*

- Population-based studies spanning 14+ years show nurses sleeping 5-6 hours per night gain significantly more weight than those sleeping 7-8 hours. — *[00:04:28](transcript.md#000428-slug)*

- Sleep deprivation may trigger eating as a self-soothing mechanism due to food's energy boost and activation of pleasure centers, independent of actual caloric need. — *[00:14:32](transcript.md#001432-slug)*

- Circadian alignment is critical: going to bed at non-typical times disrupts sleep quality even with adequate duration, similar to the sleep problems experienced by shift workers. — *[00:41:44](transcript.md#004144-slug)*

- Short naps (30 minutes or less, taken early in the day) can be beneficial if nighttime sleep is insufficient, but should not dissipate sleep pressure too close to bedtime. — *[00:44:21](transcript.md#004421-slug)*

## Tools & Protocols

### Mediterranean Diet for Sleep Quality
- **What:** A dietary pattern emphasizing olive oils, fish, fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, and legumes associated with improved sleep duration and reduced insomnia symptoms.
- **How:** Prioritize whole foods aligned with Mediterranean principles; avoid processed foods high in saturated fat and refined carbohydrates.
- **When:** Establish as primary eating pattern across all meals.
- **Ref:** [00:30:08](transcript.md#003008-slug)

### DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) for Sleep
- **What:** A diet designed to reduce blood pressure by increasing fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, and low-fat dairy while minimizing saturated fat and sodium.
- **How:** Emphasize plant-based whole foods; limit processed foods, high-sodium items, and saturated fats.
- **When:** Implement as primary dietary approach.
- **Ref:** [00:32:17](transcript.md#003217-slug)

### Three-Hour Pre-Sleep Fasting Window
- **What:** No food intake within 3 hours of bedtime to optimize sleep onset speed and deep sleep duration.
- **How:** Schedule last meal to finish at least 3 hours before bed to allow digestion and body cooling.
- **When:** Every night before sleep; adjust timing based on individual tolerance and chronotype.
- **Ref:** [00:38:55](transcript.md#003855-slug)

### Sleep-Supportive Macronutrient Tracking
- **What:** Monitor daily fiber, saturated fat, and refined carbohydrate intake to optimize sleep architecture.
- **How:** Increase fiber intake through whole grains, legumes, and vegetables; minimize saturated fat and refined carbohydrates; track impact on sleep quality via sleep tracking or sleep diary.
- **When:** Daily dietary monitoring with weekly assessment of sleep patterns.
- **Ref:** [00:38:01](transcript.md#003801-slug)

### Strategic Napping Protocol
- **What:** Structured short naps (maximum 30 minutes) taken early in the day to supplement insufficient nighttime sleep without disrupting bedtime sleep pressure.
- **How:** Keep naps brief and before 2-3 PM; avoid napping close to regular bedtime; use when nighttime sleep is genuinely insufficient.
- **When:** Only when needed due to documented nighttime sleep insufficiency; not as daily routine.
- **Ref:** [00:44:21](transcript.md#004421-slug)

### Sleep-Deprived Eating Awareness Practice
- **What:** Pause before eating when sleep-deprived to consciously assess whether hunger is physiological or driven by hormonal/reward system dysregulation.
- **How:** When experiencing urge to eat after poor sleep, ask: "Did I sleep poorly last night?" and "Do I truly need these calories or am I seeking a pick-me-up?" Make intentional choices rather than automatic consumption.
- **When:** In moments of food urges following insufficient sleep.
- **Ref:** [00:27:03](transcript.md#002703-slug)

## Resources Mentioned

### Studies

- **Nurses Health Study (2006):** 14-year longitudinal study tracking weight changes in nurses sleeping 5-6 hours versus 7-8 hours per night, demonstrating higher weight gain in short sleepers. — *[00:04:28](transcript.md#000428-slug)*

- **St-Onge et al. Acute Sleep Restriction Study:** Crossover design comparing 9-hour versus 4-hour sleep opportunity (5 nights) measuring appetite hormones, neuroimaging, and food intake; demonstrated sex-specific ghrelin and GLP-1 responses. — *[00:06:00](transcript.md#000600-slug)*

- **St-Onge et al. Chronic Sleep Restriction Study:** 6-week mild sleep restriction protocol (6 hours versus 7.5 hours nightly) in free-living adults showing increased insulin resistance and blood pressure. — *[00:20:23](transcript.md#002023-slug)*

- **St-Onge Metabolic Chamber Study:** Small study in women measuring energy expenditure during sleep restriction, showing increased costs of wakefulness but minimal net impact on total daily expenditure. — *[00:23:19](transcript.md#002319-slug)*

- **Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA):** Large cohort study examining diet quality (Mediterranean/DASH alignment) and sleep duration/insomnia symptoms. — *[00:29:27](transcript.md#002927-slug)*

- **Women's Health Initiative Study (published 2024):** Longitudinal analysis of dietary profiles and insomnia development showing Mediterranean and DASH diets protective against persistent insomnia. — *[00:30:33](transcript.md#003033-slug)*

- **Neymar Kovven 2022 Study:** Two-week sleep restriction protocol (5 hours versus 7.5 hours nightly) showing 0.5 kg weight gain without dietary intervention. — *[00:16:00](transcript.md#001600-slug)*

- **St-Onge Inpatient Diet-Sleep Study:** Polysomnography assessment of sleep during controlled feeding versus self-selected diet, measuring latency to sleep onset, deep sleep duration, and arousal frequency. — *[00:35:27](transcript.md#003527-slug)*

### Compounds/Hormones Discussed

- **Ghrelin:** Appetite-stimulating hormone elevated in men in response to sleep deprivation; drives desire to eat. — *[00:07:17](transcript.md#000717-slug)*

- **GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1):** Satiety hormone reduced in women during sleep deprivation; natural appetite suppressant. — *[00:07:25](transcript.md#007225-slug)*

- **Leptin:** Hormone reduced during sleep restriction; regulates appetite and energy expenditure. — *[00:09:34](transcript.md#000934-slug)*

- **Cortisol:** Stress hormone; circadian rhythm maintained independently of acute sleep deprivation in controlled lab settings. — *[00:17:09](transcript.md#001709-slug)*

- **Melatonin:** Sleep-promoting hormone; suppressed during daytime in shift workers attempting daytime sleep. — *[00:42:16](transcript.md#004216-slug)*

### Concepts

- **Cardioabolic Health:** Combined cardiovascular and metabolic health markers including insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, lipid profiles, and glucose regulation. — *[00:19:45](transcript.md#001945-slug)*

- **Sleep Architecture:** The composition of sleep stages including deep sleep (slow-wave sleep) and REM sleep; measured by polysomnography. — *[00:36:44](transcript.md#003644-slug)*

- **Thermic Effect of Food:** Energy required to digest food; produces heat that interferes with body cooling needed for sleep onset. — *[00:39:26](transcript.md#003926-slug)*

- **Spontaneous Physical Activity (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis/NEAT):** Unplanned movement (fidgeting, postural shifts, etc.) that contributes to daily energy expenditure and is reduced during sleep deprivation. — *[00:24:02](transcript.md#002402-slug)*

- **Sleep Pressure (Sleep Need):** Physiological drive to sleep that builds throughout wakefulness; dissipated by napping and sleep itself. — *[00:44:24](transcript.md#004424-slug)*

- **Circadian Misalignment:** Mismatch between internal circadian rhythm and external sleep schedule (e.g., shift work, late bedtimes). — *[00:41:44](transcript.md#004144-slug)*
