## [00:06:49] Gut-Brain Axis

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. Bohórquez, great to have you here. Let's start at the beginning — what is this gut-brain axis that we hear about, and what's going on in our gut beyond digestion?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Thank you, Andrew — thrilled to be here. People call it an "axis" because it was traditionally thought to be an imaginary line connected through hormones. Since 1902, when Bayliss and Starling reported the first hormone secretion, we knew that when we eat, molecules in the gut are released into the bloodstream and can have effects on distant organs. For the next 100 years the field focused primarily on hormones, so there was no conception of a direct neural line of communication between gut and brain. But as I often say, we don't say the "nose-brain axis" or the "eye-brain axis" — all organs work in sync. The gut is actually the only organ that passes through the body while still being exposed to the outside world: if you swallow a marble, it still has a chance to exit. The gut is essentially separated from the external environment by a series of chambers with valves — the epiglottis, the gastroesophageal junction, the pylorus, the ileocecal junction, and the rectum — and within each of those chambers, different sensing and processing occurs.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the gut has cells that respond to what's coming through and signal to the brain what's happening in the outside world by virtue of what passes through that passage — is that correct?

**Diego Bohórquez:** That's correct. When we swallow something, we literally have to trust our gut — and perhaps that's why we use that phrase. After swallowing, there's not much we can consciously do; it is the gut that has to make the distinction between nutrients and toxins, and either absorb them or move them through. The gut is an external surface lined by a single layer of epithelial cells — and within that layer are several cell types, including what are called enteroendocrine cells, named in 1938 by the German physician Frederick Feyrter. These cells are dispersed at roughly a 1-to-1,000 ratio throughout the digestive tract and were long thought to communicate only by releasing hormones into the bloodstream. But in 2015 we made an observation that changed the picture completely.

## [00:11:35] Gut Sensing, Hormones

**Diego Bohórquez:** For a long time we assumed enteroendocrine cells communicated only via hormones — releasing neuromodulators that diffused through the body and eventually acted on receptors near nerve terminals. That system still exists and is well established: it operates on the timescale of minutes to hours. But in 2015 we observed that anywhere from one-third to two-thirds of these cells were actually making direct contact with the nervous system. That opened a new dimension of how the gut could be communicating to the brain, because as you know, synaptic transmission operates on the order of milliseconds — orders of magnitude faster than endocrine signaling. The idea that a gut cell could send information directly to the brain in milliseconds was a completely different picture from the hormone model.

**Andrew Huberman:** So hormone signaling is slower — on the order of minutes or hours — whereas direct neural communication is on the order of milliseconds. And you're now telling us there's a fast, synaptic pathway as well as the slow hormonal one?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Exactly. The fast pathway delivers precise, targeted information in the same way that synapses in the brain do — specific in space and time. The hormonal pathway is still real and important, but it's a broader, slower signal. The tools available up to the 1980s — electrophysiology, behavior — weren't adequate to study the fast pathway, but then in the 1990s green fluorescent protein revolutionized cell biology by allowing us to tag and visualize specific cell types in living tissue. Combined with other advances like optogenetics and modified rabies virus, suddenly we had the tools to ask entirely new questions about gut-brain circuitry.

## [00:15:26] Green Fluorescent Protein; Neuropod Cells & Environment Sensing

**Diego Bohórquez:** With the GFP revolution and the development of intestinal organoids by Hans Clevers in 2009 — mini-guts grown in a dish — I was suddenly able to isolate, observe, and study these enteroendocrine cells in ways that hadn't been possible before. When I began looking at these cells closely, I noticed something peculiar: some of them had very prominent arms at the base — almost like the image from the Sistine Chapel of Adam reaching out toward God, ending in a little hand at the base. I immediately wondered: why would a cell that is simply supposed to detect food and release hormones invest so much energy in developing an arm? I initially thought it might be a direct bridge into the vasculature to deliver hormones into the bloodstream, but I couldn't find that connection. So I started studying whether these cells were contacting the nervous system instead.

**Andrew Huberman:** So these cells have a portion facing the interior of the gut — the lumen — and a portion extending toward the nervous system below?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Precisely. These cells have little cilia or microvilli exposed to the lumen — like tiny hairs that taste what's passing through — and then a basal process, which we eventually named the "neuropod," that reaches toward nerve fibers underneath. The naming process was organic and somewhat comical: we first called the extensions "axon-like basal processes," which became a mouthful we couldn't fit in an abstract. I eventually landed on "neuropod" — a pod-like structure of neuronal character — and when I pitched it to my mentor over a weekend, he came back Monday and said it had a ring to it. These neuropod cells are simply a specialized neuroepithelial cell type — electrically excitable cells that can discharge electricity and are found in epithelial layers throughout the body. They are in the inner ears, taste buds, the lining of the spinal cord ventricles, and of course throughout the gut from esophagus to colon.

**Diego Bohórquez:** The Japanese scientists Fujita, Canon, and Kobayashi wrote a beautiful book in the 1970s calling similar cells "paraneurons" — their whole concept being that there is not a discrete distinction between a neuron that lives inside the central nervous system and a neuroepithelial cell exposed to the outside world, but rather a continuum of adaptation. The organism uses this continuum to bring information from outside the body inside, process it, and guide behavior.

## [00:26:57] Brain & Gut Connection, Experimental Tools & Rabies Virus

**Diego Bohórquez:** Once we observed that these cells had arms contacting nerve fibers, we had to demonstrate actual connection rather than mere contact — because in lab meetings I'd be told repeatedly that "contact does not mean connection." One day while running I thought: what if we isolate these cells by their fluorescence and culture them in front of sensory neurons, then simply record them over time under a microscope? What we actually saw was extraordinary: within a few hours, both the gut cell and the sensory neuron would spontaneously reach toward each other and recapitulate the full gut-brain circuit in a dish — literally forming two brains in a dish. I remember the exact date: June 27, 2012. That was an eye-opener, because it showed these cells are not static — they are constantly moving and forming connections.

**Diego Bohórquez:** The next question was whether this happened in an intact organism. Using a modified rabies virus — which naturally hops from one neuron to the next across synaptic connections — we engineered it to jump only one synapse and to infect specifically neuropod cells rather than other epithelial cells, because rabies likes neurons. When we infected neuropod cells and traced where the virus went, the fluorescence appeared in the brain stem and in the bodies of neurons in the nodose ganglion — the cluster of cell bodies of the vagus nerve located just beneath the neck. There was only one synaptic stop between the surface of the intestine and the brain stem. The anatomical basis for millisecond-speed information transfer from the gut lumen to the brain was real.

**Andrew Huberman:** To clarify the rabies tool for listeners: scientists engineered the virus to jump exactly one station, attach it to a fluorescent label, and in this way they can see the precise wiring of one-synapse connections. The virus's natural behavior — making infected animals more aggressive so they bite and spread the virus — is what makes it so neurotropic in the first place.

## [00:37:00] Neuropod Cells & Nutrient Sensing

**Andrew Huberman:** So you discovered and named neuropod cells, showed they connect directly to the nervous system via a single synapse, and demonstrated this circuit exists in intact animals. Now — what does the brain actually do with that information? What nutrients do these cells detect, and how does that translate into behavior?

**Diego Bohórquez:** These enteroendocrine cells throughout the stomach, intestine, and colon express multiple receptors for multiple nutrients. The macronutrients — sugars, fats, proteins — have specific receptor types, and within each category there's a repertoire of molecular sensors. Critically, cells express different combinations of receptors depending on their location. Food is only partially digested when it enters the small intestine, but by the time it reaches the colon most nutrients have been absorbed and primarily fibers remain. So the gut has evolved a spatial map of sensing: sugars are detected more in the proximal intestine, while fiber fermentation byproducts like short-chain fatty acids — butyrate, propionate — are detected more distally or in the colon.

**Diego Bohórquez:** The computation happening at the level of a single cell is remarkable. Take glucose: glucose activates the TAS1R3 sweet taste receptor on the cell surface. Then glucose is absorbed by sodium-glucose transporters, which depolarize the cell. Inside the cell, glucose enters the TCA cycle and is catabolized to produce ATP, which further activates voltage-gated channels, further depolarizing the cell. The cell then releases glutamate as a neurotransmitter, activating both fast ionotropic receptors and slower metabotropic receptors on the adjacent vagal nerve terminal — all within milliseconds. The glucose metabolism also triggers neuropeptide release, which provides the second, longer-duration phase of the signal. All of that integration — taste, absorption, metabolism, neurotransmission, neuropeptide release — happens at the level of a single cell processing a single molecule.

**Andrew Huberman:** Beyond glucose: temperature, pH, amino acids, short- and long-chain fatty acids — are these cells eventually going to be found to detect everything we put in our mouths?

**Diego Bohórquez:** I believe so. In due time we're going to realize these cells detect just about everything we eat — through specific dedicated receptors or combinations of receptors. And beyond chemical compounds, a fascinating area that is going to grow is mechanical distension plus thermal sensing: the esophagus must adjust the temperature of food to physiological body temperature within seconds, and this rapid thermal regulation is likely sensed and signaled as well.

## [00:43:55] Gastric Bypass Surgery, Cravings & Food Choice

**Andrew Huberman:** Could you tell the story you've shared before about the woman at Thanksgiving who had gastric bypass surgery? It's a vivid illustration of what altering gut anatomy does to food preference.

**Diego Bohórquez:** Yes — this story is very personal to me and, in a sense, set the direction of my career. I was beginning my PhD at North Carolina State University, and I was invited to my first Thanksgiving. I sat next to a woman who told me about her experience with gastric bypass surgery. She said three things happened: she lost about 40% of body weight within six months; within one week of surgery her Type 2 diabetes resolved without insulin; and most strangely, a food she had found nauseating before surgery — sunny-side-up eggs, specifically the yolk — she now not only could eat but actively craved. She would soak up the yolk with toast. I remember walking away from that dinner thinking "this is major." The first clinical report documenting that altered food choices were common after gastric bypass didn't come until around 2011, and we have since replicated it in rodents: consistently, animals that undergo a bypass change their food preferences.

**Andrew Huberman:** Gastric bypass — specifically the Roux-en-Y procedure — bypasses roughly a third of the duodenum, short-circuiting the early intestine. The vertical sleeve gastrectomy simply reduces stomach size. In both cases the original rationale was purely mechanical: less surface area absorbing food equals less body weight gain. But as you describe, changes in food preference and the resolution of diabetes happen within one week, well before any significant weight is lost — suggesting something fundamentally different is happening.

**Diego Bohórquez:** Precisely. The very rapid changes in hormone and neuropeptide levels after surgery, and the rewiring of gut sensitivity, suggest the gut's sensory function shifts dramatically and immediately. After the bypass, the gut becomes more sensitive to nutrients. In the egg yolk case: the lipids from the yolk, when they now enter the reorganized intestine at a different location and concentration, may trigger a pleasurable sensory signal where before there was a repulsive one — a flip in valence driven by altered neuropod cell sensitivity. This phenomenon has important clinical corollaries: patients who have undergone gastric bypass show a two-to-sevenfold increased likelihood of developing alcohol use disorder. The gut has been rewired to be more sensitive to the rewarding properties of all kinds of stimuli, not just food.

## [00:51:14] Optogenetics; Sugar Preference & Neuropod Cells

**Diego Bohórquez:** To interrogate whether neuropod cells actually drive sugar preference behavior, we needed optogenetics — the ability to turn specific cells on or off with light. Optogenetics was pioneered around 2005 by Karl Deisseroth, Ed Boyden, and colleagues, using light-sensitive opsins from algae engineered into neurons. The challenge in the gut is that the standard rigid fiber-optic cables used in brain experiments are incompatible with the constantly-moving intestine. In 2017, Professor Polina Anikeeva from MIT came to give a talk at Duke and mentioned she had developed a flexible fiber optic — and that collaboration solved the problem.

**Diego Bohórquez:** When we placed inhibitory opsins in neuropod cells and turned them off with light precisely at the moment an animal had a choice between sucrose and a non-caloric sweetener like stevia, the animal became blind to the difference — it could no longer distinguish sugar from sweetener. Remove the light, and the cells are functional again, and the animal immediately recovers its preference for sugar. This is analogous to turning off the color-sensitive cone cells in the eye: the animal doesn't lose memory or general behavior, it loses one very specific sensory channel. The converse experiment — activating neuropod cells with light — caused animals to prefer non-caloric sweetener over water as if it were sugar, and even to drink plain water as if it were sugar. The gut's "sense of sugar" is that powerful: it can override what the mouth and conscious brain perceive.

**Andrew Huberman:** This builds on earlier work from 2007 by Ivan de Araujo at Duke, in which animals whose oral sweet-taste receptors were genetically erased could still reliably prefer sugar water over plain water — confirming that a post-oral sensing mechanism exists completely independent of taste. Troels Clausnitzer and colleagues then suggested sodium-glucose transporters in the intestine as the detection mechanism. Charles Zuker at Columbia subsequently identified brain-stem neurons that integrate this gut sugar signal and drive the behavior.

## [01:00:29] Gut-Brain Disorders, Irritable Bowel Syndrome

**Andrew Huberman:** On the painful side of gut sensing: David Julius and collaborators at UCSF and Jinra have shown that serotonin-releasing cells in the colon couple to spinal cord nerve fibers. When activated by noxious stimuli, they drive visceral hypersensitivity — the biological basis of what can develop into irritable bowel syndrome and related disorders of gut-brain interaction. These disorders are now formally called "disorders of gut-brain interactions" in the clinical world. Is that what we're talking about?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Yes — that's the aversive arm. The gut senses two fundamental dimensions of every stimulus: salience (how intense is it?) and valence (is it pleasurable or painful?). The serotonin-releasing enterochromaffin cells in the colon, which couple to spinal cord afferents, are responsible for gating noxious stimuli through to the nervous system as a pain signal — visceral hypersensitivity that can become chronic if those cells are persistently activated.

## [01:04:04] Gut & Behavior; Gastric Bypass, Cravings & Alcohol

**Andrew Huberman:** Back to the gastric bypass alcohol finding — can you say more about what's known there?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Patients commonly report that after surgery they had no particular interest in alcohol, but within a few months of the bypass they found themselves escalating from one glass of wine to two, three, four. The gut's enhanced sensitivity to nutrient stimuli appears to generalize: the rewired intestine is more responsive to the rewarding properties of alcohol as well. The biology is still not fully worked out, but the phenomenon is clinically documented at two-to-sevenfold elevated risk. It's a vivid reminder that food preferences and addiction-like behaviors are not purely psychological — they are shaped by the architecture of the gut's sensory system.

## [01:07:38] GLP-1, Ozempic, Neuropod Cells

**Andrew Huberman:** I have to ask about GLP-1 — glucagon-like peptide-1 — and drugs like Ozempic and Mounjaro, which are now being taken by millions of people for diabetes and weight loss. How does GLP-1 relate to the neuropod cell system you've described?

**Diego Bohórquez:** GLP-1 was already highly studied in this area fifteen years ago when I entered the field — primarily because it's one of the most potent stimulators of insulin release from the pancreas. After gastric bypass surgery, GLP-1 circulating levels actually increase dramatically, which was one of the first clues that something was happening beyond mere mechanical reduction of gut surface. There were already studies suggesting that GLP-1's effects were not purely through the bloodstream but rather through localized action on vagal nerve terminals. Certain enteroendocrine cells — particularly more distal in the digestive tract — do release GLP-1 in response to macronutrients, primarily sugar. That GLP-1 then acts on receptors of vagal nerve terminals and drives satiety signaling. At the brain stem level it potentiates feelings of fullness. Whether neuropod cells are specifically the GLP-1-producing cells is still being clarified, but there is clear overlap between the gut sensory system I study and the GLP-1 pathway that Ozempic mimics.

## [01:11:46] Food Preference & Gut-Brain Axis, Protein

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about protein. You've been describing a picture in which the gut is constantly evaluating what we eat and signaling the brain about it. Is there any evidence the gut has a specific sensing mechanism for protein?

**Diego Bohórquez:** There is recent compelling work showing that if you completely remove protein from a diet, the animal evaluates through gut sensing that no protein is present and stops eating that meal. Conversely, if protein is low but not absent, the animal consumes more of the diet, trying to compensate — essentially protein-leveraging. If the diet also happens to be rich in sugar or fat, that compensatory eating leads to overconsumption of calories. And there is a fascinating additional finding: in the absence of dietary protein, if the gut microbiome has sufficient highly digestible fiber, the microorganisms can switch on the ability to synthesize essential amino acids, which may partially satisfy the protein-sensing signal. This suggests our gut neurons are waiting, hoping we provide them with animal-source proteins — but if those are absent, the microbiome has a backup synthesis pathway when fed sufficient fiber.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've personally noticed that when I eat sufficient amounts of meat, chicken, eggs, and fish, my sugar cravings drop dramatically. This sounds like an example of the protein-sensing pathway reaching satiety and dampening the drive to seek sugar.

## [01:21:35] Protein & Sugar, Agriculture & 'Three Sisters'

**Diego Bohórquez:** Agriculture has long instinctively reflected what the gut needs. The classic native agricultural pairing — sometimes called the "Three Sisters" — is corn, beans, and squash or pumpkin. Corn provides carbohydrates, beans provide protein and fix nitrogen, squash provides fiber and fat. The combination is nutritionally complete at the level of carbohydrates, protein, and fiber. My parents farmed in Ecuador and always planted beans alongside corn; the beans wrapped around the corn stalks and it just seemed so natural. I now think this wasn't arbitrary — it was an instinct, perhaps even taught to us by the plants themselves, to cultivate and combine in ways that the gut would recognize as complete nourishment.

## [01:25:16] Childhood, Military School; Academics, Nutrition & Nervous System

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about your background. Where were you born and how did you get from there to studying gut neuroscience at Duke?

**Diego Bohórquez:** I was born in El Chaco, in the Napo Province of Ecuador, on the eastern slopes of the Andes on the descent toward the Amazonia — coincidentally close to the trail used by Francisco de Orellana in 1542 in the expedition that led to the discovery of the Amazon. My town got its first road in 1974; I was born in 1983. I grew up in a community that had electricity only from 7 to 9 p.m. from a diesel engine generator. My father bought the town's first color television and neighbors would come to our living room to watch movies. We had a farm; we raised cattle, goats, and sheep. Growing up there gave me a profound connection to plants, animals, food, and the rhythms of nature.

**Diego Bohórquez:** As a child I was sent to military school, which was intense — but it instilled discipline and a kind of systematic thinking. I went on to study agronomy and then nutrition, and as a PhD student at North Carolina State University studying gut physiology, the encounter at that Thanksgiving dinner redirected everything. Later I did postdoctoral training in neuroscience, combining the two fields — a combination that most thought was strange but that I believed was necessary to understand the phenomenon I had witnessed.

## [01:36:15] Plant Wisdom, Agriculture, Indigenous People

**Andrew Huberman:** You've mentioned "plant wisdom" several times. What do you mean by that?

**Diego Bohórquez:** I use the word "wisdom" rather than "intelligence" because it reflects accumulated experience rather than cognition per se. Plants have been on Earth for far longer than any animal — they have been shaped by hundreds of millions of years of interaction with their environment: climate fluctuations, other organisms, seasonal changes. A 650-year-old ceiba tree I once saw at the Mayan ruins of Copán at the Honduras-Guatemala border — that tree was there before the Spanish arrived, perhaps before the Mayans abandoned that city. The information stored in a single organism of that age, at the level of its cellular machinery including chloroplasts that have been shaped across all those centuries, is staggering. Plants are not passive substrates; they are active participants in their environment. We used to think medicine came from synthetic molecules, but according to a sign at the Oxford Botanic Gardens — established 1621 — about 80% of medicine still derives directly from plants.

## [01:41:48] Evolution of Food Choices; Learning from Plants

**Diego Bohórquez:** Our food instincts have been shaped over evolutionary time by interactions with plants and animals. Humans across all cultures arrive at nutritionally similar dietary patterns — the tortilla plus beans plus lettuce structure appears in some form everywhere, whether as a sandwich, a rice-and-protein bowl, or a taco. What looks like cultural variety in food is really a deeply conserved instinct to combine carbohydrate with protein with fiber in proportions that the gut recognizes as nutritionally satisfying. The gut-brain axis is a large part of the reason: when we eat a nutritionally complete meal, the neuropod cells along the digestive tract signal satisfaction to the brain in sequence — first sugar sensing proximally, then amino acid sensing, then fiber fermentation products distally — and the integrated signal drives the feeling of genuine satiety and well-being.

## [01:48:15] Plant-Based Medicines; Amazonia, Guayusa Ritual & Chonta Palm

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about Guayusa and the morning ritual you've described from the Amazon.

**Diego Bohórquez:** Guayusa (Ilex guayusa) is a close cousin of yerba mate (Ilex paraguariensis) native to the Amazonian foothills. It has almost as much caffeine as coffee, plus antioxidants and other compounds, but the subjective experience is noticeably smoother — much less bitter than mate, without the jitteriness of strong coffee. The Kichwa people of the Ecuadorian Amazon have a ritualized practice called "Wuasa Upina Ura" — the Hour of Guayusa — in which the community wakes between 4 and 6 a.m., brews a large pot of guayusa (adding water as it boils, drinking from the same pot for an hour or more), and uses this time to discuss the previous week's issues, plan the day, and address family or community matters. Around 5:30 they add "chonta" — a date from the chonta palm (Bactris gasipaes) — which is rich in lipids and fiber, providing sustained energy. They say guayusa gives them energy, heals pain, and suppresses appetite so they can work until noon. That combination of gentle stimulation plus appetite suppression plus community bonding is a remarkably sophisticated nutritional and social protocol developed over generations of empirical refinement.

## [01:56:58] Yerba Mate, Chocolate, Guayusa

**Andrew Huberman:** You introduced me to guayusa and I've been drinking it regularly since. The experience is genuinely different from coffee or even yerba mate — a clean, sustained alertness without anxiety. Why is the experience different if guayusa and mate both contain caffeine?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Because it's not only caffeine. Guayusa is not a shot of espresso. The full leaf contains a combination of other compounds — theobromine, theophylline, polyphenols, chlorogenic acids — that collectively modulate how caffeine is absorbed and experienced. This is what I mean by "plant wisdom": the plant doesn't just contain one active molecule; it contains a whole ensemble of molecules that together produce a qualitatively distinct experience in the gut and brain. This is also true of chocolate: the cacao plant contains theobromine, caffeine, phenethylamine, and hundreds of other bioactive compounds. Extracting a single molecule and delivering it in isolation always produces a different — and usually less complete — experience than consuming the whole plant material.

## [02:00:22] Brain, Gut & Sensory Integration; Variability

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's zoom out. You've painted a picture of the gut as a sophisticated sensory organ — almost a sixth sense — integrating information about what we eat and sending it to the brain via a fast, direct neural pathway. How does the brain then integrate that information with everything else it knows?

**Diego Bohórquez:** The gut sends its sensory information through the vagus nerve primarily to the nucleus tractus solitarius in the brain stem, which then projects broadly to the hypothalamus, the striatum (where dopamine release drives reward and craving), and other forebrain areas involved in decision-making. This is where reward circuits pick up the signal: the pleasant sensation after eating sugar is partly driven by neuropod cell activation of dopaminergic circuits in the basal ganglia. There is already evidence that these gut-originating signals connect to areas of dopamine release; the precise circuit mapping from neuropod cells to reward centers is still being worked out but the functional links are clear.

**Diego Bohórquez:** What's also remarkable is variability: the sensory landscape of the gut is not fixed. It changes depending on what you've been eating, whether you've recently had surgery, your circadian phase, and your internal state. The gut also generates slow electrical waves — analogous to slow brain oscillations — that change with fasting versus feeding and with circadian rhythms. When those gut electrical patterns are out of sync with brain oscillations, we get things like "hanger" — irritability driven by gut-brain electrical dissonance rather than purely by low blood sugar.

## [02:06:01] Electrical Patterns in Gut & Brain, "Hangry"

**Andrew Huberman:** The idea that "hanger" — being angry because you're hungry — has an electrical basis in desynchronization between gut and brain oscillations is fascinating. Circadian biology tells us appetite is highest during the day and suppressed at night in part because gut electrical patterns align with the light-dark cycle. Is gut electrical activity something that could eventually be measured and used therapeutically?

**Diego Bohórquez:** I believe so — that's one of the most exciting frontiers ahead. The gut generates what are called interdigestive migrating motor complexes, slow electrical waves that travel from stomach to ileum during fasting and that are disrupted by eating. Those waves are entrained by circadian clocks and by the composition of what we eat. If the gut's electrical rhythm goes out of phase with the brain's — as might happen with jet lag, irregular eating, or ultra-processed food that bypasses normal nutrient sensing — the result is not just discomfort but a fundamental disruption of the gut-brain dialogue. Understanding and potentially modulating those rhythms could offer new therapeutic approaches to disorders from IBS to metabolic disease to mood disorders.

## [02:12:43] Gut Intuition, Food & Bonding; Subconscious & Superstition

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about gut intuition in a broader sense. We've discussed how the gut senses food. But people routinely describe a "gut feeling" about a person or a situation — something that arrives before conscious analysis. Is there a scientific basis for that?

**Diego Bohórquez:** Carl Jung wrote about the subconscious accumulation of experience: things we have passed through in life are not gone, they are stored in the subconscious and become part of what we call intuition. The gut feeling arrives before we can articulate it — and indeed, as I argue, it literally arrives at the brain first via the fast neuropod-vagus-brainstem pathway. Many languages have a body-centered idiom for premonition: in Portuguese "frieza na barriga" (cold in the stomach), in Spanish "presentimiento" (pre-feeling or pre-sensing). It's not accidental that these phrases locate the feeling in the body rather than the head.

**Diego Bohórquez:** Food also synchronizes intuition between people. There's psychological evidence that people eating the same food — or sharing a meal in the same ritual context — are more likely to connect and agree. The guayusa ritual is an example: the act of drinking from the same pot, at the same time, in a community setting, synchronizes gut-brain states across participants and facilitates honest, open dialogue. Walter Cannon — chair of physiology at Harvard in the 1920s-30s, author of "The Wisdom of the Body" — described in a paper called "Voodoo Death" how a shaman's curse caused actual physiological death in believers through the body's belief system overriding normal function. The gut and the belief system interact at a very deep level.

## [02:22:00] Vagus Nerve & Learning, Humming

**Andrew Huberman:** The vagus nerve is often described as purely calming — the "rest and digest" parasympathetic pathway. But that's an oversimplification, isn't it?

**Diego Bohórquez:** It is, and I think it's important for people to understand this. Both experimentally and clinically, direct stimulation of the vagus nerve produces arousal, not sedation. Vagal nerve stimulators used to treat depression drive alertness and activation. Long exhale breathing and physiological sighs activate the vagus through a different mechanism — one that does slow heart rate and produce calm — but that represents a specific subpopulation of vagal fibers. The vagus is better thought of as a superhighway carrying many distinct traffic lanes: some lanes calm, some arouse, some relate to gut sensation, some to ear sensation, some to immune signaling. The baseline tone of vagal activation reflects the overall RPM of the system.

**Diego Bohórquez:** The branch of the vagus that innervates the inner ear may be why certain music at certain frequencies induces calm — the sound directly vibrates the vagus at a resonant frequency. Humming has been linked to vasodilation and a calming effect; many religious practices across cultures incorporate humming, chanting, and singing precisely because these practices modulate vagal tone. There's evidence runners who listen to music at the right tempo perform better and feel calmer because the rhythm synchronizes with their motor and gut electrical patterns.

## [02:26:46] Digestive System & Memory; Body Sensing

**Andrew Huberman:** The gut's sensory signal doesn't just drive immediate cravings — it also forms memories. How does the gut contribute to memory?

**Diego Bohórquez:** The gut's contribution to memory goes through the nucleus tractus solitarius in the brain stem, which projects to the hypothalamus, limbic areas, and the striatum. The information from a meal — its nutrient content, context, social setting — gets integrated with emotional and contextual memories. That's why taste-aversion learning (the Garcia effect) is so powerful: a single bad experience with a food, paired with nausea, creates a lifelong avoidance memory that is extraordinarily resistant to extinction. The gut's sensory signal is privileged in memory formation in a way that pure cognitive information is not. Conversely, positive food memories — a grandmother's cooking, a meal at a special moment — are among the most emotionally laden and persistent memories we have.

**Diego Bohórquez:** In one of my papers we quoted a wonderful passage from the 1853 French book "Memoirs of a Stomach," written from the perspective of the stomach speaking to the brain. The stomach says: "I communicate to the brain with rapidity through two sets of electrical wires the arrivals of the day, as we may eat, with precision — so the brain will make its own feelings and impressions." And later: "When I grew morose — when digestion failed — the brain also grew irritable and petulant." This was written in 1853, with zero cellular biology, and yet it captures the directional primacy of gut-to-brain communication with remarkable accuracy.

## [02:32:51] Listening to the Body, Meditation

**Andrew Huberman:** Meditation, mindfulness, body-scan practices — there is a large movement of people trying to tune in to what their body is telling them. Based on everything you've described, is there a scientific basis for deliberately cultivating gut awareness?

**Diego Bohórquez:** There is. Interoception — the awareness of what's happening inside the body — is a real skill that can be developed. People who practice certain forms of meditation or body-scan awareness can become more attuned to gut sensations, heartbeat, and other internal signals. That awareness can improve decision-making, food choices, and emotional regulation. However, there's also a caveat: over-awareness of internal signals can be pathological. People with high anxiety are sometimes so aware of their heartbeat that it becomes disruptive and distracting. The goal is calibrated awareness — enough to make better decisions, not so much that internal signals become overwhelming.

**Diego Bohórquez:** The guayusa morning ritual is in some ways a structured contemplative practice: quiet time, before the distractions of the day, with a gentle stimulant that sharpens focus but doesn't produce anxiety, shared with the community, in which the body is invited to be present and to communicate. It is meditation, community, and nutrition combined. I think many ancient practices — from the Japanese tea ceremony to the Mediterranean shared table — are sophisticated protocols for synchronizing the gut-brain axis in ways that support both physical and social well-being.

**Andrew Huberman:** What you're describing suggests that paying attention to what our gut tells us — not just about food, but about the full range of life decisions — is not superstition, not mysticism, but a very sophisticated information channel that has been shaped by hundreds of millions of years of evolution. The gut-brain axis, as you study it, really is a sixth sense.

**Diego Bohórquez:** Exactly. And just as with other senses, we can train our attunement to it, we can study it scientifically, and we will eventually be able to modulate it therapeutically. We are, as I like to say, on virgin beaches — there is so much ahead.
