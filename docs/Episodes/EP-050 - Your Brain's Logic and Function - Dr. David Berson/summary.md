# Episode 50: Your Brain's Logic & Function | Dr. David Berson

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. David Berson, Professor and Chairman of Neuroscience at Brown University and Huberman's personal mentor for nearly two decades, delivers a structured masterclass traversing the nervous system from the retinal periphery to the cerebral cortex. The episode covers the mechanisms of color vision, rod and cone photoreceptors, and the co-discovery of intrinsically photosensitive melanopsin cells (ipRGCs) that set the body's circadian clock via the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Berson explains how the vestibular system detects gravity and acceleration, how the cerebellum integrates sensory-motor signals for coordination and learning, and how visual-vestibular conflict causes motion sickness. The conversation ascends to the midbrain's reflexive orienting circuits and blindsight, then to the basal ganglia's go/no-go gating of deliberate action, the cortex's suppression of reflexes, and cortical neuroplasticity illustrated by a woman whose visual cortex was fully reallocated to braille reading. The episode closes with an overview of connectomics — nanometer-scale electron-microscopy wiring diagrams of neural circuits — and resources for learning neuroscience, including the Eyewire citizen-science platform.

## Guest: Dr. David Berson

**David Berson** is Professor of Medical Science, Neuroscience, and Ophthalmology at Brown University, where he also serves as Chairman of Neuroscience. His laboratory co-discovered the intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) that express the photopigment melanopsin — the neurons that set the circadian clock. His research spans vestibular-visual integration, image-stabilization circuits, and connectomics. Berson is widely regarded as one of the finest neuroscience educators in the United States; Andrew Huberman credits him as a personal mentor of nearly twenty years.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Vision is a brain phenomenon, not merely a retinal one.** Conscious visual experience arises in the cortex — you can see vividly while dreaming with no retinal input whatsoever. The eye is the camera; the cortex is where the image actually lives. [00:08:02](transcript.md#000802-how-we-see)

2. **Color is decoded by comparing relative signals across three cone types.** Short-, medium-, and long-wavelength cones each absorb a preferred frequency; the brain infers hue by contrasting those signals. Dogs have only two cone types, giving them color vision analogous to human red-green dichromats. [00:10:02](transcript.md#001002-color-vision)

3. **Melanopsin ipRGCs sit in the wrong retinal layer and use an insect-like signaling cascade.** Unlike rods and cones at the back of the retina, these ganglion cells sit at the output layer; their phototransduction cascade resembles fly photoreceptors more than mammalian ones — a preserved evolutionary primitive. They encode sustained brightness, not image detail. [00:16:56](transcript.md#001656-how-you-orient-in-time)

4. **The SCN is the master pacemaker that synchronizes billions of peripheral clocks.** The suprachiasmatic nucleus sits in the hypothalamus and receives direct melanopsin input. Without its synchronizing signal, peripheral clocks in liver, stomach, and other organs drift out of phase — explaining why totally blind patients often suffer chronic insomnia. [00:25:45](transcript.md#002545-body-rhythms-pineal-function-light--melatonin-blueblockers)

5. **Light suppresses melatonin through a multi-step pathway: retina → SCN → sympathetic nervous system → pineal gland.** Any bright light — not just blue — can slam melatonin to baseline. Blue light is more efficient, but bright red light still has the effect. Blue-blockers worn during the day are counterproductive; the goal is maximum photons during the day and minimum at night. [00:25:45](transcript.md#002545-body-rhythms-pineal-function-light--melatonin-blueblockers)

6. **A retinal pathway to the peri-habenular region links light directly to mood and self-image.** Research from Tara's lab (Dale Fernandez et al.) shows that activating this non-image-forming trunk line at the wrong time of day induces depressive behavior in animals; silencing it blocks light-driven depression. This explains why seasonal affective disorder and light therapy have dramatic mood effects. [00:36:20](transcript.md#003620-sensation-mood--self-image)

7. **Time spent outdoors reduces myopia in children — mechanism still unresolved.** The epidemiological finding is large and robust. Two candidate mechanisms: high outdoor luminance driving melanopsin signaling, or frequent accommodation to far distances. Outdoor time appears to be the decisive variable either way. [00:34:45](transcript.md#003445-spending-times-outdoors-improves-eyesight)

8. **The vestibular system encodes rotational acceleration (semicircular canals) and linear acceleration/gravity (otolith organs).** Three canals oriented like three hula hoops capture yes/no/tilt head rotations. Vestibular signals drive compensatory eye movements (vestibulo-ocular reflex) that stabilize retinal images during head movement — the same principle as optical image stabilization in phones. Pigeons bob their heads to keep the retinal image stable while their body moves forward. [00:41:03](transcript.md#004103-sense-of-balance)

9. **Motion sickness arises from visual-vestibular conflict; looking toward the horizon resolves it.** When the vestibular system reports forward acceleration but the retina sees a static phone screen, the brain interprets the mismatch as possible toxin ingestion and triggers nausea as a behavioral correction signal. Matching what the eyes see to what the body feels eliminates the conflict. [00:50:43](transcript.md#005043-why-pigeons-bob-their-heads-motion-sickness)

10. **The cerebellum is the air-traffic controller of sensory-motor integration and motor learning.** It receives copies of sensory signals and motor commands, detects errors, and refines movement timing. Cerebellar damage (ataxia) causes characteristic reach-overshoot-correct tremor and eliminates the ability to learn new motor sequences from practice. The flocculus is the specific cerebellar region where visual and vestibular information converge for image stabilization. [00:50:43](transcript.md#005043-why-pigeons-bob-their-heads-motion-sickness)

11. **The midbrain superior colliculus runs reflexive orienting across all sensory modalities.** It receives converging input from vision, hearing, touch, and — in rattlesnakes — infrared pit organs. Corroborating weak signals from two senses at the same location combines to produce a strong orienting response. This center is the main visual processing center in frogs and lizards, and remains active subcortically in humans. [01:02:35](transcript.md#010235-midbrain--blindsight)

12. **Blindsight: destruction of primary visual cortex leaves unconscious vision intact.** Patients with a completely destroyed V1 cannot consciously see, yet reflexively orient toward visual stimuli processed subcortically through the superior colliculus. This demonstrates that visual processing runs in parallel streams, only one of which reaches conscious awareness. [01:02:35](transcript.md#010235-midbrain--blindsight)

13. **The basal ganglia implement go/no-go gating of cortically planned actions via dopamine-driven pathways.** The go pathway (dopamine-facilitated) releases planned movements; the no-go pathway suppresses them. The marshmallow test maps directly onto this balance: the cortex frames the delayed-gratification decision, the basal ganglia execute or block the reach. Deliberately practicing no-go responses builds this circuitry like any other skill. [01:16:35](transcript.md#011635-basal-ganglia--the-2-marshmallow-test)

14. **The cortex suppresses hard-wired reflexes when context demands it — this is the neural basis of focused attention.** Over-thinking a rehearsed motor skill introduces cortical interference with a perfectly trained cerebellar program. Conversely, the cortex keeps you from dropping a scalding teacup in the presence of the Queen. Failures of attention largely reflect failures of this cortical suppression system. [01:24:40](transcript.md#012440-suppressing-reflexes-cortex)

15. **Neuroplasticity can fully reallocate entire cortical areas across sensory modalities.** A woman blind from birth, an expert braille reader, had a stroke in her visual cortex and lost the ability to read braille — her visual cortex had been completely reassigned to tactile fingertip processing. The reciprocal also occurs: blind individuals often develop enhanced hearing and touch as adjacent cortex is recruited. [01:33:33](transcript.md#013333-neuroplasticity)

16. **Connectomics is a nanometer-scale complete wiring diagram of neural tissue.** By imaging brain sections sequentially with electron microscopy and reconstructing every cell boundary and synapse, researchers build circuit diagrams that reveal cell types and connections invisible to any prior hypothesis. Berson's lab used connectomics to identify which cell type sets the speed-tuning of an image-stabilization circuit — anatomy drove the functional hypothesis, which electrophysiology confirmed. [01:36:27](transcript.md#013627-what-is-a-connectome)

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Bright Light Exposure
- **What:** Get bright, broad-spectrum light into your eyes within the first 1–2 hours after waking to set circadian phase via melanopsin ipRGCs.
- **How:** Go outside or sit near an open window. Even overcast outdoor light provides far more photons across the full retina than indoor artificial lighting. Maximize retinal coverage — don't look at a narrow patch of sky.
- **When:** Every morning, ideally at or near sunrise.
- **Ref:** [00:16:56](transcript.md#001656-how-you-orient-in-time), [00:25:45](transcript.md#002545-body-rhythms-pineal-function-light--melatonin-blueblockers)

### Avoid Bright Light at Night
- **What:** Minimize bright light of any wavelength after dark to prevent melatonin suppression and circadian disruption.
- **How:** Dim overhead lights, use warm-spectrum bulbs, or wear blue-light-blocking glasses. The key is total photon load, not just blue wavelengths — even red light suppresses melatonin if bright enough.
- **When:** Approximately 2 hours before intended sleep onset onward.
- **Ref:** [00:25:45](transcript.md#002545-body-rhythms-pineal-function-light--melatonin-blueblockers)

### Outdoor Time for Children's Eye Health
- **What:** Ensure children spend substantial time outdoors daily to reduce myopia risk.
- **How:** Unstructured outdoor time with natural light appears sufficient — not necessarily sports. Avoid extended near-viewing (phones, books) without outdoor light breaks.
- **When:** Daily habit throughout childhood and adolescence.
- **Ref:** [00:34:45](transcript.md#003445-spending-times-outdoors-improves-eyesight)

### Reducing Motion Sickness
- **What:** Reduce vestibular-visual conflict by aligning what you see with what your body feels.
- **How:** Look toward the horizon or out the front windshield. Avoid screens or reading while in motion. Sit in the front seat when possible — more of the forward visual field matches the vestibular forward-motion signal.
- **When:** Whenever traveling as a passenger in a vehicle, on boats, or during any motion where gaze is uncontrolled.
- **Ref:** [00:50:43](transcript.md#005043-why-pigeons-bob-their-heads-motion-sickness)

### Ear-Pressure Equalization (Altitude Changes)
- **What:** Use the Eustachian tube to equalize middle-ear pressure during altitude changes.
- **How:** Plug the nose and blow outward gently (Valsalva) when external pressure is high (aircraft descent, scuba descent). Plug nose and inhale gently when external pressure is lower (ascending altitude). Berson notes that simply opening the passageway — either direction — may be sufficient.
- **When:** During aircraft descent, mountain driving, or scuba diving.
- **Ref:** [01:00:03](transcript.md#010003-popping-ears)

### Practicing No-Go Responses to Build Impulse Control
- **What:** Deliberately withhold impulsive actions daily to strengthen basal ganglia no-go circuitry and cortical suppression pathways.
- **How:** Each day, when you feel an automatic impulse (reaching for your phone, eating impulsively, making a reactive comment), consciously pause and do not execute the action. Huberman practices 21 intentional no-gos per day. Berson confirms this is genuine skill-building.
- **When:** Daily, embedded in normal routines.
- **Ref:** [01:16:35](transcript.md#011635-basal-ganglia--the-2-marshmallow-test), [01:24:40](transcript.md#012440-suppressing-reflexes-cortex)

### Participate in Connectomics Research via Eyewire
- **What:** Contribute to real neuroscience by helping reconstruct retinal neurons from electron-microscopy images — no lab or degree required.
- **How:** Visit [Eyewire](https://eyewire.org/explore), complete the onboarding tutorial. The platform gamifies neural circuit reconstruction and results feed into published research.
- **When:** Any time; accessible from any internet-connected device.
- **Ref:** [01:45:20](transcript.md#014520-how-to-learn-more-about-the-brain)
