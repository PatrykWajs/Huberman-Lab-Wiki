## [00:00:00] Dr. David Berson

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. Andrew Huberman introduces himself as a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. He then introduces Dr. David Berson, whom he describes as his go-to source for all things nervous system for more than 20 years. The conversation is framed as a tour of the brain — how it is structured, how it works, and how the different regions coordinate to produce thought, perception, and behavior.

## [00:00:38] Visual Perception, Photons & Retinal Processing, Ganglion Cells

Visual experience begins in the brain, not the eye. Berson explains that the reason you see a truck drive past or a photograph on the wall is that your brain has some pattern of neural activity it associates with input arriving from the periphery — yet you can have a visual experience with no input from the eyes at all, as in dreaming. Under normal waking conditions, what matters is what the retina communicates to the brain. The critical cells for this communication are ganglion cells, which are neurons that form the bridge between the eye and the brain proper. The eye functions like a camera — it captures an initial image and performs early processing — but conscious visual experience only arises at the level of the cortex, while many other brain regions also receive visual input and use it for non-conscious purposes.

## [00:02:12] Color Vision, Wavelengths & Photoreceptors; Cones & Rods

Light is electromagnetic radiation, and the nervous system decodes its different wavelengths to produce the sensation of color. Certain frequencies of the electromagnetic spectrum can be detected by neurons in the retina, and different wavelengths are unpacked by comparing signals across photoreceptors tuned to different preferred frequencies. In the outermost layer of the retina, three distinct proteins — each absorbing light at a preferred wavelength — generate the signals the nervous system contrasts to extract color information. This is why looking at a golden landscape tells your brain it is late in the day: the composition of wavelengths reaching the eye shifts, and the brain interprets that shift as a change in scene. Whether one person's subjective experience of "red" matches another's is a deep philosophical question that empirical science cannot fully resolve, though the biological mechanisms for color vision appear highly conserved across individuals. Beyond the three cone types for color, the retina contains rod cells for dim-light vision and a fifth class of photopigment — melanopsin — which will be discussed separately. In most mammals such as dogs and cats, only two cone types exist, substantially limiting color discrimination compared to humans.

## [00:06:32] Melanopsin, Intrinsically Photosensitive Retinal Ganglion Cells & Brightness Detection

Melanopsin is a peculiar photopigment in the wrong place. The retina is organized like a layer cake, with photoreceptors sitting in the outermost layer — the film of the camera — where they convert photons into neural signals. But melanopsin resides in the innermost layer of the retina, inside certain ganglion cells — the same output neurons that transmit information to the brain. These intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) were not expected to be directly light-sensitive, yet they make melanopsin, absorb light, and send signals to the brain independent of the rod and cone system. Their primary role is encoding overall brightness — specifically the ambient light intensity in the environment — which is the signal that drives the circadian system. This is why people with retinal blindness can still synchronize their circadian clocks if their ipRGCs remain intact, while those without functioning ipRGCs struggle with circadian disruption and insomnia.

## [00:08:45] Circadian Clock & Synchronization, Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN); Master Clock Function

The circadian clock is distributed throughout virtually every tissue in the body, but its coordination requires a central pacemaker: the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), a small collection of nerve cells sitting in the hypothalamus. The SCN has an intrinsic rhythm of roughly 24 hours — close to, but not exactly, one day — so it must be synchronized daily using a light signal from the ipRGCs to confirm when the sun is rising and setting. Blind patients with retinal blindness commonly report insomnia because their clocks drift out of phase without that synchronizing signal. Once entrained, the SCN coordinates downstream systems through humoral (blood-borne) signals, the autonomic nervous system, and projections to higher brain centers — effectively telling the liver, stomach, immune system, and other organs when to do their respective jobs. The hypothalamus, where the SCN sits, is described as the great coordinator of drives and the source of most of our pleasures and problems.

## [00:11:30] Hypothalamus, Autonomic Nervous System & Hormonal Systems

The SCN's reach extends through the autonomic nervous system — regulating alertness and calm — and through humoral signals released into the bloodstream that travel to tissues throughout the body. One prominent downstream target is the pineal gland, which releases melatonin. Melatonin levels are low during the day and high at night, giving cells a hormonal readout of the time of day. This chain of influence — light to ipRGCs to SCN to pineal to melatonin — means that light is directly controlling hormonal output through a pathway most people never consciously think about.

## [00:12:12] Tool: Light Exposure & Melatonin Regulation, Pineal Function

If you were to measure your melatonin level hour by hour across a full day, it would be very low during daylight hours and very high at night. Getting up in the middle of the night and turning on a bright fluorescent light slams melatonin to the floor almost immediately, because the ipRGC-SCN-pineal pathway is counting photons rather than conscious context. This is one of the clearest demonstrations that light acts on the body's hormonal status through routes entirely beyond conscious awareness — the visual system is processing the toothpaste and the toothbrush while a parallel system is silently registering photon count and shutting down melatonin release.

## [00:13:06] Vestibular System, Balance & Motion Detection; Semicircular Canals

The vestibular system is designed to detect how you are moving through the world, even in the absence of visual or auditory input. It is built around hair cells in the inner ear that bend in response to movement, either exciting or inhibiting the neurons they contact. Evolution constructed a system where fluid-filled tubes — semicircular canals — amplify this principle: when you rotate your head, the fluid inside the canal lags behind, bending the hairs and generating a signal proportional to the rotation. Three semicircular canals oriented in three orthogonal planes mirror the three-axis encoding of the cone system in color vision, allowing the brain to distinguish rotation in any direction. A fourth sensor — the otolith organs — detects linear acceleration and gravitational orientation, corresponding to what Berson calls the "puppy head tilt" axis. Most of this processing happens below conscious awareness as reflexive computation.

## [00:15:45] Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex, Image Stabilization & Head Rotation

When you rotate your head to the left, your eyes automatically rotate to the right — a reflex that occurs even in complete darkness. This vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) represents the brain's effort to stabilize the image of the world on the retina, because a stable retinal image produces sharper perception. When scanning a scene, animals make rapid eye movements (saccades) for very short intervals and then rest — not drifting slowly. Pigeons bob their heads while walking so their visual world stays stationary while their body advances; the "chicken-head stabilization" viral videos capture the same mechanism. All of these behaviors — across very different species — reflect the same deep imperative: keep the retinal image as stable as possible, and when movement is unavoidable, make it fast and brief, then re-stabilize.

## [00:17:22] Motion Sickness, Visual-Vestibular Conflict; Tool: Avoiding Nausea

Motion sickness arises from visual-vestibular conflict: two sensory systems give the brain inconsistent information about how the body is moving through space. When driving, the vestibular system senses forward acceleration while the visual system sees the environment sweeping past — both signals agree, and there is no problem. But reading a phone while in a moving vehicle means the retina sees a stable screen while the vestibular system detects real motion, producing a mismatch the brain interprets as a threat, expressed as nausea. The practical implication is straightforward: to avoid motion sickness, align the visual and vestibular signals — look at the horizon or outside the vehicle rather than at a stationary screen. The nausea is the brain's way of motivating a change in behavior.

## [00:19:06] Cerebellum, Motor Coordination & Learning

The cerebellum — the "little brain" tucked beneath the cortex — functions like air-traffic control: it continuously integrates sensory signals from every system and monitors the activity of motor-planning centers elsewhere in the brain. Without the cerebellum you would not be paralyzed, because motor neurons and reflex centers remain, but coordination and timing would suffer severely. Learning a new athletic movement — such as an overhead serve in tennis — requires thousands of repetitions precisely because the cerebellum is gradually refining the sequences of muscle activation and sensory feedback until the movement becomes smooth, accurate, and automatic. The cerebellum does not initiate movement; it calibrates it.

## [00:20:28] Cerebellar Function, Precision & Timing of Movement; Cerebellar Ataxia

Cerebellar damage produces a characteristic clinical picture called cerebellar ataxia: patients are unsteady on their feet, struggle to adjust to dynamic balance demands such as riding a streetcar without a pole, and exhibit an intention tremor — reaching too far for an object, overcorrecting, and oscillating around the target. The same deficits arise not only from damage to the cerebellum itself but from damage to the input or output tracts that feed it information or carry its corrections to motor neurons. Cerebellar ataxia is one of the more common neurological presentations precisely because so many pathways converge on this structure.

## [00:21:56] Flocculus & Visual-Vestibular Integration

The flocculus is one of the evolutionarily oldest subregions of the cerebellum and serves as the primary site where visual and vestibular signals converge to calibrate the image-stabilizing network. If the vestibular apparatus sustains partial damage, the visual system signals an error to the cerebellum, which then learns to increase vestibular output to compensate — a form of adaptive motor learning. This error-correction loop is representative of the cerebellum's function across many sensorimotor domains: detect a discrepancy, compute a correction, and gradually reduce the error through practice and plasticity.

## [00:22:52] Midbrain, Brainstem & Reflexive Behavior; Superior Colliculus

Moving up from the spinal cord through the brainstem to the midbrain, the nervous system transitions from basic reflex circuits to increasingly sophisticated integration. The midbrain represents the last segment of the brainstem before the thalamus and cortex, and it contains the superior colliculus — a key visual center for orienting behavior. The superior colliculus functions as a reflex center that reorients gaze, body posture, and attention toward locations in space that carry behavioral significance: a predator appearing in peripheral vision, a sudden movement, or a splatter on the page of a book — all trigger the superior colliculus to redirect the organism without requiring conscious deliberation. This capacity evolved early because rapid, involuntary reorientation toward salient events was critical for survival long before complex cortical processing existed.

## [00:25:33] Spatial Orientation & Multisensory Integration; Rattlesnake Heat Detection

The superior colliculus and adjacent brainstem regions receive input from touch, hearing, and even thermoreception, not just vision. Berson describes working with rattlesnakes, which have specialized pit organs on their faces that image the spatial distribution of heat — an entirely separate sensory modality used to detect and localize warm-blooded prey. This infrared imaging converges with visual information in the same brainstem region, illustrating that sensory modality is less important than location in space: all the different signals — photons, sound, pressure, heat — ultimately resolve into electricity flowing through neurons, and the brain uses them interchangeably if they carry spatial information about the environment. The same logic applies to human multisensory integration: it does not matter whether the signal comes from eyes, ears, or skin — it is all electrical input being processed to make decisions about the world.

## [00:27:20] Sensory Integration & Corroboration

When multiple sensory channels report the same thing — heat on one side of your face and the smell of something baking in the oven — each signal individually may be weak, but their corroboration in the midbrain strengthens the combined percept significantly. This is why animals and humans benefit from having many parallel sensory systems: redundancy produces confidence. But when signals conflict, the brain becomes confused and may generate aversive states — Berson draws the analogy of a spy whose sources are giving contradictory intelligence reports. Sensory conflict in the midbrain is likely one route by which motion sickness produces nausea, extending the visual-vestibular conflict story to the broader multisensory integration framework of the brainstem.

## [00:28:12] Basal Ganglia, Go vs No-Go Behavior & Decision Making

The basal ganglia sit deep in the forebrain and are deeply intertwined with cortical function — neither can operate properly without the other. Their central computational role is mediating the choice between executing behavior (go) and withholding it (no-go). The cortex supplies the evaluative context — assessing contingencies, imagining consequences, weighing one option against another — while the basal ganglia implement the resulting decision by gating whether a motor or behavioral program is permitted to run. Practical examples include deciding to go for a morning run despite reluctance, choosing to stop a run when time is short, or resisting the impulse to eat a marshmallow in order to receive a larger reward later.

## [00:29:15] Tool: Impulse Control & Delayed Gratification, Marshmallow Test

The classic marshmallow test — where children can have one marshmallow immediately or two if they wait 30 seconds — is a direct behavioral measure of no-go circuit function. Children who successfully delay are running a cortically driven suppression of the reach toward the immediate reward. The cortex represents the future state ("two marshmallows is better") and instructs the basal ganglia to block the competing impulse. Individual differences in this capacity reflect both genetic variation in basal ganglia and cortical circuitry and experiential factors that shape how those circuits develop — there is no single cause and no single fixed outcome.

## [00:30:13] Individual Differences, Genetics & Experience

Brains are complicated, and the particular brain any individual has is the joint product of genetics and accumulated experience — both of which differ from person to person. The things that are easy or hard for one person will not necessarily match the pattern for another. However, the fact that the brain was not chosen does not mean it cannot change: learning new skills, practicing restraint, and accumulating structured experience all reshape the circuits we have been handed. All the structures discussed — basal ganglia, cerebellum, midbrain, cortex — operate in parallel and in constant cross talk, modifying one another continuously across the lifespan.

## [00:31:05] Visual Cortex, Neural Processing & Brain Plasticity

The cortex is the seat of higher consciousness, self-image, planning, and action — but it also contains dedicated regions for sensory processing, including a large expanse of visual cortex. The question of what happens to visual cortex when the eyes stop providing input reveals the brain's remarkable capacity for reorganization. Rather than lying dormant, deafferented visual cortex gets repurposed for processing other kinds of spatial information that remain active.

## [00:32:00] Cortical Reorganization, Braille Reading & Stroke Recovery

Berson recounts the case of a woman who had been blind from early life and rose to a senior executive secretarial role at a major corporation, reading Braille with exceptional speed. She suffered a stroke; the neurologist's initially reassuring news — that the stroke was confined to her visual cortex, which she supposedly wasn't using — proved tragically wrong. She lost her ability to read Braille. Imaging studies have confirmed that in people blind from early life, visual cortex gets repurposed as a center for tactile spatial processing, with practiced Braille readers actually allocating what should be visual cortex to the fingertips. The visual cortex is a general-purpose spatial processing machine: deprived of visual input, it recruits whatever spatial information remains available — skin, touch, texture — and applies the same computational machinery. The same plasticity can work in the opposite direction: people who lose vision can develop heightened hearing and tactile acuity as the freed cortical real estate is colonized by adjacent sensory systems.
