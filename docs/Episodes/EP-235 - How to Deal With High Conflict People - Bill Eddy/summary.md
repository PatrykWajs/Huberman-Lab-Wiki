# Episode 235: How to Deal With High Conflict People | Bill Eddy

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Bill Eddy — lawyer, licensed therapist, professional mediator, and Pepperdine University School of Law faculty member — joins Andrew Huberman to explain how to identify, manage, and disengage from high-conflict individuals. The conversation covers how high-conflict personalities (HCPs) differ from clinical personality disorders, the neuroscience of emotional contagion and fear learning, and a suite of practical tools: the WEB Method for identification, the CARS Method for engagement, EAR Statements, BIFF Responses, and the Four Fuhgeddaboudits. Eddy draws on decades of family law, mediation, and clinical therapy to explain why these patterns persist and how ordinary people can navigate them safely.

## Key Takeaways

- **High-Conflict Personality vs. Personality Disorder:** HCPs are defined by a persistent pattern of blaming others — not by any clinical diagnosis. About half of people with personality disorders have high-conflict traits; the other half do not. HCPs can exist with no formal diagnosis at all.
- **The Five High-Conflict Personality Types:** Based on NIH research (early 2000s), the five most conflict-prone patterns are narcissistic (~6% of US adults, 62% male), borderline (~6%, nearly 50/50 male/female), antisocial (~4%, 75% male), histrionic (~2%, 50/50), and paranoid (~4%). Total personality disorder prevalence in the US: ~15%.
- **Two Flavors of HCP:** Combative HCPs are openly aggressive and blaming. Victim-type HCPs appear passive and recruit "negative advocates" — people they enlist to fight their battles while positioning themselves as the wronged party.
- **The First-Year Rule:** Do not make major commitments (marriage, cohabitation, children) in the first year of a relationship. Personality disorders manifest through interpersonal dysfunction only visible in close, sustained contact — under stress, sleep deprivation, or small domestic friction.
- **Negative Advocates:** HCPs recruit third parties (friends, family, lawyers, therapists) who believe their one-sided narrative and amplify the conflict. These advocates are typically not malicious — they are deceived.
- **Emotional Contagion and Brain Plasticity:** The amygdala learns context-dependently. Repeated exposure to high-conflict dynamics can train the brain to associate arousal with dopamine reward — people can become habituated to or addicted to drama. Bullies exploit this same mechanism online.
- **Why Confronting or Labeling HCPs Backfires:** Telling a high-conflict person they have a personality disorder, or directly confronting their behavior, escalates conflict and triggers retaliation. The goal is to manage the dynamic, not to generate insight.
- **Gradual Exit from a Combative HCP:** Do not announce a breakup abruptly. Reduce contact slowly, create distance over time, and avoid dramatic confrontation. Sudden exits provoke rage; slow fading reduces risk.
- **Hoovering:** When aggression fails, victim-type HCPs often switch to extreme affection and pleading to pull a departing partner back. Recognize this as a predictable pattern, not genuine change.
- **High Conflict Divorce:** Children benefit when the legal system understands HCP dynamics. Judges, lawyers, and therapists who recognize the pattern can structure court orders to protect children and limit the HCP's leverage.
- **Personality Disorders in the Workplace:** HCPs are disproportionately present in high-visibility occupations (law, politics, entertainment). They are skilled at appearing reasonable on the surface, which complicates identification.

## Tools & Protocols

### WEB Method (Identify High-Conflict Individuals)

A three-part screening framework to evaluate whether someone has high-conflict traits:

- **W — Words:** Does the person use excessive blame language, All-or-Nothing thinking, and disparaging words about others?
- **E — Emotions (Unmanaged):** Are their emotions disproportionate to circumstances? Some HCPs hide this on the surface.
- **B — Behavior (Extreme):** Do they do things 90% of people would never do — public confrontations, boundary violations, extreme acts?

Also observe: how someone treats service staff; whether they read malice into minor slights; what multiple people in different areas of their life independently say about them.

### First-Year Rule

- No marriage, engagement, or children in year one of a romantic relationship.
- Living together can be acceptable — proximity accelerates access to information.
- Observe behavior under stress, sleep deprivation, and small-stakes friction.
- Seek independent input from people across different domains of the person's life.

### Vetting Potential Partners and Associates

- Ask open-ended questions about past relationships and jobs; listen for extreme blame of others.
- Note whether everyone in their past eventually becomes a villain.
- Watch how they treat strangers (wait staff, service workers, customer service).

### Don't Label (Empathy-First Approach)

- Never diagnose or label someone as HCP or personality-disordered to their face or to third parties.
- Focus on behavior and patterns, not character.
- Empathy and attention de-escalate more effectively than confrontation.

### Leaving a Combative HCP (Gradual Exit)

- Plan the exit quietly and in advance.
- Reduce contact gradually rather than making a dramatic announcement.
- Secure finances, housing, and legal support before the HCP is aware of the intent.
- Anticipate a blame campaign — they will recruit negative advocates and may escalate publicly.
- Stay calm and non-retaliatory — any dramatic reaction confirms their narrative.

### Disentangling from a Victim-Type HCP (Hoovering)

- Recognize the switch from aggression to desperate affection as a predictable pattern, not change.
- Do not re-engage in response to the emotional pull — it restarts the cycle.
- Maintain firm, consistent limits without cruelty.

### EAR Statements (Managing Emotions and Relationships)

A communication tool for de-escalating a distressed or angry HCP in the moment:

- **E — Empathy:** "I can see how hard this is for you."
- **A — Attention:** "Tell me more — I want to understand your situation."
- **R — Respect:** "I respect that you care so much about this."

EAR statements do not require agreement with the HCP's position. They reduce emotional temperature so productive conversation becomes possible.

### Four Fuhgeddaboudits (Topics to Avoid in High-Conflict Resolution)

Four things that never work with HCPs — do not try them:

1. **Don't give insight:** Never try to make an HCP see that their behavior is the problem. It backfires.
2. **Don't dwell on the past:** HCPs cannot resolve past events. Stay future-focused.
3. **Don't focus on emotions:** Do not yell, cry, or express frustration — it escalates conflict.
4. **Don't demand acknowledgment:** An HCP will not genuinely admit fault. Seeking it is a trap.

### CARS Method (Connecting, Analyzing, Responding, Setting Limits)

A four-step engagement framework for navigating high-conflict interactions:

**C — Connect:** Open with an EAR statement. Establish that you see the person as an equal and are paying attention. This lowers defensiveness before any problem-solving begins.

**A — Analyze:** Understand what the person actually needs (often: to be heard, to have options presented). Ask questions; listen for what is driving the conflict beneath the blame.

**R — Respond (BIFF Response):** Give a BIFF response — Brief, Informative, Friendly, Firm. Do not argue, defend at length, or counter-attack. Provide information and close the exchange. Most effective in writing (email).

- **Brief:** Keep it short. No lengthy explanations.
- **Informative:** Stick to facts.
- **Friendly:** Neutral, non-hostile tone.
- **Firm:** End the discussion; do not invite further argument.

**S — Setting Limits (SLIC):** Because HCPs do not self-regulate, others must set external limits.

- **S — Say it once:** State the limit once, clearly.
- **L — Leave or remove yourself:** Disengage if the limit is not respected.
- **I — Implement a consequence:** Follow through — do not threaten what you will not enforce.
- **C — Consistent:** Apply limits consistently. Inconsistency reinforces testing behavior.
