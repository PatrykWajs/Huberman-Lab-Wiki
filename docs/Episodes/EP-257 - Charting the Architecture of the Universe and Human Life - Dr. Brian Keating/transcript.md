## [00:02:07] Cosmology, Origin of Universe

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Dr. Andrew Huberman, it's great to meet you in person finally. I thought you were a legend.

**Andrew Huberman:** I exist in real life, and you do as well. I'm delighted that we're going to talk today because I have a longstanding adoration — there's no other appropriate word — for eyes, vision, optics, the stars, the moons, the sun. **Dr. Brian Keating:** That's right. You're a physicist, you're a cosmologist — not a cosmetologist.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's right.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** I do do hair and makeup if you're interested. Please orient us in the galaxy. So I get to study the entire universe, basically, and it's not really such a stretch that cosmetology and cosmology share this prefix. The word "cosmos" in Greek — the etymology of it — means beautiful or appearance. It reflects the fact that the night sky is also beautiful, attractive, and evokes something viscerally in us. We have to use the fossils that have made their way throughout all of cosmic time to understand what it was like at the very beginning — and perhaps about the universe as it existed before time itself began. Cosmology allows us to apply the strict, exacting laws of physics to the origin of everything in the universe. That's what makes it so fascinating.

## [00:08:33] Stars, Planets, Early Humans, Time

**Andrew Huberman:** You said something that feels intuitively true — that there's something about looking up into space, especially at night when we see the stars, that changes our perception of time. The human brain can think into the past, be present, and project into the future. Why do you think it is that when we look up at the stars it gives us a sense of expansion of time, as opposed to just expansion of space?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** We have to take ourselves back to deep prehistory. We know that ancient people were looking at constellations because they were seemingly correlated with or causative of the seasons, and that was of supreme importance to them. Their entire existence — in early agrarian, hunting, and gathering societies — depended on knowing about time. In the caves at Lascaux, which date back to 40,000 BCE, they depict constellations: Orion the hunter, Taurus the bull. The repetition of star positions over generations was passed down through oral language before written language even existed — written language is only about 10,000 years old.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The planets were so important that they actually had names for them. The word "planet" in Greek is like "plane" — it means something that moves or wanders. There were only five planets they could see up to Saturn, and they associated those not only with astronomical events but with events on Earth. That's why we have their legacy in our calendar today: Sunday (Sun), Monday (Moon), Tuesday through Saturday named after Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, and Saturn respectively. They served as a clock. People don't grasp this — we didn't have a functional clock that worked under all conditions until the 1700s. Measuring time was crucial for commerce, human culture, education, planting, and harvesting.

**Andrew Huberman:** So that's why people connected the position of Jupiter to events here on Earth — it was causative in their minds.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Exactly. So when I'm not being confused for a cosmetologist due to my lovely hair and makeup, I'm usually asked "Oh, you're an astronomer — I'm a Virgo, what's going to happen to me?" I used to politely say I'm not an astrologer. Now I just lean into it: "Oh, you're going to get a letter from the IRS next week and that lump on your back — you should get that checked."

## [00:14:53] Astrology, Ophiuchus Constellation

**Andrew Huberman:** So you don't believe in astrology.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** There's no evidence for astrology. In fact, there are many randomized controlled, double-blind studies that show it's almost anti-correlated with reality. There's also a provocative problem: we believe there are 12 zodiac signs, dating back to the Persians and Babylonians. But there's actually a 13th constellation the sun passes through — Ophiuchus — that exists in a 17-day stretch in late November to early December. That should obliterate astrology as any semblance of a science because they didn't even know that constellation existed when they built the system, yet about 12% of all people share that constellation.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Twins born on the same day have radically different histories and futures — there's no predictive power to it. When I was dating my wife, we went to an astrologer for fun. She asked my sign. I said Gemini. At the end I said, "Just to confirm — Gemini is born in September?" She said no, that's Virgo. But then she said the same things would happen to me anyway. It's unfalsifiable in the language of Karl Popper — you cannot prove it wrong. It's so flexible it can accommodate any story, and that's a hallmark of non-science or anti-scientific thinking.

## [00:19:58] Pineal Gland, Time-Keeping & Stars, Seasons & Offspring

**Andrew Huberman:** The first clock — the first timekeeping mechanism — was to evaluate the position of things in the sky relative to celestial landmarks. Irrespective of astrology, I can imagine a tribe with charts showing which stars rise above a certain ridge and when. And then there's biology: the pineal gland. Most mammals use the pineal's melatonin secretion as an intrinsic clock — more melatonin in long nights, less in short nights. Many animals, birds, and reptiles have thin enough skulls that light passes directly to the pineal. Humans have a pineal embedded deep in the brain — light cannot get through the skull. We have to relay that information through the eyes to the suprachiasmatic nucleus. A pregnant woman needs to know whether days are getting longer or shorter to predict the resources available for her child's survival. Is the movement of stars detectable enough that one could know whether days were getting longer or shorter just by looking up at night?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Absolutely — not only is it correlated, but there's something even more basic: temperature. You go back nine months from a birth date, and it often maps to winter holidays when people are indoors and procreating. CVS knows this: if you buy a pregnancy test, exactly nine months later you start getting ads for diapers.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** There's also the idea that the first astronomers were women. Think about it — their monthly cycle is exactly 29.5 days, which is almost precisely the lunar cycle. They would have looked up and noticed the renewal and diminishing of the moon. Some women are keenly aware of the ovulation event, describing it as a physical sensation. Women have an extra timekeeping device that men can't relate to — their menstrual cycle — and they were probably dialed in to what that portended for the future of their children. That's an enormous biological investment.

**Andrew Huberman:** Earlier you raised whether when a child is born with respect to the seasonal cycle impacts that child.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** There are a lot of data around this. Closer to the equator it's a very different situation. There were some data showing schizophrenia was far more prevalent as you move away from the equator. There was also some interesting data about mothers who contracted influenza during a certain phase of the second trimester having a heightened probability of schizophrenic offspring — but big caveat, none of it was causal. These are multivariable situations: identical twins who share the same chorionic sac can have one schizophrenic and one not, although concordance is higher than in fraternal twins. Time of birth relative to seasons — correlating with abundance or lack of food, abundance or lack of infectious disease — these things are relevant. But we'd have to make a really big stretch to include the effects of planet Jupiter.

## [00:37:47] Brain & Prediction; Moonset, Syzygy; Telescope, Galileo

**Andrew Huberman:** One of the core functions of the human brain is that it's a prediction-making machine. It wants to make predictions on the basis of things that feel reliable. The ability for us to link A and B, rather than work through things linearly and try to disprove our own hypothesis, is much stronger than any desire to work through things systematically — unless you're trained as a scientist.

**Andrew Huberman:** You said we have two refracting telescopes in the front of our skull. Your retinas that line the back of your eyes are part of your central nervous system — literally squeezed out of your skull during the first trimester through a genetic program. This is the only portion of your brain that resides outside the cranial vault, giving humans an enormous capacity to make judgments about space and time based on what's next to what, what's far from what, and movement of things relative to stationary objects. Can you explain what you mean by two refracting telescopes?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** I've been in love with telescopes since I was about 12, when I could first afford one. It came from recognizing the limitations of the human eye. I woke up one night in 1984 to this incredibly bright light shining into my room — brighter than a streetlight. I looked outside and it was the moon near moonset, near sunrise, a full moon. Next to it was a star that looked like a piece of the moon had broken off. Those alignments are called syzygy — a great Scrabble word worth about 80 points — meaning a conjunction or alignment of astronomical objects. I wanted to know what that star was, but this was 1984 and Google was 16 years away. I had to wait for the Sunday New York Times, which had a section called "Cosmos" showing what the night sky looked like that week. I found out it was Jupiter. You can see a planet with your naked eye — with no spaceship. That was my first astronomical research.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The telescope came from eyeglasses. The best glass was made in the Netherlands — the foremost explorers and traders who could acquire the finest silicon. Both the telescope and the microscope were invented in Holland. The guy who invented the telescope never thought to look at the night sky — he only used it as a spyglass for distant objects on the horizon. That required Galileo. Galileo was the first person ever to look up with a telescope and spot objects in the solar system that had never been seen with a scientific tool. He was the first to use the scientific method — making observations with an apparatus, forming a hypothesis, iterating, and exposing the results to scientific peer review.

## [00:46:36] Light Refraction; Telescope, Eyeglasses

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Light travels at roughly 300,000 kilometers per second — in the vacuum of space. When it enters a transparent or translucent medium, it slows down. Imagine light waves as rows of soldiers marching together at an angle to the beach. The ones that encounter water first start to slow down; the others keep moving at full speed. The whole beam of soldiers gets bent. That process is called refraction. You can see it with a coin at the bottom of a fountain — where you see it is not where it actually is. Put a pencil in a clear glass of water and the same thing happens.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Eyeglasses have a fixed standard going back to the Gutenberg Bible. The Gutenberg Bible was set in print with a fixed character size used across all of Western Europe. They would put the Gutenberg Bible in front of people at a specific distance to test their visual acuity and then correct it with lenses — which is amazing since there are only about 10 copies of the Gutenberg Bible left, all in vaults worth hundreds of millions of dollars. Galileo then took those two lenses — instead of putting one on each eye — put one in front of the other, and constructed a telescope. He didn't invent the telescope, he perfected it. Just like Apple didn't invent the smartphone, they perfected it. He was the ultimate second mouse — he would always improve things and obliterate his competition.

## [00:51:36] Earth Rotation & Sun

**Andrew Huberman:** It was Copernicus who first said the Earth revolves around the sun while rotating on its axis and tilts, giving us the equinoxes?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Correct. Galileo didn't correct Copernicus — he brought evidence to the table. Copernicus gave the hypothesis. The prevailing view before that — what's known as the Ptolemaic concept — was the Earth-centered universe. But those early scientists weren't dopes: they knew there were problems with that model. Planets like Mercury would undergo retrograde motion — making a weird S-curve — that couldn't be explained if the Earth was the center. They added epicycles, little extra orbits within orbits, to account for these apparent backward motions. It was a smart but ultimately incorrect model.

## [01:02:53] Science as Safe Space; Jupiter, Galileo, Discovery, Time

**Dr. Brian Keating:** What's great about science in general is that the best science is apolitical. There's no such thing as a Democratic constellation or a Republican asteroid. Science is a safe space — a real one. We need recovery, just as you can't work out seven days a week. Where can we recover from social media, politics, and economic stress? Astronomy in particular is ideal. It lets your mind run to what you used to do at 3 a.m. in a dorm, just thinking about big questions. We need to get back to pondering the origins of life and connecting to people who existed thousands of years before us.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think Galileo, Copernicus, and others were doing the same thing — that there was a bit of healthy escapism to it?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Definitely. Galileo in particular is a somewhat tragic figure. He built the telescope and made incredible discoveries, but he was almost always broke, had illegitimate children, and eventually went blind. He was deeply religious. He was also a brilliant businessman — he went to the Venetian Doge and said, "Come up to the Piazza San Marco and I'll show you a ship three days before it arrives in your harbor." **Andrew Huberman:** What did he see when he turned the telescope to Jupiter?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** He saw four little dots — the moons of Jupiter — orbiting not around the Earth but around Jupiter. That torpedoed the entire Ptolemaic, Earth-centered cosmology. Those moons are orbiting Jupiter, not us. He had discovered falsifying evidence for the prevailing model of the universe.

## [01:10:48] Early Humans, Stonehenge, Pyramids, Measurement Standards

**Dr. Brian Keating:** If we do a fast sprint through the greats of astronomy, you'd start with the cave painters at Lascaux — 40,000 BCE — charting stars, telling their youngsters when to hunt and when to hunker down. Then the Egyptian epoch around 5,000 BCE. Stonehenge — less controversial than the pyramids. The pyramids draw people toward thinking about aliens. What do I think? Certainly people built them. The bases of the great pyramids of Giza are all multiples of a cubit — the length of the Pharaoh's forearm. Pi wasn't known to be irrational until Euclid, but the Egyptians effectively stumbled into pi by using wheel-based surveying. They just laid it out and counted rotations.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Back then, if you were the president, you were also the metric standard for all of civilization. The Pharaoh's foot, the Pharaoh's forearm. Now we have a bar of platinum for a meter, and we've recently redefined the second in terms of cesium atom oscillations — fundamental physical quantities, not artifacts. But calibration standards were always crucial for removing systematic effects in science.

## [01:15:54] Giants of Astronomy

**Dr. Brian Keating:** After the Egyptians, fast-forward to Copernicus — who had ideas but couldn't prove them, and was still wrong (the sun isn't the center of the solar system, the center of mass is inside the sun, and orbits are ellipses not circles). Then Kepler, who discovered the laws of elliptical planetary motion. Then Galileo, who brought evidence with the telescope: phases of Venus, rings of Saturn, moons of Jupiter. William Herschel mapped our galaxy; his sister Caroline Herschel was the first female scientist recognized as a Fellow of the Royal Society. Then came the photographic era, spectroscopy, and Hubble — who discovered that the Milky Way was one of countless galaxies and that the universe is expanding, using Henrietta Leavitt's relationship between Cepheid variable star brightness and their pulsation period.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** More recently: Penzias and Wilson accidentally discovered the cosmic microwave background at Bell Labs while looking at the first communications satellites. Then the Nobel Prizes kept coming — Adam Riess and Brian Schmidt for the accelerating expansion of the universe due to dark energy in 2011, and the detection of gravitational waves in 2015-2017.

## [01:23:10] Origin of Life, Scientific Method & P-Hacking; Nobel Prize, Big Bang, Inflation

**Andrew Huberman:** I've never heard a description of the origin of life that fully made sense to me. A star exploded, elements came together, at some point there was water, at some point there were critters — what am I missing?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** I think the issue is you're a scientist and you think about experimental design. The scientific method requires a control group and a variable, but you can't control the number of stars in the early universe. P-hacking — tweaking the experimental design after data are in to establish statistical significance — is a huge problem. Your colleague at Stanford, Weiro Ens, won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2021 for work on confounding variables and p-hacking. In physics, high-temperature superconductors and cold fusion had these same problems in the 1980s.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The quarry I was searching for was what caused the Big Bang — specifically, the cosmic inflation that ignited it. Alan Guth at MIT (then at SLAC) and Andrei Linde at Stanford co-developed the theory of inflation: a quantum field in four-dimensional space whose random vacuum energy fluctuations spawned an expansion of local space. The announcement of our potential detection of it at Harvard on St. Patrick's Day 2014 was called one of the greatest discoveries of all time. It would have proven inflation, predicted the existence of other universes — the multiverse — and explained the origin of the Big Bang. My first book is called "Losing the Nobel Prize" because we ultimately had to retract that discovery.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Why did I pursue it? My father abandoned me and my brother when I was seven. He was the youngest full professor at Cornell at age 26 — a mathematician of extraordinary ability. He never won the Nobel Prize. As some sons compete with their father on the football field, I said I was going to win a Nobel Prize and show him that he regretted abandoning me. That was my thought process — not the most elevated way to be, but it was the fuel.

## [01:30:20] Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation, BICEP

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The cosmic microwave background radiation is the oldest light in the universe — heat left over from the formation of the lightest elements in the periodic table. If you existed then, you could have seen it; nobody existed back then. I had the idea to build a telescope transparent to microwaves to detect a specific curling pattern of microwave polarization — gravitational waves imprinted on this light — that would be evidence of the inflationary origin of the universe.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Penzias and Wilson at Bell Labs accidentally discovered the CMB while looking at the first communications satellites. They found a background hiss hundreds of times greater than expected with no terrestrial explanation — exactly matching the temperature predicted by the Big Bang: 3 degrees Kelvin, 3 degrees above absolute zero. If the CMB's discovery won the Nobel Prize, certainly discovering why that effect happened would too.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The experiment I invented was called BICEP — Background Imager of Cosmic Extragalactic Polarization. It was also a pun on bicep curls, because the gravitational wave polarization pattern is a curling one. I built this telescope at the South Pole in Antarctica. We upgraded it — it's like iPhones, you build one and then upgrade it. I got funding from David Baltimore, then president of Caltech, through a presidential fund, and convinced my colleague Jamie Bock to help build it. We were afraid of being scooped by a billion-dollar satellite — we were just a $1 million experiment. We published our result without peer review. The tragic end of this story is that what we mistook as the imprint of inflation was actually dust from exploded stars in our own galaxy.

## [01:49:55] Antarctica, South Pole

## [01:56:49] Light & Heat Pollution, South Pole

**Dr. Brian Keating:** You're probably thinking we go to the South Pole to avoid light pollution, but it's actually heat pollution we're avoiding. We're not looking for optical light — we're looking for microwave radiation. We want somewhere cold, far from man-made RF interference and microwave communications. The South Pole is at 9,000 feet elevation, so you're above a lot of the Earth's atmosphere. And it's so cold that the humidity — all the water vapor above the South Pole condensed to liquid — would only be about a third of a millimeter. Here in Los Angeles it's about an inch. Water absorbs microwaves (that's how your microwave oven works), and we don't want a photon from the Big Bang traveling 14 billion years to get absorbed in a water molecule in the Earth's atmosphere.

**Andrew Huberman:** So space would be better?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Yes, but a satellite is 100 to 200 times more expensive. Our $10 million ground experiment at the South Pole competed with a billion-dollar satellite and bested it in sensitivity. The South Pole's infrastructure — C-130s, computer networks — is provided by the National Science Foundation. Your taxpayer dollars funded this experiment.

## [02:10:08] Moon, Size & Horizon; Visual Acuity; Rainbow or Moon Bigger?

**Andrew Huberman:** Why does the Moon look so much bigger near the horizon than overhead?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The Moon is always half a degree wide — the same apparent angular diameter as the sun. That's unique: among the 290 moons in our solar system, only our moon has the same apparent diameter from its planet as the sun does, which is why we can have exact total solar eclipses. The Moon doesn't change in size. But when it's near the horizon, the brain has reference points — buildings, trees, a 747 — to compare it to. When it's overhead, you're just looking at it with no context. You can prove to yourself it's always the same size: it's approximately the size of your pinky fingernail held at arm's length.

**Andrew Huberman:** So for visual acuity — the standard for 20/20 vision is approximately 60 cycles per degree. A hawk can resolve 120 cycles per degree, which is why it can see individual hairs on a rodent from a lamp post.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Exactly. And here's a fun question: which is bigger, the width of a rainbow (from red to violet), or the width of the Moon? The Moon is bigger — roughly half a degree. The rainbow is about the same size as the Sun, which is also about half a degree.

## [02:15:21] Sunset, Green Flash, Color Opponency

**Andrew Huberman:** People obsess over the green flash at sunset. What's the basis for it?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The green flash is real — you can photograph it. You need a perfectly clear day and a view of the ocean horizon. At the South Pole, the sun only sets once a year, and the green flash can last for hours. The Earth's atmosphere acts like a layered slab of glass. When you look straight up, you're going through a minimum of atmosphere. When you look at the horizon, you're going through an enormous amount. This creates scattering: short wavelengths (blue, violet) scatter out of the beam much more efficiently, especially by dust, smog, and atmospheric particles. The sun's light actually peaks slightly in the green — night vision glasses are green because our eyes are most sensitive to green light. At the moment of maximum scattering, when the sun crosses the horizon, you see the green wavelengths that remain after all the longer (yellow, red) wavelengths have been scattered away.

**Andrew Huberman:** There was a biological explanation involving color opponency — that you stare at the orange-red sun, then when it disappears you see a green afterimage due to opponent color processes.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** I don't think that's in disagreement. It might explain amplification. But it doesn't explain why photographic emulsions see the green flash too. The physics of atmospheric scattering is the primary mechanism. The biology of color opponency may enhance what we perceive, but it doesn't account for it entirely.

## [02:23:05] Menstrual & Lunar Cycles; Moon Movement

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there any evidence of a true relationship between the lunar cycle and the menstrual cycle?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** I don't know for certain. The dominant effect of the moon on Earth is its gravitational pull on the oceans, producing four tides a day. Galileo actually incorrectly tried to use tides to argue the Earth went around the sun — he said the Earth's rotation and revolution combined to cause a sloshing of liquid. That's completely wrong, and it's amazing how a scientist can be so brilliant in so many ways and make such a profound blunder proportionate to their greatness.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** As for the moon moving away — it recedes about one centimeter per year. There's a gravitational competition between the moon's pull and the Earth's oceans providing friction. Eventually the moon will be too far away for total solar eclipses — there will only be annular eclipses where it doesn't fully obscure the sun. When the first life formed on Earth, the moon was probably about 30 times closer than it is now.

## [02:29:51] Constellations, Asterism; Halley's & Hale-Bopp Comets

**Andrew Huberman:** When I look at constellations, I don't see how our ancient predecessors saw a bear or a hunter. Is that because they saw more stars, had wilder imaginations, or were on psychedelics?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** A couple do look like what they're named after — it depends on how you connect the dots. The Big Dipper and Little Dipper, though, are not actually constellations — they're asterisms. An asterism is a collection of stars associated together but not the full composition of a constellation. The Big Dipper is in the tail and hindquarters of Ursa Major (the Great Bear), which is the full constellation. There are exactly 88 constellations accepted by astronomers, covering the entire celestial sphere. But you can leave your own asterism — on my podcast and Andrew's you can leave five-star reviews. You can't add a constellation, but you can add an asterism.

**Andrew Huberman:** Did you catch Halley's Comet when it came by?

## [02:32:13] Navigation, Columbus

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Columbus owes his life to astronomy. In 1498 in Jamaica, he had failed to ingratiate himself with the native inhabitants, who were going to kill him. His astronomer on board knew that in two days there would be a total solar eclipse visible over Jamaica. Columbus told the natives: "If you don't release our people, my God will obscure and kill your Sun God." They refused. Then the eclipse happened. They believed Columbus was in control of celestial events and freed his crew.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Astronomy and navigation have always been intimately related. If you know where Polaris is — the North Star, which remains accurate within half a degree for the next couple thousand years — you can know your latitude. But measuring longitude requires measuring time relative to Greenwich Mean Time. That's why London became so important. The book *Longitude* by Dava Sobel is an incredible read — about the development of the first reliable clocks for navigating at sea. The longitude prize in the 1700s — won by John Harrison, who invented a mechanical clock stable enough for ships — was an early predecessor of the Nobel Prize.

## [02:36:29] Adaptive Optics, Scintillation, Artificial Stars

**Andrew Huberman:** Adaptive optics was borrowed from astronomy into ophthalmology. The basic idea — at least my understanding — is that you use the noise in the environment as part of the microscope to get a better image. In astronomy, the "noise" is atmospheric turbulence. Can you explain how that works?

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Twinkle, twinkle, little star — that's scintillation. It's a property of point sources. Stars are so far away they subtend essentially a zero-dimensional dot. When that light passes through the atmosphere, macroscopic turbulent cells — columns of air with slightly different densities — refract the light at slightly different angles. The star appears to jitter. You can identify a planet by the fact that it does not scintillate, because it subtends a slightly larger angle (a tiny disc, not a point). Jupiter doesn't twinkle.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Astronomers in the 1960s and 70s, including Claire Max at UC, realized they needed an artificial reference star — a guide star. Shoot a laser into the atmosphere: at about 30-40 kilometers up there's a layer of sodium atoms. The laser causes those sodium atoms to fluoresce, creating a point of light that looks just like a star. You know exactly how you created it and what it should look like. Then a deformable, flexible mirror — which can flex 100 times per second — compensates in real time for the atmospheric distortion by adjusting its surface by tiny amounts. This is adaptive optics. Andrea Ghez at UCLA used it with the twin 10-meter Keck telescopes in Hawaii to measure stars orbiting the black hole at the center of the Milky Way and test Einstein's theory of relativity, for which she won the Nobel Prize.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** The technology was actually classified by the US military in the 1970s and 80s because spy satellites staring down through the atmosphere have the same problem. Now it's being used in sniper scopes for long-distance shooting through atmospheric turbulence. There are also laser-acoustic technologies that can extract sound from vibrations on a distant window — and even technologies to reconstruct images around corners using scattered laser light.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's also an issue with Starlink satellites and your work.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Starlink satellites create streaks through astronomical images, though you can paint optical satellites black to eliminate that problem. But we're looking for microwave radiation — heat — and there's no way to stealth that. It's a law of thermodynamics: anything above absolute zero gives off heat. Worse, Starlink operates in exactly the microwave spectral range we use to look at the CMB. I've asked Elon to consider turning them off when they pass over our telescope at the South Pole — it's a small ask since there's no paying user base at the South Pole. It's a work in progress.

## [02:48:28] Life Outside Earth?

**Andrew Huberman:** To what extent do you think there's life outside Earth? Not necessarily aliens, but even a centipede on Mars — that would be spectacular.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** I'm kind of an outlier here. I think the probability of life elsewhere is very low, and I can substantiate that. There is zero conclusive evidence of life anywhere else in the universe, full stop. And the best part is I can't be falsified right now — no one else has found anything either.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** In 1997, the Allan Hills meteorite from Antarctica — a rock from Mars ejected by an asteroid impact millions of years ago — was announced to have evidence of microbial life. President Clinton gave a press conference on the White House lawn. That discovery was never confirmed, never refuted — it's still unresolved. Here's the logic: impacts on Mars eject material; that material can travel through space and land on Earth. Conversely, since life emerged on Earth 3.7 billion years ago, millions of tons of Earth material — possibly containing living microbes or tardigrades — have landed on Mars. If material from Earth got there with life, and yet Mars appears lifeless, that absence is a data point.

**Dr. Brian Keating:** Fermi's paradox: if life is abundant in a galaxy with hundreds of billions of planets, and the galaxy is billions of years old, where are the aliens? They should have had plenty of time to evolve, become technologically superior, and travel our galaxy. We've been broadcasting radio waves for 85 years — they should know we're here. Antarctica makes up 8% of Earth's land mass, and you'd expect 8% of Earth's life to be there. There's nothing. Possibility is not probability. The number of hurdles to create a single cell is enormous. We have yet to make a functional cell in a laboratory. Our history of life has an N of one — it's very difficult to speculate.
