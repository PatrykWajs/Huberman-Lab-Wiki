# Episode 187: How to Improve Oral Health & Its Critical Role in Brain & Body Health

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Huberman designates oral health as the seventh pillar of mental and physical health, alongside sleep, light, nutrition, exercise, stress management, and relationships, arguing that the oral cavity is inextricably linked to cardiovascular, metabolic, and brain health — including dementia risk. The episode covers tooth and mouth anatomy, how cavities form through bacterial acid production (not sugar directly), and how teeth can remineralize and self-repair provided cavities have not yet penetrated the dentin layer. Huberman synthesizes input from five dentists — including a periodontist, a pediatric dentist, and functional dentists — to build a consensus protocol stack covering brushing timing and technique, flossing, xylitol, baking soda, salt-water rinses, tongue cleaning, mouth breathing, and the case against antiseptic mouthwashes. A major theme is that mouth pH and saliva quality are the master variables: keeping the oral environment alkaline drives remineralization, supports a healthy microbiome, preserves nitric oxide production, and reduces systemic bacterial seeding into the bloodstream, gut, and brain. Practical low- and zero-cost alternatives (baking soda, high-salt rinse) are presented alongside commercial options (hydroxyapatite toothpastes, xylitol gum), with clear guidance on what to stop doing (alcohol-based mouthwashes, hydrogen peroxide rinses, hard-bristle brushes) and when to do what.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Oral health is the seventh pillar of health** — The mouth and gut are contiguous; oral microbiome dysbiosis is directly linked to cardiovascular disease, metabolic dysfunction, and dementia. Periodontal bacteria can cross the blood-brain barrier and may contribute to amyloid plaques and tangles associated with Alzheimer's. Treating oral health as cosmetic misses its systemic importance. [00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-oral-health)

2. **Teeth are always in either remineralization or demineralization mode, never both** — The state is determined almost entirely by mouth pH, which is driven by saliva quantity and mineral content. Every food, drink, or behavior that raises or lowers oral acidity shifts the entire balance. [00:20:01](transcript.md#002001-mouth-gums-saliva)

3. **Cavities are caused by bacteria, not sugar directly** — Streptococcus mutans consumes sugars (including starches from pasta, rice, oatmeal) and produces acid that demineralizes enamel. S. mutans is communicable — transmitted by kissing and shared glasses or bottles — and is not present at birth. Limiting the time the mouth spends acidic is the practical target, not eliminating all sugar. [00:28:51](transcript.md#002851-cavity-formation-bacteria-sugar-acidity)

4. **Early-stage cavities can be reversed through remineralization** — Cavities that have not yet breached the dentin layer can be filled back in by sustained remineralization protocols. Once a cavity penetrates the dentin, drilling and filling is typically required. Always ask your dentist how deep any cavity is before agreeing to a drill. [00:13:53](transcript.md#001353-teeth-biology-cavity-repair)

5. **Fluoride vs. hydroxyapatite: two valid but distinct choices** — Fluoride substitutes into enamel crystal lattices forming bonds that are super-physiologically strong but not native (visible as wavy bonds under electron microscopy). Hydroxyapatite forms natural bonds and remineralizes without systemic fluoride exposure. Choice depends on concern about thyroid and brain effects of accumulated fluoride, especially in children. Both fluoride-based and hydroxyapatite-based toothpastes are used by the dentists consulted. [00:35:10](transcript.md#003510-teeth-remineralization-fluoride-water)

6. **Alcohol, stimulants, smoking, vaping, and mouth breathing all damage oral health** — Alcohol and stimulants (including coffee, Adderall, Wellbutrin, modafinil) acidify saliva and drive demineralization. Stimulants also promote mouth breathing, which dries the oral cavity and compounds damage. Smoking, vaping, and dip tobacco disrupt the oral microbiome and are pro-cancerous to gum tissue and underlying bone. [00:43:58](transcript.md#004358-tools-the-do-nots-of-oral-health-mouth-breathing)

7. **Nighttime toothbrushing is the single most impactful oral health habit** — Saliva production drops dramatically during sleep, removing the primary defense against bacterial acid. Brushing and flossing before bed — after the last meal or acidic drink — eliminates the bacterial food supply during the body's longest undefended window. Every professional consulted agreed: if brushing only once per 24 hours, do it at night. [00:54:48](transcript.md#005448-tools-fasting--teeth-remineralization-nighttime-toothbrushing)

8. **Use a soft toothbrush with light circular pressure** — Hard or medium bristles and vigorous scrubbing disrupt the gum-tooth interface, promote gum recession, and deepen periodontal pockets. A soft brush in circular motions is sufficient to break up biofilm. Lightly brushing the gums promotes blood flow to tooth roots and may reduce sensitivity. Biofilm that is not removed hardens into plaque and then tartar, which requires professional scraping. [01:03:23](transcript.md#010323-proper-teeth-brushing-tooth-sensitivity--gums)

9. **Xylitol kills Streptococcus mutans and stimulates saliva production** — S. mutans consumes xylitol but cannot metabolize it into acid; instead the bacterium is killed. Xylitol also reduces gum inflammation and increases saliva production when chewed as gum. Dr. Ellie Phillips's Zellies products are one example; any xylitol gum or mint after meals provides the benefit. [01:14:23](transcript.md#011423-tool-xylitol-bacteria--cavity-prevention)

10. **Antiseptic and alcohol-based mouthwashes reduce nitric oxide and harm the microbiome** — These products kill beneficial bacteria, lower nitric oxide production throughout the body (impairing vasodilation in capillaries, veins, arteries, brain, and heart), and push mouth pH toward demineralization. Hydrogen peroxide rinses are also contraindicated — they can turn minor canker sores into large quarter-sized ulcers. Replace with high-salt-water swish-and-spit or microbiome-focused products (Biom, Fygg). [01:22:38](transcript.md#012238-mouthwash--alcohol-antiseptic-mouthwash)

11. **Baking soda is a valid zero-cost toothpaste and alkalizing rinse** — Low on the abrasiveness scale, alkaline, and effective at disrupting biofilm. Dentists confirmed it is a reasonable option. A high-salt-water swish-and-spit (dissolve salt until it barely dissolves; rinse, spit, follow with plain water) serves as a near-zero-cost mouthwash replacement that promotes healthy bacterial proliferation. [01:26:29](transcript.md#012629-tools-baking-soda-hydrogen-peroxide-salt-water-rinse)

12. **Gut microbiome health reduces canker sores and supports oral healing** — There is a direct gut-oral microbiome axis. Consuming 1–4 servings of low-sugar fermented foods per day (kimchi, kefir, sauerkraut, natto, miso, yogurt), adequate prebiotic fiber, and 6–8 hours of quality sleep per night reduces canker sore frequency and promotes healing. Dr. Mark Burhenne specifically champions sleep as a critical variable for oral microbiome health. [01:34:52](transcript.md#013452-tools-canker-sore-prevention--gut-microbiome-sleep)

13. **Tongue cleaning with a dedicated separate toothbrush is preferred over metal scraping** — All dental professionals consulted agree tongue cleaning is beneficial. Most people use steel scrapers with too much force and risk damaging soft tongue tissue. Lightly brushing the tongue once or twice daily with a separate toothbrush (kept separate to prevent cross-contamination) is the preferred method. [01:37:47](transcript.md#013747-tools-tongue-brushing-toothbrush-care)

14. **Mercury-containing metal fillings should not be disturbed** — Mastic gum and hard-candy chewing can liberate mercury from filling matrices, making it bioactive in the bloodstream. Leave existing fillings in place unless a dentist determines removal is necessary; removal itself is disruptive. Tooth sealants are generally considered safe by the dentists consulted. [01:41:38](transcript.md#014138-teeth-sealants-metal-fillings-mercury-mastic-gum-root-canals)

15. **Twice-yearly dental visits track baselines; they do not replace daily care** — Professional cleanings remove tartar and identify cavity depth but do not remineralize teeth. The primary value is baseline tracking and identifying genetic susceptibilities to tartar buildup or gingivitis. People with such susceptibilities may need visits every 1–2 months. [01:48:43](transcript.md#014843-dentist-visits-daily-routines--oral-health)

16. **Intermittent fasting and meal timing extend remineralization windows** — Saliva production peaks during the day and is the primary driver of remineralization. Compressing eating windows (e.g., first meal around 11am, last bite by 8pm) extends the alkaline saliva window. Rinsing with plain water after acidic drinks clears acid without requiring a full brush. Constant snacking throughout the day keeps the mouth in an acidic, demineralizing state. [00:54:48](transcript.md#005448-tools-fasting--teeth-remineralization-nighttime-toothbrushing)

## Tools & Protocols

### Nighttime Toothbrushing
- **What:** Brush teeth immediately before sleep, after the final meal or drink of the day (excluding water).
- **How:** Soft-bristle brush, light circular pressure, angled slightly toward the gumline, two minutes. Rinse with plain water first if the last meal was acidic; do not brush immediately after acidic food.
- **When:** Every night without exception — the highest-leverage oral health habit.
- **Ref:** [00:54:48](transcript.md#005448-tools-fasting--teeth-remineralization-nighttime-toothbrushing)

### Morning Toothbrushing
- **What:** Brush before eating breakfast, not after. Brushing immediately after acidic food or coffee erodes softened enamel.
- **How:** Soft brush, light circular pressure, two minutes.
- **When:** Every morning, first thing upon waking, before food.
- **Ref:** [01:03:23](transcript.md#010323-proper-teeth-brushing-tooth-sensitivity--gums)

### Flossing
- **What:** Floss once daily minimum (ideally twice) to disrupt biofilm below the gumline that brushing cannot reach.
- **How:** Glide down the side of each tooth, dip slightly below the gumline, use a gentle circular motion, then lift up. Do not snap the floss into the gum. Waterpik is an acceptable supplement — gentler than traditional floss and at least as effective. Children under six with widely spaced baby teeth do not need to floss those gaps.
- **When:** Once daily minimum, ideally before nighttime brushing so dislodged debris is brushed away. If gums bleed, floss more consistently with correct technique, not less.
- **Ref:** [01:11:02](transcript.md#011102-proper-flossing-technique-waterpik-children--flossing)

### Xylitol Gum or Mints After Meals
- **What:** Use xylitol-containing gum or mints after meals to kill S. mutans, maintain alkaline pH, and stimulate saliva production.
- **How:** One or two pieces of xylitol gum or a few mints after each meal. Introduce gradually — do not go from zero to very high daily doses. Zellies (developed by Dr. Ellie Phillips) is one example; no affiliation.
- **When:** Immediately after meals and snacks, especially when brushing is not immediately practical.
- **Ref:** [01:14:23](transcript.md#011423-tool-xylitol-bacteria--cavity-prevention)

### Toothpaste Selection
- **What:** Choose based on fluoride preference; prioritize xylitol as a sweetener ingredient.
  - **Fluoride-based:** Super-physiologically strong fluorapatite bonds; standard dental recommendation; systemic fluoride accumulation is a concern for some, especially in children.
  - **Hydroxyapatite-based:** Physiologically native bonds; remineralizes effectively; no systemic toxicity concerns; emerging well-supported option.
  - **With xylitol:** Any toothpaste listing xylitol as a sweetener is preferable to one using other sweeteners.
- **How:** Use as directed with a soft toothbrush. Toothpaste tablets (chew then brush with a wet brush) are a valid alternative format.
- **When:** Morning and nighttime brushing sessions.
- **Ref:** [01:19:43](transcript.md#011943-toothpastes-xylitol-fluoride-hydroxyapatite)

### Eliminating Antiseptic Mouthwash
- **What:** Stop using alcohol-based and antiseptic mouthwashes (Listerine and similar). Chlorhexidine-type rinses are only appropriate when specifically prescribed for bacterial infections.
- **How:** Replace with a high-salt-water swish-and-spit (dissolve salt until it barely dissolves; swish, spit, follow with a plain water rinse) or microbiome-focused products (Biom, Fygg). Do not combine baking soda with hydrogen peroxide — hydrogen peroxide is contraindicated for oral rinsing.
- **When:** Ongoing. The salt rinse can be used after meals or as a standalone mouth rinse.
- **Ref:** [01:22:38](transcript.md#012238-mouthwash--alcohol-antiseptic-mouthwash)

### Baking Soda Brushing or Rinse
- **What:** Use baking soda as a zero-cost toothbrushing agent or post-meal alkalizing rinse.
- **How:** Wet a soft toothbrush, dip into baking soda, brush lightly. Or dissolve baking soda in water and swish as a rinse.
- **When:** In place of toothpaste or as an additional rinse after acidic meals when brushing is not practical.
- **Ref:** [01:26:29](transcript.md#012629-tools-baking-soda-hydrogen-peroxide-salt-water-rinse)

### Nasal Breathing and Mouth Taping During Sleep
- **What:** Default to nasal breathing during waking hours; tape the mouth during sleep if you are a habitual mouth breather.
- **How:** Use a small strip of medical-grade paper tape across the lips at bedtime. During waking hours, consciously close the mouth and breathe through the nose except when eating, speaking, or during high-intensity exercise.
- **When:** Ongoing. Mouth breathing dries the oral cavity, acidifies saliva, and is a leading cause of tooth decay and microbiome disruption — especially critical during sleep when saliva production is already low.
- **Ref:** [00:43:58](transcript.md#004358-tools-the-do-nots-of-oral-health-mouth-breathing)

### Tongue Cleaning
- **What:** Clean the tongue once or twice daily with a dedicated separate toothbrush (not the same one used on teeth).
- **How:** Lightly brush the tongue surface from back to front. A separate toothbrush is preferred over a steel scraper because steel scrapers are commonly used with too much force, risking damage to soft tongue tissue. Keep the tongue-brushing toothbrush separate to prevent bacterial cross-contamination.
- **When:** Once or twice daily, as part of morning or nighttime routine.
- **Ref:** [01:37:47](transcript.md#013747-tools-tongue-brushing-toothbrush-care)

### Fermented Foods for Oral-Gut Microbiome Health
- **What:** Consume 1–4 servings of low-sugar fermented foods daily to support both gut and oral microbiome health, reduce canker sore frequency, and promote healing.
- **How:** Kimchi, sauerkraut, kefir, natto, low-sugar yogurt, miso. Aim for variety. Ensure adequate prebiotic fiber through fruits and vegetables. Prioritize 6–8 hours of quality sleep per night.
- **When:** Daily, incorporated into regular meals.
- **Ref:** [01:34:52](transcript.md#013452-tools-canker-sore-prevention--gut-microbiome-sleep)

### Oil Pulling (Optional / Low Evidence)
- **What:** Swishing 1–2 tablespoons of olive oil or coconut oil in the mouth for several minutes. Evidence base is limited; some licensed dental professionals consider it a reasonable near-zero-cost addition, not a replacement for core protocols.
- **How:** Swish vigorously, pulling oil between teeth. Spit into trash (not sink). Do not swallow.
- **When:** Morning, before eating, as an occasional optional addition. Huberman neither recommends nor discourages it given insufficient peer-reviewed evidence.
- **Ref:** [01:54:17](transcript.md#015417-practices-for-oral-health-oil-pulling)
