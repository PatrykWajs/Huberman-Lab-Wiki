## National Institutes of Health (NIH), Mission

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. Jay Bhattacharya, welcome. You are colleagues at Stanford, though you've now formally moved to Washington to be director of the NIH. For those not familiar, what is the essential mission of the National Institutes of Health?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Thank you for having me. I've been wanting to do this for a very long time. Let me start with the stated mission, because the stated mission is entirely worthwhile. It is to support research that advances the health and longevity of the American people. And of course, the research we do doesn't just advance American health — it advances the health of the entire world. For a very long time, the NIH has been the premier biomedical organization supporting research. It translates into almost every drug you take. The NIH has had some role in developing almost every drug, in all the work on sleep, diet, and so on. It supports the careers of a very large number of biomedical scientists around the world. I got NIH funding for most of my career. I was a scientific reviewer for grants. It's an absolutely essential organization.

**Andrew Huberman:** I agree. My lab ran on NIH money primarily. Thank you, American taxpayers. I think for most people when they hear the word health and the NIH mission statement, there is an assumption that most of the work is human clinical studies or even mouse studies testing a particular drug. But much of what NIH does is fund basic research — for which we sometimes have not the foggiest idea of a potential upside for human health.

## Funding, Basic vs. Applied Research

**Andrew Huberman:** When we step back and look at basic versus applied research, what percentage of the NIH budget is directed toward each? And when you look at that basic research, how do you justify it?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** There are big fights over exactly where that demarcation line is, so I'm not going to commit to a single number. What I will say is that a substantial part of the NIH portfolio appropriately focuses on basic science — fundamental biological facts that can be used in many drug studies and other research where you don't necessarily know in advance what the applications will be. The NIH very appropriately funds work that is not patentable, because no drug company has an incentive to do that work, and yet it's vital.

Let me give an example. Take the research that led to understanding DNA as a double helix — Watson, Crick, Rosalind Franklin, all of them in England in the 1950s. That work is not patentable. It's hard to imagine someone trying to patent the double helix structure of DNA. So there's no interest from any specific company to support those scientists. And yet it's vital to almost everything we do in biology. The NIH very appropriately funds that kind of work. It solves a market failure — there's no incentive for the private sector to do that basic work, and yet it really advances human health in ways that are sometimes unpredictable. It's correct and right that the NIH continues to fund that kind of basic science as well as applied work where you take advances and test a drug against a disease.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's an interesting dividing line where the question becomes what should be left to the private sector. The private sector tends to fund large-scale clinical trials at the tail end of the development process. Why should the taxpayer pay for that when there are already private actors willing to do it?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** That's exactly the fuzzy and controversial dividing line. You want the NIH work translated so that patients can benefit, which means the private sector has to be involved to some degree. But the line between basic and applied is the subject of almost religious conflict. Are you a basic scientist or an applied scientist? The fundamental thing — that we have to fund basic work — I believe in pretty strongly.

**Andrew Huberman:** As a basic scientist myself, I know some beautiful cases of basic research leading to cures, with no thought at the beginning that the outcome would relate to human health. My scientific great-grandparents David Hubel and Torsten Wiesel did early work defining the structure and function of the visual system in cats and then monkeys. That work made clear that children need corrective surgeries early or the brain is forever blind to a perfectly fine eyeball if the eyes aren't correctly aligned. The work of Hubel and Wiesel saved the vision of millions of children. I would also say much of the basic work on cell biology in the second half of the last century — where are the mitochondria, what's in the mitochondria — was necessary for the development of essentially every existing cancer treatment.

There is now a fear that we are potentially looking at a redirecting of significant research dollars away from basic research. Can you clarify your intentions?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** I have no intention of shifting the balance between basic and applied work. Both are tremendously important parts of the NIH portfolio. The question for me is what's scientifically important and interesting in terms of accomplishing the NIH mission — advancing the health and longevity of the American people. Both basic and applied work can contribute. Any large-scale scientific institution that seeks to support what the NIH has must have both. I do epidemiology, health policy, health economics, and statistics — that's very applied — but I have great admiration for colleagues like you who do basic science. I think it's what advances and fuels the next generation of advances. Basic science will stay part of the NIH mission as long as I'm the director.

**Andrew Huberman:** I and many others will be very relieved to hear that answer. The fear was that the new administration is going to eliminate basic research and replace it with only applied and clinical studies and that somehow it's all going to get co-opted by private interests.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** That is not the direction. I've not heard anyone inside the administration tell me to do that or suggest it as the appropriate path. Everyone I've spoken to about my vision has said yes, that makes sense.

## Indirect Costs (IDC), Policies & Distribution

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to talk about something most people are perhaps not familiar with — the notion of IDC, indirect costs. My lab ran on NIH grants for many years. If we were fortunate enough to get a grant funded, we might receive, say, a million dollars over four years — $250,000 a year. In addition to that, my home university, Stanford, would get some percentage above that for so-called indirect costs. At Stanford that's roughly 50-something percent, so another $500,000 on top. Those indirect costs pay in principle for administrative handling of the grant, mouse care, keeping the lights on, janitorial services. IDC has become a hot button issue because the new Trump administration cut the IDC rate across the board to a maximum of 15%, which was then blocked by litigation. What are your thoughts?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** There was litigation against that 15% cap which essentially blocked it, so right now rates are whatever they were before. I can't comment on the litigation as a member of the government. But let me talk about the broader issues.

In the mid-1940s, Vannevar Bush argued that the federal government should partner with universities to organize the scientific infrastructure of the United States. The indirect costs structure came out of that commitment, and it makes sense. The federal government has an appropriate role in supporting universities to do research that's in the American interest. The question is how: how much, how should it be structured?

Let me describe the current structure. You're a brilliant scientist. You get a grant of a million dollars a year. The federal government gives you money to run your lab. You work at Stanford, which has a 55% indirect rate. So the taxpayer will pay $1.5 million roughly to Stanford per year — $1 million for your work and $550,000 to cover fixed costs: the building, maintenance, radioactive disposal, biohazard removal, all of that. That's a legitimate use of that money.

Here's the economics: in order to get fixed cost support, you have to have scientists who can win NIH grants. But in order to attract scientists, you have to have the infrastructure. It's a ratchet. This concentrates federal support in a select few universities, mainly on the coasts. There are excellent scientists elsewhere who have trouble getting NIH grants even though they're brilliant, simply because their institutions lack the infrastructure that comes from the ratchet.

There's also the question of whether the same indirect rate makes sense for everyone. My research involved epidemiology, health policy, statistics — basically me with a dataset and a computer. Does the university need the same indirect cost support to maintain my fixed costs as it does for a lab doing basic science with radioactive materials? Obviously not. And yet that's the structure we currently have.

The key policy question is how should the money be distributed across universities? Are we auditing the use of indirect costs appropriately? Should universities compete on costs? These are legitimate questions for public policy debate. For the first time in about 40 years, this is now part of the public consciousness, which I think is healthy.

## Taxpayer Funding, Journal Access, Public Transparency

**Andrew Huberman:** There are actually two important issues you're touching on. Let me backtrack to make sure people understand the first one. Taxpayer dollars pay for the basic research. If there's any technology or drug brought to the public through the private sector, the taxpayers fund the R&D but don't capture any of the upside. The general public, as taxpayers, are funding a lot of this and then have to buy it back. And it's made worse: if you want to read a paper published with work your tax dollars funded, you have to buy it from the journal. That changes in July — correct?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** That's right. It's one of the decisions I made. My predecessor Monica Bertagnolli made a great decision saying that if NIH supports a scientist's work and that leads to a journal publication, that publication ought to be available free to the public immediately upon publication. You're not allowed as an NIH-funded scientist to publish in a journal that doesn't have that policy. It was due to go into effect in December of this year. I decided: why wait till December? Let's just do it in July. So starting in July, what you just described will no longer be the case — Americans and everyone will have access to the papers that Americans already paid for.

**Andrew Huberman:** On behalf of myself and every other American citizen, thank you. We've been paying for this research forever and have had to pay to get it back. Nature charges $12,000 just to publish the paper. Then they sell access back to the general public. McMillan Press, Elsevier — we're talking billions of dollars in income and the marginal cost of publishing now is effectively zero.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Absolutely. The level of investments the public has been making for NIH and then being asked to pay $30, $50, $100 for the papers themselves is just insulting. And it actually impedes the progress of science because it creates a barrier where regular people can't get access to what scientists are talking about. There's a public transparency aspect to it. Scientists ought to be engaging with the public about their ideas, not just living in an ivory tower. During the pandemic, we saw the folly of that model.

## Taxpayer Funding, Patents; Drug Costs in the USA vs Other Countries

**Andrew Huberman:** Now that people understand how the NIH works a bit — this IDC thing, the journal issue — the question comes to mind: how much of the cost of running science at a university should the public be responsible for? And how much should private actors be allowed to profit from taxpayer-funded discoveries?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Let me tie back to something: the NIH funds your work, and your work results in a product that someone else patents and makes a lot of money off of. At least indirectly, those patents are funded by American taxpayers. So the question is: how much should the American taxpayer be funding, and should there be private actors allowed to profit?

There's a law called the Bayh-Dole Act from the mid-1980s that essentially said NIH-funded work ought to be patentable. The wisdom of it was: if you allow there to be a patent on the last mile, you've created a commercial interest to take basic science advances and translate them into something that actually benefits people. Now the price will be higher while the patent is in place, but eventually the patent goes away and the thing will be available to the public at large. It's a tradeoff: for a while, products funded by American taxpayers are at higher prices than in a purely competitive market, in exchange for more rapid access to the benefits of those investments.

**Andrew Huberman:** It's a little bit like the journal situation all over again — as taxpayers, we're funding a lot of this and then have to buy it back over and over again.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Let me add another complication: the United States versus the rest of the world. Americans pay somewhere between two to ten times more for the same drug product as people in Europe. Why? When you have one country where the price is ten times more than another country, basic economics says someone should go buy from the cheap country and resell it in the expensive one, equalizing prices. And yet for decades Americans have paid two to ten times more for the same product — often made in the same manufacturing facility. This has to do partly with the way American health insurers interact with drug companies. Drug companies essentially use Americans to fund their research and development efforts. The higher prices Americans pay fund the last-mile research that drug companies do to test new products.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the United States taxpayer is funding the late-stage and most expensive research and development that drug companies do. They sell the drugs to us at a premium, and they use the difference between the real cost and the cost abroad to keep prices cheap overseas. In other words, we are paying for the safety research that covers the entire world.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Yes — through the NIH, which is the single largest investment in basic science research in the world, and also through higher drug prices relative to the rest of the world, American taxpayers are funding essentially the entire research pipeline for the world. When so understood, the current equilibrium is not sustainable.

## Reducing Medication Prices; R&D, Improving Health

**Andrew Huberman:** What is being done to bring drug prices down in the United States? When I heard about President Trump's executive order on drug prices, my immediate question was: who's going to pay for that latest-stage safety research if Americans stop subsidizing it?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** A few cuts at this. Phase 4 surveillance that happens after a drug is marketed is typically the FDA's work — American taxpayers are paying for that. Phase 3 studies to check effectiveness and safety profiles in large populations are typically paid for by drug companies — but then American taxpayers pay for that indirectly through higher drug costs.

President Trump issued an executive order saying we have to make other countries pay their fair share. The mechanisms include linking drug prices to trade negotiations, tying them to tariff policies, and allowing reimportation of drugs. The idea is: if Europe is charging essentially nothing for a drug, someone can buy it from Europe, bring it to the US, and resell it at a much cheaper price, equalizing prices. It's not that R&D won't happen — it's just that prices everywhere will be more equal so the burden of R&D is shared more equally across the developed world.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what does this mean for the future of pharmaceutical innovation? You mentioned that since 2012, there has been no increase in American life expectancy — flat through 2019, then a sharp drop during the pandemic, only recovering last year to 2019 levels. In Sweden, life expectancy dropped in 2020 and came right back up by 2021–2022 to the previous upward trend. Whatever investments we're making as a nation in research are not actually translating into meeting the NIH mission.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** We've had tremendous biomedical advances that have allowed us to treat diseases that were previously untreatable. But they have not addressed the chronic disease crisis we face or the longevity crisis. The next generation of kids is likely to live shorter, less healthy lives than we have as parents. That's an indictment on this entire industry. We focused on managing illnesses and treating illnesses — sick care rather than health care — and we're failing at it. Equalizing drug prices will actually align our NIH investment portfolio to meet the health needs of the American people. It's a long-needed corrective. That's the reason I took this job.

## Lowering IDC?, Endowments, Monetary Distribution, Scientific Groupthink

**Andrew Huberman:** Before moving on, one more question on funding and tax dollars. There is a common sentiment: why should we pay for research at universities that have large endowments? Now it's true Stanford has a very large endowment, Harvard as well, UT Austin and others. But many fine universities throughout the United States do not have extremely large endowments, and as you pointed out, there's excellent important work being done at those places. So cutting IDC to 15% across the board potentially punishes the less wealthy universities.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** You're hitting on the exact right policy question: how should the federal investment in the fixed cost of research be distributed? Right now it's distributed in a very unequal way — top universities have access to IDC money because they have scientists who can win NIH grants. It's funny: in order to get fixed cost support, you have to have scientists who are good at getting support for the marginal cost of research. That creates a ratchet that concentrates federal support in a select few universities mainly on the coasts.

The NIH has a program called IDEA — I apologize because I don't remember the acronym — but it says for research institutions in the 25 states in the bottom half of the distribution of NIH funding, it gives them a leg up in being able to get access to this federal funding. I think it's a great program. Geographic dispersion of scientific support allows richer conversations about science, allows different scientific ideas to develop. It combats scientific groupthink.

You can also imagine a system where different universities compete on costs — a university that can more inexpensively provide fully supported lab space gets more money than one where that's more expensive. That's not the current system, but it's conceivable. I think this fight over IDC is actually a great opportunity to rethink how the NIH and the federal government support the research infrastructure of the country.

**Andrew Huberman:** Before moving to public health specifically, let me just say one more thing about study sections — the peer review process where a group of scientists convene and review grants, voting on which ones get funded. The distinct advantage is that real experts are evaluating your work. The disadvantage is it lends itself to people who are very good at grant writing continuing to get money, and new ideas, ideas outside the vein of what a researcher has been doing for years, tend to be discouraged. It makes science move very slowly and incrementally. What are your thoughts on the grant review process and innovation?

## Grant Review Process, Innovation

**Jay Bhattacharya:** You've hit on a real problem. Let me contrast it with Silicon Valley: a venture capitalist invests in a portfolio of 50 projects, 49 fail, and the 50th becomes Google or Apple. The NIH review process embeds a conservatism — a desire to make sure that every grant succeeds. But how do you make every grant succeed? You fund incremental work that you know will work. We call that turning the crank.

A professor at the Salk Institute once told me there are two kinds of science: the kind where you really test a bold hypothesis and most of the time it will be wrong, but if you hit something it may be spectacular, open up an entire field, cure a disease; or there's the science that gets you funded, where you turn the crank. You look at a different protein in a pathway that is marginally interesting but predictable in terms of creating papers. Students need papers. Postdocs need papers. And so you understand why people are incentivized toward the more incremental, pedestrian science.

Before the pandemic I had been working for a decade on measuring the innovativeness of scientific portfolios. I published a paper on the eve of the pandemic asking how innovative the NIH portfolio was in particular. I took every published paper in biomedicine and extracted all words and word combinations. The unique words introduced in each year give you a history of biomedicine. You can then ask: how new are the newest ideas in any given paper? Polymerase chain reaction was a new idea in 1982–83. If the newest idea in your paper in 2020 is polymerase chain reaction, that's an idea almost 40 years old.

My colleague Mikko Packalen at the University of Waterloo and I found that NIH-funded papers published in the 1980s tended to work on ideas that were one to three years old. Papers published in the 2010s were working on ideas that were seven to eight years old. The portfolio has become far less innovative over time.

At the same time, the age at which you could win a large NIH grant — an R01 — has shifted dramatically. In the 1980s, scientists won their first R01 in their mid-30s. By the 2010s, it was the mid-40s before getting that first large grant. This matters enormously for innovation because early-career scientists are most likely to have newer ideas in their work. In fact, the data show it's monotonic: the first year after your PhD is when you're most likely to have the newest ideas, and every subsequent year, the age of the ideas you tend to work on increases by about a year.

## R01s, Tenure, Early Career Scientists & Novel Ideas

**Jay Bhattacharya:** What we do is take the careers of young scientists and effectively put them at the service of older scientists. Early-career scientists are essentially doing the work of more established scientists — you have to do postdoc one, postdoc two, postdoc three before you have any chance of getting an assistant professor job where you can test your own ideas. The labor of young scientists is devoted to the ideas of older scientists in the current system. That wasn't always true.

**Andrew Huberman:** The dirty secret of every scientist who writes an R01 is that you show them preliminary data, saying, this is what I've done and here's what I'm going to do for the next five years — but what you're really proposing is the work you already completed in the past five years. You get the money, you do the next thing.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Exactly — the shell game that every scientist learns to play, because otherwise you get it in the neck: grant speak for you're done. You can't take students or postdocs, you fire your technicians, you close your lab and become what's called dead wood. Scientists are good people. They're just trying to survive. Most are trying to get it right.

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to put in a strong vote for encouraging the early career scientist. The older labs talk about funding the next generation of science while taking most of the pie for themselves.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** This is an open door. In my Senate testimony before I became NIH director, this was a major initiative. Right now we take early-career scientists and put them at the service of older scientists. The NIH has played a role in that. It's part of why we have had more incremental progress than I would have hoped for.

One good result from my research with Mikko Packalen: teams of young scientists — young first author — paired with a mid-career or later-career scientist as senior author. That combination is most likely to try out newer ideas in their work. You need both. Keep the experienced people around. But it's very top-heavy right now.

I have former graduate students and postdocs who are now professors working on extremely interesting questions — immune system function and autism, visual repair to cure blindness. They deserve the majority of the taxpayer dollars for discovery because that's where the discoveries will lie. There is this culture in academia of people pinning awards on each other as they go up the ladder. The titles are meaningless in the end. The discoveries that young scientists make with tax dollars — that's the most important and beautiful thing that can happen.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Let me bring this back to how we evaluate science at NIH. Study sections are inherently conservative. In the 2010s, to be a standing member of a study section — a grant review panel — you had to have an active R01. Think about that: I'm a well-accomplished scientist with a large grant and now I'm sitting in judgment of young scientists pitching ideas, some of which if true might undermine my own ideas. Everything we know about cognitive bias supports that problem.

There's another aspect: if you're on study section, your grants are evaluated differently. You can even suggest names for who reviews your grant. Being on study section helps you get grants. You have to get one first in the open water. But people are starting to understand that the system isn't corrupt — it's just structured in a way that doesn't favor bold, innovative change.

## Sociology of Grant Evaluation, Careerism in Science, Failures

**Jay Bhattacharya:** There's a sociology to science, a careerism, and sometimes it can lead to good things — competition with other scientists to make the next big advance. But I think in the current way we structure incentives in biomedical science, we very often discourage sharp innovation and encourage instead incremental advances. You have a safe scientific career for the rest of your life rather than taking a big scientific risk where you might fail — but if you succeed, you cure macular degeneration or type 2 diabetes.

The key problem is that in academic biomedicine we are too intolerant of failure. If you have a big idea that doesn't work, you're essentially out. That's not true in Silicon Valley. Silicon Valley does not punish failure that sharply, and that is the key to its success. Whereas in biomedicine as structured now, we punish failure way too sharply.

**Andrew Huberman:** I never had trouble getting grants — I'm not coming to this with cynicism. I moved on to podcasting out of joy for what I'm currently doing. But I did see excellent grants get killed and some pedestrian work progress. I completely agree with this analysis. I am very relieved to hear that young investigators, young scientists, and new ideas are going to be prioritized. I think early career R01s should be bigger than late career R01s — inversely related to the size of the laboratory.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** There are lots of smart people who have given me fantastic suggestions for this problem, which I think is the most important thing I'll be dealing with — that plus the replication crisis. We have to support young scientists, punish failure less, and change the incentives so that people want to test the big thing — the thing that translates into advances for the most intractable health problems we face. If we don't do that, we'll look back and say the NIH portfolio of investments the American taxpayer made has not paid off.

## "Sick Care" System, Health Needs

**Jay Bhattacharya:** We have very little increase in life expectancy, and for many people, unfortunately a very long period of time in a state where quality of life is not good — dementia, chronic disease leading to kidney failure, macular degeneration, peripheral vascular disease, heart disease. All of these amazing biomedical advances of the last decades have not translated to actually improving health, well-being, and longevity of the American people.

The biomedical research infrastructure of the country has to translate into results for real people. Otherwise, people are going to ask why we're doing this. The key thing is: if we're not actually improving health as a result of the research we do, we haven't accomplished our mission.

**Andrew Huberman:** The former NIH director said at the end of last year that we've developed more treatments to extend the life of older people or limit their suffering somewhat — cerebrovascular disease, cardiovascular disease, dementia. But meaningful treatments for younger populations who are dying deaths of despair or whose health is in dire condition due to obesity, diabetes, and mental health issues are lacking. Young people are getting sicker earlier and staying sicker, while older people are holding on somewhat longer. Is that accurate?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Yes. That's exactly right. What we have is a sick care system. The advances we've made have allowed people to stay sick longer, but not necessarily live longer or better. There was hope when I first started research in 2001 for a compression of morbidity — you live a long life and the time you spend really sick and disabled is compressed at the very end. That hasn't panned out.

The make America healthy again movement is asking that the health institutions of this country actually meet the health needs of the people where they are. In large part we have not successfully done that for decades. It's not solved by funding one grant or making specific decisions. It's about the incentives of the system at large, so scientists turn their ingenuity toward those health needs rather than just advancing their careers incrementally.

## Incentives in Science, H-Index, Replication Crisis

**Andrew Huberman:** This is a perfect segue to the replication crisis. The independent investigator model — one professor runs one lab, comes up with ideas, gets funded, gets tenure, and pursues new ideas — has led to one of the major issues contributing to the replication crisis: it is very difficult even with the best of intentions for two laboratories to do the same work in an identical way. Five minutes longer on a countertop at room temperature might change an antibody, leading to a different outcome. Wouldn't it be beautiful if laboratories collaborated, incentivized through more plentiful funding, to try to solve important problems related to public health?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Science is a collaborative process, but the incentives within science that reward individual advance can lead to a structure that elevates careers without necessarily producing truth. Let me flesh this out. A colleague of ours at Stanford named John Ioannidis wrote a paper in 2005 titled "Why Most Published Biomedical Papers Are False." It's a brilliant, utterly convincing paper. And it's not because scientists commit fraud. It's because science is hard, exactly in the way you just said.

You publish a result, you believe it to be true, you have a statistically significant result at P = 0.05 — which means that some percentage of the time, even when you believe the result is true and it's been peer-reviewed, the published result will be false. Peer review doesn't involve reviewers rerunning your experiments. They just read your paper, looked for logical flaws, didn't find any, and recommended publication. Peer review is not a guarantee that something is true.

Negative results aren't incentivized. It's very hard to get a good paper published showing that something isn't true. No graduate student or postdoc who values their career is going to volunteer to try to disprove the hypothesis of someone famous in the field. So the published biomedical literature — that literature I've searched basically every day for 40 years — most of the time when I'm reading papers, the result, even though it says it's true, is likely not true.

I asked a very prominent neurosurgeon what percentage of information in medical school textbooks he thought was false. He said half. And when asked the implication for human health, he said incalculable.

**Andrew Huberman:** The H-index — a metric in which a scientist with an H-index of 10 has at least 10 papers with at least 10 citations each — essentially rewards volume of papers and volume of citations, but not honesty about failures or pro-social behavior like sharing data. If I replicate someone's work and find their result doesn't hold, I'm seen as a threat to them rather than as contributing to scientific progress. We've set up a system that guarantees much of what we think is true is not true.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Exactly. We reward scientists for influence and volume of papers. We don't reward scientists for honesty about their failures. We don't reward scientists for pro-social behavior — collaborating, sharing data openly. In fact, we punish scientists for that.

The solution to the replication crisis is to address those incentives — measure the pro-social things scientists could do, and recreate incentives away from simply influence and volume. In baseball, you reward a hitter for home runs but if you don't also measure strikeouts, you'll get lots of strikeouts even if you get home runs. You want a full set of statistics measuring the things you actually want scientists to do.

We've known about this for decades, but it hasn't been adequately addressed. Drug companies do private replication work before making vast investments in phase three trials — but it's private, so only they know which results in the literature are true and false. If the NIH does it publicly, the knowledge about which results are true becomes public, making the entire scientific literature more reliable as a basis not just for drug discovery but for individual health behavior as well.

## Scientists, Data Fraud, Changing Careers

**Andrew Huberman:** Why have there been so many scandals — the former Stanford president, the NIH Alzheimer's researcher who apparently had hundreds of papers with Photoshop fraud? Is this a moral failure on the part of individual scientists?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** It's not a moral failure on the part of any individual scientist. The structure of incentives we've created produces those behaviors. **Andrew Huberman:** One of the most beautiful things in science was when Linda Buck, co-recipient of the Nobel Prize with Richard Axel for the discovery of olfactory receptors, retracted papers from her laboratory. **Jay Bhattacharya:** Absolutely. The problem of fraud is existential for scientists at every level of the career ladder. A postdoc whose paper gets retracted is starting over or leaving science. The structure makes it so that we essentially reward a set of things that create incentives for the replication crisis to happen.

## NIH & Changing Incentive Structure, Replication, Pro-Social Behavior

**Jay Bhattacharya:** How is NIH going to fix this? I think you have to do three things. First, you have to make it a viable career path to engage in replication work in creative ways. Right now you can't win a large NIH grant saying you're going to do replication work, which means you won't get tenure at a top university. You need to fund replication work and lift the status of those who do it. We should also reframe "replication" — it's a kind of discovery. Asking whether the scientific literature is true is a real fundamental advance.

So: fund replication work with large grants. Give those scientists the same scientific prestige as anyone else. I'm planning to do that.

Second, you have to have a place where you can publish this work. Right now, if you send a replication result to the New England Journal of Medicine, Science, or Nature, they will not look at it at all. The NIH will stand up a journal where replication results can be published and made searchable. This is a little like community notes on X, but formal scientific papers with method sections and credentials. It creates something like the Cochrane Collaborative — that group in the UK that grades scientific evidence on health questions, producing reports with nuanced summaries. But with published replication work as the core, and with the NIH journal providing high prestige to this work.

Third, and probably most important, measure pro-social behavior by scientists. Make it part of the suite of statistics we use to measure scientific productivity. Not just publication and influence, but: Do you share your data? Is your work subject to replication? Do you cooperate with replication efforts? Do you yourself engage in replication of others' work? If you have that fuller set of statistics — one that rewards truth rather than just influence — all the incentives to commit fraud simply dissipate. Scientists can focus on the things we actually care about: learning true things about the world.

**Andrew Huberman:** Amen to all of that. I feel very blessed that I had a graduate adviser who said, why would any scientist make up data? You're trying to figure out what's true. She also said: whatever you do, don't give the reviewers what they want. You have to stay committed to what you know and observe to be true. You're closest to the data — you would know.

**Jay Bhattacharya:** The DEI shift in terms of funded science has been to try to excise from the portfolio things that are purely ideological. ## Public Trust & Science, COVID Pandemic, Lockdowns, Masks

**Andrew Huberman:** People lost trust in science and scientists, and they will not consider restoring that trust until scientists admit they made some mistakes. The words I hear are: the scientific community did us wrong. The lockdowns were unfair to working-class populations. We were told one thing about masks, then told another. **Jay Bhattacharya:** So, I was a very vocal advocate against the lockdowns, against the mask mandates, against the vaccine mandates, against the anti-scientific bent of public health throughout the pandemic. I've also argued that scientific institutions should come clean about our involvement in very dangerous research that potentially caused the pandemic — the so-called lab leak hypothesis.

## Pandemic Mandates & Economic Inequality; Fear; Public Health & Free Speech

**Jay Bhattacharya:** I was vocally opposed to school closures. I emphasized the harm that lockdowns did to the world's poor. **Andrew Huberman:** What do you think was happening? Was there fear among the academic and science community that if anyone spoke out about different aspects of lockdowns or vaccine mandates, that somehow their existence would be at risk?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** People were genuinely scared — scientists were genuinely scared for their own mortality, especially in the early days. Older people were dying more, and there are a lot of older scientists. That clouded the way they thought. The most important epidemiological fact about COVID was the very steep age gradient in mortality. Science requires free speech — if you have an idea different from mine, you should be able to express it, we can test each other's ideas out, maybe devise an experiment to decide between us, and whatever the data say, we'll accept that. Public health has a different ethical norm: uniformity of messaging, grounded in consensus science. If I were to say smoking is good for you, I would have committed a deep ethical sin because the science on smoking causing harm is rock solid. The idea that I as a public health person shouldn't say smoking is good for you has a good ethical basis rooted in science. But the idea that closing schools protects you — that wearing a cloth mask prevents you from getting COVID — that immunity after COVID recovery doesn't exist — that the vaccine will protect you from getting and spreading COVID forever — none of that was rooted in science. And yet public health authorities decided to enforce the same ethical constraints on those topics as they do on smoking. When you say none of it was rooted in science, I mean literally no science. There were a dozen randomized trials on flu before the pandemic, and a Cochrane review concluded the evidence was weak at best that cloth masking in population settings actually prevents spread of influenza.

## Vaccines, COVID Vaccines, Benefits & Harms

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think there's any reason to think the adjuvants — what the vaccines are suspended in — are potentially harmful?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** I'm personally not aware of strong evidence for it. I think these are the kind of things that ought to be investigated, but it's very difficult to investigate because of the political aura around vaccines. If you really do investigate it and find something that the public health authorities don't like, you're going to have trouble.

Let me go back to December 2020. There were several randomized trials published for the COVID mRNA vaccines. These were large-scale randomized trials: roughly 20,000 people get the vaccine, 20,000 get a placebo, and you follow them for several months. What did those studies show?

Among patients who had never before had COVID, the patients randomized to the vaccine had lower rates of getting symptomatic COVID in those two months than those randomized to placebo. The mRNA vaccines had more deaths in the treatment arm than in the placebo arm, but the sample sizes were not large enough to say this was statistically meaningful. Death rates from COVID were something like three or four out of a thousand — you would have had to enroll hundreds of thousands or millions to get a statistically significant result about deaths. The trials also didn't ask about prevention of transmission. They didn't ask whether household members of vaccinated people had COVID at lower rates. So what you could infer from the December 2020 trial data was: for two months, people who had the vaccine were much less likely to get symptomatic COVID. That's all you could say. You couldn't say they reduced death rates.

The J&J vaccine and AstraZeneca vaccine — adenovirus vector vaccines, both single-shot — when you combined their trials, you did find lower death rates in the vaccinated arm that were statistically significant. But not for the mRNA vaccines. The trials were only about two months long.

Based on that data, I wrote an op-ed in December 2020 with Sunetra Gupta arguing that this was sufficient to recommend the vaccine for older people — because they were dying from COVID at high rates and the vaccine appeared to prevent symptomatic infection, which probably prevented death through extrapolation. But I argued we shouldn't necessarily give it to young people, because the benefit-harm calculation ran in the opposite direction for them. The benefits were small — they weren't dying much from COVID — and there were unknown harms once you give it to billions of people. So I argued: recommend it for older people, then lift the lockdowns.

Instead, public health authorities decided to use the vaccine to promise disease eradication. They were implying — without saying it directly — that if 70-80-90% of the population got vaccinated, they would achieve herd immunity as if it were a permanent state. But herd immunity is a clear mathematical construct based on the fraction of the population currently immune versus susceptible. Using it as a synonym for disease eradication was wrong. And it very quickly became clear when we saw large outbreaks in Gibraltar, in Israel, in countries with high vaccination rates. The vaccine didn't stop transmission. Instead of acknowledging that fact, public health officials doubled down. By July-August 2021, the Biden administration decided to use OSHA and CMS to mandate the vaccine for populations under their control.

## COVID Vaccines, Long-Term Effects; Long COVID, Vaccine Injury, Flu Shots

**Andrew Huberman:** Mandates essentially were a widespread restriction on basic civil liberties. In Canada, you couldn't go on public transportation if you weren't vaccinated. In New York City, you couldn't go to a restaurant without a vaccine card. What evidence was there that the COVID vaccine caused any specific harm in adults?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** In young men specifically — even adults up to 35-40 years old — there was evidence of cardiac inflammation, myocarditis, post-vaccination. Some transient, but also more severe cases. That was clear evidence. Why just boys? I don't fully understand the biology of that. There's good reason to do sex-specific studies.

On long-term detrimental effects from the vaccine: it's likely that some people with particular immunological responses have had long-term issues. There's also evidence that the production process for some vaccines involved DNA plasmids that may persist. I've looked at the literature and there's a lot of controversy, and I haven't made up my mind fully on the extent of it.

What I will say is that it's very difficult to ask questions about long-term vaccine effects. The vaccine trial was terminated — the placebo arm was vaccinated in January 2021 — so you can't get long-term data from the randomized studies. You're left with observational studies that need proper control groups. It has been difficult to get the public health authorities who were supposed to do this to do it at scale.

The FDA commissioner, Marty Makary, has issued a new framework for evaluating COVID booster shots. Rather than just requiring evidence that the booster produces antibodies, now boosters have to show actual efficacy against preventing COVID and preventing deaths and hospitalizations in order to get approved. That's an evidence-based framework: if you produce antibodies but it doesn't translate to reduction in morbidity or mortality, why recommend it?

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you believe long COVID is real?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** Yes, I think it's real. I saw a study from France in 2021 looking at kids and adults who previously had COVID versus matched people who had never had COVID, measuring long COVID rates — and in kids the rates were roughly the same for both groups, but for adults it was higher for those who had had COVID. I've also met people who have had vaccine injuries — discrete injuries after vaccination. I believe them. I generally tend to believe patients about themselves, especially when they have no incentive to dissemble.

The way forward is: if you are in favor of vaccines, you should not treat this as a religious matter. Vaccines should be evaluated the same way we evaluate other drugs — evaluate the benefits, evaluate the harms in rigorous ways including randomized studies, understand patient nuances that might be right for some and wrong for others, and don't extrapolate beyond what the evidence shows or else you risk losing the trust of the public — especially the public who might most benefit. That's essentially what Bobby Kennedy is asking for, and it's essentially the policy we're implementing.

## Do Vaccines Cause Autism?; What Explains Rise in Autism

**Andrew Huberman:** Perhaps no issue is more sensitive than the vaccine-autism issue. Andrew Wakefield's data were retracted, he lost his medical license, and there was evidence of fraud. And yet my Stanford colleague Karen Parker has verified that autism prevalence has vastly increased in recent years — now one in 32 births. Parents understandably want to know why. My stance as a scientist is: if the data are robust that vaccines don't cause autism, run a proper trial and show it. What is the evidence?

**Jay Bhattacharya:** I don't want to comment on the Wakefield situation specifically because I don't know all the ins and outs. What I will say is: replication matters, and this is about one study. There are, for the MMR vaccine, some excellent studies that fail to find a correlation or causal link between MMR vaccination and autism. There's a massive Danish study that tracked kids who were vaccinated, matched with similar kids who were not, for years, and found no difference in autism rates. That's pretty good evidence. For some other vaccines there's been less of a focus on asking whether they correlate with autism, but the MMR evidence is strong.

As a general biological matter, I think vaccines are unlikely to be the main reason for the rise in autism. The honest answer about what does cause the rise is: I don't know. There are many hypotheses: alterations in the gut microbiome, retinoids, environmental chemical exposures, events in utero, nutritional issues, possible effects on cell migration in the developing cerebral cortex. I've seen a paper from Pasko Rakic's lab at Yale years ago looking at ultrasound and cell migration — never really followed up on. I'm not suggesting ultrasound causes autism, but there were interesting ideas early on that weren't explored.

Unless you understand the etiology, it's very difficult to talk about treatment. Autism has a very wide range of clinical presentations — from kids who have some social awkwardness but are otherwise well adjusted, to kids who have severe disabilities and will never live independently. The biology is very possibly different for different parts of the spectrum.

The reason we haven't had an open-minded deep investigation into the etiology of autism is: it's dangerous to ask that question. If you're a scientist, you'll be accused of being an anti-vaxxer, which is the end of your scientific career. That kind of suppression of scientific curiosity means we won't have an answer.

What I've done is organize an initiative inside the NIH to address the question of the etiology of autism — not limited to vaccines, wide-ranging. It includes basic science work, epidemiological work, environmental exposure work. We'll bring together datasets available to researchers. We'll have a competition among scientists with peer-reviewed panels. We'll have a dozen or more scientific teams asking the question. Normally it takes a year or longer to set up something like this. By September, we'll have an open competition for these scientific projects. And I'm hoping within a relatively short period of time, we'll have a much better understanding of the etiology of autism than we do now.
