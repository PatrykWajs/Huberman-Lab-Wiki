## [00:13:44] Depression Causes, Molecule Model?, Neuroplasticity?; Metabolism

## [00:22:20] Mitochondrial Functions, Stress Response, Mental Health

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** Most people know mitochondria as the "powerhouse of the cell." These tiny little organelles take the breakdown products of the food that we're eating, they use the oxygen that we're breathing in, they create the carbon dioxide that we're breathing out, and they turn food into ATP — the energy currency of the cell. That is what the "powerhouse of the cell" refers to. There is no doubt they do that. When that process stops, humans have about six minutes and then we're dead.

But research over the last 25 years has completely upended that simplistic notion of what mitochondria are doing. They are actually doing so much more. Some people have created the reference that mitochondria are like the workers inside a cell — in order for a cell to work, you need a workforce, because there's so much that needs to be done. Mitochondria are absolutely providing the energy for all of those things to happen, but they're also orchestrating a lot of it.

For example, they play a direct role in converting food into some of the substrates for the production of neurotransmitters. They store neurotransmitters like GABA within themselves. They actually go to the cell membrane and move along the membrane dispensing vesicles of neurotransmitters. When you take the mitochondria away from the synapse but provide that synapse with ATP, vesicles don't get released — the mitochondria are doing something more than just providing energy.

They play a role in turning inflammation and immune cell activation both on and off. They help start that process and they also help coordinate its cessation. They play an instrumental role in both the first and last step in the synthesis of cortisol, and they play a role in the first step in the synthesis of all of the steroid hormones — which include estrogen, testosterone, and progesterone. So if you have dysregulation of cortisol or testosterone or estrogen or progesterone, you must understand the role of mitochondria in that dysregulation.

They are the primary regulator of epigenetics. The cell signals that affect gene expression — levels of reactive oxygen species, calcium, and others — are mostly originating within mitochondria. During the development of any cell, mitochondria actually line up in an organized fashion around the cell nucleus and take on different conformations, somehow sending signals that result in the expression or suppression of different genes.

They've been implicated in all of the phases of the human stress response to psychological stress — cortisol release, noradrenaline release, inflammation, and epigenetic changes. Researchers actually manipulated mitochondrial genes — two genes in the cell nucleus that control mitochondrial proteins and two genes in mitochondria themselves — and by manipulating these four different genes one at a time in mice, they could impact all four aspects of the stress response.

So mitochondria are somehow involved in regulating the human stress response. There are hundreds, sometimes thousands of them in our cells — most neurons have thousands of mitochondria. They move around using the cytoskeleton, they fuse with each other, they change shape — this is called mitochondrial dynamics — and that impacts all of these signaling processes. You can think of one cell as almost a village of mitochondria, all doing different things and working together to help that cell function.

## [00:39:38] Stimulants, Mitochondria, Dopamine; Alcohol

**Andrew Huberman:** I've been very interested in this. Musicians who did a lot of stimulants — cocaine and amphetamine in particular — in the 70s, 80s, and 90s seemed to drop dead of heart attacks later when they were not currently using. Do stimulants like cocaine and amphetamine deplete cells of mitochondria?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** We do know, and it's a slightly more complex story — it's all about dose. Stimulants are used to treat ADHD, and we know that people with ADHD have glucose hypometabolism in their brains — their brains are not producing enough ATP from glucose. Dopamine will increase that. So you can give people stimulants to improve their brain metabolism enough so they no longer have symptoms. That story is unequivocally true. Appropriate doses — usually low doses — can improve brain metabolism by stimulating mitochondria to produce more ATP.

However, when you overdo it, when you hyperstimulate mitochondria with high doses of stimulants, the mitochondria are essentially running on all cylinders and electrons start leaking out of the electron transport chain. That creates reactive oxygen species, which then damage the mitochondria themselves and other aspects of the cell — leading to chronic mitochondrial dysfunction and chronic metabolic dysfunction.

It's challenging with stimulants because people are looking for a yes-or-no answer. It depends on the person, the dose, the type of stimulant, and what impact it's having on that person. But we need to open our minds to the possibility that yes, some people do benefit from stimulants, and at the same time other people are harmed in catastrophic ways.

What are some others beyond hyperstimulants? Alcohol. We've known since the 1960s that researchers figured out how alcohol causes cirrhosis — it's mitochondrial toxicity in the liver cells. Alcohol gets converted into acetaldehyde, which is very toxic to lots of cell parts but in particular to mitochondria. There were over 10,000 published research articles on alcohol and mitochondria. The two organs most affected are the liver, because that's the primary organ trying to detoxify it, and the brain — because the brain is highly sensitive to metabolic disruption.

## [00:52:23] Children, Energy & Metabolic Function; Diseases of Aging & Mental Disorders

**Andrew Huberman:** Years ago, my postdoc adviser, the late Ben Barres — incredible scientist, MD, who really popularized the study of glia — used to stop us in the hallways and ask, "Why is it that as we get older we have less energy?" It always seems to circle back to mitochondria.

The diseases of aging are all of the metabolic disorders — and the mental disorders. We need to tie that together. The only way to do that is to look at the bigger picture that we call metabolism, and ultimately to look at mitochondria and mitochondrial biology.

## [01:19:14] Ketogenic Diet, Epilepsy, Schizophrenia, Bipolar

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** The ketogenic diet is a 100-year-old evidence-based treatment for epilepsy — it can stop seizures even when medications fail. We have over a dozen controlled trials of ketogenic diets in children with treatment-resistant epilepsy, and two Cochrane reviews that came out positive. Cochrane reviews are the gold standard in the medical field for meta-analyses. I've heard from probably thousands of people around the world since my work has become more public. The most common thing I hear: "You saved my life." I never met these people — all I did was share this knowledge, and then they saved their own lives with that knowledge.

## [01:22:52] Ketogenic Diet, Fasting & Mitochondria; Gut Microbiome, Brain Metabolism

**Andrew Huberman:** Does the ketogenic diet impact mitochondrial health?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** We have strong evidence that it does. The ketogenic diet mimics the fasting state — it is shifting metabolism, shifting countless signaling pathways, gene expression, in the human body and brain, resulting in effects that are highly beneficial.

Animal data strongly supports that ketogenic interventions improve mitophagy — getting rid of old and defective mitochondria, cleaning house, and replacing them with new fresh ones — and mitochondrial biogenesis. At the end of the day, the cell will have more healthy mitochondria.

Some researchers have focused on the ketogenic diet working through the gut microbiome. Researchers took feces from human children with epilepsy before and after starting a ketogenic diet, then transferred these samples to mice predisposed to seizures. The feces from seizing children made the mice more likely to seize; the feces from children on the ketogenic diet made the mice less likely to seize — even though the mice were not on ketogenic diets. So something in the feces had an anti-seizure effect.

Another research group did the same model and dissected the brains, looking for what changed — how exactly is the ketogenic diet having its effect? All the genetic changes centered on mitochondria. The changes in the gut were resulting in mitochondrial changes in the brain — brain energy metabolism. It's not gut microbiome or mitochondria — it's both. It's all interconnected.

A pilot trial by Ian Campbell and colleagues in the UK — 20 patients with bipolar disorder put on ketogenic diets — found wide-ranging improvements in metabolic health biomarkers and a reduction in brain glutamate activity, which is often associated with bipolar disorder and hyperexcitability. It's not metabolic or glutamate — it's both.

## [01:46:55] Creatine & Mitochondrial Health

**Andrew Huberman:** Could we talk about some of the other things we can do for mitochondrial health — specifically creatine?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** Creatine is a molecule that is foundational to energy transformation in cells. Creatine goes into the mitochondria and gets combined with ATP to become phosphocreatine. It then leaves the mitochondrion and goes to places in the cell where energy is needed — like a synapse or a ribosome — and it can combine with ADP to be converted back into ATP. It's basically a phosphate shuttle, foundational to energy metabolism.

Our bodies can produce creatine on their own, as long as we have the essential vitamins and nutrients, or we can consume creatine from animal-based products. People who consume fewer animal-sourced foods — vegans and vegetarians — on average tend to have lower levels of creatine in their brain and muscles.

People with neuropsychiatric disorders — schizophrenia, Alzheimer's disease, depression — have been found to have lower levels of creatine than people who do not have those disorders. So there's an association between low levels of creatine and brain disorders.

We do have randomized controlled trial data showing creatine can improve symptoms of major depression, augment antidepressants, improve symptoms of bipolar disorder, and improve cognition in Alzheimer's disease or mild cognitive impairment. But the studies are on the smaller side — nobody's going to fund a large creatine trial because creatine is off patent. Unless the government steps up, the skeptics will always say we don't have good quality data.

Do I recommend it to my patients? Not right away. But it certainly lines up with a metabolic-mitochondrial model of brain health. And before you focus on what to take, focus on what you should be doing with your lifestyle — diet, sleep, bright light in the morning, relationships, purpose — because if you're not doing those things, creatine doesn't stand a chance in hell of helping your health.

## [01:52:34] Methylene Blue & Mitochondria; Serotonin Syndrome

**Andrew Huberman:** I first learned about methylene blue from owning fish tanks. Now there's a lot of interest — ever since a video of Robert F. Kennedy Jr. putting methylene blue in his water on a plane went viral. What are your thoughts on it? What does it do? Is it going to help mitochondria?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** Methylene blue has been around for a long time. It's relatively cheap. Fascinatingly, it is primarily a mitochondrial agent. It is an electron acceptor and donor — an electron shuttle. During ATP production, electrons flow down the electron transport chain. When electrons leak out of that system, they create reactive oxygen species, which are harmful to both mitochondria and cells. Dysfunctional mitochondria produce more reactive oxygen species than they should — what's often called oxidative stress. We have decades of evidence strongly supporting that aging, neurodegeneration, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and a wide range of neuropsychiatric disorders are associated with increased reactive oxygen species.

Methylene blue can come in and take some of these wayward electrons and prevent them from creating reactive oxygen species — which might help calm things down. One of the challenges is that you can have too little and too much methylene blue. Too much and it can become a reductive stressor — the polar opposite of oxidative stress — and that has also been found in schizophrenia and bipolar disorder.

We do have pilot trials — small, not super well done — in a wide range of neuropsychiatric disorders: depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, Alzheimer's disease. Many of them suggest a benefit.

**Andrew Huberman:** Methylene blue has some MAO activity, which is why I haven't taken it — I don't want to boost serotonin. There's a recent study out of Stanford showing that serotonin drops as dopamine goes up, and that drop in serotonin is at least as important as the increase in dopamine for the reinforcing properties of certain behaviors and substances.

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** That's actually one of the warnings with methylene blue — if taken in too high a dose, you can get serotonin syndrome. Serotonin syndrome is just really excessive levels of serotonin in the system. It more often than not gets completely unrecognized — it gets mistaken for psychiatric symptoms. People can have anxiety, panic, nausea, fevers. In extreme cases it can be fatal. More often than not it gets dismissed because serotonin syndrome is most commonly experienced by people already taking SSRIs — who, by definition, are psychiatric patients — and when they come in complaining of symptoms, they get written off or given more medication.

One woman reached out to me who, after reading my book, tapered off all her meds with her psychiatrist, and it became clear that she had probably had serotonin syndrome for a long time. The meds were causing the problem, and nobody recognized it.

So if people want to consider using methylene blue: get a very reputable source, consume it in a way that makes sense, and be aware that IV infusions exist — it is a medically approved treatment — but done at too high a dose it can cause serotonin syndrome.

## [02:11:14] Vitamin Deficiencies, Iron Deficiency

**Andrew Huberman:** A few years ago I went to a McKnight meeting where somebody presented proteomics data — sequencing spinal fluid from depressed patients and finding that certain depressed patients had deficiencies in certain vitamins that could be replaced through supplementation, and their depressions went into remission. ## [02:16:06] Vitamin B12 & Folate Deficiency, Autoimmune Disorders

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** Low levels of B12 are associated with all sorts of neuropsychiatric disorders. B12 deficiency is very common among vegetarians and vegans. In India, where most people are vegetarian or vegan, 50% of the population is B12 deficient. Food sources of B12 are primarily animal-sourced foods — red meat, eggs, other meats. If you're going to be vegan or vegetarian, please take an appropriate supplement and get your levels measured at least once a year.

B12 absorption is impacted by other medications. Oral contraceptives can reduce absorption of B12. Metformin can impair absorption of B12. Picture a woman who's overweight, following a vegetarian or vegan diet, also on Metformin for her diabetes, also on birth control — she's at very high risk of being B12 deficient. B12 deficiency has been associated with higher rates of depression, anxiety, psychosis, and bipolar symptoms. People can be hallucinating and delusional from B12 deficiency. The challenge is that the neurological damage can become permanent if not recognized early enough.

We've long known that there's an autoimmune form of B12 deficiency called pernicious anemia, in which antibodies to intrinsic factor prevent B12 from being absorbed in the digestive tract. That autoimmune disorder increases with age — you could have been living a healthy life and then at age 65 develop pernicious anemia, can no longer absorb B12, and this is actually one of the treatable forms of dementia. The cardinal symptoms look like dementia. The treatment is B12 injections — taking a pill won't work because you can't absorb it.

More recently, researchers have identified a brand-new form of autoimmune B12 deficiency that targets a protein called CD320, which transports vitamin B12 across the blood-brain barrier. The peripheral B12 levels are normal — the person may be consuming enough B12 and it may be getting absorbed fine — but when researchers tested the cerebrospinal fluid, they had almost no B12 in their central nervous system. That requires a spinal tap to diagnose. They treated some of these people with immunosuppressive agents and high-dose vitamin B12, and some had reversal of neuropsychiatric symptoms.

How common is this antibody? In a general healthy control group, about 6% of people have it. In people with neuropsychiatric lupus — those who already have an autoimmune disorder and have neuropsychiatric symptoms — 20% had this antibody. In people with a demyelinating condition of unknown etiology, about 50% had this antibody. The research is still preliminary, but this is another treatable cause of psychiatric symptoms.

## [02:24:48] Mental Illness & Root Causes

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** For the majority of people — 60 to 80% of people who are suffering from neuropsychiatric disorders — I actually think using interventions that are easily available today will help them heal and recover. ## [02:29:02] Vaccines, Inflammation, Mitochondria, Autism

**Andrew Huberman:** I've become very interested in vaccine biology and the debate about vaccines and the adjuvants used to deliver them. Without being provocative, is there any evidence that vaccines or the adjuvants for vaccines can disrupt mitochondrial function? And given that some people are convinced that vaccines are tied to mental health challenges or to autism — is there any evidence that vaccines of any kind can disrupt mitochondrial function?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** It's contentious, but I'm definitely going to answer it — and I'm going to give a long-winded answer because I want to give credible information, and I don't want to come down on one extreme or the other.

First, let me ask a slightly different question: is there any evidence that high levels of inflammation impair mitochondrial function? The answer is unequivocally yes. High levels of inflammation — inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha, interleukin-6, and others — impair mitochondrial function. We know that clearly and unequivocally. When you have the flu, do you have neuropsychiatric symptoms? Yes. You're going to feel exhausted, you're going to be less risk-taking, you're going to completely lose your libido, you're going to want to hide in bed. Those are all effects on your mood, your motivation, rewarding behaviors. The inflammation is doing that. We know that interferon itself will produce all of these neuropsychiatric symptoms, and interferon interferes with mitochondrial function.

Now, is there any evidence that inflammation can lead to autism? We have decades of evidence for this. Over the course of the last century, as there were outbreaks of bacterial or viral infections in the population, we saw higher rates of neuropsychiatric and neurodevelopmental disorders in the offspring of pregnant women. Flu in pregnant mothers at the first-to-second trimester transition is correlated with statistically higher incidence of schizophrenia in the offspring. A rubella outbreak in the 1960s resulted in much higher rates of autism in the offspring. Animal models: when you inject pregnant mice with lipopolysaccharide, which causes an inflammatory reaction, the offspring are at much higher risk for showing signs of a neurodevelopmental disorder.

Now, back to your question: do vaccines increase inflammation? I think the answer is yes. Is there variation in the inflammatory response between different people? I think yes. Can some people have a hyper-exaggerated inflammatory response to a vaccine? I think yes. And in that rare condition, could that impact neurodevelopment? The science right now says yes.

There is one case of a young child who already had an existing mitochondrial disorder — it was already known — who got vaccines, and within days or weeks developed profound neurodevelopmental symptoms. That case won a lawsuit in court, and the court ruled that the vaccine did in fact contribute to this girl's neurodevelopmental condition. It was assumed to be because she had a pre-existing mitochondrial disorder. That lines up perfectly with what I've been talking about — people who have vulnerabilities with mitochondria or metabolism can only absorb so many hits.

Now, the bigger question — should people get vaccinated or not? We do have reasonably good evidence that unvaccinated people are more likely to develop autism than vaccinated people. The biggest critique I have of that research is that it's a retrospective cohort epidemiological study, and the researchers decide what they control for. But the existing research as published suggests that if you don't get a vaccine, you're more likely to develop autism than if you do. And I understand that through the lens of: infections themselves can cause neurodevelopmental disorders. So if a child gets measles, they're not only at risk of dying of measles — they're also at risk of impacting their mitochondrial function and developing a neurodevelopmental disorder as a result.

## [02:39:17] Neurodevelopmental Disorder Onset & Follow-Up

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a risk-benefit analysis here? And can we do anything to bolster the health of the child going into a vaccine?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** There is a risk-benefit analysis. It's hard to predict who's going to have a big inflammatory response and who's not, although I'm thinking about lifestyle factors — proper sleep, proper nutrition, everything you can do to support that child's health going into a vaccine. What most parents really want is a sense of control over what are inevitably a mixture of controllable and uncontrollable factors. The science that I just laid out is true — we have decades of research to support all of what I've just said. I think if you understand the biology, when a child begins to show symptoms of a neurodevelopmental disorder, instead of assigning a label — "this is autism, it's a life sentence, good luck" — we should be intervening. As soon as a child begins to show signs or symptoms, especially when it's an abrupt change — they were developing in a neurotypical way and then all of a sudden something happened — we should be doing a full workup: looking for vitamin and nutrient deficiencies, looking for central B12 deficiency. Right now the state of the field is that we assign a label, autism, and tell the parents to prepare for a disabled child for life. We can do some ABA training, try to teach them some social skills. The real outcome data on that is pretty bad. We have a lot of work to do.

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** More data is always great. There's this interagency coordinating committee on autism within NIH that's existed for decades. ## [02:51:23] Father Obesity & Autism; Poor Metabolic Health, Blood Biomarkers

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the father?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** Men with obesity are twice as likely to have an autistic child as men who are not obese. Which is 25 times greater than the increase due to age of the male — so the effect of paternal obesity is much larger than the effect of paternal age.

A lot of people are hyperfocused on vaccines causing autism, pointing to skyrocketing rates of autism. Well, I agree — rates of autism are skyrocketing, and there does have to be a reason. Maybe we're missing the elephant in the room: rates of metabolic poor health are skyrocketing. Metabolic syndrome has five biomarkers — abdominal obesity, blood pressure, high glucose, high triglycerides, and low HDL cholesterol. Only 7% of Americans are healthy in all five biomarkers.

Poor metabolic health influences neurodevelopment in offspring. We see skyrocketing rates of autism — and it's not just autism, we see skyrocketing rates of ADHD as well. As the metabolic health of the United States population declines, we are going to see more neurodevelopmental disorders. It's a critical message but also reassuring, because what you're talking about is something we can do something about: metabolic dysfunction, mitochondrial dysfunction of the parents.

## [02:56:44] Assessing Metabolic Health & Biomarkers; National Institutes of Health (NIH)

**Andrew Huberman:** Is it possible to create a metric of all-around metabolic health that would be incentivized, so that people can live better lives, their offspring can be healthier, and we can potentially avoid millions and millions of people having these so-called incurable mental health disorders?

**Dr. Chris Palmer:** There are several research groups working on exactly that. One commercial company has a product — a series of blood biomarkers that they believe represent mitochondrial dysfunction — and they believe their test might be able to predict the development of autism: every child at age one should get this blood biomarker test to tell us who's at high risk of developing a neurodevelopmental disorder.

One of the leading mitochondrial researchers in the world has a set of 20 biomarkers that he believes represent mitochondrial dysfunction broadly. And then there's another research group that has narrowed it down to just five biomarkers in men and five in women — with some overlap but slight differences — and those five biomarkers alone were able to distinguish people with chronic severe suicidal depression from healthy controls with over 90% sensitivity and specificity. To find those five biomarkers, I think they measured 400 different biomarkers.

Right now, the blood test for mitochondrial health doesn't exist — there isn't one. That's the answer. There are lots of different biomarkers that can suggest dysregulation of metabolism and mitochondrial function, but no single comprehensive test. These research groups are all working on it.

I don't think we necessarily need incentives right now. I think the majority of human beings authentically want to be healthy, and they will do anything to have healthy children. If we develop evidence-based tools that prospective parents can use to assess their own metabolic and mitochondrial health, and pair that with evidence-based strategies to help them improve it — that's the Holy Grail. It's probably Decades of research. The sooner we get started, the better.
