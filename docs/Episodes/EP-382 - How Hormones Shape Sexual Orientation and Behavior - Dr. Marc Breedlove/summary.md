# Episode 382: How Hormones Shape Sexual Orientation & Behavior - Dr. Marc Breedlove

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Marc Breedlove, professor of neuroscience at Michigan State University, joins Andrew Huberman to explore the biological underpinnings of sexual orientation across five converging lines of evidence: prenatal testosterone effects on finger-length ratios and partner preference, hypothalamic brain-structure differences between gay and straight men (the INAH-3/LeVay finding), animal models including gay rams with measurable preoptic-area differences, intersex conditions (CAH and AIS) that dissociate chromosomal sex from prenatal hormone exposure, and the fraternal birth order effect explained by the maternal immunization hypothesis. Throughout, Breedlove draws a rigorous distinction between group-level statistical findings and individual predictive power, emphasizes that no credible social correlates of sexual orientation have survived scientific scrutiny, and proposes that orientation involves not only attraction circuits but also an active aversion pathway — particularly in males — that may have its own distinct neurobiological substrate.

## Key Takeaways

- **Sexual orientation is not a choice.** First crushes typically occur before puberty — Huberman's was at age 6, Breedlove's at 6–7 — well before any hormonal or social "choice" is possible. Breedlove uses a language analogy: he speaks English not because he chose it, but because of social influences he was never aware of. Orientation may work similarly — biologically shaped well before any conscious decision is possible. [00:03:24](transcript.md#0003-hormones--sexual-orientation)

- **The 2D:4D finger-length ratio is a proxy for prenatal testosterone exposure.** Men tend to have a lower index-to-ring-finger ratio than women; this sex difference is present in 9-year-old children (Manning 1999) and replicates in mice (Wendy Brown and Breedlove confirmed it; androgen receptor knockouts abolish the mouse sex difference — because there is more androgen receptor in the growing bones of the 4th digit, so it grows relatively longer under testosterone). Breedlove's 2000 study — conducted at San Francisco and Oakland street fairs using $1 lottery scratcher tickets as incentives — found lesbians had more masculine right-hand digit ratios than straight women, while gay men showed no significant difference from straight men. A third meta-analysis by Puts, Swift-Gallant, and Breedlove confirms the lesbian effect robustly. [00:07:37](transcript.md#0007-prenatal-testosterone-finger-ratio-men--women-differences)

- **Gay men differ from straight men not in how much prenatal testosterone they received, but in how their brains responded to it.** The largest behavioral sex differences — interest in multiple partners, younger partners, visual pornography, and casual sex — show gay men are fully masculine on those axes, making an "underandrogenization" hypothesis untenable. The gay-versus-straight difference in males must therefore lie downstream at the level of brain response, not testosterone exposure per se. [00:16:46](transcript.md#0016-finger-ratios-prenatal-testosterone-gay--straight-menwomen)

- **Simon LeVay's INAH-3 finding:** A hypothalamic nucleus (INAH-3 — the interstitial nucleus of the anterior hypothalamus subregion 3, also the sexually dimorphic nucleus of the preoptic area) is larger in straight men than in gay men, with gay men's nuclei not significantly different from women's. Published in *Science* in 1991. Independently replicated by William Byne despite initial skepticism; Byne's study took longer because AIDS mortality had declined by the time he began recruiting. Causality remains unknown — smaller INAH-3 may cause homosexuality or may result from it, since adult brain structures remain plastic. The nucleus is about the size of a grain of sand. Brad Cook separately showed the medial amygdala sex difference disappears within weeks when testosterone is removed in males, illustrating that adult hypothalamic structures are androgen-dependent. [00:26:54](transcript.md#0026-brain-differences--sexual-orientation)

- **Group-vs-individual distinction is the hardest thing scientists communicate.** The 2D:4D sex difference is about 0.5 standard deviations (vs. ~2 SD for height). Even with height — the most obvious human sex difference — guessing sex from height alone is wrong ~20% of the time. Digit ratios have essentially zero individual predictive value for sexual orientation because other variables also influence them. Breedlove's teaching joke: look at someone's right hand and guess straight — you'll be right 95% of the time, regardless of what you see. [00:33:52](transcript.md#0033-group-vs-individual-differences-height-analogy-bisexuality)

- **The adult brain remains androgen-sensitive and plastic — including the hypothalamus.** The neocortex is more plastic than the hypothalamus but the difference is relative, not absolute. Anabolic steroid users (particularly trenbolone at supraphysiological doses) anecdotally report hypersexuality and reduced partner-sex specificity, reversible on cessation — suggesting adult hypothalamic androgen sensitivity. Julian Davidson's double-blind Stanford trials in hypogonadal men (including elderly prostate-cancer patients) confirmed testosterone replacement reliably restored libido and well-being; subjects always knew when they were on testosterone because they felt so much better. [01:41:32](transcript.md#0141-anabolic-steroids-hypersexuality-adult-brain-plasticity)

- **Gay rams provide compelling animal evidence for an active aversion component.** Chuck Rosselli (OHSU) documented rams that exclusively mount males even when confined with a dozen receptive females for 12 hours — strongly implying active aversion, not merely altered preference. Dissection revealed preoptic-area testosterone-processing differences between gay and straight rams matching LeVay's human pattern. A company now harvests and sells wool from identified gay rams rather than sending them to slaughter. [00:51:37](transcript.md#0051-gay-rams-brain-differences)

- **A proposed aversive pathway in males.** Breedlove hypothesizes a specific neural pathway — peptides and neurons in the preoptic area — that may suppress same-sex desire in heterosexual males; no equivalent robust circuit may exist in females. This would explain why women are statistically more open to same-sex interaction, why societal acceptance of homosexuality spread faster among women than men, and why gay rams never mount females even when no other option is available. Cultures and social standards can amplify or dampen this circuit's expression, but the underlying biology is the substrate. [00:58:00](transcript.md#0058-aversion-pathway-men-vs-women-same-sex-partner)

- **Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH):** XX individuals with CAH have a dysfunctional adrenal steroid synthesis pathway; the pituitary drives adrenal hyperplasia trying to compensate, but instead of cortisol the gland produces testosterone and other androgens prenatally, masculinizing genitalia. They are more likely to identify as lesbian than the general population, and that proportion rises with age — consistent with the prenatal testosterone model and with social destigmatization allowing belated acknowledgment of existing attraction. Modern standard of care has shifted from non-consensual infant cosmetic surgery (driven by activists like Cheryl Chase) toward a wait-and-see approach. Heterozygous CAH carriers constitute roughly 1 in 12 people; generally asymptomatic, they may have subtly elevated androgen output and stress-tolerance advantages — a heterozygote advantage analogous to sickle cell trait conferring malaria resistance. [01:06:58](transcript.md#0106-congenital-adrenal-hyperplasia-cah-intersex-phenotypes)

- **Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (AIS):** XY individuals with a non-functional androgen receptor (the AR gene is X-linked) develop entirely female-appearing bodies — no Wolffian duct masculinization, no uterus (anti-Müllerian hormone still suppresses Müllerian ducts), shortened vagina — and are raised as girls. The vast majority grow up straight and highly feminine-identifying. They cannot be used to isolate prenatal testosterone's effect on orientation because their brains also could not respond to that testosterone, leaving biological and social explanations irresolvable. [01:13:55](transcript.md#0113-androgen-insensitivity-syndrome-ais)

- **Older brothers statistically increase the probability that a younger brother will be gay** — by roughly one-third per additional biological older brother (baseline ~2% → ~2.6% with one → ~3.5% with two; you need ~12 older brothers for a 50/50 chance). In Breedlove's street-fair survey data and Blanchard's Kinsey Institute archival analysis, gay men showed 140 older brothers per 100 older sisters vs. 105:100 in straight men. The effect is linear, applies only to biological brothers from the same mother, disappears for stepbrothers (Bogaert) and is unaffected by whether brothers were raised together — ruling out social mediation. Older or younger sisters and younger brothers have no effect. Ray Blanchard estimates ~1 in 7 gay men owe their orientation to this mechanism. [01:19:25](transcript.md#0119-gay-men--older-brothers-maternal-immunization-hypothesis)

- **The Maternal Immunization Hypothesis** (Blanchard & Bogaert): Each male pregnancy exposes the mother's immune system to Y-chromosome-specific antigens at delivery. Her immune system generates antibodies — including anti-NLGN4Y (Neuroligin 4Y, a synapse-formation protein encoded exclusively on the Y chromosome) — that cross the placenta in subsequent pregnancies and may alter fetal brain development toward same-sex orientation. Mothers of gay men with older brothers show elevated NLGN4Y antibody titers versus controls. Whatever the mechanism, it is the mother's body that "remembers" prior male pregnancies — not the social environment. [01:19:25](transcript.md#0119-gay-men--older-brothers-maternal-immunization-hypothesis)

- **Bird gynandromorphs reveal cell-autonomous sex differentiation distinct from the mammalian model.** Gynandromorph cardinals and zebra finches are mosaic — one side genetically male (ZZ), the other female (ZW). In mammals, gonadal hormones would masculinize both sides; in birds, the split is clean: the HVC nucleus (a song-control region) is larger on the male side of gynandromorphs. Art Arnold showed that bird brain cells determine their own sex differentiation locally by controlling how much testosterone and estrogen they produce, independent of circulating gonadal hormones. This cell-autonomous mechanism explains why you can have a half-male, half-female bird brain — it does not happen in mammals. [01:35:41](transcript.md#0135-birds--sexual-differentiation-gynandromorphs)

- **Rough-and-tumble play is the most biologically robust behavioral sex difference.** Males of virtually every studied species — rats, monkeys, humans — engage in more physical play. It is testosterone-driven and highly resistant to social override. In mixed-sex groups the level is intermediate: females calm boys slightly, boys ramp girls slightly. Virtually every other expression of masculine behavior is substantially shaped by culture and family. No credible social correlates of sexual orientation have been found — not absent fathers, overbearing mothers, older sisters, or theater participation. [01:45:31](transcript.md#0145-age--testosterone-decline-sexual-orientation--activities)

- **Testosterone declines gradually and variably with age in men** — far more gradually than estrogen loss at menopause. Individual variation is enormous; absolute levels do not reliably predict subjective well-being (Huberman cites a CEO at low-300s ng/dL feeling excellent, with no reason to intervene). [01:45:31](transcript.md#0145-age--testosterone-decline-sexual-orientation--activities)

## Tools & Protocols

### 2D:4D Finger Ratio Self-Assessment
- **What:** A rough group-level proxy for prenatal testosterone exposure. The ratio of index finger (2D) length to ring finger (4D) length on the right hand reflects androgen receptor activity in fetal digit-bone growth. The 4th digit has more androgen receptor in its growing bones, so it grows relatively longer under testosterone, producing a lower 2D:4D ratio.
- **How:** Measure both fingers on the right hand with a ruler; divide 2D length by 4D length. In men the ring finger is typically longer (lower ratio, e.g. ~0.96); in women the two fingers are more similar in length (~0.99). The sex difference is more pronounced on the right hand.
- **When:** Informational and population-level only — has no individual predictive value for sexual orientation. Effect size is ~0.5 SD (vs. ~2 SD for height). Other variables influence digit ratios. Looking at your hand and guessing straight will be correct 95% of the time regardless of what you see.
- **Ref:** [00:07:37](transcript.md#0007-prenatal-testosterone-finger-ratio-men--women-differences)
