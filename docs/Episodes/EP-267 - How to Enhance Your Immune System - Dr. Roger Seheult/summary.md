# Episode 267: How to Enhance Your Immune System | Dr. Roger Seheult

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Roger Seheult, M.D. — board-certified physician in internal medicine, pulmonary disease, critical care, and sleep medicine at Loma Linda University, and founder of MedCram — joins Andrew Huberman for a deep dive into immune system optimization. The conversation is organized around the NEWSTART mnemonic (Nutrition, Exercise, Water, Sunlight, Temperance, Air, Rest, Trust), using it as a framework to cover every major lifestyle lever affecting immune resilience. A central thesis is that roughly 52% of solar energy is infrared, which penetrates skin and clothing to reach mitochondria directly, where it stimulates local melatonin production and improves mitochondrial efficiency — making sunlight far more than just a circadian-rhythm tool. The episode covers practical tools for preventing and recovering from infections including hydrotherapy, NAC supplementation, eucalyptus oil, and zinc, with historical precedents from 1918 flu sanitariums and Nobel Prize–winning phototherapy, and includes a frank clinical discussion of long COVID as mitochondrial dysfunction, the Swiss cheese model of layered protection, forest bathing, the measurable immune effects of trust and forgiveness, and how to advocate for better care during hospitalization.

## Key Takeaways

1. **NEWSTART is the root framework for immune health** — Dr. Neil Nedley's mnemonic covers Nutrition (whole, minimally processed foods), Exercise (mild-to-moderate; J-curve relationship with immunity), Water (internal hydration and external hydrotherapy), Sunlight, Temperance (avoid toxins including smoking and vaping), Air (fresh outdoor air and phytoncides), Rest (7–8 hours sleep), and Trust (faith, community, forgiveness). Each pillar has both mechanistic and epidemiological support. ([00:02:16](transcript.md#000216-avoiding-sickness-immune-system-tool-pillars-of-health-newstart))

2. **Infrared light from sunlight drives mitochondrial melatonin production** — About 52% of solar energy is infrared, which penetrates skin, clothing, and even bone to reach mitochondria. Mitochondria produce melatonin on-site — at concentrations 20x higher than the blood — as a powerful antioxidant to neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS). This is entirely separate from the pineal gland's circadian melatonin and operates locally within cells. Infrared also stimulates cytochrome c oxidase and nitric oxide pathways, improving overall mitochondrial efficiency. ([00:10:46](transcript.md#001046-sunlight-mitochondria-tool-infrared-light--melatonin))

3. **Green spaces amplify infrared exposure 2–4x** — Leaves and grass with chlorophyll are highly reflective of infrared light, so outdoor environments with trees provide far more infrared than open spaces. A Louisville, Kentucky study planted 8,000+ mature trees in a 4-square-mile area; 2–3 years later, with no other changes, residents' high-sensitivity CRP (an inflammation marker) dropped by 13% — comparable to exercising three times per week. ([00:26:38](transcript.md#002638-infrared-light-green-spaces-health--mortality))

4. **Morning red light measurably improves mitochondrial output** — Dr. Glen Jeffrey (UCL) showed 670 nm red light for 3 minutes in the morning increased color sensitivity in older adults by 17%, lasting for days. In a separate double-blind RCT, 940 nm near-infrared light at just 2.9 mW/cm² for 15 minutes/day for 7 days helped hospitalized COVID patients recover faster, improving oxygen saturation, respiratory rate, lymphocyte counts, and reducing average hospital stay from 12 to 8 days. The effect is time-of-day dependent. ([00:31:35](transcript.md#003135-infrared-light-mitochondrial-dysfunction-disease))

5. **Sunlight strongly protects against influenza — the H1N1 natural experiment** — A Harvard Kennedy School study used the 2009 H1N1 pandemic (which peaked in summer, uncoupling flu from winter variables) to isolate sunlight as a key protective factor. Sunlight-rich environments consistently showed lower infection rates. A 2021 European study found latitude — not temperature, not humidity — perfectly predicted the timing of autumn COVID surges across countries. Influenza mortality in the US peaks 1–3 weeks after the winter solstice; in Australia it peaks in June–July. ([00:43:32](transcript.md#004332-sunlight-incidence-of-influenza))

6. **Recommended sunlight: 15–20 minutes daily, any time the sun is up** — Based on Glen Jeffrey's research and a Brazilian RCT, 15 minutes is enough for meaningful mitochondrial benefit; diminishing returns set in quickly. Exposure can happen at any time of day — even midday through clothing (UV blocks; infrared penetrates). On cloudy days, go outside anyway — light levels outdoors are dramatically higher than indoors even in overcast conditions. In winter, artificial 850–940 nm infrared lamps at ~3 mW/cm² can partially substitute, but only showed statistically significant benefit in winter months. More intensity is not better — a biphasic response means very high levels are detrimental. ([00:48:41](transcript.md#004841-tools-sunlight-exposure-duration-winter-months))

7. **Darkness at night is equally important** — A study showed 100 lux of light during sleep (a dim nightlight) raised morning blood glucose significantly. Fifteen seconds of bright artificial light at night can meaningfully suppress melatonin. LED lights and low-E glass windows filter out infrared while letting through blue/green light — creating a biologically reversed environment of dim days and bright nights. Sleeping in total darkness (blackout curtains plus silk or faux-silk eye mask) is one of the highest-leverage sleep interventions. For bathroom navigation, shine a flashlight on the floor — not into the eyes. ([01:22:16](transcript.md#012216-sleep--darkness-tools-eye-mask-bathroom-navigation-meals--light))

8. **Flu shot is one layer in a Swiss cheese model — not binary** — Dr. Seheult gets the flu shot annually due to his ICU exposure and recommends it for immunocompromised or highly exposed individuals. He frames it via the Swiss cheese model: no single intervention is complete protection; overlapping imperfect layers (vaccine, sunlight, sleep, NAC, handwashing) together provide robust protection. Known risks include rare anaphylaxis and a historical narcolepsy cluster in Europe with one specific discontinued strain. ([01:28:27](transcript.md#012827-influenza-flu-shots-swiss-cheese-model-flu-shot-risks))

9. **Fever is an immune weapon — don't reflexively suppress it** — At 38°C (100.4°F), STAT/JAK signaling for interferon transcription increases dramatically. At 39°C (102.2°F), interferon secretion from lymphocytes increases 10-fold in vitro. Interferon is variant-agnostic — it works against any virus. Exogenous interferon-lambda in a phase 3 NEJM trial reduced COVID hospitalizations by 50%. The 1918 Army hospitals made a key mistake using high-dose aspirin to suppress fever, likely worsening outcomes. Sanitariums using hydrotherapy (no fever suppression, core heat elevation instead) had 1/6 the pneumonia progression rate. ([01:43:57](transcript.md#014357-water-sodium-innate-immune-system-fever--hydrotherapy))

10. **Cold hydrotherapy mobilizes white blood cells** — Cold water causes vasoconstriction, releasing (demarginating) white blood cells from vessel walls into circulation. Labs will show elevated WBC counts post-cold shower — not because there are more cells, but because they are now measurable in blood rather than marginated against vessel walls. Vasoconstriction also prevents the heat built up during hot exposure from dissipating, sustaining core temperature elevation. The Battle Creek Sanitarium protocol was 20 minutes hot followed by under a minute cold with physical rubbing. ([01:58:25](transcript.md#015825-cold-hydrotherapy-vasoconstriction--white-blood-cells))

11. **NAC reduces flu symptom severity by ~50%** — In a multicenter double-blind placebo-controlled trial, 600 mg NAC twice daily over a winter season did not reduce infection rate but reduced symptom severity dramatically (severe symptoms dropped from ~78% to ~28% — a number needed to treat of ~2). NAC works via two mechanisms: mucolytic (breaks disulfide SS bonds in mucus to liquefy it, acting as a natural decongestant) and antioxidant (glutathione precursor). It may also prevent the von Willebrand Factor polymerization that causes white clots in severe flu and COVID. Dose: 600–900 mg, 2–4x daily during illness; Dr. Seheult limits use to winter seasons (~3 months). ([02:09:56](transcript.md#020956-n-acetyl-cysteine-nac-glutathione-white-clots-flu))

12. **Zinc and eucalyptus oil have supporting evidence** — 40 mg elemental zinc daily (monitor copper levels long-term) serves as a co-factor in immune enzymatic reactions. Eucalyptus oil in an in vitro study dramatically increased phagocytosis by innate immune cells. Best used via steam inhalation (hot water + a few drops) or topically on the upper lip — never ingested, as reported deaths have occurred. It is the main active ingredient in Vicks VapoRub. ([02:25:25](transcript.md#022525-zinc-supplementation-copper-exogenous-interferon), [02:28:40](transcript.md#022840-eucalyptus-oil-inhalation))

13. **Forest bathing and phytoncides directly enhance innate immunity** — Japanese studies on Tokyo executives taken to Hinoki cypress forests found phytoncides (volatile organic compounds from trees) measurably increased natural killer cell activity and reduced chromogranin A (a stress marker). Effect lasted 7 days. When researchers infused the same phytoncides into a Tokyo hotel room, similar immune effects occurred — though urinary cortisol was lower only in the actual forest, suggesting additional benefit from being in nature. Even urban park walks near trees provide benefit. ([02:36:49](transcript.md#023649-fresh-air-forest-bathing-tool-go-outdoors))

14. **Long COVID is largely a mitochondrial dysfunction problem** — Research shows significant downregulation of beta-oxidation (fatty acid metabolism) enzymes in long COVID patients vs. those who recovered. Dr. Seheult treated a patient with 8/10 shortness of breath for over a year using only intermittent fasting (stop eating by 5:30 PM) and sunlight — symptoms dropped to 3/10 within one month. Mechanism: fasting allows the innate immune system to identify and clear damaged mitochondria (via DAMPs), enabling regeneration. Sunlight-driven infrared also mitigates toll-like receptor 4 inflammation, the specific pathway activated by SARS-CoV-2 spike protein. ([02:52:38](transcript.md#025238-mitochondrial-dysfunction-intermittent-fasting-sunlight))

15. **Trust, community, and forgiveness are measurable immune variables** — People with strong social networks and gratitude practice have fewer somatic complaints. A Texas survey of 1,500 Christians found unconditional forgivers had lower anxiety, better well-being, and fewer somatic complaints than conditional forgivers. The strongest predictor of unconditional forgiveness was feeling forgiven by God (OR ~2–3). Dr. Seheult routinely uses this clinically with ICU patients who express unresolved guilt. ([03:11:46](transcript.md#031146-trust-spirituality-community-faith-forgiveness))

16. **Asking intelligent questions in a hospital improves your care** — Dr. Seheult's single most actionable hospital tip: demonstrate familiarity with the patient's condition by asking informed, specific questions. This signals to physicians and staff that they will be held accountable, raising the standard of care delivered. Ask for a room near a window. Ask when events will happen and why. Anger or insults to staff have the opposite effect. Resources like MedCram provide disease-specific knowledge to ask the right questions. ([03:19:46](transcript.md#031946-hospital-admission-tool-asking-questions))

## Tools & Protocols

### Sunlight Exposure
- **What:** Direct outdoor sunlight on skin — infrared penetrates clothing, so even covered skin benefits
- **How:** 15–20 minutes outside; any time of day is fine; continue on cloudy and rainy days (infrared penetrates); wear a hat and long sleeves if UV is a concern — infrared still gets through; seek green spaces (trees amplify infrared 2–4x)
- **When:** Daily, year-round; winter requires deliberate effort — most people go weeks without any outdoor time
- **Ref:** [00:48:41](transcript.md#004841-tools-sunlight-exposure-duration-winter-months)

### Infrared/Red Light Therapy (Artificial)
- **What:** 850–940 nm near-infrared lamp at low intensity (~3 mW/cm²)
- **How:** Sit within a few feet for 15–20 minutes; do not increase intensity — biphasic response means high intensity is detrimental; only showed statistically significant benefit in winter months when outdoor light was insufficient
- **When:** Morning; winter supplement when outdoor sunlight is limited
- **Ref:** [00:55:18](transcript.md#005518-infrared-lamps-winter-sunlight-exposure-obesity--metabolic-dysfunction)

### Hydrotherapy (Fever Simulation)
- **What:** Hot bath, shower, or sauna to raise core body temperature, followed by brief cold exposure
- **How:** Hot bath/shower/sauna (~20 minutes) until sweating → brief cold shower or plunge (under 1 minute) with physical rubbing on chest → wrap in warm blankets to sustain heat; the cold step demarginalizes WBCs into circulation and locks in core heat via peripheral vasoconstriction; repeat 1–2x daily during acute illness
- **When:** At first sign of illness or viral exposure; also as regular contrast therapy for prevention
- **Ref:** [01:53:46](transcript.md#015346-fever-heat-hydrotherapy-interferon--immune-system), [01:58:25](transcript.md#015825-cold-hydrotherapy-vasoconstriction--white-blood-cells)

### NAC Supplementation
- **What:** N-Acetyl Cysteine, 600–900 mg per dose
- **How:** 600 mg twice daily (winter prevention protocol) to 600–900 mg 3–4x daily (acute illness); expect increased mucus flow — blow nose frequently; do not use as indefinite daily maintenance — Dr. Seheult limits to ~3 months per season
- **When:** Winter season with flu exposure, at first sign of illness, travel, sick contacts, or low-level congestion
- **Ref:** [02:19:28](transcript.md#021928-tool-nac-dose--regimen-mucous-flu-symptoms)

### Zinc Supplementation
- **What:** Elemental zinc, 40 mg/day
- **How:** Check bottle for elemental zinc content (not total molecular weight); monitor copper levels if supplementing long-term
- **When:** During illness or high-exposure periods
- **Ref:** [02:25:25](transcript.md#022525-zinc-supplementation-copper-exogenous-interferon)

### Eucalyptus Oil Steam Inhalation
- **What:** A few drops of eucalyptus essential oil in hot water for steam inhalation
- **How:** Heat water on stove, place towel over head and inhale steam with a few drops of eucalyptus oil added; alternatively apply topically to upper lip; NEVER ingest — reported deaths from internal use
- **When:** During congestion or acute illness to support innate immune phagocytosis and relieve congestion
- **Ref:** [02:28:40](transcript.md#022840-eucalyptus-oil-inhalation)

### Sleep in Total Darkness
- **What:** Eliminate all light exposure during sleep
- **How:** Blackout curtains plus a silk or faux-silk eye mask; for bathroom navigation, shine a flashlight on the floor — not into the eyes; avoid looking at phone screens; 15 seconds of bright artificial light at night meaningfully suppresses melatonin
- **When:** Every night
- **Ref:** [01:22:16](transcript.md#012216-sleep--darkness-tools-eye-mask-bathroom-navigation-meals--light)

### Forest Bathing / Go Outdoors
- **What:** Time in nature near trees for phytoncide exposure, infrared reflection, and fresh air
- **How:** Minimum 15–30 minutes outdoors daily near trees; 3-day forest stays produce NK cell enhancement lasting 7+ days; hotel room infusers with phytoncide oils produce similar immune effects if outdoor access is unavailable; even urban park walks provide benefit
- **When:** Daily; combines with sunlight exposure for compounded benefit
- **Ref:** [02:36:49](transcript.md#023649-fresh-air-forest-bathing-tool-go-outdoors)

### Hospital Admission — Asking Questions
- **What:** Proactive informed questioning to elevate the standard of care received
- **How:** Research the patient's diagnosis beforehand (MedCram courses are a resource); ask specific, informed questions about timing, medications, and next steps; ask for a room near a window for sunlight exposure; never insult or yell at staff — it has the opposite effect
- **When:** At hospital admission or when advocating for a hospitalized family member
- **Ref:** [03:19:46](transcript.md#031946-hospital-admission-tool-asking-questions)
