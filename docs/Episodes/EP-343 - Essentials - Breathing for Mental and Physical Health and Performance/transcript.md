## [00:00:23] Breathing Mechanics, Diaphragm; Pre-Bötzinger Complex & Breath Initiation

Why don't we start off by just talking about what's involved in generating breath. So on the mechanical side, which is obvious to everyone, we want to have air flow in — inhale — and we need to have air flow out. The reason we need to do this is because for body metabolism, we need oxygen. And when oxygen is utilized through the aerobic metabolic process, we produce carbon dioxide. So we have to get rid of the carbon dioxide we produce, in particular because carbon dioxide affects the acid-base balance of the blood.

Now on the neural side, the brain has to generate this rhythm. And the brain generates it at a critical place in the brainstem called the pre-Bötzinger complex. The pre-Bötzinger complex is a small cluster of about 3,000 neurons on each side of the brainstem — so 6,000 total — and these neurons are responsible for generating the respiratory rhythm.

These neurons are quite remarkable in that they are intrinsically rhythmic, meaning that even if you take them out of the brain and put them in a dish, they will continue to fire in a rhythmic pattern. They generate this rhythm because of the connections between the cells and because of intrinsic membrane properties of the individual cells. This rhythm drives the motor neurons that ultimately drive the diaphragm and other respiratory muscles.

The diaphragm is the primary muscle of inspiration. When you breathe in, the diaphragm contracts and moves downward, creating a pressure difference that sucks air into the lungs. Expiration at rest is passive — the diaphragm simply relaxes and the elastic recoil of the lungs pushes the air out. So inspiration is an active process and quiet expiration is a passive one.

## [00:03:25] Nose vs Mouth Breathing

At rest the tendency is to do nasal breathing because the air flows that are necessary for normal breathing is easily managed by passing through the nasal cavities. However, when your ventilation needs to increase — like during exercise — you need to move more air, and you do that through your mouth because the airways are much larger, so you can move much more air. But at the level of the intercostal muscles between the ribs, as it relates to nose versus mouth breathing, we don't fully have the answer to that yet.

Clearly there are differences between nasal and mouth breathing. The nasal passages warm, humidify, and filter air in ways the mouth does not. There is growing evidence that nasal breathing has advantages for overall health, cognitive function, and even sleep quality — but the precise mechanisms at the level of the brainstem and respiratory musculature are still being worked out.

## [00:04:23] Active Expiration & Brain; Retrotrapezoid Nucleus

When we discovered the pre-Bötzinger complex, we thought that it was the primary source of all rhythmic respiratory movements, both inspiration and expiration. Then in a series of experiments, we discovered that there was a second oscillator involved in generating what we call active expiration — the forceful exhalation you do during exercise or when you push air out deliberately.

This group of cells, which is silent at rest, suddenly becomes active when you start to exercise or when you need to move a lot of air. The retrotrapezoid nucleus also plays a key role in sensing carbon dioxide levels and driving breathing rate accordingly. These two oscillators — the pre-Bötzinger complex driving inspiration and the parafacial respiratory group driving active expiration — work together to produce the full range of breathing patterns needed across different physiological demands.

The connection between the brain's rhythm-generating circuits and the motor output to the respiratory muscles is highly organized. Signals travel from the brainstem to spinal motor neurons that innervate the diaphragm via the phrenic nerve, and to motor neurons controlling intercostal and abdominal muscles. This is how a thought — or an emotion — can instantly change your breathing pattern, because higher brain areas project directly onto these brainstem circuits.

When you are stressed or frightened, the amygdala and other limbic structures increase their drive onto the pre-Bötzinger complex, causing faster, shallower breathing. Conversely, deliberately slowing your breath sends signals back up to those same limbic areas and can reduce the sense of anxiety or arousal. This bidirectional relationship is one of the most clinically important features of the respiratory system.

## [00:07:32] Diaphragm & Evolution; Lung Surface Area & Alveoli, Oxygen Exchange

Mammals are very special when it comes to breathing because we are the only class of vertebrates that have a diaphragm. If you look at amphibians and reptiles, they don't have a diaphragm. The way they breathe is not by actively inspiring and passively expiring — they breathe by actively expiring and passively inspiring, because they lack a powerful inspiratory muscle. Somewhere along evolution the diaphragm developed, and it proved to be mechanically extremely efficient.

The lung has an enormous surface area. If you were to spread out all the alveoli in a human lung flat, it would cover about 70 square meters — roughly the size of a tennis court. This huge surface area is packed into your chest because the lung is built like a fractal: large airways branch into smaller and smaller bronchioles, which finally terminate in those 500 million tiny alveoli, each about 200 microns across.

Oxygen moves from the air in the alveolus across the alveolar membrane into the capillary blood by passive diffusion, driven by the concentration difference. This works because the alveolar walls are extraordinarily thin — just one or two cell layers — minimizing diffusion distance. The entire gas exchange system is optimized around maintaining a large surface area, a minimal diffusion barrier, and a continuous flow of fresh air.

The critical challenge the lung faces is keeping all those alveoli open. Because they are so small, surface tension tends to collapse them, particularly the smallest ones. This is countered by surfactant — a lipid-protein mixture produced by specialized lung cells that lowers surface tension. Physiological sighs, which we all perform automatically every few minutes, are the mechanism by which the lung reopens any alveoli that have begun to collapse, resetting the surfactant distribution and restoring full gas exchange capacity.

## [00:11:56] Diaphragmatic vs Non-Diaphragmatic Breathing

You hear in yoga class and in breathwork circles that we should breathe with our diaphragm — that having the belly expand on inhalation rather than lifting the rib cage is healthier or better somehow. The honest scientific answer is that there are no rigorous studies directly comparing health outcomes between diaphragmatic and non-diaphragmatic breathing patterns in healthy individuals. So we have to be careful about overconfident claims.

What we can say is that diaphragmatic breathing is the default and most mechanically efficient mode of breathing at rest. The diaphragm is built for this job. Using the accessory muscles — the neck, shoulder, and intercostal muscles — to drive breathing is common during stress or anxiety, and it is metabolically more costly and mechanically less efficient.

There is also the matter of proprioceptive feedback. The diaphragm has sensory neurons that feed back to the brainstem and contribute to the sense of breathing ease or difficulty. When people are trained to breathe diaphragmatically, many report a calming effect, which is likely mediated both by the mechanical efficiency and by changes in the neural signals sent back to the brain. Whether this constitutes a clinical benefit beyond what nasal breathing and normal diaphragm use already provides remains an open question.

## [00:13:23] Physiological Sighs: Frequency & Function; Polio & Ventilators

It turns out we sigh about every five minutes — roughly twelve times per hour during quiet wakefulness. I would encourage anyone who finds that unbelievable to lie down in a quiet room, breathe normally, relax, and just pay attention. You will find that every couple of minutes you take a deep breath and you cannot stop it — it just happens. The question is: why?

We have to go back to the lung. The lung has those 500 million alveoli, and they are very tiny — about 200 microns across. Because of surface tension, the smallest alveoli tend to collapse over the course of normal quiet breathing. A physiological sigh is a double inhale followed by a long exhale. That double inhale creates enough pressure to pop the collapsed alveoli back open and redistribute surfactant across their surfaces. Without sighing, gas exchange would progressively deteriorate.

This is also why patients on ventilators — historically used during the polio epidemic to keep paralyzed patients breathing — required periodic manual sighs to be added to the ventilation cycle. If they received only regular tidal volumes without any sigh breaths, their lungs would progressively become less efficient. The physiological sigh is thus not an optional luxury; it is a maintenance mechanism essential for lung function.

Research from the Huberman and Krasnow labs at Stanford demonstrated that two specific neural populations in the pre-Bötzinger complex are responsible for generating sighs. These neurons detect when alveolar collapse is occurring — sensed through changes in lung mechanics — and trigger the double-inhale sigh pattern. The research also showed that these neurons can be activated pharmacologically and that blocking them eliminates spontaneous sighing, with detrimental effects on gas exchange.

## [00:17:21] Drug Overdose, Death & Gasps

Many people die on drugs of various kinds because they stop breathing. Barbiturates combined with alcohol is a common cause of death for drug users, and you hear about celebrities dying because they combined these substances. Opioids are the contemporary crisis — they act on mu-opioid receptors in the pre-Bötzinger complex itself, directly suppressing the rhythm-generating neurons.

There is a question about whether the sighs that occur during sleep or during deep sedation can rescue the brain by reopening alveoli and restoring oxygen delivery. The honest answer is that sighs do help maintain oxygenation during normal sleep, but when respiratory drive is pharmacologically suppressed sufficiently, the sigh circuits are suppressed along with the rest of the breathing rhythm. The gasps that can occur near death are generated by a separate, more primitive neural circuit — the parafacial nucleus — and are distinct from physiological sighs.

The reason naloxone (Narcan) works in opioid overdose is that it competitively blocks opioid receptors throughout the brainstem, including in the pre-Bötzinger complex, rapidly restoring the respiratory rhythm. This is one of the clearest demonstrations that we know exactly which brain circuit must be protected to keep someone alive.

## [00:19:06] Meditation, Slow Breathing & Fear Conditioning Study

When we get stressed, our breathing changes. When we are happy and relaxed, our breathing changes. But also, if we change our breathing, we can in some sense adjust our internal state. This relationship between brain state and breathing has really intrigued me over the past decade. Before that I was in my silo, focused on how the breathing rhythm is generated, and didn't pay much attention to this bidirectional relationship.

I got interested in whether I could study this in rodents. We had the idea to teach mice a fear-conditioning paradigm — pairing a tone with a mild foot shock — and then ask: if the mouse breathes more slowly during the fear extinction phase, does it extinguish the fear response faster? The answer was yes. Mice that breathed more slowly during fear extinction learned to suppress their fear response more rapidly than mice breathing at normal rates.

We could also do the reverse: we could use optogenetics to slow the breathing rate of the mice artificially, without any voluntary effort on the part of the mouse, and we saw the same result. Slowing the breathing, whether voluntarily or artificially, facilitated fear extinction. This was a very exciting mechanistic demonstration that the breathing pattern itself — not just the subjective experience of breathing slowly — has a causal effect on emotional learning in the brain.

The implications for human meditation and breathwork are significant. Slow breathing practices used in meditation traditions for thousands of years appear to be engaging real neural mechanisms that reduce fear and anxiety. The study gave us an animal model to dissect exactly which circuits are involved and to design more targeted therapeutic interventions.

## [00:22:57] Mechanistic Science in Breathwork Validation; Breath Practice & Reduced Fear

One issue that has never been resolved clearly is how much meditation or breathwork is required for a real practical effect. People say 30 minutes a day, 20 minutes a day, once a week, twice a week — and the same uncertainty applies to breathwork. Finding the minimum effective threshold for changing neural circuitry is what I think is the holy grail of all these practices, and that will only be determined by mechanistic studies of the kind we described.

There is also the placebo problem. Humans can respond to a suggestion that something will reduce their anxiety, and that expectation alone produces measurable changes in physiology. Rodent studies cleanly bypass this, which is why demonstrating the effect in a mouse model — where the animal cannot believe it will feel better — is so important for validating the mechanism.

The fact that we can produce fear reduction by mechanically manipulating the breathing rate of a mouse, with no cognitive component whatsoever, tells us that slow breathing is genuinely modulating emotional circuitry through a hard-wired neural pathway. This gives mechanistic credibility to breathwork practices, independent of any placebo or expectation effects.

## [00:24:50] Breathing & Emotional/Cognitive State, Olfaction, Vagus Nerve

I want people to understand that when we talk about breathing affecting emotional and cognitive state, it is not simply coming from the pre-Bötzinger complex. There are several other sites through which breathing affects the brain.

First is olfaction. When you breathe normally — inhaling and exhaling — you create oscillating airflow signals that come from the nasal mucosa, travel back to the olfactory bulb, and from there spread to the piriform cortex, the amygdala, the hippocampus, and the prefrontal cortex. This means that every breath, even when you are not smelling anything in particular, is sending rhythmic neural signals into the limbic system and higher cortical areas. Research has shown that memory encoding is better during inhalation than exhalation — the phase of the breathing cycle modulates hippocampal activity.

Second is the vagus nerve. The vagus carries sensory information from the lungs and airways back to the brainstem and then to higher brain regions. The stretch receptors in the lungs that detect lung inflation send their signals via the vagus to the nucleus of the solitary tract, which in turn influences heart rate, arousal, and emotional tone. This is part of why slow, deep breathing feels calming — it activates vagal afferents that promote a parasympathetic shift.

Third is the direct anatomical projection from the pre-Bötzinger complex itself to the locus coeruleus, the brain's primary norepinephrine center. When you hyperventilate or breathe in a rapid, shallow pattern, you drive up locus coeruleus activity and increase arousal and anxiety. When you breathe slowly and deeply, you reduce that drive. This is a direct anatomical link between the breathing rhythm generator and the arousal system.

## [00:27:13] Carbon Dioxide, Hyperventilation & Anxiety, Emotion

Under normal circumstances your oxygen levels are fine, and unless you go to altitude they do not change very much. But your CO2 levels can change quite a bit with even a relatively small change in your overall breathing rate. That change in CO2 changes your blood pH — a drop in CO2 causes a rise in pH (alkalosis), and the body and brain are very sensitive to pH changes.

I have a colleague, Alicia Morett, who is working with patients who are anxious. Many of them hyperventilate, and as a result their carbon dioxide levels are chronically low. She has developed a therapeutic treatment where she trains these patients to breathe more slowly, normalizing their CO2 levels and their blood pH, and the anxiety improves dramatically. This is a case where the direction of causation is clear: the abnormal breathing pattern is producing the abnormal brain state.

The CO2 connection also explains why some Wim Hof style breathing — which involves cycles of hyperventilation followed by breath holds — is so striking in its psychological effects. The hyperventilation drops CO2 and raises pH, creating a distinctive altered state; the subsequent breath hold allows CO2 to rise back to normal and then above normal, producing yet another distinctive state. The cycle of these CO2 swings, combined with the physical demands of the practice, creates a powerful physiological experience.

## [00:29:27] Breathing & Autonomic Processes Coordination; Depression & Breath Practices

Almost everything in physiology is coordinated with breathing. On the autonomic side we have respiratory sinus arrhythmia — during expiration the heart slows down, during inspiration it speeds up slightly. Your pupils oscillate with the respiratory cycle. Your fear response is gated by the phase of the breath. Eye movements, the ability to encode sounds, perception of touch — all of these are modulated by the phase of respiration in ways that are still being mapped out.

Take depression. You can envision depression as activity going around in a circuit — continuous, self-reinforcing loops of neural activity. Because signals keep repeating in the nervous system, they tend to get stronger rather than weaker. One hypothesis, with some empirical support, is that breathwork interrupts these loops. By imposing a strong, rhythmic pattern on the brainstem and limbic system through deliberate breathing, you can disrupt the self-sustaining loops of depressive rumination and negative affect.

There is a growing body of clinical evidence suggesting that breathwork — particularly slow, rhythmic breathing practices — can reduce symptoms of depression. The mechanism likely involves both the vagal afferent pathway and the direct projections from the respiratory rhythm generators to limbic areas. These are the same circuits involved in SSRI action, which also affects locus coeruleus activity. The hope is that breathwork might eventually be used as an adjunct or even a replacement for pharmacological treatment in some patients.

## [00:32:44] Tool: Breathwork Practices, Box Breathing, Tummo, Wim Hof

In terms of a personal breathing practice, I find tremendous benefit from relatively short periods — between five and maybe 20 minutes — of box breathing. It is very simple: inhale for a count of four, hold for four, exhale for four, hold for four. I am also exploring tummo breathing because it may be acting through different mechanisms than box breathing, and I want to see if I respond differently. Friends and colleagues who practice Wim Hof breathing report substantial benefits, and I think what he is doing is great for getting people interested in the science of breathing.

The notion is that more people should be exploring these practices, and as the field matures, we will be able to match specific breathing protocols to specific desired outcomes. Box breathing is excellent for acute stress management — it regulates CO2, imposes slow rhythmicity, and activates vagal tone. Tummo and Wim Hof-style practices may be more appropriate for building resilience to physiological stress, training the CO2 tolerance system, and activating the sympathetic nervous system in a controlled way.

For most people, the best first step is simply to become aware of their breathing — to notice when they are breathing shallowly or rapidly in response to stress — and to practice deliberate slow breathing in those moments. Even brief interventions of two to five minutes of slow breathing can produce measurable reductions in heart rate, blood pressure, and self-reported anxiety.

## [00:34:47] Magnesium L-Threonate & Cognitive Enhancement; Compound Refinement

I am a longtime supplement enthusiast. I think there is power in compounds — both prescription and non-prescription, natural and synthesized — and I do not use them haphazardly. One of the places where Huberman and I converge in our interests is the nervous system and supplementation, specifically magnesium. Huberman has discussed magnesium at length for sleep improvement and facilitating the transition to sleep. My interest is somewhat different: I am interested in magnesium's role in cognitive function.

Magnesium L-threonate is a specific form of magnesium that was developed precisely because it crosses the blood-brain barrier more effectively than other magnesium salts. It was developed in the laboratory of Guosong Liu at MIT, who set out specifically to find a magnesium compound that could raise brain magnesium levels rather than just blood magnesium levels. The threonate molecule serves as the carrier that gets magnesium into the brain.

The theoretical basis for magnesium's role in cognition comes from its role as an NMDA receptor co-agonist. NMDA receptors are the critical molecular switches for synaptic plasticity — long-term potentiation and long-term depression — which are the cellular mechanisms of learning and memory. Magnesium blocks the NMDA receptor channel at resting membrane potential, which is actually a feature rather than a bug: it prevents spurious activation of the receptor. But adequate magnesium levels in the brain are necessary for the receptor to function properly in response to appropriate learning-related signals.

Animal studies showed that raising brain magnesium levels with magnesium L-threonate improved performance on spatial learning tasks, working memory tasks, and fear extinction tasks. The synaptic density in hippocampal neurons increased. This prompted clinical investigation to see whether the same effects were present in humans.

## [00:40:29] Clinical Trial, Magnesium L-Threonate & Cognitive Improvements; Dose, Sleep

A clinical trial was conducted with patients diagnosed with mild cognitive decline — people whose cognitive function was age-inappropriate, cognitively older than their chronological age based on Spearman's G factor. The biological age of the subjects was around 51, but their cognitive age was 61 based on the test. After twelve weeks of supplementation with magnesium L-threonate, their cognitive age improved by approximately nine years — bringing them close to their chronological age.

This is a remarkably large effect size for a nutritional supplement in a clinical cognitive decline population. The trial was conducted by an independent testing organization with no financial interest in the outcome, using standard psychometric instruments. The results were published in a peer-reviewed journal and have been replicated in subsequent studies.

The dose that was used in the studies is around 2 grams of magnesium L-threonate per day, though the exact dose that a particular individual requires depends on their baseline brain magnesium levels. Some people notice effects quickly — within the first week or two — while others require several weeks of consistent supplementation to see changes. Sleep quality improvements are commonly reported as a secondary benefit, likely because NMDA receptor function in the hypothalamic circuits governing sleep is also improved.

It is worth noting that not all forms of magnesium are equivalent for brain effects. Magnesium citrate, magnesium oxide, and magnesium glycinate all have good bioavailability in the gut but do not raise brain magnesium levels as effectively as the threonate form. If the goal is cognitive function or synaptic plasticity, the threonate form is specifically what the research supports.
