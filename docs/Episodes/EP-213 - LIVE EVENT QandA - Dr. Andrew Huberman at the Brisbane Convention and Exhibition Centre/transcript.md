## [03:48] Nicotine Discussion

**Audience:** What are my thoughts on nicotine?

**Andrew Huberman:** Nicotine causes cancer when it's consumed in the form of smoking, vaping, dipping, or snuffing — so don't do that. There's a debate now about vaping: is it bad, is it good? It's bad. Is it worse for you than smoking? Probably not. Is it better? Probably slightly. You know what's better? Just not doing it. But if you need to do it and you have to pick, I suppose vaping has allowed a good number of people to smoke less — I'll acknowledge that — and it's also clear it's not good for you. So if you're going to do something that's bad for you, do a bunch of things to offset the thing that's bad for you; that's always my advice.

Now in terms of nicotine itself — nicotine doesn't cause cancer. The mode of consumption causes cancer, that's important. Nicotine binds to so-called nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, which exist naturally in your body and on your muscles. They're the way that your nerves control contraction of your muscles. The consumption of nicotine — let's say in pouches, which is becoming fairly common in Europe and the Middle East — can be absorbed sublingually or through the gum, gets into the bloodstream, and it is truly a cognitive enhancer. It will raise attention, focus, and cognitive performance; this is well established. The problem is it also raises blood pressure and causes vasoconstriction; this is also well established. So you have to ask yourself: is it worth it? Do I do it sometimes? Do I do it often? Do I choose not to do it at all?

I don't think the young brain should be consuming nicotine even in these non-cancer-causing forms like pouches, mostly because the brain is so plastic at a young age. But I am familiar with the use of nicotine for offsetting certain neurologic diseases. When I was visiting Columbia Medical University in New York City some years ago I was in the office of a Nobel Prize winner — won't tell you who it was necessarily — and he proceeded to consume no fewer than six pieces of Nicorette gum in our half-hour meeting. He was in his late 70s and I asked him about the nicotine. He said, "Oh, it offsets Parkinson's and Alzheimer's. You can increase cognitive function. I used to smoke but I don't want to get cancer so I just chew a lot of Nicorette." He explained it can increase the amount of acetylcholine activation through the binding of these nicotinic acetylcholine receptors and might even maintain some dopaminergic neurons — the neurons that one tends to lose with age and that are lost in diseases like Parkinson's.

So nicotine can enhance focus, alertness, and learning, but it does have those other issues — blood pressure and vasoconstriction. You want to be considerate of those and not become dependent on it. My experience is that people who taste the nicotine focus from a Zyn patch end up buying them pretty regularly. I know somebody who went from one Zyn patch twice a week to a canister a morning in about a month, because the effect will wear off if you keep consuming it every day and you have to consume more and more. Probably best to avoid unless you really need the boost and you can afford the increase in blood pressure. I've never taken nicotine and I don't smoke.

## [18:54] Understanding & Addressing Burnout

**Audience:** Do you believe in burnout? If so, what would be your recommendation to relinquish burnout once it's already occurred?

**Andrew Huberman:** This is a very interesting question. We don't quite know what burnout is, and it can come from a combination of things. Typically burnout comes not during the stress period but several months afterwards. The adrenals — these two little nuggets above our kidneys in our lower back — are capable of driving so much neural energy in us that we can do all sorts of things for a very long time, even in the absence of food, as long as we have water and salt. The adrenals kick out adrenaline and cortisol, and there is no such thing as true adrenal burnout because the adrenals don't burn out — you've got enough adrenaline in your adrenals for two lifetimes. But there is an adrenal insufficiency syndrome; that's a real thing, it's rare but it exists.

Burnout seems to me to be more related to psychological burnout. I'm not a psychologist, but I'm a fan of the poet David Whyte, who has a beautiful poem that includes the word "wholeheartedness." I think that where we recover ourselves is by relating to and engaging with things and people that we wholeheartedly enjoy — even if that is simply relaxation, or gardening, or drawing, or maybe just doing nothing for a bit.

Burnout is very real, and burnout — pushed through the filter of what we were talking about earlier in the evening — is when we are not getting periodic experiences of delight, excitement, or a sense of meaning. Not everyone gets to do a job that they delight in; certainly there were years where I didn't delight in the sorts of things I had to do for certain jobs. But finding some areas of life that create those neural energy states — that carry forward and wick out into other aspects of what we're doing — is crucial. Those moments of feeling really excited about something in a way that lights you up are not just about that moment; they lift our nervous system in a way that carries us forward and allows us to do the other things that we have to do, which frankly can sometimes be drudgery.

So if you've burnt out — I know the feeling, I have burnt out before — I encourage a combination of rest but also exploration of things that can evoke that kind of internal excitement or sense of meaning. One has to be a bit of a forager in order to do that, try new things, and that can be difficult. Burnout is real and I encourage you to take it seriously, because unfortunately typically what follows burnout is depression and things can really run ashore.

## [24:40] Understanding Food & Neural Pathways

## [38:02] Breathing Techniques for Stress & Focus

**Audience:** What are the physiological and practical differences between breathing techniques — Wim Hof, physiological sigh, relaxation, stress, focus, etc.?

**Andrew Huberman:** We can make this pretty straightforward. First of all, I know Wim — we go way back to 2015. I went over to the Pyrenees and visited him and hung out, then brought him to the States. Wim Hof breathing is Tummo breathing, but in science speak we call it cyclic hyperventilation.

If you inhale vigorously and long, your heart rate goes up. If you exhale vigorously and long, your heart rate goes down — through a process called respiratory sinus arrhythmia. The volume of the heart changes when you breathe in versus breathe out; the speed at which blood moves through the heart changes as the heart gets bigger or smaller according to inhales and exhales. The net effect is: inhale, heart speeds up a little bit; exhale, heart slows down a little bit.

So if you do Wim Hof / Tummo breathing — inhale vigorously and let it fall out of your mouth — you're going to increase heart rate and increase autonomic activation. If you do a pattern of breathing like inhale-inhale-long exhale (cyclic sighing), over time you're going to slow the heart rate down and calm down. That's just how it works.

Box breathing is relatively equal ratios of inhale, hold, exhale, hold — maintaining kind of even heart rate. When you do big cyclic hyperventilation — Wim Hof / Tummo breathing — you're increasing heart rate, autonomic arousal, and release of adrenaline. Do cyclic sighing with a lot of exhales, and the opposite is true.

That should give you a framework for thinking about breathing and how to apply different breathing techniques, away from just the naming of things. I'm not trying to take anything away from so-called Wim Hof breathing, but if you're going to do it, be very, very careful not to do cyclic hyperventilation and then hold your breath and do it anywhere near water. There have been cases of people drowning from combining cyclic hyperventilation and breath holds with water, because it changes the threshold for shallow water blackout. When you hyperventilate, you remove a lot of carbon dioxide — and carbon dioxide is the stimulus to gasp. So if you blow off a lot of carbon dioxide and go underwater and hold your breath, you'll hold it longer than you normally would, but instead of feeling that impulse to breathe and shoot for the surface, you'll just black out. Don't do Wim Hof / Tummo or cyclic hyperventilation breathing even standing or seated in a puddle of water.

In summary: if you want to be more alert, increase the vigor and duration of your inhales. If you want to be more calm, increase the duration of your exhales.
