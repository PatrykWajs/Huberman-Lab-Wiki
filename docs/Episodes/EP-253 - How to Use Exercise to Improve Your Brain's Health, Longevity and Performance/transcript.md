## [00:06:55] Brain Health, Cardiovascular & Resistance Training

Let's talk about the different forms of exercise and how they relate to brain health. The existing peer-reviewed literature on exercise and brain health tends to fall into two broad categories of exercise: cardiovascular exercise and resistance training. Within cardiovascular exercise, there is low-intensity steady-state cardio (sometimes called Zone 2 or long slow distance) and high-intensity interval training (HIIT). Within resistance training, there are compound movements (squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows, pull-ups, overhead press) and isolation exercises (bicep curls, leg extensions, etc.).

The studies on brain health compare these different modalities and look at acute effects — effects on the brain that are immediate and can last for hours after exercise — and chronic effects — effects on the brain that accumulate over days, weeks, and months of consistent exercise. Both types of effects are profoundly important and they have partially overlapping mechanisms.

Before getting into specific tools and protocols, I want to step back and explain what I think is the single most important concept in understanding how exercise benefits the brain. It's the concept of arousal.

## [00:11:51] Exercise & Positive Impact on Brain Performance; Arousal

Arousal, in the scientific sense, simply means your level of alertness on a spectrum from completely asleep or unconscious all the way to extremely alert and focused. Arousal is controlled in large part by specific neurochemicals — primarily epinephrine (adrenaline), which controls your level of overall alertness, and norepinephrine, which also controls alertness and is heavily involved in selective attention and focus. Dopamine plays a role in motivation and drive. These three neurochemicals — epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine — are what I sometimes call the "arousal-generating" neurochemicals.

The reason arousal is so important in the context of exercise and brain health is that the arousal-generating effects of exercise explain the vast majority — I would say perhaps 60 to 70 percent — of exercise's positive effects on the brain, both acutely and chronically. Understanding that arousal is the key mechanism shifts how you think about using exercise to improve brain function.

There is a classic paper by Larry Cahill at UC Irvine and James McGaugh. In this paper, they had subjects do a cold pressor test — immersing their arm in ice cold water — or receive an injection of adrenaline, before or after learning a specific set of information (a story). The subjects who had the adrenaline or cold-pressor-induced arousal showed dramatically better recall of that story compared to subjects who did not receive elevated arousal. Importantly, the recall was not just better overall — it was more detailed, richer, and with greater confidence. This tells us that arousal at the time of learning (or shortly before or shortly after learning) enhances memory encoding profoundly. It also tells us that the mechanism is adrenaline-driven arousal, not some other aspect of exercise.

## [00:18:20] Learning & Arousal

How does arousal improve learning and memory? Adrenaline and norepinephrine signal to the brain that what's happening right now is important. They essentially say: "This moment is worth encoding in long-term memory." This is the foundation of flashbulb memory — why people remember in vivid detail where they were on September 11, 2001, or when they got a particular piece of important news. High arousal at the time of an event strongly enhances the encoding of that event into memory.

The practical implication is profound: if you engage in something that increases arousal — whether that's physical exercise, cold exposure, or something emotionally salient — around the time you are trying to learn something, you will learn it better and remember it more vividly. The key principle here is that arousal and learning can be combined in a variety of ways: you can exercise before learning, you can exercise after learning, or you can do both. The arousal elevates the probability that the material you are studying or encountering will be encoded more durably.

There is also a more subtle point: if you happen to be doing a very mentally focused task and you notice that you're starting to drift or lose focus, brief exercise (even just getting up and moving briskly for a minute or two) can quickly restore arousal levels and bring you back to a focused state.

## [00:26:01] Exercise & Acute Learning

Let's talk about what exercise does to cognition acutely — immediately after a bout of exercise. There is now a really robust literature showing that exercise — both cardiovascular and resistance training — improves cognitive performance in the hours that follow. The specific cognitive domains that are improved include recall, cognitive flexibility (the ability to switch between mental tasks rapidly), working memory (the ability to hold things in mind and manipulate them), and executive function more broadly. These improvements can last for up to a couple of hours post-exercise.

The magnitude of the effect depends on the intensity and duration of the exercise. Moderate to high-intensity bouts tend to produce the biggest acute improvements in cognition. The Stroop task is a common measure of cognitive flexibility and executive function, and many studies have shown that performance on the Stroop task improves significantly following a bout of exercise. The mechanism is, again, the arousal-generating neurochemicals — especially epinephrine and norepinephrine — that are released during exercise and that persist in elevated levels for some time after exercise ends.

## [00:29:16] Tool: High-Intensity Training & Cognitive Flexibility; Over-Training

High-intensity interval training (HIIT) in particular is known to enhance executive function and cognitive flexibility. One fascinating study looked at executive function after exhaustive exercise. The interesting thing about that study is that it revealed an important caveat: two bouts of HIIT in a single day can actually deplete cerebral blood flow and result in diminished cognitive performance. This is a case where more is not better. Doing one HIIT session per day is fine and beneficial; doing two in a single day can be counterproductive for brain function.

This is part of the broader concept of over-training. Over-training — doing too much exercise without adequate recovery — is harmful for brain function. The brain needs recovery just as muscles do. So while consistent exercise is profoundly beneficial for the brain, there is a limit, and going beyond that limit can impair cognitive performance. The key signal to watch for is: if you are exercising regularly and your cognitive performance, mood, motivation, and alertness seem to be declining rather than improving, you may be over-training and should consider adding more recovery.

## [00:33:32] Long-Term Brain Health; Tool: Exercise "Snacks", Cognitive Performance

Exercise "snacks" are brief bouts of physical activity inserted throughout the day. These could be jumping jacks, air squats, a brief walk, or any other form of movement that takes just a few minutes. Research shows that exercise snacks can significantly boost cognitive performance. One study examined the effect of six-second sprints repeated six times with one minute of rest between each sprint. These brief, intense sprints provided a significant improvement in cognition. The beauty of exercise snacks is that they don't require a dedicated workout session — they can be threaded into a workday without disrupting workflow.

For long-term brain health, consistent exercise across weeks, months, and years is what matters most. The accumulated effects of regular exercise on brain structure and function are dramatic. Regular exercisers have larger hippocampal volume, better-preserved gray matter, higher cerebral blood flow, and enhanced neuroplasticity compared to sedentary individuals of the same age. These effects compound over time and represent one of the most powerful anti-aging interventions available for the brain.

## [00:36:57] Exercise, Brain & Body Energy, Adrenaline, Norepinephrine

Now let's get into the mechanism by which exercise elevates adrenaline and norepinephrine and thereby increases brain function. When you exercise, your motor cortex generates motor commands that travel down the spinal cord to activate your muscles. Along the way, these motor signals also communicate with a region of the spinal cord called the intermediolateral cell column (IML). The IML contains cholinergic preganglionic neurons that connect to the adrenal medulla — specifically to the chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla.

When the adrenal medulla receives these signals during exercise, it releases epinephrine (adrenaline) into the bloodstream. This adrenaline travels throughout the body and also feeds back to the brain via the vagus nerve. Specifically, adrenaline signals to a nucleus in the brainstem called the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS). From the NTS, signals travel to the locus coeruleus — the primary norepinephrine-producing nucleus in the brain. From the locus coeruleus, norepinephrine is broadly released throughout the brain.

This pathway — motor cortex → spinal cord IML → adrenal medulla → adrenaline → vagus nerve → NTS → locus coeruleus → norepinephrine in brain — is the core mechanism by which exercise creates a state of heightened alertness, focus, and readiness to learn. A landmark paper describing the cortical connections underlying this process was published by Peter Strick and colleagues at the University of Pittsburgh. The title of the paper is "The mind-body problem: circuits that link the cerebral cortex to the adrenal medulla."

## [00:44:08] Adrenal "Burnout"?; Exercise to Increase Energy, Adrenaline

There is a persistent myth in popular health culture about "adrenal burnout" or "adrenal fatigue" — the idea that doing too much intense activity, whether physical or mental, depletes the adrenal glands to the point where they can no longer produce sufficient adrenaline. Adrenal insufficiency is indeed a real medical condition — it is called Addison's disease — but it is caused by autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex or other pathological processes, not by exercise or stress.

Exercise does not deplete the adrenal glands. In fact, the adrenal glands are extraordinarily robust organs that are designed to respond to physical demand. Regular exercise actually improves the responsiveness of the adrenal glands to stimulation, not depletes them. The idea that exercise can burn out your adrenals is not supported by the peer-reviewed literature. If you feel chronically fatigued after a period of intense training, the cause is almost certainly insufficient sleep or nutrition, not adrenal burnout.

## [00:48:20] Tool: Core, Compound Movements; Mind-Body Connection

Not all exercises are equal in terms of their ability to drive the adrenal-medullary system and raise epinephrine levels. The exercises that most robustly activate the adrenal system are compound movements that engage the core musculature. This makes physiological sense: compound exercises — such as squats, deadlifts, bench press, overhead press, dips, pull-ups, and rows — recruit more muscle mass simultaneously, create more total metabolic demand, and more powerfully stimulate the motor cortex. The greater the motor cortex activation, the greater the signal to the spinal cord IML, and thus the greater the adrenaline release from the adrenal medulla.

Core engagement is particularly important because the core musculature is heavily represented in the motor cortex and because the cortical inputs to the adrenal medulla are especially strong for movements involving the trunk and core. This is part of what Peter Strick's work has revealed: the mind-body connection via the adrenal medulla is heavily weighted toward core and compound movements.

The practical implication: if you want to maximize the brain-energizing and focus-enhancing effects of your workout, prioritize compound movements. Isolation exercises are still valuable, but structuring your workouts around compound movements will give you the biggest arousal-enhancing benefits.

## [00:55:45] Bones, Osteocalcin, BDNF & Hippocampus; Tool: Jump Training

One of the most exciting recent discoveries in exercise neuroscience is the role of bones in brain health. Bones are not just structural supports — they are endocrine organs. When bones undergo mechanical loading — particularly the kind of impact loading that comes from jumping — they release a hormone called osteocalcin. Osteocalcin crosses the blood-brain barrier and has direct effects in the brain.

In the hippocampus specifically, osteocalcin promotes the growth of new neurons (neurogenesis) and the formation of new synaptic connections. The hippocampus is the brain structure most critical for forming new memories, and it is one of the few brain regions in adult mammals where new neurons continue to be generated throughout life. Osteocalcin appears to act in part via brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) — a growth factor that promotes the survival, growth, and synaptic integration of neurons.

The practical implication is that jump training — exercises that involve jumping, landing, and absorbing impact — is uniquely valuable for brain health because it stimulates osteocalcin release from bones. The key is to include controlled eccentric loading during landing, meaning that when you land from a jump, you absorb the impact in a controlled, deliberate way rather than just collapsing. This eccentric control during landing is protective against injury and also maximizes the mechanical signal to the bones. Peter Attia has written and spoken extensively about the importance of eccentric loading and fall prevention in aging — the same eccentric control that prevents injury is what drives the most robust osteocalcin release.

## [01:01:30] Exercise, Fuel, Multifactorial Pathways; BDNF & Activity

BDNF — brain-derived neurotrophic factor — is one of the most important molecules for long-term brain health. It promotes the formation of new synaptic connections, supports the survival of existing neurons, and enhances neuroplasticity broadly. Exercise is one of the most powerful stimulators of BDNF in the human brain.

However, it's important to understand how BDNF works. BDNF is activity-dependent: it is released preferentially by neurons that are already electrically active, and it works best on neurons that are already active. This means that BDNF released in the brain during or after exercise does not non-selectively grow neurons everywhere — it promotes the growth and stabilization of circuits that are being used. This is one reason why pairing exercise with learning is so effective: the BDNF released during exercise will preferentially support the circuits involved in whatever you are doing cognitively around the time of exercise.

One important caveat: a lot of what was previously attributed to direct exercise-induced BDNF in the brain may actually be mediated by osteocalcin from the bones. That is, bones release osteocalcin during exercise, osteocalcin crosses the blood-brain barrier and acts on hippocampal neurons, and this action is in part through stimulating BDNF signaling. This doesn't change the practical message — exercise increases effective BDNF activity in the brain — but it does add mechanistic nuance.

## [01:05:06] Lactate, Astrocytes & Brain Function; VEGF & Brain Health

Lactate, often mischaracterized as a waste product, is actually a preferred fuel for neurons in the brain during intense exercise. Here is how it works: during intense exercise, muscles produce lactate as a byproduct of anaerobic glycolysis. This lactate enters the bloodstream and crosses the blood-brain barrier. In the brain, astrocytes — the glial cells that support neurons — take up glucose and convert it to lactate via a process called aerobic glycolysis. This astrocyte-produced lactate is then shuttled to active neurons as fuel. During intense exercise, the lactate from muscles supplements this astrocyte-derived lactate, essentially providing extra fuel to support high levels of neuronal activity.

Lactate also stimulates the production and release of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). VEGF is critically important for brain health because it promotes the growth of new blood vessels in the brain (angiogenesis) and, crucially, it strengthens the blood-brain barrier. A compromised blood-brain barrier is one of the hallmarks of Alzheimer's disease and other neurodegenerative conditions. Exercise-induced VEGF helps maintain the integrity of the blood-brain barrier, which is a key mechanism by which regular exercise protects against neurodegeneration.

Lactate also appears to have a role in appetite suppression — it acts on specific neurons in the hypothalamus to reduce hunger. This may be one reason why intense exercise tends to suppress appetite acutely.

## [01:11:17] Tools: Zone 2, High-Intensity Training, Time Under Tension Training

Now let's talk about how to put all of this together into a weekly exercise routine that maximizes brain health and performance. I think of this in terms of five key training components:

**Component 1: Zone 2 / Long Slow Distance Cardio.** This is low-intensity, steady-state cardiovascular exercise — the kind where you can maintain a conversation but feel like you're working. A brisk walk, easy jog, cycling, swimming — anything that elevates heart rate into the Zone 2 range (roughly 60-70% of max heart rate) for 45 to 75 minutes. Do this at least once per week, ideally two to three times. Zone 2 cardio maximizes cardiovascular health, promotes cerebral blood flow, and provides a sustained, moderate arousal stimulus. It is also the mode of exercise with the most evidence for long-term brain health, including hippocampal volume preservation.

**Component 2: High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT).** At least one HIIT session per week. HIIT — involving brief bouts of maximal or near-maximal effort followed by rest periods — produces the most potent lactate surge, the greatest BDNF release, and the most powerful VEGF stimulus. It also creates the most robust arousal response. Keep it to one session per day, and monitor for signs of overtraining.

**Component 3: Time Under Tension (TUT) Resistance Training.** This is resistance training where the goal is sustained muscle contraction throughout the set. The key features: never set the weight down mid-set; use a slow, controlled eccentric (lowering phase); aim for the muscle to be under tension continuously for 30-90 seconds per set. TUT training drives a different hormonal and metabolic response than standard resistance training, and it is particularly effective for the long-term hormonal benefits of resistance training.

**Component 4: Explosive Jumping with Eccentric Control.** This is jump training designed to stimulate osteocalcin release: box jumps, jump rope (including high knees and double-unders), jumping jacks, or any plyometric exercise. The critical feature is controlled eccentric landing — absorbing the impact in a deliberate, controlled way. Start very conservatively if you have any joint issues. Progress slowly. This component is specifically for osteocalcin release, bone health, and the bone-to-brain signaling axis.

My personal protocol involves six workouts per week: three cardiovascular and three resistance training sessions. This is detailed in the Foundational Fitness Protocol available on hubermanlab.com/newsletter.

## [01:21:37] Tools: Time Under Tension; Explosive Jumping, Eccentric Control Training

To expand on the Time Under Tension approach: within each of my three resistance training sessions, roughly one-third of the work is TUT-focused. I start with compound movements (squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows) where I use heavier weights in the 4-8 rep range with a fast concentric (lifting phase) and a slow, controlled eccentric (lowering phase) — but I do not put the weight down between reps. Then I transition to isolation exercises (single-joint movements like bicep curls, leg extensions, lateral raises) where I maintain continuous tension on the muscle, never setting the weight down mid-set, and focus on a sustained burn.

For the explosive jumping component: start conservatively. Box jumps are excellent — jump up to the box and step down rather than jumping down, especially if you're new to this. Jump rope is an excellent option as well. Progress to more challenging variations — high knees, double-unders — over weeks to months. The goal is not to do extreme plyometrics; the goal is to include loaded, impactful jumping with controlled landing at least a few times per week.

If you are new to jump training or have joint concerns, jumping jacks are an extremely low-impact way to start. Even these produce some mechanical loading of the bones and will stimulate some osteocalcin release.

## [01:25:30] Injury & Exercise, Illness

Injury is one of the biggest threats to long-term exercise consistency, and therefore one of the biggest threats to long-term brain health. Research shows that after approximately 10 days without exercise, measurable decrements in brain oxygenation begin to appear. This means that if you are forced to stop exercising due to injury, the clock starts ticking relatively quickly on the brain-health benefits you have built up.

The implication is profound: injury prevention should be a primary concern in any exercise program. Never sacrifice form for weight. Progress loading conservatively. Prioritize movement quality over movement quantity. If something hurts, stop doing it and address the issue before continuing. An injury that sidelines you for two weeks is far more costly — both for physical and brain health — than several months of slightly lighter training.

Regarding illness: do not exercise when you are sick, particularly with anything that has systemic symptoms (fever, body aches, significant fatigue). Exercise when ill can prolong illness and cause complications. Missing one week of exercise due to illness is not going to meaningfully compromise your brain health. Missing two weeks due to training through an illness that becomes more serious is much more costly.

## [01:28:09] Sleep; Injury, Sleep-Deprivation & Exercise

Sleep is one of the most important modulators of exercise's effects on the brain. Many of the long-term brain-health benefits of exercise are realized during sleep — specifically during slow-wave (deep) sleep, where memory consolidation and cellular repair occur, and during REM sleep, where emotional processing and certain forms of memory consolidation (especially procedural and emotional memories) are processed.

One particularly interesting finding: HIIT improves the quality and quantity of slow-wave sleep. So intense exercise creates a demand for recovery that the body meets by increasing deep sleep.

If you have had a poor night's sleep, should you still exercise? Generally yes, but with moderation. Research shows that exercise offsets some of the cognitive impairment caused by sleep deprivation. Even after a bad night's sleep, a moderate bout of exercise will help restore alertness, focus, and cognitive performance. However, very intense exercise on a sleep-deprived day can be counterproductive — it adds additional stress to an already-stressed system. On sleep-deprived days, opt for moderate intensity: a Zone 2 run or a moderate-intensity resistance session, rather than a maximal HIIT effort.

Exercise timing relative to sleep also matters. Exercising earlier in the day — ideally in the morning — combined with morning light exposure tends to improve both the quantity and quality of REM sleep. This matters for memory consolidation, and it is one reason why people who exercise regularly in the morning often report not just better physical performance but improved cognitive performance and mood throughout the day. Matthew Walker, who has done an excellent six-episode series on this podcast, has covered many of these mechanisms in depth.

## [01:33:51] SuperAgers, Anterior Mid-Cingulate Cortex, Grit & Persistence

One of the most exciting areas of research in the neuroscience of aging is the study of so-called "SuperAgers" — people who are 80 years old or older but who maintain cognitive function that is equivalent to or better than people in their 50s or 60s. What distinguishes SuperAgers from age-matched peers who do not maintain this cognitive sharpness?

One of the key brain structures that differentiates SuperAgers is the anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC). In most people, the volume of the aMCC shrinks with age. In SuperAgers, the aMCC not only doesn't shrink — in some cases it actually grows larger into their later years. The aMCC is the main brain structure that can be tied to the phenomenon of super-aging.

What does the aMCC do? Research, much of it summarized in a landmark review by Lisa Feldman Barrett titled "The tenacious brain: how the anterior mid-cingulate cortex contributes to achieving goals," reveals that the aMCC is the neural substrate of grit, willpower, tenacity, and goal persistence. Specifically:

Spontaneous aMCC activity predicts grit — the psychological phenomenon of leaning into challenge. Greater aMCC activity is associated with higher levels of persistence. aMCC signal tracks willingness to exert more effort. aMCC activity increases during effort magnitude estimation — even when you're just gauging how much effort something is going to take. And perhaps most dramatically, direct stimulation of the aMCC by Stanford neurosurgeon Joe Parvizi caused subjects to feel an impending challenge and a powerful determination to push through it — what subjects described as a sense of "I can do this" combined with a feeling of something hard coming.

Successful dieters increase aMCC volume; people who fail to reach their goals show shrinkage of the aMCC. This brain area is agnostic to the specific domain of challenge — what matters is that the activity involves something genuinely difficult that you would rather not do.

## [01:42:04] Tool: Embrace Challenges; Deliberate Cold Exposure, Rope Flow

This brings us to the fifth and in many ways most important component of a brain-health exercise protocol: do something you genuinely do not want to do. At least once per week, do a form of physical exercise (or physical challenge) that is psychologically and physically difficult for you — something safe, but something you would sincerely prefer to avoid.

The aMCC is activated and grown by doing things you don't want to do. It doesn't matter what the specific activity is, as long as: (1) it is genuinely hard for you — you would prefer not to do it; (2) it is physically and psychologically safe; (3) you do it anyway.

For me, the activity I am incorporating is deliberate cold exposure — specifically, cold plunge or cold shower when I am not already hot and sweaty. I love heat; I love saunas. I don't particularly enjoy the cold. But forcing myself to walk toward the cold plunge, take off the lid, and get in — even the anticipation of doing it activates my aMCC, because that anticipation is "effort magnitude estimation." Once I'm in, I do it for 1-3 minutes.

I am also incorporating rope flow — a form of coordinated movement where you move a rope in complex patterns in front of, behind, and around your body. It is not jump rope (you don't jump through it), but rather a flowing, coordinated practice that demands precise motor patterns I don't yet have. The reason I chose it: I actively don't want to do it. I find it awkward and frustrating. That resistance is exactly the signal that it is right for aMCC activation.

Other options: anything you dislike but can do safely. David Goggins, who has been on this podcast, has spoken extensively about doing the things you hate as a method of building mental resilience. The neuroscience now validates that approach at the level of specific brain structures.
