# Episode 253: How to Use Exercise to Improve Your Brain's Health, Longevity & Performance

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman explains how different forms of exercise — cardiovascular and resistance training, low and high intensity — affect brain function both acutely (during and immediately after a single session) and chronically (over weeks and months of consistent training). The central mechanism is arousal: elevated epinephrine and norepinephrine from exercise account for roughly 60–70% of exercise's positive effects on the brain, governing everything from memory encoding to executive function. Beyond arousal, the episode traces additional pathways — osteocalcin from bone impact loading driving hippocampal BDNF and neurogenesis, lactate fueling neurons and triggering VEGF to strengthen the blood-brain barrier, and the bone-to-brain endocrine axis — and shows how all of these synergize with sleep for long-term neuroplasticity. A complete five-component weekly protocol is built up across the episode: Zone 2 cardio, HIIT, time-under-tension resistance training, explosive jumping with eccentric control, and a weekly session of something you genuinely do not want to do. The final section covers the anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC) as the neural substrate of grit and SuperAging, establishing that regularly overriding reluctance to do hard things is itself a primary brain-health intervention.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Arousal drives ~60–70% of exercise's brain benefits.** Epinephrine and norepinephrine — the core arousal neurochemicals — explain the vast majority of both acute and chronic cognitive improvements from exercise. This single insight reframes how to time and structure training for maximum cognitive benefit. → [00:11:51](transcript.md#001151-exercise--positive-impact-on-brain-performance-arousal)

2. **Adrenaline at learning time strengthens memory.** Larry Cahill and James McGaugh's cold-pressor/adrenaline studies show that elevated arousal before, during, or shortly after a learning episode produces richer, more detailed, more confident, and more durable memory encoding. The mechanism is adrenaline-driven, not any other feature of exercise. → [00:11:51](transcript.md#001151-exercise--positive-impact-on-brain-performance-arousal)

3. **Arousal signals the brain that the current moment is worth encoding.** Norepinephrine and epinephrine effectively tell the brain "this is important — store it." This is the same mechanism behind flashbulb memories. Any arousal source — exercise, cold exposure, emotional salience — can prime this encoding window. → [00:18:20](transcript.md#001820-learning--arousal)

4. **The motor cortex drives the adrenal medulla via a precise neural circuit.** Exercise sends signals: motor cortex → spinal cord IML (intermediolateral cell column) → adrenal medulla chromaffin cells → epinephrine → bloodstream → vagus nerve → nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) → locus coeruleus → norepinephrine throughout the brain. This is the exact pathway by which movement creates mental alertness. Described in Peter Strick et al., "The mind-body problem." → [00:36:57](transcript.md#003657-exercise-brain--body-energy-adrenaline-norepinephrine)

5. **Adrenal burnout from exercise is a myth.** True adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) is caused by autoimmune destruction, not by exercise or stress. Regular exercise actually improves adrenal responsiveness. Chronic fatigue after intense training is almost always insufficient sleep or nutrition. → [00:44:08](transcript.md#004408-adrenal-burnout-exercise-to-increase-energy-adrenaline)

6. **Compound, core-engaging movements drive the biggest arousal response.** Squats, deadlifts, bench press, overhead press, pull-ups, rows, and dips maximally activate the motor cortex and therefore the cortical-to-adrenal pathway. The core musculature is especially heavily represented in this circuit per Strick's work. These should anchor any brain-health-focused resistance training program. → [00:48:20](transcript.md#004820-tool-core-compound-movements-mind-body-connection)

7. **Bones are endocrine organs: impact loading releases osteocalcin, which promotes hippocampal neuroplasticity.** Mechanical impact from jumping triggers osteocalcin release from bone. Osteocalcin crosses the blood-brain barrier and drives neurogenesis and synaptic connectivity in the hippocampus, partly via BDNF signaling. Controlled eccentric landing maximizes this signal while preventing injury. → [00:55:45](transcript.md#005545-bones-osteocalcin-bdnf--hippocampus-tool-jump-training)

8. **BDNF is activity-dependent and preferentially supports circuits being used.** BDNF released during exercise grows and stabilizes whichever circuits are electrically active — not non-selectively everywhere. Pairing exercise with learning amplifies neuroplastic benefit for the specific material being studied. Much exercise-driven BDNF in the hippocampus is actually mediated through osteocalcin. → [01:01:30](transcript.md#010130-exercise-fuel-multifactorial-pathways-bdnf--activity)

9. **Lactate is neuronal fuel and a blood-brain barrier protector.** During intense exercise, muscle-produced lactate crosses the BBB and fuels neurons. Lactate also triggers VEGF production, which promotes cerebrovascular growth and strengthens the blood-brain barrier. BBB compromise is a hallmark of Alzheimer's and neurodegeneration; exercise-induced VEGF protects against this. Lactate also acutely suppresses appetite via hypothalamic neurons. → [01:05:06](transcript.md#010506-lactate-astrocytes--brain-function-vegf--brain-health)

10. **One HIIT session per day improves executive function; two impairs it.** A single HIIT session enhances executive function and cognitive flexibility (Stroop task performance). A second HIIT session in the same day depletes cerebral blood flow and diminishes cognitive performance. More is not better. → [00:29:16](transcript.md#002916-tool-high-intensity-training--cognitive-flexibility-over-training)

11. **Exercise "snacks" produce meaningful cognitive benefit without a full workout.** Six-second all-out sprints × 6 rounds with one-minute rest is the best-studied short protocol. Even jumping jacks, air squats, or a brisk stair climb for 1–3 minutes boosts arousal and restores focus, and can be inserted throughout a workday without disrupting workflow. → [00:33:32](transcript.md#003332-long-term-brain-health-tool-exercise-snacks-cognitive-performance)

12. **After ~10 days without exercise, brain oxygenation decrements appear.** This makes injury prevention a primary brain-health priority. Never sacrifice form for load. Avoid training through systemic illness (fever, body aches) — missing one week is far less costly than a prolonged injury from pushing through illness. → [01:25:30](transcript.md#012530-injury--exercise-illness)

13. **Sleep mediates many long-term brain benefits of exercise.** HIIT improves deep slow-wave sleep quality, where memory consolidation and cellular repair occur. Morning exercise plus morning light improves REM quantity and quality. On sleep-deprived days, keep exercise moderate — exercise offsets some cognitive impairment from poor sleep, but maximal HIIT on a sleep-deprived day adds excessive stress to an already-stressed system. → [01:28:09](transcript.md#012809-sleep-injury-sleep-deprivation--exercise)

14. **SuperAgers maintain or grow anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC) volume; non-SuperAgers lose it.** The aMCC is the neural substrate of grit, willpower, tenacity, and goal persistence. Spontaneous aMCC activity predicts grit; direct stimulation by Joe Parvizi caused subjects to feel an impending challenge plus a powerful determination to push through it. Successful dieters increase aMCC volume; those who fail to reach goals show aMCC shrinkage. → [01:33:51](transcript.md#013351-superagers-anterior-mid-cingulate-cortex-grit--persistence)

15. **Doing things you don't want to do grows the aMCC.** The aMCC is agnostic to the specific activity — what activates and grows it is genuine reluctance followed by action. The anticipation of doing something hard counts as "effort magnitude estimation" and already begins activating the aMCC. At least one weekly session of something you would sincerely prefer to avoid is a fifth key component for brain longevity. → [01:42:04](transcript.md#014204-tool-embrace-challenges-deliberate-cold-exposure-rope-flow)

16. **A complete five-component weekly protocol addresses all major brain-health mechanisms.** Zone 2 (cerebral blood flow, hippocampal volume), HIIT (BDNF, VEGF, lactate, arousal), time-under-tension resistance training (hormonal and metabolic), explosive jumping with eccentric control (osteocalcin-to-BDNF axis), and embrace-challenges sessions (aMCC growth). Together these cover neuroplasticity, neurogenesis, neuroprotection, and cognitive longevity. → [01:11:17](transcript.md#011117-tools-zone-2-high-intensity-training-time-under-tension-training)

## Tools & Protocols

### Zone 2 / Long Slow Distance Cardio

- **What:** Low-intensity steady-state cardiovascular exercise — brisk walk, easy jog, cycling, swimming — at roughly 60–70% max heart rate. Conversational pace: able to speak in full sentences but clearly working.
- **How:** 45–75 minutes per session. At least 1× per week, ideally 2–3×. Can be stacked adjacent to resistance training days.
- **When:** Any time of day; morning preferred for REM-sleep benefit when combined with morning light.
- **Ref:** [01:11:17](transcript.md#011117-tools-zone-2-high-intensity-training-time-under-tension-training)

### High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)

- **What:** Brief maximal or near-maximal effort intervals with rest periods — sprints, cycling intervals, rowing. Produces peak lactate, BDNF, VEGF, and arousal response.
- **How:** At least 1× per week. Maximum 1 session per day. Best-studied short protocol: 6-second all-out sprints × 6 rounds, 1-minute rest between rounds. Monitor for overtraining signals (declining mood, motivation, cognition).
- **When:** Not a second session on the same day; not maximal on severely sleep-deprived days.
- **Ref:** [01:11:17](transcript.md#011117-tools-zone-2-high-intensity-training-time-under-tension-training)

### Time Under Tension (TUT) Resistance Training

- **What:** Resistance training with sustained continuous muscle contraction throughout the set — the muscle is never at rest mid-set. Slow, controlled eccentric phase; no setting weight down between reps.
- **How:** ~1/3 of each resistance session is TUT-focused. Compound movements first (squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows) at 4–8 reps with fast concentric and slow eccentric, no rest mid-set. Then isolation exercises (bicep curls, leg extensions, lateral raises) at 30–90 seconds continuous tension per set.
- **When:** Incorporated into 2–3 resistance training sessions per week.
- **Ref:** [01:11:17](transcript.md#011117-tools-zone-2-high-intensity-training-time-under-tension-training) / [01:21:37](transcript.md#012137-tools-time-under-tension-explosive-jumping-eccentric-control-training)

### Explosive Jumping with Eccentric Control

- **What:** Plyometric exercise involving jumping and deliberately controlled landing — box jumps, jump rope (high knees, double-unders), jumping jacks. The controlled eccentric landing is the key driver of osteocalcin release from bone and the bone-to-brain BDNF axis.
- **How:** Land from each jump slowly and deliberately — absorb impact through the joints rather than collapsing. Beginners: jumping jacks or step-up/step-down box work first; progress to box jumps and jump rope over weeks to months. Several times per week, integrated into any workout type.
- **When:** Any session type; even a few reps mid-workout count. Start very conservatively if joint issues exist.
- **Ref:** [01:21:37](transcript.md#012137-tools-time-under-tension-explosive-jumping-eccentric-control-training)

### Exercise Snacks

- **What:** Brief bouts of intense movement — jumping jacks, air squats, brisk stair climbs, or 6-second all-out sprints — inserted throughout the workday without a dedicated session.
- **How:** Even 1–3 minutes of high-effort movement provides meaningful arousal and cognitive restoration. Best-studied protocol: 6-second all-out sprints × 6 rounds, 1-minute rest.
- **When:** Whenever cognitive focus is waning; also useful immediately before or after a focused learning block.
- **Ref:** [00:33:32](transcript.md#003332-long-term-brain-health-tool-exercise-snacks-cognitive-performance)

### Compound Movements for Adrenal Activation

- **What:** Multi-joint exercises that engage the core and large muscle groups — squats, deadlifts, bench press, overhead press, pull-ups, rows, dips.
- **How:** Prioritize as the foundation of every resistance training session. Heavy enough to require full effort; never so heavy that form breaks down.
- **When:** Every resistance training session.
- **Ref:** [00:48:20](transcript.md#004820-tool-core-compound-movements-mind-body-connection)

### Embrace Challenges (aMCC Training)

- **What:** A weekly session or activity that is physically and psychologically difficult — something you would sincerely prefer to avoid but that is safe. Options include: deliberate cold exposure (cold plunge or cold shower, 1–3 minutes), rope flow, a workout modality you dislike, or any physically challenging practice that feels genuinely unpleasant.
- **How:** The act of choosing to start — even the anticipation — activates the aMCC (effort magnitude estimation). Do it anyway. The specific activity does not matter; genuine reluctance followed by action does.
- **When:** At least 1× per week.
- **Ref:** [01:42:04](transcript.md#014204-tool-embrace-challenges-deliberate-cold-exposure-rope-flow)

### Exercise Timing for Learning

- **What:** Using exercise before, during, or after a focused learning block to enhance memory encoding via elevated arousal.
- **How:** Even a brief high-intensity bout (10–20 minutes) before a study or work session significantly improves retention. Exercise after learning can also enhance consolidation. On heavy learning days, schedule exercise adjacent to — not hours away from — the learning block.
- **When:** Any time you need to maximize memory encoding or cognitive performance.
- **Ref:** [00:26:01](transcript.md#002601-exercise--acute-learning)
