**RD:** In terms of states of mind, I think that at the outset it's really important that we also remind listeners that there is a thing called traits, too. With regard to states, we can think of them as organized patterns of activity in the brain that have corresponding organized mental correlates, or subjective correlates. And there are certain states that occur with regularity that are part of our biological rhythms. Most human beings will have states of wakefulness, deep sleep, and REM sleep every day, and that is regulated by well-known biological rhythms. There was a paper I wrote many years ago with my dear friend Daniel Goleman, who I wrote the book *Altered Traits* with. **AH:** The after is the before for the next during. Let's drill into that.

You might think that frequent bouts of anger, which you can think of as a state, can lead to the trait of irritability — which is chronically having a low threshold. ## [00:09:06] Wakeful Brain Activity vs Deep Sleep

**AH:** I'm aware of a lot less information about brain activities and clear definitions of waking states of mind. Do you mind if we talk about this? You know — alpha waves, beta waves, theta waves. Maybe you can educate us on some of the waking brain states.

**RD:** So yeah, we can talk about those oscillations of brain electrical activity. There are broad suggestions for what kind of state they may reflect. And it's important to recognize that you can be showing alpha activity in one part of the brain and beta activity in another part of the brain simultaneously.

In humans we see a broad range of frequencies that go from approximately 1 hertz, one cycle per second, to approximately 40 hertz. Roughly 1 to 4 hertz is delta activity — typically not seen during waking, predominant during deep sleep. There is data suggesting that the density of delta activity or slow-wave activity during deep sleep is actually diagnostic of how restorative that sleep is. And there are some highly novel strategies now using neurostimulation to actually boost slow-wave activity during deep sleep, which may help to potentiate some of the skill acquisition we do during the day — including meditation. We're doing some of that work now.

The next faster brain rhythm is theta activity — roughly between 5 and 7 hertz. Theta activity is often seen during transition from wakefulness to sleep, and it's been associated with certain kinds of meditation. Alpha activity is roughly between 8 and 13 cycles per second — often characterized as "relaxed wakefulness." Beta activity is typically defined as roughly 13 to 20 hertz — associated with activation, particularly in cortical regions engaged in cognitive tasks. And then finally there's gamma activity — especially interesting in meditators, long-term meditators. Gamma has its peak frequency roughly at 40 hertz and is seen during what some have called insight — where someone working on a problem suddenly sees the solution.

## [00:23:05] Types of Meditation, Shifting from Thinking to Being

**AH:** With meditation, can we create some broad bins? And then we can go into specific practices.

**RD:** Yes, we can. We've published papers that offer typologies for classifying different meditation states. One kind of meditation we call focused attention meditation — where you are narrowing your aperture of awareness to a specific object. It could be your respiration, it could be a sound — there's a narrowing of the aperture.

Another awareness practice is what we call open monitoring meditation — where there is no specific focus, but the aperture is broadened. There is no specific intention to focus on any one thing. The invitation is simply to be aware of whatever is arising as it arises. The goal is not to try to get rid of thoughts — our minds and brains are built to generate thoughts. The invitation is to shift from a mode of doing to a mode of simply being.

**AH:** I want to talk about this thing about doing to being, because the language can sound a bit mystical and vague. **RD:** As a neuroscientist, it's related to a set of changes in the brain that involve the default mode network — which is active during mind-wandering, during self-referential processing. And when we shift to a being mode, we are actually changing the activity of the default mode network and the balance of activity between it and networks that are implicated in present-moment awareness. And that involves particularly the insula and other kinds of networks that are attuned to what's happening in our bodies and in our immediate environment.

## [00:46:31] Beginners, Expect Chaos in Mind, Exercise & Lactate Analogy

**AH:** Why do so many people find it challenging to maintain a meditation practice? Five minutes a day is nothing.

## [00:52:47] Tool: Beginning Meditation, Embrace Anxiety; Meta-Awareness, Flow

**AH:** So the protocol for beginners is: 5 minutes a day, expect and embrace the anxiety?

**RD:** Yes — and being aware of the anxiety without being hijacked by the anxiety, without being lost in the anxiety. So being able to see the anxiety as it's arising. And that's training in meta-awareness. Meta-awareness is super important — I actually think meta-awareness is a necessary prerequisite for any kind of human mental transformation.

**AH:** Could you define it?

**RD:** Meta-awareness is the faculty of knowing what our minds are doing. That is one of the really important prerequisites for all forms of mental training. Meta-awareness resides in a network including prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate, and insula.

**AH:** Can flow occur with or without meta-awareness?

**RD:** Yes — a lot of flow occurs without meta-awareness. Rock climbers, artists fully in their creative zone — that's flow without meta-awareness. But you can also have meta-awareness that coexists with something like flow. It's a subtle distinction — you can be fully in the flow while also having a slight background awareness that things are going well.

## [01:58:37] Meditation & Connectivity; Consistency, Prayer; Sleepiness; Meta-Awareness

**AH:** What brain changes are we actually seeing with 5 minutes a day?

**RD:** To be transparently honest, there's been very little imaging work on the 5-minutes-per-day protocol specifically. **AH:** Sleepiness during meditation — what do you do about it?

**RD:** It's a real problem, particularly for beginners. A few things: first, the instruction to be fully upright in your posture. Second, sometimes opening the eyes a little — what we call "soft gaze" where you're not really focusing on anything but your eyes are open — can help. Doing it at a time of day when you're more alert is important. And if you're really drowsy, it's actually okay to stand up. Standing meditation is perfectly fine. The idea that you have to be in a cross-legged position to meditate is a myth.

## [02:05:49] Tools: Pillars of Flourishing; Appreciation Practice, Loving-Kindness Practice

**AH:** You mentioned a course on flourishing. What are the components?

**RD:** The course is built on a framework we've developed on the plasticity of flourishing. It holds that there are four key pillars of human flourishing, each of which exhibits plasticity and can be trained. The first pillar is **awareness** — where mindfulness lives, voluntary attention, meta-awareness. The second pillar is **connection** — the qualities important for healthy social relationships: appreciation, gratitude, kindness, compassion. The third pillar is **insight** — a curiosity-driven understanding of the narrative that all human beings have about themselves. What's really critical for well-being is not so much changing the narrative, but changing our relationship to the narrative — so we can see it for what it is: a set of beliefs and thoughts and expectations. The fourth pillar is **purpose** — not necessarily about finding something grand, but how can we find meaning and purpose in even the most pedestrian activities of daily living?

There are three things we've discovered: first, flourishing is a skill. Second, it's easier than you think. Third, flourishing is contagious.

**AH:** What does a loving-kindness practice actually look like?

**RD:** You start by bringing to mind someone for whom love and care arise naturally and spontaneously — it could be a close friend, a pet, a child. You just let that feeling suffuse you. And then you intentionally try to extend that feeling beyond that person to others — to people you may not know so well, and eventually to all beings. And that's the practice. It's really simple. And again, the data show changes even with just 2 weeks of this practice.

## [02:15:39] Awareness & Insight, Tools: Outside View; Task Connection

**AH:** What's the outside view tool?

**RD:** If you're in a difficult situation — at work, in a relationship — imagine what a person who is different from you, someone you know or a famous person you know something about, would see from their perspective. Just allow yourself to get a taste of how their view of the situation is different from your view. That's it. And that is really helpful in giving us some distance from our own beliefs and expectations — helping us recognize that when we see the world, we're actually seeing our own construction of the world through our filters of beliefs and expectations.

The other practice I'd mention is **task connection** — finding a way to connect even very mundane activities to a sense of higher purpose. How does taking out the garbage connect to what really matters to you? If you have kids and you think about the fact that having a safe clean environment for your kids is something you care about — and you connect taking out the garbage to that — it's actually remarkably effective.

## [02:19:43] Cultivating Flourishing, Familiarity with Resistance

**AH:** Does data from meditators show that the resistance to practice gets easier over time?

**RD:** Yes. One of the things we know is that you become more familiar with what happens — and as you become more familiar, there's less resistance because you know that the initial challenge passes. Like a bike ride where the first 10 minutes are agony for me honestly — but I know that after a while it changes. And I think the same is true of meditation. Over time, you develop a sense of familiarity with the practice that allows you to access it more easily and with less resistance.

## [02:25:23] Psychedelics, Guides, Clinical vs Non-Clinical Use

**AH:** What are your thoughts on psychedelics and whether they can combine with meditation?

**RD:** I'm excited about the new research. There are really promising data from some of the clinical trials — for severe intractable depression, for alcoholism. I'm also completely in favor of this resurgence of research. I'm less sanguine about the use of psychedelics in so-called "normal" people for self-development or flourishing. I think psychedelics can produce a glimpse of a different mode of being which could be helpful — but a lot depends on what happens after that, and how that experience is actually worked with and integrated.

One of the things that deeply concerns me is the relative lack of training of the folks who guide psychedelic sessions. You can look in the United States today and see many major universities offering one-year certificate programs to become a psychedelic guide for people with very little prior training. Whereas someone who is truly qualified to guide a psychedelic session should have probably thousands of hours of their own contemplative practice before they begin to even think about guiding others.

**AH:** Can meditation and psychedelics be combined to achieve more accelerated results?

**RD:** I think they could potentially be used in combination, but the key is the sequence — meditation before and after — and having a really qualified guide. The psychedelic experience may actually provide a glimpse that makes the meditation more accessible afterwards. But it's not clear that this adds more than what meditation alone can do over time.

## [02:32:15] Neuromodulation & Meditation, Sleep; Tool: Pre-Sleep Meditation

**AH:** You mentioned combining neurostimulation with meditation. What device are you using?

**RD:** We're using transcranial electric stimulation with temporal interference — TESI. The basic idea: if you have two electrodes stimulating at a very high carrier frequency, say 15 kHz, and another at 15.001 kHz, so there's just a 1 Hz offset, the geometry of targeting allows us to reach deep brain structures where delta frequency is maximal — specifically structures where slow waves are generated during deep sleep. And the stimulation is delivered during sleep. People cannot feel it. It increases the density of slow-wave activity during deep sleep. And how do people feel in their wakeful subjective life? Better.

**AH:** What about the pre-sleep meditation protocol that anyone can do?

**RD:** Yes — in this study, we're using a micro-randomized design where on some nights participants get a pre-sleep meditation, just 5 minutes before sleep, and on other nights they do not. We want to see what the impact is on slow-wave sleep. And I'll provide a link to that paper.

## [02:37:25] Open Monitoring Meditation & Creativity

**AH:** What are your thoughts on open monitoring meditation for increasing creativity?

**RD:** The data on open monitoring meditation and creativity are honestly pretty limited — in part because the measures of creativity that psychologists typically use are fairly crude. Having said that, I do think open monitoring meditation can really boost creativity, primarily by helping people become more aware of the associative thoughts they have. Being aware of a wider field of thought, without getting lost in any particular train of thought, tends to help. And I believe we have much more creative thought occurring than we give ourselves credit for — it's simply that we forget. Open monitoring practices can help us capture that.
