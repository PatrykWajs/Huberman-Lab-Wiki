# AMA #10: Benefits of Nature & "Grounding," Hearing Loss Research & Avoiding Altitude Sickness

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

This premium-subscriber AMA (episode 154, aired August 30, 2023) covers three main topics: the scientific evidence for spending time in nature and the practice of grounding (earthing), research on sudden hearing loss prevention, and evidence-based strategies for avoiding altitude sickness. The publicly available first ~20 minutes focuses entirely on the benefits of nature and grounding. Huberman evaluates which aspects of nature exposure have strong peer-reviewed support — particularly morning sunlight viewing and proximity to moving water via negative ionization — explains why isolating individual variables in nature research is inherently difficult, and provides practical protocols for outdoor sunlight exposure, zone 2 cardio in nature, and grounding. The hearing loss and altitude sickness content is available exclusively to premium subscribers.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Morning sunlight is the most evidence-backed nature tool:** Getting sunlight into the eyes early in the day — outdoors, never through glass or screens — sets the circadian rhythm, boosts daytime mood, focus, and alertness, and improves nighttime sleep. Viewing morning sunlight also has positive effects on metabolism, either directly or via improved sleep quality. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
2. **Nature exposure produces measurable health improvements:** Dozens to hundreds of peer-reviewed studies show that 10–30 minutes outdoors in any natural environment (park, stream, forest, ocean) three to seven days per week significantly reduces blood pressure and resting heart rate while improving sleep and mood. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
3. **Negative ionization near moving water has real but limited support:** Being near waterfalls, running streams, or oceans may confer health benefits via negative ion patterns in the air. Columbia University School of Medicine has published on this topic; Huberman planned to host the lab director on a future episode. Indoor negative ionization machines produce some positive effects but do not reach the level of benefit shown by actual outdoor proximity to moving water. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
4. **Grounding (earthing) has limited but real supporting evidence:** Proposed mechanisms include electron exchange with the Earth's surface (potentially blocked by rubber-soled shoes) and tactile input through the feet. Studies have attempted to isolate these variables but are not published in top-tier journals. Huberman recommends the practice as pleasant and low-risk. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
5. **Nature's benefits arise from hundreds of interacting variables:** Sunlight, color contrast, wildlife encounters, calming sounds, negative ionization, soil smells, and fresh air all compound — making laboratory isolation of any single variable both difficult and somewhat artificial. Huberman argues this gestalt is precisely why isolating variables diminishes the value of the science. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
6. **Forest bathing (Japanese research) demonstrated real effects:** Controlled studies placing people in forest environments versus non-forest controls showed measurable mental and physical health improvements in the forest group. This inspired the broader practice of bringing plants indoors, though Huberman notes the benefits likely depend on how many plants are present and how much attention is paid to them. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
7. **SAD lamps are a valid but inferior substitute for morning sunlight:** When outdoor morning sunlight is unavailable due to seasonality, work schedule, or location, a SAD lamp used for 5–10 minutes during breakfast has been shown to improve mood and set circadian rhythm — but it is not nearly as effective as real outdoor sunlight. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
8. **Hookworm risk is real:** Grounding should be practiced on clean grass or soil only — never at dog parks or surfaces with animal waste where hookworm transmission is a genuine hazard. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
9. **Combining nature, movement, and social time is the ideal protocol:** Huberman prioritizes a Sunday ruck, hike, or jog in nature for zone 2 cardio, often with family or friends, and also aims for brief morning outdoor strolls on other days. Outdoor resistance training on other days is also noted as an option. [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight Viewing
- **What:** Direct sunlight exposure into the eyes, outdoors, shortly after waking.
- **How:** Go outside and face toward the sun; blink as needed to protect eyes. On overcast days, still go outside — diffuse daylight still works. Do NOT look through windows, windshields, or screens. A late-afternoon or pre-sunset session adds additional benefit.
- **When:** Within the first 1–2 hours of waking, every day or as often as possible.
- **Ref:** [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)

### SAD Lamp (Seasonal Affective Disorder Lamp)
- **What:** A bright artificial light device used as a morning sunlight substitute when outdoor light is inaccessible.
- **How:** Place on the counter during breakfast or morning coffee and look at it for approximately 5–10 minutes.
- **When:** Mornings when outdoor sunlight is not available (winter, night-shift schedules, travel, overcast regions).
- **Ref:** [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)

### Nature Exposure Protocol
- **What:** Regular time spent in any natural outdoor environment (park, forest, stream, ocean, open land).
- **How:** Spend 10–30 minutes outdoors in nature. Combine with physical movement (walking, hiking, rucking) for added cardiovascular benefit. Can be social or solo.
- **When:** 3–7 days per week; Huberman prioritizes Sunday hikes or rucks for zone 2 cardio and also aims for a brief morning stroll on other days.
- **Ref:** [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)

### Grounding (Earthing)
- **What:** Standing or walking with bare feet directly on the Earth — grass, soil, sand, or similar natural ground surfaces.
- **How:** Remove shoes and stand or walk on clean grass or natural soil. Can be combined with morning sunlight viewing for a dual protocol. Avoid surfaces with animal waste (hookworm risk — especially dog parks).
- **When:** Morning is ideal for combining with sunlight exposure; any time outdoors is valid.
- **Ref:** [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)

### Zone 2 Cardio Outdoors
- **What:** Sustained aerobic exercise (rucking, jogging, hiking) at a comfortable aerobic pace where conversation remains possible, performed outdoors in nature.
- **How:** Ruck (walk with a weighted backpack), jog, or hike at a steady aerobic intensity. Can be made social with family or friends; solo is equally valid. Outdoor resistance training on other days is also an option.
- **When:** Huberman targets Sundays; aim for at least once per week.
- **Ref:** [00:01:42](transcript.md#000142-scientifically-supported-benefits-of-nature)
