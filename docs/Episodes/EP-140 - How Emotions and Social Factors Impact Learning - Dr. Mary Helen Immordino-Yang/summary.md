# Episode 140: How Emotions & Social Factors Impact Learning | Dr. Mary Helen Immordino-Yang

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Mary Helen Immordino-Yang, professor of education, psychology, and neuroscience at USC and director of the CANDLE lab, joins Andrew Huberman to explore how emotions, social context, and culture are not peripheral to learning but its very substrate. The conversation covers the hierarchical organization of emotions — from basic visceral body states shared with alligators up through complex social emotions like admiration and compassion — and how those higher-order states uniquely recruit the default mode network (DMN) to build narrative, identity, and meaning. They examine how awe-inducing stories activate the DMN in ways that direct sensory observation cannot, and why "transcendent thinking" is a developmentally acquired skill that peaks in healthy adolescent brain development. The episode traces how the current Western education system actively suppresses intrinsic curiosity by rewarding performance over idea-engagement, and what interdisciplinary, emotionally grounded pedagogy looks like in practice — including a detailed account of Immordino-Yang's own experience teaching science to refugees and immigrants in South Boston. The discussion closes with the neuroscience of mirror neurons (verdict: distinct "mirror neuron" cell types do not exist, but the goal-sharing phenomenon is real), psychological safety as a prerequisite for DMN-mediated creativity, and how developing the disposition to deconstruct one's own assumptions is the only durable protection against tribalism.

## Guest: Dr. Mary Helen Immordino-Yang

Dr. Mary Helen Immordino-Yang is a professor of education, psychology, and neuroscience at the University of Southern California (USC) Rossier School of Education, and director of the USC Center for Affective Neuroscience, Development, Learning and Education (CANDLE). Her research examines how emotions and social experiences shape brain development, identity, and learning in children and adolescents across cultures. She is the author of *Emotions, Learning, and the Brain* (Norton, 2015) and has delivered a widely viewed TEDx talk on the neuroscience of inspiration. Before becoming a researcher, she taught seventh-grade interdisciplinary science at one of the most culturally diverse schools in the United States.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Emotions are the substrate of learning, not a distraction from it.** Whatever you are having emotions about is what you are thinking about — and whatever you are thinking about is what you can learn. If the emotional buzz in school is about performance outcomes (will I pass?), students learn to think about performance, not the ideas themselves. [00:05:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=354s)(transcript.md#000554-inspiration-awe-story)

2. **The brain is fundamentally a body-regulating organ; consciousness and feeling emerge from that.** Antonio Damasio's framework — that the brain's mapping of body states is the substrate for mind and consciousness — underpins the entire conversation. Feelings are not add-ons to cognition; they are its foundation. Immordino-Yang's work is an elaboration of that framework into narrative, culture, and social context. [00:09:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=599s)(transcript.md#000959-brain-body-narratives)

3. **Awe and narrative uniquely activate the default mode network.** When someone must feel into a complex story (e.g., a man eating alone whose spouse just died) rather than observe a physical event (e.g., someone breaking an ankle), the DMN lights up rather than deactivates. This was one of the first demonstrations that effortful narrative processing activates — not suppresses — the DMN. [00:33:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=2010s)(transcript.md#003330-hierarchal-emotion-organization-default-mode-network-story-emotion)

4. **Valence disappears in narrative emotion — meaning replaces it.** For direct physical stimuli, positive vs. negative drives the response. For story-based social emotions (admiration for virtue, compassion for loss), valence becomes secondary. What matters is whether the emotion requires conjuring a narrative, not whether it feels good or bad. Pain in service of meaning becomes desirable; that is how humans endure childbirth, ascetic practices, or sacrifice. [00:33:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=2010s)(transcript.md#003330-hierarchal-emotion-organization-default-mode-network-story-emotion)

5. **Emotional elaboration is a lifelong developmental trajectory.** At age two, a child loves her mother's arm concretely. At age four she can conceptualize that love in terms of ideas ("I love you more than I'm glad there's daytime"). At 80 the stories become richer still. The physiological attachment substrate changes little; what develops is the capacity to elaborate it into layered meaning. [00:15:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=958s)(transcript.md#001558-emotions-durability-lifespan)

6. **Transcendent thinking — inferring what a situation means beyond the observable — is uniquely human and developmentally protracted.** Adolescents are neurologically primed to develop this capacity. CANDLE lab research shows teens who engage in abstract purpose-oriented thinking (about their place in history, society, and the future) show measurably better long-term psychosocial outcomes, mediated by healthy DMN development. [00:46:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=2784s)(transcript.md#004624-emotional-development-lifetime)

7. **Culture shapes perception at the level of low-level attention, not just interpretation.** Kitayama's cross-cultural work shows Japanese and American participants literally notice different things in the same visual scene — a fish in an environment vs. an environment with a fish. What we learn to notice is as much imposed from within as received from without; this extends to social and moral perception as well. [00:46:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=2784s)(transcript.md#004624-emotional-development-lifetime)

8. **Genocide is a failure of narrative, not a failure of basic empathy.** When a group is recategorized as non-human, the empathy circuitry is not broken — it is operating on a different narrative scaffold that excludes the target group. The only protection is systematically developing the disposition to deconstruct one's own assumptions and notice when one's story is being hijacked. [00:57:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=3433s)(transcript.md#005713-narrative-genocide-checking-assumptions-mental-flexibility)

9. **The Western education system actively suppresses intrinsic curiosity at adolescence.** Preschool education correctly leverages natural curiosity. By adolescence — precisely when the brain is developing the neural capacity for complex narrative meaning-making — schooling doubles down on rote performance and standardized assessment, gutting students' drive to become powerful thinkers and contributing to the adolescent mental health crisis. [01:09:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=4192s)(transcript.md#010952-education-deconstructing-beliefs-curiosity)

10. **Skill acquisition should be in service of a driving question, not the goal itself.** A Sudanese immigrant student who had never passed a math class before became fascinated by fractions when they were the tool he needed to engage with Zeno's Paradox (Zeno's walking-to-the-door problem). The cart (skills) must serve the horse (the person's development and their driving question), not replace it. [01:18:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=4712s)(transcript.md#011832-emotion-learning-constructing-meaning)

11. **Good teachers oscillate between expert and novice simultaneously.** The best instructors do not relay stored facts — they reinvent the knowledge visibly in front of students, modeling intellectual curiosity. This ignites the DMN and emotional systems of learners in a way that pure expert delivery cannot. [01:28:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=5339s)(transcript.md#012859-good-teachers-curiosity)

12. **Interdisciplinary, problem-first education builds durable understanding.** Immordino-Yang's seventh-grade curriculum in South Boston (81 languages spoken, post-Rwandan genocide students arriving mid-semester) wove nuclear fission → the Sun → photosynthesis → chemical reactions into a web around a driving question. Students used science to make sense of identity and origin, not just to satisfy an assessment. [01:33:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=5605s)(transcript.md#013325-inter-disciplinary-education-development-culture)

13. **Psychological safety is a neurobiological prerequisite for creativity and civic reasoning.** The DMN — substrate for imagination, alternative perspectives, ethical reasoning, and autobiographical self — is neurobiologically incompatible with vigilance toward social or physical threat. When people feel unsafe, the DMN cannot do its work. This applies equally to classrooms, workplaces, and civic spaces. [02:03:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=7408s)(transcript.md#020328-safety-creativity-default-mode-network)

14. **Civic discourse requires the same neural and emotional skills as good education.** Holding one's views lightly, genuinely engaging with opposing perspectives, deconstructing one's own assumptions, and tolerating uncertainty are learnable dispositions — and exactly what the current education system fails to teach. One does not fully understand one's own position without being able to articulate why a reasonable person disagrees with it. [02:12:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=7935s)(transcript.md#021215-civic-discourse-education-deconstructing-ideas)

15. **Mirror neurons do not exist as a distinct cell type — but the mirroring phenomenon is real.** No special "mirror neuron" cell has been found in humans. What exists is a network of action-planning and outcome-perception regions that activates when you share the inferred goal of another's action. Social learning is mediated by shared goals and cultural context, not by reflexive copying. You cannot "mirror" someone whose goals you do not understand. [02:27:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=8851s)(transcript.md#022731-mirror-neurons-shared-social-experiences)

16. **Development of the person — not transmission of content — should be the goal of education.** Learning content is the means, not the end. The question should be: how is a person changing themselves having learned this? When education is designed around that question, intrinsic motivation and durable understanding follow. [02:35:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=9349s)(transcript.md#023549-cold-exposure-sickness-role-of-education)

## Tools & Protocols

### Leverage Emotional Relevance Before Introducing Content

- **What:** Connect new material to something the learner already finds emotionally meaningful — a personal story, lived experience, or social context that genuinely matters to them.
- **How:** Before introducing a concept, ask "Why would this person care about this?" Frame content through narrative and real-world stakes rather than abstract facts. Start with the driving question the skill will help answer, not the skill itself.
- **When:** At the point of introducing any new concept, in formal or self-directed education contexts.
- **Ref:** [01:18:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=4712s)(transcript.md#011832-emotion-learning-constructing-meaning)

### Use Awe-Inducing Narratives to Activate Deep Learning Networks

- **What:** Expose learners to stories, historical accounts, or scientific discoveries that evoke awe, admiration, or moral complexity — the kind that requires conjuring a narrative rather than just observing a physical event.
- **How:** Select emotionally and morally rich materials (not just informationally dense). Allow unstructured reflection time after exposure. Watch for behavioral markers of transcendent thinking: closed eyes, slowed speech, physical stillness, then ethical reflection ("that's not right," "it makes me realize...").
- **When:** As anchoring experiences at the start of a unit or topic.
- **Ref:** [00:05:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=354s)(transcript.md#000554-inspiration-awe-story)

### Establish Psychological Safety Before Demanding Creative or Critical Thought

- **What:** Explicitly establish that errors, uncertainty, and contradictory ideas are welcome before asking anyone to think openly or take intellectual risks.
- **How:** Model intellectual vulnerability. Reward question-asking over answer-giving. Separate exploratory discussion from evaluative assessment. If people feel they need to watch their back — literally or metaphorically — the DMN cannot do its job.
- **When:** Continuously, as a foundational classroom or team culture practice.
- **Ref:** [02:03:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=7408s)(transcript.md#020328-safety-creativity-default-mode-network)

### Practice Assumption Deconstruction

- **What:** Regularly surface and examine the assumptions embedded in one's own beliefs, especially on complex social or moral topics.
- **How:** Present two well-reasoned opposing views on an issue. Ask the learner to steelman each, then articulate what would have to be true for each to be correct. Ask children "why does that bother you?" and have them write it out — externalizing the reasoning changes the relationship to the belief. (Example from the episode: Immordino-Yang's son's letter about his teacher's behavior chart, later published in *How People Learn II*.)
- **When:** Weekly or at the entry point of any new intellectual domain; applicable from elementary school through adult life.
- **Ref:** [01:56:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=7013s)(transcript.md#015653-reframing-education-deconstructing-assumptions)

### Perspective-Swapping Debate

- **What:** Assign students (or oneself) to argue from the perspective they do not hold on a controversial topic.
- **How:** Identify a genuinely controversial issue. Have participants self-sort by their real view, then swap sides and argue the other position with full effort. The goal is not to change views but to develop the neural and emotional capacity to appreciate how a reasonable person arrives at a different conclusion.
- **When:** In high school or adult civic education contexts; periodically as a standalone exercise.
- **Ref:** [01:56:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=7013s)(transcript.md#015653-reframing-education-deconstructing-assumptions)

### Intergenerational Storytelling for Adolescent Development

- **What:** Pair adolescents with older adults in structured storytelling exchanges to develop transcendent thinking and a sense of historical continuity.
- **How:** Use facilitated programs like the "Sages and Seekers" model — structured sessions where teens ask elders about their life narratives and reflect on what those stories mean for their own futures.
- **When:** During adolescence; particularly effective in school or community settings.
- **Ref:** [00:57:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=3433s)(transcript.md#005713-narrative-genocide-checking-assumptions-mental-flexibility)

### Interdisciplinary Problem-First Curriculum Design

- **What:** Build learning units around a genuine real-world question that cannot be answered from one discipline alone, requiring synthesis across fields.
- **How:** Identify a driving question (e.g., why do people in different places look different? How does energy move through the world?). Map which academic skills (math, writing, science, history) are tools needed to answer it — not the end goal. Students acquire skills in service of a question they care about.
- **When:** Curriculum design at any education level. Particularly powerful for culturally diverse classrooms where students can bring multiple cultural lenses to the same question.
- **Ref:** [01:33:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RViuTHBIOq8&t=5605s)(transcript.md#013325-inter-disciplinary-education-development-culture)
