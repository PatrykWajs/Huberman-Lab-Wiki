## [00:07:07] Anterior Mid Cingulate Cortex Studies: Discussion on Research & Findings

The first question is up: Carolyn A. writes, "I am interested in studies about the anterior mid-cingulate cortex. Would you please share any information regarding those? Thank you for all you do in the advancement of science."

Thank you, Carolyn. The anterior mid-cingulate cortex, for those of you who may not have seen the episode on willpower and tenacity, is a region of the brain that is involved in leaning into difficult challenges of all kinds. This is an area of the brain that gets larger when we embrace effort that leads to a bit of internal anxiety — something that's good for us, an adaptive thing. And this is an area of the brain that gets smaller when we don't engage in challenging endeavors.

We need to define "challenging endeavors" very specifically. Challenging endeavors that stimulate the growth of the anterior mid-cingulate cortex are challenging endeavors that we would prefer not to do. We may want the end goal — we may want the consequence of the exercise, the consequence of the learning, the consequence of whatever the challenge is — but the challenge itself feels uncomfortable. And here of course we want it to be uncomfortable but safe. Challenges that feel difficult and safe are what will grow the anterior mid-cingulate cortex.

Another important thing: for people who successfully complete a diet, pursue fitness goals, finish a degree or exam — the anterior mid-cingulate cortex gets bigger. For people who fail to do the preparation required, it actually shows a bit of atrophy. So the anterior mid-cingulate cortex is an anatomical and neural reflection of willpower and tenacity.

What people get wrong is that the anterior mid-cingulate cortex really responds to things that feel genuinely difficult. If you're like me and you enjoy certain forms of exercise and it's just pure bliss the whole way through — that's not going to grow your anterior mid-cingulate cortex. However, if you add a bit of higher-intensity work — like if I do a long hike on Sunday and I add a couple of hill sprints at the end that I really don't enjoy — that's the sort of thing that's going to stimulate it. Not impending doom, but a feeling of "something challenging is coming, and I'm ready to meet that challenge."

The anterior mid-cingulate cortex was discovered in cases where people receiving neurosurgery for other reasons were having this brain area stimulated. The subjects reported feeling as if something challenging was impending — but the other subjective feeling they reported was feeling like they could lean into it. That's what you're looking for: that feeling of leaning into challenge and friction. If something's too easy or too delightful, it's not going to contribute to anterior mid-cingulate cortex growth.

Those challenges can come from physical endeavors, from language learning, from a hard conversation you've been putting off — as long as the endeavors are safe. Leaning into challenge does seem to activate and grow the anterior mid-cingulate cortex.

Why would you want to do that? There are studies showing that so-called "super-agers" — I don't love that name, they really should be called "super non-agers" — are people who seem to maintain healthy cognitive function much longer than most individuals. Their memory and IQ remain stable well into their later decades, and sometimes even improve. The correlation — and it is correlation — is that these people's anterior mid-cingulate cortex does not atrophy. What the studies point to is that these people are regularly engaging in things that are hard and challenging and have embraced that challenge.

I'm a big fan of trying to do something difficult at least once ideally every day, or at least every week. It doesn't take much — maybe five minutes. That feeling of "oh, I don't want to do this, I can't do this" and then continuing to lean into that challenge safely — that is going to grow that anterior mid-cingulate cortex. We hear so much about brain atrophy and memory loss as we get older; here we're really talking about how to maintain healthy cognitive function, and the anterior mid-cingulate cortex seems to be a barometer of how well we're doing that.

## [00:12:32] Evening Routines and Light: Insights on Managing Light Exposure Before Sleep

Oh, I love this question. I've made a lot of changes in the last year related to this — and yes, I know, there's this guy on the internet who talks about the importance of getting morning sunlight. I've been quite vocal about the importance of getting morning sunlight as soon as possible after waking. Of course, if you wake up before the sun comes out, you wait until the sun comes out. If it's overcast, you stay outside a bit longer. If you want to wake up before the sun comes out, turn on bright artificial lights.

But I've talked a lot about the morning routine and the early afternoon. Evening routine — okay. I'll tell you ideal, and then I'll tell you what I actually do.

I think it's very important that, if possible, you get a little splash of sunlight in your eyes in the afternoon and evening. What we're really talking about is not necessarily seeing a sunset at the beach — though that would be great — but getting some sunlight in your eyes before the sun goes down, maybe 5 to 10 minutes. It's not as important as morning sunlight, but it has a great benefit: it adjusts the sensitivity of the neurons in your retina and brain such that it protects you against brighter artificial lights later in the evening.

Based on a really nice study published in Science Reports: bright light viewed for 15 seconds or more between the hours of 10 p.m. and 4:00 a.m. — for someone on a standard schedule, not a shift worker — can dramatically quash melatonin levels. Melatonin is the hormone involved in feeling sleepy and falling asleep. If you view afternoon or late-afternoon sunlight — even just getting some daylight in your eyes before the sun goes down — that can offset that melatonin reduction by about 50%, which is pretty impressive.

You would still do well to dim the lights in the evening. One thing I've been experimenting with is using red lights. When a lot of people hear "red lights," they think of red light panels — those have their uses, they tend to be very bright. But what I'm talking about for the evening routine is much simpler and cost-effective: get some red bulbs and put them in the bedroom where you read before sleep, or in the room you spend time in during the evening.

Red bulbs at the proper wavelength eliminate all the blue wavelengths that tend to be stimulating for what we call the noradrenergic system — adrenaline, noradrenaline, cortisol. There's a study showing that when shift workers who have to be up at night use red lights, they have less of a cortisol increase than when working under standard fluorescent or LED lights.

So the number one thing: dim the lights in the evening and at night as much as you safely can. And to the extent that you can shift to red lights, that can be helpful. Many people — myself included — find that you get sleepier when you do that. I might do this for the last hour of the evening even if I'm up until 10 or 11. I have a little lamp above my bed; if I'm going to read in bed I use that, and it makes it much easier to fall asleep — probably because of the reduced cortisol associated with eliminating the blue wavelengths.

There's one brand of red light bulb that works on a dimmer, which is important. The company is called Bon Charge — B-O-N, separate word, Charge — and those are dimmable and have the proper wavelength. That's what I use. I have no financial relationship to that company; I buy them with my own money. We're not talking about red light therapy panels — we're talking about red bulbs you put in a standard light fixture to reduce blue light in your environment.

If you don't want to purchase anything new, you can also grab red party lights or Christmas lights — that's going to be better than blue lights.

For screens: there's a shortcut function on your phone where you can triple-click to switch your phone over to eliminate all the blues. This is very different from just using the nighttime function. You go into the Accessibility function, go into Color Filters, and drag down the blues. But you don't want that setting all day — so you set up a triple-click function. When I go to bed in the evening, I triple-click and switch my phone over to red-only. That's completely zero cost. And then in the morning I click three times to turn it off — though I try to get sunlight in my eyes before I look at my phone.

Another consideration: lights that are overhead going into your eyes are very different from lights set low on a surface. The cells that activate the alertness system in the brain via the retina tend to look up into the environment — they're essentially there to view sunlight. So a flashlight shining on the ground looks bright, but you're not getting those photons in your eyes the same way you would if it were pointed at your face. When you have lights that are overhead going directly into your eyes, that's very different from having light on the floor or on a low surface.

This also explains why viewing sunlight through a window takes 50 times longer to activate your circadian system than when you step outside. When you're outside, sunlight comes at you from everywhere. When you're inside, unless there's a beam of light coming through a very clear, open window directly into your eyes, you're not getting the light in the same way.

So to summarize: dim the lights, set them low in the room, switch to red lights — Bon Charge or even red party lights — and if you're going to be on screens, use the triple-click accessibility filter on your phone to eliminate blues. No financial cost.

## [00:46:41] Strength Training for Women: Protocols for Strength Without Hypertrophy

What's the best protocol for strength training without hypertrophy for women? "When I've lifted heavy weights, I tend to bulk up fast. I want to embrace more resistance training without the bulk."

Here's the interesting thing about weight training: you will never get larger than the so-called pump you achieve in a single workout. A lot of people — women in particular — are concerned about getting too big or too bulky. And indeed some women, some men, react very quickly to weight training. They have high fiber density and for whatever reason — could be hormones, but probably also different types of proteins expressed in the muscles and connective tissues — some people just grow faster from weight training.

But here's what we know from 40-plus years of physiological data:
- Very heavy weights in the range of 1 to 3 repetitions generally make people stronger but don't produce much hypertrophy
- 3 to 5 reps: mostly strength, some hypertrophy
- 6 repetitions out to even 30 repetitions, provided sets are going to failure — that's stimulating hypertrophy

Exceptions: a 1-rep set with time under tension of 75 seconds can produce hypertrophy. Weight training that accentuates the negative — the eccentric, the lowering portion — also stimulates hypertrophy.

So if you want to strength-train and don't want to put on size, I would encourage you to explore protocols that Dr. Andy Galpin and I discussed in the exercise series — largely training in the 1 to 3 repetition range after a proper warm-up. Even some training done strictly for power, moving the weight quickly at lighter loads.

Keep in mind that long rest between sets matters too — if you're doing 3 heavy reps but only resting 30 to 120 seconds, you can get a compounding effect that induces hypertrophy. If you react quickly to low-rep training, you may have to increase the weight and lower reps even further, with long rest periods.

One more important note: if you're concerned about overall health and not just powerlifting, make sure you're also doing cardiovascular training — including plenty of walking and the kinds of cardio we talked about earlier.
