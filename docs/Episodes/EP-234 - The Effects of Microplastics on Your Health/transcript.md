## [00:00:00] Microplastics

Welcome to the Huberman Lab podcast where we discuss science and science-based tools for everyday life. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are discussing microplastics. Microplastics are an extremely interesting and important topic that everyone should know about, and the reason is microplastics are indeed everywhere. They are in the air, they are in beverages we consume, they are lining the inside of soup cans, they are lining the inside of paper cups made to hold hot water, coffee and tea.

There are a lot of animal data and indeed some human data showing that microplastics, which consist of particles of different sizes, can be very detrimental to our health. At the same time it's important to realize that as of now we don't have any causal data linking microplastics to specific human diseases. That said, there's a lot of correlative data and today we are going to review those correlative data and most importantly we are going to discuss the various things that we can each and all do to limit our exposure to microplastics, or at least to facilitate the removal of microplastics from our body. Because as we'll soon discuss, you have microplastics in essentially every organ and tissue of your body right now, and you are constantly being bombarded with microplastics.

The challenge for me and indeed for you as well is to frame this topic of microplastics accurately. It's important that we understand they are out there, they are in us, and indeed they can cause serious issues for our health. However we also need to take agency — we need to understand how we can limit what's called the bioaccumulation of microplastics in our organs and tissues. I don't want to be alarmist. Today's episode is not about getting you to be petrified or about developing some sort of hypochondriasis about microplastics. It's designed to inform you about what they are, where they exist, where they exist in particularly high amounts, and the things that you can do to limit their impact on your biology.

I think it's fair to say that we are not going to rid the Earth of microplastics — they are just too pervasive now. The one caveat is that there are certain populations of people — in particular people that are pregnant, or people that have young children, and those young children themselves — that should really strive to limit their exposure to microplastics. So by the end of today's episode you can be confident that you'll understand a lot about what microplastics are, the impact that they are currently having, some of the potential impact that people are starting to investigate, and ways that you can limit their negative impact on your brain and bodily health.

## [00:05:40] Microplastics & Nanoplastics; Ingestion

Okay, let's talk about microplastics. What are microplastics? Microplastics, as the name suggests, are little itty-bitty bits of plastic. How itty-bitty? Well, microplastics range in size from one micron — which is 1/1,000th of a millimeter — all the way up to 5 millimeters in diameter. Okay, so anything in that size range is considered a microplastic. Anything smaller than that — so anything smaller than one micron in diameter — is considered a nanoplastic.

Indeed, there are lots of microplastics floating around in the air. There are lots of nanoplastics floating around in the air. There are lots of both of those things in the ocean. There are a lot of those things in food, especially packaged food. There are a lot of those things lining cups. There are a lot of those things in essentially everything that we consume.

So what does it mean to have all these microplastics and nanoplastics floating around in our environment and going into our body through fluids and foods? Well, there is some serious concern because these microplastics potentially can disrupt cellular health, organ health, and could potentially lead to certain forms of disease. However, I want to also emphasize that your body is incredibly good at dealing with foreign invaders — it's very good at getting rid of stuff that isn't good for it. However, microplastics and nanoplastics have been shown to lodge within specific tissues and stay there for long periods of time.

Now, the argument based on what seem to be a pretty high-quality publication that you may have heard is that every single week we ingest up to a credit card's worth of microplastics and nanoplastics. However, a more recent paper looked at the quantitative analysis with a different method and claimed that they vastly overestimated the amount of plastic that we ingest every week. This newer analysis of the same data claims that the credit card's worth of plastic was an overestimate by a millionfold — in fact it would take 23,000 years to consume enough plastic to lead to that credit card's worth of plastic in our bodies. So we have very discrepant analyses of the same data, and you're starting to get a picture of just how confusing this whole field is.

What's also very clear is that microplastics and nanoplastics are everywhere. If I were a PhD adviser for somebody in toxicology or environmental science and they needed to have a sure-fire publication, I'd probably suggest they work on microplastics — wherever people look for microplastics, they find them. This is true in our environment, in food, in water, and also in our tissues.

## [00:09:38] Microplastics in Human Tissues; Pregnancy, Young Kids, BPA

In the last couple of years there's been an explosion in the number of scientific studies exploring which tissues of the human body — not just animal models, but the human body — contain microplastics and nanoplastics. By examining postmortem tissues, it's been discovered that there are microplastics and nanoplastics lodged in the brain. If you take the brain of a deceased adult human, what you find is that they have about 0.5% of the total weight of the brain from microplastic — about a teaspoon of salt or sugar's worth of microplastics. That might not seem like much, but if you think about how little neurons are — a typical neuron will have a cell body of anywhere from five to eight microns across, up to as much as 100 microns in some parts of the nervous system — a half teaspoon of powder of microplastics and nanoplastics is a lot distributed across different places in the brain.

You'll find microplastics and nanoplastics in other tissues that have a blood-organ barrier. The brain is encapsulated in the so-called BBB — the blood-brain barrier — because your brain tissue doesn't turn over across the lifespan. The neurons you have when you are born are actually far more numerous than the neurons you have at the time when you die. Nature is very smart: it designed a barrier so that molecules that might be dangerous to the brain can't enter. Microplastics and nanoplastics are making it from the bloodstream into the brain.

Two other tissues that have very stringent blood-to-organ barriers are the blood-testicular barrier — because that's where the DNA, the so-called germ cells are, and you don't want things mutating those cells — and the blood-follicular barrier in females, where the eggs come from. Microplastics and nanoplastics can cross both of these barriers. In fact, there was a lot of press about microplastics and nanoplastics being present in every human testicle that was analyzed in postmortem tissue. There's also a blood-follicle barrier in females, and microplastics and nanoplastics can cross that too.

Microplastics and nanoplastics are also found in all the other tissues of the body. I don't think there's a single investigation of human or animal tissue for microplastics where they didn't find them. You can find them in the upper and lower lungs, in the bloodstream from a blood draw, in human placenta, and in what's called the meconium — the first stool that a baby takes, typically within the first 24 hours after birth. This means that microplastics and nanoplastics that mothers are ingesting or that are lodged in their bodies are making their way to the fetus.

A few years ago, bisphenol A — BPA, which is a component of microplastics and a known endocrine disruptor — was banned from sippy cups and food containers for young kids. The FDA in the United States, and European countries as well, had enough concern about this to make it illegal to have BPA-lined sippy cups or food containers for young kids. And yet the fetus clearly is being exposed to microplastics and nanoplastics. This is why at the beginning I mentioned that if you are pregnant, or if you have young children, you want to go out of your way to limit your exposure.

There wasn't much plastic around or in use prior to the 1950s. If any of you have seen the movie The Graduate with Dustin Hoffman, there's the famous scene where someone says "the future is plastics." That movie takes place at a time when plastics were really booming as an industry. Polyethylene, polyurethane — these plastic materials were developed because they were very durable, long-lasting, not biodegradable, not broken down very easily if at all, and certainly not within biological tissues. These plastics went from essentially non-existent in the 1940s and prior to being in pretty much everything involved in manufacturing.

## [00:19:21] Tools: Plastic Water Bottles; Water Filters; Alternative Water Bottles

There are ways that you can limit the long-term accumulation of microplastics and nanoplastics in your system. A few ways that you can really do yourself a service in limiting your exposure to microplastics is to limit your consumption of water from plastic bottles.

There was an analysis of the number of microplastic and nanoplastic particles in bottled water — it was initially estimated that there were about 30,000 of these particles per liter of water. Then imaging techniques for measuring the number of these different particles, particularly the really small nanoplastics, improved. A paper published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences in 2024 showed that the amount of nanoplastics in bottled water was actually vastly underestimated. Rather than 30,000 particles per liter, the reanalysis with better methods showed that it was anywhere from 110,000 all the way up to 400,000 particles per liter, with an average of 240,000 particles per liter. So the amount of microplastics and nanoplastics in bottled water is actually much, much higher than we initially thought.

A very simple way to limit your exposure is to avoid drinking water from plastic bottles — in particular, plastic bottles that have been heated up. You don't know what happened to those bottles en route to the store: they could have sat in the back of a hot truck, in a loading dock, any number of things. You're much better off having either a stainless steel bottle or some sort of ceramic mug, or using glass or some other vessel for water that is reusable and not made of plastic.

The question also arises: how much microplastic and nanoplastic is in tap water? It turns out there's quite a lot, varying by location. The best way to get those microplastics and nanoplastics out of your tap water is to use a reverse osmosis filter. Reverse osmosis filters will get rid of all the microplastics and nanoplastics, though they will also remove some key minerals from the water — so you'll have to remineralize that water. These systems can range from $300 to $600 for a home unit, and many of those units will remineralize the water. If one thinks about the total amount of money spent in a given year on plastic bottled water, the home reverse osmosis filter with remineralization will actually likely save on costs.

## [00:26:57] Tool: Sea Salt

Another major source of microplastics that was very surprising to me is sea salt. This stuff is getting out into the ocean — there's a lot of plastic in the ocean. Sea salt comes from the ocean, so a simple solution is if you're going to use salt, focus on something like pink Himalayan salt or salt that comes from a non-marine source. It's some of the best salt out there, it's not terribly expensive, and you would do well to avoid sea salt and get your salt from those other sources. In doing so, you're going to lower your exposure to microplastics and nanoplastics. There are some pretty scary pictures of sea salt under the microscope showing all the little bits of plastic in there.

## [00:30:40] Tool: Canned Soup; BPA, BPS, Phthalates

Another major source of microplastics that was very surprising to me is from the lining of canned soup. There was a study entitled "Canned Soup Consumption and Urinary Bisphenol A: A Randomized Crossover Trial." Bisphenol A is a known endocrine disruptor — it mimics estrogen in ways that can activate or block estrogenic pathways, messing up hormone pathways either by activating them or blocking them. It can also bind to androgen receptors potentially and cause some issues there.

The reason there's lots of BPA in the lining of soup cans is that soup tends to be a little fatty, and the lining helps maintain the flavor and freshness. In this study, people were given either fresh soup or canned soup for five days, then a two-day washout, then the conditions were reversed. The conclusion was wild: consumption of one serving of canned soup daily over the course of five days was associated with more than a 1,000% increase in urinary BPA. That's urinary BPA — people are excreting it — but a thousandfold increase from canned soup is hard to ignore.

My suggestion: unless you have a powerful reason to consume canned soup, don't consume canned soup. The one caveat being that if you can find canned soup that says no BPAs on the container — well, then go ahead. But I should be very clear that a lot of canned products now say no BPA but they contain other endocrine disruptors — BPS (bisphenol-S), which is another endocrine disruptor. So if you see "no BPA," sometimes there's still BPS in there.

Phthalates are something that make plastic and other containers more durable and more flexible, and phthalates have been discussed by people like Dr. Shanna Swan, who has shown that phthalates are known endocrine disruptors in development and likely in adulthood as well.

## [00:34:55] Tools: Plastic Containers & Microwave; Paper Cups & Hot Liquids

Most of you have probably heard that you're not supposed to microwave plastic containers. "Microwave safe" on a container just means it's not going to melt in the microwave — it does not mean that you aren't being exposed to microplastics, nanoplastics, BPA, BPS, phthalates, etc. In general, it's a good idea to avoid putting any kind of plastic into the microwave at least if you're going to microwave food and then consume that food.

Another surprising source of BPAs and BPSs, these endocrine disruptors, and microplastics and nanoplastics is paper cups. Those paper cups that you put hot liquids into — even if they don't have a plastic lid — the lining of the paper cup contains, typically unless it says no BPA and no BPS, lots of BPA and BPS, microplastics, nanoplastics. There was an analysis showing that if liquid heated up to 100°F is put in those containers, it starts to leech out those microplastics, nanoplastics, BPA, and BPS from the cup linings.

So try and remember to bring your mug with you — your travel mug, your stainless steel mug or your ceramic mug. Try and make those mugs ceramic, stainless steel, or some other vessel that doesn't contain BPAs or BPSs.

## [00:37:34] Measurement Tools & Advancements

Before moving on to what happens when microplastics and nanoplastics make it into tissues like the testicle or the brain, I want to briefly address the imaging tools question — because the discrepancy between the 30,000 and 240,000 particles per liter figures illustrates something really important about science. As tools for measurement get better, so does our understanding.

The reason the numbers jumped from 30,000 to 240,000 is not because the researchers got much better — it's because the tools got much better. There are new imaging techniques using stimulated Raman scattering (SRS) microscopy. What we're realizing is that there are a lot more particles of plastic in different tissues and different things that we're ingesting than previously thought, because we're getting better and better ways of separating those clumps of light into lots of little clumps of light.

The key biological implication is this: little particles can make it across barriers that big particles can't. These little nanoplastic particles are especially concerning because those are the ones found in greatest abundance in the brain, the testes, and the follicle — the tissues that nature and evolution have gone out of their way to protect with very stringent barriers like the blood-brain barrier, the blood-testicular barrier, and the blood-follicle barrier.

## [00:41:29] Nanoparticles & Tissues; Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

There are three major cases of correlative data in humans worth highlighting. The first is a study published in 2021 in the Journal of Environmental Science and Technology that found much higher levels of microplastics in the stool samples of people diagnosed with irritable bowel syndrome compared to individuals who did not have IBS. Now, I want to be very clear: there was no direct causation established. The causality, if it exists at all, could run in either direction — or it's possible that people with IBS are less good at filtering microplastics from the food and liquids they consume. Nonetheless, it's an interesting study, and if you suffer from gastrointestinal distress such as IBS, you'd be especially wise to consider the to-dos and not-to-dos covered in today's episode.

## [00:45:27] Testosterone, Phthalates, BPA & BPS; Women; Men & Sperm Health

The other area of really interesting correlative data relates to reproductive function and hormone health. There's a study entitled "Urinary Phthalate Metabolites Are Associated with Decreased Serum Testosterone in Men, Women, and Children." This is interesting for a number of reasons. First, it emphasizes that testosterone plays key roles in men, women, and children — it's involved in muscle mass, bone density, strength, libido, brain development, genitalia development, and more.

The study found that elevated levels of phthalate metabolites are associated with lower testosterone levels in all those populations. The strongest and most consistent inverse relationships between level of phthalates and testosterone were found among women ages 40 to 60 years. In adult men, the only significant inverse association was also observed among men 40 to 60 years old. One interpretation: there's a cumulative effect of these phthalates over time that reveals itself statistically in men and women once they reach 40 to 60 years.

Phthalates are things included in plastics that house liquids and foods that we eat, or that we cook with, or that simply exist in our environment and are getting broken down and inhaled. These phthalates are there of course to make plastic more flexible and durable, but they are known endocrine disruptors. Dr. Shanna Swan has done beautiful work showing that young animals and potentially humans exposed to phthalates can have a fairly major disruption in what's called the anogenital distance — in boys, a shorter penile-to-anal distance — which correlates with lower sperm counts and reduced sperm motility.

BPA and to some extent BPS and potentially phthalates can dock to androgen receptors as well. The point is that BPAs, BPSs, and phthalates are not good for endocrine function, and they are present in basically all plastics unless it says no BPA or all phthalates removed.

Microplastics and nanoplastics are found in human testes and in semen, and it is now very clear that that's correlated with reduced sperm counts and lower sperm motility. I want to be clear that just because sperm counts are significantly lower in people with certain amounts of microplastics in their testes does not necessarily mean that they're infertile — but the correlation is there.

## [00:55:25] Polyethylene & Plaques; PFAS "Forever Chemicals"; Microplastic Excretion

Another study that got people's attention relates to microplastics, nanoplastics, and cardiovascular disease. This was published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2024. What it found was that polyethylene — a component of many plastics — was detected in the carotid artery plaques of 150 patients, approximately 58% of the ones included in the study. Using electron microscopy, they could see little jagged-edge foreign particles among the plaque macrophages of the cardiovascular plaques. Whether they were the cause of those plaques or contributed to some of the occlusion is unclear, but it's reasonable to assume that they form part of the physical substrate that could impede blood flow through these arteries, which of course leads to cardiovascular events.

Then there are the PFAS — sometimes referred to as "forever chemicals" — because they are very long-standing once they get into your system. Things like perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substances. These are known to cause liver damage, they can damage the immune system, and they are not broken down — they last forever. The microplastics and nanoplastics can act as what are called vectors or carriers of things like BPAs, BPSs, phthalates, and forever chemicals.

Does that mean you can't get rid of them? No — you have a liver. Your liver has what's called phase one and phase two detoxification processes that allow you to break down and get rid of certain foreign products, including microplastics and nanoplastics.

## [01:00:02] Liver-Controlled Detoxification; Tool: Cruciferous Vegetables, Sulforaphane

Let's talk about liver-controlled detoxification. There are two types of liver detoxification processes. Phase one — also called the oxidation phase — involves cytochrome P450 enzymes. It converts toxins into less harmful components that ideally are excreted from the body. Phase two — also called the conjugation phase — involves enzymes that attach molecules to toxins, making those toxins water-soluble and easier to excrete from the body in the form of urine. It neutralizes reactive intermediates from phase one.

The liver plays a primary role in the removal of microplastics and nanoplastics, BPAs, and BPSs from the body. We have some degree of control over phase two of liver-controlled detoxification.

One way to enhance phase two liver-controlled detoxification processes is by increasing your intake of something called sulforaphane, which is present in cruciferous vegetables such as broccoli and cauliflower. Animal studies used supplemented sulforaphane at dosages comparable to the amounts a human might ingest from a large serving of broccoli — a few cups of raw broccoli or raw cauliflower. According to examine.com, supplementation of 0.1 to 0.5 mg per kilogram of sulforaphane in rats has been noted to be bioactive, and they translate that to a human dose of 1.1 to 5.5 milligrams for a 150-lb person, or 1.5 to 7.2 milligrams for a 200-lb person. They note that "these low quantities are likely attainable through raw broccoli or cruciferous vegetable products," while higher dosages may be further beneficial.

If you cook broccoli and cauliflower lightly — light boil, steam, or pan-cook in olive oil — you'll still maintain the sulforaphane in those cruciferous vegetables. For people who don't tend to eat many cruciferous vegetables, you can supplement with sulforaphane. After researching this episode, I opted to start taking 50 milligrams of sulforaphane per day — I'm going to see how that goes.

## [01:08:32] Tools: Fiber Intake, Non-Stick Pans, Carbonated Water; Microplastics & Cancer

The other way that microplastics and nanoplastics can be excreted from the body is in the bowel. One way to potentially increase the amount of microplastics, nanoplastics, BPAs, BPSs, phthalates, and forever chemicals excreted from your body is to make sure that you're getting enough dietary fiber. Fiber can bind lipophilic molecules — molecules that are able to cross cell membranes. Most people can get adequate fiber simply by eating a fair amount of fruits and vegetables.

Avoid non-stick pans. Try to cook mainly with cast iron or ceramic, making sure that those are BPA-, BPS-, and PFAS-free — just look at the packaging.

Carbonated water is another surprising source of microplastics. A few years ago there was an analysis of different popular forms of carbonated water sold in glass containers, and surprisingly there were still meaningful amounts of microplastics in those waters. The concern is that the carbonation process or other aspects of manufacturing introduce microplastics. This doesn't mean you should avoid carbonated water entirely, but it's worth being aware of.

Regarding microplastics and cancer: BPAs, BPSs, and phthalates are not good for endocrine function, and these molecules can actually control gene expression — they activate or block pathways involved not just in cell function but in the genes those cells express. This is particularly concerning for hormone-dependent cancers. Tissues that turn over cells a lot — such as the testes (producing sperm), follicle and eggs, breast tissue — are common sites of cancer. Tissues that turn over quite a bit, because of the involvement of the cell cycle, are particularly vulnerable to endocrine or hormone disruption.

## [01:15:05] Tool: Sweating & Toxin Removal

Sweating is another way to excrete toxins including BPAs, BPSs, and phthalates from the body. There's some evidence that sweating — whether from exercise, a sauna, or other means — can meaningfully contribute to the removal of these compounds through sweat. This is not to say that you should sweat excessively or that sweating is a cure-all, but it is one more pathway for toxin removal that you have some control over. Exercise-induced sweating in particular is beneficial because it also confers all the cardiovascular, metabolic, and neurological benefits of exercise.

## [01:18:21] Tools: Packaged Foods; Clothing Overconsumption & Laundry

Packaged foods are a major source of microplastic exposure. The more you can shift toward whole foods — fresh fruits, vegetables, meats purchased from a butcher rather than in plastic packaging — the better. Reducing your overall consumption of packaged foods reduces your microplastic exposure.

Clothing overconsumption and laundry are another underappreciated source of microplastics. Synthetic fabrics such as polyester and nylon shed microplastic fibers when they are washed. These fibers get into the wastewater and from there into the broader environment. Ways to reduce this include washing synthetic clothing less frequently, using a laundry bag designed to catch microplastic fibers (such as a Guppy Bag or similar), washing at lower temperatures, and reducing overall consumption of synthetic fast fashion. Choosing natural fabrics like cotton, wool, and linen where possible also reduces shedding.

## [01:25:11] Tools: Microwave Popcorn, Toothpastes

Microwave popcorn bags are lined with PFAS chemicals — forever chemicals — that help make the bags grease-resistant. When you heat that bag in the microwave, those compounds leach into the popcorn. So if you're going to eat popcorn, it's better to pop it on the stove in a pot or in an air popper.

Some toothpastes contain microbeads — small plastic particles — as an abrasive agent. While many countries have now banned microbeads in rinse-off cosmetics, they are still present in some products. Check your toothpaste label and choose one that doesn't contain plastic microbeads. More broadly, the fluoride in toothpaste is beneficial, but the vehicle it comes in matters too.

## [01:27:47] Developing Brain & Microplastics, ADHD, Autism

Regarding the developing brain: the argument has been made — I'm not making this argument, but the argument has been made — that microplastics and nanoplastics may correlate with things like autism and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder. I don't actually believe that the data there are strong enough to make those arguments at all. However, the presence of microplastics and nanoplastics in the brains found in postmortem tissue is concerning to me in the sense that there's enough of it in there, and the function of neurons in the nervous system is precise enough, that you could imagine — given that these microplastics and nanoplastics are lodged in particular categories of neurons that impact things like reward, motivation, and movement — that they could be impacting the function of the nervous system. But there's no direct causal relationship, at least not in humans. There's some interesting data in animal models.

There's evidence that microplastics can activate microglia — the brain's resident macrophages, or immune cells. When microglia are chronically activated, they can create a neuroinflammatory state. It's plausible that this neuroinflammatory state, combined with the lodging of microplastics in dopaminergic and other neural circuits, could have downstream effects on behavior and development — but this remains speculative and the human causal data are not there yet.

## [01:32:19] Tool: Receipts & BPAs; Minimizing Microplastic Exposure

Receipts — the thermal paper receipts you get from a cash register — are coated in BPA or BPS. When you handle those receipts, BPA or BPS can be absorbed directly through your skin. Some people notice this more if they have lotion or hand sanitizer on their hands, which can increase absorption. The simple solution: decline receipts when you don't need them, or if you do take a receipt, don't handle it for long and wash your hands afterward.

To summarize the minimizing microplastic exposure tools: avoid drinking water from plastic bottles; use a reverse osmosis filter if concerned about tap water; avoid sea salt and use pink Himalayan salt or other non-marine-source salts; avoid canned soup or ensure it's BPA-, BPS-, and phthalate-free; don't microwave food in plastic containers; use ceramic or stainless steel mugs instead of paper cups with plastic linings; avoid non-stick pans; reduce synthetic packaged foods; use natural fiber clothing and washing bags for synthetics; avoid microwave popcorn; check toothpastes for microbeads; handle receipts minimally. For removal and detoxification: eat cruciferous vegetables and/or supplement with sulforaphane; get adequate dietary fiber; exercise and/or use a sauna to sweat regularly.
