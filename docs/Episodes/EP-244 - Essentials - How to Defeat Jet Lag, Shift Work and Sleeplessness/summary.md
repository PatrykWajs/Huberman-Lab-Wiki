# Episode 244: How to Defeat Jet Lag, Shift Work & Sleeplessness

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

In this Essentials episode (~27 min), Andrew Huberman distills the science-backed protocols from the full jet lag and sleep episode into actionable tools. The core framework centers on a single concept — the **temperature minimum** — that allows anyone to precisely shift their circadian clock using light, exercise, and food timing, without requiring devices or supplements. The episode covers eastward and westward travel, shift work consistency, melatonin's real role and risks, and how to maintain autonomic stability when forced into irregular sleep patterns such as caring for a newborn.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Circadian rhythms govern far more than sleep.** The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) sets a 24-hour clock that controls metabolism, immune function, mood, and temperature — not just wakefulness. Disrupting it has system-wide consequences. [00:00:45](transcript.md#000045-understanding-circadian-rhythms)

2. **Light is the primary entrainment signal.** Get at least 100,000 lux of light (ideally sunlight) into your eyes before 9:00 AM. Even on an overcast day, outdoor light is 7,000–10,000 lux — far more than indoor lighting. [00:02:26](transcript.md#000226-optimizing-light-exposure-for-better-sleep)

3. **Evening light re-calibrates retinal sensitivity for nighttime.** Viewing sunlight near sunset down-regulates retinal sensitivity, reducing the clock-shifting impact of indoor artificial light later in the evening. [00:02:26](transcript.md#000226-optimizing-light-exposure-for-better-sleep)

4. **Avoid bright light from 10–11 PM to 4 AM.** After 8 PM, it takes very little photon energy to shift your clock — even dim light in this window can cause measurable disruption. [00:02:26](transcript.md#000226-optimizing-light-exposure-for-better-sleep)

5. **Jet lag shortens lifespan — and eastward travel is worse.** Peer-reviewed studies link chronic circadian misalignment to shortened lifespan. Traveling east (phase advancing) is harder than traveling west (phase delaying) because the autonomic nervous system activates alertness more easily than it shuts down. [00:05:51](transcript.md#000551-the-science-of-jet-lag--longevity)

6. **Jet lag has two distinct components: travel fatigue and time zone jet lag.** North-south travel causes only travel fatigue; east-west travel adds true circadian misalignment that must be actively corrected. [00:05:51](transcript.md#000551-the-science-of-jet-lag--longevity)

7. **Temperature minimum is the master reference point for clock shifting.** It occurs ~90 minutes to 2 hours before your average wake time. Light, exercise, and eating in the 4 hours *after* this point advances your clock (earlier wake/sleep). The same inputs in the 4–6 hours *before* this point delays your clock (later wake/sleep). [00:09:32](transcript.md#000932-temperature-minimum-key-to-circadian-adjustment)

8. **Pre-travel clock shifting works — start 2–3 days out.** You can shift your clock by 1–3 hours per day using the temperature minimum framework. For a 9-hour eastward trip, beginning pre-travel light and meal adjustments can compress full adaptation into 24–36 hours after arrival. [00:09:32](transcript.md#000932-temperature-minimum-key-to-circadian-adjustment)

9. **Melatonin helps sleep onset but not sleep maintenance, and suppresses sex hormones.** It inhibits GnRH → LH → testosterone/estrogen. Huberman recommends behavioral tools (light, temperature, exercise) over melatonin as primary interventions. [00:16:50](transcript.md#001650-melatonin-uses--misconceptions)

10. **For trips of 48 hours or less, stay on your home schedule entirely.** Clock shifting is not worth initiating for very short trips; begin adapting only when your stay will exceed 72 hours. [00:16:50](transcript.md#001650-melatonin-uses--misconceptions)

11. **Shift workers must stay on a consistent schedule for at least 14 days including weekends.** Light during alert hours and darkness during sleep hours is the operative principle — apply the same temperature minimum advance/delay logic to the shifted schedule. [00:20:50](transcript.md#002050-shift-work-managing-irregular-schedules)

12. **NSDR (Non-Sleep Deep Rest) is the primary tool for sleep-disrupted states.** For new parents, caregivers, or anyone unable to sleep continuously, NSDR protocols maintain autonomic regulation and allow the nervous system to recover even without full sleep. [00:23:06](transcript.md#002306-sleep-strategies-for-different-age-groups)

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Light Anchor

- **What:** View bright light (sunlight preferred, artificial as fallback) within the first 1–2 hours of waking.
- **How:** Go outside — even on cloudy days outdoor light is 7,000–10,000 lux, accumulating toward the 100,000 lux daily target. On winter or dark days, use a bright artificial light source.
- **When:** Before 9:00 AM (assuming 5–8 AM wake time). Accumulate 100,000 lux total before that threshold.
- **Ref:** [00:02:26](transcript.md#000226-optimizing-light-exposure-for-better-sleep)

---

### Temperature Minimum Clock Shifting

- **What:** Use your temperature minimum (Tmin) — the low point of your body temperature, ~90 min before average wake time — as the anchor reference for deliberately advancing or delaying your circadian clock.
- **How:**
  - To **advance** (earlier sleep/wake — for eastward travel): get light, exercise, and/or eat in the **4 hours after** your Tmin.
  - To **delay** (later sleep/wake — for westward travel): get light, exercise, and/or eat in the **4–6 hours before** your Tmin.
  - You don't need to do all three; any combination works. Clock can shift 1–3 hours per day.
- **When:** Begin 2–3 days before eastward travel. Apply upon arrival using the new local Tmin window.
- **Ref:** [00:09:32](transcript.md#000932-temperature-minimum-key-to-circadian-adjustment)

---

### Eastward Travel Pre-Adaptation

- **What:** Shift light exposure, meals, and exercise earlier before flying east to pre-adapt your clock.
- **How:** If your Tmin is ~4:30 AM and you're traveling 9 hours east, start waking at ~5:30 AM (just after Tmin) 2–3 days before departure. Use artificial bright light at that time. Add light exercise and an early meal to accelerate the shift.
- **When:** 2–3 days prior to departure.
- **Ref:** [00:09:32](transcript.md#000932-temperature-minimum-key-to-circadian-adjustment)

---

### Westward Travel / Delaying the Clock

- **What:** Use light, caffeine, and activity in the evening to stay awake and delay the clock upon arrival in a western destination.
- **How:** After landing (e.g., afternoon California arrival from Europe), view sunlight or bright artificial light from ~6–8 PM. Avoid long unintended naps — they cause overnight waking and worsen the cycle. Moderate caffeine can help push past the afternoon sleep urge.
- **When:** Upon arrival in western destination; through the first few evenings until clock stabilizes.
- **Ref:** [00:09:32](transcript.md#000932-temperature-minimum-key-to-circadian-adjustment)

---

### Temperature Manipulation for Clock Shifting

- **What:** Use hot/cold exposure to deliberately raise or lower body temperature to amplify clock-shift effects.
- **How:**
  - **Hot shower** followed by cooling → reinforces sleep-onset signal.
  - **Cold shower or ice bath** followed by thermogenic rebound → reinforces waking signal.
  - Time these around your Tmin to compound the effect of light and food.
- **When:** When traveling or adjusting to shift work schedules.
- **Ref:** [00:16:50](transcript.md#001650-melatonin-uses--misconceptions)

---

### Shift Work Consistency Protocol

- **What:** Lock your sleep/wake cycle to the same schedule every day for at least 14 days, including weekends.
- **How:** Identify your effective Tmin on the shifted schedule. Apply the temperature minimum advance/delay rules. View maximum light during alert hours; minimize light during intended sleep window. Rule of thumb: "If temperature is increasing, get light. If decreasing, avoid light."
- **When:** Applicable any time a regular shift begins, including informal shift workers (late-night screen use, irregular wake times).
- **Ref:** [00:20:50](transcript.md#002050-shift-work-managing-irregular-schedules)

---

### NSDR for Fragmented Sleep

- **What:** Non-Sleep Deep Rest — a form of directed relaxation (e.g., yoga nidra, body-scan) that restores autonomic tone without requiring full sleep.
- **How:** When unable to sleep (new parent, night shift, caregiving), use a free NSDR audio protocol to calm the nervous system instead of lying awake in high arousal. Huberman cites personal use: woke at 3 AM, did NSDR, fell back asleep, woke at 6:30 AM.
- **When:** Any time sleep is fragmented or unavailable; particularly useful for 3 AM wake-ups where returning to sleep is difficult.
- **Ref:** [00:23:06](transcript.md#002306-sleep-strategies-for-different-age-groups)

---

### Melatonin (Conditional Use)

- **What:** Exogenous melatonin as a sleep-onset aid for jet lag scenarios.
- **How:** Low-dose melatonin taken in the afternoon or early evening at the destination to facilitate sleep onset. Does **not** help maintain sleep. Work with a physician familiar with circadian biology for dosing.
- **When:** Only when behavioral tools are insufficient. Note the endocrine caveat: melatonin suppresses GnRH → LH → testosterone and estrogen, making it unsuitable for long-term or routine use.
- **Ref:** [00:16:50](transcript.md#001650-melatonin-uses--misconceptions)
