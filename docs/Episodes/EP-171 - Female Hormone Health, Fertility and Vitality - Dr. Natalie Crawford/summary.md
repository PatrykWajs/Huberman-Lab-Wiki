# Episode 171: Female Hormone Health, Fertility & Vitality | Dr. Natalie Crawford

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Natalie Crawford, double board-certified OB-GYN and reproductive endocrinologist, delivers a comprehensive master class on female reproductive biology and hormone health across the entire lifespan. The conversation begins in the womb — explaining how a female fetus's egg count peaks at 6–7 million at 20 weeks gestation and declines continuously from birth — and progresses through puberty timing, endocrine disruptors, the menstrual cycle's hormonal mechanics, birth control options and their real effects on fertility, ovarian reserve testing (AMH and antral follicle count), intercourse timing, egg and sperm quality, the IVF and egg freezing process in clinical detail, genetic testing of embryos (PGT-A), INVOcell as a lower-cost IVF alternative, and finally perimenopause and menopause. Alongside the mechanistic science, Dr. Crawford provides specific, evidence-based protocols covering nutrition, supplementation, sleep, sperm freezing, and male fertility factors including advanced paternal age as the strongest population-level risk factor for autism in offspring. The episode closes with a discussion of hormone replacement therapy (HRT) and the updated safety data that challenge the earlier Women's Health Initiative findings.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Egg loss begins in the womb, not at puberty — and IVF does not accelerate it.** A female fetus peaks at 6–7 million eggs at 20 weeks gestation; 1–2 million remain at birth; ~300,000–400,000 remain at puberty. Eggs exit the "vault" continuously regardless of pregnancy, birth control, or ovulation — the process cannot be stopped. IVF and egg freezing rescue eggs that exit the vault that month but would otherwise be lost; they do not pull from the vault itself. [00:04:59](transcript.md#000459-female-puberty--growth-characteristics-height)

2. **Earlier puberty does not shorten reproductive lifespan, but endocrine disruptors are accelerating puberty onset.** Age of first period does not determine how many total eggs a woman has. However, US girls now commonly begin puberty at age 10–11 (vs. 13–15 a generation ago), likely driven by endocrine-disrupting chemicals in fragrances, plastics, and processed foods. Earlier puberty also closes growth plates sooner, reducing adult height. [00:04:59](transcript.md#000459-female-puberty--growth-characteristics-height)

3. **The menstrual cycle is a vital sign; irregular cycles signal hormonal disruption.** The luteal phase is a fixed 12–14 days; the follicular phase varies person to person but should be internally consistent. Cycles that shorten over time (e.g., 28 → 24 days) often indicate declining ovarian reserve. Irregularly irregular cycles — bouncing unpredictably between 24 and 34 days — signal disrupted brain-ovary communication. [00:26:04](transcript.md#002604-menstruation-cycle--hormones-timing)

4. **Estrogen peaks before ovulation, driving libido and shifting partner preference; progesterone then suppresses libido to protect potential implantation.** Studies show women on combined oral contraceptives (which suppress the ovulatory estrogen surge) may shift mate preference toward less immunologically distinct MHC profiles, potentially affecting long-term partner compatibility. The physical sensation of ovulation — follicle rupture — is experienced by some women as a crampy mid-cycle pain called Mittelschmerz. [00:48:56](transcript.md#004856-estrogen-libido--ovulation-mittelschmerz)

5. **Hormonal birth control does not deplete the ovarian reserve; fertility typically returns within 1–3 months after stopping.** The pill suppresses FSH so eggs exiting the vault die without growing — but that loss would have occurred naturally. AMH levels may read up to 30% lower in long-term pill users due to suppressed granulosa cell activity; this is reversible. Exception: Depo-Provera (injectable progesterone) can suppress ovulation for up to 18 months post-injection and should be discontinued 1.5–2 years before attempting conception. Progesterone IUDs may thin the uterine lining enough to delay return of regular periods after removal — remove 3–6 months before trying to conceive if you have been amenorrheic on the IUD. [00:38:08](transcript.md#003808-hormonal-birth-control--ovarian-reserve-amh-testing-fertility)

6. **The birth control pill dramatically reduces ovarian and endometrial cancer risk but may modestly raise breast cancer risk in predisposed individuals.** Ten years of pill use reduces ovarian cancer risk by over 90% (highly relevant for PCOS patients with chronically unopposed estrogen) and substantially reduces endometrial cancer risk. However, oral contraceptives increase liver production of clotting factors — anyone with personal or family history of DVT or pulmonary embolism should be screened for clotting disorders (e.g., Factor V Leiden) before starting. [01:10:00](transcript.md#011000-birth-control-risks--benefits-cancers-polycystic-ovarian-syndrome-pcos)

7. **AMH at ~$79 is the most actionable fertility test available, yet ACOG recommends against routine screening — a position Dr. Crawford strongly opposes.** AMH does not predict monthly pregnancy probability, which is ACOG's rationale for dismissing it — but it is deeply actionable for life planning: it may prompt egg freezing, accelerate family conversations, or reveal underlying conditions. Paired with an antral follicle count (AFC) ultrasound, it provides both egg quantity and cohort quality context. AFC norms: ~16–20 follicles at age 30; ~14–16 at 35; ~8–10 at 40; ~2–4 at 44. [01:24:50](transcript.md#012450-tool-amh-testing-ovarian-reserve-antral-follicle-count-ultrasound)

8. **Egg quality — not quantity — is the dominant fertility variable after 35; the statistical sweet spot for egg freezing is 32–33.** Chromosome abnormalities (aneuploidy) rise steeply after 37 because meiotic spindles holding chromosomes in place degrade with age and accumulated inflammation. At age 30, ~60–70% of embryos may be chromosomally normal; at 40, only 20–25%. For women under 35, 15–20 mature eggs frozen yield roughly a 70–85% cumulative chance of one live birth. The age of the egg at freezing — not at thaw — determines quality. [01:35:40](transcript.md#013540-tools-egg-freezing-ivf-age--egg-quality)

9. **IVF success math requires realistic expectations across a multi-step attrition process.** Of eggs retrieved: ~90% survive the freeze-thaw, ~75% fertilize, ~50% reach blastocyst stage; of those, an age-dependent fraction are chromosomally normal. A single euploid (genetically tested) embryo transfer yields ~65% live birth probability. Cumulative success rises to ~88% after 2 transfers and ~95% after 3. Single embryo transfer is standard of care — transferring two does not improve outcomes and raises monozygotic twinning risk (2–3% with IVF vs. 0.3% naturally). [01:43:37](transcript.md#014337-egg-freezing--ivf-procedures-maternal-age-success-rates)

10. **Cigarettes, vaping, cannabis, and excess alcohol all impair egg and sperm quality.** Smoking depletes the ovarian vault and damages meiotic spindles, increasing chromosomal abnormality rates. Vaping shows similar IVF outcome impairment. Cannabis (THC, including edibles) reduces sperm motility, alters morphology, increases sperm DNA fragmentation, and raises miscarriage risk via paternal exposure. Alcohol beyond 1–2 drinks per week is associated with lower IVF success and higher miscarriage rates; zero alcohol once pregnant. [00:55:03](transcript.md#005503-eggsperm-quality-cigarettes-vaping-cannabis--alcohol)

11. **Paternal age is the strongest population-level risk factor for autism in offspring — yet it receives far less attention than maternal age.** Spermatogenesis renews every ~72 days, so lifestyle improvements affect sperm quality within months. However, de novo DNA mutation rates rise with age; paternal age over 40–45 carries the highest documented association with autism spectrum disorder and certain autosomal dominant conditions. Sperm freezing (~$400) before vasectomy or in the late 20s–early 30s is a simple preservation strategy. [01:51:30](transcript.md#015130-tool-sperm-freezing--paternal-age-vasectomy)

12. **Sleep is the single highest-leverage lifestyle variable for fertility — it is what patients most commonly neglect.** Poor sleep disrupts the HPG axis, reducing LH, FSH, and estrogen in women and testosterone and sperm quality in men. Chronic inflammation from poor sleep is directly toxic to both eggs and sperm. [02:20:36](transcript.md#022036-tools-sleep-nutrition--fertility-dietary-fat)

13. **Diet should be plant-forward with adequate fat and high-quality protein; fat is the backbone of hormone synthesis.** Full-fat dairy outperforms low-fat in fertility studies — the processing required to remove fat introduces compounds that may impair fertility. Fish (3 servings/week) outperforms red meat; red meat in the top consumption quartile is associated with lower embryo development rates in IVF. Processed/deli meats should be minimized. Tofu and soy do not harm fertility and may help due to antioxidant properties. Added sugar and artificial sweeteners (including stevia at high doses) are associated with increased miscarriage rates. Both underweight and obesity raise miscarriage risk and impair implantation. [02:27:32](transcript.md#022732-protein-meat-tofu-fish-sugar-artificial-sweeteners-weight--miscarriage)

14. **CoQ10 is the most evidence-backed supplement for egg quality; start 60–90 days before retrieval.** CoQ10 supports mitochondrial function in oocytes — critical for the energy-intensive meiotic chromosome separation. The 90-day window matches the egg maturation cycle; short-term supplementation provides no benefit to the current retrieval cohort. Core female stack: prenatal with methylfolate + 1 g omega-3/day + 1,000 IU vitamin D (titrate to 40–60 ng/mL) + CoQ10 200 mg × 3/day. Male sperm quality stack: multivitamin + CoQ10 + 1 g L-carnitine + 1 g vitamin C. [02:37:38](transcript.md#023738-tools-supplements-prenatal-vitamins-omega-3s-vitamin-d-coenzyme-q10)

15. **Myo-inositol (2–4 g/day) improves insulin sensitivity and restores ovulation in PCOS; metformin/berberine can lower testosterone in men.** PCOS creates an androgen-dominant environment from anovulation — excess testosterone causes abdominal fat distribution, acne, hair thinning, and metabolic syndrome in a self-reinforcing loop. Myo-inositol breaks part of this cycle by improving insulin signaling. Caution: metformin and berberine, popular among men for longevity, can suppress testosterone production via the same mechanism they suppress androgen production in PCOS ovaries. [02:42:26](transcript.md#024226-l-carnitine--male-fertility-pcos--myo-inositol-metformin)

16. **Menopause (average age 51–52 in the US) carries serious long-term health risks; HRT started at perimenopause onset is safe and substantially protective.** Low estrogen at menopause raises risks of dementia, osteoporosis, heart disease, and earlier death — not just hot flashes and vaginal atrophy. HRT (estradiol + progestin for women with a uterus) is safe when initiated within 10 years of menopause or before age 60; the Women's Health Initiative adverse findings stemmed from enrolling women already estrogen-deprived for 10+ years. Women with premature ovarian insufficiency should not delay. [03:15:10](transcript.md#031510-menopause)

## Tools & Protocols

### AMH Testing + Antral Follicle Count (AFC) Ultrasound

- **What:** Blood test (AMH) and transvaginal ultrasound (AFC) to assess ovarian reserve — quantity of remaining eggs.
- **How:** AMH is a blood draw on any cycle day (~$79 without insurance). AFC ultrasound ideally done on cycle day 2–4 by an OB-GYN or REI; counts visible antral follicles in both ovaries combined. AMH may read up to 30% lower in long-term oral contraceptive users — if result is low, stop the pill and retest after a few months.
- **When:** Baseline in the mid-to-late 20s for any woman who wants children. Repeat every 1–2 years if results were borderline. AMH measures quantity only, not quality.
- **Ref:** [01:24:50](transcript.md#012450-tool-amh-testing-ovarian-reserve-antral-follicle-count-ultrasound)

### Intercourse Timing for Conception

- **What:** Optimize timing and frequency of intercourse to coincide with the fertile window.
- **How:** Have sex every 1–2 days from approximately day 8–10 of the cycle through confirmed ovulation. Fertile window = 5 days ending on ovulation day; day before and day of ovulation are peak days. Do not reduce frequency to "save up" sperm — daily intercourse is associated with the highest conception rates. Use LH surge ovulation predictor kits (OPKs) or basal body temperature tracking. For IUI, abstain 48–72 hours before the procedure.
- **When:** Any cycle where conception is the goal. Every-other-day intercourse throughout the probable window is the practical minimum.
- **Ref:** [00:51:33](transcript.md#005133-tool-intercourse-timing--conception-artificial-insemination-ivf)

### Core Fertility Supplement Stack (Female)

- **What:** Evidence-based supplements to support egg quality, hormonal function, and early pregnancy.
- **How:**
  - **Prenatal vitamin with methylfolate** (400–800 mcg folic acid equivalent) — methylfolate preferred for MTHFR variant carriers; begin at least 3 months before conception
  - **Omega-3 DHA/EPA** — 1 g/day; fish oil or algae-based
  - **Vitamin D** — 1,000 IU/day minimum; test blood levels and titrate to 40–60 ng/mL
  - **CoQ10** — 200 mg × 3 per day (600 mg total); start 60–90 days before IVF retrieval or active conception attempts
- **When:** Begin at least 3 months (ideally 6) before attempting conception or egg retrieval. CoQ10 requires the full 90-day egg maturation cycle to affect the cohort being retrieved.
- **Ref:** [02:37:38](transcript.md#023738-tools-supplements-prenatal-vitamins-omega-3s-vitamin-d-coenzyme-q10)

### Male Fertility Supplement Protocol

- **What:** Sperm quality enhancement stack for men with abnormal semen parameters or as preventive support.
- **How:**
  - **Multivitamin** (with folic acid)
  - **CoQ10** — 200 mg × 3/day
  - **L-Carnitine** — 1 g/day
  - **Vitamin C** — 1 g/day alongside L-carnitine
- **When:** 60–90 days before IVF retrieval or planned conception; sperm renew on a ~90-day cycle.
- **Ref:** [02:42:26](transcript.md#024226-l-carnitine--male-fertility-pcos--myo-inositol-metformin)

### Myo-Inositol for PCOS

- **What:** A naturally occurring compound that improves insulin sensitivity and can restore ovulatory function in women with PCOS.
- **How:** 2,000–4,000 mg/day myo-inositol; most research formulations use a 40:1 ratio of myo-inositol to D-chiro-inositol. Timing of dose is flexible. May be used alongside or as an alternative to metformin in insulin-resistant PCOS.
- **When:** For women with PCOS experiencing irregular or absent cycles, elevated androgens, or insulin resistance. Safe when trying to conceive.
- **Ref:** [02:42:26](transcript.md#024226-l-carnitine--male-fertility-pcos--myo-inositol-metformin)

### Sleep Optimization for Fertility

- **What:** Prioritize consistent, high-quality sleep to support the HPG axis, follicle development, and sperm production.
- **How:** Target 7–9 hours per night in a dark, cool room. Consistent sleep and wake times. Treat underlying sleep apnea or insomnia. Minimize alcohol during treatment cycles — alcohol impairs sleep architecture and directly impairs fertility. Dr. Crawford ranks sleep as the single most commonly neglected fertility lever.
- **When:** Year-round, with particular emphasis in the 3–6 months leading up to IVF retrieval or active conception attempts.
- **Ref:** [02:20:36](transcript.md#022036-tools-sleep-nutrition--fertility-dietary-fat)

### Egg Freezing / IVF: Process Overview

- **What:** Controlled ovarian stimulation (COS) followed by egg retrieval and vitrification (or fertilization for IVF) to preserve younger-age egg quality.
- **How:**
  - ~2–3 week suppression phase (often via pill) to synchronize the follicle cohort
  - ~10–14 days of daily subcutaneous gonadotropin injections (FSH + LH/menopur) next to the navel
  - Monitoring ultrasounds every 2–3 days; estradiol blood tests
  - Trigger shot (HCG or GnRH agonist) 36 hours before retrieval to complete the final meiotic division
  - Egg retrieval under IV sedation (propofol + fentanyl): 15–20 minute transvaginal needle aspiration procedure — verify sedation protocol with your clinic beforehand
  - Mature eggs vitrified (flash-frozen) — 90% survive the freeze-thaw; 99% for blastocyst embryos
  - For IVF: eggs fertilized via ICSI, grown to blastocyst (day 5–6), optionally biopsied for PGT-A genetic testing, then frozen for frozen embryo transfer (FET)
  - Abstain from intercourse from ~day 5 of stimulation until next period to avoid pregnancy and ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS)
- **When:** Statistical sweet spot is 32–33. Aim for 15–20 mature eggs for women under 35 seeking approximately one future live birth. Multiple retrieval cycles may be needed.
- **Ref:** [01:35:40](transcript.md#013540-tools-egg-freezing-ivf-age--egg-quality) · [01:55:01](transcript.md#015501-hormones-egg-freezing--ivf) · [03:03:12](transcript.md#030312-egg-freezing-intracytoplasmic-sperm-injection-icsi-sperm-fragmentation)

### Sperm Freezing for Male Fertility Preservation

- **What:** Banking a semen sample to preserve younger-age sperm DNA integrity for future use.
- **How:** Standard semen collection and cryopreservation at a fertility clinic or sperm bank. FDA guidelines require infectious disease blood work at banking. Cost approximately $400. Abstain 48–72 hours before collection. Home collection and transport is feasible — sperm are stable during transport.
- **When:** Men in their late 20s–early 30s, and strongly recommended before any vasectomy (vasectomy reversals are unreliable over time). Essential for men who want children but are not yet ready.
- **Ref:** [01:51:30](transcript.md#015130-tool-sperm-freezing--paternal-age-vasectomy)

### Pre-implantation Genetic Testing (PGT-A)

- **What:** Chromosomal screening of IVF embryos at the blastocyst stage to identify euploid (chromosomally normal) embryos before transfer, improving transfer efficiency and reducing miscarriage.
- **How:** Embryo biopsy at day 5–6 of culture; cells sent to external genetics lab. Allows embryo banking — accumulating euploid embryos across multiple cycles before any transfer. A single euploid embryo transfer yields ~65% live birth probability vs. ~20–30% untested at age 40. PGT-M variant tests for single-gene disorders (cystic fibrosis, Huntington disease, etc.).
- **When:** Most valuable for women 35+, recurrent implantation failure, recurrent pregnancy loss, or anyone banking embryos for future siblings.
- **Ref:** [03:11:45](transcript.md#031145-genetic-testing-ivf-transfer--success-rate-embryo-banking)

### Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) at Menopause

- **What:** Estrogen (± progestin) replacement to relieve menopausal symptoms and reduce long-term risks of estrogen deficiency.
- **How:** Estradiol — not the synthetic ethinyl estradiol in the pill — delivered via patch, pill, or vaginal insert. Women with a uterus require concurrent progestin (daily oral, cyclic, or levonorgestrel IUD) to prevent endometrial cancer from unopposed estrogen. Lowest effective dose to relieve symptoms is the target.
- **When:** Initiate at perimenopause symptom onset or confirmed menopause. Critical window is within 10 years of menopause onset or before age 60 — starting later (after a decade of estrogen deprivation) carries higher cardiovascular and clotting risk, which drove the original Women's Health Initiative concerns. Women with premature ovarian insufficiency should not delay.
- **Ref:** [03:19:47](transcript.md#031947-hormone-replacement-therapy--menopause)
