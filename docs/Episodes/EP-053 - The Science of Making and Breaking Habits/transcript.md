## [00:06:52] Habits versus Reflexes, Learning, Neuroplasticity

Let's talk about habits. And anytime we're talking about habits, that means our nervous system learns something. Now, many people think that habits are just like reflexes, but pure reflexes are things like the eye blink reflex. You know, something comes towards your eye and you don't want it to get in your eye, you'll blink. Or if you happen to step on a sharp object, or get too close to something that's too hot, you'll reflexively move away. Those aren't habits. Those are what we call hard-wired reflexes. Habits are things that our nervous system learned, but not always consciously. Sometimes we develop habits that we're not even aware of until they become a problem or maybe they serve us well.

The fact of the matter is that habits are a big part of who we are. What we do habitually makes up much of what we do entirely. In fact, it's estimated that up to 70% of our waking behavior is made up of habitual behavior. So, you can imagine that there's a lot of biology, meaning cells and hormones and neural pathways, that are going to support development of those habits. So if habits are largely learned, consciously or unconsciously, we have to ask ourselves, what is learning? Well, learning is neuroplasticity. Neuroplasticity is simply the process by which our nervous system changes in response to experience. What changes are the connections between neurons. Neurons communicate with one another by electricity and by sending chemical signals that inspire the next neuron to be electrically active or not. At the end of the day, neuroplasticity is about forming new neural circuits, new pathways by which certain habits are likely to occur, and other ones are less likely to occur.

## [00:08:51] Goal-Based Habits vs. Identity-Based Habits

Okay, so that describes habits through the lens of neuroscience and biology, but as many of you are well aware, there are popular books about habits and there's a whole psychological literature about habits, and those two areas point to some very interesting aspects of habits that I think are worth mentioning. First of all is this notion of immediate goal-based habits versus identity-based habits. Immediate goal-based habits are going to be habits that are designed to bring you a specific outcome as you do them, so each and every time you do them. So for instance, it might be that you want to develop a habit of getting 60 minutes of zone II cardio each day, or perhaps 3-4 times per week.

I've talked before on the podcast about the fact that the scientific literature really points to the incredibly positive effects of getting 150 to 180 minutes per week minimum of what's called zone II cardio. Zone II cardio is basically any cardiovascular exercise that gets you moving, and your heart pumping, and breathing, but not so hard that you can't hold a conversation. Every time you do it you check off a little box and you say, "I did it." You met the goal. That is different than so-called identity-based habits where there's a larger, over-arching theme to the habit, where you're trying to become, quote unquote, "a fit person." It's where you start to attach some sort of larger picture about yourself or what it means for you to do that habit.

Now, why am I making this distinction? The reason is that pretty soon in our discussion today we are going to talk about dopamine, a molecule that's associated with motivation and reward, that we make in our brain, and how different schedules of dopamine release predict whether we will stick to a habit or not. And in particular, whether or not we will be able to form that habit quickly or not.

## [00:11:40] How Long It (Really) Takes to Form a Habit; Limbic-Friction

Another thing that you'll hear out there in the literature is that it takes 21 days to form a habit. Some people say 18, some people say 21, some people say 30 days, some people say 60 days. So which one is it? Does it depend on the habit that one is trying to form? Or does it depend on the person that's trying to form the habit? Well, it turns out that there's excellent peer-reviewed data on this. There's a study published in 2010, first author Lally, L-A-L-L-Y. This study found that, for the same habit to be formed, it can take anywhere from 18 days to as many as 254 days for different individuals to form that habit.

The specific behavior they were looking at in the Lally study was a health-related behavior — taking walks after dinner. There's actually a really nice literature showing that walks after a meal can speed glucose clearance from the blood stream, and can be beneficial for cardiovascular health. So a walk after dinner seems pretty straight-forward, right? Well, in order to form that habit, it took some people 18 days and other people 254 days. How did they know when they formed the habit? Well, they were doing it about 85% of the time and they also reported not having to spend that much mental effort in order to get into the mode of taking a walk after dinner.

The mystery of why certain people can form certain habits more easily than others probably has something to do with how people manage what's called limbic friction. Limbic friction is not a term that you're going to find in the formal neurobiological literature — it's a term that I coined to encompass a number of different pieces of the psychology and neuroscience literature. Limbic friction is a short-hand way that I use to describe the strain that's required in order to overcome one of two states within your body. One state is one of anxiousness, where you're really anxious and therefore you can't calm down, you can't relax, and therefore you can't engage in some particular activity or thought pattern that you would like. The other state is one in which you're feeling too tired or lazy or not motivated.

Both of those states relate to the function of the so-called autonomic nervous system, a set of neurons and hormones and chemicals in your brain and body that act as a sort of see-saw. You're either alert or calm. Limbic friction is a phrase that can be used to describe how much effort, how much activation energy you need in order to engage in a particular behavior. Some people would eat dinner, and then say, "Oh, that's right, I've been trying to develop the habit of taking a walk after dinner. So let's get up and go." Other people will feel like, "I just don't want to do it today." They're going to feel too much limbic friction, and that limbic friction could arrive from one of two sources — it could be because they are too tired to do it or it could be because they're too anxious and distracted.

So, this is a key distinction. A lot of habit formation has to do with being in the right state of mind and being able to control your state of body and mind. Limbic friction is going to be a useful metric or a way for you to check in with yourself and address whether or not you are likely to be able to form a certain habit easily or whether or not it's going to be very challenging.

## [00:16:07] Linchpin Habits

The other key concept for us to address — mainly found in the books and articles out there about habits — is this notion of what I call linchpin habits. Linchpin habits are certain habits that make a lot of other habits easier to execute. And the sorts of linchpin habits that I'm referring to are always going to be things that you enjoy doing. I'll just give you an example from my life. I happen to like exercise. Not all forms of exercise, but I happen to like resistance training and I happen to like running. So I'm personally in the habit of getting cardiovascular exercise 3 or 4 times per week, maybe 30-60 minutes per session. And I'm in the habit of doing resistance training 3 or 4 times per week, typically also for about 45 to 60 minutes per session.

I really place those activities under the umbrella of what I call linchpin habits. Why? Because those particular habits are easy to execute because I enjoy them. But they also make a lot of other habits easier to execute. Things like being alert for work, things like making sure that I get good sleep the night before, things like hydration, things like making sure that I eat the foods that are better for me. So certain habits act as linchpins, meaning that they shift a lot of other things. They can control and bias the likelihood that you will perform other habits that are harder to access, that you have less of an affinity for.

So again, there are three concepts we need to include here. We've got identity-based versus goal-based habits. We've got the concept that typically different habits take different periods of time to adopt, depending on the person and the habit. And there are these linchpin habits — certain habits that make other habits easier to execute, and those linchpin habits always, always, always are things that we enjoy doing. So our goal throughout this episode is for you to identify which habits are easy for you to perform, which ones are hard for you to perform, and which habits you want to break.

## [00:18:55] Mapping Your Habits; Habit Strength, Context-Dependence

What I'd like you to do is to take the concept of limbic friction and for you to ask yourself what habits you perform on a daily basis — these could be things as basic as brushing your teeth before breakfast, or brushing your teeth after breakfast. It could be, for instance, that you get exercise or you get it at a particular time of day, or even that you take a particular route to work. We are very habitual and we tend to do things more or less over and over in the same way, unless we intervene in ourselves. That's just the way that we are wired.

Now I'd like you to shift to thinking about a particular aspect of habits and that's habit strength. Now, you all have different habits. Habit strength is something that you will find in the psychological literature. Habit strength is measured by two main criteria. The first is how context-dependent a given habit is. Context dependence is: if you go from one environment to the next, do you tend to do the same thing, in the same way, at the same time of day? For instance, brushing your teeth first thing in the morning — when I travel, I tend to brush my teeth at more or less the same time of day relative to when I wake up as I do when I'm at home. So it's context independent — it's a very strong habit.

The other aspect of habit strength is how much limbic friction is required to perform that habit on a regular basis. This is extremely important because if you are in the process of building habits and consolidating those habits, then it's probably going to take more limbic friction to execute those habits. Let's say you set out to get 45 minutes of zone II cardio exercise every day, 5 or maybe even 7 days a week. If you're feeling particularly tired and you don't want to get out of bed, then there's a high degree of limbic friction. It takes motivation — but motivation is a bit of a vague concept, whereas limbic friction involves specific neural circuits and you can think of it in a more or less quantitative way.

The inverse is also true. Let's say that you are trying to get into the habit of meditating regularly. That's a pretty quiescent or calming activity, so if you're somebody who comes home from work very anxious, there's a lot of limbic friction to overcome in order to get into that calm state. These two aspects — context dependence and how much limbic friction is required — will tell you whether or not that habit is deeply or just shallowly embedded within your nervous system.

## [00:22:55] Automaticity

The goal of any habit that we want to form is to get into what's called automaticity. Automaticity is fancy language for the neural circuits can perform it automatically, and that's the ultimate place to be. If you have all these goals and things that you want to be doing on a regular basis, you'd love for them to be habitual because it takes less mental and physical effort, less limbic friction, in order to execute those. And so much of what's out there in the popular psychology literature is about how to get from that mode of high degree of limbic friction to automaticity.

What I'd like to do is to take the scientific literature of how the nervous system learns and engages in plasticity, and apply that to habit formation, habit maintenance, and if so desired, how to break particular habits.

## [00:24:03] Tool 1: Applying Procedural Memory Visualizations

I'd like to give you a particular tool that's gleaned from the research psychology literature. I learned about this from an excellent review article available online: "Psychology of Habit" by Wendy Wood and Dennis Ruenger, published in Annual Review of Psychology. The Annual Reviews series is a very high quality series with comprehensive review articles grounded in hundreds of quality, peer-reviewed studies.

This review mentions that with each repetition of a habit, small changes occur in the cognitive and neural mechanisms associated with procedural memory. Procedural memory, in the neuroscience of memory, is distinguished from episodic memory. Episodic memory is a recall of a particular set of events that happened, whereas procedural memory is holding in mind the specific sequence of things that need to happen in order for a particular outcome to occur. Think of it like a recipe or a protocol — for the sake of exercise, it's like sets and reps, or a particular course that you're going to run or cycle, or the number of laps you're going to swim and how you're going to perform it.

It's very clear that for anyone trying to adopt new habits, getting into the mindset of procedural memory is very useful for overcoming that barrier that we call limbic friction. How do you do that? A simple visualization exercise — it doesn't even have to be done eyes closed. If you are deciding to adopt a new habit, just think about the very specific sequence of steps that's required to execute that habit. Let's say I want to get into the habit of making a cup of espresso every morning — I would actually think through each of those steps. Walk into the kitchen, turn on the espresso machine, draw the espresso. Walking through each of those steps, from start to finish.

It turns out that just that simple mental exercise done once can shift people towards a much higher likelihood of performing that habit regularly, not just the first time, but as they continue out into the days and weeks that follow. The literature is really robust on this — just one mental exercise of thinking through what are the sequence of steps required in order to perform this habit from start to finish can shift the likelihood of being able to perform that habit from unlikely or moderately likely, to very likely over time.

That's because it pulls from a process that involves our hippocampus and our neocortex and other areas of our brain and nervous system that engage in procedural memory. It shifts the brain towards a mindset — a neural circuit set — of doing things in a particular sequence, which allows that limbic friction to come down and increases the likelihood that we're going to perform that thing. Simple tool but very powerful, according to the psychology literature.

## [00:27:48] Hebbian Learning, NMDA receptors

The cellular and molecular mechanisms that underlie that sort of procedural memory stepping-through phenomenon are known. The review by Wood and Ruenger talks about so-called Hebbian learning. Donald Hebb was a psychologist in Canada who birthed this field that has now lasted more than 50 years and is still very strong in neuroscience and psychology. Hebbian learning is when particular neurons are co-active — when they fire together, they tend to strengthen their connections with one another. This involves things like NMDA receptors — N-methyl-D-aspartate receptors.

NMDA receptors are on the neuron's surface and normally they don't contribute much to the activity of those neurons. But when a neuron gets a very strong input, a strong stimulus, that NMDA receptor triggers a number of mechanisms that recruit to the surface of the neuron more other receptors. In other words, it makes that neuron more responsive to input in the future such that it doesn't require so much input — it takes a neuron that is very unlikely to fire and makes it more likely to fire.

So this procedural stepping through of the steps of the recipe or the series of actions involved in sitting down to study and writing for an hour, or generating exercise — whatever is the habit that you're trying to learn — when you're doing that exercise, your nervous system isn't actually thinking you're performing that behavior. It knows the difference between a thought and an action. But when you do that, it sets in motion the same neurons that are going to be required for the execution of that habit. And so when you actually show up to perform that habit, it's as if the dominoes fall more easily — there's a lower threshold in order to get the habit to perform.

For those of you that just want to be more habitual about certain things, simply take the time, do it once, maybe twice, close your eyes if you like, and just step through the procedure of what it's going to take in order to perform that habit. The psychology literature and the neuroscience literature strongly support the fact that it is going to make it far easier for you to adopt and maintain that habit. And if you are somebody who used to perform a habit and you're having trouble returning to it, also use this procedural memory exercise in order to shift your nervous system towards a higher likelihood that you will return to that habit.

## [00:31:00] Tool 2: Task Bracketing; Dorsolateral Striatum

Now I'd like to discuss a second, and what I think is perhaps the most powerful tool for being able to acquire and stick to new habits. This tool is rooted in neural circuits and I do think it is important to understand a little bit about how those neural circuits work. Understanding even a little of the underlying mechanism will help solidify these concepts for you and will help ensure that the tools I offer are going to make sense in differing contexts and for different types of habits.

The tool I'm referring to is something called task-bracketing and the neural circuits associated with task-bracketing are basically the neural circuits that are going to allow you to learn any new type of habit or break any habit that you'd like to break. We have in our brain a set of neural circuits that fall under the umbrella term of the basal ganglia. The basal ganglia are involved in action execution — meaning doing certain things — and action suppression — not doing certain things. In the experimental realm, these are referred to as "go" meaning do, or "no go," don't do, certain things.

Drawing from the review by Wood and Ruenger in Annual Review of Psychology, task-bracketing involves a particular set of neural circuits within the basal ganglia — a sensory-motor loop. It turns out that there's an area of our basal ganglia called the dorsolateral striatum, or DLS. Dorsolateral striatum is a subdivision of the basal ganglia and it's very important for the establishment of behaviors that are associated with a habit. Beautiful studies in both animals and humans that record the activity in the dorsolateral striatum find that the dorsolateral striatum becomes active at the beginning of a particular habit, and at the very end and after a particular habit. Hence the phrase task-bracketing. It brackets the habit.

Other sets of neurons are going to be active during the actual execution of the habit, but what the literature on the dorsolateral striatum tells us is that we have particular circuits in our brain that are devoted to framing the events that happened just before and as we initiate a habit, and just after and as we terminate a habit. In other words, it acts as a sort of marker for the habit execution. This is very important because task-bracketing is what underlies whether or not a habit will be context dependent or not, whether or not it will be strong and likely to occur even if we didn't get a good night's sleep the night before, even if we're feeling distracted, even if we're not feeling like doing something emotionally, or if we are completely overwhelmed by other events.

If the neural circuits for task-bracketing are deeply embedded in us around a particular habit, well then it's likely that we're going to go out for that zone II cardio no matter what. Task-bracketing sets a neural imprint, a kind of a fingerprint in your brain, of this thing has to happen at this particular time of day so much so that it's reflexive. And as we'll talk about in a moment, there's a way that you can build up task-bracketing so that regardless of what it is you're trying to learn, there's a much higher probability that you're going to do that thing.

So this is sort of like warming up your body to exercise. When the dorsolateral striatum is engaged, your body and your brain are primed to execute a habit and you get to consciously insert which habit you want to perform.

## [00:37:08] States of Mind, Not Scheduling Time Predicts Habit Strength

In order to leverage the neural mechanisms of task-bracketing, in order to increase the likelihood that you're going to perform a particular habit, I have to break it to you that one thing that you've probably heard over and over about habit formation is not true. What I'm referring to is this idea that if you're very specific about exactly when you're going to perform a particular habit, that you're more likely to perform that habit. And while that is true in the short term, it is not true in the long term. The reason for that is that our nervous system tends to generate particular kinds of behavior based not on time, but on our state — meaning what level of activation is taking place in our brain and body. How much focus we happen to have, how fatigued we are, how energized we are. While schedules are important, it's not the particular time of day per se that's going to allow you to form and consolidate that habit. Rather, it's the state that your brain and body are in that's important to anchor yourself to.

## [00:38:16] Tool 3: Phase-Based Habit Plan: Phase 1

So now I'm going to offer you a tool — an entire program by which you can insert particular habits and activities at particular phases of the day, not times of day, but phases of the day. It turns out that particular phases of the day are associated with particular biological underpinnings, chemicals and neural circuits, and in doing so it will make it far more likely that you will be able to regularly engage in these habits and activities over a long period of time.

The program involves dividing the 24-hour day into what I call three phases. The first is phase one, which is 0 to 8 hours after waking up, approximately. The second phase is 9 to 14 or 15 hours after you wake up. And the third phase is 16 to 24 hours after waking up. Everything I'm describing is based on what I would call a typical diurnal schedule — most of us are asleep at night and awake during the day. Most people go to sleep somewhere around 10:00 PM, plus or minus two hours, and wake up some time around 7:00 AM, plus or minus two hours.

Phase one, which is 0 to 8 hours after waking, has a particular neurochemical signature. Regardless of what you do, the neuromodulators norepinephrine as well as epinephrine — that's noradrenaline and adrenaline — as well as the neuromodulator dopamine tend to be elevated during that first 0 to 8 hours after waking. There are a number of reasons for this related to the fact that cortisol is also higher in our brain and bloodstream upon waking (a healthy level), body temperature is increased, et cetera. There are several things that perhaps we should all be doing that further support an alert and focused state. They involve: viewing sunlight or bright artificial light if you can't access sunlight, within the first 30 minutes of waking; physical exercise of some kind, ideally pretty early in this phase; cold exposure in the form of cold showers or ice baths; caffeine ingestion; fasting — not ingesting calories, which will lend itself to increased norepinephrine and dopamine; if you are going to consume foods, foods rich in tyrosine, which is a precursor for dopamine; and for those interested in supplementation, things like alpha GPC or phenylethylamine or L-tyrosine.

All of those things place the brain and body into a state in which you are better able to engage in activities that have a high degree of limbic friction and where you need to override that limbic friction. What I'm referring to is a particular phase of the day — 0 to 8 hours after rising — where your whole system is action and focus oriented. And because of the neurochemicals that are naturally released in your brain and body, you will be more likely to overcome any limbic friction that stands in the way of performing particular habits.

So, as you list out the various habits that you'd like to adopt in your life, take the habits for which you know there's the highest degree of limbic friction — they are the hardest for you to engage in, they require the most activation energy — and put those in this 0 to 8 hours after waking. This will greatly facilitate your performance of those new habits. And by doing them in this particular phase of the day, not necessarily the same time, you're creating task-bracketing. You're making it such that your nervous system will predict when you are going to lean in against limbic friction in order to perform particular types of habits.

This is very different than saying, "I'm always going to run from 10 to 12 AM every morning." That's great, and if you can do that, terrific. But the literature indicates that people who are very rigid about when they do things tend — because of context dependence — to not necessarily stick to those habits over time. So think about the hardest habits to form and I highly suggest placing those somewhere within this phase one of 0 to 8 hours after waking.

## [00:46:29] Tool 3: Phase-Based Habit Plan: Phase 2

Phase two, as I mentioned, is about 9 to 14 or 15 hours after waking. During this phase of the day, because of the circadian shifts in our biology, the amount of dopamine and norepinephrine that's circulating in our brain and bloodstream tends to start to come down and levels of cortisol tend to start to come down. A different neuromodulator — serotonin — is starting to rise. Serotonin is definitely going to be highest in this second half of the day and tends to lend itself to a more relaxed state of being. Most people feel more alert early in the day, even anxious early in the day, and then as the afternoon progresses, they tend to be a bit more sleepy, a bit more relaxed, a bit more calm.

There are certain things we all can and should do during this phase two of each day that lend themselves to a state of mind and a state of body that will be beneficial for the generation and consolidation of certain types of habits. As the day goes on, you should try to start tapering the amount of light that you're viewing — you don't need to put yourself into dim light at 3 or 4 in the afternoon, but you want to start tapering off really bright light from artificial sources. Getting some sunlight in your eyes at low solar angle in the second half of the day can also be beneficial. Things like NSDR — non-sleep deep rest — is an umbrella term for things like meditation, for Yoga Nidra, and self-hypnosis. If you're interested in self-hypnosis, there's a great free resource called Reverie, R-E-V-E-R-I-E.com — an app developed by colleagues of mine and researchers at Stanford School of Medicine. Things like heat and sauna, hot baths, hot showers are also terrific for this phase, as they tend to support this serotonergic or high-serotonin state. For those interested in supplementation, there's ashwagandha, which reduces cortisol — and peaks in cortisol late in the afternoon and evening are associated with depression and anxiety.

How do you leverage phase two of the day for habit formation? Given what we know about the neurochemistry of learning and memory, the second half of the day is a terrific time to take on habits and things that require very little override of limbic friction. So these might be things that in common terms are kind of mellower activities — it might be journaling, practicing music, working on a particular piece of music, or trying to learn a language. Something that's a little bit challenging, but doesn't require a ton of energy in order to override that limbic friction.

For those of you who can only exercise or prefer to exercise in phase two, that's absolutely fine. However, because of the importance of sleep and in particular deep sleep for neuroplasticity and recovery, if you do train in phase two, I highly recommend that you start doing some NSDR activity after you train, within an hour or two, because that will allow you to taper down and relax so that you can get into phase three.

## [00:55:24] Tool 3: Phase-Based Habit Plan: Phase 3

Phase three of the 24-hour schedule runs from about 16 to 24 hours after waking. During that period of time, there are things that are going to support being in a state of mind and body that will allow neuroplasticity to occur — that will allow the rewiring you've triggered during the waking part of the day to actually take place. Those things are very low to no light — keeping your environment very dark or very dim. The body has to drop by about 1 to 3 degrees in order to get into sleep and stay asleep, so low light, low temperature environment is very beneficial. You can always pile on blankets if you want to be warm, but you want your environment to be cold.

It's important to make sure you're well-fed enough when you head into this third phase that you're not awake because you're hungry. Some people recommend putting a gap of 4 hours between your final bite of food and when you go to sleep; other people say 2 hours — you have to experiment for yourself. There are supplements that can support sleep — things like magnesium threonate or magnesium bisglycinate, theanine, and apigenin. You can find a toolkit for sleep at hubermanlab.com that is not just supplements but a number of different behavioral and nutrition-based things that can allow you to get to sleep and to stay asleep more readily.

Obviously not drinking caffeine too close to bed is going to be critical — ideally you wouldn't ingest any caffeine in phase two of the day, so that you could get into this deeper state of rest in which habit formation and neural plasticity can occur. If you get up in the middle of the night to use the restroom — which is perfectly normal — use a minimum of light because light inhibits the hormone melatonin and can make it very hard to fall back to sleep. The effects of light inhibiting melatonin are very potent and happen very quickly. If you have trouble falling back asleep, you might want to use something like the Reverie app's sleep script, or an NSDR script on YouTube such as NSDR Madefor, or Yoga Nidra scripts.

Phase three is really about making sure that whatever limbic friction you've been able to override in phase one, and whatever things you've been doing in phase two, get truly locked into the nervous system through those Hebbian mechanisms — NMDA receptors — that I talked about before. Neuroplasticity is the basis of habit formation, and neuroplasticity and the rewiring of neural circuits happens in these states of deep sleep. If you're not giving phase three the material it needs — and you're not avoiding things like caffeine and bright light and stress during phase three — you're simply not going to be able to build those habits that you've been working so hard to trigger in phase one and phase two.

The whole reason for placing particular types of behaviors at particular phases of the day is to set a framework for task-bracketing. When you do things at particular phases of the day, under particular conditions of neurochemistry, you're giving the brain a very predictable set of sequences that during sleep it can start to program into your nervous system, so that within a short period of time — hopefully within 18 or maybe even 6 days or fewer — you'll find that executing those behaviors is very straightforward for you and that you won't have to feel so much limbic friction.

## [01:01:34] Habit Flexibility

Some of you are probably asking: if I perform a particular habit during phase one, and then I do other habits during phase two, and I eventually get to the point where I'm engaging in those habits in a pretty effortless way, do I keep them in the same phase of the day? The good news is the literature says it doesn't matter. And in fact, moving that particular habit around somewhat randomly can actually be beneficial to you. By being able to do the same thing that we want to do regardless of time of day or circumstances, that's how we know that we've achieved a real habit formation — the habit has been moved into certain components of our neural circuitry that just allow us to do it what seems like reflexively.

The reason for that is that the hippocampus, which many of you know is associated with learning and memory, is not actually where memories are stored. The hippocampus is where memories are formed — where procedural memory of how you're going to execute a particular sequence is maintained. So like the recipe model, that's where the recipe is maintained until you know how to cook that dish and then the procedural memory literally migrates off into a different set of neural circuits — the neural circuits of the neocortex, where we have maps of sensory experience and motor maps of how to execute things. We use one part of the brain to learn something, but then that information, in the form of the electrical activity of neurons — like a script for a movie or the notes on a sheet of paper for a musical piece — is transferred off to a different brain area.

So, that whole process of really leaning into something that's hard, and then it becoming easier, and then eventually that thing becoming more or less reflexive, involves a migration of the information in the brain. And once it's migrated out to a different location in the brain, at that point it's achieved context-independence. If you want to keep it in the same phase of day, great. But if one day you decide you're going to exercise in the afternoon, the next day in the morning — and that's the habit you're concerned with — that's terrific. If you're able to do that, that means it's truly achieved context-independence. You have officially formed that habit.

## [01:04:57] Should We Reward Ourselves? How? When? When NOT to.

We really can't have a discussion about learning anything, habits or otherwise, unless we talk about reward prediction error. Reward prediction error is associated with the molecule dopamine, and it is a very good lens through which to think about whether or not we should reward ourselves for performing a given habit. Reward prediction error, quite simply, is: if you expect a reward and the reward comes, a particular behavior that was associated with generating that reward is more likely to occur again.

However, the amount of reward — in the form of this molecule dopamine — that you will experience is even greater if a reward arrives that's unexpected. So let me illustrate. If I think something's coming that's going to be great, let's say I lean into a habit, I manage to override my limbic friction, and I'm doing my 45 minutes of writing in the morning with no phone and no internet, and I'm getting toward the end, and I'm anticipating how I'm actually doing this — this is great, I did it, I'm feeling really, really good. I finish, I definitely will receive a dopamine reward, I'll make my own dopamine reward — that's where it comes from. This is all internal. However, I will also receive a dopamine reward if unexpectedly something positive happens. And typically if something unexpected but positive happens, the amount of dopamine reward is actually much greater than in any other conditions.

Reward prediction error also says that if we expect a reward and the reward doesn't come, the pattern of dopamine release will follow a particular contour. Let's say I'm writing and I'm about 30 or 45 minutes in, I'm thinking, "This is great, I'm actually succeeding in executing the behaviors I need to in order to perform this habit." Just that series of thoughts will start to generate the dopamine release within my brain and body. However, if at the 15-minute mark the phone rings and I pick up the phone or I break my protocol and go check social media, my level of dopamine is actually going to drop below the baseline — below the level of dopamine I had before I even started the habit execution.

So this system that predicts whether or not rewards are going to come — when we think a reward is going to come, dopamine release starts to arrive earlier, in the form of positive anticipation. You tell a kid, "We're going to go to the amusement park." They haven't gotten there yet, but they're excited — the dopamine release comes earlier. They get to the amusement park and they will also have some dopamine release associated with that. But most of it, believe it or not, came in the form of the anticipation. And if you then arrive and find it's closed — the dopamine level drops way below what it was before you told them you were headed there.

Reward prediction error governs virtually all aspects of effort and all aspects of learning. Why? Because when dopamine is released in the brain and body, the neural circuits of our brain and body change. There's a state change. Our overall level of energy, and the sorts of sensory events that we're paying attention to, change when there's a lot of dopamine in our system.

## [01:10:30] Tool 4: "Dopamine Spotlighting" & Task Bracketing

If you are considering adopting a new habit or if you are trying to break a habit, it's very useful to think not just about the procedural aspects of what you're going to do, but also think about the events that precede and follow that particular habit and the execution — or at least the effort to execute — that habit. What you're doing is casting a kind of spotlight around a bin of time, or a set of events, for which dopamine can be associated.

Let's say I'm someone who has a hard time getting in that 30 to 60 minutes of zone II cardiovascular exercise mid-morning. This is actually an issue for me — I much prefer to do resistance exercise than cardiovascular exercise, although once I do it, I always feel much better. What I should do is positively anticipate the onset and the offset of that session. So thinking about leaning into the effort, going out and doing that zone II cardio session, and I should think about how I'm going to feel after. Not just thinking about how great I'm going to feel after, but also thinking about how hard it's going to be at the beginning, and then trying to reward myself subjectively for the entire experience.

In other words, start rewarding task-bracketing in addition to rewarding the execution of the habit itself. This is not positive self-talk — you can't lie to yourself. The neuroscience literature, the literature of growth mindset, all basically speaks to the fact that when you lie to yourself, you know you're lying, and you actually set up the opposite of a reward system. So, you have to be brutally honest — for instance, "I don't like initiating this cardiovascular exercise, but I do like the fact that I've done it after I've done it." What you are doing is applying reward prediction error to the entire sequence of things that's involved in getting into the habit execution, getting through the habit execution, and getting out of the habit execution.

So take the procedural memory visualization exercise we talked about earlier. It would be: "Okay, up and put on my shoes, then I'm going to head out the door, then I'm going to drive up the road." But even better would be to broaden the time bin and start to positively anticipate the period headed into the habit, even before you put on your shoes. I'm not just thinking about habit execution as this isolated little set of events or this little time bin, but rather I'm drawing a larger envelope around it and starting to positively associate dopamine reward with that larger envelope.

What you're trying to do is not restrict your thinking to just the habit that you're trying to form, but rather, to grab ahold of the timing before and after that particular habit and start to positively associate reward mechanisms in your brain with that entire time bin. And for those of you thinking, "Well, this is just a psychological trick" — it's not, because you're not actually contradicting the fact that some of this is unpleasant. What you're doing is taking this entire series of events — this time envelope — and associating it with a particular reward that comes later.

Reward prediction error is beautiful not just because it's a sort of math of anticipation and reward, or a math of anticipation and disappointment — it's beautiful because you can stretch out or make more narrow the time bins in which reward prediction error works. Reward prediction error is the way in which people navigate four-year degrees. Ultimately, what you're trying to do is positively associate the motivation molecule — dopamine — with that entire time bin of the habit and everything before and after it.

And I should say that dopamine, contrary to popular belief, is not a reward molecule so much as it is a molecule of motivation and drive. The natural consequence of doing this exercise — writing things out that precede, are involved in the immediate execution of the habit, and follow the habit — will allow you to experience an increase in energy and thereby an increase in likelihood that you're going to engage in that entire sequence of events. And the reason for that is that dopamine gives us energy, and the molecule epinephrine — adrenaline — is actually manufactured from dopamine biochemically. So you can access more dopamine around even habits that you haven't yet formed by taking this broader time envelope and task-bracketing that specific habit execution.

## [01:18:22] Tool 5: The 21-Day Habit Installation & Testing System

I want to spell out a particular system that I think could be very useful to most people, that's rooted in the biology of habit formation, rooted in the psychology of habit formation, and that is entirely compatible with that phase one, phase two, phase three type program I talked about earlier. For the sake of this example, a 21-day system. I picked 21 days because that seems to be the average or most typical system for engaging neuroplasticity as it relates to the formation of new habits.

This 21-day system is one that someone I know very well uses and has used for a very long time — their kids use it as well. Basically what this involves is you set out to perform 6 new habits per day across the course of 21 days. You write down 6 things that you would like to do every day for 21 days. However, the expectation is that you'll only complete 4 to 5 of those each day. Built into this is a kind of permission to fail — but it's not failure, because this approach to forming habits is based not so much on the specific habits that you're trying to form, but the habit of performing habits. It's the habit of doing a certain number of things per day.

Another reason for not necessarily performing all 6 is that some activities probably shouldn't be performed each day — for instance, weight training every day may not allow for adequate recovery. So by having 6 things in that list, you could shuffle out that particular activity on particular days of the week and simply do 4 or 5 other activities. If you miss a day, meaning you don't perform 4 to 5 things, there is no punishment. And it's important that you don't actually try and do what, in the literature, is called a habit slip compensation — which is just fancy psychological language for if you screw up and you don't get all 4 or 5 in one day, you don't do 8 the next day in order to compensate.

I remember when I started graduate school at UC Berkeley, a neurologist named Bob Knight said: "You don't want to do anything or engage in a routine in any way that you can't keep up consistently for at least five and ideally six days per week." And he said, "If you happen to screw up and not be able to engage in whatever habits you're trying to learn for whatever reason, the next day you just get right back on the horse."

However, there's a really interesting feature from the neuroscience and psychology literature that says chunking this 21 days into 2-day bins can be very, very useful. There does seem to be something powerful about engaging in particular habits in a particular sequence for two days in a row, and then resetting. So you're thinking, "Okay I can do this for a day, and if I can do it for a day, I can probably do it for 2 days." Then resetting. Every 2 days you're resetting — you're chunking 21 days into a series of 2-day bins.

Now, everything I've described about this 21-day program has nothing neuroscientifically unique about it, except for the fact that after 21 days, you stop engaging in this 21-day deliberate 4 to 5 things per day tight schedule, and you simply go into autopilot. You ask yourself: how many of those particular habits that I was deliberately trying to learn in the previous 21 days are automatically incorporated into my schedule? How many of them am I naturally doing?

In other words, every 21 days you don't update and start adding new habits — you simply try and maintain the ones that you built in that first 21 days. This is extremely important because in all the habit literature, there wasn't a lot of information about what to do once you've formed a habit and how to evaluate whether or not that habit is likely to persist long into the future. Here's the idea: you set out these 6 things that you would like to acquire in your life, you only expect that you're going to perform 4 or 5 each day, you do that for 21 days, if you miss a day you just hop right back on the next day. But you should think about the functional units within this 21-day period as 2-day chunks.

Then in the next 21 days, you're not trying to acquire any new habits — you're simply going to assess how deeply you've rewired your nervous system to be able to perform those 6 habits of the previous 21 days. You may find that you kept up 2 of those things very consistently throughout the 21 days, and perhaps there was one of them that you did sporadically, and that there were 3 others that you didn't manage to execute. You could repeat this 21-day process: 21 days of new habit, 21 days of testing those new habits as to whether or not they're reflexive or not. The idea is that you're setting out to make that 21 days really the stimulus period in which the habits get wired in and then the following periods of 21 days are really the test bed of how well you've embedded those particular habits.

## [01:28:26] Breaking Habits: Long-Term (Synaptic) Depression

Thus far, we've almost exclusively been discussing how to form habits. But what about breaking habits? One of the challenges in breaking habits is that many habits occur very, very quickly and so there isn't an opportunity to intervene until the habit has already been initiated and in some cases completed. There are a couple of tools that neuroscience and psychology tell us can be very beneficial. Some of those things are somewhat intuitive and relate to foundation practices — things like stress reduction, getting good sleep, quality nutrition, having positive routines arranged throughout your day. All of those will support you in trying to break particular habits.

In order to identify a specific protocol that one could apply in order to break habits, we have to look at the mirror image of the sort of neuroplasticity that we talked about at the beginning of the episode. We talked about a form of neuroplasticity called long-term potentiation, involving the NMDA receptor — where if a set of neurons is very electrically active, it's likely that those neurons will communicate with themselves more easily because of changes in things like NMDA receptor activity. Now, in order to break synapses, or to break apart neural connections that are serving a habit that you don't want to engage in, we need to engage the process called long-term depression.

Long-term depression has nothing to do with a state of mental depression or a reduction in mood — it's simply called long-term depression because just as long-term potentiation says if neuron A triggers the firing of neuron B very robustly over and over again, then neuron A will not have to fire as intensely in order to activate neuron B in the future. Long-term depression says that if neuron A is active and neuron B is not active within a particular time window, then the connection between neuron A and B will weaken over time, even if they started off very strongly connected.

So how do you take two neurons that underlie a habit out of synchrony? How do you get them to fire asynchronously? Let's say for instance you have a habit of picking up your phone mid work session. If you noticed what's happened with phone use over time, most people find themselves just looking at their phone, or find themselves in a particular app, without actually having engaged in the conscious set of steps of "I'm really curious what's going on in this particular app." You just sort of find yourself doing it because the behavior of picking up your phone has become fully reflexive.

The literature says there are a number of ways to break these sorts of habitual behaviors. Most of those approaches involve establishing some sort of reward for not performing the activity or some sort of punishment for performing the activity — things like a rubber band on the wrist, tick marks on paper ("what gets measured is what gets managed"), or notifications from apps. However, a beautiful meta-analysis by first author Fritz (F-R-I-T-Z), Heather Fritz, in "Intervention to modify habits: a scoping review" from the Journal of Occupation Participation and Health (2020), found that notifications to either engage in habits or to not engage in habits were not very effective over time. They were effective in the immediate period when people started using them, but in the long term, did not predict whether or not people would effectively stick to or break habits.

## [01:35:49] Notifications Don't Work

So sadly, notifications don't seem to work very well in the long term. And what we do know from both human and animal studies is that things like electric shock or monetary penalties are pretty effective ways to break habits. The problem is when people are not being monitored for habit use — unless someone's monitoring them, people don't tend to monitor themselves completely enough, or punish themselves completely enough, that they break the behavior. In other words, the punishment isn't bad enough in order to break the habit, which just speaks to how powerful these habits are once they become reflexive. They're just very, very hard to override.

So it turns out that the key to generating long-term depression in these pathways is actually to take the period immediately following the bad habit execution — meaning, let's say you tell yourself you're not going to pick up your phone, but you find yourself doing it anyway — what actually has to happen is bringing conscious awareness to the period immediately afterward.

## [01:37:50] Tool 6: Break Bad Habits with Post-Bad-Habit "Positive Cargo"

In that moment, capture the sequence of events — not that led to the bad habit execution, but actually take advantage of the fact that the neurons responsible for generating that bad habit were active a moment ago, and engage in a replacement behavior immediately afterward. This is really interesting and I think powerful because I would have thought that you have to engage in a replacement behavior that truly replaces the bad habit behavior, right? That you would have to be able to identify your state of mind or the sequence of events leading into the bad habit. But rather, the period immediately after the bad habit execution is a unique opportunity to insert a different type of adaptive behavior.

Let's give an example. Let's say you find yourself — you're trying to do focused work, you pick up your phone, you're disappointed in yourself for picking up your phone. You could of course just put it down and re-engage in the work behavior, but if you were good at that, then you probably wouldn't have done it in the first place. What turns out to be very effective is to go engage in some other positive habit. Maybe you're working on hydration, so maybe you go have a glass of water. Maybe you're trying to do breath work or something. Maybe you're trying to enhance your language speaking skills, so you go and spend 5 minutes doing a particular type of language learning. You literally exit whatever you were doing and perform that other new positive habit in the immediate period right after that, even for a short period of time.

This creates a cognitive and temporal mismatch between the initial bad behavior — which before was what we would call a closed loop, where one behavior, one set of neural firings, leads to another, leads to another, and just kind of sets the same thing in motion — and the new added positive behavior. By changing the number of features that are in that loop, it disrupts the closed nature of that loop, it creates what we call an open loop, and in an open loop, you are better able to intervene.

This might seem counterintuitive — you might think, "Why would I want to reward the execution of a bad habit with a good habit? I don't want to reward myself for the bad habit." But really what you're trying to do is change the nature of the neural circuits that are firing so that you can rewrite the script for that bad habit. A different way to put it would be: imagine that the bad habit is like a chord on the piano that you play, and it comes very easily. Rather than trying to prevent yourself from doing it, the next time you do it, add in a new chord or sequence that you're trying to learn. What this does is change the whole nature of the sequence of neurons that are firing from bad habit through to the end of this newly applied good habit.

This is the way in which you start to dismantle — or really weaken — the likelihood that if neuron A fires, neuron B will fire. Because as you're starting off in the mode of very reflexively performing a bad habit, those neurons are firing together without you consciously being aware of it. Rather, tacking on some additional sequences — if neuron A and B fire, then you're inserting neurons C, D, E, F to fire — the C-D-E-F being the positive behavior that you're going to insert — creates a chain of neuronal activation that is then very easy to dismantle.

When people have applied this kind of approach, they find they are able to engage in re-mapping of neural circuits associated with bad habits in ways that are very, very straightforward. Because you can always identify when you've done the thing you don't want to do, and then tack on to that something additional that's positive. The nature of that positive thing is important — you don't want it to be something that's very hard to execute. You want it to be something that's positive and fairly easy to execute. This is rooted in the biology of long-term depression, and it maps very well to the behavioral change literature that really shows that — rather than just getting reminders, rather than trying to instill punishment, rather than setting up reward for breaking bad habits — perhaps the simplest way to approach this is to tack on additional behaviors to the bad habits, make sure those behaviors are good behaviors or behaviors that are adaptive for you, and in doing so, you will soon find that the initiation of the bad habit takes on a whole new form, or that you're not even inspired to do it at all.
