# Episode 60: Using Hypnosis to Enhance Health & Performance | Dr. David Spiegel

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. David Spiegel of Stanford University joins Andrew Huberman to explain what clinical hypnosis is, how it differs from stage hypnosis, and why it represents a uniquely powerful tool for changing brain states rapidly. They cover the neurobiology of hypnosis, how to measure hypnotizability, and clinical applications including trauma treatment, chronic pain, stress, sleep, grief, OCD, phobias, and cancer outcomes. Spiegel also introduces the Reveri app as a research-validated self-hypnosis resource and discusses how breathwork and breathing patterns intersect with hypnotic induction and peak performance states.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hypnosis is a state of focused attention, not loss of control.** It resembles looking through a telephoto lens — heightened detail and absorption, reduced peripheral context. Stage hypnosis exploits this state for entertainment and can harm participants; clinical self-hypnosis *increases* self-control rather than surrendering it. [00:09:09](transcript.md#000909-clinical-hypnosis)

2. **Three neural signatures define the hypnotic state.** fMRI studies show: (1) reduced activity in the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (DACC/salience network), making distraction less likely; (2) increased functional connectivity between the DACC and the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (executive control network); and (3) altered inverse connectivity between the DLPFC and the posterior cingulate cortex, producing dissociation and cognitive flexibility. [00:20:25](transcript.md#002025-neurobiology-of-hypnosis)

3. **Hypnosis also alters DLPFC-insula connectivity, enabling body control.** The DLPFC communicates through the insula — part of the salience and mind-body network — to regulate internal physiology. In one study, hypnotized subjects imagining eating their favorite foods increased gastric acid secretion by 87%; imagining abstaining produced a 40% decrease. Even after Pentagastrin injection (which triggers acid release), the hypnosis condition produced a 19% reduction. [00:20:25](transcript.md#002025-neurobiology-of-hypnosis)

4. **Highly hypnotizable people have a resting-state brain difference.** Even without being hypnotized, highly hypnotizable individuals show greater functional connectivity between the DACC and the left DLPFC. About one-third of adults are not hypnotizable; two-thirds are; roughly 15% are extremely hypnotizable. Hypnotizability is more stable over 25 years than IQ (test-retest r = 0.7 vs. 0.6). [00:20:25](transcript.md#002025-neurobiology-of-hypnosis)

5. **Hypnosis works best for specific, problem-focused conditions.** Strongest clinical evidence for: stress reduction, sleep improvement, chronic pain, PTSD/trauma restructuring, phobias, anxiety, smoking cessation, weight management, and preparation for surgical/medical procedures. People with OCD tend to be low hypnotizability (over-controlling of thought, evaluating rather than experiencing) and are less likely to respond. [00:28:22](transcript.md#002822-hypnosis-for-stress--sleep)

6. **Trauma restructuring via hypnosis uses a split-screen technique, leveraging state-dependent memory.** Patients float their body in a safe place, then place the traumatic memory on the left side of an imagined screen while activating self-protective resources on the right. This reframes the narrative from passive victimhood to active coping. Crucially, hypnosis recreates a mental state congruent with the dissociative state during the original trauma — leveraging state-dependent memory to facilitate reprocessing. A randomized trial from Israel showed adding hypnosis to PTSD treatment improves outcomes. [00:37:19](transcript.md#003719-restructuring-trauma-narratives)

7. **Hypnotizability peaks in childhood (ages 6–11) and stabilizes by the early 20s.** A Stanford longitudinal study (Hilgard and Zimbardo) tracked psychology students 25 years after initial hypnotizability measurement and found test-retest r = 0.7 — more stable than IQ over the same interval. The decline from childhood is linked to the growth of abstract cognitive frameworks (Piaget's formal operations stage) as the DLPFC matures. [01:01:50](transcript.md#010150-hypnotizability-the-spiegel-eye-roll-test)

8. **The Spiegel Eye-Roll Test screens hypnotizability in seconds.** Developed by Herbert Spiegel: look straight ahead, roll eyes upward toward the ceiling, then slowly lower eyelids while keeping eyes rolled up. More white sclera visible as lids close = higher hypnotizability. Visible iris = lower hypnotizability. The test creates a neural conflict: you activate muscles for upward gaze while the act of closing lids normally relaxes them — those who can maintain this conflicted state tend to be more hypnotizable. [01:01:50](transcript.md#010150-hypnotizability-the-spiegel-eye-roll-test)

9. **EMDR likely works through a hypnotic mechanism, not the eye movements themselves.** Lateral eye movements resemble classic hypnotic inductions (the dangling watch). Every dismantling study of EMDR has shown that removing the eye-movement component does not reduce outcomes — the active ingredient is structured exposure and trauma confrontation. Spiegel prefers more explicit trauma processing than EMDR typically offers. Lateral eye movement may contribute by suppressing amygdala activity, inducing a mild parasympathetic/hypnotic state. [01:15:36](transcript.md#011536-emdr-eye-movement-desensitization-reprocessing)

10. **Hypnosis for pain reduces suffering without eliminating protective sensation.** In a Lancet-published randomized trial of 2.5-hour arterial cutdown procedures for tumor chemoembolization, the hypnosis group reduced pain by 80% vs. standard care at 1.5 hours, used half the opioids, had fewer complications, and completed procedures 17 minutes faster on average. A year-long randomized trial with metastatic breast cancer patients showed the hypnosis group had half the pain of controls on minimal medication. [01:27:56](transcript.md#012756-the-mind-body-connection)

11. **Grief can be structured with a hypnotic split-screen technique.** Float the body safely, sit with the feeling of loss on one side of the screen for a few minutes, then project on the other side one thing the deceased bequeathed — a quality, memory, or tradition still carried. This reframes grief as painful loss coexisting with what was given. In metastatic breast cancer support groups, this approach reduced anxiety and depression over time in randomized clinical trials. [01:31:35](transcript.md#013135-dealing-with-grief)

12. **Hypnotic-like states accompany peak performance and flow.** Classical pianists and athletes in peak performance describe floating above their activity and flowing without step-by-step self-monitoring — the same neural signature as hypnosis (reduced DLPFC-posterior cingulate self-referential processing). Intense physical or musical activity can induce hypnotic states; the state does not require physical quietness or relaxation. [01:42:39](transcript.md#014239-breathing-patterns-peak-performance)

## Tools & Protocols

### Self-Hypnosis via Reveri App
**What:** A clinically validated self-hypnosis app co-developed by Dr. Spiegel and colleagues at Stanford.  
**How:** Select a specific issue (stress, sleep, pain, focus, etc.); a guided session takes the user through induction and targeted suggestion, typically 10–20 minutes. Sessions can be repeated daily.  
**When:** Any time. Particularly effective for stress reduction before sleep, managing pain during medical procedures, or breaking obsessive thought loops.  
**Ref:** [reveri.com](https://www.reveri.com) | iOS: [apple.co/3sT5RL8](https://apple.co/3sT5RL8) | [00:50:07](https://youtu.be/PctD-ki8dCc?t=3007)

### The Body-Float Protocol for Stress
**What:** A hypnotic dissociation technique for stress reduction.  
**How:** Induce a light hypnotic state (or use guided session). Imagine your body floating in a safe, comfortable place — a bath, lake, or weightless space. Then project the stressor onto an imagined screen. The rule: no matter what appears on the screen, keep your body comfortable. Observe the problem without letting it invade somatic experience.  
**When:** Acutely stressful situations, ongoing anxiety, or before sleep with intrusive thoughts.  
**Ref:** [00:28:22](https://youtu.be/PctD-ki8dCc?t=1702)

### Trauma Restructuring Split-Screen Technique
**What:** A hypnosis-based cognitive restructuring method for PTSD and traumatic memories.  
**How:** In a hypnotic state, visualize the traumatic event on the left side of an imagined screen. On the right side, visualize your protective actions or strengths during the event. This reframes the narrative from passive victimhood to active coping, reducing distress and integrating the memory.  
**When:** Used clinically by trained hypnotherapists; not a DIY protocol for severe trauma.  
**Ref:** [00:37:19](https://youtu.be/PctD-ki8dCc?t=2239)

### Spiegel Eye-Roll Test (Hypnotizability Screen)
**What:** A rapid, low-cost assessment of hypnotizability.  
**How:** Look straight ahead, then roll your eyes upward as far as possible toward the top of your head. While keeping eyes rolled up, slowly lower your eyelids. The amount of white sclera visible between the iris and lower lid as you close correlates with hypnotizability (more white = higher score). A full formal score also includes the ability to hold a dissociated arm in an outstretched position.  
**When:** Before starting hypnotherapy to calibrate expectations and approach.  
**Ref:** [01:01:50](https://youtu.be/PctD-ki8dCc?t=3710)

### Slow-Exhale Induction Breathing
**What:** A breathwork pattern used to initiate and deepen hypnotic states.  
**How:** Take one deep breath in, then exhale slowly and fully — longer exhale than inhale. This increases intrathoracic pressure, promotes parasympathetic nervous system activity, and slows heart rate. Used at the start of hypnotic inductions.  
**When:** At the beginning of any self-hypnosis session; also useful as a standalone stress-reduction technique.  
**Ref:** [01:42:39](https://youtu.be/PctD-ki8dCc?t=6159)

### Hypnosis for Pain Management
**What:** Clinical hypnosis technique to reduce suffering component of pain without eliminating protective sensation.  
**How:** In a hypnotic state, patients are instructed to imagine filtering or reducing the intensity and unpleasantness of pain — not denying it exists but reframing it as information that can be observed without dominating consciousness. Often combined with the body-float visualization.  
**When:** Chronic pain conditions, procedural pain in medical settings, post-surgical recovery.  
**Ref:** [01:27:56](https://youtu.be/PctD-ki8dCc?t=5276)

## People Mentioned

**Dr. David Spiegel** — Associate Chair of Psychiatry & Behavioral Sciences, Stanford; world-leading clinical hypnosis researcher and co-creator of Reveri ([stresshealthcenter.stanford.edu](https://stresshealthcenter.stanford.edu))

**Herbert Spiegel (David's father)** — Psychiatrist and psychoanalyst; pioneer in clinical hypnosis, trained by Gustav von Aschaffenburg in 1943; developer of the Hypnotic Induction Profile (HIP); professor at Columbia University ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Spiegel))

**Gustav von Aschaffenburg** — German psychiatrist and forensic psychiatrist; early 20th-century hypnosis researcher; trained Herbert Spiegel in hypnosis during WWII ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_von_Aschaffenburg))

**Frieda Fromm-Reichmann** — Influential German-American psychoanalyst; supervisor of Herbert Spiegel; known for innovative psychotherapy approaches; encouraged Herbert Spiegel's hypnosis work ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frieda_Fromm-Reichmann))

**Tom Hackett** — Chairman of Psychiatry at Massachusetts General Hospital; taught David Spiegel hypnosis during medical school ([MGH Psychiatry](https://www.massgeneral.org/psychiatry))

**Karl Deisseroth** — Professor at Stanford; pioneer of optogenetics and CLARITY; referenced for his Nature paper identifying retrosplenial cortex rhythmic discharge as the neural mechanism of ketamine-induced dissociation ([Deisseroth Lab](https://web.stanford.edu/group/dlab/))

**Jack Feldman** — Distinguished Professor at UCLA; world expert on the neural control of breathing; Huberman Lab guest referenced for breathwork research ([Feldman Lab UCLA](https://www.neuroscience.ucla.edu/research-areas/cellular-and-molecular-neuroscience/jack-feldman))

**Carla Shatz** — Professor of Neurobiology at Stanford and former Berkeley/Harvard faculty; originator of the phrase "neurons that fire together wire together" (often misattributed to Donald Hebb); pioneer of activity-dependent neural development ([Stanford Bio](https://med.stanford.edu/profiles/carla-shatz))

**Donald Hebb** — Canadian psychologist; developer of Hebbian learning theory; frequently (incorrectly) credited with the "fire together wire together" phrase ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_O._Hebb))

**Gordon Bower** — Late cognitive psychologist at Stanford; co-founder of cognitive psychology at Stanford; established the concept of state-dependent memory ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_H._Bower))

**Francine Shapiro** — Psychologist; developer of EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing); based in Palo Alto, CA; affiliated with the Mental Research Institute ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francine_Shapiro))

**Jean Piaget** — Swiss developmental psychologist; referenced for his framework of abstract cognitive development (formal operations stage) emerging in adolescence, which Spiegel links to declining hypnotizability ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Piaget))
