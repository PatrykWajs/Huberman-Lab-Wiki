# Episode 90: Nicotine's Effects on the Brain & Body & How to Quit Smoking or Vaping

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Huberman separates nicotine from its delivery devices (cigarettes, vapes, dip, snuff), establishing that nicotine itself is not the carcinogen — the delivery mechanisms are. He covers nicotine's four major neurochemical pathways: dopamine release via the mesolimbic reward circuit, acetylcholine-driven attentional spotlighting from the nucleus basalis, norepinephrine-driven alertness from the locus coeruleus, and GABA suppression — collectively making nicotine one of the most potent cognitive enhancers known. He explains how nicotine suppresses appetite and boosts metabolism through POMC neurons and alpha-4 beta-2 receptors, and why vaping is uniquely addictive due to crack-cocaine-like speed of nicotine delivery to the brain. The second half covers the biology of withdrawal (homeostatic dopamine downregulation making quitters feel worse than pre-use baseline), followed by evidence-based cessation methods: clinical hypnosis via Reveri (23% single-session success), bupropion/Wellbutrin (~20% success), nicotine replacement tapering with rotating delivery formats, and a first-week survival strategy using cold exposure, exercise, and social interaction to prop up dopamine during the critical 75%-relapse window.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Nicotine is not the carcinogen** — cancer, stroke, and vascular damage come from delivery devices (combustion byproducts in cigarettes: tar, ammonia, formaldehyde, carbon dioxide; vaping chemicals). Nicotine itself does not cause cancer. [00:58:35](transcript.md#005835-smoking-vaping-dipping-snuffing-carcinogens-endothelial-cells)

2. **Nicotine exists in nightshades too** — tomatoes, eggplant, sweet peppers, and potatoes all contain nicotine at far lower concentrations than tobacco. Nicotine is a plant alkaloid that evolved as an insect pesticide. [00:19:55](transcript.md#001955-where-is-nicotine-found-nicotinic-acetylcholine-receptors)

3. **Alpha-4 beta-2 receptors are the key brain target** — most of nicotine's central effects, including appetite suppression and metabolism increase, are mediated by nicotine binding to alpha-4 beta-2 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors — particularly on POMC neurons in the hypothalamus. [00:25:12](transcript.md#002512-nicotine-effects-on-the-brain-appetite-dopamine-gaba)

4. **Dopamine + GABA suppression = double accelerator** — nicotine increases dopamine release from the nucleus accumbens via the mesolimbic (VTA-to-accumbens) pathway AND simultaneously suppresses GABAergic inhibitory tone — pushing the accelerator while removing the brake. [00:25:12](transcript.md#002512-nicotine-effects-on-the-brain-appetite-dopamine-gaba)

5. **Attentional spotlighting via nucleus basalis** — nicotine raises acetylcholine from nucleus basalis neurons, creating a neurochemical spotlight that intensifies whatever neural circuits are currently active. Focus narrows onto the task at hand. Effects onset in 2–15 minutes; cognitive enhancement lasts ~30–45 minutes (half-life 1–2 hours). [00:32:11](transcript.md#003211-nicotine-acetylcholine-attentional-spotlighting)

6. **Norepinephrine from locus coeruleus drives alertness** — nicotine triggers norepinephrine release from the locus coeruleus, which projects broadly across the brain as a wake-up signal, raising energy and arousal in parallel with the acetylcholine and dopamine effects. [00:37:29](transcript.md#003729-nicotine-norepinephrine-alertnessenergy)

7. **Sympathetic activation + skeletal muscle relaxation simultaneously** — heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac contractibility rise; blood vessels constrict (reducing penile girth and tissue blood flow); yet skeletal muscle paradoxically relaxes. This combination creates an ideal state for cognitive work but is counterproductive for physical performance. [00:46:47](transcript.md#004647-nicotine-effects-on-body-sympathetic-tone)

8. **Appetite suppression via POMC neurons** — nicotine binds alpha-4 beta-2 receptors on hypothalamic POMC neurons, increasing their activity and suppressing hunger partly by reducing the neural impulse to chew. Metabolism increases 2–5% transiently. Weight gain after quitting drives relapse, especially in younger female users. [00:41:10](transcript.md#004110-nicotine-effects-on-appetite-metabolism)

9. **Vaping resembles crack cocaine in pharmacokinetics** — vaping delivers nicotine to the brain faster than cigarette smoking (faster even than intranasal cocaine). The steeper the dopamine rise in the mesolimbic pathway, the more addictive the substance. Vaping is harder to quit than cigarette smoking. Young users' brains get wired to expect rapid, self-controlled dopamine delivery on a precise schedule. [01:13:56](transcript.md#011356-vaping-nicotine-rates-of-effect-onset-dopamine-addiction-depression)

10. **Vaping strongly associated with depression** — repeated vaping downregulates the dopamine system so that only nicotine can trigger meaningful dopamine release. During use reduction or abstinence, dopamine falls below baseline, producing depression. The mesolimbic pathway is the only pathway for all reward (goals, relationships, athletics), so its impairment generalizes. [01:13:56](transcript.md#011356-vaping-nicotine-rates-of-effect-onset-dopamine-addiction-depression)

11. **Every delivery device damages endothelial cells** — cigarettes, vaping, dipping, and snuffing all disrupt blood vessel lining. Cigarettes contain 4,000–7,000 toxins; key carcinogens are tar, ammonia, formaldehyde, and carbon dioxide. Each pack per day reduces lifespan by an estimated 14 years. Dipping causes a 50-fold increase in mouth cancer. [00:58:35](transcript.md#005835-smoking-vaping-dipping-snuffing-carcinogens-endothelial-cells)

12. **Cold turkey succeeds only 5%; 75% relapse in week one** — quitting without support has a 5% success rate; of those, 65% relapse within a year. Withdrawal begins ~4 hours after last use. The core mechanism: homeostatic downregulation lowers baseline dopamine and autonomic arousal below pre-smoking levels, so quitters feel worse than they ever did before they started. [01:09:23](transcript.md#010923-how-to-quit-smoking-nicotine-cravings-withdrawal)

13. **Clinical hypnosis (Reveri) achieves 23% single-session cessation** — Dr. David Spiegel's Stanford protocol has the patient direct their own brain changes toward cessation (not stage hypnosis). The Reveri app replicates the clinical session. Monthly or weekly reinforcement sessions are recommended post-cessation to maintain the circuits preventing relapse. [01:25:06](transcript.md#012506-tool-quitting-smoking-clinical-hypnosis-reveri)

14. **Bupropion (Wellbutrin) raises success to ~20%** — a dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor that offsets withdrawal-induced mood drop. Requires a prescription. Contraindicated with seizure risk, liver/renal disease, and benzodiazepines. Typically 300 mg/day in two 150 mg doses or slow-release formula. [01:30:16](transcript.md#013016-bupropion-wellbutrin-quitting-smoking)

15. **Rotating nicotine replacement formats is most effective** — patch (~1 week) → gum (~1 week) → nasal spray creates a variable (random intermittent) dopamine release schedule. This prevents the brain from locking onto one amplitude/timing expectation and is more effective than staying on one format. [01:36:24](transcript.md#013624-tool-a-nicotine-replacement-schedule-to-quit-smoking-nicotine-patchgum)

16. **Alcohol dramatically increases relapse probability** — significant interaction between alcohol consumption and nicotine relapse; avoiding alcohol is especially critical during and after the cessation attempt. [01:41:52](transcript.md#014152-tool-biological-homeostasis-nicotine-withdrawal-the-first-week-strategy)

## Tools & Protocols

### Brief Daily Meditation for Focus
- **What:** 13-minute daily meditation shown in Wendy Suzuki's NYU lab to increase sustained attention not just post-session but persistently at all times.
- **How:** Sit or lie down, close eyes, direct attention to a point roughly one inch behind the forehead. When attention drifts, refocus to that same point. The act of refocusing is the mechanism of improvement.
- **When:** Daily; missing one day is fine, just return the next. Ideally before cognitive work.
- **Ref:** [00:03:41](transcript.md#000341-tool-brief-daily-meditation-focus)

### Alpha GPC for Acute Focus Boost
- **What:** Choline supplement raising acetylcholine and epinephrine for acute focus. Positioned as an occasional booster, not a daily replacement for behavioral tools.
- **How:** 300 mg taken 10–30 minutes before a demanding cognitive or physical work bout. If concerned about TMAO elevation (cardiovascular marker), pair with 600 mg garlic capsule to offset.
- **When:** Occasionally before demanding cognitive sessions; behavioral tools (meditation, deliberate focus) are preferred first.
- **Ref:** [00:05:59](transcript.md#000559-the-arrow-model-of-focus-alpha-gpc-garlic-supplements)

### Clinical Hypnosis for Smoking Cessation (Reveri)
- **What:** Stanford-developed clinical hypnosis protocol by Dr. David Spiegel. 23% complete cessation in one session vs. 5% cold turkey. Patient directs their own brain state — not stage hypnosis. App also covers sleep and focus.
- **How:** Access reveri.com or the Reveri app; select the smoking cessation module. App session mirrors the in-clinic Stanford protocol.
- **When:** As primary quitting intervention. After quitting, reinforce monthly or weekly to maintain the neural circuits preventing relapse.
- **Ref:** [01:25:06](transcript.md#012506-tool-quitting-smoking-clinical-hypnosis-reveri) | [reveri.com](https://www.reveri.com)

### Bupropion (Wellbutrin) for Smoking Cessation
- **What:** Prescription dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor; offsets withdrawal mood drop. Success rate ~20%, comparable to clinical hypnosis. Also an antidepressant alternative when SSRIs cause intolerable side effects.
- **How:** Typically 300 mg/day in two 150 mg doses or slow-release formula, prescribed and supervised by a physician. Requires screening for seizure risk, liver/renal disease, and benzodiazepine/sedative use (contraindicated).
- **When:** Started on or before quit date; continued through the withdrawal period.
- **Ref:** [01:30:16](transcript.md#013016-bupropion-wellbutrin-quitting-smoking)

### Nicotine Replacement Tapering (Rotating Formats)
- **What:** Non-combustion, non-vaping nicotine delivery (patch, gum, nasal spray) to maintain blood nicotine while removing the harmful device, then gradually reduce dose. Rotating formats every ~1 week is most effective because varying absorption kinetics keep dopamine unpredictable (random intermittent schedule), reducing expectation-driven craving.
- **How:** Patch ~1 week → switch to gum ~1 week → switch to nasal spray ~1 week; reduce dose/frequency across that arc. Combine with hypnosis or bupropion for best outcomes. Consult physician for personalized dosing.
- **When:** As the primary cessation bridge; duration based on use history and physician guidance.
- **Ref:** [01:36:24](transcript.md#013624-tool-a-nicotine-replacement-schedule-to-quit-smoking-nicotine-patchgum)

### Biological Homeostasis / First-Week Survival Strategy
- **What:** Framework for surviving days 1–7 post-cessation — the 75%-relapse window where homeostatic downregulation has dropped baseline dopamine and autonomic arousal below pre-use levels. Feeling worse than before starting is expected, biological, and temporary.
- **How:** During hours normally used for smoking/vaping, substitute: cold showers or ice baths (shown by Dr. Anna Lembke's research to boost dopamine), exercise, and positive social interactions. Combine with hypnosis and/or bupropion. Avoid alcohol completely during this window.
- **When:** Days 1–7 post-cessation. After week one, relapse probability drops substantially; ongoing monthly/weekly hypnosis reinforcement is advised.
- **Ref:** [01:41:52](transcript.md#014152-tool-biological-homeostasis-nicotine-withdrawal-the-first-week-strategy)
