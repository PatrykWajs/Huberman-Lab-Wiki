## [00:16:19] How the Brain Interprets the Body & Surrounding Environment; Mindfulness

I'd like to spend the next 10 minutes or so talking about the neuroscience of meditation. I'm not going to just list off a bunch of different brain areas that are active during meditation — that wouldn't be useful. What I will tell you is that you can actually manipulate those brain areas. You can turn up the activity in certain brain areas and turn down the activity in specific brain areas with specific elements of a meditation practice.

There are a few brain areas I'd like to arm you with. First is the prefrontal cortex — specifically the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. Dorsal means up, lateral means to the side, so if you touch the left side of your head and move your hand just toward the midline, you're roughly over the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. When this area is active, you are in a great position to interpret what's going on with you emotionally, to interpret your bodily signals of comfort or discomfort, and then to make really good decisions based on that interpretation.

That's because the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex is in direct communication with another brain area called the anterior cingulate cortex, or ACC. The ACC is interpreting a lot of different things about bodily signals — how fast you're breathing, whether your heart is beating quickly or slowly, and more importantly, whether that heart rate is appropriate for the circumstance you're in. If you're running up a hill and your heart is beating fast, that's appropriate. But if you're sitting in a calm room and your heart starts beating very quickly for no apparent reason, you're going to interpret that as uncomfortable or inappropriate for the context.

The ACC gets input from areas like the amygdala — your threat detection centers — but also from an enormous number of other areas of your brain and body, including your heart, your gut, your lungs. It's an absolutely critical station for making sense of what's going on in your body.

The third structure in this triad is the insula. The insula is another area that is interpreting signals of what's going on in your brain and body, and it's also interpreting information about what's going on outside of you. So your insula is saying, for instance, "Hey, this is a steep hill I'm running up, and therefore the heavy breathing and burning in my lungs makes sense." Whereas if you're sitting calmly and you suddenly feel very uncomfortable — your stomach doesn't feel right, or you start having what feels like a panic attack — in large part, that's because the shift in your bodily sensations doesn't match anything in the outside world.

So these three areas — the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, the anterior cingulate cortex, and the insula — are constantly working together to figure out what's going on inside you, whether those sensations make sense for the situation, and whether you are doing the right things as a consequence. A major emphasis of a meditation practice is to make us more mindful. Mindfulness includes something about being present — not necessarily present to one's surroundings, but present to one's bodily sensations, breathing, and thoughts in the moment.

## [00:26:07] Neuroscience of Meditation; Perceptual Spotlights

Let's now explore what a generic meditation practice looks like and evaluate how that changes the activity of these neural circuits. For most people — about 75% — a meditation practice is going to involve stopping movement, sitting or lying down, and in most cases, closing one's eyes, although it is absolutely not required.

When we close our eyes, we shut down a major avenue of what's called exteroception. We sense things inside our body all the time, and we sense things from outside of us all the time. Sensation is distinct from perception — perception is the sensations that we happen to be paying attention to. At any given moment, you are sensing many things: sound waves hitting your ears, pressure receptors on the bottoms of your feet sensing your shoes. But you're not perceiving them until you place your attention on them.

The way perception works is that you have so-called spotlights of attention. You can't perceive everything all at once. You have spotlights of perception that can be very narrow — for instance, focused on your right big toe — or you can broaden that spotlight to include both feet, your whole body, or the entire room. Perception is like a spotlight. There are very good data that we can split our attention into two but probably not more than two spotlights, and we can make those spotlights either very broad and diffuse or very narrow.

You can practice this now. Pick a spot on the wall and focus intensely on one small location, or broaden that spotlight to include the entire scene. You can also focus a spotlight on your body, say on the left upper portion of your chest, and split your attention between that and something outside you. It's very hard, although not impossible, to have three perceptual spotlights, but most people can split to two points of attention pretty easily.

Your ability to direct your attention to specific things in your environment or within your body — to split or merge those points of attention, to dial up the intensity of how closely you're paying attention — all of that is under your power because of the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex.

## [00:33:41] Interoception vs. Exteroception

When you sit or lie down and close your eyes to meditate, that's a tremendous shift in your perceptual ability. When we close our eyes, we shut down one of the major avenues for sensory input — vision — and there's a tendency for those perceptual spotlights to focus more on the surface of our skin and inside our bodies.

There are two ends of a continuum of perception. On one end is interoception — everything that we sense at the level of our skin and inward: sensations inside our stomach, our heart beating, how hot or cold we feel. At the other end is exteroception — perception of everything that's outside or beyond the confines of our skin.

By shutting our eyes and directing attention toward our so-called third eye center (just behind the forehead, which is the prefrontal cortex), or focusing on breathing — the movement of the stomach or diaphragm — we shift what's ordinarily a split perceptual state toward interoception. When we shift to interoception, the ACC and insula really ramp up their levels of neural activity, and that makes perfect sense because those are areas of your brain registering the various sensations of bodily state.

Many people are very interoceptively aware naturally, even without a meditation practice. Others are not. A pretty good measure of interoceptive awareness is your ability to count your heartbeats without placing your fingers anywhere to take your pulse. Meditative practices will improve your interoceptive awareness, but heightened interoceptive awareness is not always beneficial. Many people with excessive levels of anxiety are excessively anxious because they are very keenly aware of any subtle shift in heart rate, breathing, or stomach sensations. On the other hand, people with very little interoceptive awareness can ignore warning signs like high blood pressure or a heart attack — they're carrying about life focused on everything external with no awareness of their own body.

Closing your eyes increases interoception. Opening your eyes dramatically increases exteroception, just automatically, because 40% or more of your brain is dedicated to vision. For people who are blind or have low vision, this process translates to the auditory domain — blocking out sound with noise-canceling headphones creates an equivalent shift toward interoception.

## [00:42:20] Default Mode Network, Continuum of Interoception & Exteroception

I asked you to ask yourself whether you are more interoceptively or exteroceptively aware. Some of you won't be able to answer that question, and if you can't, chances are that you are effectively sliding along that continuum depending on activities. People with social anxiety, for instance, when someone talks to them, will be thinking about whether their cheeks are flushing or whether they look right — these are normal responses that speak to whether you tend toward interoceptive or exteroceptive awareness.

Now, there have been studies of what a meditation practice can do in terms of moving you along this continuum. To illustrate this, I want to start with a description of a classic study about something called the default mode network.

The default mode network is a collection of different brain areas that are active when we're not doing much of anything and certainly when we are not focused on one particular task or conversation. The default mode network generates mind wandering — our thoughts drifting from the past to the present to the future. Human beings can split their thinking into any two of those three time domains simultaneously, although it's very difficult to split into all three simultaneously.

The paper I'd like to share is called "A Wandering Mind Is an Unhappy Mind," published in the journal Science by Matthew Killingsworth and Dan Gilbert in 2010. They used smartphones to ping over 2,200 adults many times per day, asking "What are you feeling right now?" and "What are you doing right now?" They were looking for the match or mismatch between what people were doing and what they were feeling.

The important points from this large data set: first, people's minds wandered frequently regardless of what they were doing — in nearly half of the samples, people were thinking about something other than what they were doing. The one exception was that people tended to be very focused when making love. Second, people were less happy when their minds were wandering than when they were not, and this was true during all activities. Third — and this is most important — what people were thinking at a given moment was a far better predictor of their happiness than what they were doing.

Even if people were doing pleasant work and thinking about something else that was pleasant, that mismatch between being in an activity and having their mind elsewhere led them to report feeling more unhappy in that moment. Their conclusion states it beautifully: "A human mind is a wandering mind, and a wandering mind is an unhappy mind. The ability to think about what is not happening in a moment is a cognitive achievement that comes at an emotional cost."

This paper is absolutely key in understanding why a meditation practice is so important — because a meditation practice is really about adjusting your place along that interoceptive-exteroceptive continuum to what you happen to be experiencing in that moment. And if we take the work of Killingsworth and Gilbert seriously, being mindful — being present to what we are doing in a given moment — is one of the essential keys to happiness and improved mood, even if what we are doing is unpleasant.

## [00:53:30] Tools: Interoceptive or Exteroceptive Bias, Meditation Challenge

This brings us to a tool that any and all of us can use, whether or not we tend to be interoceptively dominant or exteroceptively dominant.

There is a simple test you can do. Sit down or lie down, close your eyes, and assess whether your attention tends to fleet to things outside of you — cars honking, people in the room — or whether you can focus on your internal landscape to the exclusion of exteroception. This will depend on context and situation, even how well rested you are. That's exactly the point — this is the sort of thing you want to do every time you decide to do a meditation practice.

I suggest you use this to determine what meditation to do at any given moment. If you stop, close your eyes, and can access and focus your attention primarily on your internal state, I would suggest doing a three-minute meditation in which you deliberately exterocept — focusing on something external — because I want most people to have an adaptive mechanism so they can slide along that continuum and don't default to whatever's easiest for them in that moment.

If instead you find that you're pulled to things happening outside of you when you try to focus inward, then actively engage an interoceptive meditation where you're focusing your perception on events at the level of the surface of your skin and internally.

As my graduate advisor used to say when things were hard: "That means you're learning." If something were easy, if you can perform any activity perfectly, there is zero reason for your neural circuits to change. It's the friction, the feeling that something is hard, that turns on the mechanisms that allow you to change your neural circuitry. So I'm encouraging you to embark on meditative practices that are not your default — to go against the grain of where your interoceptive or exteroceptive bias happens to be at a given moment.

If you are in a crowded airport and finding everything very distracting, that would be a great time to do some interoceptive-focused meditation. Whereas if you're really in your head — looping thoughts about the past and present, maybe even in obsessive thought — that would be an ideal time to do a short meditation focused on something external to you. In both cases, you are pushing back against your default mode network. And going against your bias toward being more interoceptive or exteroceptive will be far more effective at reducing or shifting the activity of that default mode network.

## [01:01:48] State & Trait Changes, Interoceptive & Exteroceptive Meditations, Refocusing

That approach is immensely beneficial both for the immediate changes you experience — what others have called a state change — and it can also lead to more neuroplasticity, more changes in the brain circuits that underlie your default mode network, leading to what are called trait changes.

I want to be very clear that I am not the first to make this state versus trait distinction. That's a distinction raised in a really wonderful book: "Altered Traits: Science Reveals How Meditation Changes Your Mind, Brain, and Body" by Daniel Goleman and Richard Davidson. Published around 2016-2017, this book really captured the most essential elements of the science of meditation. They very clearly distinguish between state changes and trait changes — trait changes being the more long-lasting ones.

My read of this book and the literature that follows is that when you sit down to meditate, it is going to be most effective to do that interoceptive-exteroceptive bias assessment — ask yourself whether you are more in your head or outside your head — and then do a meditation practice that runs counter to where you happen to be.

For interoceptive-biased meditation: set a timer, sit or lie down, close your eyes, focus on that third eye center behind your forehead or focus on your breathing or your bodily sensations.

For exteroceptive-biased meditation: pick a focal point outside or beyond the confines of your skin — a point on the wall, a plant, a point on the horizon far away. Your visual system will fatigue a little when you concentrate your visual focus at that location. It is perfectly okay and necessary to blink. This is not beaming a particular location in space with your eyelids held open.

One of the key elements of any meditative practice — whether interoceptive or exteroceptive — is that it's really a refocusing practice. The more times you have to yank yourself back into attending to one specific thing — the more times your mind wanders and you bring it back — actually, the more effective that practice is. If your mind never darts away and you don't have to bring it back, there's no neuroplasticity; nothing needs to change because your nervous system is performing perfectly.

If you're somebody who tries to meditate and finds your mind just wanders, remember: every time you scruff yourself and pull yourself back to focusing on some location externally or back on your breath or third eye center, each one of those isn't just an opportunity to do better — they are essential to the improvement process. Think about them as ascending a staircase of refocusing. Every time you refocus, you're going up one more level.

This is borne out in neuroimaging data. A lot of people think expert meditators can focus with laser precision. Actually, what they're better at is refocusing more quickly and consistently over time. A classic study done in Japan had people with varying levels of meditation experience listen to 20 tones repeated over and over. Expert meditators could stay focused on all 20 tones, whereas most people habituate by the 10th or 11th. But the modern neuroimaging studies have shown that expert meditators don't have better focus such that they're staying in a very narrow trench — what they're doing is exiting focus and going back in more quickly, over and over again. So rather than thinking about your ability to focus, think about your ability to refocus.

## [01:10:30] "Third Eye Center" & Wandering Thoughts

I want to talk briefly about this third eye center because it turns out to be pretty interesting. The third eye is actually a name given to another structure: the pineal gland. The pineal is the gland that makes melatonin — at night when it gets dark, it secretes melatonin, which makes you sleepy and helps you fall asleep but not stay asleep.

Descartes asserted that the pineal was the seat of the soul because it was the one structure in the brain that was not bilaterally symmetric — it sits as a single, midline structure. In birds and lizards and snakes, the pineal actually responds to light directly. Some species have thin skulls or holes in the top of the skull that allow light to go directly in and activate the pineal to suppress melatonin and control wakefulness and sleep rhythms. In birds, thin skulls allow light to penetrate.

In humans, the pineal sits deep, deep near the fourth ventricle, and light absolutely cannot get there. So the idea that the pineal is the third eye in humans is not true — it just isn't.

When people talk about the third eye center in meditation, they're talking about the prefrontal cortex. The brain tissue itself has no sensory neurons — you can remove the skull and do brain surgery on somebody who is wide awake and they don't need any anesthetic on the brain itself. So normally, we are perceiving things we're sensing — either externally (exteroception) or internally (interoception). But by focusing our perception and attention not on our bodily surface but to a point a couple of centimeters behind our forehead, we bring that perceptual spotlight to a location in which there is no sensation. There's nothing to feel there.

When we do that — close our eyes and focus on that third eye center, which is the prefrontal cortex — something else happens. When we are not thinking about and perceiving our sensations, because there are none there, our thoughts and emotions and memories sort of geyser up and take on more prominence in our perception. I'm thinking things all the time. By focusing my attention on the one organ for which I have no sensation — my brain — thoughts, feelings, and memories start to grow in their prominence in my awareness. This is why when you sit down to a meditation practice with eyes closed, focused on that third eye center, the thoughts seem to come by in waves. Actually, the best way to stop thinking is to really focus on something external or to focus on sensation — that's less thinking than it is perceiving senses.

I believe, based on the data, that most people have an interoceptive bias — focused more on what's going on internally than what's happening externally. And we hear so often about the need to do a meditation practice that allows us to focus inward and that we're getting yanked around by all the stressors of life. But the data clearly point to the fact that being mindful and aware of what's happening not just with us but external to us — what other people are saying and doing — can also really enhance our sense of well-being and happiness.

## [01:20:46] Meditation: Practice Types, Focal Points & Consistency

Let's briefly recap. We've talked about the brain networks activated during meditation: prefrontal cortex, ACC, the insula. We've talked about the difference between interoception and exteroception and the importance of assessing where you are along that continuum. You can be right in the middle of that continuum — if you sit down to meditate and find you're smack dab in the middle, I still suggest doing a meditation that is either exteroceptive-biased or interoceptive-biased. If you find you're more in your head or in your body, focus on an exteroceptive-biased meditation to build up that set of circuits, and vice versa.

On the question of how long to meditate: Dr. Wendy Suzuki from New York University's laboratory has popularized the 13-minute meditation — a traditional third eye, interoceptive-biased, focus-on-breathing meditation done daily for about eight weeks. That study showed great improvement in mood, ability to sleep, cognitive ability and focus, and memory. That's a terrific one.

Could you get away with five minutes or three minutes? My laboratory has shown benefits in stress reduction and improvement in sleep with a five-minute-a-day meditation. In trying to establish how long you should meditate, I would ask yourself a couple of questions. First: what is a practice you can do consistently? If you can only do one meditation session per week, I'd encourage you to go longer — maybe 10, 15, or even 30 minutes. If you can set aside five or ten or fifteen minutes per day and meditate every day, you have more flexibility in how long you meditate.

Just like with exercise, the key component is consistency — borne out in all the data covered in "Altered Traits" and in all the recent studies since that book was published. Consistency is key. Don't necessarily burden yourself with always having to do the same amount or duration of meditation.

Another key component of meditation that we haven't yet addressed is the pattern of breathing you embrace.

## [01:24:10] Breathwork: Cyclic Hyperventilation, Box Breathing & Interoception

The pattern of breathing you embrace during your meditation practice can itself be its own form of meditation. These days, we hear a lot about breathwork. I think we need to credit Wim Hof — certainly there were people before Wim doing deliberate breathwork, but around 2015, Wim Hof grew in recognition for a particular style of breathing that, in the laboratory, we call cyclic hyperventilation. Wim Hof is a Dutchman known for holding many world records for deliberate cold exposure, who also became expert in using breathing to manage those challenges.

Cyclic hyperventilation — big inhales, exhales, big inhales, exhales, typically in through the nose and out through the mouth — is very clear from studies both on Wim specifically and on the general population that this pattern generates a lot of adrenaline release. It "heats up" the body, raises body temperature, and the liberation of adrenaline shifts the state of the brain and body. Wim Hof breathing, or tummo breathing, or cyclic hyperventilation, is typically considered its own practice — not typical of most meditations discussed in the research literature.

More typically, a meditation practice involves slowing one's breathing. This could be cyclic breathing (inhale, exhale, inhale, exhale), or doubling up on inhales and then exhaling, or controlling the duration as in box breathing — where the inhale, hold, exhale, and hold are of equivalent durations.

Regardless of the cadence used, most meditative practices involve slowing and/or controlling one's breathing in deliberate fashion. This is important because when we focus on our breathing deliberately, we are forced into interoception. The diaphragm — the muscle that creates a specific cadence or depth of breathing — resides inside of us. When we focus on our breathing, we default to focusing on the movement of our diaphragm, our belly, or the rising and falling of our chest. By focusing on our breathing, we shift to interoception.

So we can say that if the first component of meditation is to direct our perception in a deliberate way to a specific location — either on the surface of or within our body or external to our body — then the second element of a meditative practice is the pattern of breathing: can it and should it be deliberate? I believe that, based on what we know about the capacity for specific patterns of breathing to shift our brain state, controlling one's pattern of breathing during meditation can be enormously useful.

## [01:30:41] Tool: Meditation Breathwork, Cyclic vs. Complex Breathwork

How should we breathe during meditation? There are some general rules of respiration physiology that can help. Before beginning a meditative practice, ask yourself a question: do you want to be more relaxed than you are at present, or do you want to be more alert when you exit the meditation? Simple question — you can decide from session to session or even switch within a session.

Here is the general rule, oversimplified intentionally so you can simply apply the tool: if your inhales are longer and/or more vigorous than your exhales, you will tend to be more alert. If you emphasize longer duration and/or more vigorous exhales relative to your inhales, you will tend to relax more and calm your nervous system. This is simply based on the way neural circuits like the pre-Bötzinger nucleus and the parafacial nucleus govern respiration physiology and alertness.

If you'd like to stay at the same level of alertness-calmness with which you started, keep your inhales and exhales relatively balanced. The introduction of things like breath holds (box breathing) or Wim Hof breathing — typically 25 or 30 deep inhale-exhales and then exhale all air, hold for 15 to 60 seconds — is a whole subject unto itself. For sake of meditation, the key thing to understand is that if you are going to do a complicated breathing practice, it will by necessity shift much of your attention to the breathing practice itself.

Cyclic breathing — where inhales always follow exhales — relies on a specific brain center called the pre-Bötzinger complex, discovered by Jack Feldman at UCLA, who was a guest on this podcast. If you are doubling up on your inhale (two inhales and then an exhale) — a pattern my laboratory has studied extensively — that relies on a different brain center, the parafacial nucleus. If you are engaging in noncyclic breathing or deliberately emphasizing inhales or exhales, some portion of your attention will be devoted to following that breathing practice.

If you are very much caught in your own head and want to get out of your head, your meditation should be focused on exteroceptive bias — focusing on something external — and I would encourage you to use a natural cyclic pattern of breathing so your attention can more easily focus elsewhere. If you are feeling pulled out of yourself and want to ground your awareness in your body, I would encourage you to use a deliberate, somewhat unnatural pattern of breathing — which by definition will force you to attend to what's going on interoceptively.

We are working on formalizing these frameworks through research with Dr. David Spiegel, who is an expert in hypnosis, associate chair of psychiatry at Stanford, and has been a guest on this podcast. We believe that a breathwork practice itself can be meditative, a meditation practice can include breathing, but the more the meditative practice focuses on the breathing itself, the more interoceptive-biased it will be.

## [01:39:22] Interoception vs. Dissociation, Trauma

This brings us to a larger theme about which particular types of meditative practices are going to be best for certain people, especially people using meditation to combat mood-based, sleep-based, or focus-based challenges. There are now tens of thousands of scientific studies showing benefits of meditation: enhancing sleep, enhancing focus, reducing inflammatory cytokines, improving outcomes in cancer, reducing pain, improving mood, reducing symptoms of ADHD, and on and on. Rather than focus on all those beautiful studies today, I've been focused on what sorts of brain and body changes occur when we meditate and what really constitutes a meditation practice.

We have this continuum of perception — interoception vs. exteroception — and we've been talking about breathing. There's a third major component that I'd like to raise. Dr. Spiegel and I are working to formalize this through research, and it's a separate continuum which is the continuum between interoception and dissociation.

Dissociation is often talked about in the context of a negative event. Dissociation is unfortunately — or I should say, adaptively — associated with traumatic events. Victims of violent or sexual trauma report feeling out of body during the experience or during recollection. Dissociation has also been described when people are in traumatic accidents or witness violence. First responders will talk about dissociating when they arrive on a scene of a car crash with incredible damage to bodies.

Dissociation lies at the opposite end of a continuum with interoception. We can define them as follows: interoception refers to a process by which your nervous system — your brain and connections with your body — senses, interprets, integrates, and regulates signals originating from within the body, providing a moment-to-moment mapping of your internal landscape at both a conscious and unconscious level. Dissociation is the opposite — a lack of bodily awareness or a removal of one's conscious experience from one's bodily experience and awareness.

The optimal place to reside on the continuum between interoception and dissociation is somewhere in the middle. We don't want to be dissociated from life's experiences, but we also don't want everything that happens in the world to profoundly impact our heart rate and our breathing. There are certain people who have challenges with what's called narrative distancing — they see someone in a movie getting hit and they almost flinch as if they're getting hit themselves. This speaks to the push-pull between our recognition that we are each distinct entities and the healthy desire to be attached to others' experiences.

Why raise yet another continuum? Because if we want to think about how meditation can serve our mental health, there's a very particular mental model that incorporates this interoceptive-dissociative continuum. If you are extremely interoceptive, you're feeling everything in your body, and those feelings nearly completely account for all of your experience. On the dissociative end, you can see and react to what's going on, but your bodily response is essentially shut down — sometimes paralyzed, sometimes still engaging in behaviors but dissociated.

## [01:47:43] Model of Interoception & Dissociation Continuum

Let's talk about this model and then a meditative practice that can anchor us at the healthy location along that continuum.

Let's imagine the ideal mental health state. In this version, you take the interoceptive-to-dissociative continuum and fold up the sides so it looks like a V. At one end, you have pure interoception; at the other end, pure dissociation. You are like a ball bearing at the base of that V — in a trench of perfectly balanced interoception and dissociation. You can feel things and register what's going on in the outside world, but your feelings are not overwhelmed by what's happening externally. You're in a perfect place to make rational decisions and still feel your feelings.

Nobody achieves or maintains that state. More typically, the model is a U shape — at one end, interoception; at the other end, dissociation — and your state is like a ball bearing at the base of the U that gets pushed from side to side. Your heart races a little because of something bad or good, and the ball bearing shifts toward interoception a little. Or your mind drifts a little while watching a movie, and the ball bearing shifts toward the dissociative state a little. Most people would agree that being mentally healthy involves this U-shaped model — it can shift back and forth, but it's not extreme.

Then there are states, a flat continuum, where you are a ball bearing at one location or another depending on whether you're very engrossed in a movie, or in an activity with a partner or friend that has you very engrossed. There are states in life where matching someone else's state is healthy and good, and then there are states where matching someone's anger or distress is not helpful.

A number of practices — including meditation, exercise, good sleep, therapy, journaling, and social engagement — are designed to bring up the edges of that flat continuum into more of a U or concave shape so that the ball bearing stays near the middle. Then there's the extreme that almost all clinicians would agree is pathologic: a convex shape, like a mountain peak. That little ball bearing at the top can either drop all the way to pure interoception — just feeling one's feelings beyond any ability to pay attention to anything else, even happy or manic states so extreme they're inappropriate for context — or drop to the other side where you're so dissociated you're truly checked out. That shape is pathologic because you are either completely absorbed or completely checked out.

This is a simple model — not exhaustive — but it incorporates a lot of what we think about when we talk about mental health and the ability to be mentally stable, to feel one's feelings, but still be actively engaged in what's happening around us.

## [01:53:39] Meditation & Dissociation: Mood, Bias & Corresponding Challenge

Sleep deprivation, for instance, tends to take us away from the V-shape or U-shape and makes the continuum more convex — we're either completely checked out and exhausted or completely labile, yanked around by whatever experience is happening, unable to manage. So sleep is the foundational layer of mental health, physical health, and performance because it tends to put us in a healthier place — in the middle of that continuum.

A meditative practice done regularly, because it can make us more interoceptively aware or more exteroceptively aware (which is itself a mild form of dissociation, which isn't always bad), can actually teach us to deliberately move along this continuum. This hasn't been discussed a whole lot in the literature. The clinical psychiatry literature will say that interoceptive awareness is terrific except for the person so aware of their internal functioning that they can't engage in the world. And the trauma literature says dissociation is terrible in cases of trauma where it can put people in positions of repeating damaging behavior — but dissociation can also be very adaptive, allowing people to create narrative distancing so they don't get pulled into every argument.

In thinking about the positive effects of meditation on mood, there are two aspects. First: being present to one's experience correlates with increased happiness — mind wandering correlates with being more unhappy. That was the "Wandering Mind" study. Second: a meditation can make us more present, but if we don't pay attention to whether we are becoming more present to interoception or exteroception, or whether our bias is toward dissociation versus interoception, the meditation can actually make things worse. If you're somebody with tremendous interoceptive awareness, meditating on your internal state may not be good — and there's some evidence it may actually be bad.

For example, in Wendy Suzuki's study showing that the 13-minute-a-day meditation benefits focus, mood, and sleep, it's also very clear that for a number of people, if they do that typical third eye meditation too close to bedtime, they have a hard time falling asleep. That makes sense — meditation is a focus and refocus practice, and falling asleep involves turning off your focus, fragmenting thoughts, and drifting off. This is why I'm a big fan of non-sleep deep rest (NSDR) or yoga nidra — those protocols are anti-focus practices, whereas meditation tends to be a focusing practice.

An exteroceptive-biased meditation — where you focus on things outside your body — can be wonderful for somebody who tends to focus too much on their inner landscape and narrative. But for people not in touch with their emotions, it can drive them down exactly the wrong path.

The solution is simple: just do the opposite of where your bias lies. If you're tilted toward interoception, do an exteroceptive-focused practice. If you're more dissociative and focused on events outside your body and want more interoceptive awareness and feeling, do a third eye center or breathing-focused practice.

## [02:00:18] Meditation & Sleep: Yoga Nidra, Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR)

One of the reasons many people meditate is that they've heard meditation can replace sleep or reduce one's overall sleep need. I dove into the literature on this, and found some very simple protocols that I think any and all of us can leverage to sleep better and maybe even reduce the total amount of sleep that we need.

I want to point to the study titled "Brief, daily meditation enhances attention, memory, mood, and emotional regulation in non-experienced meditators" — again, from Wendy Suzuki's lab. She was a guest on the Huberman Lab podcast, is now the Dean of Arts and Sciences at New York University, and has run a laboratory focused on memory for a long time. I keep returning to this paper because they used so many measures and the results were really interesting. The 13-minute-a-day guided meditation — and the control group in this study listened to a podcast for 13 minutes that did not improve attention, memory, mood, or emotion regulation as much as meditation did. They also found that if people meditated too close to bedtime, they had trouble sleeping, which makes sense because meditation is a focusing and refocusing exercise.

There are other studies that have asserted that doing two 20-minute sessions per day of meditation can reduce the need for sleep. Those results are debated. The interpretation is that the stress reduction brought about by regular meditative practice is good at offsetting some of the cortisol increases associated with reduced sleep, leading people to function cognitively and physically better on reduced sleep than they would had they not been meditating. By meditating, you get the healthy pattern of cortisol release restricted to the early part of the day, inoculating yourself somewhat against unhealthy cortisol release patterns.

A lot of people interpreted this to mean that if you can't sleep, you can just meditate the next day and you'll be fine. That is not supported by the literature. However, there is a practice called yoga nidra — which literally means yoga sleep — that is a body scan practice focused on turning off that prefrontal cortex or reducing its activity. Yoga nidra scripts can be found on YouTube and elsewhere. They are paralleled by NSDR — non-sleep deep rest. I put a 10-minute NSDR protocol on YouTube (search: NSDR Huberman).

Yoga nidra and NSDR have been shown in a fair number of studies to replenish levels of certain neuromodulators like dopamine and to reduce cortisol significantly more than with traditional meditation. There's a nice paper: "Yoga Nidra Practice Shows Improvement in Sleep in Patients with Chronic Insomnia: A Randomized Controlled Trial." The key result: "Salivary cortisol reduced statistically significantly after yoga nidra." We believe this would be paralleled by an equivalent practice of NSDR.

NSDR is a lot like yoga nidra but removes some of the mystical language and intentions, focusing more on physiology and body scans. Jon Kabat-Zinn similarly created what he called mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) — simply mindfulness meditation to reduce stress — partly to remove language barriers. There's also a beautiful study published out of Scandinavia showing that a 30-minute yoga nidra practice can replenish levels of dopamine, putting people in a position to be more action-oriented and focused when they come out of the yoga nidra.

Here's the takeaway: if you want to get better at falling asleep, staying asleep, or falling back asleep if you wake in the middle of the night, yoga nidra or NSDR done any time of day is going to be beneficial. If your goal is to increase your ability to focus, improve your mood, and maneuver yourself deliberately along the interoceptive-exteroceptive or interoceptive-dissociative continuum — to really shift your default mode network from one of mind wandering to one of focus and happiness — then a more traditional third eye center meditation or exteroceptive-focused meditation would be the better choice.

## [02:11:33] Choosing a Meditative Practice; Hypnosis

We've covered a lot of information, and I like to think I've given you some key decisions to make in developing a meditative practice. The most important one, of course, being: what will you do regularly? For people who just want the thing that allows them to feel rested when tired and to adjust their state of mind when not where they want to be — too anxious or too exhausted — a practice like NSDR or yoga nidra will be immensely beneficial, as will a more traditional form of meditation. An app that guides meditation with some information and intention setting, such as the Waking Up app from Sam Harris, can be immensely beneficial.

We've talked about determining where you are on these continuums to dictate the type of meditation practice you should do in a given moment — whether to focus your vision inward with eyes closed or focus outward, whether to do cyclic breathing (allowing your focus to more easily be off your breathing) or noncyclic breathing, whether your breathing is going to be natural or deliberate, and whether your meditation is designed to enhance focus or to relax you.

I also want to mention hypnosis, which I've discussed in detail in the episode with Dr. David Spiegel, our associate chair of psychiatry at Stanford. Hypnosis is distinct from breathwork, yoga nidra, NSDR, and meditation — though it includes some of those components like focusing your attention. It involves directing visual attention outward then inward, a specific kind of breathing, specific imagery. But hypnosis is really designed to fix or address a specific problem, whereas meditation, NSDR, and yoga nidra typically are not. Hypnosis is excellent for dealing with specific issues — clinical data show it can quadruple effectiveness for smoking cessation, reduce insomnia, reduce pain, address trauma, and more, especially when using tools like the Reveri app.

## [02:14:53] Tool: Space-Time Bridging (STB)

I'm guessing some of you are wondering where to start, or if you're already an avid meditator, where to go with all this information. For that reason, I want to offer a particular form of meditation that incorporates all of the features I've talked about in a single practice. For lack of a better name, I call it STB — Space-Time Bridging.

The time component has to do with a very simple fact: when we focus our attention on things close to or within our body, we tend to fine-slice time. You could think of your breath as more or less the second hand on your clock of existence. Whereas when we focus on things far away, we tend to parse time in bigger bins. If you've ever seen an airplane flying at a distance, it looks like it's moving very slowly — but if you were right next to it, it's going 500-600 miles per hour. How you slice the time domain of your life has everything to do with your vision, and the closer things are, the more finely you slice up time. If you focus your visual attention very far away, you're actually taking bigger time bins.

Space-Time Bridging balances interoception and exteroception, balances interoception and dissociation, and crosses the various time domains the brain can encompass using vision.

Here's how to do it — ideally outdoors or at a window:

1. Close your eyes. Focus your attention on your third eye center or your breathing. Try to put 100% of your perceptual awareness onto your breathing or your third eye center. Do this for the duration of three breaths.

2. Open your eyes. Focus on the surface of your body — I find it useful to hold out my hand at arm's distance and focus on the palm of my hand visually. Split your attention about 50/50 between your hand and your breath. Do this for three full inhales and exhales.

3. Look at some location in your immediate environment — maybe 10 to 15 feet away. Focus your attention on that location while also paying attention to your breathing. Do this for three breaths, but now you are in exteroception and interoception simultaneously.

4. Focus your attention at some distance further away — the furthest distance you can see, maybe a horizon. Do this for three breaths while also paying attention to your breathing.

5. Focus on the fact that you are a tiny speck on this big ball floating out in space. Try to acknowledge that you are a small body on this very large body — the Earth — floating in a much larger, expansive universe. Do this for three breaths.

6. Close your eyes and go right back into interoception. Focus on your interoception for three breaths.

You might march through these different locations a few times or back and forth, but typically I will do it once through: pure interoception, palm of hand, some distance in front of me, horizon, the whole globe/universe, back into body.

Why is this useful? It has you deliberately step your awareness through every position along the interoceptive-exteroceptive continuum. Many of us tend to get locked at one location — if you're scrolling your phone for a long period of time, you may forget about your bodily sensations but also forget about other things going on in the world. Being functional in work, in life, in relationships, and including your ability to fall asleep, involves stepping yourself along these different locations — which are not just physical locations but stations within space that, by virtue of how the visual system and the time domain are interlocked, set your mind in a particular time domain.

So much of the fatigue of life and the maladaptive behaviors and emotions that show up are really not about any behaviors or emotions being wrong or right, but rather inappropriately matched to the space-time domain we're in — which is just fancy nerd-speak for saying that being present and mindful is a wonderful byproduct of a meditation practice. The key element is to step yourself through a practice deliberately so that you are flexibly and dynamically able to engage in conversation, then disengage and focus, or focus and then disengage from that work and actually have a conversation or be in the world.

I realize this might sound a little vague — for that reason, I encourage you not to think about it too much but rather to try the practice and see if it works for you. I think it's a good one for people who find that a third eye center or breathing-focused interoceptive meditation is beneficial but they want to try something new, and also for people who tend to be overly exteroceptive and need to bring in more interoceptive awareness.

As we round up, I want to acknowledge that there are an enormous number of rooms within the castle that is meditation — intention setting, mantras, and an enormous variety of different features — that we simply did not have time to go into and for which the research is not completely ironed out yet. In future episodes, I'm going to be sitting down with experts in meditation who can point us toward the utility of those practices, so I eagerly await those conversations and I hope you will join me.
