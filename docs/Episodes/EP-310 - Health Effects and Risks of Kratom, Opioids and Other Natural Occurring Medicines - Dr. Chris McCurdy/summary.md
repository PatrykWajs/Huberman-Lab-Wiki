# Episode 310: Health Effects & Risks of Kratom, Opioids & Other Natural Occurring Medicines - Dr. Chris McCurdy

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Chris McCurdy, professor of medicinal chemistry at the University of Florida and one of the world's leading kratom researchers, joins Andrew Huberman for a deep-dive into the pharmacology, safety, and cultural context of kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) and other plant-derived alkaloid medicines. The conversation covers how kratom's effects change dramatically depending on dose and product form — from whole dried leaf (similar to caffeine use) to concentrated extracts and semi-synthetic isolates (carrying significant opioid-like risk) — and explains why the FDA's failure to classify kratom products into distinct categories has led to widespread consumer confusion and harm. The episode also explores the broader history of how natural plant compounds — from the coca leaf to cacao to soft drink ingredients — have shaped modern medicine and everyday consumer products.

## Key Takeaways

- **Kratom has a biphasic dose-response:** low doses (~1–5g leaf powder) produce stimulant and mood-elevating effects; higher doses (5–10g+) produce opioid-like sedation and analgesia. Traditional Southeast Asian users exploited both ends — morning energy doses and weekend social/sedative use. [00:02:51](transcript.md#000251-kratom-mitragyna-speciosa-origin-effects-low-vs-high-doses)
- **Product form is more important than dose:** whole dried leaf powder requires the body to extract alkaloids slowly; liquid extracts, concentrates, and isolates pre-dissolve alkaloids for rapid absorption, dramatically changing pharmacokinetics — analogous to the difference between consuming cannabis in leaf form versus a concentrated THC tincture. Water is the least efficient extractor; ethanol and oil pre-extract alkaloids, amplifying absorption speed and peak levels. [00:10:07](transcript.md#001007-kratom-traditional-use-vs-commercial-kratom-products-absorption)
- **An estimated 20+ million Americans use kratom daily** — far exceeding official survey estimates of 2–2.5 million — based on 1,950 metric tons/month of raw material imported from Indonesia in 2019. Products now appear in gas stations alongside energy shots, creating accidental ingestion risk especially for children who may take a whole multi-serving bottle. [00:23:16](transcript.md#002316-kratom-products--various-desired-effects-physical-dependence)
- **Kratom contains 20–40 alkaloids; mitragynine is the major one but has only weak opioid receptor affinity.** The minor alkaloid 7-hydroxymitragynine is far more potent at opioid receptors and drives pain relief and euphoria in whole-leaf products. Mitragynine also interacts with adrenergic receptors, contributing to stimulant and mood effects. When manufacturers isolate or concentrate 7-hydroxymitragynine, opioid potency rises sharply — the most concerning safety profile in the market. [01:29:09](transcript.md#012909-kratom-alkaloids-mood--stimulant-effects-multiple-pathways-for-pain-relief)
- **Alkaloids are nitrogen-containing organic molecules** — the same chemical class as caffeine, morphine, cocaine, nicotine, dopamine, and serotonin. Plants produce alkaloids primarily as a chemical defense against insects, fungi, and bacteria; that these molecules also bind mammalian neurotransmitter receptors is an evolutionary coincidence that underlies much of pharmacology. Kratom also contains nicotine — a fact most users are unaware of. [00:36:59](transcript.md#003659-alkaloid-compounds-nitrogen-nicotine-animals--self-experimentation)
- **Kratom should not be used by anyone under 18, and ideally not before the mid-20s** when the prefrontal cortex is fully developed. The developing brain is especially vulnerable to substances interacting with opioid receptors and mood-regulating pathways; adolescents are also more likely to escalate dose due to underdeveloped executive control. [01:09:06](transcript.md#010906-is-it-safe-for-kids-to-consume-kratom-products)
- **Respiratory depression risk from whole-leaf kratom is much lower than from traditional opioids**, but it increases with concentrated extracts and isolates, which lose the natural alkaloid matrix that may provide a ceiling effect. Combining any kratom product with alcohol, benzodiazepines, or other opioids meaningfully raises CNS depression risk. [01:16:56](transcript.md#011656-respiratory-depression--kratom-products)
- **Vein-color marketing (white/red/green/brown) is unreliable.** Alkaloid composition varies more by growing conditions, harvesting, drying, and supply chain than by vein color. No one has studied the non-alkaloid chemistry — terpenes, tannins, steroids, flavonoids — of kratom at all. [01:44:35](transcript.md#014435-white-red--brown-vein-kratom-leaf-processing-terpenes)
- **Kratom alkaloids are metabolized by CYP3A4** and related cytochrome P450 liver enzymes, creating significant drug-interaction potential with antidepressants, antifungals, HIV medications, and others. When CYP3A4 is inhibited, kratom alkaloid blood levels can rise unexpectedly, increasing seizure risk. Anyone on prescription medications should consult a physician before using any kratom product. [01:58:03](transcript.md#015803-kratom-drug-interactions--seizure-opioids)
- **Kratom is being studied as a harm-reduction agent for both opioid use disorder and alcohol use disorder.** NIDA and NIAAA are funding clinical trials. Ecological momentary assessment studies show the majority of users take kratom for energy, mood, and pain management — not to get high — and many report reclaiming their lives after transitioning off opioids. [01:26:59](transcript.md#012659-kratom-alcohol-consumption-respiratory-failure)
- **Coca-Cola still imports coca leaves from Peru today**, via the Stepan Company in New Jersey. De-cocainized leaf extract provides Coke's flavor profile; the extracted cocaine alkaloids are sold to the pharmaceutical industry as a topical anesthetic used in ENT surgeries. All other original soft drinks (7UP, Pepsi, Dr. Pepper) also had pharmacological origins — lithium, pepsinogen, and wellness formulas respectively. [02:09:34](transcript.md#020934-coca-cola-coca-plant--cocaine-history-of-soft-drinks)
- **Raw cacao (Theobroma cacao — "food of the gods") is a remarkable natural medicine package** containing theobromine (mild long-lasting stimulant with vasodilatory properties), phenylethylamine (PEA, associated with mood elevation and romantic feelings), and flavonoids (catechins/epicatechins with antioxidant and cardiovascular benefits) — most of which are destroyed by milk, sugar, and industrial processing. [02:01:51](transcript.md#020151-cacao-beans-chocolate)

## Tools & Protocols

### Tool: Understand Kratom Product Labels
- **What:** A framework for evaluating any kratom product before use.
- **How:** Check the label for (1) whether the product is whole-leaf/dried leaf powder vs. an extract, concentrate, or "kratom-derived" product; (2) the number of servings per container — many small bottles contain 9–15 servings, not 1; (3) the milligrams of mitragynine or 7-hydroxymitragynine listed; (4) whether it contains semi-synthetic or isolated alkaloids.
- **When:** Before purchasing or consuming any kratom product.
- **Ref:** [00:17:00](transcript.md#001700-kratom-products-serving-size-kids-semi-synthetics-tool-understand-kratom-product-labels)

### Tool: Kratom Risk Spectrum (Leaf → Isolates)
- **What:** A conceptual spectrum framework for understanding kratom product risk, analogous to the alcohol spectrum from light beer to Everclear.
- **How:** Whole dried leaf powder = light beer equivalent (lowest risk, closest to traditional use). Leaf extracts = spirits equivalent (moderate elevated risk). Kratom-derived/semi-synthetics/isolates = Everclear equivalent (highest risk, furthest from traditional use). Never assume a smaller bottle equals a smaller dose.
- **When:** Any time evaluating a kratom product or explaining kratom risk to others.
- **Ref:** [00:59:00](transcript.md#005900-alkaloids--natural-products-opium-poppy-coca-leaf-tool-kratom-leaf-vs-extracts-kratom-derivedkratom-isolates)

### Protocol: Low-Dose Kratom for Energy/Mood (If Using as an Informed Adult)
- **What:** Guidance for adults who choose to use whole-leaf kratom products for stimulant/mood purposes.
- **How:** Start with a very low dose (1–2g of dried leaf powder). Stay at the lowest dose that provides benefit — users consistently report "less is more." Do not escalate. Avoid daily use when possible; tolerance develops. Do not combine with alcohol, opioids, or benzodiazepines. Consult a physician if taking any prescription medications (CYP3A4 interaction risk).
- **When:** For adults (not under 18, ideally not under mid-20s) who have decided to use kratom after informed consideration of risks.
- **Ref:** [01:12:19](transcript.md#011219-kratom-energy-mood--pain-management-dose-caffeine)

### Protocol: Discontinuing Kratom Use
- **What:** Guidance on tapering off kratom depending on usage pattern.
- **How:** For low-dose whole-leaf users (energy/mood): expect caffeine-like withdrawal — a few days of headaches and fatigue; taper gradually. For high-dose or long-term concentrate users: expect mild opioid-like withdrawal — insomnia, muscle aches, anxiety, restlessness; taper slowly over weeks and seek professional guidance.
- **When:** When stopping or reducing kratom after regular use.
- **Ref:** [01:51:08](transcript.md#015108-kratom-as-an-anti-depressant-discontinuing-kratom-use-opioid-use)

## Resources Mentioned

- **American Kratom Association (AKA)** — Consumer and industry advocacy organization; pushed for the Kratom Consumer Protection Act model state legislation. [https://www.americankratom.org](https://www.americankratom.org)
- **Kratom Consumer Protection Act** — Model state legislation setting age limits, labeling requirements, and testing standards for kratom products. [https://www.americankratom.org/kcpa](https://www.americankratom.org/kcpa)
- **Stepan Company** — New Jersey company that processes coca leaves for Coca-Cola, separating cocaine alkaloids (pharmaceutical grade) from de-cocainized flavor extract. [https://www.stepan.com](https://www.stepan.com)
- **NIDA (National Institute on Drug Abuse)** — Funded Dr. McCurdy's kratom and Salvia divinorum research. [https://nida.nih.gov](https://nida.nih.gov)
- **NIAAA (National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism)** — Currently funding research into kratom as a harm-reduction agent for alcohol use disorder. [https://www.niaaa.nih.gov](https://www.niaaa.nih.gov)
- **Macao et al. (1972)** — Smith Kline & French paper representing the first and still most complete clinical pharmacology study of mitragynine. Shelved because mitragynine was no better than codeine and NSAIDs were emerging; rediscovered by McCurdy decades later in the context of the opioid crisis.
- **PubMed kratom literature** — Nearly 1,000 papers as of episode recording; McCurdy's lab accounts for ~10%. [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=kratom](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=kratom)
- **Lobelia inflata** — Plant containing lobeline (nicotinic alkaloid); McCurdy's early natural products research subject; historically used as an anti-smoking treatment.
- **Salvia divinorum** — Plant containing salvinorin A (kappa opioid receptor agonist); produces intense dissociative and psychedelic effects; subject of McCurdy's NIDA-funded research prior to kratom. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvia_divinorum)
