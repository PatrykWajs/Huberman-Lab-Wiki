## [00:00:00] Salt

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are going to discuss salt, also referred to as sodium. Salt has many, many important functions in the brain and body. For instance, it regulates fluid balance, how much fluid you desire and how much fluid you excrete. Salt also regulates your appetite for other nutrients, things like sugar, things like carbohydrates. We all harbor small sets of neurons — we call these sets of neurons nuclei, meaning little clusters of neurons — that sense the levels of salt in our brain and body.

## [00:00:37] Brain & Monitoring Salt

There are a couple of brain regions that sense salt levels, and these brain regions are very special because they lack the biological fences around them that other brain areas have. That fence goes by a particular name: the blood-brain barrier, or BBB. Most substances circulating in your body do not have access to the brain — large molecules in particular can't just pass in, making the brain a privileged organ. However, there are a couple of regions in the brain where that fence is weaker, and it turns out that the areas monitoring salt balance reside in little sets of neurons sitting just on the other side of these weak fences. The most important and famous of these is one called OVLT, which stands for the organum vasculosum of the lateral terminalis.

The neurons in the OVLT are able to pay attention to what's passing through the bloodstream and can detect, for instance, if sodium levels are too low or if blood pressure is too low or too high. The OVLT then sends signals to other brain areas, which can do things like release hormones that act on tissues in the periphery — for instance, having the kidneys secrete more urine to get rid of excessive salt in the body. So the OVLT is a critical sentinel organ bridging what happens in the blood with what the brain instructs the body to do about fluid and salt balance.

## [00:02:33] Thirst, Osmotic Thirst & Salt

Have you ever wondered just why you get thirsty? It's because neurons in your OVLT are detecting changes in your bloodstream that reflect global changes within your body. In response to that, your OVLT sets off certain events within your brain and body that make you either want to drink more fluid or to stop drinking fluid. There are two main kinds of thirst: the first is called osmotic thirst and the second is called hypovolemic thirst. Osmotic thirst has to do with the concentration of salt in your bloodstream — so if you ingest something very salty, the osmolarity of your blood rises and the OVLT detects that change.

Neurons in the OVLT come in two main varieties. One variety senses the osmolarity of the blood, and when salt concentration is high these neurons activate, meaning they send electrical signals to other brain areas. Those other brain areas eventually trigger the release of a hormone from the posterior pituitary called vasopressin, also known as antidiuretic hormone, which has the capacity to restrict the amount of urine secreted. Conversely, when sodium is low, that same system is turned off and antidiuretic hormone is not secreted, allowing urine to flow more freely. The OVLT communicates to the supraoptic nucleus, which orchestrates the release or suppression of vasopressin depending on whether blood osmolarity is high or low.

## [00:05:35] Hypovolemic Thirst & Blood Pressure

The second category of thirst is hypovolemic thirst, which occurs when there is a drop in blood pressure. The OVLT also harbors neurons of the baroreceptor mechanoreceptor category — baroreceptors are proteins in cells that respond to changes in blood pressure. A number of things can cause decreases in blood pressure, including significant blood loss, excessive vomiting, or extensive diarrhea. Both types of thirst — osmotic and hypovolemic — are not just about seeking water; they are also about seeking salt. In very general terms, sodium can help retain water, and sodium and water work together to generate thirst and to regulate whether the body retains fluid or excretes it as urine.

## [00:06:59] Fluid Balance, Kidney & Urine Regulation

Before diving into specifics about salt and performance recommendations, it is necessary to understand the fluid-balance mechanism, and for that we must pay attention to the kidney. The kidney is an incredible organ responsible for both retaining and releasing various substances from the body. Blood enters the kidney and goes through a series of tubes arranged into loops — the beautiful loop of Henle and other architectural features — that allow certain substances to be retained and others to be released depending on their concentration in the blood. The kidney responds to a number of hormonal signals including vasopressin in order to hold on to more fluid when the brain and body need it.

About 90% of the stuff absorbed from blood is absorbed early in this series of tubes. A simple example: if you are very low on fluid on a hot day, the OVLT will sense increased osmolarity and signal through the supraoptic nucleus, causing vasopressin to be released into the bloodstream. Vasopressin then acts on the kidney — both mechanically and chemically — to ensure the kidney does not release much water, suppressing the urge to urinate. Conversely, if you drink a large amount of water on a cool day with low salt intake, osmolarity drops, the OVLT fails to signal the supraoptic nucleus, vasopressin is not released, and the kidneys allow you to excrete water freely. The system is beautifully bidirectional.

## [00:10:13] How Much Salt Do You Need?, Blood Pressure, Dizziness & Postural Syndromes

How much salt do we actually need? First, it is crucial to know your blood pressure — whether you have prehypertension, hypertension, or normal tension — because this measurement has enormous impact on what you should do regarding sodium. Without knowing blood pressure, no one-size-fits-all recommendation can be made. There is a school of thought that everyone consumes too much salt, and dozens if not hundreds of quality papers point to the fact that a high-salt diet can be bad for various organs including the brain, because excess sodium inside brain cells causes them to swell as water follows salt into those cells. Conversely, if salt levels inside cells are too low, cells shrink as water is pulled into the extracellular space, and brain function suffers.

The data suggest that at about two grams of sodium per day, fewer health risks appear, but those risks continue to decline as you move toward four to five grams per day, and risks dramatically increase at higher intakes. The commonly referenced cutoff of 2.3 grams of sodium per day is associated with low incidence of cardiovascular events and stroke. However, people with low blood pressure — those who get dizzy when standing up, those feeling chronically fatigued — can actually benefit from increasing sodium intake. Sufficient sodium in the bloodstream draws water into the vasculature, filling the capillaries, arteries, and veins so blood pressure reaches the head appropriately. The American Society of Hypertension recommends anywhere from 6,000 to 10,000 mg of salt per day (roughly 2,400 to 4,000 mg sodium) for people suffering from orthostatic disorders like orthostatic hypotension, POTS (postural tachycardia syndrome), or idiopathic orthostatic tachycardia and syncope. Context is vital: people with high blood pressure need less salt, people with lower blood pressure may need more.

## [00:15:49] Replenish Salt for Performance, Tool: Galpin Equation & Exercise

If you are exercising a lot, or working in a particularly cold, dry, or hot environment, you ought to be ingesting sufficient amounts of salt and fluid. A useful rule of thumb for exercise-based fluid replenishment comes from what is called the Galpin equation, named after exercise physiologist Andy Galpin. The formula is: body weight in pounds divided by 30 equals the ounces of fluid you should drink every 15 minutes during exercise. We lose approximately one to five pounds of water per hour of intense exercise, and that loss directly impacts mental capacity and physical performance by changing the volume and size of cells based on how much sodium is inside or outside them.

The Galpin equation is mainly designed for exercise but serves as a good rule of thumb for any activity requiring high mental capacity. Most people are probably underhydrating — and not just from a lack of water, but also from insufficient electrolytes: sodium, potassium, and magnesium. Entering any demanding cognitive or physical activity sufficiently hydrated and regularly hydrating throughout is a foundational principle for maintaining both mental and physical performance.

## [00:17:35] Stress & Craving Salt

While excess salt is bad for blood pressure and can damage the heart and brain, situations exist where too little salt causes problems — and this has everything to do with the nervous system. The adrenal glands, which sit atop the kidneys, produce glucocorticoids like aldosterone that directly impact fluid balance and regulate craving for and tolerance of salty solutions. The stress system is a generic system designed to deal with various challenges to the organism — infection, famine, dehydration — and the stress response involves elevated heart rate, elevated blood pressure, and the ability to maintain movement and resist challenge.

It is clear from a number of studies that if sodium levels are too low, our ability to meet stress challenges is impaired. Under stress, there is a natural, hardwired craving for more sodium as a way to meet that challenge. This means that for people who experience anxiety or chronic stress, insufficient sodium may be compounding their symptoms, and a modest increase in salt intake through healthy dietary means could be beneficial.

## [00:19:18] Electrolytes: Magnesium & Potassium; Low Carbohydrate Diet

A discussion of sodium cannot be complete without discussing the other electrolytes — magnesium and potassium. Many people are probably getting enough magnesium in their diet and do not need to supplement, but some opt to for specific benefits. Magnesium malate has evidence for reducing muscle soreness from exercise. Magnesium threonate has been discussed in the context of promoting the transition into sleep and depth of sleep, and magnesium bisglycinate appears at least on par with magnesium threonate for those sleep-promoting effects. Magnesium citrate functions as a fairly effective laxative and is not known to promote sleep.

Sodium and potassium are always considered together because the kidney regulates sodium balance with potassium working in close concert. Recommendations about their ratios vary widely — from 2:1 potassium-to-sodium to 2:1 sodium-to-potassium depending on the source. For people following low-carbohydrate diets, one of the most immediate effects is excretion of more water, which also means excretion of sodium and potassium. People on low-carbohydrate diets therefore often need to ensure they are getting enough sodium and potassium. Conversely, those on moderate-to-high carbohydrate diets may need less sodium and potassium, since carbohydrates hold water and thus retain electrolytes. The interplay is individual and context-dependent.

## [00:22:07] Salt & Sweet Taste, Sugar Cravings, Processed Foods

Salt is not just a substance regulating fluid balance and blood volume — it is also a taste, and one with powerful effects on the brain. We have salt receptors — neurons that fire action potentials when salty substances are detected — throughout the tongue and digestive tract. Beautiful work from the Zuker lab at Columbia University and others has used imaging and molecular biology to define parallel neural pathways for each taste modality: salty, sweet, bitter, umami. These pathways move up through brainstem centers to the neocortex, where conscious taste perception resides, and they can interact with one another.

This interaction has important relevance for food choices and sugar cravings. Many processed foods contain hidden sugars — sometimes as artificial sweeteners — that are disguised by other flavors. By putting sugars into foods and hiding their sweetness, food manufacturers cause those foods to signal dopamine release in the brain, driving you to crave more of that food even though you cannot perceive the true sweetness. If salt is added alongside sweet, the salty taste partially masks sweetness, suppressing the homeostatic signal that would normally tell you that you've had enough sweet. Conversely, adding sweet to very salty food partially masks saltiness, reducing the homeostatic signal for salt satiety. The combination of salty and sweet can lead you to consume far more of a food than you would have if it had been purely salty or purely sweet — a diabolical but highly profitable strategy used extensively in processed food manufacturing.

## [00:26:26] Finding Your Ideal Salt Intake, Tool: Unprocessed Food Diet

Your brain has a way of representing the pure form of each taste — salty, sweet, bitter — and a way of representing their combinations, and food manufacturers have exploited this to a large degree. If you are looking to find your ideal sodium intake, whether for health, performance, or reducing sugar cravings, it is most useful to do so against a background of relatively unprocessed foods. The specific dietary framework — keto, carnivore, omnivore, intermittent fasting — matters less than keeping foods close to their basic form, avoiding large ingredient combinations, and avoiding highly processed foods. The cleaner the food background, the more quickly you will hone in on your specific salt appetite and salt needs, which vary from person to person depending on nutrition, activity level, and hormone status.

Blood pressure remains an important metric to track as you adjust sodium intake. The book The Salt Fix reviews data showing that when people increase salt intake against a background of relatively unprocessed foods, sugar cravings can be vastly reduced — consistent with the neural pathway interactions between salty and sweet taste systems described earlier.

## [00:28:14] Neurons, Salt & Action Potentials; Ingesting Too Much Water

Sodium plays a crucial role in the most fundamental operation of the nervous system: the action potential. The action potential is the fundamental way in which neurons communicate with one another, and sodium is one of the key elements that allows neurons to fire action potentials at all. Having sufficient sodium in your system is literally what allows your brain and nervous system to function. If you drink too much water — especially in a short amount of time — a condition called hyponatremia occurs: you excrete sodium very quickly, kidney function is disrupted, and brain function can deteriorate. There are documented cases of competitive endurance athletes crossing into disorientation and being unable to find a finish line after excessive sweating combined with drinking too much plain water and too few electrolytes.

The lesson is that it is not just the volume of fluid but the electrolyte content — especially sodium — that determines whether your nervous system can function under demanding conditions. Sodium is absolutely critical for neurons to fire, and any significant depletion of sodium through overhydration or insufficient intake can manifest as cognitive impairment, physical failure, or in extreme cases, a life-threatening crisis.

We explored the kidney's function as a beautiful, responsive organ governed by those hormonal signals. We discussed the relationship between the stress system and salt cravings, and why for some people under anxiety or stress, increasing salt intake through healthy dietary means may be beneficial. We covered conditions like orthostatic hypotension and POTS where increasing sodium intake may offset symptoms. We discussed how salty and sweet taste perception interact and how that drives excess consumption of processed foods. And we highlighted sodium's irreplaceable role in the action potential — the fundamental mechanism of nervous system function. Place that question in the context of your fluid intake and your overall electrolyte balance — sodium, potassium, and magnesium — and use blood pressure as a key guiding metric.
