## [00:00:00] Time Perception

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are talking about time perception. Our perception of time is perhaps the most important factor in how we gauge our life. And the reason for that is that our perception of time is directly linked to the neurochemical states that control mood, stress, happiness, excitement.

And of course, it frames the way in which we evaluate our past. It frames our present, whether or not we think we are on track or off track. And it frames our sense of the future. So let's talk about time perception. And the most fundamental aspect of time perception is something called entrainment.

## [00:00:48] Entrainment, Circannual Rhythms & Melatonin

Entrainment is the way in which your internal processes, your biology and your psychology are linked to some external thing. And the most basic form of entrainment that we are all a slave to all year round for our entire life are so-called circannual rhythms. We have neurons, nerve cells in our eye, in our brain, and in our body that are marking off the passage of time throughout the year — literally a calendar system in your brain and body. And the way this works is beautifully simple. Light seen by your eyes inhibits — meaning it reduces — the amount of a hormone released in your brain called melatonin.

Melatonin has two major functions. One function is to make you sleepy at night and the other is to regulate some of the other hormones of the body, in particular testosterone and estrogen. Throughout the year, depending on where you live, day length varies. And as a consequence, the amount of light from the sun that is available to you varies. So when days are long, the amount of melatonin in your brain and body that's released tends to be less; when days are very short, the amount of melatonin and the duration it exists in your brain and body tends to be much longer.

So melatonin correlates with day length. If we are viewing more light, we have less melatonin; if we view less light, we have more melatonin. You see different amounts of light each day, but we have a process in our brain and body that averages the amount of light you're seeing both from artificial sources and from sunlight and measures that off. It's so exquisitely precise that for a given 8-hour day in the spring — because spring in the northern hemisphere means days are getting longer — the amount of melatonin is getting progressively less and less. And that signal is conveyed to all the systems of your brain and body.

This is why most people, not all, but most people feel like they have more energy in the spring. Conversely, when you have an 8-hour day in the winter, the amount of melatonin corresponding to that day is getting progressively greater and greater because days are getting shorter. Every cell and system of your body pays attention to this, and as a consequence most people feel they have a little less — or sometimes a lot less — energy and a slightly lower mood in the winter months. The melatonin signal is the way in which your internal state, your mood, your sense of energy, even your appetite, is entrained — matched — to some external event: in this case, the rotation of the earth around the sun. The amount of testosterone and estrogen that human beings make also varies, such that in longer days people tend to make more testosterone and estrogen than in shorter days.

## [00:04:22] Circadian Rhythms, Tools: Exercise; Morning & Evening Light for Circadian Entrainment

The next bin of time that we are all entrained to is the so-called circadian time cycle — a 24-hour rhythm. This is perhaps the most powerful rhythm that we all contain and that none of us can escape from. We all have a circadian clock that resides over the roof of our mouth. The cells in that circadian clock fire — meaning they release chemicals into our brain and body — on a very regular rhythm. Not surprisingly, there are periods of every 24-hour cycle when we are very active and alert, and others when we are asleep.

This circadian clock oscillates — it goes up and down once every 24 hours and then repeats. Every cell of our body has a 24-hour oscillation in the expression of various genes. They are entrained to the outside light-dark cycle because morning sunlight, evening sunlight, and the lack of light in the middle of the night make sure that the oscillations occurring within the cells of our brain and body are matched to the outside world. I cannot emphasize enough how important it is that your circadian entrainment be precise. Disruptions in circadian entrainment cause huge health problems: they increase cancer risk, obesity, mental health issues; they decrease wound healing and physical and mental performance; and they disrupt hormones.

You want your cells to be linked to the circadian cycle that's outside you — mainly consisting of when there's sunlight and when there is not. The simple protocols from this whole discussion about circadian entrainment are: view 10 to 30 minutes of bright light, ideally sunlight, within an hour of waking. Especially if you wake up early in the day, get outside and see sunlight. Do that again in the afternoon or around evening — 10 to 30 minutes depending on how bright it is outside.

Basically, you want as much bright light ideally from sunlight coming in through your eyes throughout the day, and then in the evening you want as little bright light coming in through your eyes. There are other ways to entrain your circadian clock — one of the best is to engage in physical activity at fairly regular times of day. You don't have to do it every day, but if you're going to exercise, try and exercise at a fairly consistent time. What happens when this circadian clock starts getting disrupted? There's a classic study by Aschoff done in 1985, now repeated many times, where people went into environments without clocks, windows, or watches — sometimes even in constant dark or constant light — and researchers evaluated how well people perceived the passage of time on shorter time scales.

And what they found was really interesting: people underestimate how long they were in these isolated environments. After 42 days or so, people would guess "8 days or 36 days." They generally underestimated how long they had been in this odd environment with no clocks or sunlight. In addition, their perception of shorter time intervals was also greatly disrupted — when asked to measure off 2 minutes, normally people come within 5 to 15 seconds, but when circadian entrainment was disrupted, their time measurement on shorter scales of minutes or even seconds was greatly impaired. You want your circadian entrainment to be locked in so that your perception of time on shorter intervals can be precise, because the ability to perceive time accurately for a given task turns out to be one of the most fundamental predictors of how well or poorly you perform that task.

## [00:09:01] Ultradian Cycles, Focus & Work

Ultradian rhythms are rhythms of about 90 minutes or so, and all of our existence is broken up into these 90-minute ultradian cycles. When you go to sleep at night, whether you sleep 6, 8, or 10 hours, that entire period of sleep is broken up into these 90-minute cycles. However, when you wake up in the morning, many of the things you do are also governed by these ultradian rhythms. The 90-minute time block seems to be the one in which the brain can enter a state of focus and alertness and do hard work. And then at about 90 minutes, there's a significant drop in your ability to engage in that mental or physical work.

Everybody from the self-help literature to the business literature to pop psychology has tried to leverage these ultradian cycles, saying: if you're going to do something hard, limit it to 90 minutes or less. I am one of those people who uses 90-minute work cycles, and I think they are extremely powerful. While this isn't time perception per se, it is again an example of entrainment. What you're entraining to is the release of particular neurochemicals — in this case acetylcholine and dopamine — that allow your brain to focus for particular periods of time, about 90 minutes. After about 90 minutes, the amount of those chemicals that can be released tends to drop very low, which is why your ability to focus becomes diminished.

I always get the question: how do you know when the 90-minute cycle begins? Let's say you wake up at 8:00 a.m. and you just finished a 90-minute sleep cycle. Does that mean your next 90-minute work cycle begins right at 8:01? No. The interesting thing about these basic rest-activity cycles is that you can initiate them whenever you want — you can set a clock and decide: now the focus begins, now the work begins.

What you can't negotiate, however, is that at about 100 to 120 minutes, no matter who you are, you're going to see a diminishment in performance — you're not going to focus as well. That's because of how these 90-minute cycles are linked to the ability of neurons that release acetylcholine, dopamine, and to some extent norepinephrine — the things that give us narrow focus, motivation, and drive. After about 90 minutes, those circuits are far less willing to engage, and it becomes much harder to continue focusing to a high degree. Some people like to do multiple 90-minute cycles per day; in that case, you need to separate them. You can't chain one into the next — you can't cheat these circuits.

For me, I can do one mid-morning and probably another one in the afternoon. This is not checking email or social media — this is very focused, hard work on hard problems. This will differ for everybody. I recommend spacing them by at least 2 to 4 hours, and most people probably won't be able to handle more than two per day. Even one a day is going to feel like a significant mental investment — afterwards you're going to feel pretty taxed.

## [00:12:37] Time Perception, Past, Present & Future

So now we've talked about circannual, circadian, and ultradian rhythms, but we haven't really talked about time perception per se. We've mainly talked about the subconscious, slow oscillatory ways in which we are entrained to the year, the day, and these ultradian cycles we can leverage toward more focus. But what about the actual perception of time? What actually controls how fast or how slowly we perceive time going by? There are basically three forms of time perception that we should all be aware of.

One is our perception of the passage of time in the present — how quickly or slowly things seem to be happening. This is kind of like an interval timer, ticking off time. It's either fine-slicing rapidly or ticking in larger intervals. We have these interval timers, and I'll discuss the basis of them. We also engage in what's called prospective timing, which is like a stopwatch measuring off things as they go forward.

For instance, if I told you to start measuring off a two-minute time interval into the future, you could do that pretty well. But if I told you to measure a five-minute interval into the future without any clock or watch, you would have to set the tick marks yourself — decide how many times you were going to count off during that five-minute block. There's also retrospective time — how you measure off time in the past. If I say: "Last week you went to the park, you went out in the evening — how long was it between lunch and when you went to dinner?" You'd think: "I went to dinner at 7:00 and we had lunch around 2." You're using memory to reconstruct events in the past and get a sense of their relative positioning within time.

So we have retrospective time, current interval time measurement, and prospective time measurement into the future. The beauty of time perception in the human nervous system is that it boils down to a couple of simple molecules that govern whether we are fine-slicing time or batching time in larger bins.

## [00:14:53] Dopamine, Norepinephrine & Serotonin, Time Perception

Those molecules go by names you've probably heard — things like dopamine and norepinephrine. They're called neuromodulators because they modulate, they change, the way the other neural circuits work. Also serotonin — released from a different site in the brain than dopamine and norepinephrine, with a different effect on time perception. To give you an example of how dopamine and serotonin modulate our perception of time: the more dopamine that's released into our brain, the more we tend to overestimate the amount of time that has just passed. Let me repeat that — the more dopamine released into our brain, the more we overestimate how much time has passed.

These experiments are very straightforward and objective. You can give people or an animal a drug that increases the amount of dopamine and then ask them to measure off — without any watch or clock — when one minute has passed. As dopamine levels rise, people tend to think the minute is up before it actually is. At the 38-second mark they'll say, "Okay, I think a minute is up." They've overestimated how much time has passed. The higher the level of dopamine, the more people tend to overestimate.

Norepinephrine — also called noradrenaline — plays a very similar role to dopamine. Conversely, the neuromodulator serotonin causes people to underestimate the amount of time that's passed. This is interesting in terms of how pharmacology can be used to adjust time perception, but also interesting in the context of the circadian rhythm. There's emerging evidence that throughout the 24-hour cycle, there are robust changes in the amount of dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin present in the brain, blood, and body depending on time of day.

## [00:17:11] Dopamine & Serotonin, Daily Fluctuations, Tool: Structuring Work, Sleep

Much of the evidence points to the fact that in the first half of the day, dopamine and norepinephrine are elevated in the brain, body, and bloodstream much more than serotonin. And in the second half of the day — particularly toward evening and nighttime — serotonin levels are going up. What that means is that our perception of the passage of time will be very different in the early part of the day versus the latter half. This is important in terms of how one structures their day. All of the literature I can find on productivity points to the idea that we should be doing the hardest task — the most important task — early in the day, as a psychological tool for getting it done and feeling as if we accomplished something. I think that's an excellent protocol.

And to take us back to the critical role of the circadian rhythm: there is a lot of evidence that when sleep is disrupted — when sleep is too short, fragmented, or not of high enough quality for enough days — one of the first things to happen is a dysregulation of these dopaminergic, noradrenergic, and serotonin states throughout the day. This dysregulation directly impacts our perception of time and, as a consequence, our ability to structure and perform cognitively demanding work. Getting your sleep right is therefore not just about rest — it's about preserving the neurochemical architecture that governs how you experience the flow of time and allocate effort through your waking hours.

## [00:18:52] Trauma, "Over-clocking", Slowing Time & Emotions

There is a version of how dopamine and norepinephrine can impact our perception of the passage of time in ways that are very disruptive or even maladaptive. The best example I'm aware of is trauma. Many people who have been in car accidents or experienced some other form of major trauma do what's called overclocking. Overclocking is when levels of dopamine and norepinephrine increase so much during a particular event that we fine-slice — the frame rate is increased so much that we perceive things as happening in ultra slow motion. That might not seem like a bad thing overall, but the problem with overclocking is the way that information gets stamped down into the memory system.

The memory system — which involves areas of the brain like the hippocampus but also the neocortex — is basically a space-time recorder. Your nervous system is housed in the darkness of your skull. It doesn't have a lot of information about the outside world except light coming in through the eyes, sound waves hitting our ears, and sensory input from skin. So it has to take all those neural signals and create a record of what happened. Car accidents, trauma, and events of that sort are often stamped down into that record.

What we mean by "stamped down" is that the precise firing of the sequence of neurons that reflected some event is encoded. So let's say I'm in a car accident — certain neurons are firing because of the flipping of the car, the screams, the blood. All of that neural activity gets repeated in the hippocampus, and the sequence of the firing of those neurons is also remembered. It's not just that neuron 1-2-3-4 fired in that sequence — it's also that neuron 1-2-3-4 fired at a particular rate. So the memory is stored as firing of those neurons at that rate.

This is important for understanding memory capacity. There's both a space code — meaning the particular neurons that fire is important — and a rate code — how quickly those neurons fire, or the relative timing of their firing, is also part of the memory. This affords our memory system tremendous flexibility: you can take the same set of neurons in the hippocampus and stamp down many more memories, because all you have to do is use a match of the different rates at which different neurons were firing. Overclocking is a case in which the frame rate is so high that a memory gets stamped down and people have a very hard time shaking that memory and the emotions associated with it.

In fact, one of the first things trauma victims learn is that they aren't going to forget what happened. What's eventually going to happen — ideally with good treatment — is that the emotional weight of the experience will be divorced from the memory of the experience. Some of you are probably saying: why dopamine during trauma? I thought dopamine was the feel-good molecule. In reality, dopamine is not necessarily a molecule of reward — it's a molecule of motivation, pursuit, and drive. And because of the close relationship between dopamine and norepinephrine, they are often co-released. Whether dopamine is released during car crashes or other forms of trauma, we don't know for certain. But what we do know is that both the dopamine system and noradrenergic system are greatly increased anytime there's a heightened state of arousal.

Arousal can have negative valence — associated with an event you really hate — or positive valence. But dopamine and norepinephrine are the common hallmark of all things of elevated arousal. That's why we see evidence for dopamine being associated with these changes in time perception both for positive events and for negative events.

## [00:23:30] Event Perception (Fun vs Boring), Time & Memories

Up until now, I've been talking about how dopamine and to some extent serotonin can differentially impact your perception of how fast or slowly things are happening in the moment. But remember — we have prospective time, our experience of time in the moment, and retrospective time. And there are beautiful studies showing that the dopaminergic state changes not just how we experience things now, but how we remember things in the past and the rate at which those things occurred — and those are in opposite directions. To make this very simple: if something you experience is fun or varied — meaning it has a lot of different components and is associated with an increase in dopamine in your brain — you will experience it as going by very fast.

Imagine an amazing day for a kid in an amusement park. They can do a ton of things, it's all new, they're very excited, and they'll feel like it goes by very fast. But later they will remember that experience as being very long — a long day full of many, many events. There's a paradoxical relationship between how we perceive fun, exciting, varied events in the present and how we remember them in the past.

For those who have gone on vacation: if you've had an amazing vacation, it will seem like it goes by very fast. The last day of vacation, you sort of go, "Whoa, it went by so fast — there was so much happening." But in memory, 6 to 8 months later, you'll remember: "Wow, that was a long trip. We had this, then we had that, then we did this, then we had that." It tends to spool out in a longer memory than the actual experience. Conversely, if you're bored with something or doing something you really don't like, it's going to seem like it takes a long time in the moment. But retroactively looking back, it will seem like that moment was very short.

So we aren't just driven by circadian clocks and circannual clocks and ultradian clocks. We are driven by these timers that vary depending on our level of excitement — and they vary because of these neuromodulators dopamine and serotonin. The way I like to think about it: you have two clocks, two stopwatches. One is a dopaminergic stopwatch that fine-slices really closely, counting off milliseconds and grabbing a movie of your experience at very high resolution. And in the other hand, you have a stopwatch that's gathering big time bins, big ticks, marking off time at bigger intervals. Depending on whether you're excited or bored, you're using different stopwatches and therefore perceiving your experience differently.

## [00:26:37] Novel Experiences, Places & People, Retrospective Time

One very interesting aspect to the way that neuromodulators like dopamine and novelty interact with time perception and memory is how we perceive our relationship to places and people. Really interesting literature shows that the more novel experiences we have in a place, the more we feel we know that place — obviously — but also the longer we feel we've been there. Here's a thought experiment that illustrates what's in the literature: Let's say I were to move to New York City, a city I happen to really like. I live in a given apartment for a year and have about a hundred different exciting and new experiences. At the end of that year I would feel as if I'd lived there one year — which I would know to be true.

But if instead I lived in three different places in New York City, met three times as many people, and had three times as many novel experiences, I would actually feel as if I had been there much longer than if I'd only lived in one location. This is also true for social interactions. When we move to multiple novel environments with somebody else, we tend to feel as if we know that person much better and that they know us much better. That's all very interesting and speaks to the fact that dopamine is a kind of flexible currency in the brain — doled out, released, when something one hopes will happen actually happens. And it's released when there's a surprise, even a negative one.

But the more interesting thing is how that relates to time perception. How often and when you release dopamine is actually setting the frame rate on the entire perception of everything — not just for positive or negative events. This governance over our perception of time that dopamine has points to a very clear, very actionable tool — and that tool is habits.

## [00:28:38] Habits, Tool: Schedule Functional Units

Dopamine's governance over our perception of time points to a very clear, very actionable and very powerful tool that many people have talked about before — habits. People have discussed habits in a variety of contexts, but in the context of dopamine reward and time perception, what this means is that placing specific habitual routines at particular intervals throughout your day is a very good way to incorporate the dopamine system so that you divide your day into a series of what I would call functional units. What would this look like? It would mean waking up and having one specific habit that you always engage in — one that causes a release of dopamine.

You could say, "Well, great — that'll make me feel good." And dopamine release generally does make us feel motivated. But it would have an additional effect of marking that time of day as the beginning of a particular time bin. Then inserting another habit — perhaps the beginning of breakfast or something — and recognizing that that's a habit. You don't have to be obsessively precise about the timing, but that regular sequencing of things is going to lead not just to dopamine release as it relates to reward and motivation and feeling good — it actually becomes the way in which we carve up our entire experience of our day.

The principle here is that structured, habitual routines serve dual purposes. First, they provide the neurochemical reward of dopamine release, which generates motivation and a sense of accomplishment. Second, they serve as temporal anchors — explicit markers that divide continuous experience into discrete, manageable units. By engineering your day around these functional units, you gain not just productivity but a richer, more granular sense of time passing — which in turn shapes how you will later remember and evaluate the quality of your days.
