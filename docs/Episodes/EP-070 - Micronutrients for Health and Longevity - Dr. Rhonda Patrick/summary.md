# Episode 70: Micronutrients for Health & Longevity | Dr. Rhonda Patrick

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Rhonda Patrick joins Andrew Huberman to explain how four major micronutrient categories — sulforaphane, omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA), vitamin D, and magnesium — regulate cellular stress, antioxidant defense, inflammation, and longevity-related gene expression. The conversation opens with the unifying framework of hormesis: cold, heat, exercise, fasting, and plant compounds all activate overlapping stress-response pathways (NRF2, heat shock proteins, FOXO3) that produce net positive adaptive outcomes. Dr. Patrick provides precise protocols for food sources and supplementation, including how to measure omega-3 status via the Omega-3 Index, how to optimize vitamin D levels, and which forms of magnesium target different tissues. The second half covers deliberate cold and heat exposure in depth: how cold drives dopamine, norepinephrine, UCP1 thermogenesis, and fat browning via PGC-1alpha, and how sauna use at 174°F+ for 20 minutes reduces all-cause cardiovascular mortality by up to 50% and dementia risk by over 60% in dose-dependent Finnish cohort data. Dr. Patrick also explains why cold after strength training blunts hypertrophy via PGC-1alpha competition with mTOR, how FOXO3 mediates longevity downstream of reduced insulin signaling, and how weight-bearing exercise releases osteocalcin from bone as a hormone that supports brain health.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hormesis is the common mechanism behind all major health interventions.** Cold, heat, exercise, fasting, and plant compounds like sulforaphane all activate overlapping genetic stress-response pathways — NRF2, heat shock proteins, FOXO3 — that produce a net positive adaptive outcome. These pathways were regularly activated throughout human evolutionary history and are now largely dormant in modern sedentary life. [00:09:42](transcript.md#000942-stress-response-pathways-hormesis)

2. **Sulforaphane activates NRF2 for powerful antioxidant and detoxification effects.** Broccoli sprouts contain up to 100x more sulforaphane than broccoli. Heat destroys myrosinase (the enzyme that converts glucoraphanin to sulforaphane), but adding ~1g of raw mustard seed powder to cooked broccoli restores conversion fourfold. Sulforaphane has been shown in humans to lower DNA damage in white blood cells by 24–34% and increase brain glutathione. [00:21:12](transcript.md#002112-tools-1-sulforaphane---broccoli-sprouts-broccoli-mustard-seed)

3. **Sulforaphane preconditions the brain and body against ischemic and inflammatory injury.** Animal studies show sulforaphane preconditioning dramatically improves outcomes after hypoxia and ischemic stroke. Human data show 60%+ excretion increases in airborne carcinogens (benzene, acrolein) within 24 hours of supplementation, via NRF2-driven detoxification enzymes. Randomized trials also show benefit for autism symptom reduction. [00:25:25](transcript.md#002525-sulforaphane-antioxidants-glutathione--air-pollution-benzene-elimination)

4. **EPA omega-3s are as effective as SSRIs for major depression.** At ~2 g/day EPA, LPS-induced depressive symptoms are fully ameliorated in placebo-controlled human studies. EPA works by blunting inflammatory cytokines that cross the blood-brain barrier and suppress serotonin release — the anti-inflammatory mechanism is the primary driver, not direct receptor binding. [00:48:40](transcript.md#004840-epa-omega-3s--depression)

5. **The Omega-3 Index in red blood cells is the correct long-term biomarker for omega-3 status.** Plasma omega-3 levels reflect only recent intake (days), while the Omega-3 Index (% omega-3 in RBCs) reflects 120-day average status — analogous to HbA1c versus fasting glucose. Average American: 4–5%; Japan averages 10–11%. People at 4% vs. 8% Omega-3 Index show a five-year difference in life expectancy. Smokers at 8% index have the same life expectancy as non-smokers at 4%. Test via [OmegaQuant](https://omegaquant.com). [00:54:23](transcript.md#005423-benefits-of-omega-3-fatty-acids-omega-3-index--life-expectancy)

6. **Supplement with triglyceride-form fish oil; target 2–4 g EPA/day and verify with Omega-3 Index testing.** Ethyl ester fish oil requires food for absorption; triglyceride form is inherently more bioavailable. Store fish oil refrigerated to prevent oxidation. Krill oil contains phospholipid-form DHA (higher brain bioavailability) but is far too low-dose for therapeutic EPA levels and is commonly rancid. Check purity via IFOS; target TOTOX under 10. [01:06:07](transcript.md#010607-omega-3-supplementation-omega-3-index-testing)

7. **Vitamin D functions as a steroid hormone regulating more than 5% of the protein-encoding human genome.** 70% of the US population has inadequate levels (<30 ng/mL). Vitamin D activates tryptophan hydroxylase-2 in the brain (converts tryptophan to serotonin), regulates immune function, bone homeostasis, and blood pressure. Target blood level: 40–60 ng/mL. General starting dose: 1,000 IU raises levels ~5 ng/mL; most deficient people need 2,000–4,000 IU/day. Genetic SNPs can severely impair conversion, requiring much higher doses. [01:22:18](transcript.md#012218-role-of-vitamin-d-gene-regulation)

8. **Skin surface area is critical for solar vitamin D synthesis.** Darker skin requires 6x more sun exposure than lighter skin for equivalent D3 production. UVB does not penetrate glass and is blocked by sunscreen. Exposing large skin surface areas (torso, legs) during peak UVB hours (10 AM–2 PM) is essential; arm and face exposure alone is inadequate. [01:33:15](transcript.md#013315-tool-7-skin-surface-area--sun-exposure-vitamin-d)

9. **Magnesium is a cofactor for >300 enzymes and directly required for DNA repair.** 40% of Americans are insufficient. Deficiency causes insidious, invisible daily DNA damage. Forms differ by target: threonate crosses the blood-brain barrier (cognitive/sleep); bisglycinate is calming (sleep/anxiety); citrate has mild laxative effects at high doses; malate supports gut epithelial mitochondria and exercise recovery. Dark leafy greens are the primary food source because magnesium sits at the center of the chlorophyll molecule. [01:40:30](transcript.md#014030-role-of-magnesium-magnesium-sources-dark-leafy-green-vegetables)

10. **Deliberate cold exposure produces a long-lasting, smoothly contoured dopamine increase.** One hour at 60°F produced massive, sustained dopamine elevations lasting hours — unlike stimulant drugs which cause a spike-and-crash. Even 20 seconds at 49°F triggers significant epinephrine and norepinephrine release. The mood lift reported post-cold is supported directly by these neurochemical data. [01:50:57](transcript.md#015057-tool-9-deliberate-cold-exposure-protocol--moodanxiety)

11. **Cold activates UCP1-driven thermogenesis and PGC-1alpha-driven fat browning — but blunts hypertrophy.** Norepinephrine from cold activates UCP1 (uncoupling protein 1) in brown adipose tissue, shifting mitochondria from ATP to heat production. Repeated cold browns white/beige adipose tissue by increasing mitochondrial density via PGC-1alpha. However, this same PGC-1alpha activation competes directly with mTOR-driven muscle protein synthesis — cold within ~4 hours of strength training significantly blunts hypertrophy. Cold is appropriate after cardio/HIIT, not resistance training. [02:02:30](transcript.md#020230-tool-11-cold--fat-browning-pgc-1alpha-metabolism)

12. **HIIT drives mitochondrial biogenesis in both young and old muscle — a profound anti-aging effect.** Matthew Robinson's study showed >50% increase in mitochondrial biogenesis markers in older adult muscle after a HIIT protocol. Cold exposure independently triggers PGC-1alpha-driven mitochondrial biogenesis in muscle via a pathway distinct from the adipose-tissue norepinephrine mechanism. Combining HIIT with post-session sauna is synergistic. [02:08:04](transcript.md#020804-tools-12-exercise-hiit-tabata--sauna)

13. **Sauna reduces all-cause cardiovascular mortality by 50% and dementia risk by 60%+ in a dose-dependent manner.** Finnish KIHD cohort data (Dr. Jari Laukkanen): men using sauna 4–7x/week at ~174°F for >19 minutes show 50% lower cardiovascular mortality, >60% lower sudden cardiac death, and >60% lower Alzheimer's/dementia risk versus once-weekly users. Duration matters: even 4–7x/week at only 11 minutes yields just ~8% cardiovascular benefit. Minimum effective protocol: 20 min at 174°F, 4x/week. [02:19:53](transcript.md#021953-sauna-vasodilation--alzheimers-and-dementia-risk)

14. **Sauna physiologically mimics moderate-intensity aerobic exercise and adds synergistic benefit on top of it.** Heart rate and blood pressure elevate during sauna then drop below baseline afterward — identical pattern to moderate aerobic cycling. Cardiorespiratory fitness improves more with exercise + sauna combined than either alone. Hot baths at 104°F for 20 minutes (shoulders-down) robustly activate heat shock proteins and BDNF in humans and attenuate ~40% of disuse muscle atrophy. [02:25:30](transcript.md#022530-sauna-benefits-cardiorespiratory-fitness-heat-shock-proteins-hsps)

15. **Heat shock proteins defend against protein aggregation linked to Alzheimer's disease and cardiovascular plaques, and predict longevity in humans.** A single 30-minute sauna at 163°F raises HSP levels ~50% above baseline; in rodents, elevation persists at least 48 hours. Humans with one HSP70 longevity SNP live on average 1 year longer; two copies confer 2 additional years. Heat shock worms and flies also live 15% longer. [02:25:30](transcript.md#022530-sauna-benefits-cardiorespiratory-fitness-heat-shock-proteins-hsps)

16. **FOXO3 is a master longevity transcription factor activated by heat, fasting, and reduced insulin signaling.** In C. elegans, suppressing insulin/IGF-1 signaling keeps FOXO3 constitutively active and doubles lifespan. FOXO3 drives autophagy, DNA repair, and antioxidant responses. In humans, a longevity allele of FOXO3 is enriched in centenarian populations. Sauna and intermittent fasting both activate FOXO3. [02:31:29](transcript.md#023129-insulin-signaling-foxo3--longevity)

17. **Weight-bearing exercise releases osteocalcin from bone as a longevity hormone that benefits the brain.** Osteocalcin enters the bloodstream during load-bearing and impact exercise (running, resistance training), crosses the blood-brain barrier, and promotes neuronal proliferation, synapse growth, and BDNF in the hippocampus. Non-impact exercise (cycling, swimming) provides less osteocalcin stimulus. Even 2–3x/week resistance training yields significant benefit. [02:37:41](transcript.md#023741-tool-17-exercise--longevity-osteocalcin)

18. **Traditional Finnish sauna (80–100°C) substantially outperforms infrared sauna for most documented benefits; sweating also excretes heavy metals.** Almost all longevity, cardiovascular, and dementia research used traditional saunas. Infrared saunas peak at ~140°F, requiring extended 60+ min protocols to approach similar heat stress. Sweating in any sauna meaningfully excretes heavy metals: cadmium 125x, lead 17x, aluminum 4x above baseline. Red light therapy is a distinct photobiomodulation mechanism — not a substitute for thermal heat stress. [02:41:37](transcript.md#024137-tools-18-red-light-sauna-infrared-sauna-sauna--sweating-of-heavy-metals)

## Tools & Protocols

### Tool 1: Sulforaphane — Broccoli Sprouts & Mustard Seed

- **What:** Sulforaphane activates NRF2 — the master antioxidant and detoxification transcription factor — plus heat shock proteins and other stress-response pathways.
- **How:** Eat 30–50 g (~1–2 cups) raw broccoli sprouts daily (up to 100x more sulforaphane than broccoli). For cooked broccoli: lightly steam, then add ~1 g (generous pinch) raw mustard seed powder immediately before eating to restore myrosinase activity and recover sulforaphane content fourfold. The powder should have a slight kick; no kick means it has expired.
- **When:** Daily, with any meal. Raw sprouts can be eaten anytime.
- **Ref:** [00:21:12](transcript.md#002112-tools-1-sulforaphane---broccoli-sprouts-broccoli-mustard-seed)

---

### Tool 2: Moringa — NRF2 Antioxidant Response (Heat-Stable Alternative)

- **What:** Moringa (Moringa oleifera) activates NRF2 similarly to sulforaphane without requiring enzymatic activation, making it heat-stable and convenient for smoothies or cooked meals.
- **How:** Add one heaping tablespoon of moringa powder (e.g., Kuli Kuli brand, validated by Dr. Jed Fahey) to smoothies or food daily.
- **When:** Daily.
- **Ref:** [00:23:50](transcript.md#002350-tool-2-moringa--nrf2-antioxidant-response)

---

### Tool 3: Omega-3 Fatty Acids — EPA & DHA

- **What:** Marine omega-3s (EPA and DHA) resolve systemic inflammation, support brain membrane structure, reduce depression risk, and are among the most powerful dietary anti-inflammatory interventions available.
- **How:** Target Omega-3 Index ≥8% (test via [OmegaQuant](https://omegaquant.com) — finger-prick blood spot test). Supplement with 2–4 g/day EPA (antidepressant, anti-inflammatory, cardiovascular); 1–2 g/day DHA (brain structure, membrane fluidity, serotonin/dopamine receptor function). Choose triglyceride-form fish oil; verify purity via [IFOS](https://certifications.nutrasource.ca/about/ifos) (target TOTOX <10). Store in refrigerator. Ethyl ester form requires food for absorption. Krill oil is not a viable substitute at therapeutic EPA doses — typically too low-dose and commonly rancid.
- **When:** With meals containing fat for optimal absorption. Allow 120 days before re-testing Omega-3 Index.
- **Ref:** [00:35:08](transcript.md#003508-tools-3-omega-3-fatty-acids-ala-epa--dha-fish-oil-oxidation)

---

### Tool 4: Food Sources of EPA Omega-3s

- **What:** Dietary EPA from whole fish for people who prefer food-first approaches.
- **How:** Sardines (canned in water or olive oil): ~1.5–2 g omega-3 per 3.5 oz serving; wild Alaskan salmon, mackerel, herring, anchovies also high. ALA (plant-based omega-3 from flaxseed, chia, walnuts) converts to EPA at <5–10% efficiency — not adequate as a sole source. Vegans/vegetarians should use microalgae oil supplements instead.
- **When:** Aim for several servings per week.
- **Ref:** [00:59:24](transcript.md#005924-tool-4-food-sources-of-epa-omega-3s)

---

### Tool 5: Food Sources of DHA Omega-3s

- **What:** Dietary DHA from whole food sources, with emphasis on phospholipid-form DHA for enhanced brain delivery.
- **How:** Wild Alaskan salmon (not farm-raised, which is grain-fed and lower in omega-3s); salmon roe (fish eggs) — extremely high phospholipid-form DHA, particularly relevant for APOE4 carriers with elevated Alzheimer's risk; oysters; sea vegetables. Vegans/vegetarians: use algae oil supplements (direct DHA source, bypasses inefficient ALA conversion).
- **Ref:** [01:14:40](transcript.md#011440-tool-5-food-sources-of-dha-omega-3s)

---

### Tool 6: Vitamin D — Testing & Supplementation

- **What:** Vitamin D3 supplementation to reach and maintain optimal blood levels (40–60 ng/mL), where it functions as a steroid hormone regulating >5% of the human genome.
- **How:** Test 25-hydroxyvitamin D (standard lab or at-home test). General rule: 1,000 IU/day raises blood levels ~5 ng/mL. Most deficient people need 2,000–4,000 IU/day. Take as vitamin D3 (not D2). Take with a fat-containing meal (fat-soluble). Combine with vitamin K2 (100–200 mcg/day) to direct calcium to bones rather than arteries. Genetic SNPs affecting D3 metabolism are common and may require much higher doses — always re-test after supplementing.
- **When:** Daily. Test levels at baseline and again after 3–6 months of supplementation.
- **Ref:** [01:25:30](transcript.md#012530-tool-6-vitamin-d-testing--vitamin-d3-supplementation)

---

### Tool 7: Skin Surface Area & Sun Exposure for Vitamin D

- **What:** Maximize solar vitamin D synthesis through strategic skin exposure.
- **How:** Expose torso, arms, and legs (not just face and hands) during peak UVB hours (10 AM–2 PM). Darker skin requires up to 6x longer exposure than lighter skin for equivalent D3 production. UVB does not penetrate glass or most clothing. Chemical sunscreens block UVB; consider 10–20 min unprotected exposure before applying sunscreen.
- **When:** Midday, when UVB reaches the surface (varies seasonally and by latitude).
- **Ref:** [01:33:15](transcript.md#013315-tool-7-skin-surface-area--sun-exposure-vitamin-d)

---

### Tool 8: Magnesium Supplements

- **What:** Magnesium supplementation to support DNA repair, ATP production, nerve conduction, sleep, and muscle recovery. Forms differ critically by target tissue and GI impact.
- **How:**
  - **Magnesium Threonate (Magtein):** Crosses blood-brain barrier; best for cognitive function and sleep depth. ~144 mg elemental Mg per dose.
  - **Magnesium Bisglycinate/Glycinate:** Highly bioavailable, calming; best for anxiety reduction and sleep onset. 200–400 mg elemental Mg.
  - **Magnesium Malate:** Best for exercise recovery and daytime use; supports gut epithelial mitochondria (malic acid); does not cause drowsiness. 200–400 mg.
  - **Magnesium Citrate:** Good general bioavailability; mild laxative effect at higher doses. Use lower doses (~135 mg) to avoid GI distress.
  - Food sources: dark leafy greens (spinach, chard, kale — magnesium sits at the center of the chlorophyll molecule), pumpkin seeds, almonds, black beans, dark chocolate, green apples, tart cherries.
- **When:** Threonate/glycinate 30–60 min before sleep. Malate in the morning or pre-workout.
- **Ref:** [01:44:50](transcript.md#014450-tool-8-magnesium-supplements-citrate-threonate-malate-bisglycinate)

---

### Tool 9: Deliberate Cold Exposure — Mood & Dopamine

- **What:** Cold immersion or cold shower to produce a long-lasting, smooth dopamine and norepinephrine increase; reduces anxiety and improves alertness.
- **How:** Target water temperature: 45–60°F (7–15°C). Duration: 1–5 minutes (build from 30–60 sec). Method: cold plunge, cold shower, lake, or ocean. Avoid going directly from hot sauna to cold plunge without 3–5 min transition (vasodilation → vasoconstriction shock risk is real). Frequency: 3–5x/week minimum; daily for mood benefits.
- **When:** Morning preferred for alertness; post-cardio also effective. Avoid within ~4 hours of strength training if hypertrophy is the goal.
- **Ref:** [01:50:57](transcript.md#015057-tool-9-deliberate-cold-exposure-protocol--moodanxiety)

---

### Tool 10: Cold Exposure — UCP1 Thermogenesis & Mitochondrial Adaptation

- **What:** Cold activates UCP1 (uncoupling protein 1) in brown adipose tissue, switching mitochondria from ATP production to heat generation; repeated exposure increases BAT density and metabolic flexibility.
- **How:** Same cold protocol as Tool 9. Shivering is an early, inefficient heat response; with adaptation, UCP1-driven uncoupled thermogenesis replaces shivering. Over time, you can tolerate cold longer with less discomfort while gaining more thermogenic capacity.
- **Ref:** [01:59:22](transcript.md#015922-tool-10-cold-exposure-mitochondria-ucp1--heat-generation)

---

### Tool 11: Cold Exposure — Fat Browning via PGC-1alpha

- **What:** Cold drives PGC-1alpha in adipose tissue to increase mitochondrial density per fat cell ("browning"), improving metabolic flexibility and heat tolerance. Critical timing caveat applies.
- **How:** Same cold protocol. Do NOT use cold within ~4 hours post-strength training if hypertrophy is a priority — PGC-1alpha activation from cold directly competes with mTOR-driven muscle protein synthesis. Cold after cardio/HIIT is compatible and additive for mitochondrial biogenesis.
- **Ref:** [02:02:30](transcript.md#020230-tool-11-cold--fat-browning-pgc-1alpha-metabolism)

---

### Tool 12: HIIT & Tabata for Mitochondrial Biogenesis

- **What:** High-intensity interval training drives PGC-1alpha in muscle tissue, creating new mitochondria — a profound anti-aging adaptation especially potent in older adults.
- **How:** Tabata: 20 sec max effort / 10 sec rest, repeated for 10 minutes minimum (push resistance above instructor recommendation). HIIT: 30–60 sec at ~90% max effort, 4–8 rounds with 2–3 min rest. Frequency: 3–4x/week. Combine with immediate post-session sauna for synergistic cardiorespiratory and mitochondrial benefit.
- **Ref:** [02:08:04](transcript.md#020804-tools-12-exercise-hiit-tabata--sauna)

---

### Tool 13: Sauna — Endorphins, Dynorphins & Lasting Mood Elevation

- **What:** Sauna heat stress releases dynorphins (kappa opioid receptor ligands causing discomfort), which sensitize mu opioid receptors to endorphins — producing a lasting euphoric state that extends well beyond the session and improves stress tolerance over time.
- **How:** Traditional Finnish sauna at 80–100°C (176–212°F). Duration: 15–20 min minimum; 30 min with heat adaptation. Exit when very uncomfortable, cool down 5–10 min, repeat 2–3 rounds. Frequency: 4–7x/week for maximum longevity benefit; minimum 2–3x/week for mood.
- **When:** Post-exercise for additive cardiovascular and mitochondrial benefit.
- **Ref:** [02:13:30](transcript.md#021330-tool-13-sauna-endorphinsdynorphins-mood)

---

### Tool 14: Mild Stress & Adrenaline for Memory Consolidation

- **What:** Acute adrenaline release from sauna, cold, or intense exercise consolidates memories of content learned immediately before the stressor, via the McGaugh inverted-U adrenaline-memory function. Vasodilation in sauna also improves cerebral blood flow, further supporting cognition.
- **How:** Study or review important material (presentations, papers, protocols) immediately before a sauna session or intense exercise bout. Optimal stress level = uncomfortable but not panicking. Sauna is better than cold for this use case; cold pushes too far down the inverted-U curve.
- **Ref:** [02:17:45](transcript.md#021745-tool-14-mild-stress-adrenaline--memory)

---

### Tool 16: Sauna Protocols, Hot Baths & Fertility Caution

- **What:** Practical sauna frequency and duration targets; hot bath as a sauna substitute; fertility warning for males.
- **How:** Minimum effective dose: 20 min at ~174°F (80°C), 4x/week. Hot bath alternative: 104°F water, shoulders-down immersion for 20 min (robustly activates HSPs and BDNF; attenuates ~40% of disuse muscle atrophy in human studies). Male fertility caution: prolonged scrotal heat reduces sperm motility and count; effects are reversible within 6 months of abstaining from heat exposure. Men trying to conceive should limit sauna to 1–2x/week or take 3-month breaks. Avoid sauna during pregnancy (hyperthermia risk to fetus). Never use sauna after drinking alcohol.
- **Ref:** [02:33:22](transcript.md#023322-tools-16-sauna-protocols-hot-baths--fertility)

---

### Tool 17: Weight-Bearing Exercise & Osteocalcin for Brain and Longevity

- **What:** Load-bearing and impact exercise releases osteocalcin from bone into the bloodstream, where it acts as a hormone crossing the blood-brain barrier to support hippocampal neurogenesis, synapse growth, BDNF, and longevity.
- **How:** Prioritize weight-bearing exercise (resistance training, running, jumping) over non-impact activities (cycling, swimming) to maximize osteocalcin release. Even 2–3x/week resistance training provides significant benefit. Running also provides distinct mind-wandering cognitive benefits (problem-solving, creativity) during steady-state effort.
- **Ref:** [02:37:41](transcript.md#023741-tool-17-exercise--longevity-osteocalcin)

---

### Tool 18: Sauna Type — Traditional vs. Infrared vs. Red Light

- **What:** Selection guidance for sauna type based on evidence strength and temperature requirements.
- **How:** Traditional Finnish sauna (dry or steam, 80–100°C / 176–212°F) is best supported by all longevity, cardiovascular, and dementia research. Infrared saunas peak at ~140°F — insufficient for most documented benefits unless protocols are extended (e.g., Japanese Waon therapy: infrared + warm wrapping for 60+ min total, showing cardiovascular benefit). Infrared saunas do produce significant sweating, which excretes heavy metals: cadmium 125x, lead 17x, aluminum 4x above baseline. Red light therapy (670–720 nm photobiomodulation, e.g., Joovv, KOZE) is a distinct mechanism and not a substitute for thermal heat stress.
- **Ref:** [02:41:37](transcript.md#024137-tools-18-red-light-sauna-infrared-sauna-sauna--sweating-of-heavy-metals)

**Dr. Michael Holick** — Vitamin D researcher at Boston University School of Medicine ([bu.edu](https://www.bu.edu/medicine/profile/michael-holick/))

**Cynthia Kenyon** — Pioneer in longevity science; discovered that reducing insulin/IGF-1 signaling in C. elegans doubles lifespan via FOXO3 ([ucsf.edu](https://www.ucsf.edu))

**Matthew Robinson** — Exercise physiology researcher at Oregon Health & Science University; published landmark HIIT and mitochondrial biogenesis data in young and old adults

**Dr. Gordon Lithgow** — Researcher at the Buck Institute; showed heat shock extends lifespan in nematodes and flies by ~15%

**Dr. Mark Shigenaga** — Researcher; published work on malate, citrate, and short-chain fatty acids as gut epithelial mitochondrial energy substrates

**Glen Jeffery** — Neuroscientist at University College London; researched red light therapy for age-related vision improvement ([ucl.ac.uk](https://www.ucl.ac.uk))

**Dr. Satchin Panda** — Researcher at the Salk Institute; known for intermittent fasting and circadian rhythm research ([salk.edu](https://www.salk.edu))

**James McGaugh** — Memory researcher (UC Irvine); defined the inverted-U relationship between adrenaline levels and memory consolidation
