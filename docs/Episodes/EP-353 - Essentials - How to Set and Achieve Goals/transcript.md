## [00:00:00] Goal Setting

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we're talking all about goals and the science of goal setting and achieving your goals.

## [00:00:23] Brain & Goal Pursuit

When we think about goal seeking and the pursuit of goals of any kind in the brain, it doesn't matter what the goal is — it involves a common set of neural circuits. One of the brain areas is the so-called amygdala, most often associated with fear. A lot of our goal-directed behavior is to avoid punishments, including things like embarrassment or financial ruin, and so the amygdala and some sense of anxiety or fear is actually built into the circuits that generate goal seeking and our motivation to pursue goals.

The other key area is the ventral striatum, part of what's called the basal ganglia — a neural circuit that helps us generate "go" (initiation of action) and "no-go" (prevention of action) behaviors. One circuit within the basal ganglia gets us to do things like run five miles in the morning, while the no-go circuit is the one that says no, I'm not going to eat that second cookie. So we have go and no-go circuits working in tandem.

Beyond the basal ganglia, there are two sub-regions of the cortex involved in goal-directed behavior. The lateral prefrontal cortex handles executive function — planning, thinking about things under different time scales, not just what we want immediately but what we might want tomorrow and how our current actions relate to the future. The orbital frontal cortex is involved in meshing emotionality with our current state of progress and comparing that to where we might be emotionally when closer to a goal.

To summarize the four key brain areas: the amygdala handles anxiety and fear; the basal ganglia drives action and inaction through go and no-go circuits; the lateral prefrontal cortex handles planning and thinking across different time scales; and the orbital frontal cortex processes emotionality — where we sit emotionally now compared to where we'll be when we reach our goal.

## [00:03:32] Goal Value, Action Steps

What is going on in these circuits can basically be boiled down to two particular things. The first is value information — trying to understand whether or not something is really worth pursuing. The other component is associated with action: which actions to take and which actions not to take given the value of a particular goal at a given moment in time.

There is basically one neuromodulator system that governs our goal setting, goal assessment, and goal pursuit, and that is dopamine. Dopamine is the common currency by which we assess our progress toward particular things of particular value — it is essentially how we assess the value of our pursuits. The neural circuit involving the amygdala, striatum, orbital frontal cortex, and prefrontal cortex works together to divide the whole process into two general categories: assessing value and taking action steps.

There are basically only three or four elements to goal setting and goal pursuit. An individual has to identify a specific thing they're going to attain. Assessment of whether one is making progress toward those goals is a second necessary step. And then there's the business of goal execution — do more of this, do less of that, do this, don't do that.

As we move through the neuroscience, keep in mind what are some things you've either accomplished or would like to accomplish going forward. This will help you build toward a set of protocols that you can plug your particular goals into — a route to those goals grounded in science that I think will allow you to reach them more quickly and with less effort.

## [00:05:49] Peripersonal vs Extrapersonal Space

To understand how vision relates to goal pursuit, we first need to understand the difference between peripersonal space and extrapersonal space. Peripersonal space refers to the space within your immediate reach — your interoception, your internal body perception (heart rate, breathing, skin sensations), and anything you can immediately access and consume, like picking up a coffee mug. These are consumatory behaviors tied to things already in your possession.

Extrapersonal space is everything beyond the confines of your reach. How we feel about a particular goal is truly a feeling we experience in the here and now even though the goal is in the future. Moving toward any goal involves orienting our thinking toward the extrapersonal space. If we are evaluating whether or not we made progress today or yesterday, that's an evaluation happening in the immediate peripersonal space, but the goal itself lives in extrapersonal space.

When we focus on an external point, we are engaged in a process of exteroception — focus on the extrapersonal space rather than the peripersonal space. This distinction becomes crucial when we understand how different visual modes recruit different neural and chemical systems in the brain.

## [00:07:17] Tool: Visual Focus & Pursuing Goals

Research from the laboratory of Emily Balcetis at NYU has demonstrated the practical power of visual focus on goal pursuit. In their studies, participants had to exercise wearing 15-pound ankle weights and move a certain distance to reach a goal line. One group was told to visually focus on the goal line; the other was not. The group that focused on the goal line reached it with 17% less perceived effort and 23% more quickly — simply by looking at it.

We have two branches of the visual system. When our vision is brought to a common focused point — what we call a vergence eye movement — the visual system engages a set of neurons involved in resolving fine detail and evaluating small changes over small periods of time. When our attention is focused in this way, blood pressure rises and adrenaline is released in low amounts, readying the body for action. The other pathway, the magnocellular pathway, is engaged when we take in global information about everything around us — movement, peripheral events — and is associated with a relaxation of alertness and a reduction in goal-directed behavior.

Understanding these two visual modes gives us a practical tool. If you already know what goal you want to pursue — a workout, a focused work session — the process is simple: focus your visual attention on one point beyond your peripersonal space. It could be a point on your computer, the wall, or a distant horizon. Focus your visual attention there and hold it with effort. This harnesses the physiological and neurochemical systems that put your body and brain into a state of readiness and forward motion toward your goal.

Your mental frame and attention are always positioned either toward peripersonal space (focused on your immediate possessions and state) or toward the extrapersonal space. Crucially, you have the ability to dynamically travel back and forth between those two modes — and doing so deliberately is at the heart of effective goal pursuit.

## [00:11:47] Visualization & Goal Pursuit, Tool: Visualize Failure

It turns out that visualization of the big win — the end goal, whether that's a championship, graduation, or a certain amount of money — is effective in getting the goal pursuit process started, but it is actually a pretty lousy and potentially counterproductive way of maintaining pursuit of that goal over time. There is a much better approach that also involves visualization.

If you look at the scientific literature, there is a near doubling in the probability of reaching one's goal if you focus routinely on foreshadowing failure. Think about the ways in which things could fail if you take action A or action B, and therefore choose action C. This makes perfect sense when we think back to the neural circuit: the amygdala, that center involved in anxiety, fear, and worry, is one of the four core components of our goal-setting and goal-pursuit circuitry.

While it would be nice to say that all you should think about is the wonderful things that will happen when you achieve your goals, the truth is you should be thinking mainly about how bad it is going to get if you don't. How disappointed you will feel. How it will negatively impact you in the long term if your goal is to reach your goal. The more specific you can get by writing down, thinking about, or talking about how bad it will be if you don't achieve your goals, the more likely you are to achieve them.

If you are going to visualize in a positive way, do that at the very beginning of a goal pursuit — and perhaps intermittently every once in a while. But most of the time, if you want to be effective, you should be focusing on avoiding failure, and being very clear about what those failures would look like and feel like.

## [00:14:08] Tool: Goal Setting & Difficulty Level

The goal should be significant, inspirational, and aggressive yet realistic — but what does that actually correspond to in terms of brain and body readiness? Research shows that the probability of achieving a goal goes up or down depending on whether one sets a goal that is easy, moderate, or seemingly impossible.

If the goal is too easy, too within reach, it doesn't recruit enough of the autonomic nervous system to make pursuit of that goal likely. On the other end, if the goal is too lofty and far from one's current abilities, it also doesn't recruit enough systolic blood pressure — people can't get excited in a way that places the body into a state of readiness because it doesn't feel tangible that they could actually achieve it.

When goals were moderate — just outside one's immediate abilities, where someone felt "that would take a lot of effort, but it's within range; maybe I can do it, maybe I can't" — there was a near doubling of the likelihood that they would engage in ongoing pursuit of that particular goal. Goals need to be realistic and truly challenging at the same time.

Don't set goals so challenging and so lofty that they crash the blood pressure system in the other direction and make you feel unmotivated. The sweet spot is that moderate level of challenge that sits just beyond current ability but remains genuinely attainable with real effort.

## [00:15:47] Dopamine & Goal Pursuit, Reward Prediction Error, Tool: Weekly Goal Assessment

Any discussion about goals and goal pursuit would be incomplete without a discussion about the molecule dopamine. Dopamine is often thought of as the molecule of pleasure and reward, but actually it is the molecule of motivation. In classic animal studies, rats with dopamine neurons depleted will still enjoy pleasure when it is placed directly in front of them, but their motivation to move even one body length to achieve that pleasure is vastly reduced. Naturally occurring conditions in humans that deplete dopamine produce the same result — the ability to experience pleasure remains, but the ability to pursue the series of action steps required to achieve pleasure is diminished.

Dopamine sits at the heart of our motivational state to seek out goals across all time scales — whether the goal plays out over minutes, days, weeks, or a lifetime. One of the most important features of how the brain releases and uses dopamine is called reward prediction error. The largest dopamine releases occur in response to things that are positive and novel — something unexpected and good happening. When we anticipate something positive and then it happens, we get some dopamine during anticipation and some when the event occurs, but less than if it were a complete surprise.

Critically, there is also the case in which we predict something good will happen, but then it doesn't — for instance, friends who don't show up for dinner. In that case, dopamine drops below our initial baseline. That drop is the chemical essence of what we call disappointment. Understanding reward prediction error is important for goal assessment: our brains are constantly updating predictions about what outcomes are worth pursuing based on dopamine fluctuations.

This has a direct application for goal assessment. Rather than checking in on your goals every day — which risks depleting dopamine through repeated prediction without reward — consider a weekly goal assessment schedule. Review your progress once per week, assess honestly where you stand, and adjust your action steps accordingly. This pacing allows dopamine systems to remain engaged over the long arc of goal pursuit without burning out motivation through daily micromanagement.

## [00:21:00] Dopamine & Vision; Importance of Behavioral Tools

One particularly important feature of the dopamine system is how it interacts with the visual system. When we focus on a particular point in visual space or a particular goal or horizon, blood pressure, epinephrine, and dopamine all get recruited to put us into a state of readiness and willingness to pursue things in the extrapersonal space. Conversely, when our visual attention is diffuse — when the aperture of our visual system is very broad — those systems relax and we tend to be more comfortable staying where we are in peripersonal space.

The relationship works in both directions. When we use our visual system to focus on a point, it recruits the chemical and neural systems that put us into a state of pursuit. When we increase chemicals like epinephrine and dopamine, our visual attention for particular things out in space also increases. Some people leverage this by using things like caffeine or other compounds to increase dopamine, and it works both ways.

I am a particular fan of using behavioral tools always prior to supplementation or other chemical tools, because behavioral tools have a feature that supplementation does not: used over time, they engage neuroplasticity. As we practice using our visual system to harness our attention to particular locations and thereby move toward particular goals, we get better and better at using those systems. The systems for focus and motivation themselves have plasticity — we get better at being motivated and focused when we deliberately place our visual attention at a given location.

## [00:22:57] Recap of Tools for Goal Setting & Pursuit

Let me briefly recap what has been covered up until now. First, set goals that are challenging but possible — those moderate goals, not super easy, not super difficult, but moderately challenging, seem to be the most effective in moving people toward their goals over both the short and long term.

Second, plan concretely. You need a concrete set of actions you are going to follow in order to reach your goals. Third, foreshadow failure. Imagining success and visualizing success can be useful at the outset of a goal and maybe every once in a while, but it is not effective for maintaining constant pursuit. Foreshadowing failure — visualizing failure and all the terrible things it will bring — seems to be more effective and maps well to what we know about the amygdala's role in the neural circuitry of goal pursuit.

Fourth, focus on particular visual points to harness your attention and remove distractors. Removing distractors and getting your body and brain into a mode of activation — achieving that healthy increase in systolic blood pressure that puts you into forward motion — is absolutely key. These four tools work together as a system: challenging goals create the right autonomic arousal, concrete plans create actionable go and no-go decisions, failure foreshadowing keeps the amygdala engaged as a motivator, and visual focus recruits the full chemical readiness package.

## [00:24:09] Goal Pursuit, Time; Tool: Space-Time Bridging

One tool that has been especially powerful and is grounded in both neuroscience and psychology research is something I call space-time bridging. It is based on the understanding that we can move our cognition and perception from a place of interoception and focusing on peripersonal space to exteroception — focusing on things outside the confines of our skin, beyond our reach, things that are literally goal-directed in nature — and that we can shift deliberately back and forth between these modes.

Here is how you do space-time bridging. Close your eyes and focus as much of your attention — including your visual attention — on your inner landscape and interoception: your breathing, heart rate, and the surface of your skin. Hold this for approximately three slow breaths. Then open your eyes and focus your visual attention on some area on the surface of your own body — for instance, the palm of your hand — and do three more breaths, maintaining roughly 90% of your attention internally while allowing 10% to focus externally on that body surface.

Then move your visual attention to a location in the room or external environment approximately 5 to 15 feet away, shifting roughly 90% of your attention outward and 10% inward (to track those three breaths). Then move your visual attention to yet another station even further away — typically a horizon or the farthest point you can see — for three breaths, attempting to move 99 to 100% of your attention to that external location.

Finally, expand both your vision and cognition to the broadest possible sphere, engaging the magnocellular pathway — dilating the aperture of your field of view to see as much of the visual landscape as possible — for three more breaths. Then return immediately to your internal landscape with eyes closed for three final breaths. This full cycle of stations — from internal to progressively more distant external locations and back — is space-time bridging. Practiced daily or semi-daily, it builds the neural flexibility to shift between peripersonal focus (consumatory, present-moment) and extrapersonal focus (goal-directed, future-oriented) with increasing ease.

## [00:31:38] Recap, Key Takeaways

To summarize the full episode: we covered the neuroscience of goal-seeking behavior — the four core brain areas involved (amygdala, basal ganglia, lateral prefrontal cortex, and orbital frontal cortex), and the role of dopamine as the common currency of motivation and value assessment. We discussed how the visual system can be used to better achieve goals through focused attention, and why forecasting failure can be more effective than forecasting success.

We also described the practice of space-time bridging — using the visual system and your ability to deliberately step your focus from stations within your body out into the world, further and further, and then back again, as a way to harness and cultivate the systems that link vision, space, time, and reward systems. As you set out to accomplish your goals, clearly identify what the long-range ultimate goal is. Identify what the milestones will be. Set a reward schedule for assessing progress at those milestones and at your ultimate goal.

My hope is that you will be able to incorporate these tools — if not all of them, perhaps just one or two — in pursuit of whatever particular goals you happen to be focused on now and in the future. And last, but certainly not least, thank you for your interest in science.
