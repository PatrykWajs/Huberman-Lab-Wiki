# Episode 330: Enhance Your Learning Speed - Health Using Neuroscience Based Protocols - Dr. Poppy Crum

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Poppy Crum, neuroscientist, adjunct professor at Stanford, and former Chief Scientist at Dolby Laboratories, to discuss how technology and environment continuously reshape the brain through neuroplasticity. They explore how smartphones alter the cortical homunculus, how AI can increase learning efficacy or erode cognitive skill depending on how it is used, and how emerging sensor technologies and digital twins can personalize health optimization in real time. Dr. Crum also shares concrete protocols for using AI as a self-testing tool, interpreting wearable data to modify behavior, and applying closed-loop feedback to accelerate skill acquisition - all grounded in neuroscience.

## Guest: Dr. Poppy Crum

**Dr. Poppy Crum** is a neuroscientist, adjunct professor at Stanford University, and former Chief Scientist at Dolby Laboratories. Her work sits at the intersection of sensory neuroscience, neuroplasticity, and technology design - studying how environments and tools shape the brain, and how emerging technologies can be built to enhance rather than diminish human cognitive capacity. She is an expert in auditory neuroscience, hearing thresholds, perceptual compression, and the use of biometric sensors and computer vision for real-time cognitive state assessment.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Every technology reshapes the brain.** Each time we engage with a technology - smartphones, GPS, audio compression - it changes cortical representations. The question is not whether technology changes us, but whether we are intentional about those changes. Dr. Crum frames this as the core challenge and opportunity of our era.

2. **The homunculus shifts with use.** The cortical map (homunculus) allocates representational space proportional to usage. Decades of smartphone texting have likely expanded thumb and index-finger representations at the expense of regions associated with handwriting and fine motor variety - a measurable, real neuroplastic change.

3. **Environment shapes hearing thresholds.** Chronic exposure to urban noise raises hearing thresholds (reduces sensitivity to soft sounds) in ways that are detectable and city-specific. Early technology environments - particularly loud or spectrally narrow ones - constrain which auditory maps the brain develops, including whether absolute pitch can be acquired.

4. **Neuroplasticity requires incentive.** Research on barn owls (Knudsen lab at Stanford) shows that brain maps remap significantly faster when survival depends on it. This principle scales to humans: the strongest driver of rapid learning is genuine necessity or high-stakes consequence - not passive exposure.

5. **Use AI to increase efficacy, not to replace cognition.** Dr. Crum draws a sharp distinction between two modes of AI use: (1) using AI to enhance understanding and access richer insights (brain-expanding), and (2) using AI to skip the cognitive work entirely (brain-replacing). GPS navigation is the canonical brain-replacing example. Germane cognitive load - the productive mental effort that drives learning - must be preserved.

6. **Self-testing is the most impactful learning tool.** Retrieval practice (self-testing) is the single most evidence-supported method for durable memory formation, because memory is fundamentally anti-forgetting. Using AI to generate practice questions from material you are studying is a direct, zero-cost application of this principle.

7. **Digital twins are domain-specific optimization engines, not replicas of the self.** A digital twin is a model of one domain of your life or environment - a swimming stroke, an HVAC system, a sleep environment - that collects data, identifies patterns, and suggests or makes adjustments. Social media platforms already operate behavioral digital twins of every user; the goal is to build personal ones that serve the individual rather than the platform.

8. **Pupil size (pupillometry) is a powerful real-time cognitive state signal.** Pupil dilation tracks cognitive load, arousal, and emotional engagement in ways measurable non-invasively. Dr. Crum's work uses pupillometry alongside other biometrics to infer internal state and adapt environments or experiences in real time.

9. **Fewer sensors, not more, is the right design direction.** Rather than covering the body in wearables, the most elegant approach uses ambient sensors - computer vision, CO2 levels, temperature, HVAC data, smart mattresses - to infer state from environment. Passive Logic is cited as a leading example of ambient intelligent sensing in buildings.

10. **Sleep scores illustrate effective behavioral gamification.** The sleep score (Oura, Whoop, etc.) works as a behavioral tool because it gives people a goal to improve without framing them as failures. Huberman and Crum agree that equivalent wakefulness scores measuring focus quality and cognitive engagement during the day would be the logical next step.

11. **Spiderwebs are extended sensory organs.** Dr. Crum's research involves using sound to vibrate spider silk and study how spiders sense their environment through the web itself. Spider silk responds to environmental vibrations - air movement, insect struggles, seismic signals - making the web a distributed sensory array. This exemplifies the broader principle that sensory systems extend beyond the body into the environment.

12. **Moths evolved ultrasonic jamming against bat echolocation.** Some moth species produce ultrasonic clicks that jam bat echolocation - a stunning example of sensory system co-evolution under survival pressure. This illustrates how high-stakes necessity drives extreme neurological and physiological adaptation.

## Tools & Protocols

### AI as a Self-Testing Partner
**What:** Use AI to generate practice questions from material you want to learn and retain.
**How:** Paste notes, a paper, or a transcript into an AI tool. Ask it to quiz you at varying difficulty levels. Answer without looking. Review gaps. Repeat.
**Why it works:** Retrieval practice forces active recall, which is the primary mechanism of durable memory encoding. AI makes on-demand self-testing accessible for any topic at zero cost.

### Closed-Loop Video Analysis for Skill Acquisition
**What:** Record physical skill performance with a smartphone camera, then use AI-powered computer vision to identify deviations from ideal form.
**How:** Record with a standard smartphone. Use AI analysis tools or a simple computer vision app to analyze movement patterns frame-by-frame. Compare to a target model. Dr. Crum used this approach to analyze her daughter's swimming stroke.
**Why it works:** Immediate, objective feedback closes the loop between intention and execution - the same principle behind high-performance athletic coaching, now democratized.

### Wearable + Ambient Sensor Stack for State Optimization
**What:** Combine sleep tracking (smart mattress or ring), CO2 monitoring, and light/temperature sensing to understand how environment affects cognitive state and sleep.
**How:** Track sleep score nightly. Monitor room CO2 during work blocks (high CO2 correlates with cognitive impairment). Adjust temperature and lighting based on data patterns.
**Why it works:** Most environmental effects on cognition are invisible without measurement. Data reveals what intuition misses.

### Germane Cognitive Load Preservation
**What:** A decision heuristic for AI tool use - before offloading any task to AI, ask: "Does doing this myself build a skill or understanding I want to retain?"
**How:** Reserve AI for tasks where speed matters more than skill-building (research synthesis, formatting, scheduling). Preserve effortful engagement for tasks where the doing is the learning (writing, problem-solving, spatial navigation).
**Why it works:** Cognitive skill degrades without use. Conscious allocation of mental effort is the primary lever against AI-driven cognitive atrophy.

### Concepts
- Homunculus - cortical body map; Penfield's representation of the body surface in somatosensory cortex
- Bayesian priors - the brain's use of prior experience to predict and interpret incoming sensory data
- Germane cognitive load - productive mental effort that directly drives schema formation and learning (Sweller's cognitive load theory)
- Perceptual compression / lossy algorithms - MP3, Dolby formats that discard perceptually irrelevant audio data to reduce file size
- Pupillometry - measurement of pupil diameter as a proxy for cognitive load, arousal, and emotional engagement
- Digital twin - a domain-specific computational model that collects data, identifies patterns, and adapts behavior for a defined system
