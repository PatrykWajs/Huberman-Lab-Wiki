## [00:00:00] Habits

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we're talking all about habits. In particular, we're going to discuss the biology of habit formation and the biology of how we break habits. Habits are things that our nervous system learned, but not always consciously. Sometimes we develop habits that we're not even aware of until they become a problem or maybe they serve us well. The fact of the matter is that habits are a big part of who we are. In fact, it's estimated that up to 70% of our waking behavior is made up of habitual behavior.

## [00:00:43] What are Habits?, Neuroplasticity

So if habits are largely learned consciously or unconsciously, we have to ask ourselves what is learning? Well, learning is neuroplasticity. Neuroplasticity is simply the process by which our nervous system changes in response to experience. But at the end of the day, neuroplasticity is about forming new neural circuits, new pathways by which certain habits are likely to occur and other ones are less likely to occur. As many of you are well aware, there are popular books about habits and there's a whole psychological literature about habits. Those two areas point to some very interesting aspects of habits that I think are worth mentioning.

## [00:01:15] Goal-Based vs Identity-Based Habits

First of all is this notion of immediate goal-based habits versus identity-based habits. Immediate goal-based habits are going to be habits that are designed to bring you a specific outcome as you do them — so each and every time you do them. So maybe you're somebody that wants to get more zone 2 cardio for instance; that would be an immediate goal-based habit. If your goal is to get that cardio maybe four times a week, every time you do it you could check off a little box and say "I did it," you met the goal. That is different than so-called identity-based habits where there's a larger overarching theme to the habit — where you're trying to become a fit person or an athlete of some sort. It's where you start to attach some sort of larger picture about yourself or what it means for you to do that habit, where there's both the immediate goal — complete the exercise, check off that box — but you're also linking it to some sort of larger identity. Another thing you'll hear out there in the literature is that it takes 21 days to form a habit, though some say 18, some say 30, some say 60 — and the answer turns out to be far more nuanced than any single number.

## [00:02:33] How Long Does It Take to Form a Habit?; Limbic Friction

It turns out there's excellent peer-reviewed data on habit formation timelines. There's a study published in 2010, first author Lai Ly, which found that for the same habit to be formed, it can take anywhere from 18 days to as many as 254 days for different individuals. So people are highly variable — and if you can't form one habit easily, it doesn't mean that you can't form other habits easily. The mystery of why certain people can form certain habits more easily than others probably has something to do with how well people manage what's called limbic friction.

Limbic friction is not a term you're going to find in the formal neurobiological literature — it's frankly a term I coined to encompass a number of different pieces of the psychology and neuroscience literature. It's a shorthand way to describe the strain that's required to overcome one of two states within your body. One state is one of anxiousness where you're really anxious and therefore you can't calm down, can't relax, and therefore can't engage in some particular activity or thought pattern that you would like. The other state is one in which you're feeling too tired, lazy, or unmotivated.

Both of those states — feeling too alert and too calm — relate to the function of the so-called autonomic nervous system, a set of neurons and hormones and chemicals in your brain and body that act as a seesaw. You're either alert or calm, either asleep or stressed. Limbic friction is a phrase that describes how much effort, how much activation energy you need in order to engage in a particular behavior. A lot of habit formation has to do with being in the right state of mind and being able to control your state of body and mind. I'm going to teach you how to measure your degree of limbic friction — that is, how much activation energy it will take in order for you to execute a new habit — and how to measure your limbic friction for being able to break a habit you don't want to have.

## [00:05:31] Tool: Linchpin Habits

The other key concept found mainly in the books and articles about habits is this notion of what I call linchpin habits. Linchpin habits are certain habits that make a lot of other habits easier to execute. Now, the sorts of linchpin habits I'm referring to are always going to be things that you enjoy doing. I'll give you an example from my life: I happen to like exercise — not all forms of exercise, but I happen to like resistance training and I happen to like running. For reasons I'll get into a little bit later, I place those activities typically early in the day because of the neurochemistry and the various types of hormones associated with performing those activities. I place those activities under the umbrella of what I call linchpin habits.

Why? Because those particular habits are easy to execute because I enjoy them. But they also make a lot of other habits easier to execute — things like being alert for work, things like making sure that I get good sleep the night before, things like hydration, things like making sure that I eat the foods that are better for me than some of the other foods I would more reflexively reach for if I weren't doing that training. Certain habits act as linchpins, meaning they shift a lot of other things — they can control and bias the likelihood that you will perform other habits that are harder to access and that you have less of an affinity for. And those linchpin habits always, always, always are things that we enjoy doing.

## [00:07:23] Habit Strength, Context Dependence & Limbic Friction

Now I'd like to shift to thinking about a particular aspect of habits, and that's habit strength. Habit strength is measured by two main criteria. The first is how context-dependent a given habit is. Context dependence means: if you go from one environment to the next, do you tend to do the same thing in the same way at the same time of day? For instance, brushing your teeth first thing in the morning — when I travel, I tend to brush my teeth at more or less the same time of day relative to when I wake up as I do when I'm at home. So it's context-independent, and therefore it's a very strong habit.

The other aspect of habit strength is how much limbic friction is required to perform that habit on a regular basis. This is extremely important because if you are in the process of building and consolidating habits, it's probably going to take more limbic friction to execute those habits. These two aspects — context dependence (whether you're likely to do the thing regardless of where you are) and how much limbic friction is required — will tell you whether or not that habit is deeply or just shallowly embedded within your nervous system. The goal of any habit we want to form is to get into what's called automaticity — fancy language for when the neural circuits can perform it automatically. That is the ultimate place to be.

## [00:09:13] How We Form Habits, Tool: Review Procedural Steps

I'd like to give you a particular tool gleaned from the research psychology literature. I learned about this from an excellent review article called "Psychology of Habit" by Wendy Wood and Dennis Runger, published in Annual Review of Psychology. They discuss the various ways that habits form in the nervous system, and they mention that with each repetition of a habit, small changes occur in the cognitive and neural mechanisms associated with procedural memory. Procedural memory is holding in mind the specific sequence of things that need to happen in order for a particular outcome to occur.

Let's say I want to get into the habit of making myself or someone else in my household a cup of espresso every morning. I would actually think through each of those steps — walk into the kitchen, turn on the espresso machine, draw the espresso — walking through each step from start to finish. It turns out that just that simple mental exercise done once can shift people toward a much higher likelihood of performing that habit regularly, not just the first time, but as they continue out into the days and weeks that follow. This procedural stepping through of the steps — whether it's a recipe, sitting down to study, generating exercise, whatever the habit you're trying to learn — sets in motion the same neurons that are going to be required for the execution of that habit. When you actually show up to perform that habit, it's as if the dominoes fall more easily, with a lower threshold. So simply take the time, do it once or maybe twice, sit down, close your eyes if you like, and just step through the procedure of what it's going to take in order to perform that habit.

## [00:11:21] Tool: Task Bracketing

I'd like to discuss what I think is perhaps the most powerful tool for being able to acquire and stick to new habits: task bracketing. We have in our brain a set of neural circuits that fall under the umbrella term of the basal ganglia. The basal ganglia are involved in action execution — doing certain things — and action suppression — not doing certain things. In the experimental realm, these are referred to as "go" (do) or "no go" (don't do) certain things. It turns out that there's an area of our basal ganglia called the dorsolateral striatum, which is very important for the establishment of behaviors associated with a habit but not necessarily the habit itself.

Beautiful studies in both animals and humans that record the electrical activity in the dorsal striatum find that the dorsal striatum becomes active at the beginning of a particular habit and at the very end and after a particular habit — hence the phrase task bracketing. It brackets the habit. This is very important because task bracketing is what underlies whether or not a habit will be context-dependent or not, whether it will be strong and likely to occur even if we didn't get a good night's sleep the night before, even if we're feeling distracted, even if we are not feeling like doing something emotionally.

If the neural circuits for task bracketing are deeply embedded in us — meaning they are very robust around a particular habit — well then it's likely that we're going to go out for that zone 2 cardio no matter what. Brushing our teeth is a pretty good example: for most people, even if you got a terrible night's sleep, even if everything in your life is going wrong, you're going to brush your teeth. To leverage the neural mechanisms of task bracketing, you want to create a kind of imprint, a fingerprint in your brain of "this thing has to happen at the start of this" so that it's reflexive. There's a way to orient your nervous system toward the execution process — sort of like warming up your body to exercise. When the dorsal striatum is engaged and primed, you can consciously insert which habit you want to perform, then leverage those neural mechanisms of task bracketing to increase the likelihood of actually performing that particular habit.

## [00:15:02] Should You Schedule Habits?; Phase-Based Habit Plan

To leverage the neural mechanisms of task bracketing and increase the likelihood you'll perform a particular habit, I have to break it to you that one thing you've probably heard over and over about habit formation is not true — and what I'm referring to is this idea that if you are very specific about exactly when you're going to perform a particular habit, you are more likely to perform that habit. While that is true in the short term, it is not true in the long term. The reason is that our nervous system tends to generate particular kinds of behaviors based not on time but on our state — meaning what level of activation is taking place in our brain and body, how much focus we happen to have, how fatigued or energized we are.

So while schedules are important, it's not the specific time of day per se that's going to allow you to get into, form, and consolidate a habit. Rather, it's the state that your brain and body are in that's important to anchor yourself to. I'm going to present a very straightforward but neurobiologically grounded program by which you can insert particular types of habits at particular phases of the day — not times of day, but phases — because particular phases of the day are associated with particular biological underpinnings, chemicals, and neural circuits. It involves dividing the 24-hour day into three phases: Phase 1 is 0 to 8 hours after waking up; Phase 2 is 9 to 14 or 15 hours after waking up; and Phase 3 is 16 to 24 hours after waking up.

## [00:17:00] Phase 1 (Morning) & Challenging Habits

Phase 1, which is 0 to 8 hours after waking, has a particular neurochemical signature. Regardless of what you do, the neuromodulators norepinephrine and epinephrine — noradrenaline and adrenaline — as well as dopamine tend to be elevated during that first 0 to 8 hours after waking. In that first phase, your whole system is action- and focus-oriented. We know that when you are action- and focus-oriented, and because of the neurochemicals that are naturally released into your brain and body, you will be more likely to overcome any limbic friction that stands in the way of performing particular habits.

So as you think about the various habits you'd like to adopt in your life, take the habits for which you know there's the highest degree of limbic friction — the ones that are hardest for you to engage in, that require the most activation energy — and put those in this zero to eight hours after waking. This will greatly facilitate your performance of those new habits. What you're doing is creating task bracketing — making it such that your nervous system will predict when you are going to lean in against limbic friction in order to perform particular types of habits.

## [00:18:23] Phase 2 (Afternoon), Relaxation; Mellow Habits

Phase 2 is about 9 to 14 or 15 hours after waking. During this phase of the day, because of the circadian shifts in our biology, dopamine and norepinephrine and cortisol are starting to taper down naturally, and a different neuromodulator — serotonin — is starting to rise. Serotonin is going to be highest in this second half of the day and tends to lend itself to a more relaxed state of being. There are certain things that we all can and should do during Phase 2 to lend themselves to a state of mind and body that will be beneficial for the generation and consolidation of certain types of habits.

First of all, as the day goes on, you should try to taper the amount of really bright light you're getting, unless it's sunlight — viewing the sun as it's at what we call low solar angle, headed toward the horizon, can be beneficial for a number of brain and psychological systems. Things like heat — sauna, hot baths, hot showers — are terrific things to do in the second half of the day; they tend to support this serotonergic, high-serotonin state and lend themselves to more calm and relaxation. This phase two is one in which you're alert, present, working, engaging socially, paying attention to a number of things, but you should really be trying to taper off your stress level.

Given what we know about the neurochemistry of learning and memory, and about habit formation and its reliance on certain forms of neuroplasticity, this second half of the day is a terrific time to take on habits that require very little override of limbic friction — so-called mellower activities. It might be journaling, practicing music, something that's a little bit challenging but doesn't require a ton of energy. The hallmark features of those basal ganglia circuits for go and no go are that they are very sensitive to the neurochemicals dopamine and serotonin and acetylcholine. By aligning particular phases of the day with those neurochemical states, those states start to be associated with the process of beginning and ending those particular habits, and the task bracketing becomes deeply embedded.

## [00:21:46] Phase 3 (Evening), Enhancing Sleep & Habit Consolidation

Phase 3 runs from about 16 to 24 hours after waking. During that period of time, there are a few things that are going to support being in a state of mind and body that will allow neuroplasticity to occur — that are going to allow the rewiring you've triggered during the waking part of the day to actually take place. Those things are very low to no light, meaning keeping your environment very dark or very dim. Keeping the room temperature low is very beneficial for getting and staying in deep sleep.

A lot of people recommend putting a gap between your final bite of food and when you go to sleep at night — some say four hours, others say two hours. Very important: if you get up in the middle of the night, use a minimum of light to navigate your surroundings, just as much as you need to safely do so, because light inhibits the hormone melatonin and can make it very hard to fall back asleep. Neuroplasticity — the basis of habit formation — happens in states of deep sleep. The rewiring of neural circuits happens during this Phase 3. So if you're not giving Phase 3 the materials it needs, you're compromising the habit-formation process that you worked hard on in Phases 1 and 2.

## [00:25:00] Habit Flexibility & Daily Timing

Some of you are probably asking: if I perform a particular habit during Phase 1 and I eventually get to the point where I'm engaging in those habits in a pretty effortless way, do I keep them in the same phase of the day? The good news is the literature says it doesn't matter. In fact, moving that particular habit around somewhat randomly can actually be beneficial to you, because moving it from one time of day to another is what achieves context independence — the thing we're really seeking, being able to do the same thing regardless of time of day or circumstances. That's how we know we've achieved real habit formation, that the habit has been moved into certain components of our neural circuitry that just allow us to do it reflexively.

The hippocampus — which many of you know is associated with learning and memory — is not actually where memories are stored; it's where memories are formed. That whole process of really leaning into something that's hard, then it becoming easier, then eventually becoming more or less reflexive, involves a migration of the information in the brain. Once it's migrated out to a different location, at that point it's achieved context independence. So once something has become reflexive, you should play with it a little bit in terms of time of day. If you want to keep it in the same phase, that's fine; but experimenting with shifting it around is perfectly acceptable and can even be beneficial.

## [00:27:33] Tool: 21-Day Habit Program; Habit Missteps

I want to spell out a particular system that I think could be very useful to most people — one that's rooted in the biology and psychology of habit formation and is entirely compatible with the Phase 1, 2, 3 program. This is at least for sake of example a 21-day system. I picked 21 days because that seems to be the most typical timeframe for engaging neuroplasticity as it relates to the formation of new habits.

Basically, what this involves is setting out to perform six new habits per day across the course of 21 days. The idea is you write down six things that you would like to do every day for 21 days. However, the expectation is that you'll only complete four to five of those each day. Built into this is a kind of permission to fail — but it's not failure. This approach to forming habits is based not so much on the specific habits you're trying to form, but the habit of performing habits — the habit of doing a certain number of things per day. Some activities probably shouldn't be performed each day: for instance, if I were to weight train or run every day, my biology doesn't recover so well, so I wouldn't want to do resistance training every day, but I might want to do it four days a week.

What about missteps? If you execute that bad habit — you didn't go to the gym when you were supposed to, or you ate something you didn't want to eat — what you should not do is feel so bad about yourself that the next day you feel even less motivated to perform those other habits. The data suggests that if you miss a day, that's actually fine. What you want to do is get back on track the very next day or opportunity. The reason this 21-day program has a built-in expectation of imperfection is precisely because neuroscience tells us that missing a day or two is not catastrophic for habit consolidation, as long as you return quickly.

## [00:32:47] Tool: How to Break Habits & Replacement Behaviors

Thus far, we've almost exclusively been discussing how to form habits. But what about breaking habits? Many people would like to break habits that they feel don't serve them well. One of the challenges in breaking habits is that many habits occur very, very quickly and so there isn't an opportunity to intervene until the habit has already been initiated and in some cases completed.

It turns out that the key to generating long-term depression in these neural pathways is actually to take the period immediately following the bad habit execution and in that moment capture the sequence of events — not that led to the bad habit, but to take advantage of the fact that the neurons responsible for generating that bad habit were active a moment ago — and to actually engage in a replacement behavior immediately afterward. Let's say you're trying to do focused work and you pick up your phone. You could just put it down and re-engage in the work behavior; but if you were good at that, you probably wouldn't have done it in the first place. What turns out to be very effective is to go engage in some other positive habit immediately afterward.

This has two major effects. First, you start to link in time the execution of a bad behavior to this other good behavior — in other words, you create a kind of double habit that starts with a bad habit and then ends with a good habit. It might seem counterintuitive — why would I want to reward the execution of a bad habit with a good habit? But really what you're trying to do is change the nature of the neural circuits that are firing so that you can rewrite the script for that bad habit. When people have applied this kind of approach, it removes the emotional component — the shame and guilt — from the bad habit execution and actually converts those neurons into an engine for building good behaviors.
