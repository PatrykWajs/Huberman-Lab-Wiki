# Episode 269: How to Achieve Inner Peace & Healing | Dr. Richard Schwartz

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Richard Schwartz, PhD, founder of Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy, joins Andrew Huberman to explain how the mind is naturally composed of multiple distinct "parts" — each shaped by lived experience and trauma — and how a core "Self" can learn to lead and heal those injured parts rather than suppress them. The episode includes a live IFS session in which Dr. Schwartz guides Huberman through exploring a protective "titanium teddy bear" part rooted in a conflict with a family member, demonstrating in real time how curiosity replaces judgment and how protectors guard deeper exiles. Dr. Schwartz then leads listeners directly through a self-exploration exercise using the same IFS framework. Topics span the three-part model (exiles, managers, firefighters), the 8 C's of Self, legacy burdens, shame and racism, romantic relationship patterns and the repetition compulsion, suicidal and addictive firefighter parts, the role of technology as a distraction enabler, IFS combined with ketamine and psychedelics, self-led activism, and the global vision of IFS as a cultural intervention. The episode closes with practical tools for ongoing daily self-practice without a therapist.

## Key Takeaways

- **The mind is multiple, not unitary.** Every person has subpersonalities called "parts" that developed to help them survive. This multiplicity is normal, not pathological. [00:02:11](transcript.md#000211-internal-family-systems-ifs-self-parts)
- **Three classes of parts:** Exiles carry pain (shame, terror, worthlessness) from trauma and are locked away. Managers proactively control life to prevent exile activation (critics, perfectionists, pleasers, caretakers). Firefighters reactively douse exile pain with impulsive behaviors (addictions, rage, dissociation, suicidal ideation, scrolling). [00:09:44](transcript.md#000944-trauma-parts-exiles-roles-critic-managers-firefighters)
- **Trauma is about abandonment, not the event.** A bad event only becomes traumatizing when no Self comes to rescue the hurt part — when it gets locked away rather than witnessed and comforted. [00:09:44](transcript.md#000944-trauma-parts-exiles-roles-critic-managers-firefighters)
- **The Self is always intact.** Beneath all parts lies a core Self — never damaged by trauma — characterized by the 8 C's: Curiosity, Calm, Clarity, Compassion, Confidence, Creativity, Courage, and Connectedness. When the Self leads, healing is possible. [00:40:04](transcript.md#004004-titanium-teddy-bear-the-self-curiosity-tool-the-8-cs-self)
- **Protectors protect for a reason.** No parts are bad. The inner critic, the workaholic, the judgment-suppressor ("titanium teddy bear") — all are trying to prevent exile pain from flooding the system. Earning a protector's trust before asking it to step aside is essential. [00:29:35](transcript.md#002935-exploration-of-inner-frustration-judgement-firefighters-protectors)
- **Somatic localization bypasses cognitive defenses.** Finding a part in the body (location, size, texture, temperature) short-circuits the narrator/thinking part and opens direct access to the part itself. [01:00:26](transcript.md#010026-cognitive-vs-somatic-feelings-tools-localize-body-feeling-curiosity)
- **Legacy burdens are inherited trauma.** Parts can carry extreme beliefs and emotions absorbed from parents, ancestors, or culture — not only personal history. Groups in conflict (e.g., Israel-Palestine) often act from these inherited burdens. [00:53:47](transcript.md#005347-role-confusion-conflict-self-clarity-legacy-burdens)
- **Romantic relationship problems repeat childhood wounds.** Exiles desperate for redemption from a parent wound drive subconscious partner selection toward parent-resembling people, generating the repetition compulsion. Healing the exile from within — not from the partner — breaks the cycle. [01:21:29](transcript.md#012129-unhealthy-romantic-relationships-child-parent-relationship)
- **Suicidal and addictive parts are firefighters, not enemies.** Both believe the pain would otherwise be unendurable. The IFS approach — getting curious about what they fear rather than fighting them — creates space to unburden the exile driving them. [01:42:51](transcript.md#014251-protectors-managers-firefighters-suicidal-addiction-behaviors)
- **Overworking is a firefighter behavior rooted in fear.** Huberman describes working 80-85 hours a week as dissociation from worthlessness and, at the deepest layer, terror of mortality. Peeling back the layers through IFS-style questioning revealed the root fear. [01:48:37](transcript.md#014837-overworking-fear-mortality)
- **Technology is a firefighter enabler.** Phones and social media fill every gap, preventing exiled parts from surfacing. The fear of sitting with nothing is the fear of what comes up when there is nothing to distract. [01:54:35](transcript.md#015435-technology-distraction-exiles-worthlessness)
- **Psychedelics put manager parts to sleep.** Ketamine (and other psychedelics) quiet protective/manager parts, creating faster access to exiles. In a 3-day IFS + ketamine retreat for 32 leaders, 15 minutes of post-ketamine IFS work equaled roughly five standard therapy sessions. [01:04:11](transcript.md#010411-ifs-psychedelics-ketamine-big-self-journal-retractions)
- **Early morning and breathwork are natural windows.** In the liminal waking state managers are still offline and exiles surface more readily. Cyclic hyperventilation similarly quiets prefrontal dominance, producing a comparable window. [01:11:18](transcript.md#011118-early-morning-breathwork-exiles-healing)
- **Shame dissolves with curiosity, not suppression.** The racist voice, the judgmental part, the addicted part — all carry burdens they did not choose. Attempting to exile them amplifies their grip. Getting curious, hearing them out, and helping them unload transforms them. [01:15:41](transcript.md#011541-shame-racism-protectors-carrying-burden-compassion)
- **Self-led activism is more effective.** People fighting injustice from a blended, righteous protector-part generate defensiveness in others. The same message delivered from Self — with calm, clarity, and compassion — is actually heard. [02:02:58](transcript.md#020258-culture-expanding-problems-activism-self)
- **IFS can be practiced without a therapist.** Protector-level work — getting to know parts, introducing them to Self, updating them on your age — yields significant relief without ever touching the exiles. Exiles generally require a trained therapist to safely access. [01:27:06](transcript.md#012706-therapist-self-exploration-protectors-introduction-to-self)

## Tools & Protocols

### Protocol: The 8 C's — Recognizing Self Energy
- **What:** A checklist for detecting whether you are operating from Self vs. from a blended part. The 8 C's are: Curiosity, Calm, Clarity, Compassion, Confidence, Creativity, Courage, Connectedness.
- **How:** Before or during any inner work, notice which C-word qualities are present. If multiple are active simultaneously, you are in Self energy. Any departure from them signals a protector has taken over.
- **When:** At the start of any IFS session, in difficult conversations, during daily check-ins, and whenever you feel reactive or blended.
- **Ref:** [00:40:04](transcript.md#004004-titanium-teddy-bear-the-self-curiosity-tool-the-8-cs-self)

### Protocol: Find the Part in Your Body
- **What:** Somatically localize a part to bypass cognitive defenses and access it directly.
- **How:** 1. Bring to mind a recurring emotion or reaction you dislike. 2. Focus on it and ask: where do you feel this in or around your body? 3. Note its location, size, texture, temperature, and any imagery. 4. Stay with it without urgency to fix it.
- **When:** At the start of any IFS exploration; especially useful for highly cognitive people who tend to narrate rather than feel.
- **Ref:** [01:00:26](transcript.md#010026-cognitive-vs-somatic-feelings-tools-localize-body-feeling-curiosity)

### Protocol: The Curiosity Check
- **What:** Verify that you are in Self before engaging a part, rather than reacting from another sub-part.
- **How:** After locating a part, ask: "How do I feel *toward* this part right now?" If the answer is negative (I hate it, I'm afraid of it, I want it gone), that feeling belongs to another part. Ask the reactive parts to step back and repeat until genuine curiosity is present.
- **When:** Immediately after localizing a part; before asking any questions to the part.
- **Ref:** [00:19:35](transcript.md#001935-feelings-curiosity-self-exploration-protecting-other-parts)

### Protocol: Self-Directed IFS Listener Exercise
- **What:** A structured inner dialogue sequence guided by Dr. Schwartz live in the episode, designed for anyone to run independently.
- **How:** 1. Pick a protective part — a critic, perfectionist, people-pleaser, or workaholic. 2. Focus on it; find where it lives in or around your body. 3. Notice how you feel toward it. If negative, ask the reactive parts to give space. 4. From curiosity: ask it what it wants you to know about itself — don't think, wait. 5. Ask: what is it afraid would happen if it stopped doing this? 6. Ask: what has it been protecting you from? 7. Extend appreciation — let it know you hear it. 8. Ask: if what it protects were healed, what might it like to do instead? 9. Ask: how old does it think you are? Update it if it has your age wrong. 10. Ask: what does it need from you going forward?
- **When:** Any time, alone, without a therapist. Not recommended for those with severe trauma or fear of the process.
- **Ref:** [01:31:08](transcript.md#013108-tool-questions-for-a-self-exploration-of-internal-protectors)

### Protocol: Daily Check-In Writing Practice
- **What:** Sustain and deepen the relationship with a part discovered in a session or exercise, preventing regression.
- **How:** Each morning, write to the part(s) you have been working with: "How is that part doing today?" / "What does it need from me today?" / "Does it still feel heard?" / "What does it want me to know?" Listen inwardly and write whatever comes.
- **When:** Daily, after any initial IFS session or listener exercise, for as long as the part needs attention. Dr. Schwartz reports doing this every morning himself.
- **Ref:** [01:39:30](transcript.md#013930-writing-forming-new-relationships-with-parts-leading-with-self)

### Protocol: Early Morning Exile Access
- **What:** Use the liminal waking state — before managers come fully online — to gently encounter exile material that protectors would normally suppress.
- **How:** On waking, keep eyes closed. Stay still. Notice what surfaces without trying to control it. Observe the contours of whatever arises without trying to resolve or interpret it.
- **When:** Immediately upon waking, before checking phone or getting out of bed. Do not attempt near water or in any state requiring alertness.
- **Ref:** [01:11:18](transcript.md#011118-early-morning-breathwork-exiles-healing)

### Protocol: Breathwork to Quiet Manager Parts
- **What:** Temporarily reduce prefrontal/manager dominance through cyclic hyperventilation, creating a window of access to deeper parts.
- **How:** A few minutes of cyclic hyperventilation (double inhale + full exhale). Creates a brief window where manager parts are quieted and exile material can surface.
- **When:** Prior to inner work when access feels blocked. Use safely — never near water, never while driving, never in any state requiring full alertness.
- **Ref:** [01:11:18](transcript.md#011118-early-morning-breathwork-exiles-healing)

## People Mentioned

- **Dr. Richard Schwartz, PhD** — Founder of Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy; therapist, author, and director of the IFS Institute. Developed IFS in the early 1980s working with bulimic patients after family therapy approaches failed. [https://ifs-institute.com](https://ifs-institute.com)
- **Andrew Huberman** — Host of Huberman Lab podcast; professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. [https://hubermanlab.com](https://hubermanlab.com)
- **Martha Beck** — Author, life coach, and podcaster. Referenced by both Huberman and Schwartz for her teachings on the Self (capital S) and owning shameful parts without identifying as them. [https://marthabeck.com](https://marthabeck.com)
- **Oliver Sacks** — Neurologist and author. Huberman references Sacks's memoir *On the Move* as a portrait of how established fields suppress new ideas, paralleling Schwartz's experience with institutional psychiatry. [https://www.oliversacks.com](https://www.oliversacks.com)
- **Carl Jung** — Psychiatrist and psychoanalyst. Huberman notes that Jung also described multiple inner subpersonalities, predating IFS. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Jung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Jung)
