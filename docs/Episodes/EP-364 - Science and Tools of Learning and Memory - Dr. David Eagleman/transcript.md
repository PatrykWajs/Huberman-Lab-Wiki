## [00:02:35] Neuroplasticity & Learning; Cortex, Flexibility & Repurposing, Savantism

**Andrew Huberman:** David, welcome back. I remember when I was a senior — because you got into this public-facing science education long before I did — and you've had an amazing career also in your laboratory work. Today I want to talk about all of it, mostly by listening and you doing the talking. There are so many topics in neuroscience that are fascinating, but I think perhaps the most fascinating thing about the human brain is its ability to change itself.

**David Eagleman:** Plasticity. So, I know how I think about neuroplasticity, but I want to make sure I understand how you think about it. The cortex — the outermost part of your brain — is a massive set of neural circuits. What's remarkable is how flexible it is. If you take someone who's blind from birth, the visual cortex doesn't just sit there doing nothing — it gets repurposed and taken over by other senses, particularly touch and hearing. That's cortical plasticity in action.

The brain's drive to change is competitive. Neural real estate gets allocated to whatever is getting used. That's why, when you practice something intensively, the representation of that thing in the brain literally expands. String musicians have a larger cortical representation of their left hand fingers than non-musicians. Taxi drivers who have to memorize city maps have larger hippocampi. The brain is constantly reshaping itself based on experience.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's also the phenomenon of savantism, where someone loses function in one area and suddenly gains extraordinary ability in another. Can you explain what that tells us about neuroplasticity?

**David Eagleman:** Yes. So, this is one of the most striking examples of neural repurposing. There are cases — some traumatic, some from frontotemporal dementia — where people who never showed any artistic or musical ability suddenly begin producing extraordinary art or playing piano with no prior training. What seems to happen is that circuits that were being used for language or social inhibition get damaged, and the underlying capacity — which was always there, possibly locked away — gets released.

This is really important to understand: the brain has enormous latent capacity. We all have circuits for things we've never exercised. The question is whether they ever get the opportunity to express themselves. In savantism, inhibitory circuits are removed, and something new emerges. It suggests that "average" human performance is partly a story of inhibition — we're constantly suppressing capacities we don't regularly use.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a profound framing. The brain's default is constrained performance, and when you remove constraints — whether through injury or deliberately — latent capacity emerges. What about the general question of how much the cortex can repurpose itself? Are there limits?

**David Eagleman:** There are limits, but they're far less rigid than people assume. We used to think the adult brain was essentially fixed — you're born with your neurons, you lose some, and that's that. We now know that's not true. The brain continues to change throughout life, just more slowly. The degree of plasticity decreases with age, partly because of changes in the levels of neuromodulators like acetylcholine and dopamine, which signal to the brain that something important and worth encoding is happening. But the capacity never disappears entirely. Adults absolutely can learn new skills, new languages, new instruments. It just takes more effort and more repetition to drive the same degree of change.

## [00:13:27] Specialization vs. Diversification, Practice; Internet & Curiosity

**Andrew Huberman:** I don't know if you saw this study that was published in Science recently that explored early specialization in sport or creative endeavor versus kids who played a bunch of different sports or were involved in a bunch of different creative endeavors. It turns out that specializing too early, on average, doesn't play out so well in terms of peak of success later. There are exceptions, right? But it turns out that being a bit more diversified in your physical activities or creative activities may better serve you.

**David Eagleman:** That makes complete sense from a neuroscience perspective. When you specialize very early, you're carving a deep, narrow groove in the brain. Those circuits become highly efficient but also rigid. There's less cross-pollination between different skill sets, which is actually what gives rise to creativity. Creativity, in many ways, is the unexpected connection between things that previously seemed unrelated. So you need diverse inputs to have that raw material for creative connection.

The other thing is motivation. Early specialization can lead to burnout. If a child is forced into one narrow track, even if they're initially good at it, the intrinsic curiosity — the drive that actually sustains long-term learning — often gets extinguished. The kids who have a richer exploratory phase are better positioned to find what they're genuinely passionate about, and passion is a massive amplifier of practice.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about the internet? We have more access to information and stimulation than at any point in history. Does that help or hurt curiosity-driven learning?

**David Eagleman:** It can do both, and which direction it goes depends heavily on how it's used. The internet is an extraordinary tool for curiosity. You can follow a thread — you wonder about one thing, click into it, find three related things, go deeper — and that kind of self-directed exploration is exactly the mode of learning that the brain loves. It's active, it's motivated, it's generating predictions and then checking them. That's a recipe for strong encoding.

The problem is that the internet is also optimized for engagement, not learning. Platforms are designed to capture attention and keep it, and that often means providing novelty without depth, stimulation without meaning. Scrolling social media and genuinely learning are very different modes of engagement with the internet, even though they use the same device. The former fragments attention; the latter requires sustained focus.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the critical variable is not internet access per se, but the mode of engagement — whether you're being passive and reactive or active and directed.

**David Eagleman:** Exactly. Active learning — where you're seeking, hypothesizing, testing — versus passive consumption. The brain is fundamentally a prediction engine. When it's actively predicting and then getting feedback on those predictions, that's when learning is happening at its deepest level. When you're just taking in a stream of content without generating predictions, very little gets encoded in a durable way.

## [00:22:05] Building a Well-Rounded Brain; Tool: Critical Thinking & Creativity

**Andrew Huberman:** We come into the world with some pre-programming of our brain circuitry, but we have some control over what the inputs are. As adolescents, as adults — assuming plasticity extends into adulthood — we have some control over what we learn and do. What do you think are the most important categories of input for building a well-rounded brain?

**David Eagleman:** I think about it in terms of range. You want exposure to things that are genuinely different from one another — not just variations on a theme. A musician who only plays classical piano is different from a musician who also studies jazz, West African drumming, and electronic music. Each of those exposes the brain to different rhythmic structures, different harmonic vocabularies, different relationships between sound and emotion. The brain's underlying computational machinery is very general-purpose, and the richer the variety of inputs, the richer the representations it builds.

Critical thinking is another crucial component. The habit of asking: what's the evidence for this? What would disprove it? What am I assuming? These metacognitive practices actually shape how neural circuits are organized. You're not just learning content — you're training the process by which you evaluate content. And that process, once well-developed, applies across everything.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the idea that some people are just "not creative"? Is that a genuine fixed trait or is it a reflection of how their education shaped them?

**David Eagleman:** Largely the latter, I think. Creativity isn't a single thing — it's a cluster of capacities: the ability to generate novel combinations, to tolerate ambiguity and uncertainty while you explore, to recognize when something unexpected is actually valuable. All of these can be cultivated. What shuts them down is environments that heavily reward correct answers over interesting questions, or that treat mistakes as failures rather than as information.

The good news is that people who were told they weren't creative often discover, when given the right conditions, that they have a lot of creative capacity. The conditions matter enormously: psychological safety, time for exploration, exposure to diverse domains, and permission to make mistakes.

## [00:28:18] Neuroplasticity & Adults; Tools: Novelty & Challenge

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there things we can do to extend the window of plasticity? Or are there activities — like learning an instrument or a game — that give our capacity for plasticity more height or more width, as opposed to just the same principles?

**David Eagleman:** You need to focus on the thing, then you need to make errors, then you need to do error correction. You get to sleep that night, and that's when consolidation happens. And then you repeat. But there are things that prime the brain to be in a more plastic state. Novelty is one of the biggest. When you encounter something genuinely new, the brain releases norepinephrine, which flags the experience as worth paying attention to and encoding. Challenge is the other key ingredient — not so hard that you fail constantly and feel helpless, but hard enough that you're operating at the edge of your current competence. That zone is where plasticity is maximized.

**Andrew Huberman:** The classic crossword puzzle problem — it's good for you until you're good at it.

**David Eagleman:** Exactly. And this is what older adults often get wrong. They think they're being cognitively active because they're doing familiar activities that require mental engagement. But if the activity is no longer challenging — if you're not making errors and correcting them — then you're in maintenance mode, not growth mode. The key is to continually find the next thing that you're not good at yet. That feels uncomfortable, and that discomfort is the signal that plasticity is happening.

**Andrew Huberman:** So novelty and challenge are the two key levers. What else?

**David Eagleman:** Sleep. This cannot be overstated. The consolidation of learning happens during sleep — particularly during the slow-wave and REM stages. If you learn something and then don't sleep, a large fraction of that learning doesn't stick. The sleeping brain is not idle; it's actively replaying and reorganizing the day's experiences, finding patterns, linking new information to existing structures. People who chronically undersleep are essentially paying a tax on their learning capacity every night.

## [00:32:41] Neuromodulators & Plasticity, Psychedelics; Directed Plasticity

**Andrew Huberman:** Most discussions of plasticity involve boosting the levels of some neuromodulator — dopamine, acetylcholine, norepinephrine, epinephrine, serotonin. But what's so interesting is that it seems like you can boost the levels of any of those and get plasticity. It's not like one neuromodulator gives you the opportunity for plasticity. So many of the interesting studies on psychedelics are using substances that are serotonergic — they act on serotonin receptors. What do you make of that?

**David Eagleman:** It's a fascinating observation. The neuromodulators are essentially the brain's gain controls — they determine how strongly a given signal gets amplified and encoded. And you're right that multiple different neuromodulators can open what we might call plasticity windows. The commonality may be that they all, in different ways, increase the signal-to-noise ratio for novel and salient information. They tell the brain: pay attention to this, it matters.

With psychedelics, what seems to happen — at appropriate doses — is a kind of global increase in neural connectivity. Patterns of communication that are normally suppressed become available. Default assumptions get loosened. This is why people often report that psychedelics allow them to look at familiar things with fresh eyes, or make unexpected connections between ideas. From a plasticity standpoint, that's valuable — you're temporarily destabilizing the existing structure so that new structure can form.

**Andrew Huberman:** The concept of directed plasticity — the idea that you can open a window of enhanced plasticity and then use it to deliberately encode specific things — seems important here.

**David Eagleman:** Very much so. Just opening the plasticity window without directing what gets encoded is of limited use. The promise of directed plasticity is that you combine something that enhances the brain's receptivity — whether that's a neuromodulator boost, a psychedelic, or even just deep focused attention — with specific learning content. The two together should produce faster, deeper encoding than either alone. This is an active area of research, and it's genuinely exciting.

## [00:39:41] Building a Better Future Self; Tool: Ulysses Contract to Avoid Bad Behaviors

**Andrew Huberman:** How should we think about thinking about our future self? Should we just avoid that loop and get stoic about it and live in ten-minute time blocks? It raises really interesting questions.

**David Eagleman:** It raises a really interesting set of questions. There's research by Hal Hershfield and others showing that people who feel connected to their future self — who can imagine that person as a real, continuous version of themselves rather than a stranger — make better decisions for the long term. They save more, they exercise more, they take better care of their health. The psychological disconnection from our future self is actually one of the key mechanisms by which we make choices that harm us down the road.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about the Ulysses contract? This is something you've written about. What is it exactly?

**David Eagleman:** Ulysses — Odysseus in the Greek — knew he was going to sail past the Sirens, whose song was so irresistible that sailors would steer their ships into the rocks. He wanted to hear the Sirens but not die. So, he had his crew tie him to the mast and plug their own ears with beeswax. He precommitted to a constraint on his future self, knowing that his future self in the moment of temptation would make bad decisions. That's the Ulysses contract: a precommitment made by your current self, binding your future self when you know it will be weak.

**Andrew Huberman:** And this applies to so many modern contexts — social media, diet, alcohol, late-night screen time.

**David Eagleman:** Absolutely. The structure of the modern world is largely designed to exploit the weakness of our in-the-moment decision making. Tech companies spend billions of dollars making their products as compelling as possible in the immediate moment. You cannot fight that with willpower alone — willpower is a limited resource and it depletes. The solution is to restructure the environment so that the temptation doesn't arrive in a moment when you're weak. Remove the alcohol from the house. Have your phone charge in a different room. Put website blockers on during work hours. These are Ulysses contracts. They shift the moment of decision to when you're clearheaded and deliberate.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the key insight is that the battle shouldn't be fought in the moment of temptation — it should be fought earlier, when you're calm and have full access to your values and goals.

**David Eagleman:** Exactly. And this connects to something deeper about what we are as a species. Humans are fundamentally temporal creatures. We live in time, we plan across time, we have a relationship with our past and future selves. The ability to make precommitments is uniquely human — it requires the capacity to model your future self accurately enough to constrain it. No other animal does this. It's one of the most powerful cognitive tools we have, and it's massively underutilized.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the relationship between identity and behavior change? You hear things like "I'm not a smoker" being more effective than "I'm trying to quit smoking."

**David Eagleman:** Yes, and this ties into the neuroscience of self-models. The brain constructs a model of who you are, and that model constrains behavior — you act consistently with who you believe yourself to be. If you identify as "a smoker who's trying to quit," the model still includes smoking as part of your identity. If you shift to "I'm not a smoker," you've updated the model, and behavior naturally aligns. The Ulysses contract works partly through the same mechanism — when you make a precommitment, you're also making a statement about who you are: I'm the kind of person who keeps their commitments.

## [00:50:13] Brain Chatter, Aphantasia & Practice

**Andrew Huberman:** Some people who are kind of neurotic tend to overthink things. They've got a lot of brain chatter. If they're going to go running at 8 a.m. and it's 8:02, they're like, "I can't go because it's 8:02. I'll go at 9:00. Got to do it on the hour." And then I know people who just do things and don't think about it as much — they're good at suppressing that voice. We assume the chatter and neurosis doesn't exist for them, but I think it does.

**David Eagleman:** I think you're right. What differs isn't the presence of the internal voice but the relationship to it — whether you're identified with it or can observe it from a slight distance. People who are effective at acting despite the voice have learned, often unconsciously, to let it run in the background without treating every one of its objections as a command. That's a learnable skill. Mindfulness practices help with this — not by silencing the inner voice but by changing your relationship to it.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about aphantasia? Can you explain what it is and what it tells us about mental imagery and practice?

**David Eagleman:** Aphantasia is the condition where people cannot generate voluntary mental imagery. If you ask them to picture an apple, they get — nothing. No visual image at all. And this is more common than most people realize; something like two to five percent of the population. What's fascinating is that many aphantasics don't discover they have it until adulthood, because they assumed everyone experienced the same blank that they do.

The implications for practice are interesting. Mental imagery — or mental rehearsal — is a well-documented tool for improving performance. Athletes who mentally rehearse their sport show real neurological changes. The motor circuits activate in similar patterns whether the movement is imagined or executed. So, a question that has emerged is: does aphantasia impair mental rehearsal? The answer seems to be: not as much as you'd expect. Aphantasics often develop compensatory strategies — they may rehearse in a more verbal or procedural mode. The brain finds a way.

## [00:56:57] Specialization vs. Diverse Experience, Childhood & Brain

**Andrew Huberman:** Does what we see in terms of specialization reflect the fact that people get siloed into different tracks — and on the whole that's advancing our species? Or does it reflect that chance actually plays a bigger role than we think?

**David Eagleman:** I think it's a combination. There's real value in having people who go very deep in narrow domains — that's how we make progress at the frontier. But the cost is that deep specialists often struggle to communicate across fields, or to recognize when an insight from one domain applies to a problem in another. The history of science is full of examples where major breakthroughs came from people who bridged fields — who brought the tools or concepts from one domain to a problem in another.

**Andrew Huberman:** And for children specifically — is there a critical period where diversity of experience matters most?

**David Eagleman:** Early childhood is a period of exceptional plasticity precisely because so much fundamental architecture is being laid down. The child's brain is solving a massive problem: what kind of world am I in, and how do I need to be structured to navigate it? The richer the input during that period — different languages, different physical environments, different social contexts, different types of problems — the more flexible and powerful the resulting architecture. But I want to be careful here: this doesn't mean children should be overwhelmed with scheduled enrichment activities. Play is one of the richest learning inputs that exists. Unstructured play in particular — where the child sets the agenda, encounters problems, and invents solutions — is extraordinarily valuable.

## [01:00:50] Space & Time Perception; Tool: Space-Time Bridging Meditation

**Andrew Huberman:** I was struck by the somewhat obvious thing that we can close our eyes, focus on our interoception — our skin, our breathing — we can meditate, bring our awareness into the present. Or we can open our eyes and focus on something some distance away. Or we can imagine the pale blue dot — Earth from space. And it seems like those map onto different modes of time perception as well. I call this space-time bridging. What's your take on it?

**David Eagleman:** I love this framing. There's a deep connection between the spatial scale at which you're operating and the temporal scale that becomes relevant. When you're focused on interoception — on your heartbeat, your breathing — you're operating on a timescale of seconds to minutes, and you're very much in the present. When you zoom out spatially — imagining the Earth from space, thinking about geological time — your temporal frame of reference also expands. Time becomes longer, you feel smaller relative to it, and interestingly, that shift often produces a kind of calm and perspective.

**Andrew Huberman:** And I think there's something actionable here for everyday life. Spending even a few minutes deliberately moving through those different spatial frames — close, medium, distant — might be a way to modulate your relationship to time and stress.

**David Eagleman:** Absolutely. And what's interesting neuroscientifically is that this probably works through the default mode network — the brain network involved in self-referential thinking, future projection, and perspective-taking. Deliberately varying the scale at which you're imagining things may modulate the default mode network in ways that affect both mood and time perception.

## [01:06:17] Are We Good at Estimating Time?; Fear, Time & Memory

**Andrew Huberman:** How good are people at perceiving time? And why am I always late?

**David Eagleman:** [Laughs] The why-are-you-always-late has to do with the Ulysses contract thing — it just requires a commitment to say, "I'm going to be the kind of guy who's always on time." And the way to do that is to say, "I'm going to commit to always being five minutes early." You get to a place early and you just hang out in your car and take care of some texts or whatever. That's the way to be always on time.

As for time perception generally: we're actually quite bad at it in absolute terms. The brain doesn't have a single master clock — it has many subsystems that track time at different scales, and they don't always agree. Our sense of duration is particularly susceptible to distortion by attention and emotion. When you're bored, time feels like it's dragging — your brain is checking the clock more often. When you're engaged and absorbed, time flies — you're not monitoring the clock as frequently.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the relationship between fear, stress, and time perception seems especially important. What actually happens in highly stressful or fearful situations?

**David Eagleman:** So this is something I studied directly. The popular idea is that during extreme stress, like a car accident or a fall, time seems to slow down because the brain is suddenly operating at a faster "frame rate" — taking in more perceptual frames per second. I decided to test this empirically. We built an apparatus that subjects fell from at an amusement park — 150 feet of free fall. If the slow-motion effect were due to faster perception, then subjects should be able to read numbers on a display that flickered too fast to read under normal conditions.

They couldn't. What's actually happening is not faster perception in the moment — it's denser memory encoding afterward. The amygdala, which is activated by fear, signals the brain to encode memory more thoroughly. So, you're not actually experiencing more frames per second, but the memory that gets laid down is denser — there's more of it relative to an equivalent duration of non-fearful experience. And when you replay a dense memory, it seems to take longer. Time slows down in retrospect.

## [01:12:53] Fearful Situations & Time Perception; Joyful Events & Novelty; Tool: Do Things Differently

**Andrew Huberman:** Is it accurate to say that your perceptual frame rate during a highly stressful experience is not different — you're not taking a higher frame rate movie?

**David Eagleman:** Correct. The frame rate is the same. What's different is the density of memory that gets encoded. But there's an interesting asymmetry here. In a fearful situation, your unconscious frame rate — the rate at which the amygdala and associated systems are tracking information — is higher. More is being recorded at the unconscious level. But your conscious perceptual speed hasn't changed. What you get out is a richer memory trace, not a slower-motion experience in real time.

**Andrew Huberman:** And you've extended this to thinking about joyful experiences and novelty — the idea that how you use your time, in terms of novel versus routine experience, affects how long your life feels.

**David Eagleman:** This is one of my favorite topics. Think about why childhood summers felt infinite and adult years fly by. As a child, almost everything is new — every experience is being actively encoded because it's the first time the brain has encountered it. As an adult, most of your daily life runs on autopilot — your brain has already built models of your commute, your office, your routine, and it stops encoding the details. Less is encoded, so the memory trace for that period is thin, and when you replay it, it feels brief.

**Andrew Huberman:** So, actively seeking novel experiences — travel, new skills, new routes home — actually enriches the felt sense of time passing.

**David Eagleman:** Exactly. And this is a tool for life. If you want your life to feel rich and long in retrospect, build in novelty and new challenges. Not hedonistic novelty — just genuinely new experiences that require the brain to actively build new models. This can be as simple as taking a different route to work, cooking a dish from a cuisine you've never tried, or striking up a conversation with someone from a very different background.

## [01:18:56] Staying in the Present, Mental Illness & Time Domains, Addiction

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm still trying to wrap my head around how much time we should spend trying to be present. My understanding of the dopamine system and addiction: an addiction is a progressive narrowing of the things that bring you pleasure. But addiction also involves continued use despite negative consequences. How does time perception relate to mental illness and addiction?

**David Eagleman:** Different mental illnesses are characterized by pathological relationships to different time domains. Depression is fundamentally a past-orientation disorder — the depressed person is trapped in rehearsing past failures, past losses. Anxiety is a future-orientation disorder — the anxious person is catastrophizing about things that haven't happened yet and may never happen. Both pull you out of the present. The present is actually where effective action is possible.

With addiction, the time horizon collapses. The addicted brain becomes intensely focused on the immediate future — the next hit, the next drink, the next scroll — and loses the capacity to adequately value the longer-term consequences. The reward system hijacks temporal reasoning. You can intellectually know that this is bad for you in the long run, but that abstract future harm doesn't carry the motivational weight of the immediate reward.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about mindfulness — the practice of deliberately being present? Is that retraining the temporal reasoning circuits?

**David Eagleman:** I think it is. What mindfulness practices seem to do, neurologically, is strengthen the capacity to observe your own mental states without immediately being captured by them. You notice the craving arising rather than being swept away by it. You notice the anxious thought without immediately treating it as fact. That observational capacity gives you a window for deliberate choice — the gap between stimulus and response that we sometimes call freedom. And yes, it's a trainable skill. The circuits involved — anterior cingulate cortex, prefrontal cortex — show structural changes in people who meditate regularly.

## [01:27:09] Social Media, Addiction, Curiosity

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think that constant engagement in TikTok-type social media upregulates the receptors for expectation, making it harder for people to stop using it? Because the drug addiction definition you gave — dopamine receptors for methamphetamine or cocaine — but for an experience like gambling or social media, do those receptors become more like circuitry for that type of experience?

**David Eagleman:** The short answer is yes. What we understand from addiction neuroscience is that any behavior that reliably triggers dopamine release can, if repeated often enough, produce changes in the dopamine system — both in receptor density and in the set point for what feels rewarding. With social media, the variable reward schedule is particularly powerful. Slot machines are addictive precisely because the reward is unpredictable — you never know which pull will pay out. Social media is designed on exactly the same principle: sometimes a post gets likes, sometimes it doesn't; sometimes you scroll past five boring things and then find one fascinating one.

**Andrew Huberman:** And curiosity — is it genuinely protective? Can people use their curiosity as a kind of immune system against shallow engagement?

**David Eagleman:** I think curiosity is one of the most powerful forces we have. It's intrinsically motivated — it doesn't depend on external reward. And crucially, it has a different neurological signature than the dopamine spikes of social media addiction. Curiosity activates the circuits for sustained attention and deep processing. It's the difference between the itch you need to scratch immediately and the long, engaging puzzle you want to work on for hours. Cultivating curiosity — following genuine questions wherever they lead — is among the best things you can do for both learning and mental health.

## [01:30:51] Vision & Auditory Deficits, Sensory Substitution, Neosensory Wristband

**Andrew Huberman:** You had a company — Neosensory — which was about combining different senses: people wearing bracelets to feel sounds. Can anyone do this even if they're not deficient in vision or hearing?

**David Eagleman:** So I got really interested in this topic about pushing information into the brain via unusual sensory channels. The classic example is Paul Bach-y-Rita's work from the 1960s: he found that if you translated visual information from a camera into patterns of vibration on someone's back, blind individuals could learn to interpret that vibration as a form of vision. The information came in through the skin but got processed by the visual cortex.

That tells you something profound about the brain: it's input-agnostic at a deep level. The cortex doesn't care what channel the information comes in through — it learns to decode and represent the patterns. With Neosensory, we built wristbands that translate sound into tactile patterns. Deaf individuals who wore them were able, over time, to develop meaningful comprehension of speech through touch.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the brain repurposes auditory circuits to process touch information that represents sound?

**David Eagleman:** Exactly. The auditory cortex, which would normally be processing sound, gets recruited to process the tactile information that represents sound. The brain is pragmatic — if there's reliable, structured information arriving through any channel, it will devote circuits to decoding it. This has huge implications not just for sensory deficits but for human augmentation — you could potentially add entirely new senses by developing new input channels.

## [01:37:13] Sensory Reliance, Echolocation, Potato Head Theory, Sensory Addition

**Andrew Huberman:** Ed Young wrote a book called An Immense World about different sensory modalities that different animals use. He says something that you totally agree with — we shouldn't think so much about whether a given animal can do this or that, but rather what it's like to be that animal, to live in its Umwelt.

**David Eagleman:** Exactly. The concept of the Umwelt — coined by the biologist Jakob von Uexküll — refers to the perceptual world as experienced from inside a particular organism. A tick's Umwelt consists of temperature and the smell of butyric acid. A dog's Umwelt is rich with olfactory information that we can barely imagine. A bat's Umwelt is dominated by echoes. Each organism's brain is tuned to extract the specific information most relevant to its survival.

The Potato Head theory is my way of illustrating this. Think of a Mr. Potato Head toy. The face is just a substrate — you can put eyes anywhere, put the ears anywhere, give it six ears if you want. The brain is like that: the substrate can accept inputs from wherever they're placed and will learn to process them. This is why echolocation can be learned by sighted humans — Daniel Kish has shown that with practice, humans can navigate complex environments purely through echolocation. The brain learns to process the echo information and build spatial maps from it.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the implications for sensory addition — adding senses beyond the usual five — are staggering.

**David Eagleman:** They are. We've demonstrated that you can add a sixth sense. We gave people with a Neosensory wristband that vibrated in patterns representing the orientation of the compass — north, south, east, west — and over time, they developed an intuitive sense of their orientation in space that didn't require conscious interpretation. It just felt like knowing where you were. The brain incorporated the new channel seamlessly.

## [01:41:36] Why We Dream, Vision & Neuroplasticity, REM Sleep, Blindness

**Andrew Huberman:** Everything that we perceive as out there — beyond our reach — is occurring by virtue of electrical and chemical events in our brain. Dreams are a unique situation: eyes are closed, often paralyzed during REM sleep, and yet we have very visual dreams. What do you think is the origin of the visual component of dreams?

**David Eagleman:** My favorite theory, which I explore in Livewired, is that dreams are the brain's way of defending its visual cortex. Here's the problem: the visual cortex is enormously valuable neural real estate, and it's sitting there every night in darkness — not receiving visual input. If the cortex is plastic, then other senses, which are active even during sleep (touch, proprioception, vestibular sense), could potentially begin colonizing that visual territory. Dreaming may be the brain's mechanism for preventing this — by generating its own visual content, it keeps the visual circuits active and defended.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a stunning idea. And what evidence supports it?

**David Eagleman:** Several lines of evidence. First: animals that are more vulnerable to predation during sleep, and therefore need to stay more vigilant, have less REM sleep. The tradeoff is between defensive dreaming and vigilance. Second: people who lose their sight as adults continue to have visual dreams for many years but gradually lose visual dream content over time — consistent with the visual cortex slowly being colonized by other senses. People born blind almost never have visual dreams — they never needed the defense mechanism. Third: newborns have an enormous amount of REM sleep — far more than adults — which makes sense if they're doing intense visual cortex development and need to constantly keep those circuits active and stimulated even while sleeping.

**Andrew Huberman:** So REM sleep is not primarily for emotional processing or memory consolidation — it's for defending the visual cortex?

**David Eagleman:** I wouldn't say "not primarily" — I think it does multiple things simultaneously. But the defense of neural real estate is an underappreciated function. Sleep is not a single state; it's a complex, active process with multiple phases each doing different work.

## [01:49:55] Victims, Fear, Memory Drift & Recall, Eyewitness Testimony & Jury Education

**Andrew Huberman:** If there are two individuals in a high-stress, highly traumatic interaction — and one is more stressed than the other, maybe they're the victim — does that mean their density of memory is higher, and therefore more accurate, even though there's a perceptual difference?

**David Eagleman:** That's a really important question, and the answer is nuanced. More emotional arousal generally leads to better central memory encoding — you remember the core of the event very well. But it also leads to worse peripheral memory — the details at the edges. The weapon focus effect is a good example: people who witness a crime in which a weapon is present tend to have excellent memory for the weapon and poor memory for the perpetrator's face, because attention was captured by the threatening object.

**Andrew Huberman:** And memory changes over time. What happens to memories between encoding and retrieval?

**David Eagleman:** This is one of the most important and least appreciated facts about memory: it's not a recording. It's a reconstruction. Every time you recall a memory, you're not playing back a file — you're rebuilding it from pieces, and in the process of rebuilding, it becomes susceptible to modification. Details can be subtly altered, new information can be integrated, and entire events can be confabulated. This is the mechanism behind false memories.

**Andrew Huberman:** And this has enormous implications for eyewitness testimony in legal proceedings.

**David Eagleman:** Enormous, and often tragic. Eyewitness testimony is still treated in many legal systems as highly credible, even though it's among the least reliable forms of evidence we have. The way police lineups are conducted, the questions that investigators ask, the social pressure in the courtroom — all of these can subtly (or not so subtly) alter what a witness reports remembering. Elizabeth Loftus has spent her career documenting this, and she's probably saved more innocent people from wrongful conviction than almost any other scientist. The solution is to educate juries about the reconstructive nature of memory, to change how lineups are administered (blind administration, sequential rather than simultaneous), and to stop treating confident recall as equivalent to accurate recall. Confidence and accuracy are poorly correlated.

## [01:56:10] Kids vs. Adults, Memory Manipulation; Photos

**Andrew Huberman:** We tend to believe what kids say. We assume kids don't understand all the incentive systems around lying that some adults do.

**David Eagleman:** But kids are actually more susceptible to memory manipulation. Elizabeth Loftus at Irvine ran these studies where she showed that children are more easily implanted with false memories than adults. Their memories are more fluid, which reflects their higher neuroplasticity — their brains are more open to modification in general, and that includes memories. It's a double-edged sword.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about photographs? People often feel that taking photos of an experience helps them remember it, but there's research suggesting the opposite.

**David Eagleman:** The "photo-taking impairment effect." When you offload the memory to a device, the brain can't be bothered to encode it as thoroughly — there's less motivation to commit it to biological memory when you have an external backup. This is part of a broader pattern: the more we externalize memory (Google, photos, GPS), the less actively we maintain those circuits internally. Whether that's a problem depends on how you value the particular memories. But if your goal is rich autobiographical memory — the feeling of having genuinely lived those moments — being present without a camera is probably better.

## [01:59:27] Polarization, In vs. Out Groups, Empathy; Fairness

**Andrew Huberman:** The world seems increasingly polarized. You've done interesting work on the neuroscience of polarization. What do the brains of highly polarized people look like in terms of in-group versus out-group processing?

**David Eagleman:** The brain has deeply ingrained circuits for distinguishing in-group from out-group members, and those circuits are very old evolutionarily — they predate political parties by millions of years. When you perceive someone as part of your in-group, empathy circuits activate. The anterior insula, the anterior cingulate, the mirror neuron systems — they engage. You feel their pain as something real. When you perceive someone as out-group, those circuits don't activate in the same way. They can actually suppress. You become capable of rationalizing harm to them in ways you never could with in-group members.

**Andrew Huberman:** And is this malleable? Can the in-group/out-group boundary be expanded?

**David Eagleman:** Yes, and this is the hopeful part. The categorization is highly context-dependent. You can be from opposing political parties and yet be in the same in-group as fans of the same sports team. What determines in-group membership is shared identity markers, and those can be deliberately expanded. Research shows that cooperative tasks — working together toward a shared goal — is particularly effective at dissolving out-group boundaries. When you need someone from the other tribe to help you solve a problem, and they help you, your brain updates the model. They become less other.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the sense of fairness — is it universal? Does it show up across cultures?

**David Eagleman:** Fairness intuitions appear to be universal — the ultimatum game shows this beautifully. Across cultures, people will reject unfair offers even when accepting would leave them materially better off. They'd rather get nothing than reward unfair behavior. This seems to be a deep social mechanism for maintaining cooperative norms. But what counts as "fair" — and who counts as deserving of fair treatment — varies enormously based on in-group/out-group dynamics. Fairness within the tribe, injustice toward the out-group can coexist in the same person without any felt contradiction.

## [02:06:31] Polarization, Reward vs. Punishment; Propaganda, Language, Complexification

**Andrew Huberman:** There's intense polarization around events being debated from both sides — the same collection of videos producing two totally different interpretations because of in-group/out-group framing.

**David Eagleman:** And the neuroscience of narrative is key here. When you're told a story in which a particular person is the protagonist — the hero — your empathy circuits align with them. When they become the antagonist, the same events produce opposite emotional responses. This is how propaganda works: it's fundamentally a technology for narrative construction that controls who you're empathizing with. The events don't change; the emotional frame does.

**Andrew Huberman:** Reward versus punishment as a framework for political and social policy — is this a genuine difference in how people's brains are oriented?

**David Eagleman:** There's evidence that it is. Some people are more sensitive to reward signals — they're oriented toward what might be gained, what's possible, the upside of change. Others are more sensitive to threat signals — they're oriented toward what might be lost, what's at risk, the downside of change. These differences are partly temperamental, partly shaped by experience. And they map — imperfectly but meaningfully — onto political orientations. The conservative temperament tends to weight loss aversion more heavily; the progressive temperament tends to weight potential gain more heavily.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about language? You've talked about "complexification" as a potential antidote to polarization.

**David Eagleman:** Complexification is the deliberate practice of introducing nuance and ambiguity into polarized discussions. When you hear a strong claim about the other side — they're all evil, they're all idiots — complexification is asking: what's the most charitable interpretation of why a reasonable person might hold that view? What valid concerns might be driving it? This doesn't mean agreeing — it means refusing to let your brain settle into the comfortable simplicity of pure out-group contempt.

The neuroscience behind this is interesting. The brain is a compression machine — it simplifies. Out-group members get compressed into a single, simplified representation: bad. Complexification is computationally expensive — it requires holding multiple, contradictory things in mind simultaneously. But it's precisely that computational cost that makes it valuable: it prevents the kind of runaway simplification that ends in dehumanization and violence.

**Andrew Huberman:** And exposure to people across the divide — not just abstract information about them, but actual human contact — remains the most powerful intervention we have?

**David Eagleman:** By far. The contact hypothesis — first articulated by Gordon Allport in the 1950s — holds that meaningful contact with out-group members, under conditions of equal status and shared goals, reduces prejudice. Decades of research have largely confirmed it. Nothing rewires the in-group/out-group circuits as effectively as genuine human experience of the other as a complex, sympathetic person. This is why segregation and echo chambers are so corrosive — they prevent the very experience that would update the model.
