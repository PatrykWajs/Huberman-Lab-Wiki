## [00:02:14] Direct vs Involuntary Attention, Mental Fatigue, Attention Restoration Theory

**Marc Berman:** Attention is deeper than most people think. We think that elements of attention are sort of involved in controlling all of our behaviors. When our attention is depleted, we don't have as much impulse control, we might behave more aggressively, and we may not be able to achieve our goals. With a lot of things in the modern world, our attention is just being fatigued and we're depleted — and it's really hard to recharge the battery or know what to do to recharge the battery. One of my mentors, Steve Kaplan, would talk about this directed attention fatigue problem that a lot of us are facing. Our ancestors thousands of years ago weren't bombarded with so much information like we are now; the modern human has to sort of pick and choose what to pay attention to, and it's kind of overwhelming.

**Marc Berman:** Steve Kaplan had this idea that humans kind of have two different kinds of attention. One kind is called directed attention — that's the kind of attention where you as an individual person are deciding what to pay attention to. Humans are really good at being able to focus on a lecture, reading a paper, or finishing a math problem, but we can't do it forever. Everybody has had that sensation where at the end of a long workday, maybe three or four o'clock, you might be just staring at the computer screen and you can't focus anymore. We call that a directed attention fatigue state. The other kind is involuntary attention — that's the kind of attention that's automatically captured by interesting stimulation in the environment, like bright lights or loud noises, which we don't have much control over.

**Marc Berman:** We think this involuntary attention is less susceptible to fatigue or depletion. You don't often hear people say, "Oh, I can't look at that beautiful waterfall anymore. It's just too interesting. I got to step away." What's happening in modern times is that our directed attention is being fatigued, but maybe we can restore it by going into environments that can softly capture our involuntary attention.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do we know the basis of attentional fatigue? I could imagine it's something in the noradrenergic, dopamine, catecholamine world. I could also imagine that it's literally a fatigue of the visual system and/or auditory system. If we allow our eyes to rest, it actually gets easier to look back and fixate on a target. What is the basis of the attentional fatigue for this focused attention — or what you call directed attention?

**Marc Berman:** It's a really great question. I'm not sure I have a great answer yet. Brain metabolism is about 20% of overall metabolism no matter what people are doing — except for really extreme exercise where brain metabolism goes down a little bit — but if you're asleep or if you're doing a hard calculus problem, I think the brain is still using 20% of metabolism. So it's sort of a puzzle: why do we get this mental fatigue state? I think this ability to direct attention is most likely in frontal cortex, whereas involuntary attention — sometimes called bottom-up or exogenous attention — is probably more activated by things in the parietal cortex, occipital cortex, or auditory cortex depending on what the external stimulation is.

**Andrew Huberman:** So we evolved to move through space and direct our attention to particular locations and let the rest of the world fall away. ## [00:13:50] Focused Work, Tool: Pre-Work Nature Breaks to Enhance Focus

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to talk about the data around what walks in nature and interactions with other components of nature can do for our cognition and our level of focus. Intuitively people appreciate that a nice walk in nature, not looking at one's phone, is very pleasant and relaxing. And then you get back to your desk and you can really focus. What about the laboratory data that support this? Maybe you could describe a few of the incredible studies that you've done.

**Marc Berman:** The kind of seminal experiment that we did was back in 2008. At that time when people did nature walk studies, they would ask people, "How do you feel after the walk?" and it was very subjective. People reported, "Yeah, I feel much more refreshed after the walk in nature." But I always felt a little bit dissatisfied by that. I wanted to see, well, does objective performance change? So we designed a study that was experimentally controlled with objective measures — how did people perform cognitively before and after going on a walk in nature.

**Marc Berman:** We brought people into the laboratory and gave them some challenging working memory and attention tasks — the backwards digit span task, where you would hear digits out loud at a pace of about one digit per second and then the participant would need to repeat them back in backwards order. At about five digits you're ready to pull your hair out. We then gave them a map of a walk — either through the Ann Arbor Arboretum (a nature walk by the psychology building at the University of Michigan) or a walk on busy Washington Street in downtown Ann Arbor. We also took participants' cell phones because we didn't want them texting or chitchatting; we wanted their attention fully on the environment. We gave them a GPS watch to make sure they actually went on the walk.

## [00:15:54] Nature Walks & Cognitive Benefits, Comparing Nature vs Urban Environments

**Marc Berman:** People did the backwards digit span task, then went on a 50-minute walk in nature or an urban environment, came back to the lab, and did the same task again. Then they repeated the whole procedure a week later with the opposite condition. What we found was pretty incredible: people's working memory capacity and their ability to direct attention improved by about 20% after the walk in nature versus the walk in the urban environment.

**Marc Berman:** People might think maybe it's just because the nature walk was more pleasant — people did tend to like the nature walk more. We measured improvements in mood but didn't find a very strong correlation between mood improvements and improvements in working memory and directed attention performance. The even stronger demonstration that this wasn't mood-driven is that we had people walk at different times of the year. Some participants walked in June at 80°F — they said, "Marc, I can't believe you're paying me to go for a walk in nature." Really healthy benefits. We also had participants walk in January at 25°F. People said, "Mark, I was freezing my butt off out there. I did not enjoy that walk." But incredibly, the people that walked in January when it was freezing cold and didn't enjoy the walk obtained the same working memory and attention benefits as the people that walked in June. You didn't even have to like the nature interaction to get this directed attention benefit.

**Andrew Huberman:** So there's something deep about processing auditory, visual, maybe even tactile stimulation of nature that somehow is good for our brains and restores our ability to direct attention. Could we speculate what might be occurring in the nature walks that is enhancing or allowing restoration of directed attention? I could imagine it's the irregularity of nature environments — maybe trees are spaced out in different ways, whereas indoor environments and city environments tend to have a lot of right angles. Whereas in the forest, there are things to break up that regularity.

**Marc Berman:** I think both elements are at play. Let's pretend we're on the walk in the urban environment. That walk required people to cross a lot of streets — so you had to be vigilant, still using directed attention. There's also a lot of car traffic, noise, advertising — things that kind of require some directed attention, so you can't really just mind-wander. The walk through the Ann Arbor Arboretum didn't have a lot of those distractions. And then there's this idea of soft fascination: the colors, fractalness, curved edges of nature that we think sort of captures our involuntary attention in what we say is softly fascinating ways.

## [00:21:31] Nature, "Softly Fascinating Stimulation", Fractals

**Marc Berman:** Let's pretend we're looking at a waterfall. The waterfall is really beautiful. You can hear the rush of the water going down, you can see the bubbles and maybe some of the froth. It captures our attention, but we can still kind of mind-wander and think about other things at the same time. So, it doesn't really harshly capture all of our attention and resources. If we're in Times Square, also super interesting — lots of interesting stimulation to look at — but it kind of captures all of our attention resources in an all-consuming way that doesn't allow for any reflection or mind wandering. While Times Square does capture our involuntary attention, we say it does so in a very harshly fascinating way, whereas the waterfall captures our involuntary attention in a softly fascinating way. And we think that's the way that's going to be restful of directed attention.

**Marc Berman:** We think two elements created why the nature walk was restorative. One: it didn't place as many demands on directed attention. And two: it had this softly fascinating stimulation that activated involuntary attention, but not in an all-consuming way. What causes soft fascination? It could be elements of the structure of nature. It's interesting that we can get these effects of nature improving cognitive performance from people just looking at pictures of nature versus pictures of urban scenes, listening to nature sounds versus urban sounds, watching nature videos versus urban videos. There's something about the visual aesthetic of nature that we think is producing some of those benefits — somehow our brain maybe processes that fractal stimulation in more efficient or easier ways than the 90-degree angled built environment.

## [00:27:12] Nature Images & Sounds, Cognitive Benefits

**Marc Berman:** The format for the image study was basically the same as the walking study. People come into the lab, we give them the backwards digit span task, but then we take them into a room in the lab where they just have a computer screen that's flipping through nature scenes or urban scenes. They look at the scene for a couple seconds and we also have them rate the scene on a scale of 1 to 3 for how much they like it — just to make sure they're awake and engaged. That whole procedure takes about 10 minutes. Then they come out and take the backwards digit span task again to see if there are changes in performance. And they'd come back a week later to repeat the whole procedure with the opposite condition.

**Marc Berman:** Even just seeing the pictures of nature we see improvements in working memory and directed attention. However, I would caution that the effects are not as large as they are for the actual walk. Just seeing 10 minutes of nature pictures is incredible that it works — you can get some of these benefits — but the benefits are not as strong as they are for the real thing. The same procedure happens with sounds of nature versus urban sounds: we put headphones on people, play nature sounds or urban sounds, and find that when people listen to nature sounds, they show improvements on working memory performance and directed attention.

## [00:30:03] Urban vs Nature Images, Complexity & Image Compression; Semantics

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm starting to wonder whether we have brain areas and/or circuits that are devoted to nature. Visual perceptions are built up from their elementary units — low-level visual cortex cares about circles and angles, higher-level cortex puts it into recognition of a person or building — it's literally built up from elementary units.

**Marc Berman:** We started to have some ideas around soft fascination — I love it as a concept, but it still is a little bit squishy. I wanted to get some quantitative parameters around it. One idea was that maybe nature scenes are actually more compressible than urban scenes. Because there are repeated patterns in nature, I don't need to store all of the information — I can kind of smush it down into fewer bits, and that might be easier for my brain to process. One of my students, Nwan, we ran a JPEG compression algorithm on thousands of nature and urban scenes. It turns out nature scenes get compressed down into fewer bits.

**Andrew Huberman:** So nature scenes have a lot of repeated structure — you can capitalize on that, meaning there's a lot of redundancy and you don't need all of that information. Natural images also tend to have a lot of high-frequency spatial content — a lot of little changes or little contrast changes that we don't really need. Whereas in the urban environment, there are more big contrast changes that we do need.

**Marc Berman:** We think that maybe the brain is doing that — in nature, because you can throw away a lot of the information, it's more softly fascinating and easier to process versus the urban environment. There's another element here too which we haven't got completely yet but which I'm very interested in. That's looking at semantics — the language that we use to describe a scene. When I see a nature scene, I don't have a huge language repertoire: lake, tree, river, shrub, sand, desert. But when I'm in the urban environment, I can say Volkswagen Beetle, BMW M3, Gothic architecture. My vocabulary is so much more complex for an urban scene. So the semantic demands of processing urban environments may contribute to their cognitive costliness.

## [00:40:44] Time Perception & Nature; Art Galleries

**Marc Berman:** I would say the gallery would have a similar effect to nature — my guess — because Steve Kaplan's attention restoration theory really is not specific to nature. An art gallery might meet those two criteria. If you don't have to be tested on the artwork and you can just kind of go there and you don't have an agenda, there's going to be a lot of very softly fascinating stimulation in an art gallery. ## [01:45:30] Physical Health Benefits of Nature, Trees & Indoor Greenery; Aquariums

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to make sure that I ask you about stroke, diabetes, and heart disease. You have some really interesting data that people who take on this practice of getting into nature can actually improve their health outcomes beyond just being able to focus.

**Marc Berman:** We had data on a large sample of people in Toronto — hundreds of thousands of people — looking at health metrics. We found that having more trees on the street was related to a 1% increase in people's health perception. That sounds pretty modest, but to get that equivalent benefit monetarily, you'd have to give everybody in that neighborhood $10,000. It was also related to being seven years younger, controlling for age, education, and income. We also had data on more objective health measures — stroke, diabetes, heart disease. We found if you increased the amount of trees on the street by one tree per neighborhood, that was related to a 1% reduction in stroke, diabetes, and heart disease. To get that equivalent benefit monetarily, you'd have to give every household $20,000, or it was related to being one and a half years younger.

**Andrew Huberman:** For people that don't have the opportunity to plant more trees in their neighborhood, would getting and tending to an indoor plant have any positive effect?

**Marc Berman:** People have found some effects of having indoor plants — not necessarily to these physical health benefits, but to some attention benefits of having indoor greenery. There's also been some benefits: hospitals are starting to take this seriously that patients subjectively feel better when there's greenery around. I've seen other work too that people having painful procedures actually benefit from having greenery brought into hospital rooms — it can be helpful for reducing feelings of pain.

## [01:53:26] Thoughts, Feelings & Physical Spaces, Biophilic Design, Bringing Nature Indoors

**Marc Berman:** Our thoughts and feelings are deeply influenced by the physical spaces we inhabit. This is the basis of biophilic design — incorporating elements of nature into built environments. We found studies where we collaborated with a foundation called TKF Foundation, now called Nature Sacred, that built many parks in the Baltimore, DC, Annapolis, Maryland area. Under the bench in each park was a journal, and people could write things in the journal. We ran a topic model on these journal entries and found that if the park was rated as being more natural, people actually wrote more about things related to naturalness, spirituality, and religion — suggesting a deep connection between natural environments and our inner reflective life.

**Marc Berman:** Even in architectural scenes, people will see nature in a building — maybe curved edges mimicking natural forms — and they may not even be conscious of it. But they like that kind of architecture better and find it more comforting. Another student of mine, Kate Schertz, led studies where we looked at architectural images and found people were using naturalness to make similarity judgments. So even in an architectural scene, people will like seeing nature in a building, and it may not even be conscious. They find that architecture with more natural features more comforting.
