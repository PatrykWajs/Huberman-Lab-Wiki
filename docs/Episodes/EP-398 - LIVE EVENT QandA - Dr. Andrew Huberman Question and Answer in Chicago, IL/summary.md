# Episode 331: LIVE EVENT Q&A: Dr. Andrew Huberman Question & Answer in Chicago, IL

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Recorded at The Brain Body Contract live event at The Chicago Theater in Chicago, IL, this episode is the full audience Q&A session from the evening. Andrew Huberman fields ten questions spanning brain health and aging, shift work sleep, clinical hypnosis, psychedelics in clinical therapy, NSDR vs. meditation for sleep, the podcast's impact on his life, upcoming frontiers in health science, daylight saving time, neuroplasticity for a 19-year-old, and reforming the American education system.

## Key Takeaways

- **Brain health in old age:** Zone 2 cardio (150–200 min/week) and resistance training (2–3x/week) are the primary levers against cognitive decline. Load-bearing exercise releases bone-derived hormones that cross the blood-brain barrier and may protect hippocampal neurons. Grip strength asymmetry between hands and loss of fine extremity control (e.g., inability to spread toes) are early correlates of dementia. [00:02:42](transcript.md#what-is-your-best-advice-to-keep-my-brain-healthy-in-old-age)

- **Acetylcholine and cognitive protection:** Nobel laureate Richard Axel chews Nicorette gum for nicotine's putative neuroprotective effect against Parkinson's and Alzheimer's. Declines in dopamine and acetylcholine are correlated with cognitive decline. Nicotine raises blood pressure and causes vasoconstriction — approach with caution. Emerging cholinergic and dopaminergic drugs are an active area of interest. [00:02:42](transcript.md#what-is-your-best-advice-to-keep-my-brain-healthy-in-old-age)

- **Shift work sleep optimization:** The swing shift is the worst pattern; staying on the same schedule for at least two weeks is key. Bright light — sunlight or a 900 lux LED — is the primary wake signal via acute melatonin suppression. For chronic shift workers: get whatever sleep you can. For acute schedule disruptions (e.g., travel), align to the local schedule instead. [00:07:07](transcript.md#how-can-i-optimize-sleep-while-working-24-hour-shifts)

- **Clinical hypnosis:** Clinical self-hypnosis is a brain state of simultaneous high focus and deep relaxation — the opposite of the normal alertness-vs.-rest trade-off. David Spiegel's Spiegel eye-roll test predicts hypnotizability by testing whether a person can maintain upward gaze while closing their eyelids, reflecting the ability to be simultaneously alert and relaxed. High hypnotizables show strong outcomes for smoking cessation and pain relief. Huberman advocates renaming it to escape the stage-hypnosis stigma, analogous to renaming yoga nidra as NSDR. [00:10:17](transcript.md#how-does-hypnosis-therapy-work)

- **Psychedelics in clinical therapy:** Psilocybin and LSD act primarily on serotonin receptors, creating neuroplasticity opportunity. A growing hypothesis holds that the serotonin release itself — not the perceptual trip — drives clinical benefit. MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD has more robust data and may see near-term legalization. Cautions: adolescent brains, predisposition to bipolar disorder or schizophrenia, lack of guided integration. Microdosing data are not yet compelling. [00:17:15](transcript.md#psychedelics-in-clinical-therapy)

- **NSDR vs. meditation for sleep:** 10–13 minutes of meditation improves memory and focus but can disrupt sleep if done close to bedtime. NSDR (yoga nidra) at any time of day — especially after waking mid-night — helps return to sleep and offsets acute sleep loss. Matt Walker's position: meditation does not replace sleep. Huberman's position: 10–20 min of NSDR each morning after insufficient sleep can replace the felt wakefulness lost, even if it does not fully replicate biological sleep restoration. He first learned yoga nidra at an addiction and trauma recovery center in Florida in 2017. [00:17:15](transcript.md#psychedelics-in-clinical-therapy)

- **How the podcast changed Huberman's life:** Describes himself as fundamentally introverted, cycling through four modes: sleep/recovery → foraging information → organizing it → dispersing it. Values the opportunity to share science he finds personally useful. Misrepresentation and out-of-context quotes are the main challenge. References postdoc advisor Ben Barres as a key iconoclast influence; Huberman spent Barres's final year recording interviews with him. [00:30:23](transcript.md#how-has-your-podcast-changed-your-life)

- **Next big things in health science:** (1) Non-protein amino acids in seeds and nuts — Stanford's Ed Rubenstein's largely overlooked hypothesis: if consumed in abundance, especially in liquid form, they may incorporate into body proteins, causing misfolding and potentially contributing to neurodegeneration. (2) Higher cognitive states: creativity, abstract reasoning, spirituality, and making room for experiences beyond current scientific explanation. [00:35:21](transcript.md#what-do-you-feel-is-the-next-big-thing-in-the-health-space)

- **Daylight saving time:** Uniformly anti-health — increases car crashes, heart attacks, and depression. NIMH chronobiologist Samer Hattar calls it anti-biology. Key habit: view low-angle sunlight morning and evening; clouds are not a barrier — extend viewing duration. No meaningful cataract or macular degeneration risk from low solar angle viewing (confirmed by Stanford ophthalmology chair). [00:44:31](transcript.md#daylight-saving-time-is-it-worth-the-productivity-trade-off)

- **Neuroplasticity at 19:** Young brains are hypoplastic — stress equals learning, provided sleep quality is maintained. Core advice: build cardio and resistance training habits, start journaling or meditation for self-awareness, surround yourself with people you like, learn an instrument (strongly improves cross-domain learning), and develop sensitivity to your internal "this feels right" signals. [00:46:34](transcript.md#enhancing-neuroplasticity-strategies-for-a-19-year-old-college-student)

- **Reforming education:** Schools should teach students early about their own brains and bodies: stress regulation, sleep, social connection, and zero-cost self-regulation tools. Goal: practical biological and psychological levers for navigating difficult life experiences, accessible across socioeconomic and language boundaries. [00:50:17](transcript.md#how-can-we-transform-the-american-education-system-to-be-more-effective)
