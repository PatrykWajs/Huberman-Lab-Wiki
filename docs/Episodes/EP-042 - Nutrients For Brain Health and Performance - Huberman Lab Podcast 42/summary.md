# Episode 42: Nutrients For Brain Health & Performance

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman walks through 10 science-backed tools — specific nutrients and behavioral protocols — for optimizing immediate and long-term brain health, cognitive performance, and neuronal integrity. The second half of the episode covers the neuroscience of food preference: how unconscious gut signals (neuropod cells), blood-glucose metabolism, and top-down beliefs combine to drive what we seek and eat — and how to deliberately rewire those preferences toward brain-healthy foods.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Fat is the brain's structural foundation.** Roughly 60% of the dry mass of the brain is fat, primarily in the phospholipid membranes of neurons. Dietary fat quality directly determines neuronal membrane integrity and function. ([transcript](transcript.md#000922-eating-to-enhance-brain-function--foundational-aspects-of-brain-health))

2. **EPA omega-3s (1–3 g/day) reduce neuroinflammation and support mood.** EPA — not DHA — drives the anti-inflammatory effect. Studies show 1 g/day EPA can match antidepressant efficacy; 2–3 g/day is the range with strongest cognitive evidence. ([transcript](transcript.md#001300-eating-fats-for-brain-health-efas-phospholipids-tool-1-1-3g-epa-omega-3day))

3. **Phosphatidylserine (300 mg/day) slows cognitive decline and supports memory.** It is a phospholipid naturally found in cell membranes; supplementation supports neuron signaling. The FDA allows a qualified health claim for cognitive decline reduction. ([transcript](transcript.md#002035-phosphatidylserine-tool-2-300mgday))

4. **Choline (1–2 g/day) is a precursor to acetylcholine — the neuromodulator for focus and learning.** Egg yolks are the richest dietary source (~150 mg per yolk). Most people are deficient. Alpha GPC (300 mg, 2–4x/week) is the high-bioavailability supplement form. ([transcript](transcript.md#002215-choline-egg-yolks-tool-3-1-2gday-threshold))

5. **Creatine (5 g/day) enhances brain energy metabolism, especially under cognitive load or sleep deprivation.** Neurons use phosphocreatine as a rapid ATP buffer. Benefits are strongest for vegetarians/vegans who get no dietary creatine. ([transcript](transcript.md#003222-creatine-for-cognition-tool-5-5gday))

6. **Anthocyanins from dark berries reduce DNA damage in neurons and support cognitive function.** Effective at 60–120 g (roughly ½–1 cup) of fresh blueberries/day, or 428–598 mg/day of anthocyanin extract (equivalent to ~5.5–11 g of blueberry extract). Best sources: blueberries, blackberries, dark currants. ([transcript](transcript.md#003628-anthocyanins-dark-skin-berries-tool-6-10mgday-extract-1-2-cups-berries))

7. **L-Glutamine (1–10 g/day) supports the gut-brain axis and reduces neuroinflammation.** Precursor to both GABA and glutamate; especially useful for brain fog stemming from gut inflammation, sleep apnea, or high systemic inflammation. ([transcript](transcript.md#004119-l-glutamine-tool-1-10gday--offsetting-apnea--inflammation))

8. **Neuropod cells in the gut send subconscious dopamine-triggering signals to the brain based on amino acid, sugar, and fatty acid content.** Discovered by Diego Bohorquez at Duke, these neurons operate completely outside conscious awareness and powerfully shape food preference. ([transcript](transcript.md#005950-gut-neurons-controlling-food-preference-neuropod-cells-tool-7-fermented-foods))

9. **Non-caloric sweeteners consumed alongside glucose-elevating foods cause dysregulated insulin release.** The brain is conditioned to release insulin upon tasting sweetness; when real glucose also arrives, insulin spikes inappropriately. Consume non-caloric sweeteners only in isolation from glucose-elevating foods. ([transcript](transcript.md#011815-non-caloric-sweetener--insulin-tool-8-dont-have-wglucose-elevating-foods))

10. **The insula integrates taste, interoception, and belief to determine food valuation.** Top-down beliefs about what a food contains modulate insula activity and can change the subjective experience of eating — and even alter physiological outcomes like ghrelin levels. ([transcript](transcript.md#012217-beliefs--thoughts-the-insula-tool-9-pairing-based-reshaping-food-preferences))

11. **Pairing a brain-healthy food with a fuel-elevating food conditions preference within 7–14 days.** This exploits the neuropod cell → nodose ganglion → dopamine circuit to make nutritious but initially unappealing foods become genuinely craved. ([transcript](transcript.md#013042-liking-neuro-healthy-foods--bettering-brain-metabolism-tool-10-food-wars))

12. **Fermented foods (2–4 servings/day) outperform high-fiber diets for improving gut microbiome diversity.** Greater microbiome diversity has downstream effects on brain function, mood, and neuroinflammation. Capsule probiotics show weaker evidence than whole fermented foods. ([transcript](transcript.md#005950-gut-neurons-controlling-food-preference-neuropod-cells-tool-7-fermented-foods))

## Tools & Protocols

### Tool 1: Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA)
**What:** EPA omega-3 — the anti-inflammatory, mood-supportive fraction of fish oil; supports neuronal membrane fluidity and reduces neuroinflammation.
**How:** 1–3 g of EPA per day from liquid fish oil or capsules. Verify EPA content is listed separately on the label (not just "fish oil" total weight).
**When:** Daily, with meals.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:13:00](transcript.md#001300-eating-fats-for-brain-health-efas-phospholipids-tool-1-1-3g-epa-omega-3day)

### Tool 2: Phosphatidylserine
**What:** Phospholipid component of neuronal membranes; supports memory encoding and cognitive endurance. FDA qualified health claim for cognitive decline.
**How:** 300 mg/day from supplement (sunflower or soy lecithin-derived).
**When:** Daily, with or without meals.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:20:35](transcript.md#002035-phosphatidylserine-tool-2-300mgday)

### Tool 3: Choline / Alpha GPC
**What:** Choline is the dietary precursor to acetylcholine — the neuromodulator of attention, learning, and neuroplasticity.
**How:** Target 1–2 g/day total choline. Best food source: egg yolks (~150 mg each; 6–8 yolks needed to reach 1 g). Supplement form: Alpha GPC 300 mg, 2–4x per week (not daily — some concern with long-term daily use and TMAO).
**When:** Food-based choline daily; Alpha GPC on high cognitive-demand days.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:22:15](transcript.md#002215-choline-egg-yolks-tool-3-1-2gday-threshold)

### Tool 4: Hydration & Electrolytes
**What:** Even mild dehydration (1–3% body weight) measurably impairs cognitive performance, attention, and working memory.
**How:** Drink water consistently throughout the day; include electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) especially if eating low-carb or exercising. Pale yellow urine is the target.
**When:** Ongoing throughout the day; prioritize before and during cognitive work sessions.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:28:26](transcript.md#002826-hydration--electrolytes-tool-4)

### Tool 5: Creatine
**What:** Creatine monohydrate replenishes phosphocreatine in neurons, boosting ATP availability during high cognitive demand or sleep deprivation.
**How:** 5 g/day of creatine monohydrate. No loading phase needed. Mix into water or coffee.
**When:** Daily, any time. Most impactful for vegetarians/vegans and on sleep-deprived days.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:32:22](transcript.md#003222-creatine-for-cognition-tool-5-5gday)

### Tool 6: Anthocyanins
**What:** Polyphenol pigments from dark berries that protect neurons from oxidative DNA damage and are associated with reduced cognitive decline and improved verbal learning/memory.
**How:** 60–120 g/day of fresh blueberries or other dark-skinned berries (blackberries, dark currants). Supplement: 428–598 mg/day of anthocyanin extract (studies used this range, equivalent to roughly 5.5–11 g of blueberry extract). Higher end (~598 mg anthocyanins / ~11 g extract) appears more beneficial.
**When:** Daily with meals or as a snack.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:36:28](transcript.md#003628-anthocyanins-dark-skin-berries-tool-6-10mgday-extract-1-2-cups-berries)

### Tool 7: Fermented Foods (Gut-Brain Axis Support)
**What:** Live-culture fermented foods increase gut microbiome diversity, which supports the gut-brain signaling axis and reduces systemic neuroinflammation.
**How:** 2–4 servings/day of fermented foods: kimchi, sauerkraut, plain yogurt, kefir, natto, kombucha. Shown in head-to-head trial to outperform high-fiber diets for microbiome diversity.
**When:** With meals. Start at 1 serving/day if gut is sensitive; increase over 2 weeks.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:59:50](transcript.md#005950-gut-neurons-controlling-food-preference-neuropod-cells-tool-7-fermented-foods)

### Tool 8: Avoid Non-Caloric Sweeteners With Glucose-Elevating Foods
**What:** Co-ingestion of artificial/non-caloric sweeteners with glucose-elevating foods trains insulin to release upon tasting sweetness alone — leading to rapid insulin dysregulation.
**How:** If you consume any non-caloric sweetener (stevia, sucralose, aspartame, monk fruit, erythritol), do NOT consume it in the same meal or drink as glucose-elevating foods (bread, fruit, rice, potatoes, starchy vegetables). Consume them separately or in meals without carbohydrate load.
**When:** Always. Conditioning occurs within days to weeks of repeated co-ingestion.
**Ref:** [transcript 01:18:15](transcript.md#011815-non-caloric-sweetener--insulin-tool-8-dont-have-wglucose-elevating-foods)

### Tool 9: Pairing-Based Reshaping of Food Preferences
**What:** Use the brain's metabolic conditioning circuit (neuropod cells → nodose ganglion → dopamine → nucleus accumbens) to make brain-healthy foods become genuinely preferred and craved.
**How:** Eat a brain-healthy food you do not yet enjoy (e.g., sardines, leafy greens, fermented vegetables) in the same meal as a food that reliably elevates your preferred metabolic fuel — glucose-elevating foods for most people, ketone-elevating fats for ketogenic dieters. Repeat consistently for 7–14 days.
**When:** Every meal for the initial 2-week conditioning window; preference self-maintains afterward.
**Ref:** [transcript 01:22:17](transcript.md#012217-beliefs--thoughts-the-insula-tool-9-pairing-based-reshaping-food-preferences)

### Tool 10: Belief-Driven Food Metabolism Optimization
**What:** Conscious beliefs and framings about food actually change physiological outcomes — ghrelin levels, satiety signaling, and brain metabolism — via top-down modulation through the insula and prefrontal cortex.
**How:** When eating brain-healthy foods, consciously identify what the food contains and its specific benefit (e.g., "these sardines provide EPA that reduces neuroinflammation"). Avoid dietary tribalism that creates rigid food identities — these lock in suboptimal patterns and resist updating. Let the evidence base, not identity, guide food choices.
**When:** Ongoing mindset practice at every meal.
**Ref:** [transcript 01:30:42](transcript.md#013042-liking-neuro-healthy-foods--bettering-brain-metabolism-tool-10-food-wars)

### L-Glutamine
**What:** Conditionally essential amino acid that supports gut lining integrity, reduces neuroinflammation, and serves as a precursor to both GABA and glutamate.
**How:** 1–10 g/day. Start at 1 g and increase based on tolerance. Powder form is most cost-effective.
**When:** Daily. Can split doses across the day or take with meals. Particularly valuable for those with sleep apnea, gut permeability issues, or chronic inflammatory conditions contributing to brain fog.
**Ref:** [transcript 00:41:19](transcript.md#004119-l-glutamine-tool-1-10gday--offsetting-apnea--inflammation)

## People Mentioned

**Andrew Huberman** — Host; professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. ([hubermanlab.com](https://hubermanlab.com))

**Diego Bohorquez** — Neuroscientist at Duke University; discovered and characterized neuropod cells — neurons in the gut mucosal lining that sense amino acids, sugars, and fatty acids and send rapid subconscious electrical signals to the brain via the nodose ganglion, triggering dopamine release and shaping food preference without conscious awareness. ([Duke University profile](https://scholars.duke.edu/person/diego.bohorquez))

**Ivan de Araujo** — Neuroscientist at Mount Sinai School of Medicine; key researcher on gut-brain signaling, food reward, and post-ingestive metabolic cues that shape food preference independently of taste. Co-author of "Rethinking Food Reward." ([Mount Sinai profile](https://icahn.mssm.edu/profiles/ivan-de-araujo))

**Dana Small** — Neuroscientist at Yale University; expert in multi-sensory flavor processing and the interaction between taste, caloric content, and dopamine-driven food preference; research on how sweetness combined with metabolic signaling conditions insulin responses. Co-author of "Rethinking Food Reward." ([Yale profile](https://medicine.yale.edu/lab/small/))

**Mark Schachter** — Co-author on the "Rethinking Food Reward" review paper (Annual Reviews of Psychology, 2019). ([paper](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev-psych-122216-011643))

**Robert Lustig** — Pediatric endocrinologist at University of California, San Francisco; prominent researcher and public communicator on hidden sugars in processed foods, fructose metabolism, and their relationship to obesity and metabolic disease. ([UCSF profile](https://profiles.ucsf.edu/robert.lustig))

**Charles Zuker** — Neuroscientist at Columbia University in New York; studies the neural circuits underlying taste perception. His lab identified non-overlapping cortical neuron populations that respond to sweet vs. bitter, and demonstrated that food preference is a central (brain-level) phenomenon that can be experimentally decoupled from peripheral taste. ([Columbia profile](https://zmbbi.columbia.edu/faculty/charles-zuker))

**Justin Sonnenburg** — Professor at Stanford University; co-led the Cell study (with Chris Gardner and others) showing that 2–4 servings/day of fermented foods improves gut microbiome diversity more effectively than high-fiber diets. ([Stanford profile](https://sonnenburglab.stanford.edu))

**Chris Gardner** — Professor of medicine at Stanford University; co-investigator on the fermented foods vs. high-fiber diet study published in Cell. ([Stanford profile](https://med.stanford.edu/profiles/christopher-gardner))

**Alia Crum** — Psychologist at Stanford University; her research on belief and mindset effects showed that participants' physiological responses (insulin release, ghrelin, satiety) to a milkshake were significantly altered by what they were told the shake contained — demonstrating a top-down "belief effect" on metabolism. ([Stanford profile](https://psychology.stanford.edu/people/alia-crum))
