# Episode 188: How to Optimize Cognitive Function & Brain Health | Dr. Mark D'Esposito

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Mark D'Esposito — neurologist and professor of neuroscience and psychology at UC Berkeley — joins Andrew Huberman to unpack the brain mechanisms underlying cognition, executive function, and memory. The conversation covers the prefrontal cortex's role as the brain's CEO, the inverted-U relationship between dopamine and working memory, pharmacological and behavioral tools for optimizing cognitive performance, and how to restore function after concussion, TBI, Alzheimer's, and Parkinson's disease. Practical tools — from physical exercise and mindfulness to guanfacine and BrainHQ training — are grounded in clinical research and actionable for everyday use.

## Guest: Dr. Mark D'Esposito

Dr. Mark D'Esposito is a neurologist, professor of neuroscience and psychology at the University of California, Berkeley, and director of the Henry H. Wheeler Jr. Brain Imaging Center. He is a world expert in the brain mechanisms of working memory and executive function, and runs a laboratory studying cognitive neuroscience with applications to brain injury, aging, and disease. He also leads The BrainHealth Project, developing objective biomarkers of brain health.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The prefrontal cortex is the brain's CEO.** It provides top-down control over all other brain regions, enabling goal-directed behavior, impulse control, context-appropriate rule-following, and cognitive flexibility. Damage or stress to this region degrades virtually every aspect of daily functioning. The prefrontal cortex connects to every cortical and subcortical region — its anatomical centrality is why disrupting it (sleep deprivation, stress, injury) has such outsized effects. [00:06:23](transcript.md#000623-brain--frontal-lobes-prefrontal-cortex-executive-function)

2. **Frontal lobe development completes around age 25.** The prefrontal cortex is the last brain region to fully mature. This slow development is adaptive — it allows greater flexibility and exploration — but explains why adolescent decision-making is often impulsive. Insurance companies understood this before neuroscientists did. The frontal lobe has ~25 distinct sub-regions: the lateral prefrontal cortex supports executive function; the orbitofrontal cortex governs social and emotional behavior. [00:10:31](transcript.md#001031-frontal-lobe-development-children)

3. **Working memory is the active manipulation of information held in mind.** It is not passive storage — it involves holding, organizing, and operating on information in real time. It is fundamental to planning, problem-solving, and goal achievement, and it depends critically on dopaminergic signaling in the prefrontal cortex. D'Esposito calls it "our superpower while awake" — it translates knowledge into action. The mechanism is persistent neural activity in frontal lobe neurons that keeps representations active in posterior brain regions. [00:44:37](transcript.md#004437-working-memory-dopamine)

4. **Dopamine follows an inverted-U curve for working memory.** Too little dopamine impairs working memory; too much also impairs it. Optimal performance exists at an intermediate level. This means interventions that raise dopamine help low-baseline individuals but can hurt high-baseline individuals. Individual genetic variation (COMT enzyme) predicts where someone sits on this curve — roughly 25% of people have an overactive COMT (lower dopamine tone) and 25% have an underactive COMT (higher dopamine tone). A behavioral proxy: working memory digit span — recalling 7+ items correlates with higher baseline dopamine; recalling only 5 suggests lower baseline. [00:54:22](transcript.md#005422-dopamine-levels--working-memory-cognitive-tasks-genetics)

5. **Bromocriptine (a D2 dopamine agonist) can improve working memory at low doses.** At the doses studied (under 2.5 mg), subjects feel nothing — no subjective high — but working memory performance measurably improves. It is short-acting (4–6 hours, onset ~90 min) and well-tolerated. It was used by Olympic sprinters for reaction-time advantage (now a banned substance). The key insight: it only helps those who are low on the inverted-U curve — giving it to high-dopamine individuals worsens performance. COMT inhibitors (used in Parkinson's) achieve a similar effect. [01:00:03](transcript.md#010003-bromocriptine--working-memory-dopamine)

6. **Guanfacine (alpha-2A adrenoceptor agonist) boosts working memory via norepinephrine.** Originally a blood pressure medication, guanfacine has gained traction for cognitive deficits including Long COVID brain fog (a clinical trial tested guanfacine + N-acetylcysteine). It works through the norepinephrine system and is FDA-approved. Pupil dilation at a fixed luminance level serves as a rough biomarker for norepinephrine/locus coeruleus tone — larger pupils in bright light indicate higher norepinephrine. D'Esposito prefers guanfacine and bromocriptine over Adderall/Ritalin because they modulate specific transmitter systems rather than flooding the brain with all catecholamines at once. [01:06:21](transcript.md#010621-guanfacine-neurotransmitter-levels-pupil-dilation--biomarker-tests)

7. **Concussion and TBI are far more prevalent than recognized — and often undertreated.** TBI occurs not just in football but in car accidents, falls, domestic violence, and military service. The mechanism is axonal shearing — violent head movement tears axons, most commonly in the frontal lobes. Even mild concussion without loss of consciousness can produce persistent post-concussion syndrome. D'Esposito compares the current clinical "just work through it" attitude to telling someone with a torn ACL to jog it off. A 1% drop in cognitive performance can significantly derail daily function. [01:19:27](transcript.md#011927-concussion-traumatic-brain-injury-tbi)

8. **Poor sleep significantly degrades prefrontal cortical function.** Even partial sleep deprivation produces measurable drops in executive function comparable to the difference between an elite and mediocre athlete. Virtually every neurological condition also disrupts sleep — concussion patients almost universally report sleep problems, compounding their deficits. Sleep is not just protective; for TBI patients it is a primary rehabilitation tool. [01:25:22](transcript.md#012522-sleep-tbi-concussion--executive-function-brainhq)

9. **Estrogen boosts dopamine in the prefrontal cortex.** The frontal lobes contain more estrogen receptors than any other brain region, in both men and women. Higher estrogen correlates with higher dopamine levels and better working memory. Emily Jacobs (then in D'Esposito's lab) genotyped Berkeley undergraduates for COMT status and scanned them at peak vs. trough estrogen during the menstrual cycle — showing frontal lobe function fluctuates with the estrogen/dopamine interaction. The implication extends to post-menopausal women and to men: low estrogen in men impairs cognition and vascular health. [01:58:37](transcript.md#015837-estrogen--dopamine-cognition-tool-physical-exercise)

10. **Physical exercise is one of the most powerful tools for brain health.** Art Kramer's group at the University of Illinois showed aerobic exercise can be just as effective as structured cognitive therapy in improving executive function. Neurologists now routinely discuss exercise, sleep, and nutrition with patients — a paradigm shift from 30 years ago. Even elderly patients can access aerobic benefits via recumbent bicycles. [01:58:37](transcript.md#015837-estrogen--dopamine-cognition-tool-physical-exercise)

11. **Mindfulness meditation enhances executive function by training focused attention.** Adding mindfulness to goal management training produces better outcomes than either alone. Any form of mindfulness that involves noticing distraction and redirecting attention is effectively a prefrontal exercise. All forms of this type of mindfulness appear similarly effective — research has not yet determined whether specific styles produce different outcomes. [02:04:43](transcript.md#020443-tool-mindfulness-meditation--executive-function)

12. **Brain modularity — networks operating somewhat independently — predicts cognitive performance and treatment response.** Individuals with more modular resting-state brain networks perform better on cognitive tasks and respond better to goal management training. In a study of 12 TBI patients, baseline modularity predicted who would benefit from training. Sepideh Sadaghiani's work showed moment-to-moment modularity predicts perceptual accuracy in real time. The Neuroscouting company is working on extracting modularity metrics from wearable physiological data (heart rate variability, blood oxygen). [02:17:08](transcript.md#021708-modularity-brain-indices)

## Tools & Protocols

### Aerobic Exercise

- **What:** Regular moderate-to-vigorous aerobic exercise.
- **How:** Approximately 150 minutes per week (running, cycling, swimming, brisk walking, recumbent bicycle for elderly). Intensity sufficient to elevate heart rate into a moderate zone.
- **When:** Consistently throughout the week as a lifelong habit.
- **Ref:** [01:58:37](transcript.md#015837-estrogen--dopamine-cognition-tool-physical-exercise) — Art Kramer's group found aerobic exercise as effective as structured cognitive therapy for improving executive function.

---

### Mindfulness Meditation

- **What:** A practice of sustained focus on a simple object (breath, body sensation) with deliberate redirection whenever attention wanders.
- **How:** Any established form. Sit comfortably, close eyes, focus on breath. When the mind wanders, notice it and gently return focus. Even 10–20 minutes daily provides benefit. Adding it to goal management training amplifies cognitive outcomes.
- **When:** Daily practice. Can be combined with structured cognitive training programs.
- **Ref:** [02:04:43](transcript.md#020443-tool-mindfulness-meditation--executive-function) — Described as a "focus exercise" that directly trains prefrontal attentional control, the core mechanism of executive function.

---

### Goal Management Training (Structured Cognitive Rehabilitation)

- **What:** A structured behavioral program teaching metacognitive skills: stopping, identifying the goal, organizing sub-goals, executing, and checking progress. Developed by Brian Levine (Rotman Research Institute, Toronto). Evidence-based for frontal lobe injury, aging, and healthy adults.
- **How:** Therapist-directed, project-based training. Participants develop real-world projects (planning a meal, a vacation, a podcast) and work through goal hierarchy, focus maintenance, sub-goal decomposition, distraction filtering, and progress monitoring.
- **When:** Applicable during complex cognitive tasks — work, learning, project planning. Especially useful post-TBI or concussion.
- **Ref:** [01:39:26](transcript.md#013926-tools-brain-health--boosting-executive-function-books)

---

### BrainHQ (Cognitive Training Software)

- **What:** Computerized cognitive training platform developed by Michael Merzenich's group at UCSF (Posit Science). Exercises are adaptive and target specific cognitive circuits including working memory, processing speed, and attention.
- **How:** Available at https://bit.ly/3I6y5d6. Consistent use (multiple sessions per week) needed for benefit.
- **When:** As a standalone brain health maintenance practice or as part of rehabilitation after TBI/concussion or during aging-related cognitive decline. D'Esposito recommends it over generic brain training games because the science is rigorous.
- **Ref:** [01:25:22](transcript.md#012522-sleep-tbi-concussion--executive-function-brainhq)

---

### Sleep Optimization

- **What:** Prioritizing 7–9 hours of quality sleep per night as a non-negotiable foundation for prefrontal cortical function.
- **How:** Standard sleep hygiene: consistent wake time, dark/cool room, avoiding caffeine late in the day, limiting light exposure in the evening. For TBI/concussion patients, sleep is especially critical.
- **When:** Nightly. Particularly critical during cognitive rehabilitation — poor sleep dramatically slows TBI recovery and compounds cognitive deficits.
- **Ref:** [01:25:22](transcript.md#012522-sleep-tbi-concussion--executive-function-brainhq) — D'Esposito compares the executive function drop from a poor night's sleep to the performance difference between an elite and mediocre athlete.

---

### Bromocriptine (Pharmacologic — Clinical Context)

- **What:** A dopamine D2/D3 receptor agonist that improves working memory in individuals with low baseline dopamine tone. Short-acting (~4–6 hours, onset ~90 min).
- **How:** Used at very low doses in research and clinical settings (typically 1.25–2.5 mg). Subjects cannot distinguish it from placebo subjectively. Requires physician supervision. Not for general self-administration.
- **When:** Investigated for TBI cognitive rehabilitation, Parkinson's disease, and low-dopamine-state cognitive impairment. Was used by Olympic sprinters for reaction-time advantage (now a banned substance).
- **Ref:** [01:00:03](transcript.md#010003-bromocriptine--working-memory-dopamine) and [01:12:46](transcript.md#011246-bromocriptine-olympics-pharmacology--cognitive-function-adderall)

---

### Guanfacine (Pharmacologic — Clinical Context)

- **What:** An alpha-2A adrenoceptor agonist that boosts norepinephrine signaling in the prefrontal cortex, improving working memory and attention. FDA-approved (originally as antihypertensive; also approved for ADHD as Intuniv).
- **How:** Prescription medication. Used at doses of 1–4 mg. Currently studied for Long COVID brain fog (combined with N-acetylcysteine). Requires physician supervision.
- **When:** Relevant for ADHD, TBI cognitive deficits, Long COVID, and potentially age-related prefrontal decline. D'Esposito prefers it over Adderall/Ritalin for its transmitter specificity.
- **Ref:** [01:06:21](transcript.md#010621-guanfacine-neurotransmitter-levels-pupil-dilation--biomarker-tests)

---

### Reading Fiction and Novel Learning

- **What:** Engaging regularly with fiction, history, or non-professional reading to exercise working memory circuits through narrative tracking, character development, and sustained attention.
- **How:** Deliberate reading of books — D'Esposito resumed fiction and history reading after 20 years away. Any sustained reading that requires holding a plot in mind and resisting distraction qualifies.
- **When:** As part of a regular cognitive health practice, analogous to cardiovascular exercise for the brain.
- **Ref:** [01:39:26](transcript.md#013926-tools-brain-health--boosting-executive-function-books)

---

### Cognitively Demanding Activities (Chess, Improv, Cooking)

- **What:** Activities that require holding multiple rules, goals, and sub-goals simultaneously in working memory while filtering distractions.
- **How:** Chess, improv theater, cooking complex meals — any activity with a goal hierarchy, novel rule application, and real-time adaptation. Improv forces context tracking and novel rule application on the fly.
- **When:** As ongoing cognitive maintenance, especially outside of professional work.
- **Ref:** [01:39:26](transcript.md#013926-tools-brain-health--boosting-executive-function-books)

## Additional Topics Covered

### Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's is a multifactorial neurodegenerative disorder. Plaques and tangles remain accepted pathology — a fraudulent dataset controversy did not invalidate the overall amyloid hypothesis. The only approved pharmacological treatment is acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil) that boost acetylcholine — they provide some symptomatic benefit but do not slow disease progression. D'Esposito argues a cocktail of neuromodulators (dopamine + acetylcholine + norepinephrine) has never been adequately trialed. Socialization and engaging with family photos/films improved patient behavior better than drugs in some studies. Music accesses preserved emotional memories in Alzheimer's patients. [01:47:26](transcript.md#014726-alzheimers-disease-genetics-pharmacology)

### Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's results from degeneration of dopaminergic neurons. L-DOPA combined with carbidopa (which prevents peripheral breakdown) is the primary treatment — carbidopa was key because early L-DOPA doses caused side effects before brain delivery was optimized. Bromocriptine is also used. Mucuna pruriens (velvet bean) supplements contain ~99% L-DOPA but peripheral metabolism limits brain delivery — Huberman tried it and found it unpleasant and short-lived. [01:51:48](transcript.md#015148-parkinsons-disease-l-dopa-coping-with-alzheimers-nicotine)

### Nicotine and Neurodegeneration

An unnamed Nobel Prize-winning neuroscientist told Huberman he chews Nicorette daily based on the claim that nicotine is protective against Parkinson's and Alzheimer's. D'Esposito notes nicotine activates the cholinergic system, and early Alzheimer's studies explored nicotine for this reason — the cholinergic basis is plausible. He does not confirm the neuroprotective claim as established. [01:51:48](transcript.md#015148-parkinsons-disease-l-dopa-coping-with-alzheimers-nicotine)

### Psilocybin and Brain Connectivity

Huberman raises the apparent contradiction: psilocybin studies show increased resting-state connectivity (less modularity), yet modularity predicts better cognition. D'Esposito resolves this by distinguishing brain state modularity (which normally decreases during demanding tasks — that is adaptive) from brain trait modularity (baseline resting state). Psilocybin's connectivity effects are likely a state shift, not a change in trait baseline, so the comparison is not direct. [02:22:53](transcript.md#022253-psilocybin-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation)

### Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

TMS is FDA-approved for depression and D'Esposito refers patients for that use. Small studies show TMS to prefrontal cortex improves working memory, but large definitive trials for cognitive enhancement have not been done. He expects TMS will eventually become part of a multi-modal treatment approach alongside drugs and behavioral training. [02:22:53](transcript.md#022253-psilocybin-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation)

### Smartphones and the Frontal Lobe

The rule-sets learned through smartphone use (scroll, react, rapid context-switch) do not generalize to other domains — D'Esposito finds his phone useless in the ER for diagnostic reasoning. Over-reliance on GPS may be eroding spatial problem-solving skills. By contrast, activities with coherent rule sets and goals (soccer, reading a novel, chess, improv theater) train prefrontal circuits in ways that generalize. [00:37:26](transcript.md#003726-smartphones-social-media)
