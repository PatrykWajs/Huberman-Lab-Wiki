## [00:06:38] Categories of Longevity Approaches

**Peter Attia:** For people that want to live as long as possible, I think there are at least four categories of approaches broadly speaking. The first I'll just call the "dos and don'ts" — you want to move appropriately and often enough, get enough Zone 2 cardio, do your resistance training, keep the nerve-to-muscle connection strong, avoid the sorts of things that would lead to falling and being immobile, eat right, and know your genetics and make good decisions on the basis of them. The second category covers calories, glucose, insulin — things that funnel into mTOR, mammalian target of rapamycin — a molecule robustly expressed during development and essentially all cells of the body, and then across the lifespan, tapers off. This second category includes caloric restriction or taking drugs such as rapamycin, in an effort to reduce mTOR activity and essentially slow cellular growth. The third category involves molecules that target specific cellular pathways deemed potentially interesting for longevity — the NAD pathway being the major example we'll discuss today, including NR, NMN, and NAD itself along with the enzymatic steps in between. A fourth category is the kitchen-sink approach to longevity — doing everything even the most esoteric of things — exemplified by individuals like Bryan Johnson who takes very high doses of polyphenols, limits caloric intake to just the early part of the day, and tries everything from red light to PRP.

**Andrew Huberman:** I would say from a framework standpoint, the behavioral fundamentals — sleep, nutrition, exercise — are category one. Then there are exogenous molecules that target diseases specifically, such as Metformin, an SGLT2 inhibitor, GLP-1 agonists, PCSK9 inhibitors, or statins — these directly delay the onset of cardiovascular disease, cancer, neurodegenerative disease, and metabolic disease. Then there's a third category of geroprotective molecules that target hallmarks and pathways of aging — things like increased inflammation, greater abundance of senescent cells, reduced nutrient sensing capacity of mTOR. Rapamycin is the single best example: it targets a very specific hallmark of aging. There are only two interventions that have ever extended life across species — caloric restriction and rapamycin.

## [00:25:01] NAD Pathway: Energy & DNA Repair; Knock-Out & Knock-In, Klotho

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about NAD. It's in essentially every cell of the body. It's generally thought to be associated with energy production and mitochondrial pathways in every single cell.

**Peter Attia:** NAD is one of the most ubiquitous molecules in the body. Most of what it does — somewhere between 500 and 600 pathways utilize NAD as a cofactor, meaning it's not consumed in a chemical reaction but rather serves as an electron shuttle. NAD and NADH basically play catch with electrons, and that's 99% of what NAD is doing in the body. For that reason, NAD is so tightly regulated in the body — just as glucose is tightly regulated, just as pH is tightly regulated. We cannot survive outside of a very narrow band of pH. Similarly, NAD is managed across all ages and all physiologic conditions in a super-tight band. There's another place where NAD shows up: as a substrate, consumed by sirtuins in the process of DNA repair. This is really where the story picks up — the sirtuins consume NAD as an actual substrate. This is one of the major areas where the story began, when the sirtuins were mutated in one direction or the other — gain of function or loss of function. I recently did a podcast with Dina Dubal from UCSF on klotho, which is an amazing scientific story: a researcher in Japan was interested in understanding hypertension, knocked out a certain gene, and the mouse died very quickly with devastating neurodegenerative disease. When they overexpressed that same gene, the animal lived 15–20% longer — both necessary and sufficient for extended life. That's what you can say is a longevity gene.

## [00:32:25] Yeast, Sirtuins, Caloric Restriction & Lifespan

**Peter Attia:** The sirtuin story goes back to the late 1990s. Matt Kaeberlein was a postdoc when he did an experiment in a strain of yeast — the W303 strain — and overexpressed sir2, and lo and behold the yeast lived longer. A year later, someone else in the same lab took a different strain of yeast and calorically restricted them, and they also lived longer. An understandable hypothesis emerged: caloric restriction — which had been known to be life-extending — is working through sirtuins. But that hypothesis sort of fell apart about four years later when Matt Kaeberlein, this time with Brian Kennedy, did another experiment in yet a third strain of yeast. When you took the W303 strain and you calorically restricted them, no change. Even more odd: when you took the other strain and overexpressed sir2, no change. The story didn't make sense. When Brian and Matt published in 2004, in yet a different strain, if you calorically restricted them they lived longer, if you overexpressed sir2 they lived longer, if you did both they lived even longer — it was additive, suggesting caloric restriction and sir2 overexpression independently and separately extended lifespan. These are parallel pathways. My reading of the literature — and every person I have talked to who works in this space, including Matt Kaeberlein — is that there is no evidence that caloric restriction and sirtuins operate through the same pathway.

**Andrew Huberman:** So caloric restriction extends life across model systems. What about in humans?

**Peter Attia:** That experiment has never been done and never will be done. The joke is: nobody wants to be in the treatment group either, because it requires eating so little. Severe caloric restriction will absolutely reduce the risk of most chronic diseases — there's very good reason to believe that if an individual constitutively consumed 25% fewer calories than they were meant to eat, their risk of cardiovascular disease, cancer, and Alzheimer's would go down. The problem is what goes up — what does that do to your immune system, to sarcopenia, to your risk of falling and frailty? You trade one set of diseases for another. It's not at all clear that lifespan goes up. And when you even look at animal literature using different strains of mice that are not inbred and don't put them in hermetically sealed situations, they don't live longer. So caloric restriction probably reduces the onset of chronic disease but may not translate to an all-cause mortality benefit.

## [00:38:56] Sirtuins, Transgenic Mice, Gender & Lifespan

**Peter Attia:** There is at least one transgenic mouse experiment that demonstrated a survival advantage — a 2012 study that overexpressed SIRT6, and indeed, for the male mice, lifespan increased by 10–15%. The female mice did not experience a difference. That's not uncommon or unheard of in longevity research — there generally are sex-specific differences and you always have to read the fine print. The first thing I always look at in a study when I see a difference in sexes is how long did the controls live. I went back and actually looked at the Kaplan-Meier curves on that exact study, and yes, I think that's a real effect. So let's take stock of two pieces of information that I think we could say is probably true: it is probably true that in a handful of strains of yeast, if you overexpress sirtuins the yeast will live longer, and that's completely independent of caloric restriction. And there is at least one transgenic strain of mice where, if you overexpress SIRT6, you will at least make the male mice live 10% longer.

## [00:43:42] DNA Repair, Sirtuins, Cancer; Resveratrol

**Peter Attia:** When sirtuins are activated, we believe they improve mitochondrial biogenesis, increase DNA repair — that's probably the biggest one and is what brings us to the NAD story — and also reduce SASPs, the soluble products of senescent cells. So sirtuins tamp down on senescent cells, increase mitochondrial biogenesis, and increase DNA repair. This business of DNA repair and reducing DNA fragmentation or mutations has been a hot idea in the field of aging for a long time. As we age, we are less and less able to repair DNA, and one of the arguments is that we don't have enough of the substrate that sirtuins need to repair DNA — and that substrate is NAD. If NAD levels are declining with age and sirtuins need NAD to repair DNA, the logical conclusion is: should we give more NAD? The most famous sirtuin activator of all time is resveratrol, a chemical found in trace amounts in the skin of grapes and therefore wine. About 20 years ago, one lab doing one experiment was able to convince some people — including a very large pharma company — that resveratrol increased lifespan. The thesis was: resveratrol activates sirtuins, sirtuin activation improves mitochondrial biogenesis, suppresses senescent cells, and enhances DNA repair. One experiment worked — but it was a bizarre mouse model force-fed an enormously high-fat diet where the liver fat encroached the thoracic cavity, and the mouse died prematurely because it couldn't breathe. Resveratrol rescued the mice from that. When the Interventions Testing Program — the ITP — tested resveratrol, they did it in combination with the people who found the original result, asking what dose to give, following those recommendations exactly, and found nothing. Resveratrol categorically failed the ITP.

## [00:54:18] NAD & NADH, Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS), Mitochondrial Health

**Peter Attia:** So the NAD story is: NAD levels are declining with age in most tissues — most prevalent in skin, where you see about a 60% reduction over a lifetime. In blood, humans show about a 10–20% reduction over four decades. But a 2015 study in PNAS that looked at NAD levels in whole blood over time found that NAD levels were going down about 10–20% over four decades, but NADH levels were going up by the same amount — suggesting the total amount of NAD and NADH is the same. What's declining as we age should be thought of less as a reduction in NAD and more as a reduction in what's called redox potential — the ability to shuttle electrons. Why are you and I sitting here? We ate something that contained chemical energy — fats and carbohydrates with carbon-carbon bonds and carbon-hydrogen bonds. We break those down, shuttle them into the mitochondria, and via the electron transport chain — those four complexes building up an electron gradient — we transfer electrons between NAD and NADH, ultimately making ATP. The finished product is water and carbon dioxide. What NAD and NADH are doing is playing an absolutely essential-to-life role in facilitating the transfer of chemical energy to electrical energy back to chemical energy. So when people say NAD levels decline with aging — the answer is yes, but what's really declining is redox potential. Our mitochondria are not as good as we age. Many so-called anti-aging approaches target reactive oxygen species (ROS), which impede mitochondrial function. The work of Glenn Jeffrey at University College London is at least intriguing — he has published studies showing that exposing the aged eye (40 and older) to red light and near-infrared light for a couple of minutes a few times a week can spare certain processes involved in vision and photoreceptors. The idea — not yet proven — is that it's reducing reactive oxygen species and thereby improving mitochondrial function in perhaps the most metabolically active cell type in the entire body: photoreceptors.

## [01:02:17] NAD vs NR vs NMN Supplementation; IV & Oral Routes

**Andrew Huberman:** If we are to back up from NAD and look at the pathway leading to it — NR, NMN, and NAD — and the competition in the market is around either infusing or in some cases ingesting NAD directly, taking NMN which is the precursor to NAD orally, or taking NR which is the precursor to NMN.

**Peter Attia:** My personal view is there's not really much of a difference in what you do. At the end of the day, all of these things are generally going to increase NAD levels in the blood. I've taken NR in capsule form and NMN typically in powdered form placed sublingually under the tongue. I have done intravenous NAD — probably five or six times. For the first 10 minutes of the infusion you feel like somebody is stepping on your chest with a boot, your legs cramp up, you feel nauseous. The discomfort of the experience shouldn't be used as evidence that it's doing something good physiologically. You can slow the infusion — typically anywhere from 3 hours down to as brief as 30 minutes for 500 milligrams. After the drip is done you feel better than you did prior. But when I compare that to how I feel after a saline IV — I also feel pretty good. It's hard to disentangle those things, especially when they put other things in the bag like glutathione and vitamin C. The typical doses tested in mice are on the order of 500 to 1,000 milligrams per kilogram — so if you weigh 100 kilograms, you're talking 50,000 to 100,000 milligrams. We're not approaching that at all with human supplementation.

## [01:11:33] NR vs. NMN, Doses, Side Effects; Interventions Testing Program

**Peter Attia:** It's clear to me based on my read of the data that NR can cross the cell membrane directly — there's no obstacle to NR getting into cells. NMN cannot because of that extra phosphate group. So if you take NMN sublingually or ingest it orally, it goes into the gut, the phosphate group is cleaved, and becomes NR before entering cells. Because of that, the argument is that if one were to compare NR versus NMN, there are more data to support NR as a more effective precursor to NAD than orally ingested NMN. Some people say they'll just take more NMN, and this gets into the realm of cost-effectiveness — at the dosages people recommend, NR at 300–600 mg is quite expensive; NMN at 1500 mg is also quite expensive. The ITP — the Interventions Testing Program — is an NIH-funded program running out of three independent labs doing experiments in triplicate. The ITP has tested north of 50 molecules, and very few have extended lifespan. The notable failure is NR. NR was tested at a very robust dose, either 500 or 1,000 milligrams per kilogram, and there was no extension of life, no improvement in healthspan, no change at all. Contrast this with rapamycin — when they gave rapamycin to 21-month-old mice (equivalent to a 60-year-old mouse), it still worked. Canagliflozin also worked, as did acarbose — both without requiring weight loss, pointing to the importance of tight glycemic control independent of weight. 17-alpha estradiol also worked, but only in male mice.

## [01:17:43] Fatty Liver Disease & NR; NMN & Glucose; Clinical Significance

**Peter Attia:** One study that people point to for NR benefits was a three-arm randomized study in humans with fatty liver disease — roughly 30 people per arm — where the primary outcome was a reduction in hepatic fat via MRI. The product tested was Basis (NR plus pterostilbene). At the end, there was no difference at high dose or low dose in hepatic fat, body weight, inflammatory markers, or glycemic markers. There was one subanalysis finding: limiting the analysis to people who had a hepatic fat score below 27%, the single-dose group went from 20% to 15% hepatic fat — which was statistically significant but not clinically significant, since they're still 3x above the threshold for fatty liver disease. The second study that people point to was a 2021–2022 study from Washington University looking at NMN and glucose disposal. They found a statistically significant increase in glucose disposal with insulin infusion in the NMN group, but it was quite small — clinically very insignificant. Never confuse statistical significance with clinical significance. If I gave you a drug that lowered your blood pressure from 160/100 to 157/97, that could be statistically significant if the variance is small enough, but it has no clinical significance. These two studies are what people cite to say there was some benefit, but when you read the fine print, neither supports a strong conclusion.

## [01:25:17] Safety & FDA, NMN & NR Supplementation; Skin Cancer Benefits

**Peter Attia:** The FDA has essentially said that NMN should not be sold as a supplement, but it is still being sold as a supplement — a little bit of ignoring of the FDA's request. NR, as far as I know, is authorized for sale as a supplement; it has an FDA designation of GRAS — generally recognized as safe — meaning anybody can sell it, with no FDA oversight. The one benefit I could find that looks genuine for NR/NMN is a study on skin cancer rates — somewhere between a 60 and 80% reduction in basal cell and squamous cell carcinomas, with no difference in melanomas. Basal and squamous cell carcinomas are very common and are very clearly associated with sun exposure. If indeed there is something that can reduce the risk by 60–80%, that may be a rationale for taking it — especially for individuals with high sun exposure or those at risk due to skin type or line of work. Skin also shows the greatest decline in NAD across the body — about a 60% lifetime reduction — which could explain why this might be the tissue where you see the strongest signal from NR/NMN supplementation. Keratinocytes in skin turn over every 28 days, so there's a constantly renewable population that would have fresh expression of sirtuins and NAD. I would like to see this skin cancer experiment repeated because if the result holds, there's a real case to be made for high-sun-exposure individuals.

## [01:41:00] Rapamycin & Immune Function

**Peter Attia:** Rapamycin historically is thought of as an immune suppressant — that's the context in which it was approved for patients undergoing organ transplantation. But Joan Mannick and Lloyd Klickstein published a paper in 2014 using everolimus (an mTOR inhibitor) where they took a group of 65-year-olds and randomized them to either placebo or different doses and dosing schedules of everolimus, and found enhanced immunity in response to an influenza vaccine. That was the turning point for me — when rapamycin went from something interesting based on the first ITP results in 2009 to "maybe we should be taking this" starting in 2014. If you take rapamycin daily it seems to suppress your immune system, but if you pulse it once a week it seems to improve immune function. That means it's an immune modulator that can go up or down depending on dosing. The commercial interest now is in rapalogs — analogs of rapamycin being investigated for new indications, such as improving immune function specifically.

## [01:55:24] Radiation & Cancer Risk

**Andrew Huberman:** What about normal radiation levels and cancer risk? Is there any meaningful cancer risk from the low-level radiation we encounter in daily life — from flights, from background radiation?

**Peter Attia:** I don't think so. The dose makes the poison. The linear no-threshold model of radiation risk has been controversial in the radiation biology community for a long time. Background radiation varies enormously depending on where you live — people living at high altitude in Denver are exposed to meaningfully more radiation than people at sea level. If background radiation were meaningfully carcinogenic at those levels, you would expect to see higher cancer rates in Denver versus Miami, and you don't — if anything, the opposite tends to be true, which may be due to confounding factors like sun exposure. The risks of flying are from the radiation? I fly a lot. I don't lose sleep over the radiation exposure from flights. There are so many other things to be concerned about from a health perspective.

## [02:26:52] NAD Pathway Supplementation for Longevity?

**Peter Attia:** So where does that leave us on NAD pathway supplementation for longevity? There are multiple leaps of faith required: first that sirtuins matter (likely true in yeast, uncertain in mammals), second that caloric restriction and sirtuins operate through the same pathway (probably not true), third that this matters in humans (no evidence), fourth that sirtuin activators like resveratrol work (ITP says no), and fifth that increasing NAD levels will produce a positive benefit (no lifespan evidence). For longevity — lifespan extension — the answer appears to be no, based on available evidence. For healthspan — quality of life improvements — the skin cancer finding is genuinely interesting and warrants replication. Taking NR or NMN at a reasonable dose (300–600 mg of NR or 500–1,500 mg of NMN) is probably safe, is probably not harmful, and might provide some health-span benefits, particularly for skin. But anyone taking it thinking they're going to live longer is taking multiple leaps of faith with no solid evidence to back them up. Rapamycin remains the only pharmacological intervention — aside from caloric restriction — that has robustly extended lifespan across multiple species, and the evidence base for its geroprotective effect is meaningfully stronger than anything in the NAD pathway.
