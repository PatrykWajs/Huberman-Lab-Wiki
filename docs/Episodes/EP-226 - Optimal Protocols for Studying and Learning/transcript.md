## [00:06:45] Offsetting Forgetting

Let's talk about how best to study and learn. People have different preferred learning styles — some prefer reading, some group study, some highlighting, and people commonly identify as auditory or visual learners — but when one looks at the research on preferred learning styles, pretty much all of that dissolves. It turns out that the best way to study and learn is defined not by the medium through which material arrives, but by whether that approach accesses components of your memory systems that offset forgetting.

The theme to keep in mind throughout today's episode is this: rather than thinking about studying to learn and retain information, think about studying to offset the natural process of forgetting that everybody experiences when exposed to new material of any kind — cognitive, motor, musical, mathematical. You are trying to inoculate against forgetting. That is the way to remember things, that is the way to gain mastery over them, and the data from the peer-reviewed literature will teach you how to do this best.

## [00:08:22] Learning & Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the incredible feature of the nervous system — which includes the brain and spinal cord — that allows it to change in response to experience. Any form of learning involves neuroplasticity. At the level of cells called neurons or nerve cells, neuroplasticity involves three different mechanisms: the strengthening of synaptic connections between neurons, the weakening of connections between neurons, and — rarely in the adult human nervous system — the addition of new neurons called neurogenesis. Neurogenesis grabs enormous media attention but is responsible for a near-trivial amount of the neuroplasticity relevant to most learning discussions.

The major mechanism by which learning and memory occurs in humans is the strengthening and weakening of existing connections, and the formation of new connections between already-existing neurons. Importantly, the weakening of connections is not purely negative — much of the neuroplasticity that underlies the acquisition of a new motor skill is actually a reflection of removing connections. A baby's improving motor coordination from infancy through adolescence is largely the reflection of removing neural connections so that the ones that remain become much more robust and reliable.

## [00:16:09] Focus & Alertness, Sleep, Tool: Active Engagement

Neuroplasticity and learning — that is, converting your studying efforts into retention of knowledge — is a two-step process. Step one is active engagement: you must be alert and focused to pay attention to the information you are trying to learn. It is the process of being focused and attending that cues your nervous system — via neurochemicals in the brain and body — to tell the neurons that they are going to have to change their connections in order to retain and use that information at a future time.

Sleep is without question the best nootropic available. Your ability to be alert and focused is going to be dramatically greater if you slept well the night before. Here is what everyone should do in order to learn better: get a great night's sleep, limit excessive external stress, stay well hydrated, and use the appropriate amount of caffeine for you. Don't be a passive participant in learning — instead, voluntarily ramp up your level of focus and alertness by telling yourself that the information is important, and learn to engage your attention and focus deliberately and volitionally. That strain you feel when forcing yourself to attend and focus is actually good: it reflects the release of neuromodulators like epinephrine and adrenaline, which serve to cue neural circuits that they need to change at a later time.

## [00:21:37] Tool: Improve Focus, Mindfulness Meditation, Perception Exercise

The scientific data support the fact that doing a brief five-to-ten-minute mindfulness meditation each day significantly improves focus and memory recall ability. Data from Wendy Suzuki's laboratory at New York University show that people who do a ten-minute meditation per day — simply sitting or lying down, closing their eyes, focusing on breathing, and bringing attention back each time it drifts — improve their level of focus and their memory and recall ability on a lasting basis.

If you prefer not to call it meditation, think of it as a perceptual focusing exercise. You can even do it with eyes open by focusing on a visual target at a comfortable distance — allowing yourself to blink as needed, but deliberately forcing your visual and cognitive attention back to that target over and over again for one to ten minutes. This deliberate practice of bringing your attention back to a particular location is known to create significant improvements in your ability to focus, which is critical for your ability to study and learn. The circuits for focus and attention themselves are subject to neuroplasticity.

## [00:24:38] Sleep & Neuroplasticity, Tool: Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR)

The actual changes in the nervous system — the strengthening and weakening of connections between neurons that underlie learning — do not occur during the focused exposure to material, but instead during deep sleep and sleep-like states. The actual reordering occurs most prominently during rapid eye movement sleep, which tends to predominate in the latter half of the night, so make sure you are getting enough sleep — for some people six hours, for others eight.

There is also something called the first-night effect: the information you learn on a given day is mostly consolidated during the night's sleep following that first learning session. This does not mean that a single poor night's sleep will cause permanent forgetting, but the first night after learning you want to get the best sleep possible — so studying late into the night while consuming lots of caffeine and pulling all-nighters actively works against your learning. Non-sleep deep rest (NSDR), sometimes referred to as yoga nidra, is a ten-to-twenty-minute practice you can do to restore mental and physical vigor and to enhance neuroplasticity. Where you place it in your schedule is less important than simply doing it regularly.

## [00:28:29] Tools: Study Habits of Successful Students

A really nice paper surveyed close to 700 medical students — approximately equal numbers of male and female — and analyzed the learning habits associated with the most successful performers. There are at least ten study habits used by highly effective students; focusing on the top five or six accounts for most of the effect. First, they set aside dedicated time to study — they literally schedule time. Second, they eliminate distractions: they put their phone away and off, tell friends and family they will be unreachable, and study alone. Third, they study for about three to four hours per day but break that into two or three sessions rather than one marathon block.

Fourth, the best performing students teach their peers — they learn the material in isolation and then bring it to other students in the same course to teach it. The "watch one, do one, teach one" principle is an excellent means to develop proficiency and even mastery. Fifth, scheduling those study times at consistent times each day — even across two or three days — allows your brain to entrain, just as with sleep-wake cycles, so that you become better at focusing and attending during those specific windows. Sixth, the best students have a long-term aspirational understanding — a broad abstract belief about how their success will impact their family and life arc — that pulls them forward through study efforts even when desire wanes or fatigue increases.

## [00:37:33] Studying & Aspiration Goals; Challenging Material

Before moving into specific ways to study in order to maximally offset forgetting, one important additional point from the study on highly effective medical students: the best-performing students had a very long-term, somewhat abstract aspirational understanding of how their success would impact their lives and families. This broad aspirational component is not something they were constantly dwelling on — it simply served as a north star that pulled them forward through difficult material.

A key theme running through all the excellent peer-reviewed research on how best to study is this: studying that feels challenging is the most effective. Everyone wants to hear about flow, about information sinking in by osmosis, but we have known for hundreds if not thousands of years that effort is the cornerstone of learning. The strain you feel in forcing yourself to learn and focus is precisely the signal that neuroplasticity needs to take place; if you already knew the material seamlessly, your nervous system would not need to change.

## [00:42:54] Tool: Testing as a Learning Tool

There is a classic study done in 1917 where grade-school children read biographies and were then divided into groups. One group read and reread those biographies over and over. Another group read the biographies once and then tested themselves — simply by thinking about what they could remember — an equal number of times instead of rereading. When measured on percent of accurate recall, the children who read the biography once and then deliberately thought about the information in their own minds — effectively testing themselves — vastly outperformed the children who reread the biographies repeatedly.

Reading and rereading material is far less effective than reading and then thinking about that material, testing yourself on it, forcing yourself to bring it to mind. This is not just for volume of material retained but also for accuracy of recall. More modern versions of this experiment compared groups following patterns of study-study-study-study (SSSS), study-study-study-test (SSSТ), and study-test-test-test (STTT), with a final delayed test for all groups. Performance on the final test was essentially proportional to the number of tests already taken — the more tests taken on the material, the better the retention.

## [00:48:23] Self-Testing, Repeated Testing

One of the most powerful aspects of testing is recognizing exactly what you know and do not know. The key is running up against the gaps — those moments where you say "I can't remember what comes next" or "I was certain that was the answer but I got it wrong." That error signal is what cues your nervous system to lock in the correct information. Even getting 40 or 50 percent or less correct on tests taken immediately after studying is still a better strategy than rereading the material.

What is even more surprising — and a little alarming — is the relationship between confidence and actual mastery. Students who study material four times think they are going to perform best on the ultimate exam, while students who study once and test themselves three times report much lower confidence. Yet the exact opposite is true in performance: repeated exposure creates the illusion of learning through familiarity, while repeated testing creates actual encoding. When you see or hear something over and over, you think you have learned it — but you have not, compared to people who are tested on the material, often straining to get the answers right and sometimes getting them completely wrong.

## [00:55:29] Testing Yourself & Knowledge Gaps

The approach to self-testing can be highly individual. For Andrew Huberman studying neuroanatomy in college, his process involved lying down on his bed, closing his eyes, and mentally flying through the nervous system from different entry points, reviewing structures and their locations. When he reached a point where he did not know what came next, he would open the textbook, look it up, and return to the mental exercise — which was effectively repeated self-testing.

To this day when reading a paper, a useful practice is to take a walk afterwards and think about the key components and most important graphs, trying to recall them from memory. The circuits for focus and attention themselves are subject to neuroplasticity, and this kind of retrieval practice directly exploits the neurobiological difference between familiarity — merely recognizing something — and recollection — being able to bring material to your focused attention and use it. Familiarity and mastery engage different brain areas and are fundamentally different cognitive states.

## [01:02:23] New Material & Self-Test Timing

A critical finding from multiple experiments concerns the timing of self-testing relative to first exposure. Three groups studied material and then were tested at a later point: Group 1 studied then tested very soon after (same day or next day), then had a long delay before the final test. Group 2 studied, had a delay, then tested, then had another delay before the final test. Group 3 studied, had a long delay, then tested very close to the final test.

Counterintuitively, Group 1 — who tested themselves very soon after first exposure — performed best on the final test. Group 3 — who crammed a test right before the final, received correct answers, and then tested again the next day — performed worst. The key message: test yourself on the material that you were just exposed to very soon after your first exposure, because that is what offsets the natural forgetting of new material. A single self-directed test taken immediately after learning reduces forgetting by approximately 50 percent — 5-0 — compared to not testing at all, and this effect has been demonstrated across musical, mathematical, language, and motor learning.

## [01:07:21] Familiarity vs Mastery

There seems to be something fundamentally different about first exposure to material versus testing yourself on that material. Neural imaging data in humans suggest this has to do with the distinction between familiarity — recognizing something — and recollection — actively bringing material to memory and using it. These two processes engage different brain areas. When you read something over and over, you think you are learning it — your confidence increases with each subsequent exposure — but actually you are building familiarity, not mastery.

Testing, by contrast, forces the recollection process, and it is in the struggle to retrieve and the error of getting things wrong that the nervous system is cued to lock in correct information and remember what was wrong so that it can be corrected. This is what familiarity — which mere rereading provides — fundamentally fails to do. A review of the relevant neuroscience is available in the journal Hippocampus, which specifically addresses the distinction between familiarity-based and recollection-based memory processes.

## [01:10:55] Self-Testing & Offsetting Forgetting

When you self-test material, you dramatically reduce the amount of forgetting that occurs compared to simply being exposed to the material. This is the single most important fact in today's discussion. The fact that one trial of self-testing reduces forgetting by about 50 percent — and that this effect persists even out to a year later — reframes how we should think about studying entirely. Most neuroscientists and educators have been taught the framework of: exposure, focus, then sleep-based consolidation. All of that is true, but we need to add that most information entering our nervous system each day is forgotten unless something is done to offset that forgetting.

The best types of tests for use as study tools are open-ended short-answer or long-answer questions, not multiple-choice questions. Multiple choice questions allow for familiarity and recognition — you scan the options looking for something that sounds right. Open-ended questions require you to actually recall the information with a much greater degree of mastery. The exception is multiple-choice tests that include tricks — where superficially obvious answers are incorrect and deeper mastery of the material is required to identify the true answer. Regardless of how you will ultimately be evaluated, your self-testing should be open-ended wherever possible.

## [01:15:53] Best Type of Self-Tests; Phone & Post-Learning Distractions

The literature on testing as a learning tool makes clear that testing should not be confused with evaluation. Testing as a studying tool — whether self-directed or given by a teacher — is for the purpose of revealing what you know and do not know, being shown the correct answers, and consolidating that information. It is not for the purpose of grading. Students commonly dislike pop quizzes, and teacher evaluation scores go down when pop quizzes are given — but this likely reflects students confusing testing-as-evaluation with testing-as-learning.

One critically underappreciated finding is the negative impact of phone use and other distractions immediately after learning. Even briefly checking a phone or social media after a learning session appears to interfere with the early consolidation process. The best practice after a learning session is to avoid distracting inputs and allow the brain's self-directed replay to proceed — which is part of why gap effects and NSDR after studying are so useful.

## [01:22:03] Tool: Gap Effects; Testing as Studying vs. Evaluation

Gap effects are periods during and after learning in which you do nothing — no phone, no other input — and allow your hippocampus to replay and consolidate what you just learned. Data show that during these brief silent pauses, the hippocampus replays the preceding experience at a rate twenty to thirty times faster than in real time, similar to what occurs during rapid eye movement sleep. Periodically pausing throughout a class, lecture, or practice session allows the brain to generate more internal repetitions of the material than it would if you simply barreled through without stopping.

If you are a teacher or a learner, build in these pauses. Allow your hippocampus to run its rapid forward replay. Combined with soon-after self-testing, these gap effects represent powerful, zero-cost tools for enhancing learning and offsetting forgetting. The reframe is important: testing is not merely evaluative — it is one of the best forms of studying available, and possibly the best overall tool for consolidating information into neural circuits.

## [01:25:40] Tool: Emotion & Learning, PTSD, Deliberate Cold Exposure, Caffeine

More emotionally laden experiences are remembered more durably — this is the basis of phenomena like PTSD, where one-trial learning occurs readily because the nervous system deploys massive amounts of epinephrine and norepinephrine. These neuromodulators allow the neural circuits active just before and during a stressful experience to strengthen their connections in a single exposure — which is adaptive from an evolutionary standpoint, ensuring that dangerous situations are not forgotten.

The same neuromodulatory principle applies to positive learning: anything that increases emotional salience — whether through a dynamic teacher, personal excitement about the material, or even physiological arousal — enhances encoding. Medieval practitioners apparently threw students into cold water immediately after instruction specifically to deploy adrenaline and consolidate memory. While cold plunging after learning is not recommended as a primary strategy, it does make neurobiological sense — and caffeine, which modestly increases epinephrine levels, also provides some benefit to learning. The practical takeaway: cultivate genuine interest and emotional engagement with the material you are trying to learn, and use your internal state deliberately to amplify the signal to your nervous system that this material matters.

## [01:33:28] Tool: Interleaving Information; Unskilled, Mastery & Virtuosity

Interleaving of information is a counterintuitive but real phenomenon: when an instructor or learner periodically introduces little anecdotes, tangentially related information, or pseudo-random material amid the main learning content — rather than delivering a steady unbroken stream of new information — overall learning ability improves. Interleaving appears to work because it gives neural circuits brief pauses similar to gap effects, and also because the brain is able to take new information and incorporate it with existing or distantly related knowledge, rather than treating it as an isolated island.

There are four useful levels to distinguish on the spectrum of competence: unskilled (limited understanding, limited ability to use information), skilled (can recognize and use information in basic to advanced ways), mastery (close to full depth of knowledge in a domain, able to use it flexibly), and virtuosity (such a degree of mastery that the person can actively invite uncertainty and spontaneity into their performance — as great musicians or athletes do — and find themselves performing in ways that even surprise themselves). The tools in today's episode — focused attention, sleep, NSDR, deliberate self-testing soon after learning, gap effects, emotional engagement, and interleaving — are all aimed at moving you along this continuum from unskilled toward mastery and beyond.
