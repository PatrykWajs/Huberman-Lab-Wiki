# Episode 181: How to Prevent & Treat Colds & Flu

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman explains the biology of the common cold and influenza — what causes them, how they spread, and why no single cure exists for the cold (over 160+ rhinovirus serotypes, each with a different surface protein shape). The immune system's three layers are covered mechanistically: physical barriers (skin, nasal mucosa, eye-surface chemicals), the innate immune system (rapid, non-specific), and the adaptive immune system (antibody-generating, memory-forming). Behavioral tools discussed include nasal breathing, sleep, targeted exercise (the J-curve of immune benefit vs. suppression at >75 minutes), sauna heat-shock protocols, morning water swishing to seed the gut microbiome, and charting personal health patterns to identify vulnerability windows. Supplement evidence is evaluated critically: NAC and zinc have the strongest peer-reviewed data, vitamin D matters primarily if deficient, the key vitamin C meta-analysis was retracted due to data errors, and echinacea is unsupported and potentially counterproductive at high doses. Transmission facts are also clarified: contagion begins ~24 hours before symptoms; cold virus survives up to 24 hours on surfaces while flu virus dies within ~2 hours; and handshaking triggers unconscious face-touching that routes pathogens to mucous membranes.

## Key Takeaways

1. **No cure for the cold exists because it is hundreds of viruses.** Over 160 serotypes of rhinovirus cause the common cold, each with a distinct surface protein shape. Antibodies to one serotype do not protect against the next, explaining both the lack of a cure and why multiple colds per year are possible. [00:06:00](transcript.md#000600-common-cold-source--transmission-cold-temperature-myth)

2. **Cold temperatures do not cause colds.** Cold viruses thrive in cool air and spread more in winter because people crowd indoors, increasing aerosol exposure — the temperature itself is not the cause. [00:06:00](transcript.md#000600-common-cold-source--transmission-cold-temperature-myth)

3. **You become contagious ~24 hours before symptoms appear.** Both cold and flu viruses are shed — and therefore transmissible — roughly a full day before you feel sick, making post-symptom isolation an incomplete protection strategy. [00:28:44](transcript.md#002844-spreading-cold--flu-symptoms)

4. **Flu virus dies on surfaces within ~2 hours; cold virus survives up to 24 hours.** Flu spreads primarily via human-to-human aerosol or direct contact; cold can linger on shared surfaces like door handles. You remain most contagious during the 3 days of peak flu symptoms. [00:17:43](transcript.md#001743-flu-virus--transmission-flu-shots)

5. **Flu shots reduce risk by ~40–60% for the targeted strain only.** Shots are directed at the specific strains predicted to be most abundant that season. They provide zero protection against colds or other respiratory viruses, and they reduce symptom severity even if the targeted flu is contracted. [00:17:43](transcript.md#001743-flu-virus--transmission-flu-shots)

6. **Handshaking triggers unconscious hand-to-face contact — a key transmission route.** Research from Dr. Noam Sobel's lab at the Weizmann Institute shows people touch their eyes, nose, or upper lip within seconds of shaking hands, unconsciously sampling chemical signals from the other person. Awareness of this reflex can reduce transmission. [00:39:33](transcript.md#003933-tool-social-connection--touch-avoiding-flu--cold)

7. **The immune system has three layers: physical barriers, innate, and adaptive.** Physical barriers (skin, nasal mucosa, lacrimal chemicals on eyes, oral mucosa) are the first line. The innate system launches a rapid, non-specific attack using neutrophils, natural killer cells, macrophages, and the complement system. The adaptive system generates serotype-specific IgM then IgG antibodies and maintains immunological memory in stem cell populations. [00:30:43](transcript.md#003043-immune-system-physical-barriers)

8. **Nasal breathing is a critical immune barrier — mouth breathing compromises it.** The nasal mucosa traps pathogens and the nasal microbiome produces antimicrobial compounds distinct from the oral microbiome. Mouth-breathing children and adults have altered oral-nasal microbiota and higher rates of upper respiratory infections. [01:06:19](transcript.md#010619-tool-microbiome--nasal-breathing)

9. **Two to four daily servings of low-sugar fermented foods support the gut-immune axis.** Refrigerated sauerkraut, kimchi, kefir, kombucha, natto, and live-culture yogurt promote microbiome diversity along the entire digestive tract. The gut microbiome directly supports innate immune function. A prebiotic/probiotic capsule is a secondary option. [01:10:58](transcript.md#011058-tools-enhance-gut-microbiome-foods--water-protocol)

10. **Exercise ≤60 minutes boosts innate immunity; >75 minutes suppresses it — the J-curve.** Per the 2019 Journal of Sport and Health Science review, moderate-to-high intensity exercise up to 60 minutes increases T-cell function, natural killer cell activity, macrophage output, and cytokine release for up to 24 hours afterward. Marathon-level exertion causes profound immune suppression — natural killer cells and T-cell function drop far below baseline, and stress hormones remain chronically elevated. [01:21:39](transcript.md#012139-tool-exercise--preventing-sickness)

11. **If feeling whole-body malaise, rest — do not exercise.** Interleukin-1 and interleukin-6 from innate immune activation act on the dorsal raphe nucleus and hypothalamus to produce sickness behavior and sleepiness. The appropriate response is a hot shower and early bedtime. Pushing through with exercise prolongs illness. [01:16:13](transcript.md#011613-exercise-when-feeling-sick-sleep)

12. **Sauna activates innate immunity via heat-shock proteins and leukocyte mobilization.** A 2023 study (10 Finnish sauna sessions over 3 weeks) found three 15-minute rounds at 176–210°F with 2-minute cool-offs significantly increased leukocyte count and cortisol — both markers of innate immune upregulation. Heat-adapted athletes required more sessions to reach statistical significance than untrained individuals. [01:34:52](transcript.md#013452-tool-sauna--enhance-immune-system)

13. **NAC (N-acetylcysteine) has the strongest antiviral supplement evidence.** A 1997 RCT (600 mg NAC twice daily for 6 months) showed clinically apparent influenza in 79% of placebo participants vs. only 25% of NAC participants — roughly a 66% reduction. NAC boosts glutathione (the master antioxidant) and acts as a mucolytic (thins and mobilizes mucus). Clinicians in ICU settings report taking it preventively during flu season. [01:55:08](transcript.md#015508-n-acetylcysteine-nac-decongestants)

14. **Zinc at ≥90–100 mg/day shortens cold duration; doses below 75 mg have no measurable effect.** One study showed 90 mg/day zinc acetate produced a 3× faster recovery rate from an existing cold. Zinc should be taken with food to avoid nausea. Avoid in children under 15 without medical guidance; use caution during pregnancy. [01:50:58](transcript.md#015058-echinacea-zinc)

15. **The key vitamin C meta-analysis was retracted — high-dose C has marginal benefit at best.** The 2023 paper "Extra Dose of Vitamin C Based on Daily Supplementation Shortens the Common Cold" (9 RCTs) was retracted due to double-counting placebo groups. High-dose vitamin C (6–8 g/day) causes gastric distress in most people and shows only marginal, inconsistent effects. Food-derived or foundational-supplement levels are likely sufficient. [01:42:20](transcript.md#014220-supplements-vitamin-c-vitamin-d)

16. **Echinacea: no convincing evidence and potential downside at high, continuous doses.** No well-controlled trials show echinacea prevents or shortens colds. At high continuous doses it may reduce white blood cell count and natural killer cell activity. If used at all, limit to peak illness season and no longer than four consecutive weeks. [01:50:58](transcript.md#015058-echinacea-zinc)

## Tools & Protocols

### Nasal Breathing
- **What:** Breathe exclusively through the nose during sleep, rest, and low-to-moderate exercise to maintain nasal microbiome integrity and mucosal pathogen filtering.
- **How:** Focus consciously on nasal breathing throughout the day except when speaking, eating, or exercising at high intensity. Use mouth tape during sleep if needed (referenced alongside the book *Jaws* by Sandra Kahn and Paul Ehrlich).
- **When:** Continuously; non-negotiable during sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:06:19](transcript.md#010619-tool-microbiome--nasal-breathing)

### Sleep Optimization
- **What:** Prioritize sufficient quality sleep as the foundation of innate immune function — even 50–75% of normal sleep significantly compromises innate immune response.
- **How:** Maintain consistent sleep timing, cool and dark room, avoid bright light at night. Use NSDR or yoga nidra if sleep is disrupted. The immune system generates interleukin-driven sleepiness when fighting infection — honor it and get extra rest.
- **When:** Every night; especially critical at onset of any illness or high-exposure period.
- **Ref:** [01:16:13](transcript.md#011613-exercise-when-feeling-sick-sleep)

### Gut Microbiome Support — Fermented Foods
- **What:** Two to four daily servings of low-sugar fermented foods to support gut microbiome diversity and, through the gut-immune axis, innate immune function.
- **How:** Refrigerated sauerkraut (with live cultures), kimchi, kefir, kombucha, natto, live-culture yogurt. Choose low-sugar options — high sugar counteracts the benefit. Prebiotic/probiotic capsule (e.g., AG1) is a secondary option.
- **When:** Daily as a baseline habit year-round.
- **Ref:** [01:10:58](transcript.md#011058-tools-enhance-gut-microbiome-foods--water-protocol)

### Morning Water Swish Protocol
- **What:** Upon waking (before brushing teeth), take a sip of water, swish it around the mouth, and swallow — to seed the gut microbiome with oral bacteria cultivated overnight during nasal-breathing sleep.
- **How:** Pour a small glass of clean water the night before. In the morning, swish one sip and swallow before any other oral hygiene. No cost; no RCT data yet but mechanistically supported by oral-gut microbiome science.
- **When:** Each morning before brushing.
- **Ref:** [01:10:58](transcript.md#011058-tools-enhance-gut-microbiome-foods--water-protocol)

### Exercise for Immune Health
- **What:** Moderate-to-high-intensity aerobic or resistance exercise lasting ≤60 minutes, most days, to boost innate immune surveillance.
- **How:** Exercise at sufficient intensity (subjectively hard but not all-out) for up to 60 minutes. Avoid sessions >75 minutes — these tip into immune suppression. If feeling whole-body malaise, an unusual fatigue, or throat tickle: skip exercise entirely, take a hot shower, and rest in bed.
- **When:** Regular training on healthy days. Cancel entirely on days of confirmed illness.
- **Ref:** [01:21:39](transcript.md#012139-tool-exercise--preventing-sickness)

### Post-Exercise Carbohydrates
- **What:** Ingest complex carbohydrates (rice, oatmeal, pasta, fruit) within ~45–60 minutes after long or fasted training sessions to attenuate post-exercise cortisol and inflammatory markers by approximately 30–40%.
- **How:** Particularly important when training fasted and going beyond 60–75 minutes. A carbohydrate-containing meal (with protein) after exercise is the practical implementation.
- **When:** After strenuous or long-duration training, especially during cold/flu season.
- **Ref:** [01:32:24](transcript.md#013224-tool-exercise-recovery--carbohydrates)

### Sauna Protocol
- **What:** Deliberate heat exposure via Finnish-style dry sauna to elevate heat-shock proteins (HSP-70) and activate innate immunity via leukocyte mobilization and cortisol upregulation.
- **How:** Three rounds of 15 minutes at 176–210°F (80–99°C), with 2-minute cool-off (cool shower or room air) between rounds. A cold plunge between rounds is optional but permitted. Alternatively, one 20–30-minute session. Stay hydrated. Do not sauna if already severely ill with full-blown symptoms.
- **When:** Two to three times per week for prevention; at first signs of feeling run-down (before full symptom onset).
- **Ref:** [01:34:52](transcript.md#013452-tool-sauna--enhance-immune-system)

### NAC (N-Acetylcysteine)
- **What:** N-acetylcysteine as antiviral prophylaxis and mucolytic during active infection; boosts glutathione, reduces reactive oxygen and nitrogen species, and loosens respiratory mucus.
- **How:** Prevention: 600 mg twice daily (1,200 mg/day total) through flu season. Active infection (Huberman's personal protocol): 600–900 mg three times daily (morning, late morning, afternoon) — avoid dosing close to sleep as increased mucus flow disrupts sleep.
- **When:** Preventively through cold/flu season; therapeutically once a cold is confirmed.
- **Ref:** [01:55:08](transcript.md#015508-n-acetylcysteine-nac-decongestants)

### Zinc Supplementation
- **What:** Zinc at ≥90–100 mg/day to shorten cold duration; doses below 75 mg/day are ineffective.
- **How:** Take zinc acetate or equivalent with a full meal to avoid nausea. Can split into two 50 mg doses. Cap at ~120 mg/day. Not recommended for children under 15 without medical supervision; discuss with physician during pregnancy.
- **When:** At onset of cold symptoms or after confirmed exposure; not required as routine prevention.
- **Ref:** [01:50:58](transcript.md#015058-echinacea-zinc)

### Vitamin D
- **What:** Correct vitamin D deficiency — deficient individuals have significantly higher rates of acute respiratory tract infections.
- **How:** Test blood levels (Huberman tests every 6 months). If deficient: 1,000–5,000+ IU/day D3 depending on measured levels. 1,000–2,000 IU/day is a reasonable general buffer. Do not megadose if levels are already sufficient — excess vitamin D can be detrimental.
- **When:** Year-round if deficient; especially in low-sunlight months.
- **Ref:** [01:42:20](transcript.md#014220-supplements-vitamin-c-vitamin-d)

### Decongestant Use
- **What:** Use OTC decongestants (alpha-1 agonists causing vasoconstriction) sparingly — nasal spray decongestants cause rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa) after 3–5 days. Huberman prefers NAC as a mucolytic alternative.
- **How:** Use saline nasal rinse first. If using OTC decongestants, limit to short courses and prefer oral formulations over nasal sprays.
- **When:** Active cold or flu with significant congestion; not preventively.
- **Ref:** [01:55:08](transcript.md#015508-n-acetylcysteine-nac-decongestants)

### Tracking Personal Health Patterns
- **What:** Log sleep quality, training intensity, stress events, and travel in a simple daily shorthand (~30 seconds/day) to identify personal vulnerability windows before illness strikes.
- **How:** Use a simple rating system (e.g., workout intensity 1–10, sleep: good/poor). After any illness, look back at the preceding days. Huberman identified two-a-day training and multi-night sleep deprivation as his primary precursors to illness.
- **When:** Ongoing; especially valuable for people who travel frequently, work with children, or work in high-exposure environments.
- **Ref:** [00:23:56](transcript.md#002356-tools-injections--safety-charting-health-trends--sickness)
