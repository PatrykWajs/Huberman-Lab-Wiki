## [00:06:00] Happiness, Emotion & Cognition; Emotional Contagion

**Andrew Huberman:** Laurie, it's really great to have you here. I know you've thought deeply about what happiness actually is, and I'd love to start there. What is happiness from a scientific standpoint?

**Laurie Santos:** Yeah so scientists who study happiness tend to think of it as what they technically call subjective well-being. There are kind of two components to subjective well-being. There's what we might call your emotional well-being, which is really about how you feel in day-to-day life—are you feeling positive emotions like joy, gratitude, and awe on a frequent basis? And that's distinct from your cognitive well-being, which is more about what you think about your life as a whole—if you step back and ask yourself, am I satisfied with my life overall? That cognitive component tends to correlate with things like what you've accomplished, whether you've achieved your goals, those kinds of things.

**Andrew Huberman:** So those are the two components of what we call happiness.

**Laurie Santos:** Right. And both matter, but they can come apart. You can have a really positive emotional life on a moment-to-moment basis but still feel unsatisfied with where you've ended up overall. And you can have accomplished a lot but still feel like emotionally something's missing. Both components matter for a good life.

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about emotional contagion. How does the emotional state of others around us affect our own?

**Laurie Santos:** This is one of my favorite topics because it shows just how social our emotional systems are. We literally catch emotions from the people around us—this is what scientists call emotional contagion. When you're around people who are happy, you tend to get a boost in positive emotion yourself. And unfortunately the same is true of negative emotions. We tend to catch the emotional states of the people we're surrounded by. And this has been shown in remarkable ways—in large network studies, Nicholas Christakis and James Fowler have shown that happiness actually spreads through social networks up to three degrees of separation. So your friend's friend's friend's happiness influences your own emotional state.

## [00:11:18] Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic Rewards

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the difference between extrinsic and intrinsic rewards, and how does that relate to happiness?

**Laurie Santos:** This is such a foundational issue for happiness science. So extrinsic rewards are things outside of yourself—you're doing an activity because of what you'll get from it, whether that's money, social approval, or status. Intrinsic rewards are more about the activity itself—you're doing it because you love it, because it's interesting, because it's meaningful to you.

What we know from decades of research is that when we focus too heavily on extrinsic rewards, it can actually undermine our intrinsic motivation—this is called the over-justification effect. There's this classic example from childhood studies where if you give kids a reward for drawing, something they naturally love to do, they actually draw less once you stop giving them the reward. The external reward changed the nature of why they were doing it.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think about a story involving the author David Sedaris and his Fitbit. He talked about how once he started wearing a Fitbit, his relationship with walking completely changed. He had loved walking for decades, and then the Fitbit turned it into something he was doing for the step count.

**Laurie Santos:** That's a perfect example. And there's a real lesson there. The Fitbit—or really any gamification of an activity you naturally enjoy—can inadvertently shift the motivation from intrinsic to extrinsic, and that often reduces the joy. That said, there are times when external structure or tracking can be helpful, especially for activities you're trying to build a habit around that you don't naturally love. The issue arises when you gamify things you already intrinsically enjoy.

## [00:14:43] Money, Comparison & Happiness

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about one of the biggest topics in happiness research: the relationship between money and happiness.

**Laurie Santos:** This is an area where our intuitions are notoriously wrong. For a long time, the classic reference study in this field was by Daniel Kahneman and Angus Deaton from around 2010, which suggested that emotional well-being—that day-to-day feeling component—plateaus around $75,000 a year in the US. Above that point, more money doesn't seem to buy more moment-to-moment happiness. More recently, Matthew Killingsworth has done work suggesting the relationship continues a bit higher, perhaps up to around $100,000 or so, but still with diminishing returns.

**Andrew Huberman:** So money does matter, but only up to a point.

**Laurie Santos:** Right. And the key insight is why. Below a certain income threshold, not having enough money is genuinely stressful—it means you can't meet basic needs, you're dealing with financial uncertainty. Above that threshold, the story changes. And here's the thing that really surprised me: the reason having more money beyond that point doesn't help so much is because of comparison. There's a wealth psychologist named Clay Cockrell who works with ultra-wealthy clients in New York City, and he describes this phenomenon where people with $50 million feel genuinely poor because they're comparing themselves to their peers who have $500 million. The comparison itself is the problem, not the absolute level of wealth.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the hedonic treadmill applies to wealth as much as anything else.

**Laurie Santos:** Exactly. And social comparison is one of the biggest drivers of why money doesn't buy happiness the way we expect it to. The research on this is really clear: how happy you feel about your income depends enormously on how it compares to your peers, your neighbors, your colleagues. And that comparison is almost always tilted in an unfavorable direction.

## [00:21:39] Tool: Increase Social Connection; Real-Time Communication

**Andrew Huberman:** What does the science say is the single biggest behavioral driver of happiness?

**Laurie Santos:** If I had to pick one behavioral lever that has the most robust evidence, it's social connection. More specifically, real face-to-face or at least real-time human connection. This is backed by enormous amounts of converging evidence. One of my favorite demonstrations of this comes from Nick Epley at the University of Chicago, who has done a series of studies I love to call the commuter train studies. Basically, Epley and his team randomly assigned commuters to either connect with a stranger sitting next to them or to sit in solitude or just do whatever they normally do. And the people who were assigned to connect with strangers reported the most positive experience.

**Andrew Huberman:** And most people would predict the opposite, right? That solitude would be preferable?

**Laurie Santos:** Exactly! And that's what makes this data so compelling. Before the study, when Epley asked commuters which condition they thought they'd prefer, most predicted they'd want the solitude condition. But the data showed the opposite—conversation with strangers was the most enjoyable. The key issue is we have this prediction error about what will make us happy. We think strangers won't want to talk to us, that the conversation will be awkward. But it turns out most people do want to connect, and those connections are more positive than we expect.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about people who are more introverted? Is there evidence that social connection is universally beneficial, or does it depend on personality?

**Laurie Santos:** This is something we'll come back to more when we talk about introverts and extroverts, but the short answer is: yes, even for introverts, social connection is beneficial. The question is more about the form and amount. Introverts may need more alone time to recharge, but they're not immune to the happiness benefits of social connection when they do engage. And importantly, we tend to underestimate how positive social interactions will be regardless of where we fall on the introvert-extrovert spectrum.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the type of communication—in-person versus texting versus social media?

**Laurie Santos:** This is where the research gets really interesting. There is emerging evidence that the mode of communication matters quite a bit. The neuroscientist Seth Roberts at UC Berkeley did some interesting self-experimental work suggesting that seeing a human face in real time—literally seeing someone's face as they talk—has particular benefits, possibly related to activity in an area called the fusiform face area. We seem to have evolved to be around other people's faces, and that visual social information is deeply reinforcing.

Texting doesn't provide that. And the late David Byrne of Talking Heads actually wrote quite thoughtfully about how technology has systematically eliminated human presence from our transactions—what he called "eliminating the human." You used to go to a bank and interact with a person; now you use an ATM. You used to call someone and talk; now you text. And each of these replacements, while convenient, strips out something our brains seem to crave.

## [00:33:47] Technology, Information, Social Interaction

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk more about the relationship between technology and social connection. Specifically phones.

**Laurie Santos:** The phone data is really striking. There's a study from Princeton that looked at what happens when you remove phones from a study environment, and students' performance on cognitive tasks improved by double digits—this is huge. Our phones are not just providing a distraction; they're occupying cognitive bandwidth even when we're not actively using them. Just having the phone present reduces our ability to be present with the people around us and with our own thoughts.

Liz Dunn at UBC has done some elegant work showing that when people are on their phones in social contexts, they smile about 30% less. That's a significant reduction in positive social signaling and reciprocal connection. The phone creates a kind of bubble around you that others can see and that subtly signals you're not fully available for connection.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think about it like a wheelbarrow—you have this ability to do something meaningful with your attention, but instead you're filling it with something that looks productive but isn't.

**Laurie Santos:** That's a great analogy. Katherine Price, who wrote a book about fun and has done a lot of thinking about why our leisure time doesn't feel as restorative as it should, asks a really great question that I find myself thinking about: "Is this fun?" She literally suggests asking yourself that question in the moment. And when she's talking about genuine fun, she means activities that involve three things: playfulness, flow, and social connection. A lot of what we do on our phones checks none of those boxes.

## [00:39:22] Loneliness, Youth, Technology

**Andrew Huberman:** The data on youth loneliness is pretty alarming, isn't it?

**Laurie Santos:** It's quite alarming. We're seeing surveys suggesting that somewhere in the range of 70 to 75 percent of young people report feeling extremely lonely on a regular basis. And this is happening at a time when they're more connected in a technological sense than any generation in history. Jonathan Haidt's work has been particularly influential here in making the argument that the rise in youth mental health problems—anxiety, depression, loneliness—correlates strongly with the rise of smartphone use and particularly social media use among teenagers.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the mechanism? Why does social media make us feel lonelier?

**Laurie Santos:** There are several mechanisms, but one of the key ones is that social media provides what you might call a diluted form of social reward. Our brains are deeply wired to seek social information—information about what other people are doing, thinking, saying about us. That's probably deeply evolutionarily adaptive. Social media delivers a near-constant trickle of that social information, which generates small dopamine hits. But it's not the same as genuine social connection. It's more like getting the smell of food without actually eating—it activates the craving system without satisfying it.

## [00:42:16] Cravings, Sustainable Actions, Dopamine

**Andrew Huberman:** There's something important about the relationship between craving and satisfaction in the dopamine system that's relevant here.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes. The dopamine system is fundamentally a system about prediction and anticipation, not just about reward. When we get a reward that we predicted, dopamine barely moves. When we get an unexpected reward, dopamine spikes. When we expect a reward and don't get it, dopamine actually drops below baseline—it creates an aversive state. Social media is very clever at engineering this unpredictability in a way that keeps us endlessly scrolling.

**Andrew Huberman:** One thing I always try to convey is to beware any dopamine hit that isn't preceded by real effort, real investment. The pattern of effort followed by reward is not just psychologically satisfying—it seems to be the structure that keeps the dopamine system healthy. When we short-circuit that—when we get the dopamine without the effort—we're borrowing against future motivation.

**Laurie Santos:** That's a really important point, and it connects to the difference between addiction and well-being. Addiction, at its core, involves a narrowing of the repertoire of activities that feel rewarding. You become dependent on one source of dopamine—whether it's a substance, a behavior, or in this case, a particular kind of social validation—and everything else starts to feel flat by comparison. Genuine happiness involves the opposite: a broadening of what feels rewarding, more sources of pleasure and meaning, not fewer.

## [00:47:01] Social Connection & Predictions; Introverts & Extroverts

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's go deeper on introverts and extroverts. There's a lot of conventional wisdom here about what they need—is it accurate?

**Laurie Santos:** The conventional wisdom is that extroverts love socializing and introverts need solitude to recharge, and that's broadly true. But there are some really interesting nuances that the research reveals. One of the most interesting comes from studies by Nicholas Epley and others looking at how people across the introvert-extrovert spectrum respond to being pushed to engage in more social behavior than usual. Introverts and extroverts both predict that behaving more extrovertedly—being more outgoing, talkative, and warm—would feel more tiring and less authentic for introverts. But when you actually run the study, introverts who behave more extrovertedly report higher well-being, just like extroverts do.

**Andrew Huberman:** That seems counterintuitive. Why would that be?

**Laurie Santos:** One hypothesis is that what we call the introvert-extrovert spectrum might partly reflect a learning history about social interaction. Introverts may have learned that social interactions are more effortful, or had more negative experiences. But when the context is right and the interaction goes well, the positive emotions are there for introverts too. Now, there's a caveat: this doesn't mean introverts should force themselves to be extroverted all the time. There really is something to the idea of authentic self-expression. What it suggests is that our predictions about what will be draining versus energizing might be off. We should experiment.

**Andrew Huberman:** There was a journalist who actually conducted this experiment—living as an extrovert for a year.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! Jessica Pan wrote a great book about this called "Sorry I'm Late, I Left Without You." She's a self-described introvert who spent a year forcing herself to say yes to social situations, to talk to strangers, to do improv comedy—all the things that are stereotypically extroverted. And while it was uncomfortable at times, she reports genuinely increasing in happiness over the course of that year. It's a vivid illustration of the experimental approach to self-discovery.

**Andrew Huberman:** I like the leaky tire analogy you sometimes use.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! If your car has a slow leak in the tire, you might not notice it because you're filling it up regularly. But if you don't fill it, eventually you're going to have a problem. For a lot of people, their social connection needs are like a slow leak—they don't notice how depleted they're getting until something changes and they're running flat. Building in social connection even when you don't feel like you need it is a kind of preventive maintenance.

## [01:00:41] Social Connection & Frequency; Tools: Fun; "Presence" & Technology

**Andrew Huberman:** Beyond just quantity of social connection, what about quality and the types of social activities?

**Laurie Santos:** Both matter a lot. On the quality side, one of the things that matters is whether you're being present. And this ties back to technology—the phone at the dinner table dramatically reduces the quality of the social interaction even if you're not actively using it. People in those interactions feel less cared for and less connected.

On the fun dimension: we often fill our so-called leisure time with things that aren't actually fun. Katherine Price distinguishes between what she calls "fake fun" and "true fun." Fake fun is things like scrolling through social media, binge-watching TV—they can feel pleasant in the moment but they don't leave us feeling recharged or more connected. True fun involves playfulness, flow, and genuine connection. The question she suggests asking is not "am I enjoying this?" but "is this fun?"—and they're different questions.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the frequency of social interaction? Does it matter how often we connect with people?

**Laurie Santos:** Frequency is actually quite important. We tend to assume that deep, meaningful interactions matter more than frequent casual ones. But the data suggest that the frequency of positive social interactions is a strong predictor of happiness. Even brief, positive exchanges—what some researchers call "micro-connections"—matter. Saying good morning to a neighbor, having a brief friendly conversation with a barista—these small moments of connection add up.

## [01:07:53] Technology & Negative Effects; Tool: Senses & Grounding; Podcasts

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk practically about managing phone use. What does the research support?

**Laurie Santos:** The evidence points pretty clearly toward some version of phone-free periods being beneficial—both for well-being and for cognitive performance. Some of the simplest interventions: keeping your phone out of the bedroom, leaving it in another room during meals, designating phone-free blocks during the workday. Even just putting your phone face-down instead of face-up reduces distraction.

There's also something to be said for sensory grounding as an alternative—actively engaging your senses with the physical environment around you. Some people use a technique of the 5-4-3-2-1 practice: naming five things you can see, four you can hear, three you can touch, two you can smell, one you can taste. This kind of sensory anchoring pulls attention back to the present moment, which tends to be associated with better mood.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about podcasts? There's obviously some irony in asking you this while you're on a podcast. Are podcasts problematic?

**Laurie Santos:** I think podcasts occupy an interesting category. When you're listening to a podcast and also exercising, or driving somewhere you have to go anyway, you're not necessarily trading off against something better. The question is what you're doing instead of. If the alternative is scrolling social media, a thoughtful podcast is probably a better use of attention. But if you're putting in earbuds during a walk and thereby cutting yourself off from your sensory environment and any potential social connection, that's worth questioning.

## [01:15:11] Negativity Bias, Gratitude, Tool: "Delight" Practice & Shifting Emotions

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the negativity bias. Humans seem to pay more attention to negative things than positive ones—is that the right framing?

**Laurie Santos:** Yes, and it's remarkably well-documented. In general, negative events have more psychological impact than equally sized positive events. Losing $50 feels worse than finding $50 feels good. We remember negative feedback more than positive feedback. We scan our environments for threats. This is probably adaptive from an evolutionary perspective—missing a predator is more costly than missing a nice meal. But in our modern environment, where genuine threats are fewer, the negativity bias tends to work against us.

**Andrew Huberman:** And this is where practices like gratitude come in?

**Laurie Santos:** Exactly. Gratitude practices are one of the most well-supported interventions in positive psychology. Sonja Lyubomirsky's work, among many others, shows that regularly writing down things you're grateful for—typically three to five things, a few times a week—produces measurable improvements in well-being. The mechanism seems to be something like counteracting the negativity bias by deliberately directing attention toward positive experiences.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's also a related practice you talk about—the delight practice.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! This comes from Katherine Price, who also wrote a beautiful book called "The Book of Delights" with the author Ross Gay—although that's a separate book by Ross Gay that Katherine introduced me to. The delight practice is about noticing and savoring small moments of unexpected joy in daily life. A shaft of light through a window, the smell of coffee, a bird doing something funny. The practice is less effortful than formal gratitude journaling—it's more like cultivating a habit of attention.

What's interesting is that this kind of noticing seems to actually shift emotional state quite rapidly. There's work by researchers suggesting that even briefly describing something you're experiencing as delightful can shift your emotional state within minutes. It's not toxic positivity—it's not pretending things are better than they are. It's more like flipping on a light switch in a room that already has some nice things in it.

## [01:26:17] Importance of Negative Emotions; Judgements about Happiness

**Andrew Huberman:** One thing I want to make sure we address: there's a risk of overcorrecting toward positivity. Negative emotions serve a purpose, right?

**Laurie Santos:** Absolutely, and this is something I feel strongly about. Negative emotions are not pathological—they're information. They're like warning lights on the dashboard of your car. If your check engine light comes on and you put tape over it, you haven't fixed anything, you've just made it harder to notice the problem. Anxiety is often telling you something important about a threat you should pay attention to. Sadness is often a signal about something you've lost that mattered to you. Anger is often pointing to an injustice. These emotions have function.

The goal of happiness science is not to maximize positive emotion at all costs or to eliminate negative emotion. The goal is what researchers sometimes call emotional well-being, which includes having appropriate negative emotions—emotions that are proportionate and relevant to the situation—not having excessive or prolonged negative states that serve no adaptive function.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about this "feel good, do good" effect you mentioned earlier?

**Laurie Santos:** This is a lovely robust finding. When people are in a positive emotional state, they tend to be more prosocial—more helpful, more generous, more creative. This has been shown by Constantine Sedikides and others across many contexts. And it creates a wonderful positive feedback loop: feeling good tends to lead to behavior that helps others, which in turn tends to generate more positive feelings. This has practical implications. When you're in a positive state, that's often a good time to tackle challenges that require creativity or collaboration.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about climate change and other large-scale challenges? There's a lot of data suggesting that many people feel hopeless and don't take action because the problem feels too big.

**Laurie Santos:** There's good research on this. Despair and helplessness tend to undermine action rather than motivate it. Feeling like the problem is too big—what researchers sometimes call "ecological grief" or "eco-anxiety"—when it tips into hopelessness, it actually makes people less likely to engage in pro-environmental behavior. Whereas feeling positive emotions like hope, and connecting with others who care, tends to be associated with more action. This is one of the best arguments for not marinating in bad news.

## [01:34:16] Happiness & Cultural Differences, Tool: Focus on Small Pleasures

**Andrew Huberman:** Let me ask about cultural differences in happiness. Why is Scandinavia so high on the happiness rankings?

**Laurie Santos:** The World Happiness Report consistently shows Nordic countries like Finland and Denmark at the top, which has made them a subject of enormous fascination. There are several features of those societies that might explain it: strong social safety nets that reduce economic anxiety, high social trust, good work-life balance, strong community ties. But there's also something cultural—what the Danes call "hygge," which translates roughly as coziness or togetherness. It's a cultural emphasis on simple, small pleasures shared with others: a warm cup of coffee, candlelight, comfortable clothing, good company. That emphasis on small pleasures is actually something the happiness research suggests is quite beneficial.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the science behind focusing on small pleasures?

**Laurie Santos:** When we think about what will make us happy, we tend to think big—major life events, major accomplishments, major purchases. But the data suggest that small, frequent positive experiences contribute more to daily emotional well-being than large, infrequent ones. The reason comes back to hedonic adaptation—we get used to things. The new car that thrilled you in the first week becomes the background of your life. But a good cup of coffee, a beautiful sunset, an unexpected funny moment—these can generate genuine positive emotion on a regular basis if you're paying attention to them.

## [01:41:00] Dogs, Monkeys & Brain, "Monkey Mind"

**Andrew Huberman:** You've done fascinating work with non-human primates and dogs. What can animals teach us about happiness?

**Laurie Santos:** So much! I think about animal cognition and behavior a lot when I think about happiness for humans, because the contrast is really instructive. Let me start with dogs. Dogs seem to have this incredible quality of presence—they're really there, in the moment, in a way that humans often aren't. And I think part of that reflects their neural architecture. Dogs have limited prefrontal cortex relative to humans, which means they have less capacity for the kind of counterfactual thinking, social comparison, and rumination that gets humans into trouble.

**Andrew Huberman:** Which brings up the concept of "monkey mind" from Buddhist practice.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! And I've always felt that "monkey mind" is an unfair characterization of actual monkeys. Real monkeys—at least the rhesus macaques I've spent years studying at Cayo Santiago, a field site on an island off the coast of Puerto Rico—are not particularly prone to the kind of restless, comparative thinking the term implies. When I sit near a monkey who's watching the ocean, I'm quite confident it's not thinking "what do I have to cook for dinner, and what does my husband think of me." I think it's more like... ocean. Just that. They're profoundly present in a way that humans rarely are.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about their cognitive capacities more generally?

**Laurie Santos:** Rhesus macaques are sophisticated in many ways—they can do multi-step social planning, they understand who's watching when they can steal something, they can form alliances. But what they can't do is what humans do so effortlessly and so destructively: simulate worlds that don't exist. I can imagine 700 scenarios that have never happened and probably never will. I can model what another person thinks that a third person thinks. I can regret choices I didn't make. This counterfactual simulation is an enormous cognitive achievement, and it's the basis of human creativity, fiction, planning—and also of rumination, social comparison, and a lot of unhappiness.

## [01:47:40] Monkeys, Perspective, Planning

**Andrew Huberman:** What about perspective-taking and planning in monkeys?

**Laurie Santos:** Monkeys can do what I'd call present-tense planning—planning based on what's available in the immediate environment right now. But they struggle with the kind of future-oriented, abstract planning that humans do routinely. And their perspective-taking has limits too—they're better at tracking gaze than at genuinely modeling what's in someone else's mind. The eggplant story is a good illustration: we were doing studies on Cayo Santiago where we had eggplants as stimuli, and a monkey stole one of our eggplants when we weren't looking. What was impressive wasn't that the monkey understood we wanted the eggplant—it was simpler: it noticed our attention was diverted and took advantage of the opportunity. That's gaze following and tactical deception, but it's not deep theory of mind.

**Andrew Huberman:** I love the point you're making that what we call sophisticated cognition in animals is often sophisticated in its own right, just different from human cognition—not lesser.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes, exactly. Rhesus monkeys aren't doing worse planning than humans; they're doing a different kind of planning that works beautifully for their ecological niche. The same is true of dogs, who have remarkable social cognition with humans that's almost certainly a product of tens of thousands of years of co-evolution with us. And birds—the corvids, the diving birds—do things computationally that would blow your mind if you thought about them. Diving birds have to correct for the refractive index of water when targeting a fish below the surface. "Bird brain" is a serious underestimate.

## [01:53:58] Dogs, Cats, Dingos; Pets & Happiness

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk pets and happiness. You've studied dogs formally. Do they actually make us happier?

**Laurie Santos:** Yes, and there's actually good data on this. Pet owners, and particularly dog owners, are on average statistically happier. And when you look at why, it makes sense from a behavioral standpoint. Dogs provide social connection—with the dog itself, which taps into our caregiving system, but also with other humans. Having a dog gives you a reason to walk through your neighborhood, and people talk to you. The "bulldog tax" you mentioned is a real phenomenon—having a cute dog is a social lubricant that gets you into micro-connections with strangers constantly.

Dogs also get people exercising—and we know from meta-analyses that approximately thirty minutes of cardio a day is comparable to antidepressant medication in terms of reducing symptoms of depression. So even if you wouldn't go for walks for your own sake, you'll do it for your dog. And they help people be more present—when you're interacting with a dog, you're usually not simultaneously on your phone.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about cats versus dogs?

**Laurie Santos:** There's less formal data comparing cat owners to dog owners in terms of happiness, but my sense from what data exists is that dogs may have a slight edge, at least partly because of the social connection mechanism—the walks, the stranger interactions. That said, cats clearly provide comfort, companionship, and sensory pleasure through touch. I think the dog-person versus cat-person distinction may come down to what I'd call the unconditional love ratio. Dogs are wired to want to please you almost unconditionally—they've been bred for that. Cats have their own will in a much more pronounced way. People who appreciate that independence tend to be cat people; people who want the unconditional warmth tend to be dog people.

**Andrew Huberman:** You've also worked with dingoes in Australia, right?

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! We got to work with genetically pure dingoes at a sanctuary in Australia—one of the few places where you can do that. And what was fascinating is that dingoes look like dogs but behave much more like cats in some respects. They had this quality of warmth and social interest in us, up to a point, and then just... no. That's enough. They have this strong independent will that domestic dogs have largely had bred out of them over millennia. It was a fascinating window into what that last stage of domestication—the removal of "no push me," as you put it—actually changed.

## [02:00:49] Time Famish; Tools: Time Affluence Breaks; Time Confetti & Free Time

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's shift to the topic of time and happiness. What does the research show about how our experience of time affects well-being?

**Laurie Santos:** There's a really important concept here that researchers call time affluence—the subjective sense that you have enough free time. And its opposite, time famine—the sense of being starved of time, of always being rushed. The data are striking: experiencing time famine is as big a hit on well-being as being unemployed. That's not being poor—just not having time to breathe is apparently as bad for well-being as not having a job.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's remarkable. And I think a lot of people—especially high-achieving people—experience this chronically.

**Laurie Santos:** Right. And what's interesting is that the key word is "subjective"—it's not necessarily about how many hours objectively are free. It's about the felt sense of having some slack in your life. This creates a useful hack: you can sometimes increase your felt sense of time affluence simply by framing things differently. One of my favorite demonstrations of this is what I do in my happiness class at Yale. I tell students there's a lecture on time affluence, and then when they come to class they get a flyer saying class is canceled—go use the next hour and a half however you want. And students reported it feeling like an enormous gift, even though it was only ninety minutes. One student cried because it was the first free time she'd had in three months.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the "time confetti" concept?

**Laurie Santos:** Journalist Bridget Schulte coined that term to describe the fragmented nature of free time in modern life. We actually have more leisure hours than people did fifteen or twenty years ago, if you add them up, but those hours are chopped into tiny pieces—five minutes here, ten minutes there. And because they feel inconsequential, we tend to waste them on low-value activities like checking email or scrolling social media. The insight is that those little pieces of time confetti can actually be used intentionally. Use the five-minute gap between meetings to text a friend, get outside in sunlight, do a few minutes of something physical. These small intentional choices add up.

## [02:07:46] Hedonic Adaptation; Tool: Spacing Happy Experiences

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about hedonic adaptation—the fact that we get used to things.

**Laurie Santos:** Hedonic adaptation is one of the central findings of happiness science and one of the most counterintuitive. We are remarkably good at getting used to things—both good things and bad things. The good things become the background of our lives. The bad things stop hurting as much. There's a famous study by Philip Brickman and colleagues that found that lottery winners and people who had become paraplegic had similar levels of happiness about a year after those events. At one year out, happiness had returned to roughly baseline.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the dopamine reward system is fundamentally contrast-based.

**Laurie Santos:** Exactly. The system is built on relative comparisons, not absolute levels. And this has both a good side and a bad side. The bad side: every good thing in life, if it sticks around and stays constant, eventually becomes boring. The person who said "I love you" for the first time generates a huge dopamine response. The same person saying "I love you" for the thousandth time in the same way barely registers. The good side: even the worst things adapt. Grief becomes survivable. Disability becomes a new baseline. This is part of what makes humans remarkably resilient.

**Andrew Huberman:** What can we do with this knowledge about hedonic adaptation?

**Laurie Santos:** Liz Dunn at UBC uses the phrase "scarcity engineers happiness." One of the best tools we have against hedonic adaptation is spacing out positive experiences. If you have a favorite restaurant, going every week means you'll habituate to it. Going every few months means each visit is still exciting. This applies to music, to activities, to social occasions. Counterintuitively, sometimes the way to get more happiness out of good things is to have them less frequently.

For good things you can't easily space out—like your relationship with your children—you can use imagination. This is where negative visualization comes in, which we'll get to in a moment.

## [02:15:27] Contrast, Comparison & Happiness; Tool: Bronze Lining, Negative Visualization

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the role of contrast and comparison in happiness.

**Laurie Santos:** Comparison is one of the most powerful forces in our emotional lives, and mostly in a negative direction. The silver medalist data is my favorite illustration. When researchers coded the facial expressions of athletes on the Olympic medal podium, the silver medalists—who are objectively second best in the world—showed expressions consistent with grief or contempt. The bronze medalists were often happier than the gold medalists. Why? Because the silver medalist's reference point is the gold—they were so close to being the best, and the counterfactual "I could have won" is vivid and painful. The bronze medalist's reference point is not being on the podium at all—they almost didn't make it.

**Andrew Huberman:** This gives rise to the tool you call the "bronze lining."

**Laurie Santos:** Right. Instead of looking for the silver lining in a difficult situation—finding the positive in what happened—sometimes it's more effective to do what the bronze medalist unconsciously does: find a reference point that's worse than your current situation. Think about how things could have gone more badly. Think about not having something you have. This isn't toxic positivity; it's using contrast deliberately.

The most powerful version of this is negative visualization, from the Stoic tradition. Marcus Aurelius and other Stoics practiced meditating briefly on losing the things you have—your health, your loved ones, your achievements—and then stopping and recognizing you still have them. This interrupts hedonic adaptation without actually having to lose the good thing. It's a mental trick that restores contrast and with it, appreciation.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a beautiful line in Pulp Fiction about this—something like finding it almost made losing it worth it.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! That captures exactly the mechanism. When you temporarily lose something you've habituated to and then get it back, it's astonishing how much you suddenly value it. The negative visualization practice tries to achieve that restoration of appreciation without the actual loss. And the research supports it: briefly imagining the absence of something good does increase appreciation for it in the present.

## [02:24:08] Visualization, Bannister Effect; Tool: Imagine Obstacles

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the conventional wisdom around positive visualization—manifesting your future self, visualizing success?

**Laurie Santos:** This is a place where I want to push back on the culture a little. There's research by Gabriele Oettingen at NYU showing that purely fantasizing about a positive future outcome—what she calls indulgent positive fantasizing—can actually reduce motivation to achieve that outcome. What seems to happen is that the brain partially simulates having the reward already, and this partly satisfies the motivational system. You feel some of the good feelings without doing the work.

**Andrew Huberman:** What actually works?

**Laurie Santos:** Oettingen's research points to what she calls "mental contrasting" or the WOOP method: Wish, Outcome, Obstacle, Plan. You identify what you want, you imagine achieving it vividly, and then—crucially—you imagine the specific obstacles that will get in the way and make a concrete plan for overcoming them. The obstacle imagining part is key. It activates problem-solving rather than just reward anticipation.

And this is where the Bannister effect comes in. Roger Bannister ran the first sub-four-minute mile in 1954 when people believed it was physiologically impossible. Within two months, someone else broke the barrier. Now high schoolers do it. The belief that something is achievable is crucial to motivation—but the way to actually achieve it is to plan for the obstacles, not just fantasize about the outcome.

## [02:29:12] Culture; Arrival Fallacy, Tool: Journey Mindset

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a concept called the arrival fallacy. What is it?

**Laurie Santos:** The arrival fallacy is the belief that once you achieve some particular goal or acquire some particular thing, you will finally be happy. "I'll be happy when I get that promotion." "I'll be happy when I find the right partner." "I'll be happy when I get to $50 million." The problem is that, due to hedonic adaptation, arriving at that destination delivers far less happiness than we predicted, and often less than we got during the striving. The gold medalist who expected to win is less happy at the moment of victory than you'd think, because they fully expected it—no reward prediction error.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what's the antidote?

**Laurie Santos:** The journey mindset. This is the recognition that the process of working toward something—the striving, the learning, the small wins and setbacks along the way—is where most of the actual life is happening. If you can pay attention to the journey rather than just keeping your eyes fixed on the destination, you get much more happiness out of the whole endeavor. I had Michelle Kwan on my podcast—the Olympic skater who won silver, never gold—and she talked about how the moments she loved most weren't about medals at all. It was things like tying her skates and seeing the Olympic rings in the ice and knowing she was about to skate over them.

## [02:37:11] Mortality, Memento Mori, Tool: Fleeting Experiences & Contrast

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about mortality and happiness. There's the Stoic concept of memento mori—remember that you will die. How does contemplating death affect happiness?

**Laurie Santos:** The research on this is really interesting. Reminding people of their mortality—or of the finite nature of positive experiences—can actually increase appreciation and happiness in the moment. There's a study with senior college students who were reminded that they only had a certain number of days left before graduation. Those who received the "days" framing—which made the time feel short—reported higher happiness and were more likely to take action on things they'd been putting off. The finitude activated the appreciation.

This is the deeper insight behind memento mori: not that we should be morbid, but that recognizing the limited nature of something actually helps us value it more. I've been experiencing this personally as my mother-in-law gets older—recognizing that there aren't infinite holidays left with her has made me more present for the ones we have.

**Andrew Huberman:** And this connects back to negative visualization and hedonic adaptation. Reminding yourself that positive experiences are temporary can serve as a kind of forward-looking negative visualization.

**Laurie Santos:** Exactly. It's contrast, operating prospectively rather than retrospectively. There's a bittersweet quality to it—memento mori is explicitly meant to be bittersweet—but the sweetness is real. And I think this explains why people voluntarily expose themselves to difficult experiences for the contrast: horror movies, spicy food, cold plunges. The getting-out-of-the-cold-plunge feeling is partly so good because of what preceded it.

## [02:44:33] Awe

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about awe as an emotion. You mentioned it earlier in the context of happiness.

**Laurie Santos:** Awe is one of the most fascinating emotions that researchers have started studying seriously in recent years. Dacher Keltner at Berkeley has done a lot of the foundational work here. Awe is characterized by two things: a sense of vastness—something that feels bigger or more complex than your current framework—and a need for accommodation, where you have to revise how you think to incorporate this new thing. And it tends to produce a kind of "small self" effect: the normal preoccupations of your ego recede, and you feel part of something larger.

**Andrew Huberman:** The SpaceX rocket catch was awe-inspiring for me precisely in that way.

**Laurie Santos:** And I think that's a wonderful example of what Keltner would call human-generated awe—awe at what humans can do. In addition to natural awe—mountains, ocean, starry sky—there's this category of awe that comes from witnessing human creativity, ingenuity, and coordination at a scale that exceeds your expectations. Both are powerful.

What's interesting about awe is that it's not purely pleasant—it's destabilizing. The world becomes a little bigger than you expected, a little more complex. But we tend to find this destabilization positive, probably because it breaks us out of habitual, repetitive patterns of thought and feeling. If you're feeling emotionally flat, seeking out experiences of awe—in nature, in art, in human achievement—is one of the more reliable ways to shake things up.

## [02:48:15] Timescales; Community Engagement & Signature Strengths; Tool: Job Crafting

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to zoom out and think about happiness across timescales—the immediate, the medium-term, and the meaning dimension.

**Laurie Santos:** I think you've identified something really important. Happiness operates across at least three timescales. The shortest is hedonic: moment-to-moment sensory experience and emotion. The medium-term involves narrative—the story we tell about our lives and how we're doing. And the longest is meaning and purpose—what psychologists sometimes call eudaimonic well-being—the sense that you're living in accordance with your values, that your life matters.

**Andrew Huberman:** And these require different strategies.

**Laurie Santos:** They do. For the hedonic level, the tools we've discussed—sensory grounding, delight practice, negative visualization, spacing experiences—are most relevant. For the meaning level, the research points strongly to finding ways to use what positive psychologists call your signature strengths.

**Andrew Huberman:** What are signature strengths?

**Laurie Santos:** The positive psychology researcher Chris Peterson, building on work by Martin Seligman and others, identified a taxonomy of twenty-four character strengths—things like bravery, humor, love of learning, curiosity, kindness, creativity, social intelligence, leadership, fairness, and so on. The idea is that each person has a subset of these that feel most authentically theirs—these are their signature strengths. And the research shows that people who regularly use their signature strengths in daily life report higher well-being and sense of purpose.

There's a great tool for this called job crafting, researched by Amy Wrzesniewski at the University of Pennsylvania. Even in jobs that seem to offer very little flexibility, people find ways to infuse their signature strengths. She has these remarkable stories from hospital janitorial workers—someone in a chemotherapy ward who defined his job not as cleaning up vomit but as making sick people laugh, because his strengths were humor and social intelligence. And a worker in a coma ward who rearranged the artwork and plants every day because her strength was creativity. Around a quarter to a third of those janitorial workers said their job was a calling—because they'd found ways to craft their work around what made them feel alive.

**Andrew Huberman:** Where can people find the signature strengths assessment?

**Laurie Santos:** viacharacter.org is the place—it's free to take the full assessment. And I'd suggest doing it with a friend or partner and comparing results, which leads into what researchers call a "strength date"—planning an activity that exercises your shared strengths.

## [02:56:55] Strength Date, Leisure Time; Tool: Doing for Others, Feel Good Do Good

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell me about using strengths in leisure time specifically.

**Laurie Santos:** We tend to bring our strengths to work—we often self-select into careers where we can use what we're good at. But we forget to bring them to leisure. Instead, leisure often defaults to passive consumption: TV, scrolling, whatever's easy. If you design your leisure time around your signature strengths—the person who values learning goes to a museum, the person who values humor goes to a comedy show, the person who values creativity makes something—leisure becomes much more restorative and meaningful.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned earlier that doing for others is one of the most underrated contributors to happiness. Let's explore that.

**Laurie Santos:** This is one of my favorite findings in all of happiness science, partly because it so dramatically contradicts the cultural narrative. We're told "treat yourself," "self-care," "do what makes you happy." But when you look at what happy people actually do, they are remarkably other-oriented. Controlled for income, happier people donate more to charity. They volunteer more. They help strangers more.

And the causal evidence supports it: when you force people to spend money on others versus themselves—Lara Aknin and colleagues' famous twenty-dollar study—the people who spent on others reported being happier at the end of the day. Spending the same amount of money on yourself produced less happiness than spending it on someone you care about.

**Andrew Huberman:** That really is counterintuitive.

**Laurie Santos:** It is. And I think the reason is that prosocial behavior hits multiple happiness drivers at once: it creates social connection, it gives you a sense of purpose and efficacy, it's often intrinsically motivating, and it generates positive emotion that persists.

## [03:01:42] Tool: Asking for Help

**Andrew Huberman:** There's another prosocial behavior that people often overlook: asking for help.

**Laurie Santos:** Yes! This is one I find particularly moving. When someone asks you for help with something you can genuinely help with, how does it feel? Usually pretty good. You feel useful, competent, connected. You feel like someone trusted you. Asking for help is actually a gift to the helper. When we refuse to ask for help because we don't want to be a burden, we're depriving other people of that good feeling.

**Andrew Huberman:** And many people—especially high-achievers—struggle with this.

**Laurie Santos:** Enormously. There's often a story about self-sufficiency, about not being a burden, about vulnerability. But reframing asking for help as something you're doing for the other person—giving them an opportunity to feel useful and connected—can make it easier. And it's also just practically good advice: other people often want to help and are waiting to be asked.
