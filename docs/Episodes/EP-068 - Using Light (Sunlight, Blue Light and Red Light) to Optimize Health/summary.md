# Episode 68: Using Light (Sunlight, Blue Light & Red Light) to Optimize Health

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman delivers a mechanistic tour of how different wavelengths of light — ultraviolet through visible to near-infrared — affect cells, hormones, the nervous system, and behavior, tracing over a century of phototherapy science back to Niels Finsen's 1903 Nobel Prize. The episode explains how light transduces environmental signals into biological action through three simultaneous channels: electrical (neural), hormonal (endocrine), and direct gene-expression changes inside cells. Specific focus is given to UVB light's role in regulating the melatonin–hormone axis, activating the skin-brain-gonad pathway to raise testosterone and estrogen, suppressing pain via endogenous opioids, and boosting spleen-mediated immune function. Red and near-infrared light (630–1000 nm) are shown to penetrate deep into tissue, energize mitochondria by increasing ATP and reducing reactive oxygen species, reverse age-related vision loss per a rigorous UCL protocol, and preserve neuronal health in skin, eyes, and wound tissue. The episode closes with MIT's GENUS research showing that 40 Hz flickering light entrains brain-wide gamma oscillations, reduces amyloid and tau, and represents a non-invasive frontier for neuroprotection against Alzheimer's disease.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Light acts through three simultaneous biological channels** — electrical signals in the brain, hormonal cascades, and direct gene-expression changes within individual cells. This is why a single light stimulus can affect sleep, hormone levels, immunity, and mood at once. ([00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-using-light-to-improve-health))

2. **Different wavelengths penetrate tissues to different depths** — short-wavelength light (UV, blue) stays near the skin surface; long-wavelength light (red, near-infrared) passes through skin into deeper tissues and even reaches organelles like mitochondria inside cells. ([00:15:38](transcript.md#001538-how-light-penetrates-tissues))

3. **UVB drives a seasonal melatonin calendar** — longer days suppress pineal melatonin output via intrinsically photosensitive melanopsin retinal ganglion cells; shorter winter days extend melatonin duration. This rhythm regulates sleep, bone mass, gonadal function, and immune tone across the year. ([00:28:35](transcript.md#002835-light-seasonality--melatonin))

4. **Skin is an endocrine organ — UVB to skin raises testosterone and estrogen** — A 2021 Cell Reports study confirmed that UVB exposure to skin upregulates p53 in keratinocytes, activating the MC1R pathway and directly increasing sex steroids in both sexes. Testosterone tracked with day length across the calendar year; romantic passion rose after just one to two UV treatments. ([01:02:13](transcript.md#010213-tool-skin-sun-exposure--testosterone))

5. **UVB raises pain tolerance via two parallel pathways** — Skin exposure triggers release of beta-endorphins; eye exposure activates melanopsin cells that signal through the ventral lateral geniculate nucleus to the periaqueductal gray (PAG), releasing enkephalins. Pain tolerance is measurably higher in long-day/summer conditions. ([01:06:13](transcript.md#010613-light--improved-pain-tolerance))

6. **UVB boosts immune function through the spleen** — Light to the eyes activates sympathetic nervous system pathways that trigger spleen deployment of T-cells, B-cells, and cytokines. Wound healing, hair growth, and skin turnover also accelerate in longer-day conditions via this eye-to-sympathetic-to-periphery circuit. ([01:19:44](transcript.md#011944-light--enhanced-immune-function))

7. **Avoid UVB light 10 PM–4 AM — it activates a depression circuit** — The perihabenular nucleus receives input from melanopsin cells and, when activated at night, reduces dopamine output and worsens mood. Even without mood issues, 100 lux of ambient light during sleep increases heart rate, lowers HRV, and raises next-morning insulin resistance (PNAS study). Aim for under 3 lux in the sleep environment. ([01:32:44](transcript.md#013244-light-conditions--sleep-optimization))

8. **Red/near-infrared light (630–1000 nm) energizes mitochondria** — Long-wavelength light penetrates to organelle depth, activates cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV), increases ATP, and reduces reactive oxygen species. This mechanism underlies benefits for skin healing, acne, scarring, and wound repair confirmed in randomized controlled trials. ([01:39:00](transcript.md#013900-infrared-light-skin--wound-healing))

9. **670 nm red light reverses age-related vision loss — but only within 3 hours of waking** — Dr. Glen Jeffery's UCL studies showed 2–3 minutes of 670 nm red light at safe distance, delivered within 3 hours of waking, produced a 22% improvement in Tritan color contrast sensitivity in adults over 40, reduced drusen deposits, and improved photoreceptor ATP recovery. No effect in under-40s; effect lost when exposure occurs later in the day. ([01:49:20](transcript.md#014920-infrared-light--age-related-vision-loss))

10. **Local vs. systemic light exposure produce fundamentally different outcomes** — High-intensity local light damages the epidermis to trigger stem-cell renewal at that site; systemic illumination of large skin areas plus the eyes activates hormone, immune, and pain pathways body-wide. Choosing the right target area and intensity for the intended outcome matters. ([01:26:18](transcript.md#012618-light-therapies-local-vs-systemic-exposure))

11. **Seasonal Affective Disorder requires 10,000 lux morning light therapy** — A bright-light box (10,000 lux) for 20–30 minutes immediately upon waking is the evidence-based protocol. A low-cost alternative is a 930–1,000 lux drawing panel used throughout the day. Even overcast outdoor light vastly exceeds indoor artificial sources. ([01:12:48](transcript.md#011248-tools-sunlight-uvb-blue-light-blockers-seasonal-affective-disorder-sad))

12. **Red light is the safest choice for night-shift work or all-nighters** — Dim red light (not blue or white) maintains alertness without suppressing melatonin and without triggering a late-evening cortisol spike, which is associated with depression. ([02:05:22](transcript.md#020522-infrared-light-at-night-shift-work))

13. **40 Hz flickering light (GENUS) drives brain-wide gamma oscillations and is neuroprotective** — MIT's Li-Huei Tsai lab showed that 40 Hz visual flicker entrains gamma activity beyond the visual cortex via the thalamus, reducing amyloid plaques and phosphorylated tau in Alzheimer's models. Clinical trials in humans are ongoing; protocol dosing not yet established for healthy individuals. ([02:08:35](transcript.md#020835-light-flicker-phototherapy--neuroprotection))

14. **Melatonin supplementation is high-risk — the seasonal rhythm matters more than the molecule** — It is the rise and fall of melatonin across seasons, not a static nightly dose, that provides antioxidant, anti-cancer, and regulatory effects. Most supplements are super-physiological. Melatonin affects bone mass, gonadal function, placental development, and CNS state — approach supplementation with extreme caution, especially during pregnancy. ([00:33:40](transcript.md#003340-melatonin-regulatory--protective-effects))

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight Exposure
- **What:** Daily outdoor light to eyes and skin within the first 1–2 hours of waking to calibrate melatonin rhythm, mood, and cortisol timing.
- **How:** Step outside without sunglasses; do not look directly at the sun. Eyeglasses and contacts are fine — they focus light onto the melanopsin cells. Even overcast skies provide far more photons than indoor artificial lighting. Never substitute a window or windshield — glass filters UVB.
- **When:** Daily, year-round. Increase duration in winter. Blue-light blockers should not be worn outdoors in the morning.
- **Ref:** [00:38:49](transcript.md#003849-tools-optimizing-melatonin-levels)

### UVB Skin Exposure for Hormones (Testosterone / Estrogen)
- **What:** Expose large skin areas (torso, arms, legs) to direct sunlight or a UVB source to activate the p53-keratinocyte pathway and raise sex steroids in both sexes.
- **How:** 20–30 minutes, 2–3 times per week, in minimal culturally appropriate clothing — short sleeves and shorts are sufficient. No hat. Avoid sunscreen on exposed areas during this window unless skin cancer risk is elevated. Do not sunbathe to the point of burning.
- **When:** Year-round; prioritize spring/summer. Use a UVB lamp in winter for northern latitudes.
- **Ref:** [01:02:13](transcript.md#010213-tool-skin-sun-exposure--testosterone)

### UVB Exposure for Chronic Pain
- **What:** Regular UVB exposure to skin and eyes to elevate endogenous opioids (beta-endorphins, enkephalin) via the PAG pathway and raise pain tolerance.
- **How:** Same 20–30 minute protocol as hormone exposure, 2–3x per week. Consistent practice compounds the effect; single sessions provide some benefit but ongoing protocol is superior.
- **When:** Ongoing; not a one-session intervention.
- **Ref:** [01:11:55](transcript.md#011155-protocol-sun-exposure--chronic-pain)

### Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) Light Box
- **What:** Bright artificial light to offset winter depression, low energy, and melatonin dysregulation.
- **How:** 10,000 lux light box for 20–30 minutes immediately upon waking, positioned at eye level or above — do not look directly at the lamp. Low-cost alternative: 930–1,000 lux drawing panel used throughout the day.
- **When:** Fall and winter, or any period of insufficient natural daylight.
- **Ref:** [01:12:48](transcript.md#011248-tools-sunlight-uvb-blue-light-blockers-seasonal-affective-disorder-sad)

### UVB Supplementation for Immune Function (Winter)
- **What:** Deliberate UVB skin exposure during winter to maintain spleen-mediated immune output and faster wound healing.
- **How:** 20–30 minutes on skin using a dedicated UVB lamp (verify it emits true UVB, not just visible light), 2–3 times per week.
- **When:** October through March in the northern hemisphere; adjust by latitude.
- **Ref:** [01:23:30](transcript.md#012330-tool-light-during-winter-months)

### Mood Optimization via Daytime Light and Night Avoidance (Hattar Protocol)
- **What:** Maximize daytime UVB and broad-spectrum light to eyes and skin to sustain dopamine, serotonin, and endorphin tone; strictly avoid light 10 PM–4 AM to prevent perihabenular-driven dopamine suppression.
- **How:** Get as much safe outdoor light as possible from morning through afternoon. At night, use dim red or amber light only; use blue-light-blocking glasses (orange lens) if using screens. Keep sleep environment below 3 lux — verify with the free Light Meter app.
- **When:** Daily practice; most critical in winter and for anyone prone to low mood or depression.
- **Ref:** [01:28:54](transcript.md#012854-tool-improving-mood-timing-of-natural--artificial-light)

### Red Light Therapy for Vision (670 nm, Early Morning)
- **What:** Brief deep-red light exposure to recharge photoreceptor mitochondria and reverse age-related color contrast sensitivity loss.
- **How:** View a 670 nm red light panel or device at a comfortable, non-painful distance (~30 cm) for 2–3 minutes. Blinking is allowed; indirect/diffuse gaze is fine. Most commercial red-light panels are too bright to look at directly — set at safe distance. Must occur within the first 3 hours of waking; do not use at night.
- **When:** Once per week is sufficient per Jeffery's studies. Primarily beneficial for adults over 40.
- **Ref:** [01:59:36](transcript.md#015936-tools-infrared-panels-morning-exposure)

### Red/Near-Infrared Panel for Skin and Wound Healing (LLLT)
- **What:** Low-level light therapy (LLLT) using red (630–700 nm) and near-infrared (800–1000 nm) to improve acne, scarring, wound healing, and skin collagen via mitochondrial activation in sebaceous glands and stem cells.
- **How:** Position panel 15–30 cm from skin; follow manufacturer guidelines for session duration (typically 10–20 minutes). Use early in the day. Note: infrared saunas generally do not reach sufficient temperature to replicate the specific mechanisms shown in LLLT skin studies.
- **When:** As needed for healing; daily use acceptable for maintenance per LLLT literature.
- **Ref:** [01:46:00](transcript.md#014600-infrared-light-therapy--skin-low-level-laser-light-therapy-lllt)

### Red Light for Shift Workers / Night Alertness
- **What:** Use dim red light instead of white or blue-spectrum light during night shifts, all-nighters, or late-night childcare to maintain alertness without suppressing melatonin or spiking cortisol.
- **How:** Replace overhead white/blue lighting with dim red bulbs or panels. As dim as possible while still completing necessary tasks.
- **When:** Any required wakefulness between approximately 10 PM and 4 AM.
- **Ref:** [02:05:22](transcript.md#020522-infrared-light-at-night-shift-work)

### 40 Hz Flicker (GENUS) for Neuroprotection
- **What:** Exposure to 40 Hz flickering light to entrain brain-wide gamma oscillations, reduce amyloid and tau accumulation, and support cognitive health.
- **How:** View a 40 Hz visual flicker source (dedicated device or screen). Animal studies used ~1 hour per day; human optimal dosing is not yet established. Do not use if prone to epilepsy — flickering lights can trigger seizures.
- **When:** Experimental — monitor ongoing clinical trial results before adopting a fixed protocol.
- **Ref:** [02:08:35](transcript.md#020835-light-flicker-phototherapy--neuroprotection)

## People Mentioned

**Niels Finsen** — Icelandic-Danish physician awarded the 1903 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for pioneering phototherapy (concentrated UV light) to treat lupus vulgaris; established the scientific foundation for modern light-based medicine. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Finsen))

**Glen Jeffery** — Professor of neuroscience at University College London; leading researcher on red and near-infrared light for restoring retinal and neuronal function; published the key 670 nm studies on age-related vision decline, mitochondrial ATP recovery, and drusen reduction. ([UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/ion/people/glen-jeffery))

**Samer Hattar** — Director of the Chronobiology Unit at the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH); world-leading expert on light's impact on mood, circadian rhythms, and appetite; co-discoverer of intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs). Recommends maximum safe daytime light exposure and strict light avoidance at night for mood and metabolic health. Previously a guest on Huberman Lab. ([NIMH profile](https://www.nimh.nih.gov/research/research-conducted-at-nimh/principal-investigators/samer-hattar))

**Li-Huei Tsai** — Professor at MIT and director of the Picower Institute for Learning and Memory; lead researcher on GENUS (gamma entrainment using sensory stimulation); demonstrated that 40 Hz light flicker drives brain-wide gamma oscillations and reduces Alzheimer's disease markers in animal models and early human trials. ([MIT profile](https://picower.mit.edu/li-huei-tsai))
