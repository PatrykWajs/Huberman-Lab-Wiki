## [00:00:00] Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are going to talk all about attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, or ADHD.

Just a quick reminder that anytime we discuss a psychiatric disorder, it's important that we remember that all of us have the temptation to self-diagnose or to diagnose others. The clear and real diagnosis of ADHD really should be carried out by a psychiatrist, a physician, or a very well-trained clinical psychologist. So right now the current estimates are that about 1 in 10 children and probably more have ADHD. Fortunately about half of those will resolve with proper treatment, but the other half typically don't.

The other thing that we are seeing a lot nowadays is increased levels of ADHD in adults. For sake of today's discussion, attention, focus and concentration are essentially the same thing. So people with ADHD have trouble holding their attention.

## [00:01:10] ADHD Challenges, Attention, Impulsivity, Hyperfocus, Time Perception, Working Memory

What is attention? Well, attention is perception. It's how we are perceiving the sensory world. For instance, right now you're hearing sound waves, you are seeing things, you are sensing things against your skin, but you are only paying attention to some of those. The ones that you're paying attention to are your perceptions — so if you hear my voice, you are perceiving my voice and not paying attention to your other senses at the moment.

So attention and focus are more or less the same thing, but impulse control is something separate because impulse control requires pushing out or putting the blinders on to sensory events in our environment. It means lack of perception — impulse control is about limiting our perception. People with ADHD have poor attention and they have high levels of impulsivity. Yes, they are distractable, they are impulsive, they are easily annoyed by things happening in the room, and they sometimes have a high level of emotionality as well.

However, people with ADHD can have a hyperfocus — an incredible ability to focus on things that they really enjoy or are intrigued by. This is a very important point because people with ADHD have the capacity to attend, but they can't engage that attention for things that they don't really want to do. People with ADHD often run late and often procrastinate, but what's interesting is that if they are given a deadline, they actually can perceive time very well, and they often can focus very well if the consequences of not completing a task or not attending are severe enough.

The other thing that people with ADHD have real trouble with is so-called working memory. People with ADHD often can have a terrific memory for past events and can remember upcoming events quite well, but one aspect of memory in particular — working memory — is often disrupted. Working memory is the ability to keep specific information online, to recycle it in your brain over and over again so that you can use it in the immediate or short term. People with ADHD tend to lose the ability to remember things they just need to keep online for anywhere from 10 seconds to a minute or two.

In general, the full picture is challenges with attention and focus, challenges with impulse control, getting annoyed easily, having an impulsivity, being unable to stay on task, time perception being off, and having a hard time with anything that's mundane. But people with ADHD are also able to obtain heightened levels of focus — even hyperfocus — for things that are exciting to them and that they really want to engage in.

## [00:05:22] Dopamine & Focus; Default Mode Network & Task Network

So let's drill into this issue of why people with ADHD actually can focus very intensely on things that they enjoy and are curious about. Enjoyment and curiosity are just the way that we describe our human experience of liking things and wanting to know more about them. But from a neurobiological perspective, they have a very clear identity and signature, and that's dopamine.

Dopamine is released from neurons — it's what we call a neuromodulator. Dopamine creates a heightened state of focus, it tends to contract our visual world, and it tends to make us pay attention to things that are outside and beyond the confines of our skin — what we call exteroception. Dopamine when it's released in our brain tends to turn on areas of our brain that narrow our visual focus and our auditory focus, creating a cone of auditory attention that's very narrow and a tunnel of visual attention that's very narrow. Whereas when we have less dopamine, we tend to view the entire world, see the whole scene, and hear everything all at once.

We're moving away from attention as this kind of vague ambiguous term and giving it a neurochemical identity — dopamine — and a neural circuit identity. Two general types of neural circuits that dopamine tends to enhance are: the default mode network, which is active when we're not doing anything and just sitting idle at rest; and the task networks, the networks of the brain that make you goal-oriented. These are a completely different set of brain areas, but the default mode network and these task networks are communicating with one another in very interesting ways.

The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, the posterior cingulate cortex, and the lateral parietal lobe are three brain areas that normally are synchronized in their activity. In a person with ADHD, or even a person who hasn't slept well, what you find is the default mode network is not synchronized — these brain areas are just not playing well together. In a person without ADHD, the task networks and the default mode networks are going in a kind of seesaw fashion — they are anti-correlated. In a person with ADHD, they actually tend to be more correlated, more coordinated. Brain imaging studies confirm that when somebody gets better when treated for ADHD, or when they age out of ADHD, the default mode networks and the task networks tend to become anti-correlated again.

What dopamine is doing in this context is acting like a conductor — saying this circuit should be active, then that circuit should be active. In ADHD, there's something about the dopamine system that is not allowing it to conduct these networks and keep them out of phase, anti-correlated.

## [00:10:15] ADHD & Low-Dopamine Hypothesis, Stimulants, Sugar

In the year 2015, an important paper came out and it formalized the so-called low dopamine hypothesis of ADHD. It turns out that if dopamine levels are too low in particular circuits in the brain, it leads to unnecessary firing of neurons that are unrelated to the task one is trying to do and unrelated to the information one is trying to focus on. When dopamine is low, neurons fire more than they should in these networks that govern attention.

If you look anecdotally at what people with ADHD have done for decades, what you find is that they tend to use recreational drugs or they tend to indulge in non-drug stimulants — things like smoking half a pack of cigarettes and drinking four cups of coffee a day, or using cocaine or amphetamine as a recreational drug. All of those substances increase levels of multiple neurotransmitters, but all have the quality of increasing levels of dopamine in the brain, particularly in the regions that regulate attention and these task-related and default mode networks.

Young children fortunately don't have access to those kinds of stimulants most of the time. But if you look at children — even very young children — with ADHD, they show things like preference for sugary foods, which also act as dopamine-inducing stimulants. For a long time, it was thought that children with ADHD consumed too many sugary foods because they had poor levels of attention and were too impulsive. Knowing what we now know about dopamine, an equally valid idea is that these children and these adults are actually trying to self-medicate by pursuing these compounds.

What happens when somebody with ADHD takes cocaine? It turns out they actually obtain heightened levels of focus — their ability to focus on things other than things they absolutely care intensely about goes up. Likewise, children who consume anything that increases their levels of dopamine tend to be calmer and able to focus more. That dopamine hypothesis is what led to the idea that treating people with dopaminergic compounds would somehow increase their ability to focus.

## [00:14:33] ADHD Prescriptions, Ritalin, Adderall, Stimulants

If you look at the major drugs that were developed and now marketed by pharmaceutical companies for the treatment of ADHD, those drugs have names like Ritalin (methylphenidate), Adderall, and Modafinil, among other derivatives. They all serve to increase levels of dopamine, particularly dopamine in the networks that control task-directed behavior and that coordinate the default mode network and task-related networks.

Ritalin, also called methylphenidate, is very similar to amphetamine — what's typically called "speed" in street drug nomenclature. Adderall is basically a combination of amphetamine and dextroamphetamine. Their net effect in the brain and body is essentially the same — to increase dopamine primarily, but also to increase levels of norepinephrine (also called noradrenaline). So the drugs used to treat ADHD are stimulants that look very much like, in fact nearly identical to, some of the so-called street drug stimulants. However, at the appropriate dosages and working with a quality psychiatrist or neurologist or board-certified MD, many people with ADHD achieve excellent relief with these drugs — not all of them, but many do, especially if these treatments are started early in life.

## [00:16:23] Children, Learning to Focus & ADHD Prescriptions

Now knowing what these drugs are, why prescribe them to children? Children have a brain that's very plastic, meaning it can remodel itself and change in response to experience very quickly compared to adults. Taking stimulants as a child if you are diagnosed with ADHD allows that forebrain task-related network to come online, to be active at the appropriate times. And because those children are young, it allows them to learn what focus is and to sort of follow or enter that tunnel of focus.

By taking a drug, it's creating focus artificially — not because they're super interested in something, but chemically inducing a state of focus. A lot of childhood and school and becoming a functional adult is about learning how to focus even though you don't want to do something. To get some of those answers, Huberman consulted a pediatric neurologist specializing in the treatment of epilepsy and ADHD in kids from age 3 to 21.

Their observation was that they've seen more kids benefit than not benefit from early treatment. Neuroplasticity is greatest in childhood and tapers off after about age 25, but neuroplasticity from age three until age 12 or 13 is exceedingly high. If you have the opportunity to work with a quality physician and treat these things early, these drugs can allow these frontal circuits and task-related circuits to achieve their appropriate levels of functioning, and for kids to learn how to focus in a variety of different contexts.

## [00:19:31] Attentional Blinks, Tool: Improve Focus, Open-Monitoring, Panoramic Vision

Let's talk about what would make us better at focusing and what focusing better really is. What we are specifically going to talk about are what are called attentional blinks. Attentional blinks are really easy to understand if you think about a Where's Waldo task — a visual search for an object that has distinct features but is embedded in an ocean of other things. When you find Waldo or when you identify a visual target, your nervous system celebrates a little bit through the release of neurochemicals, and you pause.

In that moment of pause and mild celebration, you are not able to see another Waldo sitting right next to it. So in attending to something and identifying a visual target, your attention blinked — it shut off for a second. If you see something you're looking for or are very interested in, you are definitely missing other information in part because you're overfocusing on something. This leads to a very interesting hypothesis about what might go wrong in ADHD — maybe people with ADHD are experiencing more attentional blinks than people who do not have ADHD, and there are data now to support this possibility.

What they really need is a property called open monitoring. Your visual system has two modes of processing: it can be highly focused, a soda-straw view; but there's also a property that allows you to dilate your gaze to be in so-called panoramic vision. Panoramic vision is mediated by a separate stream of neural circuits going from the eye into the brain — it's not just a wide-angle view, it also processes things in time more effectively and its frame rate is higher. You can use panoramic vision to access the state of open monitoring, where you are able to attend to and recognize multiple targets.

This is something that can be trained up — people can practice whether or not they have ADHD. What it involves is learning how to dilate your gaze consciously. You can consciously go into open gaze and then contract your field of view as well. Remarkably, just doing that once for 17 minutes significantly reduced the number of attentional blinks that people would carry out — their focus got better in a near-permanent way without any additional training.

## [00:23:31] Blinking, Dopamine & Time Perception, Tool: Visual Focus Training

Let's talk about actual blinks — the sort that you do with your eyelids. Your perception of time is also changed on a rapid, moment-to-moment basis by how often you blink. One study particularly worth highlighting is titled "Time Dilates After Spontaneous Blinking." It examined the relationship between fluctuations in timing and blinking, and what they found is that right after blinks, we reset our perception of time.

What's immediately important about this is that the rate of blinking is controlled by dopamine. So dopamine is controlling attention; blinks relate to attention and focus; and therefore, the dopamine and blinking system is one way that you constantly modulate and update your perception of time — and fortunately it's one that you can control. The basic takeaway is that blinking controls time perception, that levels of dopamine can alter your sense of time, and that blinking and dopamine are inextricably linked and working together to control your attention.

People with ADHD have low dopamine and are not good at managing their time — they tend to run late or are disorganized. Low dopamine leads to underestimating time intervals, so it makes perfect sense that they would be late and lose track of time. This means that children with ADHD, adults with ADHD, or people with normal levels of focus who want to improve their ability to focus can do so through a training that involves learning how often to blink and when and how to keep their visual focus on a given target.

One study entitled "Improvement of Attention in Elementary School Students Through Fixation-Focused Training Activity" found that a short period of focusing on a visual target allowed school children to greatly enhance their ability to focus on other types of information. A significant component of the effect was due to the way that they were controlling the shutters on their eyes — their eyelids — and controlling their blinks. They had kids focus their visual attention on some object that was relatively close, like their hand, for a minute or so, allowing them to blink. It only took a few minutes each day — 30 seconds in one condition, or maybe a minute — then at another station looking a little bit further out and a little bit further out. Before this visual focus task or training, they did a series of physical movements with the kids so that the kids could sort of eliminate or move out some of their desire to move, thereby enhancing their ability to sit still. Your eyelids are there to regulate the amount of information going into your nervous system, to regulate how long you are bringing information in, and whether you are binning time finely or widely — set by how often you blink.

## [00:27:43] Ritalin, Adderall, Modafinil, Side Effects, Tapering

Switching back to the drugs typically used — methylphenidate (Ritalin), Modafinil, Armodafinil, and Adderall. Again, all of these work by increasing levels of dopamine and norepinephrine. It is important to understand the extent to which they all carry more or less the same side effects: high propensity for addiction and abuse. Amphetamines of any kind can cause sexual side effects because they're vasoconstrictors. They also almost all carry cardiac effects — they increase heart rate and have effects on constriction of blood vessels and arteries and veins in ways that can create cardiovascular problems.

The best use of things like Adderall, Modafinil, Armodafinil, and Ritalin is to combine those treatments with behavioral exercises that actively engage the very circuits you're trying to train up and enhance, and then perhaps taper off those drugs so that one can use those circuits without any need for chemical intervention.

## [00:29:32] Omega-3 Fatty Acids, EPA, DHA & Attention Effects; Phosphatidylserine

Despite any controversy that might be out there, it's fair to say that the consumption of omega-3 fatty acids can positively modulate the systems for attention and focus. The question becomes how much EPA and how much DHA is helpful — and it does differ from what's helpful for depression. Reviewing the studies, it appears that a threshold level of 300 mg of DHA turns out to be an important inflection point. Typically fish oils or other sources of omega-3s will have both DHA and EPA. It's typically the EPA that's harder to get at sufficient levels — you have to take quite a lot of fish oil in order to get above the 1,000 or 2,000 milligram threshold to improve mood and other functions. But for attention specifically, 10 studies have explored this in detail, and the most convincing studies point to the fact that getting above 300 milligrams per day of DHA is really where you start to see the attentional effects.

There's another compound, phosphatidylserine, that has been explored for its capacity to improve the symptoms of ADHD. Phosphatidylserine taken for 2 months at 200 milligrams per day was able to reduce the symptoms of ADHD in children. It has not been looked at in adults yet, as far as is known, but this effect was greatly enhanced by the consumption of omega-3 fatty acids. So there are synergistic effects of omega-3 fatty acids and phosphatidylserine.

## [00:31:28] Modafinil, Armodafinil

Modafinil and the closely related drug Armodafinil are gaining popularity both for treatment of ADHD and narcolepsy, but also for communities of people trying to stay awake for long periods of time. It's actively used in the military by first responders, gaining popularity on college campuses, and people are using it more and more as an alternative to Adderall, Ritalin, and excessive amounts of coffee. It does increase focus to a dramatic extent.

Unlike Ritalin and Adderall, Modafinil and Armodafinil are weak dopamine reuptake inhibitors, and that's how they lead to increases in dopamine. This mechanism differs from the stronger dopamine-releasing action of amphetamine-class drugs, which is one reason they carry somewhat different risk profiles.

## [00:32:18] Acetylcholine, Alpha-GPC

Acetylcholine is a neurotransmitter that at the neuron-to-muscle connections — the so-called neuromuscular junctions — is involved in generating muscular contractions for all movements. Acetylcholine is also released from two sites in the brain. There is a collection of neurons in your brain stem that send projections forward like a sprinkler system — diffuse release — residing in an area called the pedunculopontine nucleus (PPN). And then there's a separate collection of neurons in the basal forebrain called nucleus basalis that also floods the brain with acetylcholine, but in a much more specific way — more like a fire hose to a particular location. Those two sources collaborate to activate particular locations in the brain and bring about a tremendous degree of focus to whatever is happening at those particular synapses.

Not surprisingly, drugs that increase cholinergic or acetylcholine transmission will increase focus and cognition. One such compound is alpha-GPC, which is a form of choline that increases acetylcholine transmission. Dosages as high as 1,200 milligrams per day have been shown to offset some of the effects of age-related cognitive decline and improve cognitive functioning. Typically when people use alpha-GPC to study or to enhance learning of any kind, they will take somewhere between 300 and 600 milligrams. You have to check with your doctor and decide if the safety margins are appropriate for you.

## [00:34:21] L-Tyrosine, Dopamine, Preexisting Conditions & Caution

L-Tyrosine is an amino acid that acts as a precursor to the neuromodulator dopamine. The dosing can be very tricky to dial in — sometimes it makes people feel too euphoric, too jittery, or too alert that they are then unable to focus well. Dosage ranges in the evidence are huge, from 100 milligrams all the way up to 1,200 milligrams. It's something that really should be approached with caution, especially for people that have any kind of underlying psychiatric or mood disorder, because dysregulation of the dopamine system is central to many of the mood disorders such as depression but also especially mania, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia.

## [00:35:18] Smartphones & Focus, Tool: Limiting Smartphone Use

Everybody nowadays seems to have a smartphone. They grab our attention entirely, but within that small box of attention, there are millions of attentional windows scrolling by. Just because it's one device we look at does not mean that we are focused — we are focused on our phone, but because the way context switches so fast within the phone, the brain is struggling to leave that rapid turnover of context. The question is whether that sort of interaction on a regular basis leads to deficits in the types of attention we need in order to perform well in work, school, and relationships. The short answer is yes — we are inducing a sort of ADHD.

Whether or not you have ADHD, if you're an adolescent, limiting your smartphone use to 60 minutes per day or less, and if you're an adult, to 2 hours per day or less, is going to be among the very best ways to maintain your ability to focus at whatever level you can. Most of the things that we get recognized for in life — success in every endeavor, whether it's school, relationships, sport, creative works of any kind — are always proportional to the amount of focus that we can bring to that activity.

## [00:37:23] Recap & Key Takeaways

We covered a lot of information about ADHD and the biology of focus and how to get better at focusing. We talked about the behavioral and psychological phenotypes of ADHD — challenges with attention, impulse control, emotionality, time perception, and working memory, alongside the paradox of hyperfocus. We talked about the underlying neural circuitry — the default mode network and task networks, and how dopamine acts as a conductor keeping them anti-correlated in healthy function. We also talked about the neurochemistry and the various prescription drug treatments aimed at increasing dopamine and norepinephrine, and the non-prescription approaches including omega-3 fatty acids, phosphatidylserine, alpha-GPC, L-tyrosine, visual focus training, and reducing smartphone use.
