# Episode 294: Behaviors That Alter Your Genes to Improve Your Health & Performance - Dr. Melissa Ilardo

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Melissa Ilardo, professor of biomedical informatics at the University of Utah and world expert in human genetics and epigenetics, joins Huberman to explore how behavior and environment alter gene expression — both within a lifetime and across generations. The episode centers on two extraordinary free-diving populations — the Bajau sea nomads of Indonesia and the Haenyeo women of South Korea — who have evolved measurably larger spleens, altered thyroid hormone levels, and cardiovascular adaptations through thousands of years of breath-hold diving. Topics include the mammalian dive reflex and its practical activation at home, mate selection via immune-system odor cues (MHC), Denisovan introgression into Homo sapiens enabling Tibetan altitude adaptation, and the role of mindset in producing measurable biological outcomes. The conversation also covers admixture and hybrid vigor, diet as a driver of evolution (lactase persistence, Inuit lipid metabolism), cognitive super-performance and autism genetics, CRISPR ethics, and whether humans are still actively evolving.

## Key Takeaways

- **Everyone with blue eyes descends from a single ancestor.** A single base-pair mutation arose once in history; the trait spread via sexual selection. Green eyes are rarer — likely arising from multiple independent mutational events. [00:02:35](transcript.md#000235-nature-vs-nurture-gene-expression-eye-color)

- **UV light exposure darkens eye color over a lifetime.** Blue-eyed babies have lighter eyes at birth than at age 15 or 80. This is one of the clearest examples of behavior (sun exposure) altering gene expression at the visible surface of the body. [00:02:35](transcript.md#000235-nature-vs-nurture-gene-expression-eye-color)

- **Epigenetic changes triggered by trauma and famine can be inherited.** Molecular marks caused by starvation (e.g., the Dutch Hunger Winter) or refugee stress are passed to offspring — adaptive in the original context but potentially maladaptive once conditions change. [00:10:24](transcript.md#001024-epigenetics-trauma-mutations-hybrid-vigor-mate-attraction)

- **Humans select mates partly by immune-system smell.** Studies show people prefer the scent of partners whose major histocompatibility complex (MHC) genes differ most from their own — unconsciously optimizing offspring immune diversity, exactly as seen in mice. [00:10:24](transcript.md#001024-epigenetics-trauma-mutations-hybrid-vigor-mate-attraction)

- **Humans are still actively evolving; globalization is accelerating it.** Genetic combinations now forming between previously isolated populations produce both novel resilience and novel disease risk. Natural selection operates as long as anything affects reproductive success. [00:15:47](transcript.md#001547-globalization-homo-sapiens-mating-evolution-mutations)

- **Tibetans' high-altitude adaptation came from interbreeding with Denisovans.** An archaic hominid gene variant was latent in the ancestral population; it became strongly advantageous only after those humans moved to extreme altitude — illustrating that "standing variation" can drive rapid evolution once the right environment appears. Svante Paabo's ancient DNA work even found a first-generation Homo sapiens / archaic hominid individual, suggesting such interbreeding was common. [00:15:47](transcript.md#001547-globalization-homo-sapiens-mating-evolution-mutations)

- **The mammalian dive reflex produces a ~10% increase in available oxygen** by contracting the spleen (releasing stored red blood cells), slowing heart rate, and vasoconstricting the extremities. The primary trigger is stimulation of vagal nerve fibers in the face by cold water (~10°C / 50°F) during a breath hold. The boost is active only during the breath hold — the spleen reuptakes red blood cells on surfacing. The reflex is present in all mammals (even trained mice) and predates modern human evolution. [00:35:36](transcript.md#003536-mammalian-dive-reflex-oxygen-spleen-cold-water-face-exercise)

- **The spleen also contracts during exercise** — which may explain the massive spleens of horses and greyhounds, and why some oxygen benefit occurs without water. Training-induced spleen enlargement has been reported in recruited non-divers, but Ilardo's data show no size difference between Bajau divers and non-divers, indicating genetic dominance over training effect in that population. [00:35:36](transcript.md#003536-mammalian-dive-reflex-oxygen-spleen-cold-water-face-exercise)

- **The Bajau have spleens ~50% larger than neighboring non-diving populations — including Bajau non-divers.** This genetic origin means the larger spleen acts as a "biological scuba tank." The relevant gene variant (PDE10A) correlates with higher-than-average thyroid hormone levels and increased red blood cell count via an erythropoietin-independent mechanism — confirmed also in European carriers of the same variant. [00:46:00](transcript.md#004600-free-diving-spleen-thyroid-hormone-performance-enhancement)

- **The Haenyeo (Korean female free divers) show two distinct adaptations.** First, a trained ability: heart rate drops >40 bpm within 15 seconds of diving — seen only in active divers, not non-diving Haenyeo. Second, a genetic variant lowering diastolic blood pressure during diving — likely protecting against hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (preeclampsia) caused by repeated breath-hold apnea while pregnant. The average Haenyeo diver is ~70 years old; the oldest in Ilardo's study was 81. [01:05:07](transcript.md#010507-korean-female-free-divers-adaptations-cardiovascular-pregnancy)

- **Diving through pregnancy drove fast evolution in the Haenyeo.** Any selection pressure acting on pregnant women eliminates two generations simultaneously if no protective variant exists — making pregnancy a uniquely powerful accelerant for natural selection. Preeclampsia was historically a death sentence; awareness and treatment are relatively recent. [01:05:07](transcript.md#010507-korean-female-free-divers-adaptations-cardiovascular-pregnancy)

- **Diet is one of the strongest drivers of natural selection in humans.** Lactase persistence (digesting milk past infancy) evolved independently in African and European populations. Greenlandic Inuit evolved enhanced ability to metabolize high-lipid marine-mammal diets to prevent cardiovascular disease. The drive to secure protein and fat likely shaped far more of the human genome than recognized. [01:03:20](transcript.md#010320-human-evolution-diet-lactase-fat)

- **Moken children see better underwater than European children** by constricting the pupil to a very small aperture. A researcher later trained European children to reach equivalent performance — but Ilardo argues this does not disprove a genetic advantage; trainability to the same endpoint does not mean no initial advantage existed. [01:19:03](transcript.md#011903-free-diving-underwater-vision-super-performers-genetics)

- **Cognitive traits like hyperfocus (associated with autism spectrum) may be genetically selected for in high-STEM environments.** Princeton data showed engineering students had significantly more family members with autism than average. Oliver Sacks noted people with Tourette syndrome may have faster processing speeds. Variation disadvantageous in one context can confer real advantage in another; most people fall on a continuum rather than into hard categories. [01:25:01](transcript.md#012501-cognitive-performance-autism-creativity-genetic-determinism-mindset)

- **Genetic determinism is dangerous and biologically self-fulfilling.** In a study, people falsely told their genetics made them "good" or "poor" training responders showed measurable molecular differences aligned with what they were told — not their actual genotype. Mindset and belief interact with biology at a molecular level. [01:25:01](transcript.md#012501-cognitive-performance-autism-creativity-genetic-determinism-mindset)

- **CRISPR is currently too imprecise for routine human embryo editing.** Off-target editing effects remain a concern. The ethical line between correcting a defect and enhancement is murky — some "defects" (e.g., blindness) are not considered defects by those who have them. He Jiankui edited CCR5 in human embryos in 2018 and was imprisoned; the international community condemned the act as premature. Deep embryo sequencing (e.g., Orchid) is already commercially available, raising access-equity questions. [01:36:30](transcript.md#013630-genetics-ethics-crispr-embryo-genetic-screening)

- **Admixture does not imply separate species.** Even in "superhuman" diving populations, the genetic differences reduce to single or small sets of base-pair variants out of 3.5 billion. Ilardo's position: no living human population approaches the threshold for separate species classification. [01:44:36](transcript.md#014436-admixture-genetics-are-humans-a-single-species)

## Tools & Protocols

### DIY Mammalian Dive Reflex Activation

- **What:** Trigger spleen contraction to release stored red blood cells (~10% oxygen boost) and slow heart rate via vagal nerve stimulation.
- **How:** Hold your breath and submerge your face in a bowl of cold water (~10°C / 50°F). Duration = as long as you can hold your breath comfortably. Do not hyperventilate beforehand — this removes the CO2 warning signal and can cause blackout with no warning. Do not do alone.
- **When:** No established clinical protocol for frequency or duration. Effect is active only during the breath hold; spleen reuptakes red blood cells immediately on surfacing.
- **Ref:** [00:35:36](transcript.md#003536-mammalian-dive-reflex-oxygen-spleen-cold-water-face-exercise)

### Free Diving Safety

- **What:** Principles that prevent shallow-water blackout and drowning during breath-hold activities.
- **How:** Never breath-hold swim alone. Never hyperventilate before a dive. The urge to breathe is a CO2 signal — oxygen can fall to blackout level silently. Learn from a certified instructor (e.g., Mark Healey) who teaches land-based technique first.
- **When:** Before any breath-hold water activity, especially for beginners.
- **Ref:** [00:25:28](transcript.md#002528-sea-nomads-bajau-moken-groups-free-diving-dangers-gasp-reflex)

### Cold-Water Swimming for Healthspan

- **What:** Regular low-impact, full-body swimming including in cold water.
- **How:** Anecdotally supported by Haenyeo populations (average age ~70, active to 80s+) and reported cases of extreme longevity among regular swimmers. Haenyeo historically dove without wetsuits in very cold water. No controlled longevity trial exists.
- **When:** Ongoing; treat as promising but unproven for longevity specifically.
- **Ref:** [00:55:17](transcript.md#005517-female-free-divers-haenyeo-cold-water-age-protein)

### Mindset as a Biological Intervention

- **What:** Framing genetic potential positively produces measurable physiological improvements in training response.
- **How:** In a study, people told they were genetically "good responders" showed better molecular biomarker outcomes than people told they were poor responders — with no actual genotype difference between groups. Applies to how parents, coaches, and educators communicate capability to children. Avoid telling anyone (including yourself) that genes limit potential in a given domain.
- **When:** Relevant whenever genetic information — or family appearance as a proxy — is used to set expectations about performance or capability.
- **Ref:** [01:25:01](transcript.md#012501-cognitive-performance-autism-creativity-genetic-determinism-mindset)
