## [00:00:20] Longevity, Anti-Aging, Aging as a Disease

**Andrew Huberman:** I just want to start off with a very simple question: What is the difference between longevity, anti-aging, and aging as a disease? Because I associate you with this statement, "Aging is a disease."

**David Sinclair:** Right. Well, so longevity is the more academic way we describe what we research. Anti-aging is kind of the same thing, but it's got a bad rap because it's been used by a whole bunch of people that don't know what they're talking about. So I really don't like that term, anti-aging. But aging as a disease and longevity are perfectly valid ways to talk about this subject.

**David Sinclair:** When I started my research here at Harvard Medical School, it was considered, if there's something that's wrong with you and it's a rare thing — has to be less than 50% of the population — that's definitely a disease. And then people work their whole lives to try and cure that condition. So I looked up what's the definition of aging, and it says well, it's a deterioration in health and sickness, and you can die from it, typically you do — something that sounds pretty much like a disease. But the caveat is that if more than half the population gets this condition, aging, it's put in a different bucket. First of all, that's outrageous because it's just a totally arbitrary cutoff.

**David Sinclair:** Think about this: we're ignoring the major cause of all these diseases. Aging is 80% to 90% the cause of heart disease, Alzheimer's. If we didn't get old and our bodies stayed youthful, we would not get those diseases. And actually, what we're showing in my lab is if you turn the clock back in tissues, those diseases go away. So, aging is the problem. And instead, through most of the last 200 years, we've been sticking band-aids on diseases that have already occurred because of aging, and then it's too late. One is we want to slow aging down so we don't get those diseases, and when they do occur, don't just stick a band-aid on — reverse the age of the body, and then the diseases will go away.

## [00:02:27] Causes of Aging; Epigenome & Genes

**Andrew Huberman:** Can we point to one specific general phenomenon in the body that underlies aging?

**David Sinclair:** Fortunately, during the 2000s, we settled on eight or nine major causes of aging. These eight or nine causes, at least for the first time, allowed us to come around and talk together. We put them on a pizza so everyone got equal slices, but I think that there's one slice of the pizza that is way larger than the others. And we can get to that, but that's the information in the cell that we call the epigenome.

**Andrew Huberman:** Well, tell us a little bit more about the epigenome, and frame it for us, and then we'll get into ways that one can adjust the epigenome in positive ways.

**David Sinclair:** Yeah, so in science what I like to do — I'm a reductionist — is to boil it down, and I actually ended up boiling aging down to an equation, which is the loss of information due to entropy. It's a hard thing to overcome the second law of thermodynamics, that's fair. But this equation really represents the fact that aging is a loss of information in the same way that when you Xerox something a thousand times you'll lose that information, or you try to copy a cassette tape, or even if you send information across the internet, some of it will get lost. That's what I think is aging.

**David Sinclair:** There are two types of information in the body. There is the genetic information, which is digital — A, T, C, G, the chemical letters of DNA — but there's this other part of the information in the body that's just as important, it's essential, and that's the systems that control which genes are switched on and off, in what cell, at what time, in response to what we eat, et cetera. It turns out that 80% of our future longevity and health is controlled by this second part, the epigenetic information, the control systems. I liken the DNA to the music that's on a DVD or a compact disc. And then the epigenome is the reader that says, "Okay, in this cell, we need to play that set of songs, and in this other cell, we have to play a different set of songs." But over time, aging is the equivalent of scratching the CD and the DVD so that you're not playing the right songs, and cells, when they don't hear the right songs, they get messed up and they don't function well. That is what I'm saying is the main driver of aging, and these other hallmarks are largely manifestations of that process.

## [00:04:53] CD & Scratches Analogy, DNA, Silencing & Expressing Genes

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the scratches that you're referring to?

**David Sinclair:** So, DNA is six feet long per cell — if you join your chromosomes together you get six feet per cell, enough to go to the moon and back eight times in your body. And it has to be wrapped up to exist inside us, but it's not just wrapped up willy-nilly; it's wrapped up very carefully in ways that dictate which genes are switched on and off. When we're developing in the embryo, the cell marks the DNA with chemicals that say, "Okay, this gene is for a nerve cell. You, cell, will stay a nerve cell for the next 100 years, if you're lucky. Don't turn into a skin cell. That would be bad." Those chemicals — one's called methylation — those little methyls mark which songs get played for the rest of your life.

**David Sinclair:** What we're saying is that the body controls the genome through the ability to mark the DNA and then compact some parts of it, silence those genes, don't read those genes, and open others that should stay open. That pattern of genes that are silent and open is what dictates the cell's type, the cell's function. The scratches are the disruption of that — genes that were once silent, involving skin, start coming on in the brain where they shouldn't. Cells over time lose these structures, lose their identity, they forget what they're supposed to do, and we get diseases. We call that aging, and we can measure it — in fact, we can predict when somebody's going to die based on the changes in those chemicals.

## [00:06:44] Physical Appearance & Aging

When they're 70, they still look 50, or less. ## [00:07:36] Childhood Development & Aging, Horvath Clock, Accelerate Aging

**Andrew Huberman:** So I started off in developmental neurobiology, and one of the things I learned early on is that development doesn't stop at age 12, or 15, or even 25, that your entire life is one long developmental arc. The early portion of infancy, and especially puberty, seem like especially rapid stages of aging. Are periods of immense vitality the same periods when we're aging faster?

**David Sinclair:** Yes. Really good question. So, those chemicals we can measure — it's also known as the Horvath clock, the biological clock. It's separate from your chronological age. There are some people that are 10–20 years younger than other people biologically. It turns out if you measure that clock from birth, or even before birth, there's a massive increase in age based on that clock early in life. You have accelerated aging during the first few years of life, and then it goes linear towards the rest of your life.

**David Sinclair:** There's another interesting thing: we're finding that the genes that get messed up — that get scratched — leading to aging are those early developmental genes. They come on late in life and just mess up the system, and they seem to be particularly susceptible to those scratches. So what's causing the scratches? We know of a couple of things. One is broken chromosomes, DNA damage, particularly cuts to the DNA. If you have an X-ray, a cosmic ray, or even if you go out in the sun, you'll get broken chromosomes — that accelerates the unwinding of those beautiful DNA loops. We can actually do this to a mouse, accelerate that process, and we get an old mouse 50% older: it has a bent spine, kyphosis, gray hair, old organs. So we now can control aging in the forward direction. The other thing that accelerates aging is massive cell damage or stress — we pinched nerves and saw that their aging process was accelerated as well.

## [00:10:13] Rates of Puberty & Aging, Growth Hormone

## [00:11:50] Fasting, Calorie Restriction & Longevity, Sirtuins, Insulin & Glucose

**Andrew Huberman:** So, the first category relates to food, blood sugar, insulin. Fasting is good for us — but rarely do I hear why. Why is it that having elevated blood sugar, glucose, and insulin ages us more quickly, and/or why is it that having periods of low glucose can extend our lifespan?

**David Sinclair:** Well, let's start with what I think was a big mistake — the idea that people should never be hungry. It was based, I believe, on the 20th-century view that you don't want to stress out the pancreas and try to keep insulin levels pretty steady. What we actually found is that when you look at animals — whether it's a dog or a mouse or a monkey — the ones that live the longest, by far 30% longer, and stay healthy, are the ones that don't eat all the time. It was first discovered in the early 20th century, rediscovered in the 1930s when Clive McCay did caloric restriction — he put cellulose in the food of rats so they couldn't get as many calories even though they ate, and those rats lived 30% longer.

**David Sinclair:** It came back in the 2000s in a big way. My lab and others showed that there are longevity genes in the body that come on and protect us from aging and disease. The group of genes that I work on are called sirtuins — there's seven of them. We showed in 2005 in a Science paper that if you have low levels of insulin, and another molecule called insulin-like growth factor, those low levels turn on the longevity genes. One of them that's really important is called SIRT1. But by having high levels of insulin all day — being fed — means your longevity genes are not switched on. So you're falling apart; your epigenome, your information that keeps your cells functioning over time, just degrades quicker. Your clock is ticking faster by always being fed.

**David Sinclair:** The other thing that might be happening by always having food around is that it's not allowing the cell to have periods of rest and re-establish the epigenome. There's also the fact that having low levels of glucose in your body will trigger your major muscles in your brain to become more sensitive to insulin and suck the glucose out of your bloodstream, which is very good. You don't want to have glucose flowing around too much. And that will ward off Type 2 diabetes.

## [00:15:14] Tool: Skip a Meal

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the protocol that people can extrapolate from that?

**David Sinclair:** Well, if there's one thing I could say, I would say definitely try to skip a meal a day. That's the best thing.

**Andrew Huberman:** Does it matter which meal, or are they essentially equivalent?

**David Sinclair:** Well, as long as it's at the end or the beginning of the day, because then you add that to the sleep period where you're hopefully not eating. Beware that the first two to three weeks when you try that, you will feel hungry, and you'll also have a habit of wanting to chew on something. There's a lot of physical components to it. But try to make it through the first three weeks and do without breakfast or do without dinner, and you'll get through it.

## [00:15:50] Longer Fasts & Autophagy, "Deep Cleanse"; Fluids, Electrolytes

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you ever do longer fasts, like 48 hours or 72 hours or week-long fasts?

After two, and actually even better if you go for three days without eating, it kicks in even greater longevity benefits. There's a system called the autophagy system, which digests old and misfolded proteins in the body — macroautophagy is its name. She just put out a big paper that said, "If you trigger this process in an old mouse, it lives 35% longer."

**Andrew Huberman:** When you are fasting, are you also ingesting electrolytes? Some people get light-headed, start to feel shaky, and the addition of sodium, potassium, or magnesium is becoming more in vogue.

**David Sinclair:** Well, it makes sense, but I haven't had a need to do it, so I don't. I drink tea during the day, and coffee when I'm first awake. I don't get the shakes, so I don't fix what's not broken.

## [00:17:28] Sirtuins, Glucose, mTOR & Fasting; Leucine, Tool: Pulsing Behaviors

**Andrew Huberman:** How is it that blood glucose triggers these longevity mechanisms mechanistically? How are glucose and sirtuins actually tethered to one another?

**David Sinclair:** What we now know is that these longevity pathways — these longevity genes — talk to each other. There are systems set up to detect what you're eating. The sirtuins mainly respond to sugar and insulin, and then there's this other system called mTOR, which is sensing how much protein or amino acids are coming into your body. They talk to each other. Together, when you're fasting, you'll get the sirtuin activation — which is good for you — and you'll also, through lack of amino acids, particularly three of them, leucine, isoleucine, valine, the body will downregulate mTOR. It's that up sirtuin, down mTOR that is hugely beneficial and turns on all of the body's defenses: chewing up old proteins, improving insulin sensitivity, giving us more energy, repairing cells. These two pathways are the most important for longevity.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned leucine. Many people are actively trying to ingest more leucine on a regular basis to maximize wellness and muscle growth. But what you're saying is that leucine, because it triggers cellular growth, is actually pro-aging in some sense. Is that right?

**David Sinclair:** That's what the evidence suggests. It goes back to the debate about growth hormone or testosterone — all of these activities will give you immediate benefits, but based on the research, it's at the expense of long-term health. My view of longevity, the way I treat my body, is I don't burn both candles. I have one end of the candle lit. I pulse things — I get periods of fasting, then I eat, then I take a supplement, then I fast, then I exercise — taking supplements and eating in the right timing to allow me to build up muscle sometimes. Because you can't just expect to take something constantly and do something constantly for it to work. It's taken me about 15 years to develop my protocol.

**David Sinclair:** What you want to do is get the cells to be perceiving adversity. Our modern life, we're sitting around, we're eating too much, we're not exercising. Our cells respond — they go, "Hey, everything's cool, no problem," and they become relaxed and don't turn on their defenses, and we age rapidly. We can see it in the clock. People who exercise and eat less have a slower-ticking clock. It's a fact.

## [00:21:37] Breaking a Fast, Tools: Do Your Best; Transitions

**Andrew Huberman:** One of the questions I get asked all the time is, "Does ingesting 'blank' break the fast?" People get pretty extreme with this. Your limbic system is going to go, "Hey, do it, do it, do it." But once you get through it, you'll be better. Do it in stages. Don't go cold turkey, because it's a fact that if you try to do a strict diet right out of the gates, you'll almost always fail.

## [00:24:13] Sirtuins, NAD, NMN Supplementation

**Andrew Huberman:** Along the lines of supplementation, what about NMN? How does one incorporate that into a supplementation protocol?

**David Sinclair:** Disclaimer: I don't recommend anything, but I talk about what I do. The sirtuin genes — discovered first in yeast cells when I was at MIT, then in animals as I moved to Harvard. One of my first postdocs, Chaim Cohen, published a paper and found that turning on the Sirtuin 6 gene — remember there's seven — number six is very potent; it extended the lifespan dramatically of mice that he engineered, both males and females. So what you want to do is naturally boost the activity of these sirtuins.

**David Sinclair:** NAD levels are really important for keeping those sirtuin defenses at a youthful level. I take a precursor to NAD called NMN, and the body uses that to make the NAD molecule in one step. I know from measuring dozens of human beings that if you take NMN for about two weeks, you'll double — on average — your NAD levels in the blood. I've studied dozens of people who take NMN at a gram, sometimes two grams, and without any exceptions, if you do what I do, your NAD levels go up by about twofold or more. Anecdotally, if I don't take it, I start to feel 50 years old — I can't think straight. It may be placebo, but who knows? We're now doing very careful clinical trials.

## [00:26:18] Iron & Senescent Cells; Personalize Medicine

**Andrew Huberman:** I was surprised to learn that iron can actually accelerate the aging process in various contexts.

**David Sinclair:** This is a new finding out of Spain — Manuel Serrano's lab has found that excess iron will increase the number of senescent cells in the body. Senescent cells are these zombie cells that accumulate as you get older, and they sit there and cause inflammation mainly, and also can cause cancer. If you get rid of these cells, or never accumulate them, you stay younger — in animals, and there are some really interesting studies out of Mayo Clinic in humans as well.

**David Sinclair:** What I find, for example, is people who are really healthy and live the way I do, and have a diet that's fairly vegetarian but not strict, still have slightly low hemoglobin levels, slightly low iron, slightly low ferritin — but we have super amounts of energy, we're not anemic, and we're getting along great in life. But a doctor who just looks at that might say, "Oh, we need to give you more iron." What I'm getting at is an example of why we need to personalize medicine and look at people over the long run to know what works for them — not just work towards the average human, but work towards what's optimal for that human.

## [00:27:48] Tool: Blood Markers, CRP

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any blood markers that you pay particular attention to?

**David Sinclair:** The first is that you should be tracking things, because one measurement isn't enough — these things vary over time, and if you can have a decade or more of data it's super informative. But there are some main ones: your blood sugar levels, you want to do your HbA1c, which is your average glucose levels over the month; there's CRP for inflammation.

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about C-reactive protein for a second — it's been shown to be an early marker of macular degeneration, of heart disease, a variety of different things.

**David Sinclair:** It is the best marker for cardiovascular inflammation, and also we use it as a predictor of longevity. Its levels go up with mortality. If you have high levels of CRP, you need to get your levels down quickly. The levels usually go up with age and with levels of inflammation. So, the ways to get it down would be to switch the diet, eat less, try to eat more vegetables — you'll find it will come down. There are also drugs that can do it. But CRP — it's actually hsCRP, high-sensitivity CRP. Your doctor will know. Get one of those readings, because a lot of people have normal blood sugar but have high CRP, which is just as bad for you long-term, and can predict a future heart attack.

## [00:31:02] Estrogen, Fasting & Fertility; Aging & Rejuvenation

**Andrew Huberman:** What about estrogen? Women's ovaries change over time. Do you think they can maintain estrogen levels over longer periods of time using some of these same protocols?

**David Sinclair:** I'll tell you the science. If you take a mouse and put it on fasting or caloric restriction up until the point where it should be infertile — so that's about at a year of age, a mouse gets infertile due to aging — then you put them back on regular food, and they become fertile again for many, many months afterwards. The effect of slowing down aging is also on the reproductive system. These pathways that we work on — these sirtuins — are known to delay infertility in female animals.

**David Sinclair:** Case in point: I'm one of the lead authors on a paper where we used NMN — this is the gas, the fuel, the petrol for the sirtuins. We gave old mice, one group 16 months old — remember, they became infertile at 12 — gave them NMN, and about six weeks later they had offspring. They became fertile again, which goes against textbook biology, which is that female mammals run out of eggs. Turns out that's not true. You can rejuvenate the female reproductive system, and even get them to come out of mouse-pause, as we call it. So that's a whole new paradigm in biology as well.

**Andrew Huberman:** What we're learning is that the body has remarkable powers of healing and recovering from illness and injury.
