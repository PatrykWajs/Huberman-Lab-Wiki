The vagus nerve is an extremely interesting nerve because, when we hear the word nerve, we often think of a small connection between one thing and another — wires called axons — but actually the cranial nerve is an extensive pathway, a whole set of connections that link the brain and body. In many respects it looks kind of like its own nervous system within the traditional nervous system of the brain and the spinal cord.

The vagus nerve is so vast, it spreads out through so much of the body, and is connected to so many different brain areas, that it deserves an entire episode. The pathways of the vagus nerve have been charted in more detail in recent years, and the ways to stimulate its actions to achieve those endpoints are now very well understood.

## [00:05:41] Cranial Nerves, Inputs (Afferents) & Outputs (Efferents), Sensory & Motor

The vagus nerve is very different from the other cranial nerves because while it does have connections with areas on the face, head, and neck, it also receives and provides connections to areas within the body — the chest, the abdomen, and even into the lower intestines. The word "vagus" translates more or less to "vagabond," meaning "wandering," because early neuroanatomists saw that this nerve had connections to large areas of the body and head. There is nothing random about the wiring of the vagus nerve, however; it is incredibly precise in where it receives information from and where it provides information to.

The nervous system carries different types of information along different pathways. Sensory information is the kind that converts light into electrical signals at the eyes, or sound waves into your understanding of speech. Motor neurons control the movement of limbs, lips, or airways. Within the body, organs also need motor control — the gut is not a passive tube but contracts and relaxes to move food; even the spleen has a contractile ability to release immune cells into circulation. The vagus nerve is unique in that it is both a sensory pathway and a motor pathway — and this is something that 99% of discussions about the vagus nerve miss. Whether you want the calming effects, energizing effects, immune-enhancing effects, or learning-enhancing effects of vagal activation, you need to know whether you're trying to activate a sensory or a motor pathway.

## [00:12:40] Vagus Nerve & Sensory Pathways, Body & Brain

About 85% of the neurons in the vagus nerve have a cell body sitting in an area near the neck and back of the head called the nodose ganglion. Think of it as a cluster of grapes. Each neuron has an axon extending out to a particular organ of the body — short if it terminates in the neck, longer if it reaches into the chest, and even longer for the lungs, pancreas, liver, spleen, and lower organs. These neurons are bipolar: they have a second axon that extends up into the brain stem, terminating generally in one of three brainstem nuclei.

This architecture means that 85% of the vagus nerve works by having neurons with axons in organs like the spleen, lungs, or heart that collect sensory information about what's going on, and relay that information upward past the cell body and into the brain stem. Sensory information coming from the body includes both chemical information and mechanical information. Mechanical information is straightforward — if your gut is full and distended, you have mechanoreceptors that sense stretch in the gut lining and send that information up to the brain stem. Chemical information includes things like how much serotonin is in the gut, how acidic the gut is, and the oxygen-carbon dioxide ratio from the lungs. In response to all of this sensory information arriving in the brain stem, your brain will change its levels of alertness, prime you to learn better, or even turn on a fever based on what the axons are sensing in the periphery.

## [00:18:30] Sensory Information, Chemical & Mechanical Information

The sensory information coming from the body includes both chemical and mechanical information from organs like the gut and lungs. The gut communicates mechanical stretch — when it is full and distended, mechanoreceptors in the gut lining fire and the signal travels up past the nodose ganglion into the brain stem. Chemical information coming from the gut includes the level of serotonin present, the acidity of the environment, and the presence or absence of particular molecules. These two types of sensory signal are carried by neurons with different receptors — ones that respond to mechanical stretch and others that respond to the chemical milieu.

When your lungs expand and contract as you breathe, that mechanical information is also relayed up through and past the nodose ganglion into the brain stem. The lungs additionally communicate chemical information about the oxygen-carbon dioxide ratio. Understanding that vagal sensory information is both chemical and mechanical is not academic — it determines what interventions will work. If you want to change the activity of the vagus system to calm down, improve immunity, or improve mood, you need to ask whether you are going to do that through a mechanical change or through changing the chemical milieu of a given organ. This distinction explains why some vagal interventions (like extended exhales or fermented foods) work through fundamentally different mechanisms than others.

## [00:23:49] Sympathetic & Parasympathetic Nervous Systems, Vagus Nerve, Tool: Calming & Auricular (Ear) Sensation

Every medical student should know that cranial nerve 10, the vagus nerve, is classified as a parasympathetic nerve. The autonomic nervous system controls your levels of alertness and calm and has two major branches. The sympathetic nervous system is responsible for increasing alertness — everything from alert wakefulness all the way up to a full panic attack. The parasympathetic nervous system is often called the "rest and digest system" and controls digestion, falling asleep, and deep relaxation. Hyperactivation of the parasympathetic can make us sleepy when we don't want to be, or even contribute to coma states. These two branches work in a seesaw — you're always tilted toward one or the other, and they're always both active.

There is a branch of the vagus nerve that comes from the head — specifically from behind the ear and in some deeper components of the ear — carrying sensory information about mechanical pressure or touch. Rubbing behind the ear or inside the outer ear stimulates this branch and does indeed produce a mild calming effect. However, the reality is it doesn't bring your overall level of autonomic arousal way, way down — it brings it down a bit, depending on how active your sympathetic nervous system happens to be. If you're in the middle of a panic attack, it will help a little but will not suddenly produce calm. The vagus nerve is classified as parasympathetic, but it is a misnomer because there are pathways within the vagus that, when activated, make you more alert — not less. This is the myth the episode aims to dispel: activating the vagus nerve does not simply calm you down. It depends entirely on which branch you activate and in what context.

## [00:33:38] Vagus Nerve Motor Outputs

While 85% of the vagus nerve is sensory, the remaining 15% consists of motor outputs coming from brainstem nuclei. These motor pathways don't monitor mechanical or chemical information — they control the organs of the body directly. This is critically important for leveraging the vagus nerve for health, mental performance, physical performance, and accelerated learning. Research is beginning to show that selectively activating these motor pathways can accelerate recovery from stroke. The neurons in these brainstem nuclei have efferents — outputs — back to the various organs of the body, including the heart, throat, and other viscera.

One particularly important motor nucleus is called the nucleus ambiguus. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, located in the left upper front of the skull, has outputs to areas called the cingulate and the insula, which in turn communicate with the nucleus ambiguus. This pathway is the anatomical basis for deliberate, conscious control over heart rate. Nucleus ambiguus contains neurons that project down to the sinoatrial node of the heart, and those neurons are responsible for deceleration of heart rate. Because this pathway receives input from the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — which is involved in deliberate action and planning — it is subject to conscious control. If you decide to activate this deceleration pathway, you can do it.

## [00:36:00] Autoregulation, Improving Heart Rate Variability (HRV) Tools: HR Deceleration

Autoregulation is not just a fancy word for calming down. It is the background process by which the vagus nerve keeps the sympathetic-parasympathetic seesaw from tilting too far toward sympathetic dominance — preventing your heart rate, breathing rate, and alertness from getting dangerously high. It happens constantly during the day and during sleep without requiring conscious attention.

The control by the vagus nerve of the sinoatrial node and heart rate is elegantly coordinated with your breathing. When you inhale, your lungs expand, your diaphragm moves down, and your heart has more space in the thoracic cavity. Blood moves slightly more slowly through the expanded heart, which is sensed by neurons in the heart that signal the sympathetic system to speed heart rate up. Put simply: inhaling speeds heart rate up. The converse is equally true: when you exhale, the lungs deflate, the diaphragm moves up, the heart shrinks slightly, blood moves faster per unit volume, and the brain activates the nucleus ambiguus to release acetylcholine at the sinoatrial node and slow heart rate down. Exhaling slows heart rate down via vagal control.

This means that the physiological sigh — two inhales through the nose followed by a long complete exhale through the mouth — is the fastest way to activate the parasympathetic nervous system. The first inhale is longer and the second is a sharp top-up to maximally inflate all the alveolar sacs, then a long slow exhale empties the lungs. This works through two simultaneous mechanisms: a chemical signal from offloading carbon dioxide (which rapidly reduces arousal) and a mechanical heart deceleration signal. Both arrive in the brain quickly and both promote calm.

To improve HRV chronically — both during waking states and during sleep — deliberately extending your exhale 10–20 times spread throughout the day is one of the most effective and most overlooked interventions available. Each deliberate extended exhale activates the nucleus ambiguus → sinoatrial node pathway. Because this pathway is subject to neural plasticity — use it and it strengthens; neglect it and it weakens — frequent activation during the day trains the pathway to operate more robustly in the background during sleep as well. Research by Nolan Williams' lab at Stanford shows that transcranial magnetic stimulation of the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex produces heart rate deceleration via the same vagal pathway and durably increases HRV even after stimulation ends. The behavioral equivalent is the voluntary extended exhale.

## [00:49:46] Aging, Declining HRV, Health, Activity, Tool

As we age, Heart Rate Variability declines significantly. This decline occurs partly because the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex undergoes atrophy with age, and partly because physical activity levels decrease. People who suffer lesions of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex from strokes, or those with accelerated atrophy from normal aging, show pronounced decreases in HRV. HRV is correlated with a host of positive health outcomes related to brain, body, longevity, and performance. High-intensity interval training is one established behavioral way to keep HRV elevated over time through exercise. The deliberate exhale protocol — pinging the vagal deceleration pathway many times throughout the day — is another, accessible approach that requires no equipment. If you keep this pathway engaged, you can maintain higher HRV as you age, which in turn is associated with numerous positive health outcomes.

## [00:52:31] Tool: Exercise, Increase Alertness for Cognitive & Physical Activity, Motivation

Research from Peter Strick at the University of Pittsburgh, using neural tracing methods in non-human primates confirmed in humans, identified three general groups of brain areas that communicate with the adrenal glands: motor activation areas (upper motor neurons controlling the muscles), cognitive and planning areas (prefrontal cortex), and emotional areas — all capable of triggering adrenaline release from the adrenals. Of these, perhaps the most practically important finding is that moving the large muscles of the body — especially the legs (quadriceps, hamstrings) and trunk muscles — directly causes the adrenals to release epinephrine (adrenaline).

Adrenaline does not cross the blood-brain barrier, so it cannot directly alert the brain. But there is a beautiful circuit that bridges this: adrenaline released by the adrenals binds to receptors on the sensory axons of the vagus nerve itself. The vagus nerve then releases glutamate — an excitatory neurotransmitter — into the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) in the brain stem. The NTS then activates the locus coeruleus, which releases norepinephrine widely across the brain via a sprinkler-system-like distribution. This norepinephrine flood increases alertness and motivation throughout the brain, including areas involved in motivation and the propensity to move.

The practical implication is profound: if you are feeling unmotivated or lethargic and want to exercise — or want to sharpen your brain for cognitive work — moving the large musculature of your body at high intensity (sprinting, resistance training, jumping) triggers a cascade through this adrenal-vagus-locus coeruleus circuit that makes it dramatically more likely you will feel motivated to continue moving and thinking. Notably, neurophysiologists routinely stimulate the vagus to wake up animals or patients who are drifting toward too-deep anesthesia — stimulating the vagus wakes the brain. Conversely, this same pathway is reflexively triggered in emergencies (an intruder, a loud explosion) to immediately alert both body and brain. You can deliberately access this circuit simply by doing high-intensity exercise involving your large muscles.

## [01:06:14] Adult Neuroplasticity & Learning, Acetylcholine, Alpha GPC Nicotine

Adult neuroplasticity requires two prerequisites that distinguish it fundamentally from childhood plasticity: you must be alert and you must be focused. In children, passive exposure to a teacher or environment can change the brain. In adults, passive exposure is insufficient — you need alertness and focus together. Fortunately, if you are alert and focused and undertake incremental learning (going after small bits of plasticity repeatedly over time), you can achieve as much neuroplasticity as observed in childhood; it simply takes longer.

A critical molecule permissive for plasticity is acetylcholine released from a brainstem area called nucleus basalis. When acetylcholine from nucleus basalis floods the brain, it acts as a spotlight on whatever is being attended to — opening a window during which neuroplasticity is substantially more likely. Research by Mike Merzenich and colleagues showed that stimulating nucleus basalis to release acetylcholine while exposing an animal to a sensory stimulus produces enormous, rapid cortical remapping. Pharmacologically, you can enhance this window with Alpha-GPC (a precursor to acetylcholine), huperzine (which inhibits acetylcholine breakdown), or nicotine (which activates nicotinic acetylcholine receptors). Nicotine is extremely habit-forming, increases blood pressure, and is a vasoconstrictor — these are real drawbacks to weigh carefully, particularly in young people. Vaping is absolutely harmful to health.

The non-pharmacological route to stimulating nucleus basalis acetylcholine release goes through the vagus nerve. Electrical stimulation of the vagus nerve increases alertness in the brain via two pathways: the adrenals → vagus → locus coeruleus → norepinephrine route (increasing alertness) and a separate NTS → nucleus basalis route that releases acetylcholine (opening the plasticity window). These findings have allowed stroke patients to recover motor capabilities dramatically faster when vagus stimulation is combined with rehabilitation compared to rehabilitation alone. High-intensity exercise activates this same circuit behaviorally.

## [01:11:48] Tools: High-Intensity Exercise, Increase Alertness, Focus & Learning; Sleep

Because alertness (via locus coeruleus norepinephrine) and focus (via nucleus basalis acetylcholine) are the two prerequisites for adult neural plasticity, and because high-intensity exercise drives both through the vagal circuit, scheduling bouts of cognitive learning in the one to four hours following exercise that leaves you energized but not exhausted represents a powerful, non-pharmacological strategy for neuroplasticity. You do not want to exercise to exhaustion because that will drive a strong parasympathetic rebound that leaves you depleted and cognitively slow.

Actual rewiring of circuits — the physical consolidation of plasticity — occurs not during the learning attempt but during subsequent sleep, particularly deep sleep and REM sleep. Sleep-like states such as non-sleep deep rest and meditation also support this consolidation. This explains why you can struggle with a piano piece or a math concept repeatedly without mastering it, then wake one morning to find the skill consolidated: the trigger happened during waking effortful practice, but the implementation happened in sleep. Therefore, combining high-intensity exercise, focused incremental learning sessions, and high-quality sleep is the full behavioral stack for maximizing adult neuroplasticity via vagal pathways.

## [01:18:14] Serotonin, Gut, Brain & Mood, Depression & SSRIs

Serotonin is a neuromodulator that modulates the activity of many circuits and is critically important for mood. In the gut, it is critically important for gut motility, ease of digestion, and gut health. Ninety percent of the serotonin manufactured in your body is made in the gut, where enterochromaffin cells convert tryptophan (from food) into serotonin through a biochemical pathway requiring several enzymatic steps. This gut serotonin stays in the gut — it does not travel to the brain. A common misconception is that improving gut serotonin automatically improves brain serotonin; that is not how it works.

SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors), which have the net effect of increasing serotonin at synapses, are used to treat depression but are controversial because serotonin is used in multiple brain circuits and side effects are common. For some patients they alleviate certain depression symptoms; for others they produce significant side effects. Whether SSRIs are good or bad is highly patient-dependent. Serotonin is also not the only factor in depression, though elevating serotonin in the brain can increase neuroplasticity, which may allow people with major depression to form new, more adaptive contingencies about life outcomes. Adequate serotonin levels are critical for overall well-being — a sense of being okay with where life is, the ability to lean into effort — for depressed and non-depressed people alike.

## [01:21:34] Serotonin, Improve Mood & Gut Health, Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS), Tools: Low-Sugar Fermented Foods, Tryptophan

Gut serotonin levels powerfully impact brain serotonin levels through the vagus nerve. Serotonin produced by enterochromaffin cells in the gut binds to receptors on the sensory axons of the vagus that innervate the large intestine, small intestine, and stomach. This activates specific vagal afferents that relay the signal up through the nodose ganglion to the NTS, which then sends a powerful signal to the dorsal raphe nucleus in the brain — the structure responsible for releasing the majority of brain serotonin. So high gut serotonin → vagal signal → NTS → dorsal raphe → brain serotonin release.

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), which vexes many people with unpredictable gut distress, is powerfully associated with inadequate levels of gut serotonin. To improve gut serotonin, two inputs are required simultaneously. First, a healthy, diverse gut microbiome: the microbiota produce short-chain fatty acids that are critically required for the enzymatic conversion of tryptophan into serotonin. The best evidence-based way to support this (from research by Justin Sonnenburg and Christopher Gardner at Stanford) is to consume one to four servings of low-sugar fermented foods per day — refrigerated kimchi, sauerkraut, sour pickles, kefir, low-sugar yogurt, and kombucha (noting that kombucha contains small amounts of alcohol). Second, sufficient dietary tryptophan: dairy products, white turkey meat, and a variety of other foods provide this amino acid. Fermented foods improve microbiome diversity only if enough tryptophan is available as a substrate.

## [01:28:49] Mood, Depression, Gut Health & Vagal Signaling, Probiotics

A randomized double-blind controlled trial examined probiotics plus magnesium orotate plus coenzyme Q10 for the treatment of major depressive disorder. Results showed a reduction in major depression symptoms that appeared around the four-week mark and extended to ten weeks before the effect diminished. This suggests that in the short term, improving the gut microbiome environment may support mood improvement, but sustained benefit likely requires ongoing dietary practice combined with other interventions (exercise, adequate sleep, and where appropriate, pharmacologic treatment under a physician's guidance).

The gut microbiota create short-chain fatty acids that are critically involved in converting tryptophan into gut serotonin. Gut serotonin levels are then communicated to the brain by the vagus, stimulating the dorsal raphe to release brain serotonin. This mirrors how alertness levels in the body and brain are matched through the vagus: body activity → vagal signal → brain alertness; and gut serotonin → vagal signal → brain serotonin. You can exert positive control over this axis through dietary choices. For people with low mood or IBS, supplementing with a quality probiotic on occasion (rather than chronically at high doses) can also support this process by improving microbiome diversity.

## [01:32:12] Calming Down via Vagus Nerve, Tool: Neck Peri-Arterial Vagus Stretch

A major branch of the vagus extends from the brainstem, running along the vasculature and musculature deep to the neck muscle (the sternocleidomastoid) that sticks out when you turn your head to one side. This peri-arterial vagus runs along a portion of the neck on both sides. Mechanically stretching this pathway by moving elbows down away from the ears while turning the head up and to one side does activate some of the sensory and motor fibers along this stretch of the vagus nerve. Two neurosurgeon and neurophysiology colleagues confirmed to Huberman that this indeed activates the vagal pathway to some degree — enough to produce a calming parasympathetic effect, though less powerful than electrical stimulation or exhale-based breathing.

The protocol is to sit or lie with palms facing down, elbows at the edge of a table surface, push elbows down and away from the ears, then turn the head up and to the right, feeling a stretch on both sides of the neck. Hold for several seconds, then repeat on the other side — up and to the left. Alternating a few times back and forth does lead to a calmer state following. The majority of the parasympathetic effect of this mechanical activation is on the right-hand side, where the vagal pathway is most accessible. This stretch does not rival the physiological sigh for speed or robustness, but it is anatomically and functionally supported and many people enjoy the neck stretch as a stand-alone practice.

## [01:42:00] Tools: Calming Down, Humming, Extended Exhales

Humming can activate branches of the vagus that innervate the larynx and deeper throat structures, which include neurons from nucleus ambiguus that travel with vagal fibers to the speech machinery of the throat, mouth, tongue, and lips. To engage the calming vagal pathway, you must hum in a specific way: emphasize the "H" part of the hum rather than the "M" part. The H-hum generates vibrations from the back and deeper parts of the throat — where vagal activation is strongest — whereas the M-hum shifts the vibration forward toward the mouth and lips. The goal is to move the vibration downward from the back of the throat, through the neck, into the chest, and ideally into the abdomen and diaphragm. A deep H-hum is also a slow extended exhale — which means it collaterally activates the deceleration pathway through nucleus ambiguus → sinoatrial node, slowing the heart rate. Gargling with water produces a similar vibration at the back of the throat and is also vagally activating.

Singers use extended low-frequency humming to relax the throat before hitting deep notes, working on exactly this same mechanical principle. For personal use, this deep H-hum practiced lying down and letting the vibration travel from throat through chest to belly can produce rapid, noticeable calm. Extended exhales throughout the day — whether through physiological sighs, simple prolonged exhales, or humming — all converge on the same nucleus ambiguus → sinoatrial node deceleration circuit and progressively strengthen that pathway through neural plasticity.

## [01:46:38] Recap

The vagus nerve is a bidirectional superhighway of sensory and motor connections between brain and body. It is not just a calming pathway — it controls heart rate autoregulation (improving HRV), increases brain alertness and motivation through exercise-triggered adrenal-vagal-locus coeruleus signaling, and coordinates gut serotonin levels with brain serotonin and mood via the enterochromaffin-vagal-dorsal raphe axis. Practical tools include: the physiological sigh for fastest calming; deliberate extended exhales spread throughout the day to improve HRV chronically; high-intensity exercise to wake up the brain and open plasticity windows; low-sugar fermented foods and adequate dietary tryptophan to support the gut-brain serotonin axis; and the neck peri-arterial stretch and deep H-hum as additional calming protocols. Electrical vagus nerve stimulation and TMS of dorsolateral prefrontal cortex achieve stronger versions of some of these effects via the same pathways, but require clinical equipment. You have more conscious agency over your vagus nerve than is commonly understood, and understanding the specific pathways is what makes that agency real.
