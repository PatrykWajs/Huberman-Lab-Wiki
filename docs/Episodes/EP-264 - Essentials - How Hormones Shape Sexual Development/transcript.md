## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Hormones, Sexual Development

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. This podcast is separate from my teaching and research roles at Stanford. Today we're going to explore hormones — what they are, how they work, what leads to masculinization or feminization of the brain and body.

What we're trying to do today is really get to the biology, the physiology, the endocrinology, and the behavior. Hormones by definition are a substance, a chemical that's released in one area of the body — typically from something we call a gland, although they can also be released from neurons. They travel and have effects both on that gland but also on other organs and tissues in the body. That differentiates hormones from things like neurotransmitters, which tend to act more locally. Examples of tissues that produce hormones would be the thyroid, the testes, the ovaries, and then of course there are areas of the brain like the hypothalamus and the pituitary which release hormones that cause the release of yet other hormones out in the body.

So let's start with development. Sperm meets egg — this is mammalian reproduction — and that egg starts to duplicate, it starts to make more of itself. It makes more cells and eventually some of those cells become skin, some become brain, some become muscle, some become fingers — all the stuff that makes up the brain and body. In addition, there are hormones that come both from the mother and from the developing baby, the developing fetus, that impact whether or not the brain will be what they call organized masculine or organized feminine. As I say this, I want you to try and discard the cultural connotations or the psychological connotations of what masculinization and feminization are, because we're only centering on the biology.

## [00:01:25] Chromosomal Sex, Gonadal Sex, Hormonal Sex, Morphology

Typically people have either two X chromosomes — and the traditional language around that is that person is female — or an X chromosome and a Y chromosome, and that person will become male. Now it's not always the case: there are cases where it's XXY, where there are two X chromosomes plus a Y chromosome. There are also cases where it's XYY, where there are two Y chromosomes, and these have important biological and psychological impacts.

So the first thing we need to establish is that there is something called chromosomal sex — whether or not there are two X chromosomes or an X and Y chromosome. But the next stage of separating out the sexes is what we call gonadal sex. Typically — not always, but typically — if somebody has testes for their gonads we think of them as male, and if somebody has ovaries we think of them as female, although that's not always the case either.

The transition from chromosomal sex to gonadal sex is a very important distinction; it's kind of a fork in the road that happens very early in development while fetuses are still in the embryo. On the Y chromosome there are genes that have particular functions — they suppress female reproductive organs. On the Y chromosome there's a gene which encodes for something called Müllerian inhibiting hormone, a hormone programmed by the Y chromosome that inhibits the formation of Müllerian ducts, which are an important part of the female reproductive apparatus. Other genes on the Y chromosome like the SRY gene promote the formation of testes while also inhibiting the formation of the Müllerian ducts.

So we have to distinguish between chromosomal sex, gonadal sex, and then what we call hormonal sex — the effects of the steroid hormones estrogen and testosterone and their derivatives on what we call morphological sex, meaning the shape of the baby: the genitalia, the jaw, and all these other things. It's actually quite complicated. It's a long distance from chromosomes to gender identity, and gender identity has a lot of social influences and roles. Just getting from chromosomal sex to gonadal sex, hormonal sex, and morphological sex involves a number of steps.

## [00:06:06] Steroid Hormones, Sexual Characteristics

Hormones generally have two categories of effects: they can either be very fast or they can be very slow. There are hormones like cortisol and adrenaline which act very fast, and then there are hormones like testosterone and estrogen — what we call the sex steroid hormones. These molecules are what's called lipophilic, which just means that they like fatty stuff; they can actually pass through fatty membranes. Because the outside of cells as well as the nuclear envelope — where all the DNA contents are stuffed inside — are made of lipid, these steroid hormones can actually travel into cells and then interact with the DNA of cells in order to control gene expression. So they can change the sorts of things that cells will become, and they can change the way that cells function in a long-term way.

That's actually how the presence of genes like SRY and Müllerian inhibiting hormone lead to reductions — elimination, really — of things like the Müllerian ducts, and promote instead what's called in males the Wolffian ducts, or promote the development of testes rather than ovaries. Hormones have short-term and long-term effects, and the long-term effects are actually related to their effects on genes and how those genes are expressed or repressed. These steroid hormones are exceedingly powerful.

If we're going to have a discussion about masculinization or feminization, you also need to think about the counterpart. It's not just about masculinizing the body or feminizing the body and brain — it's also about demasculinizing the brain in many cases, as a normal biological function of typically XX females, and defeminization — the suppression of certain pathways that are related to feminization of the body and brain. You would think it's straightforward: you have a Y chromosome, you suppress the female reproductive pathway like the Müllerian ducts, you promote the development of testes, and then testes make testosterone and then it organizes the brain male. But it turns out that isn't how it works at all.

## [00:09:10] Primary & Secondary Sexual Characteristics, Dihydrotestosterone (DHT), Kisspeptin

We have to understand that there are effects of testosterone and estrogen on what are called primary sexual characteristics — the ones that you're born with — and secondary sexual characteristics, which are the ones that show up in puberty. These are happening in the brain, body, and spinal cord.

Let's talk about the development of primary sexual characteristics. One of the more dramatic examples comes from the role of testosterone in creating the external genitalia. It turns out that it's not testosterone that's responsible for the development of the penis in a baby with an X and Y chromosome — it's a different androgen. Testosterone is converted in the fetus to something called dihydrotestosterone, and that's accomplished through an enzyme called 5-alpha reductase. Dihydrotestosterone is what we would call the dominant androgen in males. It's responsible for aggression, for a lot of muscular strength, it's involved in beard growth and male pattern baldness. Dihydrotestosterone has powerful effects in determining the genitalia while the baby is still in the embryo. Testosterone is made, and that testosterone gets converted by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase in a little structure called the tubercle that will eventually become the penis.

That baby will then grow up and later during puberty there will be the release of a molecule called kisspeptin — K-I-S-S-P-E-P-T-I-N — which will cause the release of gonadotropin-releasing hormone and luteinizing hormone, which will stimulate the testes to make testosterone. So in puberty, testosterone leads to further growth and development of the penis, as well as pubic hair growth, deepening of the voice — all the secondary sexual characteristics.

There's a very interesting phenomenon published in the journal Science in the 1970s for which now there's a wealth of scientific data. This relates to a genetic mutation where 5-alpha reductase — the enzyme that converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone — doesn't exist; it's mutated. This was first identified in the Dominican Republic. What happens is: a baby is born, and if you were to look at that baby it would look female — there would be very little or no external penis. What was observed is that from time to time that baby, after being raised as a girl, would around the age of 11, 12, or 13 start to sprout a penis. There's actually a name for this — it's called "guevedoces," the translation of which is more or less "penis at 12." In these children, the child is born with testes that are not descended — up in the body. They weren't able to convert testosterone to dihydrotestosterone because they lack the enzyme 5-alpha reductase. As a consequence the primary sexual characteristic of external male genitalia doesn't develop. Then the baby grows up and testosterone starts getting secreted from the testes because kisspeptin in the brain signals through gonadotropin and luteinizing hormone — the testes start churning out testosterone and there's secondary growth of the penis. The point here is that dihydrotestosterone, not testosterone, is responsible for the primary growth of the penis, and that testosterone later is involved in the secondary sexual characteristics like deepening of the voice.

## [00:13:23] Masculinization of Brain, Estrogen

Now this is where the information gets even more interesting and applies to essentially everybody. You might think that testosterone — because it masculinizes the body via secondary sexual characteristics and because dihydrotestosterone masculinizes the primary sexual characteristics — must also masculinize the brain. But the masculinization of the brain is not accomplished by testosterone. It is accomplished by estrogen.

Testosterone can be converted into estrogen by an enzyme called aromatase. There are neurons in the brain that make aromatase and convert testosterone into estrogen. In other words, it's estrogen that masculinizes the XY individual's brain. This has profound effects on all sorts of things — behavior, outlook in the world, etc. Most people don't realize that it's estrogen that comes from testosterone that masculinizes the male brain — not testosterone, nor dihydrotestosterone.

The work I'm guiding a lot of today's conversation on is from the book "Behavioral Endocrinology" by Randy Nelson and Lance Kriegsfeld, true experts in the field. I'm going to talk about some of the work from Tyrone Hayes at UC Berkeley about environmental toxins and their impacts on things like testosterone and estrogen. There are things in our environment — things that people use — that can actually impact hormone levels and sexual development in fairly profound ways.

## [00:14:41] Herbicides, Atrazine, Hormone Effects, Sperm Counts

I want to mention some tools. There are things that are environmental and things that people use that can impact hormone levels and sexual development in fairly profound ways. I want to be very clear: this is not me pulling from some rare journal I've never heard of. This is pulling from textbooks and from federally funded labs at major universities that have been peer-reviewed and published in excellent journals.

I remember way back when I was a graduate student at UC Berkeley in the late '90s — I remember Tyrone Hayes. He was studying frogs and talking about developmental defects in these frogs that live in different waters around California and elsewhere. He identified a substance present in a lot of waterways throughout this country and other countries — which is atrazine. Atrazine causes severe testicular malformations. At sites in western and midwestern sections of the United States, 10 to 92% of male frogs had testicular abnormalities. The most severe testicular malformations were in the testes themselves rather than in the sperm — so it's actually the organ itself, the gonad itself.

It is very well known now that atrazine is in many herbicides. Whereas in the '80s and '90s the discussion around herbicides and their negative effects was considered kind of fringe, now there's very solid data from federally funded labs at major universities showing that indeed many of these herbicides can have negative effects — primarily by impacting the ratios of these hormones in either the mothers or in the testes, altering the testes of the fathers, or through direct effects on developing young animals and potentially humans.

Across human populations, sperm counts are indeed declining. In 1940 the average density of human sperm was 113 million per milliliter of semen. By 1990 this figure had dropped to 66 million per milliliter in the United States and Western Europe. Researchers also estimated that the volume of semen produced by men has dropped 20% in that time. Between 1981 and 1991, the ratio of normal spermatogenesis decreased from 56.4% to 26.9%. A lot of this is happening primarily because of herbicides that are in widespread use. These are going to have profound effects not just on sperm counts but on development and sexual development at the level of the gonads and the brain — because you need testosterone to get dihydrotestosterone for primary sexual characteristics, and you need estrogen that comes from testosterone to masculinize the brain.

Many of these herbicides are also disrupting estrogens in a similar way, which might explain why puberty is happening so much earlier in young girls. Does this mean that you have to neurotically avoid anything that includes atrazine? That's up to you. But it does seem that these have pretty marked effects in both the animal studies and the human studies.

## [00:20:29] Female Sexual Development, Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome

Let's talk about female sexual development. There is a mutation called androgen insensitivity syndrome, and understanding how it works can help you really understand how hormones impact sexual development.

Here's how it works: there are individuals who are XY — they have a Y chromosome — and who are born making testosterone. They have testes and they don't have Müllerian ducts because of the Müllerian inhibiting hormone on the Y chromosome. However, these individuals look completely female and in general they report feeling like girls when they're young, and women when they're older. What's happening is the testes are making testosterone but the receptor for testosterone is mutated. Therefore the testes never descend, they don't have ovaries — they have testes, but the testes are internal. These individuals typically find out something is different when they don't menstruate around the time of puberty. They discover that they are actually XY — their chromosomal sex is male, their gonadal sex is technically male, but the gonads are inside the body. They don't develop a scrotum, they don't make ovaries, and they never menstruate.

In order to have its effects, a hormone doesn't just have to be present — that hormone actually has to be able to bind its receptor and take action on the target cells. The receptors are vitally important for most all of the secondary sexual characteristics: body hair, penis growth during puberty, etc.

Perhaps the simplest way to understand how estrogen and testosterone impact masculinization or feminization of the brain and behavior comes from a statement — actually the closing sentence of an abstract — that my colleague Nirao Shah at Stanford School of Medicine published: estrogen, again the estrogen that is aromatized from testosterone by aromatase, sets up the masculine repertoire of sexual and territorial behaviors in animals and in humans. It sets up the circuitry in the brain. Estrogen sets up the masculine circuitry in the brain, and testosterone is then what controls the display of those behaviors later in life. You would think it was just testosterone did one thing and estrogen did another, but it turns out nature is far more interesting than that.

## [00:24:03] Cannabis & Aromatase Activity, Gynecomastia; Alcohol & Estrogen Activity

What are some things that impact sexual development early in life and later in life? Let's talk about cannabis and alcohol.

Cannabis — marijuana, THC — there are many studies that point to the fact that THC and other things in cannabis promote significant increases in aromatase activity. It's not clear if it's THC itself or other elements in the marijuana plant. Cannabis promotes aromatase activity, and this has been observed anecdotally: pot smokers have a higher incidence of developing gynecomastia — breast bud development or full-blown breast development in males. Earlier I said that estrogen is what masculinizes the male brain in utero — that's true. But the way cannabis seems to work, at least from the studies I was able to identify, is that it promotes circulating estrogen in the body and therefore can counteract some of the masculinizing effects of things like testosterone and dihydrotestosterone on primary and secondary sexual characteristics.

Cannabis use is far more widespread nowadays, and certainly during puberty it can have profound effects on these hormonal systems. Yes, cannabis promotes estrogenic activity by increasing aromatase.

Most everyone can appreciate that drinking during pregnancy is not good for the developing fetus. Fetal alcohol syndrome is a well-established negative outcome of pregnancy with real cognitive effects. Drinking during puberty is probably not good either, because alcohol — in particular certain things like beer and other grain alcohols — can increase estrogenic activity. This isn't just about protecting young boys from estrogenic activity; it's also about protecting girls from excessive or even hypoestrogenic effects of alcohol during puberty. Many teenagers and college students drink, and it's important to point out that puberty doesn't start on one day and end on another. Puberty has a beginning, a middle, and an end, but development is really our entire lifespan.

## [00:27:00] Cell Phones & Gonads

Let's talk about cell phones. First of all, I use a cell phone — I use it very often — and I do not think they are evil devices. I think that they require some discipline in order to make sure they don't become a negative force in one's life. I personally restrict the number of hours I'm on the phone, and in particular on social media.

What about the cell phone itself? When I was a junior professor pre-tenure, I taught a class on neural circuits and health and disease, and one of the students asked me whether cell phones were safe for the brain. All the data at that point suggested they were, or at least there were no data showing they weren't. I still don't have the answer on that, frankly. I'm not personally aware of any evidence in quality peer-reviewed studies showing that cell phones are bad for the brain or that holding the phone to the ear is bad, or that Bluetooth is bad.

However, I was very interested in a particular study published back in 2013 on rats. It basically took a cell phone and put it under a cage of rats and looked at testicular and ovarian development — and saw minor but still statistically significant defects in testicular and ovarian development. Since then, returning to the literature, I've seen an absolute explosion of studies — some in quality journals, some less so — identifying defects in testicular and/or ovarian development from mere exposure to cell phone-emitted waves. The literature is pointing in a direction where chronic exposure of the gonads to cell phones could be creating serious issues in terms of cell-level health and then in terms of output: sperm production, sperm swimming speed, estrogenic output, and regularity of ovarian cycles. It's fair to say based on the literature that there are effects of cell phone-emitted waves on gonadal development. The question is the proximity of the cell phone to the gonads — you have to take these studies with a grain of salt.

## [00:29:46] Beard & Hair Growth, DHT

There are some interesting things about hormones that you can observe on the outside of people — things that tell you not just about their level of hormones but also about their underlying genetics. These relate to beard growth and baldness.

The molecule dihydrotestosterone — made from testosterone — is the hormone primarily responsible for facial hair growth, and it's also the hormone primarily responsible for lack of hair on the head, for hair loss. Not incidentally, the drugs designed to prevent hair loss are 5-alpha reductase inhibitors. Remember 5-alpha reductase from the guevedoces story? The people who discovered guevedoces went on to do a lot of research on the underlying biochemistry of DHT. They identified 5-alpha reductase, and 5-alpha reductase inhibitors are the basis of most of the anti-hair-loss treatments out there.

The side effect profiles of those hair loss treatments are quite severe in many individuals. Remember: DHT is the primary androgen for libido, for strength, for connective tissue repair, for aggression — even ambition, which is related to dopamine but within the testosterone pathway. DHT is, at least in primate species including humans, the dominant androgen for most of those effects.

Everyone can predict whether or not they're going to go bald based on looking at their family — we're always taught to look at the mother's father. The pattern of DHT receptors on the scalp will dictate whether or not you're going to go bald everywhere or just in the front or in a crown-type pattern. The density of the beard tells you about the density of DHT receptors. This varies by genetic background — there are areas of the world where all the men seem to have the same pattern of baldness. Elsewhere, testosterone levels and DHT levels in the blood can be very high and yet people will have very light beards or no beards — because they don't have a lot of DHT receptors in the face. Hair loss drugs are directly aimed at preventing the conversion of testosterone into dihydrotestosterone, and that's why they to some extent prevent hair loss but also have a bunch of side effects associated with low DHT.

## [00:33:04] Hyenas, Hormones, Androstenedione; Plants

I want to tell you a story about hyenas. When I was a graduate student at UC Berkeley we had a professor in our department named Steve Glickman. Steve Glickman had a colony of spotted hyenas that lived in enclosures in Tilden Park behind the UC Berkeley campus. Hyenas exhibit an incredible feature of their hormones and social structure. Unlike many species, hyenas have a situation with their genitalia where the male penis is actually smaller than the female clitoris. The hyena clitorises are extremely large.

In spotted hyenas the females are dominant: after a kill, the females eat, then their young eat, and then the male hyenas eat. When the female hyena gives birth, she gives birth not through the vaginal canal that we're accustomed to seeing, but through a very enlarged clitoris-like phallus — although it's a clitoris, not a phallus — and it literally splits open. Many fetuses die during the course of hyena birth. It was a mystery how female hyenas have this androgenization of the genitalia. Through careful research done by Steve Glickman, Christine Drea, and others, it turns out that it's androstenedione — essentially a prohormone to testosterone — at very high levels that's produced in female hyenas and creates this enlargement of their genitalia. Androstenedione is made into testosterone through the enzyme 17-beta hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase.

You may recall during the '90s and 2000s there were a lot of performance-enhancing drug scandals, in particular in Major League Baseball. It was purported that the major performance-enhancing drug of abuse at that time was androstenedione.

Going back to the marijuana plant — the marijuana plant has these estrogenic properties. A plant biologist told me that there are plants that make what is essentially the equivalent of testosterone, like pine pollen, and other plants that make what is essentially estrogen. Why would they do that? The reason some plants have evolved this capacity to increase estrogen levels in animals that consume them is that plants are engaged in a kind of plant-to-animal warfare — they increase the estrogen of the males in a population to lower their sperm counts, keeping those populations at certain levels so that the plants can continue to flourish. Hormones aren't just impacting tissue growth and development within the individual, or between the mother and offspring — plants and animals are in this communication too. It's a fascinating area of biology.
