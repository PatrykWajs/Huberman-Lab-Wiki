## [00:06:03] What is Emotional Intelligence?; Self & Others

**Marc Brackett:** Thank you. Great to be here. I'm excited to talk to you today about many things related to emotions.

**Andrew Huberman:** We hear the word emotions and we have all sorts of ideas about what they are, what they aren't. We also hear about emotional intelligence quite a lot these days, and I have a feeling that the way it's discussed is often not the way it really is. So to just kick things off — could you clarify for me, for everyone, what is emotional intelligence, what does it pertain to, and then maybe we can use that as a way to drill into the deeper question of what are emotions?

**Marc Brackett:** Sure. I think at the simplest level it's how we reason with and about our emotions and feelings — that's like the simple definition. The way I talk about it is as a set of skills, and we use the acronym RULER to describe those skills. The first is Recognizing emotions — I'm trying to read your facial expression right now, are you interested or are you bored already? The second is Understanding emotions — where are they coming from, why am I feeling this way, what's the consequence of that feeling? The third is Labeling emotions — so being precise with the words that we use to describe our feelings. The fourth is Expressing emotions — knowing how and when to express emotions with different people across context and culture. And then the big one is the final R, which is Regulating emotions — what are the strategies we use to help us deal with everyday emotions?

**Andrew Huberman:** So if I were to take an emotional intelligence test, would it have me looking at pictures of facial expressions, reading paragraphs about emotional exchanges, gauging who felt what and why?

**Marc Brackett:** If you were to take a test from like 20 years ago, yes. We try to be a little bit more innovative now in our measurement of the skills. I just finished with a bunch of colleagues publishing a test of emotion perception — it's not static images, it's video clips that are around three to four seconds showing subtle emotions. It's about vocal tone, it's about body language, and we're trying to get people to accurately label these emotions in face, body, and voice.

**Andrew Huberman:** When I think about most uses of the words "emotional intelligence," it seems to correlate with one's ability to tolerate others' emotions and make sense of the emotions of others. You're describing emotional intelligence as a self-perception as well?

**Marc Brackett:** Yes. And so is our task therefore to do the equivalent of what in my little anecdote this other person was doing — to be able to parse one's own emotions in a fine enough way to understand the experience in a kind of third-person way so that one can regulate their behavior, what they say, how they act?

The whole set of skills are intra- and interpersonal — that's really important. It's about self and others always. For example, right now we are co-regulating each other's emotions. Our facial expressions, our vocal tones — we're influencing how we each feel. When you think about the recognition piece: there's self-awareness, like "Marc, how are you feeling right now?" I'm having mixed emotions. This is a great podcast, I want to be articulate, I'm excited but I'm a little overwhelmed because I've got so much I want to share. And that's why it starts off with kind of just a general awareness — am I pleasant, am I unpleasant, do I have a lot of energy or do I feel depleted? We call that your core affect. And then I could start asking myself questions: what are you doing right now, how does that make you feel, what comes up for you with that? And then I try to label that feeling. And I'm doing the same thing for you — I'm looking at your facial expressions, your body language, I'm listening to you, I'm trying to understand if I say something, do you shift. So it's self and other.

## [00:11:18] Language & Emotion

**Andrew Huberman:** Given that we're both scientists interested in emotions — you're the expert, I'm also just the student today — I think it's worth pointing out to people that there isn't one location in the brain that governs this complex process that you just described. It's a network-wide phenomenon. But you did mention the body — to what extent does somebody who has high emotional intelligence have more or less body awareness, or somatic awareness, as opposed to somebody who's "in their head"?

**Marc Brackett:** I think another big deal about emotional intelligence is that we like to think of it as this construct — I don't think that's the best way to look at it. I think it's much more interesting to look at it as a set of discrete skills that come together. They're not that highly correlated. Within RULER there are subskills — the body awareness is one of them. Some people are more cognitive, very language oriented; some people are more somatic. But at the end of the day we have to know how we feel, we have to know what we want to do with those feelings, and we have to know how the people we live with and love and work with — how they feel too. And so we need language in the end.

**Andrew Huberman:** Some years ago I went to a personal development course where each day started with going around the circle and you'd have to say how you feel — but you couldn't use evaluation, you couldn't say good or bad. I found it very difficult. A couple of questions: when it comes to using language to describe our emotions, how important is it to have a broad buffet of options? A previous guest, Lisa Feldman Barrett, mentioned that in some cultures there's very specific language for specific emotions — there's even a word to describe the feeling of sadness one has after getting a really bad haircut. What is the relationship between language labels and emotion?

**Marc Brackett:** I'm very similar to Lisa in terms of understanding emotion concepts or categories. Let's use the anger category — if you only have one word for anger, that means all you know is there's one form of anger. But if you start teaching people there are other words we could use — peeved, irritated, angry, enraged, livid — and you have rich conversations about what makes you feel peeved versus enraged, what does it feel like in your body, then you start to really understand the emotions. Having a common language and a common understanding is crucial because otherwise we can't communicate.

I do a lot of corporate training, and I'm in a room filled with lawyers or executives and I ask them how they're feeling. Nobody's really sure. Then I'll do these little quizzes — tell me the difference between anxiety, stress, pressure, fear, and overwhelmed. The number-one response is: they're all the same. After some time, they finally start defining them, and then they realize that if I'm anxious because I'm worrying about the future, maybe a breathing exercise isn't as helpful because I need a cognitive strategy. Helping people make connections between the feeling and the reason for the feeling has been very helpful for learning how to regulate the emotion.

**Andrew Huberman:** So connecting the feeling and the reason for the feeling — it's the "why" that you really have to deal with?

**Marc Brackett:** Correct.

## [00:18:52] Emojis; Anger vs. Disappointment; Behavior & Emotion

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you feel about emojis? From everything you're saying, they seem like more than just benign.

**Marc Brackett:** I could imagine that the emoji culture — I don't think that's a real word. All right, it is now — you can set up a Wikipedia page tomorrow. The emoji culture is a serious problem because it's what we call in science too much lumping. In science you have lumpers and splitters. If you lump too much you create more confusion and often create problems. I see emojis as lumping this incredible set of different continuums within us that we call emotions into literally a small icon, and I can imagine this would lead to all sorts of problems not just in communication but in understanding our own emotions.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think the use of emojis has degraded our level of emotional intelligence and processing?

**Marc Brackett:** I haven't done the research, but from my perspective it's not helpful because the goal is to get granular. Think about the difference between anger and disappointment — 95% of the people I ask cannot define those two things. Disappointment is unmet expectations; everything was legitimate, it just didn't work out. Anger is perceived injustice. That's a really important distinction.

We also make gross mistakes by labeling emotion from behavior — there's no real correlation between behavior and emotion. I could stomp my feet as a boy because I'm feeling sad, because it's more culturally acceptable for me to stomp than to cry. Going back to the anger-disappointment distinction and the strategy: if I failed my yellow belt test because my blocks genuinely weren't strong enough — that's disappointment. The strategy is: get a tutor, practice every day. But if I failed because bullies at my school were in the room giving me dirty looks and I was terrified — that's fear, shame, and overwhelm. You can see how a parent or teacher would have to really differentiate their support, but they have to get at the actual experience. And that means having relationships that are trustworthy and caring — otherwise we just take the behavior at face value and say "why are you acting that way?" instead of asking how they're feeling.

## [00:31:24] Anonymity, Online Comments

**Marc Brackett:** Anonymity causes challenges. I gave a speech at Twitter — now known as X — about five years ago, and I had analyzed quite a lot of data. I was curious about how people respond to public figures making mistakes online, and it was 99.99% ripping people to shreds. People feel protected by anonymity when the target is a more famous person, and in politics people are very clear how they feel. But it's not emotionally intelligent. I always ask people: what's your goal here? What are you getting at by being nasty? I perceive it as evacuative — I look at that and I think, what they must feel inside to be able to say those things can't be good.

In my earlier research with Facebook we analyzed millions and millions of posts. People can be intentionally mean and hurtful — they want to rip people to shreds and instill fear. And it's very hard to disentangle intent from interpretation. I could say "nice shirt" and it might mean exactly that, or it might mean I'm making fun of your shirt.

**Andrew Huberman:** As the target, if I'm the person who said something and then got ripped apart, what are the data on how those negative comments influence how somebody feels or performs thereafter?

**Marc Brackett:** It doesn't feel good. What we know is that we think about negative comments far more than we think about positive ones. It's the negativity bias in emotions that's really powerful. I think this is causing tremendous damage to a lot of people's emotional lives.

## [00:35:46] Happiness vs. Contentment; Knowing Oneself

**Andrew Huberman:** A counter-example for the anger versus disappointment distinction on the positive valence side — what's a positive set of feelings that people often conflate, like happiness and contentment?

**Marc Brackett:** Yes. When you think about happiness, it's usually about achieving something — "I'm going to be happy when..." Contentment is the opposite. Contentment is: everything is just great as it is, I feel complete, I have enough. Part of my argument against the happiness research is that we don't spend enough time helping people strive for contentment. We push people to strive for happiness, which research shows can backfire — if you're waking up every day saying "what am I going to do to be happy?" chances are it's not going to work out a lot.

**Andrew Huberman:** As soon as you describe contentment that way, I imagine waking up and rather than thinking about what needs to be done and the things I want to achieve — which I want to achieve and which bring me joy — this notion of contentment might lead me to pay attention to how good it feels to have gotten some sleep, to feel the comfort of the mattress for a moment before barging into the day to chase happiness.

**Marc Brackett:** I think the idea that we have to be happy all the time is also ridiculous. I'm a neurotic professor — I'm never happy. And also, given that my dispositional affect is more on the lower-energy, contented-with-a-little-anxiety side, when I'm around people who are high energy and pleasant all the time, I have a difficult time — I feel overwhelmed and smothered.

## [00:41:33] Introversion & Extroversion; Personality & Emotional Intelligence

**Andrew Huberman:** The introversion-extroversion bit is going to prick up people's ears. I like time alone. I also get my best work done either alone in nature or in Manhattan where there are people around me but I'm completely isolated. How should we think about introversion and extroversion? It's got to be on a continuum.

**Marc Brackett:** For some people it's very clear — they are a clear-trait introvert, for others they're endlessly extroverted. What research shows for example with creative people is they tend to be both — they're introverts when they're doing their art and extroverts when they're out there selling it. The easiest way to think about it is just this: it's a proclivity — how you want to use your energy. The introvert is more of a container; they want to be in small groups, less frenetic environments. The extrovert has a proclivity for more sensation-seeking, larger social groups. It's a preference.

I always say I'm an introvert with pretty good social skills. I can appear to be extroverted and most people think I'm outgoing, but I always tell them: I don't even like people that much. It's just not my natural state. If I'm at a party I struggle.

**Andrew Huberman:** When you say you don't necessarily like people that much — being in the presence of a lot of people doesn't draw you out to want to be closer to or get to know all these people simply because they're there, whereas an extrovert seems to really like forming and engaging in relationships?

**Marc Brackett:** Exactly. The relationship between personality and emotional intelligence is interesting because sometimes people think introverts have lower emotional intelligence. That's not true. Introverts can be exceptionally emotionally intelligent. Extroverts can have incredibly low emotional intelligence. They're separate constructs. The emotionally intelligent introvert will use that time with themselves to really do deep introspection. They understand their own feelings in this rich way. But then they have to really work at the social part.

The reason we want to build emotional intelligence in everyone is that many of the qualities of emotional intelligence can be really helpful, like knowing when to speak up. For an introvert, courage is like a primary virtue — sometimes we're going to have to step out of our comfort zone. For the extrovert, wisdom is a primary virtue — don't say everything you're thinking. Think before you act. Think before you open your mouth, because sometimes you say things that are just impulsive and not well-thought-out.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the best way, the most practical way, to start determining whether you're an introvert or an extrovert?

**Marc Brackett:** I think the key thing is just paying attention and doing kind of what we're describing in this podcast, which is a lot of reflection. Try to think about: when do I feel energized and when do I feel depleted? Introverts tend to feel energized by their time alone and depleted in large social groups. Extroverts tend to feel energized in social groups and often feel depleted when they're isolated. But always remember it's a continuum.

## [01:00:37] Tool: Mood Meter, Energy & Pleasantness Scale

**Andrew Huberman:** Could you describe the mood meter — the energy and pleasantness axes — and create a mental picture for people?

**Marc Brackett:** We have a horizontal axis we'll call pleasantness — in this moment, am I feeling highly pleasant or am I feeling unpleasant? Do I feel like approaching my day and my colleagues, or do I feel like avoiding them? Do I feel safe and comfortable, or do I feel uncomfortable? That's the x-axis. And on the y-axis is energy or activation — I think energy is a better term than the technical "arousal." It's either you're highly energized or you're deactivated, low in energy. That's mental energy, physical energy — how much fuel you have.

We cross those two axes to create what we call in our work the Mood Meter, and there are four quadrants. Yellow is high pleasant, high energy — emotions like happy, excited, elated, ecstatic, optimistic. Green is low energy and still pleasant — calm, content, tranquil, peaceful, relaxed. Then we have the unpleasant side. Blue is low energy and unpleasant — sad, down, disappointed, hopeless, despairing. And red is high energy and highly unpleasant — feelings of anger and anxiety.

We find it very helpful for both preschoolers and CEOs because when you're not really sure how you're feeling, you can have a tool with four quadrants where you ask: am I pleasant? I guess I'm kind of pleasant but my energy is low — all right, I'm in the green. Now what are my options? In my work on building curriculum for schools, the Mood Meter has been a powerful tool for building self-awareness, for starting to understand why you're in a particular emotional state, and for then strategizing about what to do next.

## [01:06:28] Emotion Suppression; Permission to Feel, Emotions Mentor

**Andrew Huberman:** How do we resolve the contradiction between the message to feel our feelings versus to just recognize that feelings are transient and moving through us? Should we feel our feelings in order to best recognize them? Is there any value to suppressing our feelings, or does that tend to just grow the feeling?

**Marc Brackett:** It tends to grow. There are cultural differences, but in Western culture, suppression tends not to have great outcomes. Finding ways to reappraise tends to be more helpful. This really gets into the core of my work though, because for 20 years I was running the Center for Emotional Intelligence and I would see a lot of resistance. I've had fathers come up to me and say things like "Mark, you're so vulnerable — I would never in my wildest dream ever share with my own son that I was bullied as a kid." And the guy was afraid his son would think he was weak.

We have a mindset about feelings that we have to talk about. People have feelings about their feelings — sometimes called meta-emotions. Sometimes it's just that happy is good and anger is bad. My whole recent research has focused on something I call "Permission to Feel." I had a pretty rough childhood that included abuse and a lot of bullying. I had two parents who loved me but my mother was a very anxious woman who never had strategies — she was always saying "I'm having a nervous breakdown" and she'd lock herself in her room. My father was what we might call today the tough guy — "Son, you've got to toughen up."

I had an uncle, his name was Marvin, my mother's brother, and he was this incredible man. Every time he would visit, he would find me and he would sit down with me and he'd say, "Marc, how are you feeling today? Tell me what's going on." And I remember thinking, why is he doing this? And then I'd start telling him what was happening — the bullying at school, this kid who was picking on me — and he would say, "How does that make you feel?" He validated my feelings and I think it changed my life. And when I figured that out in my adult life — that this one person had this profound impact — I named him my emotions mentor. The research on emotions mentors is now quite compelling: people who have one person in their life who validates their emotions have significantly better mental health outcomes.

**Andrew Huberman:** Giving ourselves and others permission to simply have a conversation about what one is feeling — as an exercise for both people to explore that in the right way.

**Marc Brackett:** And the key is that we don't say "What happened to you?" We don't make it about the event. We start with "How are you feeling?" and we mean it. We stay with that feeling, we ask follow-up questions, we validate — "That makes sense, I understand why you feel that way." We don't jump to problem-solving because when people are in a heightened emotional state, they're not ready for problem-solving. We need to be the emotion scientist, not the emotion judge.

## [01:19:42] Discussing Feelings; Emotional Self-Awareness

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm recalling numerous instances in my life where there seemed to be the requirement for an activity excuse as an entry point to any conversation about emotions — like I currently have a very good relationship with my father, but I remember when we had to talk about science or watches as an entry point. What you're describing makes me realize that no matter who anybody is or what their age or background, as human beings we don't just need permission — we really should think about just having a conversation about how others feel, as opposed to making an activity a prerequisite for that conversation.

**Marc Brackett:** I couldn't agree with you more. I'll give you another example. My father — like you, we ended up having an excellent relationship. My mom died when I was young, and he remarried. His wife called me about two years after they were married and said, "Mark, I can't take it anymore — your father is driving me out of my mind. He's angry all the time. I might have to leave him." And I asked him, "When was the last time you told her that you love her?" He goes, "I tell her every night." I said, "When was the last time you told her that you're proud of her?" Silence. He went home, and within a few weeks everything shifted. The key was helping him understand that his anger was really sadness and fear — fear of losing her, fear of getting it wrong. Once he understood that, he could do something about it.

**Marc Brackett:** Let me give you another example. I'm a professor at Yale, teaching courses on emotional intelligence. I ask my students to fill out surveys on how they're feeling every class — the number-one emotion was "stressed." Everybody's stressed. I decided I had a hard time with this, remembering that I'd defined stress as having too many demands and not enough resources. I didn't feel like that was the actual feeling. So one way to get better at it was to have students do journaling — when you're stressed, write about it, what's on your mind, what are you thinking about. The number-one emotion after we did the qualitative analysis? It wasn't stress. It wasn't fear. Take a guess.

**Andrew Huberman:** Envy.

**Marc Brackett:** Envy. Endless social comparisons — your father is richer than my father, your mother is more connected, you've got better grades. So envy is wanting what someone else has; anxiety is about uncertainty; stress is about too many demands and not enough resources. And here I was having deeper knowledge of what the underlying feeling actually was — which was envy, not stress. Understanding the true cause of the emotion is what allows you to strategize about what to do. For envy, the answer isn't a breathing exercise — the answer is gratitude, perspective, and perhaps a conversation about values.

**Andrew Huberman:** How do we understand the cause of our emotions? Because I think a lot of people are walking around with stress, anxiety, or whatever word they use, and they genuinely don't know why they feel that way.

**Marc Brackett:** The best tool I've found is just inquiry. Ask yourself: what happened? What was I thinking about at that moment? What did I want to happen that didn't? What do I fear might happen? The more you practice asking these questions — and this is what journaling can help with — the more you start to see patterns. You start to understand your own triggers. And once you understand your triggers, you can start to work with them rather than just being at their mercy.

## [01:33:40] Framing Empathy, Compassionate Empathy

**Andrew Huberman:** Conflict resolution brings me back to the situation you were talking about earlier where a woman was going to find her child somebody to help him — his feelings mentor. Now there's a whole field of feelings mentors cropping up. When we were talking about that, one of the things that surfaced was the notion that some people have a natural empathic attunement, and yet if the emotion the other person is feeling is about us, we're not able to really be present to help that person. To what extent is empathic attunement a positive trait? Are there people who are better at turning it off or directing it in appropriate ways?

**Marc Brackett:** I disagree with the framing that empathy is the problem. What you're describing is empathy without emotional intelligence. I work with a lot of doctors, and doctors have been taught from early on to "leave your empathy at the door." I challenge that. When you're a patient with cancer, the last thing you want is an unempathic doctor. The assumption is that if you're empathic you can't make good decisions — I don't buy it. What I believe in is what I call compassionate empathy. It's the ability to feel what others feel but also to have the emotional intelligence to regulate your own feelings so that you can be helpful to the other person rather than becoming overwhelmed or paralyzed by their emotional state.

There's also a difference between emotional empathy — I feel what you feel — and cognitive empathy — I understand intellectually what you're going through without necessarily experiencing the emotion. Both are important, but the most powerful combination is to have both plus the regulation skills to stay present and effective.

**Andrew Huberman:** So compassionate empathy allows one to be present to the other person's experience without being hijacked by it?

**Marc Brackett:** Exactly. And developing this requires practice. It requires self-awareness so that you can monitor when you're starting to get overwhelmed or defensive, and then use the regulation strategies — distancing, reframing, deep breathing — to bring yourself back to a place where you can be genuinely helpful.

## [01:42:28] Asking Question; Tools: Reframing, Hot Air Balloon; Distancing

**Andrew Huberman:** To do this properly is to ask questions as opposed to telling people what they need to do. Your friend — the effective source of support — said "Can you get in the hot air balloon and look down on your life?" — and importantly, they didn't say "Get in the hot air balloon and then do this." No one likes to be shifted. No one wants to be told what to do no matter what state they're in. Even if it's probably a great thing to do, perhaps there's a different way, posed in the form of a question, that would be more effective.

The hot air balloon example also brings to mind that when we're stressed, our mental aperture, our visual aperture, our auditory aperture — everything shrinks. But getting a different spatial perspective gives us a different temporal perspective. We can start thinking about our life in larger pieces and get the perspective of things that are going well.

**Marc Brackett:** Distancing — that's exactly what it is. And making this a practice not in a moment of stress but as a regular exercise. The research on distancing is very compelling. There's work by Ethan Kross and others showing that simply changing perspective — literally stepping back and viewing your situation from a distance, either spatially or temporally — can significantly reduce emotional reactivity and improve problem-solving. The hot air balloon is one metaphor. Another is what Ethan calls "time traveling" — projecting yourself into the future and asking: "How will I feel about this in 10 years?" Often the answer is: not that much.

**Andrew Huberman:** Distancing, reframing — are there other tools in the RULER toolkit for regulation?

**Marc Brackett:** There are many. Mindfulness and meditation — being present to the emotion without being overwhelmed by it. Reappraisal — changing how you think about a situation. For example, instead of "this is terrible," you think "this is challenging and I'm learning from it." Physical strategies like exercise and sleep, which profoundly impact our emotional states. Social strategies — reaching out to someone you trust. And finally, problem-solving — once you've regulated enough to think clearly, identifying concrete steps you can take to address the situation.

## [01:49:44] Stereotypes, "Emotional"

**Andrew Huberman:** Dating back to the 1930s through the end of the 1970s, there seemed to be a tendency to couch people as more stoic, less emotionally expressive. Now I think there's an invitation for a broader range of emotional expressions. But emotionality — the notion of people being "overly emotional" — has a negative connotation, whereas somebody being "passionate" sounds like a pretty good thing.

**Marc Brackett:** "Emotional" — historically it means you are not in control of your emotions, like you're hysterical. I just don't like to use that term ever. I find it a useless term. It just has connotations from the past that I don't think are helpful.

**Andrew Huberman:** Why do you think we still reward people for being passionate even if it's tinged with some anger, but we don't say they're being "emotional"?

**Marc Brackett:** Because passion is perceived as directed toward an end. It's purposeful. Whereas just anger or sadness geysering out of us doesn't seem like it's directed toward anything productive. But "emotional" as a judgment — when I say "Andrew, you're so emotional" — it's also a form of gaslighting. I'm trying to get you to believe something about yourself that I want you to believe, which may not be a reality at all. I think most of our low self-esteem comes from gaslighting in our childhoods where people said things like "stop being so sensitive" or "you're overreacting." It plants seeds of doubt about the validity of your own emotional experience, and that's deeply damaging.

## [01:53:49] Emotions, Learning & Decision Making; Intention

**Andrew Huberman:** One of my most wonderful quotes I anchor to quickly: "All learning has an emotional base." And it was none other than Plato who said that. What is the relationship between emotions and learning and decision-making?

**Marc Brackett:** Emotions drive our attention — that's so clear. If we're not feeling engaged or curious, we're going to be bored. Boredom is not a bad emotion — it just means what's being presented isn't meeting my needs. So my brain needs to do something. In environments where there's a lot of curiosity and high engagement, attention is much better.

In my work building curriculum to help educators integrate emotions into their everyday classroom, one of the things we help them understand is that they tend to present in a way consistent with their own emotional style. If you're someone who lives in the green — calm, tranquil — people might find your presentation low energy. If you're always in the red — highly activated — you might overwhelm people. Part of emotional intelligence in leadership and teaching is being able to modulate your own emotional expression to meet people where they are and bring them along.

The other thing is decision-making. There's extensive research showing that people who have damage to the emotional centers of their brain — the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, the amygdala — actually make worse decisions, not better ones. Emotions are data. They carry information about what matters to us, what we value, what we fear, what we love. The key is to use that information wisely rather than being driven by it blindly.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the distinction between being motivated by emotion and being driven blindly by emotion?

**Marc Brackett:** Intention. Being able to pause and ask yourself: what am I feeling right now, what does this feeling tell me about what I want or need, and is acting on this feeling consistent with my values and my long-term goals? That pause — that moment of reflection — is the key. Without it, you're at the mercy of whatever emotional state you're in. With it, you have choice.

## [02:02:43] Emotion App & Self-Awareness; Gratitude Practice

**Andrew Huberman:** There's an app that's freely available that allows people to denote where they are on the energy versus pleasantness scale at numerous times throughout the day. We'll provide a link to this app in the show notes. It's called How We Feel.

**Marc Brackett:** I've used it before. What's really cool about the app is that technology can be super helpful in this instance for building self-awareness. If you set reminders to check in — in the morning, maybe after lunch, or right before you go home — you aggregate your data across time. And what's also cool is that you can disaggregate your data by things like who you're with, where you're at, or what you're doing. You might think you're more in the yellow at work, but you're actually more in the blue. Or you thought you felt calm with a certain person, but when you look at your data, you're always anxious with that person. It runs a reverse correlation. Then it asks you questions to get more insights, and we've embedded a lot of the strategies I've been talking about — distancing strategies, breathing exercises, mindfulness, gratitude.

Speaking of gratitude — I was thinking about the Envy Reduction Program. I think the number-one tool is gratitude. If our brains are endlessly searching for what's better out there than what I have, we're not experiencing any gratitude for what we have. I spent a lot of time helping people understand: take a look at where you're at, look at the opportunities you have in life, and all of a sudden it's like "oh yeah, my life is pretty good." Gratitude sometimes feels clichéd these days, but I can't speak strongly enough about both the practice and the science that supports it.

## [02:07:13] Bullying

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to get back to your origin story a bit to understand the motivation behind your goal. You've written about and spoken a little today about the fact that you were bullied pretty viciously, and also that you were the target of abuse. I'm guessing that in order to really disintegrate the bullying problem down to zero, we need to get into the minds of both the bullied and the bullies. Could you tell us a little bit about how, as a kid, you perceived your bullies?

**Marc Brackett:** When I think about my 8-, 10-, 11-year-old self being bullied — remember, bullying is about a power imbalance. That's one of the core elements. It's about the intent to harm. It's not conflict, it's not sibling rivalry — it's intent to harm. There's a power imbalance, and then there's the repetition of it. Those are the three key factors: it's repeated, it's intended to harm, and there's a power imbalance. That puts you in a really powerless position.

When you have nobody to support you, no upstanders, no one around you to help you get out of the situation, what happens is that you feel fear. And I felt shame — because when you're bullied, you are made to feel like you are not worthy. Your self-worth is diminished. And what happens then is we often make it about ourselves — "why is this happening to me," "what did I do to deserve this," "am I not worthy of being protected?" Those were the questions I would ask myself.

As an adult, doing a lot of work in therapy and in my own research, I now understand that bullies often engage in this behavior because they themselves are in pain. They have high levels of anxiety or shame or fear, and their way of dealing with it is to exert power over others. But understanding that doesn't make bullying acceptable. What it tells us is that both the bully and the bullied need emotional intelligence education.

**Andrew Huberman:** What do the data show about what actually works to reduce bullying in schools?

**Marc Brackett:** Creating caring school environments. Research shows that when students feel safe, when they feel like their teachers genuinely care about them as people not just as students, when they feel connected to their peers — bullying rates go down. The most evidence-based approaches are systemic — they change the culture of the school, not just the behavior of individual bullies. Programs that teach emotional intelligence skills across the board have shown some of the most promising results, because they address both the empathy deficit in the bully and the regulatory skills needed by everyone in the environment.

## [02:18:06] Courage & Bullying; Emotion Education

**Marc Brackett:** Eight years ago I was giving a speech at Yale to a bunch of funders. I went on first, and then a colleague — who I will not name, who has a rough reputation for being a bully — got on stage after me. He changed his presentation and showed a video of a kid being horrifically bullied, which had nothing to do with his research, and he's like doing this kind of laughing to himself, and he said, "I just want to let people know — that was Marc before he got his black belt."

In that moment, I regressed to being 10 years old. All the memories, all the feelings and the bodily reactions — boom. Luckily I have a fifth-degree black belt. Luckily I have a PhD in psychology. Luckily I've had therapy and I've been teaching emotional intelligence for 25 years. I had to make a choice — and after he was finished, I went up to him and I said, "I have no idea what motivated you to share that video, but number one, it was not cool, and number two, it can never happen again. Never." And I can cry now thinking about it, because it was very difficult for me even as an adult.

The reason I tell this story is to illustrate two things: first, that the wounds of bullying don't fully heal — they leave marks. And second, that courage is something we can choose even when we're scared. Having the courage to face a bully — in a way that's grounded and regulated rather than explosive — is one of the most powerful things we can do. Interestingly, this person treated me like the president of the university after that. The courage to stand up in a regulated way — not from the red, from the green — is the key.

**Andrew Huberman:** Emotion education in schools — where does it stand?

**Marc Brackett:** It's growing, but we still have a long way to go. In some schools and districts, social-emotional learning is well-integrated — it's part of the school day, it's part of teacher training, it's part of the culture. But in many schools it's still seen as a "nice to have" rather than a "need to have." We have to change that narrative. Academic achievement is important, but without the emotional skills to manage stress, navigate relationships, and make good decisions, academic achievement doesn't translate into wellbeing and success in life.

## [02:25:33] Punishment; Uncle Marvin

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's spend a minute on punishment.

**Marc Brackett:** Punishment never works unless it's consistent and harsh, and nobody wants to be punished because it doesn't feel good and it doesn't teach people anything. "Go to your room" — what does that teach me? It teaches me to go to my room and ruminate and get angrier.

I'll never forget this one moment where I was in a principal's office. A kid had given the teacher the finger and got thrown to the office. It was Tuesday, and the next week was going to be a holiday break. The kid was going to get a two-day suspension, which would run till Thursday, and then Friday, and then a week break. I literally heard the principal say, "Let's just make it three days so we don't have to see this kid for ten days." And I'm thinking: what is this person learning about empathy, about self-regulation, about emotional awareness? Nothing. They're going to be thrown out with no skills into an environment that's probably not supportive. This has to change.

I'm a prevention scientist. I don't want to wait until everyone has an anxiety disorder and everyone's been bullied. I want to cultivate a society where people have the skills they need to navigate their emotions, know how to build healthy relationships, make sound decisions, have good mental health, and achieve their dreams.

And it occurred to me just now that you're effectively doing what your uncle did for you but for millions and millions of people.

God bless Uncle Marvin. One of the most profound moments of my career was just after I had written my book and I was on my book tour. I'm in Westchester, New York, giving a speech, and I had never spoken about my uncle in that level of detail. A man in the audience had an epiphany. He's like, "Are you talking about Marvin Moore, the sixth-grade social studies teacher from Monticello, New York State?" I'm like, yes. He's like, "Marc, you're not going to believe this — but your uncle Marvin was my Uncle Marvin. Your uncle was my sixth-grade teacher 45 years ago, and he is the reason why I teach."

I was shaking. I was so excited that I had never met one of my uncle's students — and this man, after all those years, was still paying it forward. That's the power of an emotions mentor.
