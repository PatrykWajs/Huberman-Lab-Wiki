# Episode 228: How to Increase Your Emotional Intelligence | Dr. Marc Brackett

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Marc Brackett, professor at Yale University and director of the Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence, presents a rigorous, skills-based framework for understanding and working with emotions across all life domains. The conversation covers emotional intelligence as a learnable set of discrete skills — encapsulated in the RULER acronym — and provides practical tools including the Mood Meter, compassionate empathy, and cognitive distancing strategies. The episode also addresses bullying, the hidden cost of emotional suppression, the role of emotions in learning and decision-making, and the transformative impact of having even one "emotions mentor" in one's life.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Emotional intelligence is a set of learnable skills, not a fixed trait.** The RULER framework (Recognize, Understand, Label, Express, Regulate) provides a concrete roadmap that can be developed at any age. [00:06:03](transcript.md#000603-what-is-emotional-intelligenceself--others)

2. **Emotion granularity — precise labeling — is the gateway to effective regulation.** The difference between "stressed" and "envious," or between "disappointed" and "angry," determines which strategy will actually work. Using vague or binary language creates a cognitive and strategic dead-end. [00:11:18](transcript.md#001118-language--emotion)

3. **Behavior is not a reliable proxy for emotion.** Stomping your feet may signal sadness; yelling may signal shame. Labeling someone's emotion from their behavior is a pervasive error that leads parents, teachers, and partners astray. [00:18:52](transcript.md#001852-emojis-anger-vs-disappointment-behavior--emotion)

4. **Emojis and text messaging degrade emotional intelligence.** Both collapse the rich continuum of human emotion into simplified icons or ambiguous text, stripping out vocal tone, body language, and contextual nuance. Research shows teens now prefer texting over face-to-face interaction, which impairs genuine connection. [00:52:40](transcript.md#005240-texting--relationships)

5. **The Mood Meter is a simple, universal self-awareness tool.** Plotting yourself on a 2x2 grid of energy (y-axis) versus pleasantness (x-axis) creates four quadrants — Yellow (high energy/pleasant), Green (low energy/pleasant), Blue (low energy/unpleasant), Red (high energy/unpleasant) — usable by anyone from preschool age to C-suite executives. [01:00:37](transcript.md#010037-tool-mood-meter-energy--pleasantness-scale)

6. **Emotional suppression makes emotions grow; reappraisal makes them more manageable.** In Western culture the research is consistent: suppression amplifies both the subjective experience and physiological markers of emotion. Cognitive reappraisal — changing how you think about a situation — is far more effective. [01:06:28](transcript.md#010628-emotion-suppression-permission-to-feel-emotions-mentor)

7. **Having even one "emotions mentor" significantly improves long-term mental health outcomes.** Someone who consistently asks "how are you feeling?" and genuinely means it, validates the response, and does not rush to fix or judge creates a blueprint for emotional safety that shapes the rest of a person's life. [01:06:28](transcript.md#010628-emotion-suppression-permission-to-feel-emotions-mentor)

8. **The root cause of the emotion — not just the label — determines the right strategy.** Yale students journaling about "stress" were actually feeling envy. Breathing exercises do not resolve envy; gratitude practices and perspective-taking do. Connecting the feeling to its cause is the step most people skip. [01:25:00](transcript.md#012500-understanding-cause-of-emotions-stress-envy)

9. **Compassionate empathy — feeling with others while staying regulated — is more effective than either detachment or emotional flooding.** The medical training model of "leave empathy at the door" is counterproductive. The key is pairing genuine empathy with emotional regulation skills to remain present and helpful. [01:33:40](transcript.md#013340-framing-empathy-compassionate-empathy)

10. **Introversion and extroversion are separate from emotional intelligence — but interact with it.** Introverts may excel at introspection; extroverts at outward expression. Both need to develop what doesn't come naturally: courage for introverts (step out of your comfort zone), wisdom for extroverts (think before you speak). [00:41:33](transcript.md#004133-introversion--extroversion-personality--emotional-intelligence)

11. **Emotions are data — essential for good learning and decision-making.** Brain damage to emotional centers produces worse decisions, not better ones. Boredom signals the learning environment is not engaging the student's emotional system. The key distinction between healthy and unhealthy emotional motivation is intention — the ability to pause and ask whether acting on a feeling aligns with your values and long-term goals. [01:53:49](transcript.md#015349-emotions-learning--decision-making-intention)

12. **Calling someone "emotional" is often a form of gaslighting.** The word historically implies loss of control and has been used to invalidate people's inner experience. Telling someone they are "overreacting" or "too sensitive" plants seeds of doubt about the validity of their own feelings — a pattern that underlies much low self-esteem. [01:49:44](transcript.md#014944-stereotypes-emotional)

## Tools & Protocols

### RULER Framework

- **What:** A five-skill model of emotional intelligence — Recognize, Understand, Label, Express, Regulate.
- **How:** Practice each skill deliberately: pause to notice your emotional state (R); ask why and what the consequences are (U); choose a precise word for the feeling (L); consider context when sharing (E); apply a regulation strategy matched to the cause (R).
- **When:** Continuously — as a habit of mind in daily interactions with self and others.
- **Ref:** [00:06:03](transcript.md#000603-what-is-emotional-intelligenceself--others)

### Mood Meter

- **What:** A 2x2 grid plotting energy (y-axis: high to low) against pleasantness (x-axis: unpleasant to pleasant), creating four quadrants — Yellow (high energy/pleasant), Green (low energy/pleasant), Blue (low energy/unpleasant), Red (high energy/unpleasant).
- **How:** Several times daily, ask yourself where you fall on both axes. Name the quadrant. Then ask: why am I here, what do I need, and what strategy fits this state?
- **When:** Morning check-in, before important meetings or conversations, during transitions, and before bed.
- **Ref:** [01:00:37](transcript.md#010037-tool-mood-meter-energy--pleasantness-scale)

### How We Feel App

- **What:** Free mobile app that logs your emotional state on the Mood Meter scale throughout the day and aggregates data over time, broken down by context (who you are with, where you are, what you are doing). Embeds strategies including distancing, breathing exercises, mindfulness, and gratitude.
- **How:** Set reminders 2–4 times per day. Tap your location on the mood meter. After several weeks, review your aggregated report to identify patterns — which people, places, or activities consistently shift your emotional state. The app runs reverse correlations and surfaces follow-up questions to deepen insight.
- **When:** Ongoing self-awareness practice; especially useful during high-stress periods or when trying to understand emotional patterns.
- **Ref:** [02:02:43](transcript.md#020243-emotion-app--self-awareness-gratitude-practice)

### Emotion Labeling / Granularity Practice

- **What:** Replacing vague emotion words (stressed, bad, fine) with precise ones (envious, ashamed, overwhelmed, peeved) to unlock insight into cause and strategy.
- **How:** When you notice a feeling, resist the first generic label. Ask: is this really anger or is it disappointment? Is this really stress or is it fear? Explore the distinctions in your body — what does peeved feel like versus enraged? Use the Mood Meter's emotion vocabulary in each quadrant as a reference.
- **When:** Whenever you notice an emotional state; especially before responding to conflict or before making an important decision.
- **Ref:** [00:11:18](transcript.md#001118-language--emotion)

### Cognitive Distancing / Hot Air Balloon

- **What:** A reappraisal technique that shifts perspective from inside the stressor to a wider vantage point — spatially (imagining viewing your life from above, like from a hot air balloon) and/or temporally (imagining how you will feel about this in 10 years, a technique Ethan Kross calls "time traveling").
- **How:** When activated (red quadrant), mentally "rise" above your current situation. Ask: what does this look like from a distance? What parts of my life are going well that I cannot see from inside the stressor? Or ask: will this matter in five or ten years? Pose this as a question rather than a directive — people resist being told what to do but respond well to invitations to reflect.
- **When:** During acute stress, conflict, or before a high-stakes decision when emotional reactivity is clouding judgment. Best practiced regularly so it is available in the moment of need.
- **Ref:** [01:42:28](transcript.md#014228-asking-question-tools-reframing-hot-air-balloon-distancing)

### Gratitude Practice (Envy Antidote)

- **What:** A structured gratitude practice specifically framed as a counter to social comparison and envy — redirecting the brain from "what others have that I do not" to "what I have that I value."
- **How:** Daily (morning or evening), write or state specific things you are grateful for, emphasizing why they matter to you personally. Specificity activates the emotional circuitry more effectively than generic statements.
- **When:** Daily as a habit; also as an acute intervention when you notice envy or social comparison driving your emotional state.
- **Ref:** [02:02:43](transcript.md#020243-emotion-app--self-awareness-gratitude-practice)

### Becoming an Emotions Mentor

- **What:** Deliberately adopting the practice of consistently asking people in your life "how are you feeling?" and genuinely staying with the answer — validating without judging, without rushing to fix.
- **How:** Ask "how are you feeling?" and mean it. When they answer, reflect back: "That makes sense." Ask a follow-up: "Tell me more about that." Resist the urge to problem-solve until they have felt fully heard. Validate the emotion without endorsing any resulting behavior.
- **When:** With children, partners, colleagues, and anyone you care about — especially during moments of visible distress or transition.
- **Ref:** [01:06:28](transcript.md#010628-emotion-suppression-permission-to-feel-emotions-mentor)

### Connecting Feeling to Cause Before Strategizing

- **What:** A reflective pause between noticing an emotion and choosing a response, used to identify the actual cause before selecting a regulation strategy.
- **How:** When you notice a strong feeling, ask: What just happened? What was I thinking about? What did I want or expect that did not occur? What do I fear might happen? The answers reveal whether the underlying emotion is disappointment (unmet expectations), anger (perceived injustice), envy (social comparison), fear (threat), or something else — each requiring a different strategy. Journaling accelerates this process by revealing recurring patterns and triggers.
- **When:** Before responding to conflict; when your emotional reaction feels disproportionate; when a default strategy (like breathing exercises) is not working.
- **Ref:** [01:25:00](transcript.md#012500-understanding-cause-of-emotions-stress-envy)
