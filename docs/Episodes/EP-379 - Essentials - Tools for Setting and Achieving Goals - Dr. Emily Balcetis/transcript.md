## [00:00:21] Adjusting Vision to Meet Goals, Exercise, Tool: Narrow Visual Target

**Andrew Huberman:** For a long time, because as a vision scientist who is also very interested in real life tools and goal setting and motivation, your work lands squarely in the middle of those interests. Just to kick things off, you could tell us just a little bit about goal setting and goal retrieval. What's the deal with vision and motivation? How do those two things link up?

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** Yeah, totally. When psychologists ask people like, "How are you — what are you doing to help make progress on your goals?" They say all kinds of things. A couple things always pop to the top, which is, you know, self-pep talks or "I remind myself of how important it is to do this" or put up post-it notes around to like constantly be nagging me about what I need to do. All of that takes a lot of time and effort and commitment. And so, what a surprise that people burn out, right? It's exciting to work on a goal when you first set it. You might make some initial progress, but then eventually we get, you know, not even to the halfway point before things get real — things are challenging and we fall by the wayside.

So then I, with my team, was trying to think of like, well, what are strategies that don't require as much effort that we can automate, that we can take advantage of what's already happening within ourselves, within our body, within our mind, that might overcome one of those challenges? And that's when we started to land on the idea of vision. We thought, you know what, there are strategies that we can use to look at the world in a different way and that we can automate, that might help us to overcome some obstacles, to make progress on our goals, to maybe literally see opportunities that we hadn't been able to see before.

**Andrew Huberman:** You've published a number of studies in this area, but maybe you could highlight some of the more important findings in the area of how people can adjust their vision in order to meet goals more quickly and more efficiently.

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** So, we started thinking about what are the goals that are most important to people that they struggle with the most. And regardless of where you look or who you ask or when you ask it, people's number one goal is something related to their health. So, one of the first things that I did was go over to Brooklyn. There's a couple armories all around the boroughs here around New York City. And the one in Brooklyn in particular is now a YMCA. A physical therapist said, "Hey, you should come out and check out what's happening here with your interest in exercise and trying to find new ways of helping people, new tactics that they can add to their tool belt." And it turns out, these are some of the fastest runners in the world — like one of the people there had won the gold medal for the 400 meter at the last Olympics before I showed up.

I thought when these people are running, I bet they are hyper-aware of everything that's going on in their surroundings. Where are they relative to the competition? What's happening in their peripheral vision? Who's behind them? Who's in front of them? They probably have this master visual plan at any point in time. And that's probably what makes them elite. So when I started asking them, is that the case? They said no. Like, all of them said no. And sometimes when I do that, it's a mistake. So that was surprising — it totally went against my intuition.

What they said instead was that they are hyperfocused. They assume this narrowed focus of attention, almost like a spotlight is shining on a target. When they're running a short distance, that target might literally be the finish line. If it's a longer distance, they set sub-goals — like the person with shorts up ahead that they're trying to beat, or some stable landmark like a sign they'll pass by. Like a spotlight is shining just on that, or like they have blinders on the sides of their face. That's all they're paying attention to.

And I thought, well, that's something we can play with, right? They are elite and accomplished. So then we started thinking, okay, what about people who aren't competitive runners? Is this a tactic we can teach people? The answer is yes. You can tell people about what these Olympic athletes are doing. Imagine that there's a spotlight shining just on a target. Choose something up ahead — the stop sign two blocks up that you can just see. Imagine that you have blinders on so that you're not really paying attention to the people passing by or the buildings or the garbage cans or the trucks. Tune those out and focus in on that target until you hit it, then choose another one — sort of recalibrate and choose the next goal.

One of the first studies that we did was teach that strategy and compare it against a group that we said "just look around naturally." We gave them a finish line, created an exercise that's moderately challenging but possible. We put ankle weights on that accounted for about 15% of their body weight and told them to lift their knees up, sort of high-stepping to a finish line. Some of these people had narrowed their focus of attention and some were just looking more expansively or naturally.

What we found is that those people we trained — just everyday normal people doing this moderately challenging exercise — they were able to move 27% faster. They could do the exercise more quickly, and they said it hurt 17% less. Everybody was in the same circumstance, but their experience was really different. It meant that this strategy could be used on people who are not elite athletes. A quick training session can teach people to look at the world in a different way, and it had a big outcome — a big difference in the way that they were engaged in the exercise.

**Andrew Huberman:** Are they focusing on a specific point, or is it kind of the entire horizon of that goal? Because the finish line is indeed a line.

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** In our interviews and focus group studies, it seems like it's more like a circular point. That's in fact what we're teaching people to do. So rather than going broadly looking across a line from left to right, we are encouraging them to imagine a circle of light shining on some target. Now, of course, a finish line is a line, but if they're staying in their lane, on a track, you can imagine there is a circle shining just on where in their lane they'll cross that finish line. Or if it's a stop sign, you could imagine a circle of light illuminating that. That's what we're teaching people and what seems to be effective — to maintain that focus rather than being pulled to engage with peripheral vision.

And there are some amazing runners in history, like Joan Benoit Samuelson. She's one of the first female marathon competitors who has won multiple marathons, and she talks about finding the shorts on somebody ahead of her and focusing on those shorts until she passes them, then resetting that goal.

## [00:07:39] Goal Setting, Do Vision Boards Work?

**Andrew Huberman:** Can all of us use this strategy to make the starting line a goal point? Because for a lot of people it's not about going from start to finish — it's about getting to start. Is there any physiology to reflect the idea that maybe just visually focusing on the start line would actually get me more excited to engage in effort?

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** There's certainly vision science tied up in that very first stage of goal setting, like identifying what that goal is in the first place and taking those first steps. A lot of people's go-to strategies that involve vision are vision boards or dream boards or post-it notes — they're creating some sort of visual representation of what it is that they want to accomplish, almost like a scrapbook collecting visual icons that reflect where you want to be to motivate yourself. It's a really common tactic that's effective for identifying what you want, but it may not actually be effective for helping you to meet the goal, to get the job done.

Colleagues of mine at New York University have probed why. Why is it that just thinking about what you want in your life and putting yourself vicariously into those shoes, imagining what your life will be like if you can accomplish everything on this list — why doesn't that work? Well, first of all, does it work? The answer is no. Why does it not work? Because what happens — colleagues Gabriele Oettingen and her research team have found — is that going through and dreaming about or visualizing how great your life will be when you get X, Y, and Z done is like a goal satisfied. You have identified what you want. You've experienced it even if just in an imaginary way. You've had that positive experience of thinking about how great your life is going to be when you get this thing done, and you start to sort of rest on your laurels.

She's actually measured systolic blood pressure and heart rate. People who do that — who go through that experience of visualizing how great life will be — their systolic blood pressure decreases. Now, I'm all about finding ways to relax, but motivation scientists know that systolic blood pressure is actually an indicator of the body's readiness to get up and act, to do something. Systolic blood pressure actually goes up in anticipation of your body or your mind needing to do something, taking the first steps on a goal.

So it helps us understand: if I've just created this dream board and put myself psychologically in that space of a goal satisfied, why is it bad that blood pressure goes down? Because it means your body is chilling out — like, cool, I just accomplished something pretty major, I actually now don't have the physiological resources at the ready to take the first step right now. Creating these dream boards, these vision boards or to-do lists, might actually backfire because in and of itself it is the creation of a goal and the satisfaction of the goal, and then people give themselves time to just enjoy that positive experience.

## [00:11:16] Tool: Effectively Plan Goals, Plan for Obstacles

**Andrew Huberman:** Everything you're saying is consistent with what we know about the physiology of dopamine circuits for motivation. I have a good friend who is a cardiologist at a major university who said that one of the major errors people make with book writing is they will tell people they're going to write a book, people will say "oh, you definitely should write a book, everyone's going to love it," and they never end up writing it. His theory is that they get so much dopamine reward from that immediate feedback, with all the protection of never having the book criticized, that they never write the book. What's the better strategy?

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** I'm not saying that people who enjoy dream board creation should stop, but the process of goal setting shouldn't stop with articulating what the goal is. At that same point that we're trying to figure out what we want to do, what is my vision for the future — in those planning sessions, we need to simultaneously think about a couple other things.

One is: how are we going to get there? Take it out of the abstract, take it out of this idyllic visual iconography and start thinking about the practical day-to-day. We need to break it down into more manageable goals. Not just my 10-year plan, but my two-week plan. What can I accomplish in the next two weeks, and the two weeks after that, that's going to set me on the right trajectory? Plan big picture, but also break it down more concretely.

Then Gabriele Oettingen has identified a third, often overlooked stage that has to happen in the goal setting process: thinking about the obstacles that stand in your way. And sometimes people think that's counterintuitive — like, if I want to increase my motivation, I need to think about how hard it's going to be, all the ways I'm going to fail? Yes, because it's like coming up with a plan B, plan C, plan D in advance of actually experiencing that.

If you were on a boat and the boat started to sink, that's not the time you want to start looking for life jackets. You already want to know where one is so you can go to it right away. Same thing with goal setting — you want to know what you're working towards, how you're going to get there, and if you experience this obstacle, here's what you're going to do about it. You may never experience that obstacle, but if you do, you're probably going to be shy on time, thin on resources, maybe experiencing an anxiety that hijacks your brain so you're not functioning at that optimal level of judgment and decision-making. You want to already have the snap next step in place so you can just hop to it.

Michael Phelps — incredible athlete, right? Back in 2008, the Beijing Olympics, he was on the brink of doing something no one else in Olympic history had done: win eight gold medals in a single Olympiad. At the time of this story, he had already won seven and had just the 200 butterfly in front of him. And the fly is his thing — this should have been easy. As soon as he dove into the pool, his goggles started to leak. By the time he had done three lengths and flipped around, his goggles were completely filled with water and he was swimming blind.

I would have panicked. I would have sunk to the bottom of the pool. But for him, it wasn't a moment of panic like it probably would have been for nearly every other person, because he had foreshadowed that kind of possible failure. He had imagined that obstacle hitting him in advance and not even just imagined it — he had practiced it. He routinely practiced swimming with his goggles not fully secured on his face. His coach would rip the goggles off his head and smash them on the ground so that he didn't even have any goggles to grab during practice.

So because he had foreshadowed that possibility and the solution — if my goggles start to leak, I will start counting my strokes — he knew exactly how many strokes it would take to get from one end of the pool to the other. He started counting his strokes, won the 200 fly, won his eighth gold medal, and would go on to win 15 more in his career.

We might not all be swimmers. We might not all aspire to Olympic-level performance. But I love that example because it helps demystify and give us an alternative perspective on the importance and the motivational reasons why thinking about obstacles in advance — thinking about the two, three, four ways your plan might go awry — is actually effective at helping us to overcome the obstacle that might otherwise lead us to throw in the towel.

## [00:16:40] How Fitness Shapes the Way People See the World

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love for you to tell us a bit about how unfit people view the world versus how fit people view the world, or how unmotivated people visually see the world as opposed to highly motivated people. Maybe you could describe that study. I think it's a particularly important one, mostly because it identifies physiological or psychological differences between motivated and unmotivated, fit and unfit people, but it also provides a path to remedy that.

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** So out of my lab and also out of several other labs, there's been work looking at that relation between states of the body and visual experiences. They were looking to see: do visual experiences change as a function of different states of our body? So they've looked at people who experience chronic fatigue, the elderly, people who are overweight, those wearing heavy backpacks. What happens to their perceptions of the environment?

What they find is that distances look further to those that are overweight, chronically tired, older rather than younger, weighted down with extra baggage. Distances look further and hills look steeper. We've done some of those studies too, where we try to give people more energy or deprive them of energy and see does that change their perception of space.

In this case, what we did was give people Kool-Aid to drink. For some people, that Kool-Aid was sweetened with sugar — an actual caloric entity that could give them energy. Other people drank Kool-Aid sweetened with Splenda. It's sweet, but it actually doesn't have any caloric value. When you put it into Kool-Aid, a pretty flavorful powder, it actually masked it for everybody and nobody had any idea. We asked them to guess what they got and they were wrong — nobody was able to guess with accuracy what their drink was sweetened with. We gave them about 10 to 15 minutes for that sugar to metabolize and measured their circulating blood glucose levels. Then we asked people to estimate distance.

What we found is that those people who had been given more energy by drinking Kool-Aid sweetened with sugar perceived their space as more constricted. That visual illusion of proximity was induced — they felt that their finish line was closer to them. In just the same way that people who are chronically tired or physically weighted down perceive the world as harder and more costly, we could create the reverse experience: if you have more energy, the world looks easier, distances to a finish line don't look as far.

Now, as a motivation researcher, the big question is: what's the point? We think that's fundamental to one of the reasons that people experience difficulty when exercising, when it's really harder for your body because of its physical state to move within a space. Because that distance they're supposed to walk, or that hill they should hike, looks more challenging to them than it does to somebody in better physical health. And when you set yourself up psychologically for that kind of failure experience — this looks really hard, I don't know that I have the resources to get this job done — you're already motivationally in a place where this task is closer to impossible for you.

To put it all together: people whose bodies make it more challenging for them to exercise are seeing the world in a more challenging way, and that is having downstream motivational and psychological effects that makes it less likely for them to try to take on the task in the first place, or to experience it as harder than other people do.

## [00:22:03] Visual Spotlight, Exercise & Physical Fitness Level

**Andrew Huberman:** Is the solution the same? Meaning, if these people are taught to adjust their visual goal line or to set a visual spotlight on an intermediate goal, can they overcome some of this challenge they face simply by virtue of their skewed perception?

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** Yes. In all of the studies that we have done looking at that connection between this narrowed focus of attention and improvements in exercise, we do not find that it only works for people who are in shape or that it backfires for people who are out of shape. It works for everybody. This is a strategy everybody can adopt because it's simply about what do you allocate attentional resources to, what do you ignore and what do you focus on. That visually induces the same kind of illusion for everybody, regardless of whether you're overweight or at your target weight or struggling to get there or have already accomplished where you want to be. That visual illusion can be induced for everybody and it has the same kinds of consequences.

## [00:23:02] Stimulants & Motivation

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any studies looking at how adrenaline or epinephrine or any other stimulants impact motivation?

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** If you actually are more physiologically aroused or jazzed or amped up, or you just think you are, in our studies we have found that they work in the same way — it can produce the same kinds of consequences. And I like that because it tells us you can actually change the state of your body to induce these kinds of experiences, or you can just think that you can — you can trick yourself, you can placebo-effect yourself, and produce the same kinds of effects.

I had to give up coffee like 12 years ago. Not for any big reason — I love the taste, so decaf is my thing. But I can't drink the caffeine because it just made me feel sweaty and jittery and anxious and I couldn't focus. And I happen to marry the same kind of person — he also can't drink caffeine but loves the taste of coffee. The interesting thing is that we both have to have coffee in the morning to feel like we're ready to go for the day. So it's just part of our routine — we have that taste and that sensation to feel like we're ready to take on the day. Even though, yeah, decaf still has some caffeine in it, but we're not drinking that much to probably actually create a caffeinated experience in our body — we're tricking ourselves psychologically into doing that thing that in years past used to work for us both.

## [00:24:24] Cognitive Goals, Tools: Overcoming Bad Memories; Deadlines

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love to ask about other kinds of goals, meaning non-physical goals. Is there any example or tactic that people could use to better approach cognitive goals — of school, work — that don't exist in the fitness and sports domain?

**Dr. Emily Balcetis:** Totally. A couple years ago when I was writing the book, I also had a child the same month. And I started to realize I became a lot less interesting once he was around. He was fascinating, but I was changing diapers and feeding him and that was it. People would come over and ask, "What's going on in your life?" And all I had to talk about was that. So I decided I want to learn to play drums and be a one-hit wonder as a rock-star drummer. That's a goal I set for myself at the same time that my son came into this world, when I was also thinking about goal setting and how to improve our ability to get a job done when you're faced with some pretty big obstacles. So I got to practice all these techniques on myself.

One that I found particularly helpful was overcoming my bad memory. Everybody's memories are faulty, right? Everybody has a warped perception of the past. It might be skewed more positively than maybe we deserve, or it might be skewed more negatively — if what looms large in your mind as you reflect on something from the past are the mistakes you've made, the social faux pas, the challenges at work. We probably aren't getting the world right, and that is a problem when it comes to assessing our own goal progress.

When we want to be our own accountant and determine how we're doing — if I want to become a drummer, am I on track for getting there before my time runs out? — and if we just rely on our memory, we're probably going to do a bad job of assessing that trajectory. I found that to be the case as I was thinking about whether I'd actually be able to learn a song in time for a show I'd committed to.

So I decided I was going to put on a show. I was going to invite everybody I knew and also people I didn't know, and I was going to play my one song for them. In the process of figuring out whether I'd be able to play the show, I sent out invitations. The date was committed. People were coming to listen to my one song.

And it felt awful. It just felt like I was not making progress, because there are a lot more things that are pressing — the kid needs to get fed, I have to go to my day job, the editor is asking for the next draft of the book. Things command your bandwidth even when you have this goal you've committed to. So I felt this looming anxiety about a goal that would require committed investment for a sustained period of time. That's because I was relying on my memory and my brain to recall: how many times did you practice? Have you gotten any better? And it felt like no.

Then I thought: I should stop relying on my brain to tell me where I'm at. I need to look at the data. I started to collect data on myself. I downloaded this app called the Reporter app — there are lots of these kinds of things out there. Basically, it just sets up your phone to randomly ping you with whatever questions you want your phone to ask, records your answers, you can download the data and make graphs to see how you've changed over time.

So I did that for a month. I had my phone ask me a couple times a day: did you practice since last time I asked you? If yes, it would funnel a couple other questions like, how did you do? How do you feel? I did that for a month, then downloaded the data. First I took stock before looking at the numbers — how do I think I did over the last month? And I thought: same as every other month, I didn't really get anywhere. Yeah, I practiced but I still feel awful.

But what I found from the data was that my memory was totally wrong. I had practiced far more times than I remembered. And when I looked at my emotion words, it was a clear upward trajectory. Yeah, I did cry — that part I hadn't misremembered. But by the end of that month, I had gotten a compliment from my husband, who is actually a drummer, saying, "Hey, that wasn't that bad."

All of which is to say: I needed to collect that data on myself and look at it objectively, accurately, and completely, because my brain wasn't doing that for me. That visual experience of downloading that data and looking at what my actual experience was gave me a better insight as I was trying to assess the trajectory of my progress. I became a more accurate accountant of my own progress, which is important for setting goals or resetting them when you need to calibrate in light of what's left to do and how much time you have to do it in.
