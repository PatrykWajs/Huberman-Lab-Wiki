## [00:11:38] Cavities & Teeth De-/Remineralization, Fluoride

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about cavities. What is a cavity — what's actually happening in the tooth when a cavity forms?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So our teeth undergo a natural process of demineralization and remineralization all day long. So when we eat, acids are produced. The Streptococcus mutans, or that cavity-causing bacteria, is producing acids to try to dissolve the tooth. And so the tooth will start to de-mineralize. But then in the alkaline state, which is our saliva — saliva is very mineralizing — as long as we're not snacking all day, our saliva can remineralize that tooth. So our teeth can repair themselves. Cavities are largely a lifestyle disease. Ancestrally, you know, we didn't have the rampant amount of cavities we see today.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So when we first look at a tooth and we see early de-mineralization — we see a white spot on the tooth — those can be remineralized. Those can be reversed. And a cavity is simply when that demineralization process has gone beyond the point of return and there's a hole in the tooth. But there's a continuum — early cavities can absolutely be reversed and stopped in their tracks. And so that's really important for people to understand.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the role of fluoride in all of this?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So fluoride is very interesting. It was thought to help re-mineralize teeth. Hydroxyapatite is the main mineral of our teeth, and fluoride can displace the hydroxyl group and create fluorapatite, which is harder and more resistant to acid. So there are pros and cons. The problem is that fluorapatite is much more brittle than hydroxyapatite, and the research really does show that hydroxyapatite — the native mineral of the tooth — is actually superior to fluoride in terms of remineralization without the risks of fluoride. And we'll talk more about fluoride in water, fluoride in toothpaste and the systemic concerns later.

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the toothpaste that you'd recommend? I'm assuming they have hydroxyapatite?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes. So hydroxyapatite toothpastes are my top recommendation. Brands like Risewell and Boka use nano-hydroxyapatite, which is the same mineral naturally found in teeth and bones. They don't have SLS, they don't have harsh abrasives. And the research really does support hydroxyapatite as being as effective or more effective than fluoride toothpaste for cavity prevention.

## [00:19:14] Cavities, Tool: Meal Frequency, Fasting

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned that eating triggers acid production. So what's the ideal meal frequency for oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** This is such an important question. So the Stephan curve is important here — it describes the pH changes in the mouth after eating. After you eat, the pH in your mouth drops significantly for about 20 to 30 minutes. And then your saliva, which is alkaline, buffers it back up to neutral. So if you're grazing all day — snacking every hour — your mouth never returns to that neutral or slightly alkaline state. You're just constantly in an acidic environment, which is constantly demineralizing your teeth.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So ideally we want structured meals — two to three meals a day — with no snacking in between. And this actually aligns really well with intermittent fasting and time-restricted eating from a metabolic standpoint. It's also really beneficial for the oral microbiome. You give your saliva the opportunity to do its job and remineralize your teeth in between meals.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the old wisdom of eating six small meals a day is terrible for oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes. From an oral health standpoint, that's really problematic. Every time you eat, you're triggering that acid cascade. So if you're eating every hour, you're just constantly bathing your teeth in acid. Three meals with no snacking is ideal from a dental standpoint.

## [00:21:51] Sugar, "Dissolvable" Carbs & Cavities, Tools: Feed the Rainbow, Clean Diet

**Andrew Huberman:** What foods are particularly bad for the teeth and what are good?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So it's not just about sugar — it's about fermentable carbohydrates, which are carbohydrates that can be metabolized by the bacteria in our mouth and produce acid. So things like crackers, chips, bread, cereals — these dissolve and stick to the teeth and feed the cavity-causing bacteria just as much as or sometimes more than candy, because candy tends to be rinsed away more easily. Crackers and chips get stuck in the grooves of the teeth.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** What's interesting is that ancestrally, our diets were very different. Weston A. Price documented indigenous populations that had beautiful, wide arches, no cavities, straight teeth — and they were eating whole, unprocessed foods. When those populations adopted a Western diet, they rapidly developed cavities and dental crowding.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about things like citrus and acidic foods?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So acidic foods and drinks — citrus, vinegar, sparkling water — do lower the pH of the mouth and can erode enamel. But the key is: don't brush your teeth immediately after having something acidic. Wait 30 to 60 minutes. The acid softens the enamel and if you brush right away you're actually abrading away softened enamel. Give your saliva time to re-harden the enamel first.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Foods that are really beneficial for teeth include things like cheese, which is high in calcium and phosphate and actually raises the pH in the mouth. Leafy greens are great — high in minerals. Crunchy vegetables like carrots and celery are like nature's toothbrush — they stimulate saliva and actually clean the teeth. The rainbow of vegetables that we're always told to eat is also beneficial for our oral microbiome. Nitrate-rich vegetables — arugula, beets, spinach — are particularly important for the oral bacteria that convert nitrate to nitrite to nitric oxide, which is important for cardiovascular health.

## [00:27:41] White Teeth, Bleaching, Hydroxyapatite, Mouth Breathing

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about teeth whitening. What are the options and what's the science?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the most common teeth whitening methods use hydrogen peroxide or carbamide peroxide, which penetrate the tooth and bleach the chromogens — the pigmented molecules — within the tooth. The problem is that these bleaches are quite harsh on the tooth structure and the pulp. They can cause significant sensitivity and there's evidence that they can actually damage the proteins within the enamel and dentin.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** A much gentler and actually more effective approach is hydroxyapatite toothpaste. Hydroxyapatite is white and it fills in the micro-scratches and voids on the surface of the tooth, which actually makes the teeth look whiter — not by bleaching, but by optical effect. And it strengthens the enamel at the same time. So you get a whitening effect without the damage.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about oil pulling? I hear about people swishing coconut oil. Does that whiten teeth?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Oil pulling can have some whitening effect, yes — it's an emulsifying action where the oil binds to the biofilm and helps pull bacteria and debris off the teeth. It has some whitening effect, some antimicrobial effect, and it supports gum health. Coconut oil specifically has lauric acid, which has some selective antimicrobial properties — it tends to target the pathogenic bacteria while being more gentle on the beneficial ones.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about mouth breathing and its impact on teeth color and health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Mouth breathing is devastating to oral health in many ways. When you mouth breathe, you dry out the mouth — you reduce saliva flow. And saliva is everything. Saliva is antibacterial, antifungal, antiviral. It remineralizes the teeth. It has antimicrobial proteins. When the mouth dries out, you lose all of that protection. The front teeth tend to get the most exposure, which is why mouth breathers often have more cavities on the front teeth and darker, more stained front teeth.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** And it's not just the teeth — mouth breathing changes the entire microbiome of the mouth. When you breathe through your nose, you're filtering, humidifying, and warming the air. Nasal breathing produces nitric oxide in the sinuses which is antibacterial. So mouth breathing is really problematic on multiple levels.

## [00:34:34] Antibiotics, Gut & Oral Microbiome, Tool: Probiotics

**Andrew Huberman:** What happens to the oral microbiome when someone takes antibiotics?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So antibiotics can be really disruptive to both the gut and oral microbiome. They can wipe out the beneficial bacteria, creating a void that can be filled by pathogenic bacteria. After a course of antibiotics, it can take months for the oral microbiome to recover, and in some cases, particularly with multiple courses, it may never fully recover to its original state.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** This is one reason why we try to be very judicious about prescribing antibiotics in dentistry. We have other tools — like ozone therapy, which kills bacteria without disrupting the microbiome, and photobiomodulation or laser therapy.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about taking probiotics after antibiotics? Would that help the oral microbiome?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes, and specifically oral probiotics. We do have lactobacillus and streptococcus strains that are specifically formulated for the oral microbiome. Taking oral probiotics — particularly allowing them to dissolve in the mouth rather than swallowing them — can help repopulate the beneficial bacteria after antibiotic use. There are also products like BLIS K12 and BLIS M18 which are oral-specific probiotic strains that have good evidence for preventing bad breath and supporting overall oral microbiome health.

## [00:36:20] Mouthwash, Alcohol, Astringents, Cardiovascular Risk, Bad Breath

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about mouthwash. People love their mouthwash. What's the concern?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the biggest concern with conventional mouthwashes — Listerine, Scope — is the alcohol content. Alcohol-based mouthwashes kill bacteria indiscriminately. They wipe out the good and the bad. And there are several issues with this. One, as I mentioned, it disrupts the microbiome. Two, it specifically kills the nitrate-reducing bacteria in the mouth — the commensal bacteria on the tongue that convert dietary nitrate from vegetables into nitrite, which then gets converted to nitric oxide in the body. Nitric oxide is crucial for cardiovascular health — it vasodilates blood vessels, it reduces blood pressure. There was actually a landmark study showing that people who used alcohol-based mouthwash twice daily had a significantly increased risk of hypertension compared to those who didn't use mouthwash or used it less frequently.

**Andrew Huberman:** So alcohol mouthwash can increase blood pressure?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes, that's what the research suggests. By killing the nitrate-reducing bacteria, you're impairing your body's ability to produce nitric oxide from dietary nitrates. This is why eating nitrate-rich vegetables like spinach, arugula, and beets is particularly important — but if you're then swishing with alcohol mouthwash twice a day, you're undermining that entire pathway.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** And then in terms of bad breath — alcohol is also drying. It dries out the mouth. A dry mouth is a major contributor to bad breath because bacteria thrive in a dry, anaerobic environment. So you get a temporary fix from the mouthwash freshening your breath, and then it creates a worse environment that produces more bad breath long term.

## [00:42:21] Saliva, Dry Mouth, Salivary Analysis, Tools: Hydration; Nasal Breathing

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about saliva in more detail. You've mentioned it's important — what exactly is it doing?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Saliva is absolutely remarkable. It's about 99% water but that 1% is doing incredible things. It contains antimicrobial proteins like lactoferrin, lysozyme, secretory IgA, which are our first line immune defense. It contains minerals — calcium, phosphate — that remineralize the teeth. It has buffering agents — bicarbonate — that neutralize the acids in the mouth. It has enzymes like amylase that start digesting food. It lubricates, it protects. It's truly multifunctional.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So dry mouth — xerostomia — is a serious condition. And it's incredibly common. Many medications cause dry mouth — antihistamines, antidepressants, antihypertensives, diuretics. Aging itself causes reduced saliva production. Mouth breathing causes dry mouth. And the consequences are significant — increased cavity risk, gum disease, oral infections, difficulty swallowing and speaking.

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some ways to increase saliva production?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Hydration is the number one thing — drink adequate water. Most people are chronically mildly dehydrated. Nasal breathing is crucial. Chewing food — mechanical chewing stimulates salivary gland function. There are also things like xylitol, which stimulates saliva production, and mastic gum, which I love for many reasons. Acupuncture can actually be helpful for dry mouth — there are studies showing that stimulation of certain acupuncture points can increase salivary flow.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can saliva be analyzed to get information about health status?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes! Salivary diagnostics is a really exciting and growing field. You can test the oral microbiome — companies like OraVital, Oral DNA Labs, Bristle Health — they offer saliva testing that can identify the specific bacteria in your oral microbiome. This is incredibly useful because you can identify if you have high levels of pathogenic bacteria like Porphyromonas gingivalis — which is one of the red complex periodontal bacteria implicated in Alzheimer's disease — and then treat specifically. You can also test for HPV in saliva, test inflammatory markers, hormonal markers. It's really a window into systemic health.

## [00:47:23] Mouth vs. Nasal Breathing, Hard & Soft Tissue Issues

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's do a deeper dive on mouth breathing versus nasal breathing. What does chronic mouth breathing actually do to the face and the oral cavity?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So mouth breathing has profound effects starting in childhood that persist into adulthood. In children especially, during the critical period of facial development, mouth breathing can cause what we call adenoid face — the face develops more vertically, the jaw is more narrow and recessed, the palate is narrow and high vaulted, the teeth become crowded because there isn't enough room. The tongue, instead of resting on the palate — where it belongs and where it acts as a natural palate expander — drops to the floor of the mouth. So you lose that palatal expansion force that the tongue is supposed to provide.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** In adults, chronic mouth breathing continues to have effects. You get gum recession. You get more cavities on the front teeth. You get drying and cracking of the lips. You get changes in the soft palate. And of course the systemic issues — you're not getting the nitric oxide produced in the sinuses, you're not filtering and humidifying the air, so the lower airways are more susceptible to infections.

**Andrew Huberman:** And is there a connection to sleep apnea?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Absolutely. Mouth breathing, narrow airways, narrow palates — these are all strongly correlated with sleep-disordered breathing including sleep apnea. The narrow jaw and recessed chin and tongue position can all lead to airway obstruction during sleep. So addressing mouth breathing and structural airway issues is really important for sleep quality and for long-term health.

## [00:54:19] Deviated Septum, Therapies, Kids & Adults, Mouth Breathing & Sleep Disorders

**Andrew Huberman:** What about people with deviated septums — can they really breathe through their nose?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So a deviated septum can certainly impair nasal airflow and many people use it as an excuse — "I can't breathe through my nose because I have a deviated septum." But the reality is, the vast majority of people can learn to nasal breathe even with a deviated septum. James Nestor talks about this beautifully. The nose is remarkably adaptive — when you commit to nasal breathing and keep your mouth closed, the nasal passages tend to open up over time. The turbinates and the nasal mucosa respond to increased airflow.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Now, there are some people with severe structural obstruction where surgical intervention is warranted. But that should not be the first step. First, try myofunctional therapy, nasal breathing training, addressing allergies and inflammation. There are also devices like nasal dilators, nasal strips that can help. Some people have significant improvement with treating allergies — nasal steroids, addressing food sensitivities that contribute to nasal congestion.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's myofunctional therapy?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Myofunctional therapy is essentially physical therapy for the tongue and the muscles of the face and oropharynx. A myofunctional therapist will assess tongue posture, tongue strength, swallowing patterns, lip seal. They use exercises to retrain the oral facial muscles — to get the tongue to rest on the palate, to strengthen the lip seal, to improve nasal breathing patterns. It's incredibly effective especially in kids, and it can also be very helpful in adults. It can help with sleep apnea, snoring, TMJ issues, and of course with transitioning from mouth breathing to nasal breathing.

## [00:01:00:42] Gum Health, Flossing; Sexual Health

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the connection between gum health and sexual health — I think this surprises a lot of people.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So gum disease and periodontal pathogens have been linked to erectile dysfunction. The connection is through systemic inflammation and the nitric oxide pathway. The same bacteria causing gum inflammation are getting into the bloodstream and causing systemic inflammation, which impairs endothelial function — the function of the blood vessel lining. Endothelial function is critical for erections. Nitric oxide is produced by the endothelium and causes vasodilation. So if you have gum disease that's impairing nitric oxide availability and causing endothelial dysfunction, that's going to affect sexual function.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** For women, periodontal disease has been linked to fertility issues and adverse pregnancy outcomes — premature birth, low birth weight, preeclampsia. There's a strong epidemiological connection between gum disease and female reproductive health.

## [00:01:01:50] Shifting to Nasal Breathing, Mouth Taping, Tools: 3-Minute Test, Kiss The Sky

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about practical ways to transition to nasal breathing. You mentioned mouth taping — should people really be taping their mouths shut at night?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Mouth taping at night is something I do recommend for people who are confirmed mouth breathers at night — people who wake up with a dry mouth, who snore, who have been observed to breathe through their mouth during sleep. The concept is to use a gentle tape — Somnifix is a popular brand — just across the lips, not sealing them shut, but providing a gentle reminder to keep the mouth closed. It's not for everyone and it should not be done if you have any concern about airway obstruction or severe sleep apnea without proper evaluation first.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a quick test to determine if you're a nasal breather or mouth breather?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes — there's a simple three-minute test. Simply sit quietly and time how long you can comfortably breathe only through your nose. Most healthy nasal breathers can do this indefinitely. If you find yourself feeling the urge to open your mouth within a few minutes or feeling air-hungry, that suggests you have some work to do. Another quick assessment is to look at your lips in the morning — if they're dry and cracked, you're probably mouth breathing at night.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The "Kiss the Sky" exercise that Brian McKenzie talks about is a great one — it involves chin tucking and opening the throat while breathing through the nose, which helps train the correct posture and airway alignment for nasal breathing. It's a combination of posture work and breathing training.

## [00:01:05:53] Chewing Gum, Mastic Gum, Tool: Xylitol; Chewing Food, Breastfeeding

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on chewing gum — specifically mastic gum and xylitol gum?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Chewing gum in general has benefits — it stimulates saliva production and the mechanical action helps clean the teeth. But the type of gum matters enormously. Sugar-containing gums are obviously counterproductive. Xylitol gum is actually beneficial — xylitol is a sugar alcohol that the cavity-causing bacteria cannot metabolize, so it actually competes with fermentable sugars and starves the pathogenic bacteria. Xylitol also appears to disrupt biofilm formation. A dose of about four to five grams per day of xylitol has good evidence for cavity prevention.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Mastic gum is fascinating. It's a natural resin from the mastic tree from the island of Chios in Greece. It has specific antimicrobial properties against Helicobacter pylori — the bacteria implicated in peptic ulcers and gastric cancer. It's also anti-inflammatory and appears to have beneficial effects on the oral microbiome. I recommend mastic gum particularly for people who have concerns about H. pylori or those looking to improve their oral microbiome. It's also great for the jaw muscles — it's a much harder, more resilient chew than regular gum, so it gives a real workout to the masseter and other chewing muscles.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about jaw development — does chewing harder foods matter?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Enormously. The chewing hypothesis for modern jaw development is really well-supported. Our ancestors ate much tougher, harder foods that required vigorous chewing. This mechanical stimulation drives bone growth in the jaw — the mandible and maxilla respond to mechanical stress by growing wider and more robust. Modern soft diets — everything pureed or processed — mean our jaws don't get that stimulation, and we develop narrow jaws with crowded teeth. Breastfeeding is the best possible start for jaw development in infants — the mechanism of suckling from the breast is very different from bottle feeding and drives proper palatal arch development.

## [00:01:13:15] Gum Disease, "Leaky Gums", Cardiovascular Disease, Dementia, Cancer

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about gum disease and its systemic effects. You mentioned it's linked to cardiovascular disease and dementia.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes. So gum disease — periodontitis — involves infection and inflammation of the gums and the bone supporting the teeth. The key concept is what we can think of as "leaky gums." Just like we talk about leaky gut, the gums can become inflamed and permeable, and bacteria and inflammatory mediators can get into the bloodstream directly from the oral cavity. The gum tissue, when healthy, forms a tight seal around the teeth. When you have gum disease, that seal is broken and you essentially have an open wound — a portal of entry for bacteria into the systemic circulation.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The research linking periodontitis to cardiovascular disease is very robust. Porphyromonas gingivalis and other periodontal pathogens have been found in atherosclerotic plaques in the arteries. These bacteria can travel through the bloodstream and actually colonize the arterial walls and contribute to the inflammatory process of atherosclerosis.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the dementia connection?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The dementia connection is one of the most alarming and compelling areas of research right now. Porphyromonas gingivalis has been detected in the brains of Alzheimer's patients — and not just detected but found to be producing gingipains, which are proteases that chew up brain proteins. Gingipains have been found in the hippocampus, the substantia nigra — areas that are particularly affected in Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease. There are now clinical trials of drugs specifically targeting gingipains as a potential Alzheimer's treatment. It's quite extraordinary that a bacterium originally from the mouth might be driving neurodegeneration.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about cancer?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** There is increasing evidence linking the oral microbiome to various cancers. Fusobacterium nucleatum — another oral bacterium that's elevated in gum disease — has been found in colorectal cancer tumors and appears to promote tumor growth. There are also associations with pancreatic cancer, esophageal cancer, and other GI cancers. And of course, oral HPV infection — which is transmitted through the oral-oral or oral-genital route — is the major driver of oropharyngeal cancers, which are increasing dramatically.

## [00:01:20:05] Antibiotics, Ozone Therapy, Oil Pulling, Mold, Tool: Coconut Oil; Facial Trauma

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about some alternatives to antibiotics in dentistry. You mentioned ozone therapy.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Ozone therapy is one of my favorite tools in my practice. Ozone is O3 — a highly reactive form of oxygen that kills bacteria, viruses, and fungi on contact. And crucially, bacteria cannot develop resistance to ozone — it works through oxidation, not through a biochemical receptor mechanism. We can use ozone gas to treat cavities — and there's really good research showing that ozone can stop early cavities in their tracks, kill the bacteria causing the cavity, and create an environment for remineralization. We can also use ozone-infused water or ozone oil to treat gum disease and other oral infections.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The beautiful thing about ozone is that it's selective — it's much more toxic to bacteria than to human cells, because our cells have antioxidant defenses that bacteria largely lack. So you can effectively sterilize the mouth without disrupting the microbiome the way antibiotics do.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about oil pulling — how does it actually work?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Oil pulling is the practice of swishing oil — traditionally sesame oil in Ayurvedic practice, but coconut oil is popular now — in the mouth for 10 to 20 minutes and then spitting it out. The mechanism is thought to be saponification — a chemical reaction between the oil and the bacteria and debris in the mouth that essentially creates a soap-like effect and emulsifies the bacteria. Coconut oil specifically contains lauric acid, which has antimicrobial properties selective for pathogenic bacteria. There's decent evidence that oil pulling reduces Streptococcus mutans and reduces gingivitis — but the effect size is not huge and it should be seen as an add-on, not a replacement for brushing and flossing.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned mold — what's the connection to oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Mold and mycotoxins are increasingly recognized as an issue in dental materials and in the environment. Some patients who are struggling with unexplained oral symptoms, recurrent infections, unusual fatigue — we're finding that mold exposure and mycotoxin accumulation can be contributing factors. Mold can actually grow in dental materials and in water lines in some dental offices. It's a relatively underexplored area but one I think about a lot in my functional dentistry practice.

## [00:01:28:07] Nicotine Gum & Pouches, Oral Health; Coffee, Teeth Whitening

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the effect of nicotine gum and nicotine pouches on oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So compared to smoking or chewing tobacco, nicotine gum and pouches are obviously much less harmful because they eliminate the combustion products and the tobacco leaf itself. But nicotine itself is not without oral effects. Nicotine is a vasoconstrictor — it reduces blood flow to the gums — and this can mask the signs of gum disease. The gums of nicotine users may not bleed as much even when they have gum disease because the blood supply is reduced. So it can actually mask a problem. Nicotine also impairs wound healing and immune function in the oral tissues, so recovery from oral surgery or gum disease treatment is slower.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Nicotine gum in particular — because it involves chewing and saliva — has less impact on blood flow than pouches, which sit statically against the gum tissue for extended periods. So if someone is going to use nicotine products, gum is probably less harmful to oral health than pouches, which can cause localized gum recession and tissue irritation at the site of repeated contact.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about coffee? Coffee stains teeth but people love their coffee.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Coffee does stain teeth — it contains chromogens that bind to the enamel and pellicle layer on the teeth. It's also acidic. But coffee also has some beneficial effects on the oral microbiome — there are compounds in coffee that appear to be antimicrobial against some pathogenic bacteria. So the net effect of coffee on oral health is complex. The staining can largely be addressed with good oral hygiene — brushing after coffee or at least rinsing with water. And hydroxyapatite toothpaste can help fill in the micro-scratches where chromogens tend to accumulate.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about using coffee whitening strips or products?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Activated charcoal is sometimes marketed for teeth whitening and it does have some stain-removing properties through adsorption. But activated charcoal is quite abrasive and with regular use it can actually abrade away enamel, which is counterproductive. I'd stick with hydroxyapatite toothpaste and good brushing technique. For people who want more significant whitening, in-office whitening using carefully controlled peroxide concentrations is far superior to at-home strips, which people tend to overuse and cause sensitivity and enamel damage.

## [00:01:34:16] Whole Foods, Hydroxyapatite Toothpaste; Tool: Testing Oral Microbiome

**Andrew Huberman:** What's your overall dietary recommendation for oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The foundational dietary principles align with just eating whole, real foods. Minimize ultra-processed foods — they're typically high in fermentable carbohydrates, low in fiber and minerals that support remineralization. Eat plenty of vegetables especially the nitrate-rich ones. Include good protein sources. Good fats — omega-3s are anti-inflammatory and support gum health. Adequate minerals — calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, vitamin D which is critical for calcium absorption and immune function, vitamin K2 which helps direct calcium into the teeth and bones rather than the soft tissues. Vitamin A is important for enamel formation.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned testing the oral microbiome — tell me more about that.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes. Companies like Bristle Health offer at-home saliva testing where you can find out the composition of your oral microbiome. They'll tell you what bacteria you have, which ones are beneficial, which ones are pathogenic, and they give you personalized recommendations based on your results. I think this is really the future of oral health — moving away from a one-size-fits-all approach to a personalized, precision approach based on your actual microbiome. You can track changes over time and see how interventions are working. I recommend this particularly for people who have persistent bad breath, recurrent cavities, or gum disease despite good hygiene habits.

## [00:01:39:39] Water Fluoridation, History, Other Fluoride Sources, Neurocognitive Issues

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about water fluoridation. This is a very timely topic. What's the history and what's the science?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the history of water fluoridation is fascinating. It starts in the early 20th century with a dentist named Frederick McKay who noticed that people in certain areas of Colorado had brown-stained but cavity-resistant teeth. The staining was eventually traced to naturally occurring fluoride in the water. Further research led to the conclusion that at certain concentrations, fluoride in water seemed to reduce cavity rates. By 1945, Grand Rapids, Michigan became the first city to deliberately add fluoride to its water supply, and the practice spread throughout the US.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The original evidence for water fluoridation came from a very different era — a different diet, different dental hygiene practices, different exposures. And while fluoride does have some benefit for teeth, we're now in a situation where we have multiple sources of fluoride exposure — fluoridated toothpaste, fluoride treatments at the dentist, fluoride in processed foods made with fluoridated water, fluoride in some medications, fluoride in certain pesticide residues. The total fluoride exposure is much higher than it was in the 1940s when the original studies were done.

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the neurocognitive concerns?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** This is where it gets really important and really controversial. There is a growing body of evidence — including a meta-analysis of 55 studies, mostly from China — showing an association between higher fluoride exposure and lower IQ in children. More recently, the National Toxicology Program, which is part of the NIH, released a systematic review in 2024 concluding with moderate confidence that fluoride is associated with lower IQ in children. This was a significant finding from a U.S. government agency and it's part of what has led to renewed calls to reconsider water fluoridation.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The mechanism may involve fluoride's effects on the thyroid — fluoride is a known thyroid disruptor, and thyroid function is critical for brain development in children. Fluoride can also cross the blood-brain barrier and accumulate in the pineal gland, which is particularly susceptible.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about at current levels in US water — typically 0.7 ppm?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the US currently uses 0.7 milligrams per liter, which was reduced from 1 milligram per liter in 2015. Many of the studies showing neurocognitive effects were done at higher levels — above 1.5 ppm, which is the WHO guideline for safe levels. But some of the more recent research suggests effects at lower levels, and when you add up all the other sources of fluoride exposure, the cumulative dose even from 0.7 ppm water may be higher than intended. Also, certain populations are more vulnerable — formula-fed infants who have fluoridated water mixed into their formula, people with kidney disease who excrete fluoride less efficiently, people with hypothyroidism.

## [00:01:54:57] Drinking Water & Fluoride, Toothpaste, Cavities

**Andrew Huberman:** If someone wants to reduce their fluoride exposure from water, what are the options?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the main options are filtration systems. Reverse osmosis is the most effective — it removes 90% or more of fluoride from water. Activated alumina filters also remove fluoride. Standard carbon filters — Brita, PUR — do not remove fluoride. So if you want to remove fluoride from water, you need a dedicated reverse osmosis system or an activated alumina filter. Many people are now choosing to use filtered or bottled water for drinking and cooking, especially for infant formula preparation.

**Andrew Huberman:** And fluoride in toothpaste — is that a concern?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So fluoride in toothpaste is a topical exposure — you're supposed to spit it out. The concern is swallowing, especially with children who swallow a significant amount of toothpaste. This is why fluoride toothpaste for children under 3 should be a rice-grain amount and for 3 to 6 should be a pea-sized amount. But given that we now have hydroxyapatite toothpaste that performs as well or better than fluoride toothpaste for cavity prevention without the fluoride exposure, I recommend hydroxyapatite toothpaste as the default, especially for children and pregnant women.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** For adults who are at high cavity risk and are already well-informed about the trade-offs, fluoride toothpaste used correctly — brushed in, spit out, not rinsed — can still be a reasonable choice. But for most people, especially families with children, I would switch to hydroxyapatite.

## [00:02:01:07] Water Fluoridation & Levels

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's continue on water fluoridation. When was the level in the US changed?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The US Public Health Service lowered the recommended level from 1.0 ppm to 0.7 ppm in 2015. This was based partly on data showing that fluorosis — the cosmetic mottling of teeth from excess fluoride — had increased significantly, with over 60% of adolescents showing some degree of fluorosis by recent estimates. Dental fluorosis is largely cosmetic — it doesn't affect tooth function — but it was recognized as evidence that Americans were getting too much fluoride.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a consensus view on water fluoridation in the dental and public health communities?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** There is a mainstream consensus that water fluoridation at 0.7 ppm is safe and effective, and major dental organizations like the American Dental Association still support it. But there is a growing number of scientists and health professionals — myself included — who believe that given the newer evidence on neurodevelopmental effects and the availability of alternative cavity prevention strategies, it's time to seriously reconsider universal water fluoridation. The cost-benefit calculus has changed. The original cost-benefit was that people didn't have fluoride toothpaste and didn't have good dental care access. Now we do have those things, and we also have hydroxyapatite. The question is whether the risks to brain development are worth the marginal benefit to teeth when we have better alternatives.

## [00:02:04:52] Oral Health & Fertility

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned the connection between oral health and fertility. Can you go deeper on that?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes. So for women, there is quite robust data linking periodontitis to subfertility — it takes longer to conceive if you have gum disease. There are associations with premature birth, low birth weight, preeclampsia, and gestational diabetes. The mechanisms involve systemic inflammation and the oral bacteria themselves — Fusobacterium nucleatum has been found in the placenta of women who had preterm births, suggesting these bacteria can travel from the mouth to the reproductive organs.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** For men, there's the connection to erectile dysfunction I mentioned earlier, which of course affects fertility. But also, there are emerging links between oral bacteria — particularly through the inflammation pathway — and sperm quality. Chronic systemic inflammation impairs spermatogenesis and sperm motility.

**Andrew Huberman:** So if a couple is trying to conceive, oral health should be part of the picture?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Absolutely. And this is something that the mainstream fertility world is starting to recognize. I think oral health should be part of any preconception workup. Get a comprehensive periodontal evaluation. If you have gum disease, treat it before trying to conceive. Make sure you're using a good, non-toxic oral care routine. It's a relatively easy intervention with potentially significant benefits for fertility outcomes.

## [00:02:07:03] Toothbrushing, Flossing, Waterpik, Tool: Toothbrushing & Meals

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the mechanics of oral hygiene. When should you brush your teeth?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the timing of toothbrushing relative to meals is important and somewhat counterintuitive. If you've eaten something acidic — coffee, orange juice, citrus — you should wait 30 to 60 minutes before brushing. As I mentioned, the acid softens the enamel and brushing immediately after can abrade the softened enamel. So it's actually better to brush before breakfast in the morning, which also disrupts the overnight biofilm buildup before you swallow it or ingest food.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** At night before bed is the most critical brushing — you want to remove the day's biofilm buildup before sleep, because saliva flow decreases during sleep and the mouth is more vulnerable to bacterial activity overnight. So brush at night without fail. And if you can only do one thing — brush at night.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about electric versus manual toothbrush?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Electric toothbrushes have good evidence for superior plaque removal compared to manual — particularly oscillating-rotating types like Oral-B. But technique matters a lot for manual brushing. The Modified Bass technique — angling the brush at 45 degrees to the gum line and using short circular or vibrating strokes — is very effective. The key mistake most people make is brushing too hard, which can cause gum recession and enamel abrasion. You should feel like you're massaging your gums, not scrubbing them.

**Andrew Huberman:** And flossing?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Flossing is essential — you can't clean between the teeth without it. The standard C-shaped technique — wrapping the floss in a C around each tooth and sliding it up under the gumline — is ideal. Floss picks are better than not flossing but not as effective as proper string floss. Waterpiks — water flossers — are excellent adjuncts and are particularly good for people with braces, bridges, implants, or anyone who struggles with string floss. They're very effective at flushing debris and bacteria from the gum line.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** For optimal gum health, I recommend string floss first, then brushing, and a Waterpik as an addition, especially before bed. The sequence matters — floss before brushing so you break up the biofilm and then brush it away.

## [00:02:13:23] Teeth Spots & Markings, Fluorosis, Hypoplastic Enamel

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the white spots and markings that some people have on their teeth?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** There are a few different causes. White spot lesions that are chalky and opaque — those are often early demineralization. If you catch them early, these can often be remineralized with hydroxyapatite or with careful dietary and hygiene interventions. Fluorosis — which is caused by excessive fluoride intake during the period of tooth formation in childhood — presents as white spots, streaks, or in more severe cases, brown staining and pitting of the enamel. Hypoplastic enamel is another condition where the enamel didn't form properly during development — this can be caused by illness, fever, nutritional deficiencies, or certain medications during the period of enamel formation in childhood.

**Andrew Huberman:** What age does enamel form?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** So the primary teeth start forming in utero — maternal nutrition and health during pregnancy directly affects the enamel formation of the primary teeth. Permanent teeth start forming around birth and the enamel of most permanent teeth forms in the first few years of life. The third molars form last — enamel formation continues into the teenage years. So early childhood nutrition and health has a profound and lasting impact on the structural integrity of the permanent teeth.

## [00:02:19:10] Oral Health, Women, Pregnancy, Menopause & Burning Mouth

**Andrew Huberman:** How do hormonal changes affect oral health in women?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Women's oral health is deeply intertwined with hormones. During puberty, elevated estrogen and progesterone make the gums more sensitive to bacteria — puberty gingivitis is a real thing. During pregnancy, the hormonal milieu makes women more susceptible to gingivitis — "pregnancy gingivitis" is extremely common and if it progresses to periodontitis, it has implications for the pregnancy as we discussed. During menopause, the decline in estrogen leads to decreased bone density including the jaw bone, changes in saliva production and composition, and can cause oral symptoms including burning mouth syndrome.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Burning mouth syndrome is a real and often debilitating condition — a chronic burning sensation in the mouth, usually on the tongue and lips. It's more common in perimenopausal and postmenopausal women and the connection to estrogen decline is thought to involve changes in the mucosal lining, changes in saliva, and potentially effects on the trigeminal nerve. Treatment is challenging but addressing the hormone decline, optimizing nutrition, and addressing any underlying nutritional deficiencies — B12, iron, zinc — can be helpful.

## [00:02:23:16] Geographic Tongue; Lip Balm; Mouth Breathing, Symmetrical Chewing

**Andrew Huberman:** What is geographic tongue?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Geographic tongue is a benign but visually striking condition where smooth, red patches appear on the tongue surrounded by white borders, giving the tongue a map-like appearance. The patches migrate over time. It's thought to be related to immune function — there are associations with psoriasis, with food sensitivities, particularly gluten. It tends to flare with stress and with certain foods. It's generally harmless but can be a sign of systemic inflammatory states and is worth addressing underlying factors.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about lip balm use — is there a dependency issue?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** There's a phenomenon of "lip balm addiction" — the more you use lip balm, the more you need it. The reason is that lip balm creates an occlusive barrier that prevents the lips from producing their own natural moisturizing factors and may actually cause the lips to produce less oil themselves over time. The best approach for chronically dry lips is nasal breathing — keeping the mouth closed so the lips aren't constantly evaporating moisture — and addressing any nutritional deficiencies. If you need lip balm, look for one without camphor, menthol, or phenol, which can be irritating.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about chewing symmetrically?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Symmetrical chewing is really important and something very few people think about. Most people have a dominant chewing side and chew predominantly on one side. This creates asymmetric muscle development — the masseter and temporalis muscles on the dominant side become more developed — which can contribute to TMJ issues, facial asymmetry, and uneven tooth wear. I encourage patients to consciously chew on both sides equally. This takes some practice but over time it can improve facial symmetry, reduce TMJ strain, and distribute tooth wear more evenly.

## [00:02:27:09] Tongue Tie, Chewing & Speech Function, Intervention

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about tongue ties. What is a tongue tie and when does it need to be treated?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** A tongue tie — or ankyloglossia — is when the frenulum, the piece of tissue connecting the tongue to the floor of the mouth, is too short, too thick, or attaches too far forward, restricting the tongue's range of motion. There's also a lip tie — the frenulum between the upper lip and the gum can be restrictive as well. And these can occur together.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Tongue ties in infants can cause breastfeeding difficulties — the baby can't latch properly, the mother experiences pain, the baby doesn't transfer milk efficiently and may not gain weight adequately. In older children, tongue ties can affect speech — certain sounds require the tongue tip to contact the palate or the front teeth, and a restricted tongue can't do this. They can also contribute to mouth breathing, as the tongue can't rest on the palate. In adults, they can contribute to TMJ dysfunction, neck tension, sleep-disordered breathing, and swallowing issues.

**Andrew Huberman:** When should it be treated and what's the treatment?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** A frenectomy — the release of the frenulum — can be done with a laser or scissors. In infants, it's a very quick procedure and if done early — in the first few weeks of life if breastfeeding is significantly affected — can be transformative. In older children and adults, it should ideally be combined with myofunctional therapy before and after the procedure. Without myofunctional therapy, the tongue may not learn to use its new range of motion and the benefits can be limited. The timing and indication really needs individualized assessment — not every tongue tie needs to be treated. Functional assessment is key.

## [00:02:32:27] Red Light Therapy, Peptides & Exosomes

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned Joovv and red light therapy earlier. What's the connection to oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Red light and near-infrared light therapy — photobiomodulation — has some very interesting applications in dentistry. It can accelerate wound healing after oral surgery. It can reduce inflammation in the gums. There's evidence that it can help with dry mouth by stimulating the salivary glands. It may also have some benefits for bone healing after tooth extraction or implant placement. In my practice, I use a handheld red light device for post-procedural care. It's gentle, non-invasive, and patients respond really well to it.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about peptides and exosomes in oral health?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** These are cutting edge and exciting. Certain peptides — like BPC-157 — have been studied for tissue repair and may have applications for oral wound healing and for supporting the oral mucosal barrier. Exosomes — the small vesicles released by cells — are being investigated in regenerative dentistry for applications like stimulating pulp regeneration, promoting bone regeneration around implants, and potentially even remineralizing enamel. This is very much on the frontier. We're not at the point where I'm routinely prescribing these for oral use, but the science is compelling and I think we'll see more clinical applications in the coming years.

## [00:02:34:40] Mercury Fillings, Ceramic Composites, Retainers, Sealants

**Andrew Huberman:** Should people have their mercury amalgam fillings removed?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** This is a nuanced question. Old amalgam fillings — silver fillings — do contain approximately 50% mercury by weight. The concern is that over time they can release small amounts of mercury vapor, particularly when you chew, grind, or expose them to hot liquids. Mercury vapor is neurotoxic. However, removing amalgam fillings is not risk-free — the removal process itself can release significantly more mercury than the filling would release just sitting there. So I do not recommend indiscriminate removal of stable amalgam fillings.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The approach I recommend is: when a filling needs to be replaced anyway because it's failing, replace it with a ceramic composite or another biocompatible alternative. If someone is highly concerned and has multiple large amalgam fillings and has symptoms potentially related to mercury toxicity — neurological symptoms, unexplained fatigue, etc. — then removal with a SMART protocol dentist who takes precautions to minimize exposure during removal is worth considering. The SMART protocol — Safe Mercury Amalgam Removal Technique — involves rubber dams, high-volume suction, sectioning the amalgam into large chunks rather than drilling it, and providing oxygen during the procedure.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about plastic in retainers and sealants?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** This is a growing concern. Many dental materials contain BPA or BPA derivatives — including some composite resins and sealants. The bisphenol compounds can be estrogenic and there are concerns about endocrine disruption. I choose BPA-free materials in my practice. For retainers — both fixed wire retainers bonded behind the teeth and removable clear retainers — there are questions about the plastics they're made from. The clear plastic aligners and retainers can also potentially leach compounds, especially if they're put in with hot liquids or exposed to acidic foods and drinks. This is an area where the science is still evolving but I encourage people to avoid using their retainers with hot liquids and to choose more biocompatible materials when they have options.

## [00:02:39:08] Dentists, Depression, Anxiety, Suicide; Insurance

**Andrew Huberman:** There is a well-known statistic about dentists having high rates of depression and suicide. Can you address that?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Yes. Dentistry does have one of the highest rates of depression, anxiety, and burnout of any profession, and the suicide rate among dentists is significantly elevated — estimates suggest twice the rate of the general population. There are multiple contributing factors. The physical demands of the job — working in very precise, confined spaces in awkward postures for hours — lead to high rates of musculoskeletal problems. The financial stress — dental school debt is enormous and the business of running a practice is stressful. Patient anxiety — most patients are anxious or sometimes hostile about dental visits, which creates a chronic low-level stressor. The lack of systemic recognition — dentistry has been siloed from medicine and often undervalued despite its critical role in health.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** And then there's the insurance system. Dental insurance in the US is actually not insurance in the meaningful sense — it's more of a dental benefit plan with very low annual maximums — typically $1,000 to $2,000 per year — that haven't changed significantly since the 1970s despite massive inflation. The reimbursement rates for many procedures don't cover the actual cost of providing care. So dentists are caught between wanting to provide good care and the economic constraints of a broken insurance model.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's incredibly frustrating, especially given what we now know about the systemic importance of oral health.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Exactly. The fact that dental care is separated from medical insurance in the US is a systemic failure. If we recognized oral health as the window to systemic health that it is, we would integrate dental care into medical care, cover comprehensive dental care the way we cover other medical care, and we'd likely see significant reductions in cardiovascular disease, dementia, adverse pregnancy outcomes, and cancer. The cost savings would be enormous. It's a policy failure as much as it's a clinical gap.

## [00:02:45:05] Recap, Top Behavioral Tools for Oral Health, Tongue Scraping

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's end with practical takeaways. If someone wants to drastically improve their oral health starting today, what are the top behavioral tools?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Let me give you my top tools. First — nasal breathing. This is foundational. Work toward breathing through your nose 24/7. If you're a chronic mouth breather, start with nose breathing during rest and sleep and use mouth tape at night if appropriate. Second — optimize your diet. Eat whole, unprocessed foods. Reduce fermentable carbohydrates — especially the snacking frequency. Include nitrate-rich vegetables. Ensure adequate vitamins D, K2, A, and minerals. Third — switch to hydroxyapatite toothpaste. Ditch the SLS, ditch the harsh mouthwashes. Fourth — brush at night without fail, ideally after flossing. Brush before breakfast in the morning. Wait 30 to 60 minutes after acidic foods before brushing. Fifth — floss daily with proper technique, or use a Waterpik.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Sixth — use xylitol gum or mints between meals rather than snacking. Seven — consider oil pulling with coconut oil 3 to 5 times per week for 10 to 15 minutes as an add-on. Eight — use a tongue scraper every morning — scraping from the back of the tongue forward removes the biofilm that accumulates overnight and is a major source of bad breath and bacterial load.

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell me about tongue scraping — how to do it properly.

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** Tongue scraping is one of the most impactful and underused oral hygiene tools. Use a U-shaped stainless steel or copper tongue scraper — not a plastic one and definitely not just your toothbrush, which just redistributes bacteria. Extend your tongue, place the scraper as far back as you can comfortably, and with gentle pressure, draw it forward to the tip. Rinse the scraper and repeat 5 to 10 times until you're not bringing up any more white or yellow coating. Do this first thing in the morning before eating or drinking. Copper has natural antimicrobial properties, so copper scrapers have an edge.

**Andrew Huberman:** Any final thoughts?

**Dr. Staci Whitman:** The key message is that the mouth is not a separate entity — it is the entrance to the body and the gut. What happens in the mouth profoundly affects every system in the body. Oral health is not just about aesthetics. It's about longevity, about cardiovascular health, about brain health, about fertility, about immune function. And conversely, systemic health affects oral health — it goes both ways. We need to stop treating dentistry as a separate silo and start integrating it into a comprehensive whole-body approach to health. And the good news is that the practical steps to dramatically improve oral health are really within everyone's reach — they largely involve diet, breathing, and simple hygiene practices that don't require a lot of expensive products or procedures.
