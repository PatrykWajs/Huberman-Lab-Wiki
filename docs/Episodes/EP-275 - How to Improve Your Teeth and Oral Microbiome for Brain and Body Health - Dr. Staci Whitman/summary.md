# Episode 275: How to Improve Your Teeth & Oral Microbiome for Brain & Body Health | Dr. Staci Whitman

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Guest Dr. Staci Whitman, DMD — a functional dentist treating adult and pediatric patients — reframes oral health from an aesthetic concern into a systemic health pillar. The conversation covers the oral microbiome, how common oral care products damage beneficial bacteria, the science of cavity remineralization, the fluoride controversy, and the far-reaching systemic consequences of gum disease — from cardiovascular disease and dementia to fertility. Practical tools for brushing, flossing, nasal breathing, tongue scraping, and diet are provided throughout, along with a clear protocol to dramatically improve oral health starting today.

## Key Takeaways

- **The mouth is the gut.** The oral cavity is the entrance to the gastrointestinal tract. Pathogenic oral bacteria translocate to the bloodstream and drive systemic disease — cardiovascular disease, dementia, cancer, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. [00:02:04](transcript.md#000204-oral-health--oral-microbiome)

- **Conventional oral care products cause harm.** Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS), alcohol-based mouthwashes, and strong essential-oil toothpastes disrupt the oral microbiome, dry the mouth, and kill beneficial bacteria. Toothpaste should not foam or burn; less is more. [00:05:01](transcript.md#000501-oral-healthcare-ingredients-sodium-lauryl-sulfate-sls-canker-sores)

- **Teeth can remineralize — cavities are largely a lifestyle disease.** Early demineralization (white spot lesions) can be reversed. The key is restoring saliva-driven remineralization by reducing acid exposure time and switching to hydroxyapatite toothpaste. [00:11:38](transcript.md#001138-cavities--teeth-de-remineralization-fluoride)

- **Meal frequency matters as much as food choice for cavity risk.** Every eating event triggers a 20–30 minute acid cascade (the Stephan curve). Continuous grazing prevents the mouth from returning to neutral pH. Two to three structured meals with no snacking dramatically reduces cavity risk. [00:19:14](transcript.md#001914-cavities-tool-meal-frequency-fasting)

- **Alcohol mouthwash increases blood pressure by impairing nitric oxide production.** Alcohol kills the nitrate-reducing bacteria on the tongue that convert dietary nitrates into nitric oxide. Using alcohol mouthwash twice daily is associated with elevated hypertension risk. [00:36:20](transcript.md#003620-mouthwash-alcohol-astringents-cardiovascular-risk-bad-breath)

- **Mouth breathing is devastating to oral and systemic health.** Chronic mouth breathing dries the mouth, raises cavity risk, changes jaw and facial development in children (adenoid face, narrow palate, tongue dropped from palate), contributes to sleep-disordered breathing, and bypasses nasal nitric oxide production. Nasal breathing is the single most impactful structural intervention for oral health. [00:47:23](transcript.md#004723-mouth-vs-nasal-breathing-hard--soft-tissue-issues)

- **Gum disease ("leaky gums") is a portal for systemic infection.** Porphyromonas gingivalis and other periodontal bacteria travel through inflamed gum tissue into the bloodstream. P. gingivalis has been detected in atherosclerotic plaques and in the brains of Alzheimer's patients, where it produces gingipains that damage brain tissue; clinical trials now target gingipains as an Alzheimer's treatment. [01:13:15](transcript.md#011315-gum-disease-leaky-gums-cardiovascular-disease-dementia-cancer)

- **The NTP (NIH) concluded with moderate confidence in 2024 that fluoride is associated with lower IQ in children.** Cumulative fluoride exposure from water, toothpaste, processed foods, and pesticide residues is higher than in the 1940s when fluoridation began. Hydroxyapatite toothpaste is as effective or superior to fluoride for cavity prevention without neurodevelopmental risk. [01:39:39](transcript.md#013939-water-fluoridation-history-other-fluoride-sources-neurocognitive-issues)

- **Gum disease impairs fertility in both men and women.** In women: periodontitis is linked to subfertility, preterm birth, and preeclampsia — Fusobacterium nucleatum has been detected in placentas of preterm births. In men: endothelial dysfunction from gum disease impairs erectile function via the nitric oxide pathway; systemic inflammation also reduces sperm quality. [02:04:52](transcript.md#020452-oral-health--fertility)

- **Hormonal changes profoundly affect women's oral health at every life stage.** Puberty and pregnancy increase gum susceptibility to gingivitis. Menopause-related estrogen decline reduces jaw bone density, alters saliva, and can cause burning mouth syndrome — a chronic burning on the tongue and lips often underdiagnosed in perimenopausal women. [02:19:10](transcript.md#021910-oral-health-women-pregnancy-menopause--burning-mouth)

- **Brush at night — that is the most critical brushing session.** Saliva flow drops during sleep, leaving the mouth more vulnerable to bacterial activity. Floss first, then brush. Wait 30–60 minutes after acidic foods before brushing; brush before breakfast in the morning to disrupt overnight biofilm before swallowing it. [02:07:03](transcript.md#020703-toothbrushing-flossing-waterpik-tool-toothbrushing--meals)

- **Tongue scraping is one of the most impactful and underused oral hygiene tools.** The overnight biofilm on the tongue is a major source of bad breath and bacterial load. Scrape from back to front with a stainless steel or copper U-shaped scraper every morning before eating or drinking. [02:45:05](transcript.md#024505-recap-top-behavioral-tools-for-oral-health-tongue-scraping)

## Tools & Protocols

### Hydroxyapatite Toothpaste
- **What:** Remineralizes enamel using the tooth's native mineral; whitens by optical effect (fills micro-scratches); no systemic fluoride risk; as effective or superior to fluoride for cavity prevention
- **How:** Brands Risewell or Boka (nano-hydroxyapatite, no SLS); brush in and do not rinse after to maximize contact time
- **When:** Morning and night
- **Ref:** [01:34:16](transcript.md#013416-whole-foods-hydroxyapatite-toothpaste-tool-testing-oral-microbiome)

### Tongue Scraping
- **What:** Removes overnight tongue biofilm; reduces bad breath and bacterial load; copper scrapers add natural antimicrobial effect
- **How:** U-shaped stainless steel or copper scraper (not a toothbrush — that redistributes bacteria); place as far back as comfortable, draw forward with gentle pressure; 5–10 passes; rinse between passes
- **When:** Every morning before eating or drinking anything
- **Ref:** [02:45:05](transcript.md#024505-recap-top-behavioral-tools-for-oral-health-tongue-scraping)

### Nasal Breathing (24/7)
- **What:** Protects oral microbiome, maintains saliva, generates nasal nitric oxide, prevents adenoid face in children, reduces sleep-disordered breathing
- **How:** Commit to nose breathing at rest and during sleep; use Somnifix mouth tape at night only if confirmed mouth breather with no unaddressed airway obstruction; use myofunctional therapy for retraining
- **When:** Always; taping optional at night
- **Ref:** [01:01:50](transcript.md#010150-shifting-to-nasal-breathing-mouth-taping-tools-3-minute-test-kiss-the-sky)

### Meal Frequency Reduction
- **What:** Reduces cumulative acid exposure time; allows saliva to remineralize teeth between meals per the Stephan curve
- **How:** 2–3 structured meals per day; no snacking between meals; six-small-meals-a-day patterns are particularly harmful for oral health
- **When:** Daily dietary pattern
- **Ref:** [00:19:14](transcript.md#001914-cavities-tool-meal-frequency-fasting)

### Xylitol Gum or Mints
- **What:** Starves Streptococcus mutans (cannot metabolize xylitol); disrupts biofilm formation; stimulates saliva production
- **How:** 4–5 g xylitol/day via gum or mints; dissolve slowly in mouth; use between meals as snack replacement
- **When:** Between meals
- **Ref:** [01:05:53](transcript.md#010553-chewing-gum-mastic-gum-tool-xylitol-chewing-food-breastfeeding)

### Oil Pulling
- **What:** Reduces pathogenic bacteria via saponification/emulsification; mild whitening; coconut oil's lauric acid is selectively antimicrobial; add-on, not replacement for brushing and flossing
- **How:** Swish 1 tbsp coconut oil for 10–15 min; spit into trash (not drain, will clog); do not swallow
- **When:** 3–5 times per week before brushing
- **Ref:** [01:20:05](transcript.md#012005-antibiotics-ozone-therapy-oil-pulling-mold-tool-coconut-oil-facial-trauma)

### Floss Before Brushing (Nightly)
- **What:** Breaks up interdental biofilm before brushing removes it; brushing alone cannot clean between teeth
- **How:** C-shaped technique around each tooth; slide under gumline; string floss is optimal; floss picks acceptable; Waterpik excellent for braces, bridges, implants, or difficulty with string floss; sequence: floss → brush → Waterpik (optional)
- **When:** Nightly
- **Ref:** [02:07:03](transcript.md#020703-toothbrushing-flossing-waterpik-tool-toothbrushing--meals)

### Delay Brushing After Acidic Foods
- **What:** Avoids abrading acid-softened enamel; allows saliva to re-harden enamel first
- **How:** Wait 30–60 minutes after coffee, citrus, sparkling water, or any acidic food/drink before brushing; alternatively brush before breakfast
- **When:** After any acidic exposure
- **Ref:** [02:07:03](transcript.md#020703-toothbrushing-flossing-waterpik-tool-toothbrushing--meals)

### Oral Microbiome Testing
- **What:** Identifies pathogenic bacteria (e.g., P. gingivalis, Fusobacterium nucleatum) for targeted intervention; personalizes oral care away from one-size-fits-all
- **How:** Bristle Health saliva test; mail sample; receive species-level results with personalized recommendations; track changes over time
- **When:** Initial baseline; repeat every 3–6 months, or for persistent bad breath, recurrent cavities, or gum disease
- **Ref:** [01:34:16](transcript.md#013416-whole-foods-hydroxyapatite-toothpaste-tool-testing-oral-microbiome)

### Reverse Osmosis Water Filter
- **What:** Removes 90%+ of fluoride from drinking water; standard carbon filters (Brita, PUR) do not remove fluoride; especially important for infant formula preparation
- **How:** Install RO system under sink; activated alumina filters also work
- **When:** Daily drinking and cooking water
- **Ref:** [01:54:57](transcript.md#015457-drinking-water--fluoride-toothpaste-cavities)

### Oral Probiotics
- **What:** Repopulates beneficial oral bacteria; BLIS K12 reduces bad breath; BLIS M18 supports overall oral microbiome; especially critical after antibiotics which can take months to recover from
- **How:** Dissolve lozenge slowly in the mouth (do not swallow) to colonize oral tissues
- **When:** After any antibiotic course; ongoing for microbiome support
- **Ref:** [00:34:34](transcript.md#003434-antibiotics-gut--oral-microbiome-tool-probiotics)

### Nitrate-Rich Vegetables
- **What:** Feeds nitrate-reducing oral bacteria that generate nitric oxide — critical for cardiovascular health and blood pressure regulation; undermined if combined with alcohol mouthwash
- **How:** Arugula, spinach, beets, celery — daily dietary staple
- **When:** Daily
- **Ref:** [00:36:20](transcript.md#003620-mouthwash-alcohol-astringents-cardiovascular-risk-bad-breath)

### Myofunctional Therapy
- **What:** Physical therapy for the tongue and orofacial muscles; retrains tongue posture to palate, lip seal, and swallowing patterns; effective for mouth breathing, sleep apnea, TMJ, snoring, and post tongue-tie release
- **How:** Find certified orofacial myofunctional therapist; daily exercises for several months; pair with frenectomy when releasing tongue tie
- **When:** Mouth breathers, children with narrow palates or crowded teeth, adults with sleep-disordered breathing or TMJ
- **Ref:** [00:54:19](transcript.md#005419-deviated-septum-therapies-kids--adults-mouth-breathing--sleep-disorders)

### 3-Minute Nasal Breathing Test
- **What:** Quick baseline assessment of nasal breathing capacity; dry/cracked lips in the morning also indicate nighttime mouth breathing
- **How:** Sit quietly; breathe only through nose; healthy nasal breathers can do this indefinitely; if air-hungry within minutes, nasal retraining is warranted
- **When:** One-time baseline; repeat after training
- **Ref:** [01:01:50](transcript.md#010150-shifting-to-nasal-breathing-mouth-taping-tools-3-minute-test-kiss-the-sky)
