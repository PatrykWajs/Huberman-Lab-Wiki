# Episode 28: Maximizing Productivity, Physical & Mental Health with Daily Tools

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman presents a comprehensive "office hours" episode organizing 17 science-backed protocols across a full 24-hour day — from waking to sleep. Each protocol explains the underlying neuroscience and physiology, then translates it into a concrete daily behavior tied to a specific time in the circadian cycle. The episode covers morning light viewing, forward ambulation for amygdala suppression, caffeine timing relative to adenosine clearance, intermittent fasting and its neurochemical rationale, 90-minute ultradian deep-work blocks, a 3:2 exercise ratio for brain and body health, food composition timed to desired cognitive state, non-sleep deep rest (NSDR) and hypnosis for neuroplasticity, afternoon light viewing to buffer against evening light disruption, sleep environment optimization, targeted supplements for sleep onset, strategies for middle-of-the-night waking, and weekend circadian maintenance. Huberman also covers sex hormone optimization through behavioral levers — cold exposure, resistance training, adequate sleep, and dietary cholesterol — and discusses specific supplements including alpha-GPC, omega-3 EPA, magnesium threonate/bisglycinate, apigenin, and theanine. The episode serves as a practical master reference integrating science from the prior 27 episodes into a single daily framework.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Track your wake time to anchor your temperature minimum** — Your body temperature minimum occurs ~2 hours before your typical wake time and is the master reference point for timing light, exercise, and deep-work blocks. [00:08:50](transcript.md#000850-protocol-1-record-your-daily-waking-time--temperature-minimum)

2. **Get into forward ambulation within 30–60 minutes of waking** — Optic flow from self-generated forward movement suppresses amygdala firing via brainstem pathways, producing a calm-yet-alert state. At least half a dozen peer-reviewed papers support this effect, including work from Botond Roska's lab (*Neuron*) and a lateral eye-movement study in *Journal of Neuroscience*. [00:12:07](transcript.md#001207-protocol-2-self-generate-forward-motion-outdoors)

3. **View natural light for 10–30 minutes every morning** — Morning sunlight activates melanopsin retinal ganglion cells, triggering a healthy cortisol pulse, setting the SCN circadian clock, and cascading into melatonin onset ~16 hours later. Even on overcast days, outdoor photon levels far exceed indoor bulbs. Never use sunglasses for this practice. [00:17:00](transcript.md#001700-protocol-3-view-natural-light-for-10-30min-every-morning)

4. **Do not wear blue blockers during the day** — Blue light is precisely the wavelength that stimulates the circadian-setting melanopsin cells. Blocking it during daylight is counterproductive; reserve dim-lighting or blue blockers for late evening only. Huberman's colleague Samer Hattar (NIMH) concurs. [00:22:43](transcript.md#002243-what-to-do-if-you-cant-view-the-sun-blue-light)

5. **Hydrate with electrolytes immediately upon waking** — Drink 16–32 oz of water with ~½ teaspoon sea salt before caffeine or food. Neurons require sodium, magnesium, and potassium for electrical signaling; overnight dehydration is the norm. [00:26:50](transcript.md#002650-protocol-4-hydrate-correctly)

6. **Delay caffeine 90–120 minutes after waking** — Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors. Taking it too early causes a rebound crash when it clears and accumulated adenosine floods back in. Waiting allows the natural morning adenosine clearance and cortisol rise to establish alertness first, producing a stable energy arc through the day. [00:28:00](transcript.md#002800-protocol-5-delay-caffeine-90-120m-after-waking)

7. **Fast (or fat-fast) until late morning or noon** — Fasting raises adrenaline (epinephrine), which at moderate levels sharpens focus and information encoding. Yerba mate and guayusa teas stimulate GLP-1, promoting fat oxidation during the fasted window. Reusing mate leaves enhances GLP-1 stimulation. What breaks a fast is highly individual and depends on insulin sensitivity and glycogen status. [00:30:48](transcript.md#003048-protocol-6-fast-or-fat-fast-until-noon)

8. **Do your hardest cognitive work 4–6 hours after your temperature minimum** — Set your screen at or above eye level (downward gaze reduces alertness via brainstem-eyelid coupling), use low-level white noise to engage dopamine and attention circuits, and work in 90-minute ultradian blocks. Expect the first 5–15 minutes of each block to be a ramp-up. Block the internet and silence the phone entirely during this window. [00:37:30](transcript.md#003730-protocol-7-optimize-deep-work-visual-elevation-ultradian-cycles-white-noise)

9. **Exercise ~60 minutes, 5 days per week, in a 3:2 ratio** — Alternate 3 days resistance and 2 days endurance (or vice versa) in 10–12 week blocks. Use an 80/20 intensity split: 80% below failure/burn threshold, 20% at or above. Resistance training releases osteocalcin from bone which supports neuron health; endurance work promotes BDNF, reduces inflammatory IL-6, and increases anti-inflammatory IL-10. Sessions longer than ~60 min raise cortisol in a detrimental way. [00:52:07](transcript.md#005207-protocol-8-optimal-exercise-32-ratio)

10. **Use alpha-GPC sparingly for cognitive or physical performance peaks** — 300 mg of alpha-GPC before a demanding workout or deep-work block increases acetylcholine, improving neuromuscular drive and focus. Use intermittently rather than daily due to potential TMAO elevation with chronic use. Human effect data available at examine.com. [01:03:54](transcript.md#010354-tools-for-training--mental-focus-fasting-salt-stimulants-alpha-gpc)

11. **Match food composition to desired cognitive state** — Protein and healthy fats (tyrosine → dopamine/norepinephrine) support daytime alertness; starchy complex carbohydrates in the evening raise serotonin and melatonin. Large meal volumes impair cognition regardless of food type. Supplement at least 1,000 mg EPA daily for mood; eat Brazil nuts for selenium to support thyroid function. Take a brief 5–30 minute walk after the mid-day meal to accelerate metabolism and nutrient utilization. [01:10:00](transcript.md#011000-protocol-9-eat-for-brain-function--mood)

12. **Optimize sex hormones with behavioral levers before supplements** — Cold exposure, adequate sleep, resistance training, healthy body fat levels, and sufficient dietary cholesterol (sex steroids are synthesized from cholesterol) all move testosterone and estrogen without prescriptions. Optional: tongkat ali 400 mg/day (may increase free testosterone by reducing SHBG binding); Fadogia agrestis (may raise luteinizing hormone). Both have limited human peer-reviewed data. [01:17:39](transcript.md#011739-protocol-10-get-your-testosterone--estrogen-in-an-ideal-range)

13. **Do NSDR daily — hypnosis preferred over naps or yoga nidra** — A 10–15 minute Reveri hypnosis session uniquely co-activates executive function networks, the default mode network, and the insula (per Jiang et al., *Cerebral Cortex*) — accelerating plasticity, restoring striatal dopamine, and enabling a focused fog-free re-entry into the afternoon. Yoga nidra and meditation are valid alternatives. Do NSDR after the post-lunch walk; hydrate immediately after. [01:24:00](transcript.md#012400-protocol-11-reset-the-mind--body-enhance-neuroplasticity-revericom)

14. **View late afternoon/evening sunlight to buffer against night-light disruption** — Getting 10–30 minutes of outdoor light as the sun descends (~4–5 PM) lowers retinal sensitivity so that artificial light later in the evening causes less melatonin suppression. Data from David Berson's lab (Brown) and Samer Hattar's lab (NIMH) show 10 PM–4 AM bright light disrupts dopamine, learning, immunity, and mood in long-lasting ways. [01:33:29](transcript.md#013329-protocol-13-view-late-afternoonevening-light-to-support-sleep--dopamine)

15. **Eat starchy complex carbohydrates at dinner to support serotonin and sleep** — Complex carbohydrates shift the tryptophan-to-LNAA ratio in blood, increasing tryptophan transport into the brain → serotonin → melatonin. Do not supplement directly with serotonin, 5-HTP, or tryptophan — they fragment sleep architecture. Avoid refined sugars (activate nodose ganglia-vagus-dopamine loop that drives craving). [01:39:00](transcript.md#013900-protocol-14-eat-dinner-that-promotes-serotonin-calm-sleep)

16. **Optimize sleep with temperature, darkness, and a targeted supplement stack** — Keep the room cool and dark; a hot bath or sauna 1–2 hours before bed paradoxically accelerates sleep onset by pulling core heat to the periphery via AVA portals in palms, face, and feet. Sleep supplement stack (30–60 min before bed): magnesium threonate or bisglycinate 300–400 mg + apigenin 50 mg + theanine 100–200 mg. Avoid high-dose melatonin (suppresses sex steroids, doesn't sustain sleep), alcohol, and chronic cannabis. [01:44:27](transcript.md#014427-protocol-15-optimize-falling--staying-asleep-tools--supplements-that-work)

17. **If you wake in the middle of the night, use NSDR not willpower** — Pattern 1 (wake at 2–3 AM, can't return to sleep): melatonin pulse started too early — fix by going to bed later or using brief bright light at 7–8 PM to delay melatonin onset. Pattern 2 (anxiety-driven waking): use long exhale breathing, yoga nidra, or Reveri sleep hypnosis. Avoid bright light. [01:55:00](transcript.md#015500-protocol-16-preventing-middle-of-the-night-waking)

18. **Maintain wake time on weekends; limit drift to ≤1 hour** — Sleeping in more than one hour shifts the circadian clock (social jet lag). After a bad night, still get morning light and movement, wake at regular time, and use an afternoon NSDR session to recover. After a late night, do not go to bed very early the following night. [01:59:10](transcript.md#015910-protocol-17-weekends-recovering-from-a-poor-nights-sleep)

## Tools & Protocols

### Record Daily Wake Time & Temperature Minimum

- **What:** Write down exact wake time each morning. Temperature minimum = wake time minus 2 hours. It is a time in the 24-hour cycle, not an actual temperature value.
- **How:** Keep pen and notepad at the bedside. Average over several days for accuracy. Use as the scheduling anchor for all other protocols.
- **When:** Every morning; reference point for the entire day.
- **Ref:** [00:08:50](transcript.md#000850-protocol-1-record-your-daily-waking-time--temperature-minimum)

---

### Self-Generated Forward Motion (Morning Walk)

- **What:** Walk, run, or cycle outdoors within 30–60 minutes of waking to generate optic flow and suppress amygdala activation.
- **How:** Any forward locomotion works — even a slow shuffle. Aim for 10–30 minutes outdoors. For low/no-vision individuals, auditory flow from forward movement produces equivalent effects. Combine with morning light viewing.
- **When:** First thing in the morning.
- **Ref:** [00:12:07](transcript.md#001207-protocol-2-self-generate-forward-motion-outdoors)

---

### Morning Natural Light Viewing

- **What:** Get 10–30 minutes of natural outdoor light reaching the eyes each morning, no sunglasses.
- **How:** Go outside — even on overcast days (stay longer: 20–30 min vs. ~60 seconds on a bright clear day). Do not look directly at the sun. Light through glass windows is insufficient. Combine with the morning walk.
- **When:** Within the first 30–60 minutes of waking.
- **Ref:** [00:17:00](transcript.md#001700-protocol-3-view-natural-light-for-10-30min-every-morning)

---

### Indoor Light Alternative

- **What:** Use a 930-lux or 10,000-lux LightPad on the desk during the morning work window when outdoor light is truly unavailable.
- **How:** Position the lamp in front of or above eye level. Standard drawing light pads are cheaper than dedicated dawn simulators and equally effective. Do NOT wear blue blockers during daytime — they block the wavelength that sets circadian rhythms.
- **When:** Same morning window as outdoor light viewing.
- **Ref:** [00:22:43](transcript.md#002243-what-to-do-if-you-cant-view-the-sun-blue-light)

---

### Morning Hydration with Electrolytes

- **What:** Drink 16–32 oz of water with ~½ teaspoon sea salt (or an electrolyte packet like LMNT) immediately upon waking.
- **How:** Before any food or caffeine. Optionally add a 99 mg potassium tablet. Electrolytes support neuronal electrical activity and quell early-morning hunger signals.
- **When:** First thing upon waking, before exercise or caffeine.
- **Ref:** [00:26:50](transcript.md#002650-protocol-4-hydrate-correctly)

---

### Delay Caffeine 90–120 Minutes After Waking

- **What:** Do not consume caffeine for at least 90–120 minutes after waking.
- **How:** Complete morning light, movement, and hydration first. Preferred caffeine sources: yerba mate, guayusa tea, or coffee. Avoid energy drinks.
- **When:** 90–120 minutes post-wake.
- **Ref:** [00:28:00](transcript.md#002800-protocol-5-delay-caffeine-90-120m-after-waking)

---

### Intermittent Fast (or Fat-Fast) Until Late Morning/Noon

- **What:** Delay the first meal until ~11 AM–noon to maintain adrenaline-driven alertness and focus.
- **How:** Drink water, electrolytes, yerba mate, or guayusa during the fasted window. Small amounts of fat (almond butter, cream) may not break the fast for many people — depends on insulin sensitivity and recent eating history. Athletic greens (green powder) was tolerated by Huberman personally within the fast.
- **When:** From waking until late morning or noon.
- **Ref:** [00:30:48](transcript.md#003048-protocol-6-fast-or-fat-fast-until-noon)

---

### Yerba Mate / Guayusa for GLP-1 and Fat Oxidation

- **What:** Yerba mate and guayusa stimulate GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide 1), promoting fat oxidation and appetite suppression during the fasted window.
- **How:** Brew with a metal strainer; pour hot water over leaves. Reuse leaves for the same day or next day (GLP-1 stimulation may be enhanced with subsequent pour-overs). Both contain caffeine — count toward total daily caffeine load.
- **When:** Fasted morning window, as the primary caffeine source.
- **Ref:** [00:34:30](transcript.md#003430-fat-loss--glucagon-like-peptide-1-glp1-yerba-mate-guayusa-tea)

---

### Deep Work: Ultradian Cycles, Visual Elevation, White Noise

- **What:** Structure focused cognitive work in 90-minute ultradian blocks with screen at or above eye level and low-level white noise playing in the background.
- **How:**
  - Position screen at or slightly above eye level — downward gaze reduces alertness via brainstem-eyelid coupling; locus coeruleus and reticular activating system activity increase with upright posture.
  - Use low-volume white noise (not usually headphones) to engage dopamine circuits and attention-related brain areas (two peer-reviewed papers cited in the transcript).
  - Block all internet and social media (e.g., Freedom app); turn phone completely off.
  - Accept the first 5–15 minutes of each block as normal ramp-up time; do not quit.
  - The bladder-alertness connection (direct neural link from bladder to brainstem) can also be used as a natural alertness enhancer — avoid leaving the desk for the bathroom unless necessary.
- **When:** Start 4–6 hours after temperature minimum (on the steepest slope of the body temperature rise); typically 9–11 AM for someone who wakes at 6:30–7 AM.
- **Ref:** [00:37:30](transcript.md#003730-protocol-7-optimize-deep-work-visual-elevation-ultradian-cycles-white-noise)

---

### Optimal Exercise: 3:2 Resistance/Cardio Ratio

- **What:** Train 5 days per week (~60 min per session ± 15 min), alternating resistance and endurance in a 3:2 ratio that flips every 10–12 weeks.
- **How:**
  - Resistance days: compound movements; 80% of work below failure, 20% to failure.
  - Endurance days: 80% below lactate-burn threshold (Zone 2 conversational pace), 20% at or above the burn (lactate threshold) to stimulate glia-derived brain-protective compounds and fuel lactate metabolism in the brain.
  - Keep sessions ≤60 min to avoid cortisol overshoot.
  - Train after the morning deep-work block.
  - Train fasted where possible (per Satchin Panda's work on cellular health benefits of fasted exercise).
  - Take 2 full rest days per week.
- **When:** After the morning focus block; late morning to early afternoon.
- **Ref:** [00:52:07](transcript.md#005207-protocol-8-optimal-exercise-32-ratio)

---

### Alpha-GPC (Optional — Performance Supplement)

- **What:** Alpha-GPC increases acetylcholine, improving neuromuscular drive and cognitive focus. 300 mg has been shown in studies to improve physical and cognitive performance.
- **How:** Take 300 mg, 30–60 minutes before a demanding workout or deep-work block. Use intermittently rather than daily due to possible TMAO elevation with chronic daily use. Research at examine.com → Human Effect Matrix → alpha-GPC.
- **When:** Pre-workout or pre-deep-work on demanding days only.
- **Ref:** [01:03:54](transcript.md#010354-tools-for-training--mental-focus-fasting-salt-stimulants-alpha-gpc)

---

### Eating for Brain Function and Mood

- **What:** Time food composition to match the desired cognitive or emotional state at each part of the day.
- **How:**
  - **Daytime/alertness:** Protein and healthy fats (meat, fish, eggs, nuts — provide tyrosine → dopamine/norepinephrine). Keep carbohydrates low or zero if focus is required. About 25% of people carry genes allowing high carbohydrate intake without cognitive dulling — most do not.
  - **Evening/sleep:** Starchy complex carbohydrates (rice, pasta, oats, root vegetables) shift tryptophan-to-LNAA ratio, raising serotonin and melatonin. Avoid refined sugars (activate nodose ganglia → vagus → dopamine craving loop).
  - **Volume:** Eating to ~80% full; large volumes divert blood to gut and impair cognition regardless of food type.
  - **Omega-3:** At least 1,000 mg EPA/day (fish oil or algae-based) — equivalent to antidepressants in 12+ studies; increases dopamine and related neuromodulators.
  - **Selenium:** A few Brazil nuts daily to support thyroid hormone, which governs both peripheral and brain metabolism.
  - **Post-lunch walk:** 5–30 minutes after eating accelerates metabolism, nutrient utilization, and provides additional daytime light exposure.
- **When:** First meal at 11 AM–noon; evening meal emphasizing carbohydrates 2–4 hours before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:10:00](transcript.md#011000-protocol-9-eat-for-brain-function--mood)

---

### Testosterone and Estrogen Optimization (Behavioral)

- **What:** Support sex hormone levels through behavioral and dietary levers before considering supplements or prescriptions.
- **How:**
  - Eat sufficient dietary cholesterol (butter, eggs) — testosterone and estrogen are synthesized from cholesterol.
  - Cold exposure (cold shower or plunge) — raises luteinizing hormone and testosterone.
  - Adequate sleep — the majority of daily testosterone release occurs during sleep.
  - Resistance training — well-documented upregulation of testosterone and growth hormone.
  - Maintain healthy body fat — excess adipose aromatizes testosterone to estrogen.
  - Minimize BPA (endocrine disruptor in plastics).
  - Optional: tongkat ali 400 mg/day (may increase free testosterone by reducing SHBG binding); Fadogia agrestis (may raise luteinizing hormone, thereby raising testosterone and estrogen). Limited peer-reviewed human data for both; always check with a physician.
- **When:** Ongoing lifestyle integration; morning sunlight, fasting, and exercise all contribute.
- **Ref:** [01:17:39](transcript.md#011739-protocol-10-get-your-testosterone--estrogen-in-an-ideal-range)

---

### Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR) — Hypnosis Preferred

- **What:** A 10–15 minute mid-day rest practice using clinical hypnosis, yoga nidra, or meditation to restore cognitive capacity and enhance neuroplasticity.
- **How:**
  - **Hypnosis (preferred):** Use the Reveri app (reveri.com) — free, science-backed, 10–15 min sessions. Jiang et al. (*Cerebral Cortex*) shows hypnosis uniquely co-activates executive function networks, default mode network, and insula simultaneously. Enables focused re-entry into the afternoon without grogginess.
  - **Yoga nidra:** Lie down and follow guided long-exhale body-scan scripts (free on YouTube or iRest). No movement required.
  - **Meditation:** Any focused-attention or open-monitoring practice.
  - Hydrate immediately after the NSDR session.
- **When:** After lunch and the post-lunch walk, ideally before 3 PM. Also use during nocturnal waking.
- **Ref:** [01:24:00](transcript.md#012400-protocol-11-reset-the-mind--body-enhance-neuroplasticity-revericom)

---

### Afternoon Hydration and Nap Rules

- **What:** Re-hydrate after NSDR; if napping, keep naps short and well-timed.
- **How:**
  - Drink water with electrolytes after training and after NSDR.
  - Naps: 20–90 minutes maximum. 20-minute naps are generally safe. 90-minute naps complete one ultradian cycle. Avoid if napping disrupts your nighttime sleep — individual variation is large.
  - Naps must end at least 6–7 hours before intended sleep time.
- **When:** Early to mid-afternoon.
- **Ref:** [01:31:15](transcript.md#013115-protocol-12-hydrate-correctly-nap-rules)

---

### Late Afternoon / Evening Light Viewing

- **What:** Get 10–30 minutes of natural outdoor light exposure as the sun descends (~4–5 PM), no sunglasses.
- **How:** Go outside around 4–5 PM (varies by season and latitude). You do not need to see the sunset directly — ambient outdoor light during this window lowers retinal sensitivity, buffering against melatonin suppression from artificial light later in the evening. Do not wear blue blockers at this time — that would increase rather than decrease retinal sensitivity to later light.
- **When:** 4–6 PM, late afternoon; the "Netflix inoculation."
- **Ref:** [01:33:29](transcript.md#013329-protocol-13-view-late-afternoonevening-light-to-support-sleep--dopamine)

---

### Evening Meal for Serotonin and Sleep

- **What:** Eat a dinner emphasizing complex starchy carbohydrates and moderate protein.
- **How:** Rice, pasta, oats, potatoes, root vegetables, and bread shift the tryptophan-to-LNAA ratio in blood, increasing tryptophan transport into the brain → serotonin → melatonin. Do not supplement with serotonin, 5-HTP, or tryptophan directly — these fragment sleep architecture and cause waking at 2–4 AM for many people. Avoid very large meal volumes immediately before sleep.
- **When:** 2–4 hours before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:39:00](transcript.md#013900-protocol-14-eat-dinner-that-promotes-serotonin-calm-sleep)

---

### Sleep Optimization — Temperature, Darkness, Supplements

- **What:** Use behavioral, environmental, and supplemental tools to optimize sleep onset and maintenance.
- **How:**
  - **Temperature:** Keep sleep room cool (~65–68°F / 18–20°C). Hot bath, shower, or sauna 1–2 hours before bed accelerates sleep onset by driving heat to the periphery via AVA (arteriovenous anastomosis) portals in the palms, upper face, and feet — then core temperature drops and sleep onset is faster.
  - **Darkness:** Make the bedroom as dark as possible. Eliminate bright overhead lights 2 hours before sleep.
  - **Supplements (30–60 min before bed):**
    - Magnesium threonate or magnesium bisglycinate: 300–400 mg elemental. Cross the blood-brain barrier, promote GABA, reduce rumination and executive-function chatter. Also have possible neuroprotective effects.
    - Apigenin: 50 mg (chamomile-derived). Binds GABA receptors, reduces anxiety, quiets the forebrain.
    - Theanine: 100–200 mg. Increases GABA and activates chloride channels to lower neural activity. Use 100 mg if prone to very vivid dreams.
    - The three together act synergistically.
  - **Avoid:** High-dose melatonin (3–10 mg — suppresses sex steroids, doesn't sustain sleep; low doses of 0.5–1 mg are less problematic but still caution warranted). Alcohol (disrupts REM). Chronic cannabis (similar to alcohol for sleep architecture).
- **When:** Temperature protocols 1–2 hours pre-sleep; supplements 30–60 min pre-sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:44:27](transcript.md#014427-protocol-15-optimize-falling--staying-asleep-tools--supplements-that-work)

---

### Preventing Middle-of-the-Night Waking

- **What:** Address the two most common patterns of nocturnal awakening with targeted fixes.
- **How:**
  - **Pattern 1 — Wake at 2–3 AM unable to return to sleep:** Melatonin pulse likely initiated too early (body was tired at 8:30–9 PM but stayed up). Fix: go to bed earlier and align sleep time with natural melatonin onset, OR use brief bright light at 7–8 PM to delay melatonin onset if staying up later is necessary.
  - **Pattern 2 — Anxiety-driven waking:** Use long exhale breathing, yoga nidra, or Reveri sleep hypnosis. Do not try to fight the mind with the mind. NSDR puts the brain into a state resembling sleep even if full sleep doesn't return.
  - Keep lights dim; use dim red light if you must move around; flip them off as soon as possible.
- **When:** Middle of the night as needed.
- **Ref:** [01:55:00](transcript.md#015500-protocol-16-preventing-middle-of-the-night-waking)

---

### Weekend Circadian Maintenance and Poor-Sleep Recovery

- **What:** Maintain circadian anchor points on weekends; use NSDR to recover from bad nights without further disrupting rhythm.
- **How:**
  - Limit sleeping in on weekends to no more than 1 hour past your usual wake time.
  - After any late night, wake up at the regular time rather than sleeping in excessively.
  - After a bad night, still do morning light and forward ambulation — these matter most when sleep was poor.
  - Do not go to bed extremely early the night after a bad night (e.g., 6 PM); aim for close to normal bedtime.
  - Use a 20-minute NSDR/yoga nidra session in the early afternoon to recover.
  - Huberman personally takes one full unstructured day per week to decompress.
- **When:** Weekends and after poor-sleep nights.
- **Ref:** [01:59:10](transcript.md#015910-protocol-17-weekends-recovering-from-a-poor-nights-sleep)
