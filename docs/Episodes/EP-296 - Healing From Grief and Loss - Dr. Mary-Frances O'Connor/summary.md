# Episode 296: Healing From Grief & Loss | Dr. Mary-Frances O'Connor

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Mary-Frances O'Connor, Professor of Clinical Psychology and Psychiatry at the University of Arizona and director of the Grief, Loss, and Social Stress (GLASS) Laboratory, joins Andrew Huberman to discuss the neuroscience and psychology of grief. The conversation covers what happens in the brain and body during bereavement, why grief feels physically painful, the serious health risks associated with bereavement, and science-based tools for moving through loss while honoring what was lost. Dr. O'Connor is the author of "The Grieving Brain" and "The Grieving Body," and is a world expert in the science of grief and loss, including research on attachment, dopamine, and the dual-process model of bereavement.

## Key Takeaways

- **Grief vs. Grieving:** Grief is the immediate emotional state in a given moment; grieving is the process of how that state changes over time. Like a stock market chart, grief fluctuates daily but can trend toward adaptation over months and years. [00:02:22](transcript.md#grief-vs-grieving-love--bonding-gone-yet-everlasting)

- **Gone Yet Everlasting:** The brain holds two simultaneous and conflicting streams about a deceased loved one — the explicit knowledge that they are gone, and the implicit attachment belief that they still exist somewhere. When these two streams collide in awareness, a wave of grief results. This is neurobiologically normal. [00:02:22](transcript.md#grief-vs-grieving-love--bonding-gone-yet-everlasting)

- **Attachment Biology Drives Grief:** Grief cannot be understood separately from love and bonding. The attachment system creates an implicit belief that "you will always be there for me." Death violates this belief at the neurobiological level, not merely the cognitive level — which is why even people who anticipated a death can still experience it as "sudden." [00:10:29](transcript.md#sudden-vs-slow-death-attachment-reframing-relationship)

- **Integration, Not Letting Go:** The goal is not to "let go" of the deceased but to integrate the relationship in a new form. Ongoing internal bonds — telling your loved one about your day, noticing what they would have loved, finding them in your own face in the mirror — are neurobiologically real and adaptive. Throughout history, religious and cultural systems (Día de los Muertos, intercessory prayer, ancestor veneration) existed precisely to scaffold this continuing relationship. [00:14:52](transcript.md#religion-integrating-the-new-relationship)

- **Yearning and Dopamine:** A core feature of grief is yearning — an intense longing for the deceased. Neuroimaging shows direct correlation between yearning intensity and activation in the nucleus accumbens (caudate/ventral striatum), the brain's reward-learning region. This is the same system that drives motivation and anticipation — but yearning for a loved one is better understood as homeostatic thirst: the brain signals "reach for this person" just as thirst signals "reach for water." [00:20:46](transcript.md#yearning-dopamine-brain-addiction)

- **Protest and Despair:** Grief involves cycling between two states: protest (amped-up, refusing to accept loss, searching behavior, driven by go-circuitry in the basal ganglia) and despair (low energy, withdrawal, driven by no-go circuitry). Despair is not failure — it is metabolically adaptive, stopping the energetically costly search behavior. Both are normal, neither is linear. [00:43:21](transcript.md#protest-despair--transmutation-changing-attachment-hierarchy)

- **Transmutation:** The adaptive move is to take the energy of protest and despair and transmute it — transforming blocked action into new, meaningful engagement. This requires prefrontal context-dependent reprocessing: "I can't hand this letter to them, but maybe I can do something else with it." Grief is learning, and transmutation is the learning that emerges. [00:43:21](transcript.md#protest-despair--transmutation-changing-attachment-hierarchy)

- **Serious Health Risks of Bereavement:** On the day a loved one dies, a person is 21 times more likely to have a heart attack than on any other day. In the first three months after a spouse's death, men are nearly twice as likely to have a fatal heart attack compared to age-matched married men (women: ~1.8x). Bereavement also disrupts immune function, sleep, and general physiological regulation because the deceased served as an "external pacemaker" for the surviving partner's cardiovascular system. [01:05:27](transcript.md#culture-alcohol--death-dying-of-a-broken-heart-medical-risk)

- **Complicated Grief (Prolonged Grief Disorder):** Approximately 1 in 10 bereaved people develop complicated grief, characterized by grief not improving over time. Risk factors include sudden or traumatic loss, prior attachment difficulties, lack of social support, and loss of a child. These individuals need targeted evidence-based psychotherapy (Complicated Grief Treatment, developed by Katherine Shear) rather than general support. [00:52:04](transcript.md#bereavement-support-medical-risk)

- **Grief Is Not Depression:** While grief and depression share features, grief is attachment-related, episodic, triggered by reminders, and centrally involves yearning. Depression is more pervasive and not specifically tied to reminders of loss. They can co-occur but require different interventions.

- **Grief Stages Model Is Outdated:** Kübler-Ross's five stages (published 1969, "On Death and Dying") were developed from interviews with people facing their own deaths, not for bereavement. Longitudinal studies show acceptance increases and yearning decreases over time, but non-linearly. Multiple states can occur simultaneously, in any order, and repeatedly. Using the model as a prescription can trap people ("I haven't felt anger yet so I'm not done grieving"). [01:23:57](transcript.md#grief-stages-permission--coping-judgment--guilt-lessons-from-grief)

- **Dual Process Model of Bereavement:** Developed by Stroebe and Schut, adaptive grieving oscillates between loss-oriented coping (processing the grief itself) and restoration-oriented coping (rebuilding life, taking on new roles). The capacity to oscillate between both is the sign of healthy grieving; being stuck in only one mode is the marker of complicated grief. [02:07:46](transcript.md#mental-oscillation-dual-model-of-bereavement)

- **Avoidance Prolongs Grief:** Some avoidance is normal in the moment. But sustained avoidance — refusing to drive by the hospital, avoiding grief-associated social situations — prevents the brain from learning the new reality. As Dr. O'Connor observed: "The only way to prolong the process is by trying to shorten it." [02:14:00](transcript.md#avoidance-remembering-a-loved-one-resilience-getting-worse--seeking-professional-help)

- **Grieving Suicide:** Loss by suicide carries unique features: elevated "would've, could've, should've" rumination, stigma, guilt, and self-blame. These ruminations are an infinite loop — the brain can generate endless counterfactuals. The path through is not answering them but recognizing them as thoughts and returning to present reality. A third of Dr. O'Connor's psychology of death and loss students (20-year-olds) now raise their hand when asked if they know someone who died by suicide. [01:35:44](transcript.md#grieving-suicide-rumination-tool-shifting-environment)

- **Religion, Belief Systems, and Grief:** The Changing Lives of Older Couples Study (Michigan, NIH-funded, 10-year prospective) found that having any framework for understanding life and death — religious, philosophical, or otherwise — predicted lower grief severity after spousal loss. Loss can also temporarily destabilize prior beliefs, adding a layer of existential stress. [01:47:24](transcript.md#belief-systems-religion--grief)

- **Motivation vs. Energy in Grief:** Grief suppresses both. Lack of motivation can often be overcome with behavioral activation; lack of energy requires rest. Dr. O'Connor draws on her experience with MS: knowing deprivation makes abundance feel larger, and fine-grained time perception (savoring small moments) is a cultivatable practice. [02:22:15](transcript.md#time-perception--end-of-life-motivation--energy)

## Tools & Protocols

### Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR)
- **What:** Systematically contract and relax muscle groups from head to toe while attending to the felt difference between tension and relaxation.
- **How:** Use freely available guided audio online. Brief contractions per muscle group, working head to toe. Can be done lying down, in bed, seated at work, or in a grocery store.
- **When:** During waves of grief; before sleep; whenever the body is in an amped-up protest state.
- **Why it works:** In Dr. O'Connor's clinical trial with widows and widowers (mindfulness vs. PMR vs. waitlist), PMR outperformed mindfulness training for reducing grief severity. Emotions are outputs — working with the body's physiological state changes emotional state more directly than top-down cognitive approaches.
- **Ref:** [01:15:28](transcript.md#navigating-grief-emotions--body-tool-progressive-muscle-relaxation)

### Shifting Environment to Break Rumination
- **What:** Physically move to a different space when caught in a rumination loop.
- **How:** Get up, walk outside, change rooms — any literal environmental shift.
- **When:** When noticing repetitive "if only / would've / could've" thoughts, especially common after suicide loss.
- **Why it works:** Context-dependent cognition means the same thought spiral is harder to sustain in a new physical context.
- **Ref:** [01:35:44](transcript.md#grieving-suicide-rumination-tool-shifting-environment)

### Contemplating Death (Memento Mori Practice)
- **What:** Deliberately engage with one's own mortality as a regular practice to reduce existential anxiety and clarify priorities.
- **How:** Journaling about mortality; reading Stoic texts or Ernest Becker's *The Denial of Death*; Huberman's practice of imagining his last exhale on the first long exhale of yoga nidra/NSDR; writing a personal death document ("If I die, here's what I want you to know") and updating it periodically.
- **When:** Any time; building this practice before loss occurs is especially protective — the Changing Lives of Older Couples Study shows prior engagement with mortality reduces grief severity.
- **Why it works:** Terror management theory shows avoidance of death awareness creates chronic anxiety. Deliberate engagement reduces that anxiety and increases appreciation of the present.
- **Ref:** [01:58:35](transcript.md#tools-contemplating-death-life-celebration-terror-management-empathy)

### Continuing Bonds Practice
- **What:** Maintain a conscious, evolving internal relationship with the deceased rather than attempting to "let go."
- **How:** Talk to them internally; notice what they would have loved; look in the mirror and address them; share memories with others who knew them; frame achievements as "this one's for you."
- **When:** Throughout the grieving process and indefinitely thereafter.
- **Why it works:** The internal representation of the deceased is neurobiologically real and permanent. Working with it — evolving it over time — is how adaptation actually occurs.
- **Ref:** [00:14:52](transcript.md#religion-integrating-the-new-relationship)

### Social Support Network Activation
- **What:** Identify specific people to contact during acute grief waves before the waves hit.
- **How:** Know your short list. Reach out when in the "puddle" — to be witnessed and to find your way back out. In-person presence (touch, eye contact, smell of another person) provides co-regulation that the deceased once provided.
- **When:** During acute waves; in the first days and weeks when cardiac risk is highest; whenever avoidance of social situations is occurring.
- **Ref:** [00:52:04](transcript.md#bereavement-support-medical-risk)

### Life Celebration and Grief Rituals
- **What:** Engage with culturally sanctioned rituals around death and grief; design your own where needed.
- **How:** Attend and participate in funerals, wakes, memorials, annual commemorations. Write a personal death document specifying how you want your death commemorated. Design rituals that fit your framework (religious, secular, or personal).
- **When:** Around the time of death; at recurring commemorations. Planning occurs before death.
- **Why it works:** Grief literacy is transmitted through ritual. Cultures with richer death rituals show better community processing of loss. Commemorations shift over time from memorials of death to celebrations of life.
- **Ref:** [00:27:58](transcript.md#culture--grief-literacy-protest-despair--hope-new-relationships)

### Exercise During Grief
- **What:** Physical movement to shift out of sustained protest or despair states.
- **How:** Match to current state — intense exercise (running) for protest/hyperactivation; calming movement (yoga, walking) for those needing to soothe. Both end with the body in a different physiological state.
- **When:** Daily if possible; particularly useful when stuck in one pole of the protest/despair oscillation.
- **Ref:** [01:15:28](transcript.md#navigating-grief-emotions--body-tool-progressive-muscle-relaxation)

### Seeking Professional Help — When and Why
- **What:** Evidence-based psychotherapy, specifically Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT) developed by Katherine Shear.
- **When:** Grief consistently getting worse rather than trending upward; not improving after 6–12 months; inability to function in daily life; sustained inability to accept the reality of loss; social isolation; suicidal ideation; memories growing more intense rather than less over time.
- **Why:** Prolonged grief disorder does not respond to time alone — it requires specific therapeutic intervention to get back on a typical grieving trajectory.
- **Ref:** [02:14:00](transcript.md#avoidance-remembering-a-loved-one-resilience-getting-worse--seeking-professional-help)
