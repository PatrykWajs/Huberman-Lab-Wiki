## [00:02:37] What is Stress?

Okay, so what is stress? We hear all the time that stress is bad; we hear people saying they're really stressed out. Stress at its core is a generalized system — it wasn't designed for tigers attacking us or any one thing. It's a system to mobilize other systems in the brain and body. That generalized nature gives it a certain advantage in taking over the state of our brain and body, but it also gives us an advantage in controlling it, because it's based on hardwired biological mechanisms — cells, chemicals, pathways, and tissues that exist in you right now and require no neuroplasticity. Those hardwired mechanisms allow you to put a brake on stress.

Let's be clear about what we already know: stressors can be psychological or physical. If I put you outside on a cold day without a jacket for a very long time, that is stressful. If I have you prepare for too many exams at once and you can't balance it with your sleep schedule and your other needs for comfort and well-being — food, rest, sleep, and social connection — that is also stressful. So let's talk about the stress response, because when you understand the mechanism you'll be in a far better position to incorporate these tools, teach them to others, and modify them as your life circumstances change.

## [00:04:23] Short-Term Stress Response

When something stresses us out — either in our mind or because something enters our environment — a collection of neurons that start at roughly your neck and run down to about your navel, called the sympathetic chain ganglia, becomes activated like a bunch of dominoes falling all at once. It's very fast. When those neurons are activated, acetylcholine is released, and then the postganglionic neurons respond to that acetylcholine and release epinephrine, which is equivalent to adrenaline. So we have this system where, very fast, whenever we're stressed, these neurons down the middle of the body release these chemicals.

Adrenaline is then released at particular organs and acts in particular ways. Things like the muscles of your legs, your heart, and other tissues that need to be active when you're stressed have beta receptors that respond to epinephrine — blood vessels dilate, blood rushes into the legs, heart rate speeds up. At the same time, epinephrine activates other receptors on tissues we don't need in the moment: those involved in digestion, reproduction, and things of that sort, which are luxuries for when things are going well. So the stress response is two-pronged: a yes for certain things and a no for others. Fundamentally, the stress response is just this generic signal that says "do something" — you're going to feel agitated because it was designed to move you. This is important: if you want to control stress, you need to learn how to work with that agitation.

## [00:06:49] Breathwork to Reduce Stress; Tool: Physiological Sigh

I'd like to give you a tool at this point. If you want to reduce the magnitude of the stress response, the best thing you can do is activate the other system in the body designed for calming and relaxation — the parasympathetic nervous system. The parasympathetic nervous system is really interesting because the cranial nerves — the ones up in the brain stem and neck area — have a direct line to various features of your face, in particular the eyes. They control eye movements, pupil dilation, the tongue, facial muscles, and so on. The tool that, to my knowledge, is the fastest and most thoroughly grounded in physiology and neuroscience for calming down in a self-directed way is what's called the physiological sigh.

The physiological sigh is based on a very real medical-school-textbook relationship between the brain, the body, and the breathing apparatus — meaning the diaphragm, lungs, and heart. Here's how it works: when you inhale, the diaphragm moves down, the lungs expand, and your heart actually gets a little bit bigger in that expanded space. Blood in there is now at a lower volume and moving a little bit more slowly. A small group of neurons called the sinoatrial node in the heart registers that blood is moving more slowly and sends a signal up to the brain — and the brain sends a signal back to speed the heart up. So if you want your heart to beat faster, inhale longer and more vigorously than your exhales. The opposite is equally true: when you exhale, the diaphragm moves up, the heart gets a little more compact, blood flows more quickly through that compact space, the sinoatrial node registers this, sends a signal up to the brain, and parasympathetic neurons in the brain stem send a signal back to slow the heart down. So if you want to calm down quickly, you need to make your exhales longer and/or more vigorous than your inhales.

## [00:11:52] Physiologic Sigh, Carbon Dioxide & Rapid Stress Reduction

The reason this tool is so attractive for controlling stress is that it works in real time — it doesn't involve a practice you have to sit down and do separately from life. The physiological sigh is something that people naturally start doing when they've been crying and trying to recover, or when they're in claustrophobic environments. The diaphragm is an internal organ you can control voluntarily through the phrenic nerve (p-h-r-e-n-i-c), which innervates the diaphragm. You can anytime you want double or triple up your inhales or exhale more than your inhales.

The physiological sigh is a double inhale followed by a long exhale. I mentioned a minute ago that if you inhale more than you exhale, you speed the heart rate up — which would promote more stress. But the double inhale before the exhale takes advantage of the fact that when you double inhale, even if the second inhale is sneaking in just a tiny bit more air, the little sacks in your lungs — the alveoli — get reinflated. When stressed, those alveoli tend to collapse and carbon dioxide builds up in the bloodstream, which is one reason we feel agitated. When you do the double inhale and then the long exhale, that long exhale is now much more effective at ridding your body and bloodstream of carbon dioxide, which relaxes you very quickly. The physiological sigh done just one to three times — double inhale, exhale; double inhale, exhale — will bring down your level of stress very fast. Be aware that your heart rate will take about 20 to 30 seconds to come down to baseline, and you may need to repeat the physiological sigh a few times.

## [00:13:30] Short-Term Stress, Positive Benefits, Immune System

Let's think about stress now not in terms of acute versus chronic, but on three different time scales — short-term, medium-term, and long-term — because that framework is what allows us to arrive at what this is all about as it relates to emotions. We've all heard so many times that stress is bad. We've seen these pictures showing the healthy plump brain on the left versus the withered stressed brain on the right, and the smaller hippocampus in people who are chronically stressed. But in that conversation, something has unfortunately been eclipsed: the really positive things that stress does for us in the short term.

When the stress response hits, it is good for your immune system — and I know that might be a tough pill to swallow, but it's absolutely true. Stress often comes in the form of bacterial or viral infection, and the stress response is in part organized to combat bacterial and viral infection. Short-term stress and the release of adrenaline (epinephrine) in particular is good for combating infection. This is just not discussed enough. Short-term stress also brings certain elements of the brain online that allow you to focus — it narrows your focus. You're not good at seeing the so-called big picture, but that narrowed focus allows you to do what I call duration-path-outcome types of analysis: to evaluate your environment, evaluate what you need to do, and prime your whole system for better cognition and priming your immune system to combat infection. This all makes sense when you think about the fact that famine, thirst, bacterial infections, viral infections, and invaders all liberate a response in the body designed to get you to fight back.

## [00:16:35] Tool: Deliberate Hyperventilation, Adrenaline & Infection

Short-term stress and the adrenaline response takes advantage of the fact that when adrenaline is released in the body from the adrenals, it also liberates a lot of killer cells from the immune organs — in particular from the spleen but also from elsewhere — and interactions with the lymphatic system that combat infection. The way this works in the real world is best captured by a study that can be mapped back to so-called Wim Hof breathing. Wim Hof breathing is named after the so-called Iceman, Wim Hof. There are two components to the breathing protocol he developed, which was also based on what's called Tummo breathing. Many people now call this super-oxygenation breathing — it's deliberate hyperventilation.

Deliberate hyperventilation done for maybe 25 cycles — inhale, exhale, inhale, exhale, in rapid movements of the diaphragm — will liberate adrenaline from the adrenals. This was beautifully shown in a study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences USA, in which people were injected with endotoxin or a bacterial wall fragment that mimics infection — it gives you a fever, makes you nauseous, and makes you feel sick. Half the people did a particular pattern of breathing: 25 deep inhales and exhales followed by an exhale breath hold, then repeating. After 25 to 30 cycles you'll start feeling heated up and feel the adrenaline response — you're liberating adrenaline in your body. Then exhale, hold for about 15 seconds, and repeat. That group essentially experienced zero symptoms from the E. coli injection — no fever, no nausea, no vomiting, no diarrhea. This makes total sense because the acute stress response is designed to combat all stressors.

Important safety note: never ever do this anywhere near water — people have passed out (so-called shallow water blackout), and people have died. Please don't do it at all unless you get clearance from your doctor, because there are pulmonary effects, and breath holds should definitely not be done by anyone who has glaucoma or pressure concerns for the eyes. Also think about the common experience of working very hard for a long period, then finally going on vacation and getting sick — that's because the adrenaline response crashed and the immune system crashed with it.

## [00:21:01] Raising Stress Threshold, Tool: Eye Dilation

Medium-term stress is going to be stress that lasts anywhere from several days to several weeks. Managing it has a lot to do with raising our stress threshold — our ability to cognitively regulate what's going on in our body. This is about capacity. The tools for this look a lot like the tools I just described: they involve deliberately placing yourself in a situation where adrenaline is increased somewhat — not to the extreme — and then when you feel flooded with adrenaline and would normally panic, it's about cognitively, mentally, emotionally calming yourself and becoming comfortable with that response in your body.

You can use cyclic hyper-oxygenation breathing, cold showers, or exercise where you bring your heart rate up very high — sprint, go hard on a bike, whatever brings your heart rate up to 80–90% of maximum. In those moments, the key is to learn to relax the mind while the body is very activated. One way to do this involves the visual system: when we're stressed our pupils dilate, which creates tunnel vision — we no longer see in panorama. By deliberately dilating your gaze — going from tunnel vision to broader, panoramic vision and literally seeing more of your environment all at once — it creates a calming effect on the mind. It releases a particular circuit in the brain stem that's associated with alertness and stress. If you're running at max capacity or hitting 80–90% on the bike and you dilate your gaze, you'll find the mind can relax while the body is in full output. This isn't about unifying mind and body — it's about using the body to bring up your level of activation and then dissociating the mental/emotional response from what's going on in your body. Do this maybe once a week and you'll start becoming comfortable at higher activation states — what once felt overwhelming and like a lot of work now feels manageable and tolerable.

## [00:25:00] Mitigating Long-Term Stress; Tool: Social Connection, Delight

Long-term stress is bad. You do not want adrenaline elevated in your system for a very long time. Ideally your stress goes up various times throughout the day but never stays elevated and never prevents you from getting a good night's sleep. When you are no longer able to achieve good sleep, you're moving from acute to chronic stress. We know that chronic stress — especially in so-called type-A personalities — creates heart disease, the leading killer in most countries. The best mechanisms we know to modulate long-term stress might surprise you.

The data really point to social connection, and certain types of social connection in particular, as what's going to mitigate or reduce long-term stress. This is a particularly important issue nowadays, where we have all these proxies and surrogates for social connection — online, texting with people. The way to think about social connection and how it mitigates long-term stress is through the systems of neuromodulation like serotonin. Serotonin is a neuromodulator — a kind of playlist for the brain that tends to amplify or bias the likelihood that certain brain and body circuits are activated. Serotonin generally gives us feelings of well-being; at very high levels it makes us feel blissed; and it tends to make us feel like we have enough in our immediate environment. When we see somebody we recognize and trust, serotonin is released in the brain, with positive effects on the immune system, neural repair, synapses, and reinforcing connections in the brain — preventing that long-term withering. Social connection can take many forms: romantic attachments, familial attachments, friendship, pets, and even attachments to things that just delight us. Having a sense of delight — really enjoying something you see, engage in, witness, or participate in — is associated with the serotonin system. Finding just a few people, even one, or an animal, or something you delight in, has very positive effects on mitigating long-term stress.

## [00:28:58] Melatonin, Caution

There are compounds that are not prescription drugs that can modulate the stress system, and sometimes because of the way that life is, we just don't have the opportunity to control life or our response to stress. I want to focus on three compounds — one of which I think you need to be cautious about. The first is melatonin. Melatonin is a hormone secreted from the pineal gland in direct relationship to how much darkness you're in — light suppresses melatonin. Melatonin helps you fall asleep; it doesn't help you stay asleep. I personally do not recommend supplementing melatonin because it's typically supplemented at very high levels — 1 to 3 milligrams or even more — which is an outrageously high dose. It also has a number of potentially negative effects on the reproductive axis and hormones. Use caution with melatonin.

## [00:30:06] L-theanine, Ashwagandha

L-theanine — which, provided it's safe for you, can be taken at 100 or 200 milligrams about 30 to 60 minutes before sleep — can enhance the transition to sleep and depth of sleep for many people. It increases GABA, this inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain, tends to reduce activity in the forebrain and ruminating systems, and helps people fall asleep. Theanine has also been shown, for people who are chronically anxious or chronically stressed, to significantly increase relaxation. It is known to have a minor effect on anxiety but eight studies have shown it has a notable effect on stress. The other supplement that can be very useful is ashwagandha. Ashwagandha is known to lower anxiety and cortisol. I don't take it on a regular basis — only when I'm in times that are particularly stressful and I'm not managing short and medium-term stress well. So social connection, supplementation (ashwagandha, L-theanine), and of course diet, exercise, and sleep are the tools for long-term stress.
