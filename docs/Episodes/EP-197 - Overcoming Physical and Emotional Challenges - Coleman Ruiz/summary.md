# Episode 197: Overcoming Physical & Emotional Challenges | Coleman Ruiz

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Coleman Ruiz is a former Tier One U.S. Navy SEAL who served in Afghanistan and Iraq and as a BUD/S training officer. In this conversation he traces his life from a modest upbringing in East New Orleans — through wrestling, the Naval Academy, and elite special operations — to civilian life, PTSD, hitting rock bottom with severe depression and suicidality, and an eventual climb back through therapy, community, and intentional daily practice. The episode is framed through Joseph Campbell's 17-stage hero's journey (monomyth). Ruiz speaks with rare vulnerability about grief, mentorship, the death of close friends, alcohol, and the moment he came to the edge of suicide. Huberman weaves in relevant neuroscience on adolescent dispersal, the stress response, and pharmacological support for PTSD recovery.

## Guest: Coleman Ruiz

Coleman Ruiz grew up in East New Orleans in a working-class family — his father was a welder, his mother a dental assistant. He found direction through wrestling in seventh grade, attended NAPS (Naval Academy Preparatory School), graduated from the U.S. Naval Academy in 1998, and served as a Navy SEAL for 13 years, reaching Tier One special operations. After leaving the military in 2011 he entered civilian life, worked in private equity, and eventually hit a severe PTSD-driven depression following psychedelic-assisted experiences. He has since rebuilt through talk therapy, structured daily routine, and community work. At the time of the episode he serves as Chief Operating Officer at Lid Sports Group, the largest brick-and-mortar licensed sports retailer in North America.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Wrestling as a redirector of wildness** — Ruiz's disruptive behavior in seventh grade stopped entirely when he entered the wrestling room. Combat sports gave his aggression a structured, respected outlet. His grades improved immediately and only during wrestling season. [00:13:24](transcript.md#001324-wrestling-combat-sports--respect)

2. **Three predictors of BUD/S success (anecdotal)** — As a first-phase officer in charge at BUD/S in 2005, Ruiz and his team found that nearly every candidate who completed Hell Week had at least one of three things: (a) varsity athletic experience in high school or college, (b) divorced parents, or (c) a school suspension. The unifying factor appears to be prior exposure to aloneness, adversity, and self-reliance under pressure. [01:04:51](transcript.md#010451-buds-success-predictors-divorce--aloneness-rebellion)

3. **The 24-hour horizon as a coping framework** — Ruiz learned early in military training to shrink his time horizon to the next 24 hours when facing overwhelming circumstances. This mental tool — refusing to project beyond the immediate — became a lifelong anchor in adversity, applied from Hell Week through his civilian crisis. [00:40:08](transcript.md#004008-growth-mindset-24-hour-horizon)

4. **Mentors as load-bearing structures** — Several mentors defined Ruiz's trajectory: a wrestling coach who instilled growth mindset, Gunnery Sergeant Flynn at prep school, Master Chief Martin (100 combat missions from Vietnam), and especially Doug Zembiec — whose death in combat forced a confrontation with Ruiz's own mortality. Losing a mentor who seemed indestructible revealed that external toughness guarantees nothing. [01:36:08](transcript.md#013608-mentor-death--facing-mortality)

5. **Warriors feel compassion for all sides** — Ruiz observed that in war, every combatant on every side believes they are fighting for something right — their family, their people, their values. This realization expanded his capacity for compassion without compromising his mission clarity. [01:47:49](transcript.md#014749-warriors--compassion-trauma-family)

6. **The hero's journey applies to civilian reintegration** — Drawing on Joseph Campbell's 17-stage monomyth, Ruiz describes leaving the military as a forced return to the "ordinary world" without the rituals or recognition that mark transition in traditional cultures. He expected civilian life to be easier; when it wasn't, PTSD, depression, and alcohol followed this unacknowledged re-entry. [01:57:39](transcript.md#015739-hero-with-a-thousand-faces-civilian-return--ptsd)

7. **Psychedelics: stabilize first, then consider** — Ruiz used ibogaine and 5-MeO-DMT two days apart in a clinical context. He describes the combined experience as "the nuclear option" — it shattered his defenses and left him to rebuild without support. He explicitly advises stabilizing through skilled talk therapy before any psychedelic intervention — not the reverse. [02:14:10](transcript.md#021410-psychedelics-connection-warrior-culture)

8. **Rock bottom: one day near the line** — After the psychedelics stripped away his defense mechanisms, Ruiz descended into severe depression — sweating, shaking, sleepless nights. There was one day where he seriously considered suicide. His internal reframe: either "I am fundamentally bad" or "something bad got inside me that I must remove." He chose the second interpretation, which opened the path to seeking help. [02:30:42](transcript.md#023042-suicide-asking-for-help--support)

9. **Asking for help is the hardest hard thing** — A lifelong athlete and warrior who had charged into gunfights found that sitting down with a therapist and talking was more terrifying than combat. The resistance to vulnerability was the final barrier — and breaking it was the actual hero's journey. [02:44:54](transcript.md#024454-healing-process-unsatisfaction--asking-for-help)

10. **Low-dose Wellbutrin as a pharmacological bridge** — Ruiz used approximately four months of low-dose Wellbutrin (bupropion) during his recovery to support dopamine and norepinephrine while stabilizing sleep and engaging in therapy. He frames it not as a fix but as scaffolding — like using a gun sight — that enabled him to do the deeper work. [02:38:32](transcript.md#023832-therapy-ptsd-recovery-dread-pharmacology)

11. **Range over specialization in building manhood** — Citing David Epstein's book, Ruiz argues that the most important quality in a man — and a parent — is range: the ability to operate across many domains, tolerate discomfort at the edges of competence, and model that for children. He also emphasizes releasing the grip rather than overgripping an already uncontrollable world. [03:02:22](transcript.md#030222-manhood-range-parenthood-surrender)

12. **Physical movement is necessary but not sufficient for mental health** — Ruiz trains five days a week (cycling, running, swimming, kettlebells) and uses sauna on two rest days. He also applies the 90-minute caffeine delay daily, which he dislikes but finds transformative for sustained energy. Movement supports mental health but does not replace therapy, social connection, and introspection. [02:54:03](transcript.md#025403-daily-routine-movement-nutrition)

## Tools & Protocols

### 24-Hour Horizon
- **What:** Compress your planning and emotional window to the next 24 hours when circumstances feel overwhelming.
- **How:** Refuse to project into the future beyond one day. Focus only on what the next 24 hours require. Originally learned in military training to survive Hell Week and multi-day operations; re-applied during civilian crisis.
- **When:** During periods of extreme stress, grief, or uncertainty.
- **Ref:** [00:40:08](transcript.md#004008-growth-mindset-24-hour-horizon)

### Daily Physical Training (Ruiz Protocol)
- **What:** Five-day-per-week mixed training; two rest days with sauna.
- **How:** Cycling, running, swimming, and kettlebell work rotated across the week. On non-training days: minimum one hour in a sauna at home. Treated as standard maintenance — not biohacking.
- **When:** Every morning, year-round, regardless of mental state.
- **Ref:** [02:54:03](transcript.md#025403-daily-routine-movement-nutrition)

### 90-Minute Caffeine Delay
- **What:** Waiting 90 minutes after waking before consuming coffee.
- **How:** Delay the first cup of coffee by 90 minutes from wake time to allow cortisol to peak naturally. Ruiz describes this as something he dislikes doing but notices a marked improvement in sustained energy throughout the day.
- **When:** Every morning as part of daily routine.
- **Ref:** [02:54:03](transcript.md#025403-daily-routine-movement-nutrition)

### Talk Therapy as Primary PTSD Intervention
- **What:** Regular sessions with a skilled therapist as the foundation of PTSD recovery.
- **How:** Commit to consistent attendance even when resistance is high. The things hardest to say are the most important to say. Begin before or instead of psychedelics, not after. Ruiz emphasizes that stabilizing through deliberate, Dr. Conti-level help first could have prevented the severity of his breakdown.
- **When:** As early as possible after identifying PTSD symptoms; stabilize life situation first before adding any other interventions.
- **Ref:** [02:38:32](transcript.md#023832-therapy-ptsd-recovery-dread-pharmacology)

### Low-Dose Wellbutrin Bridge
- **What:** Short-term use of a low-dose norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor to support engagement in therapy and stabilize mood during acute depression.
- **How:** Prescribed pharmacologically under clinical supervision. Ruiz used it for approximately four months at a low dose (a higher dose nuked his memory). Framed as scaffolding — "like using a gun sight" — enabling him to show up for therapy rather than as a standalone solution.
- **When:** During acute depressive episodes alongside talk therapy; not indefinitely.
- **Ref:** [02:38:32](transcript.md#023832-therapy-ptsd-recovery-dread-pharmacology)

### Space-Time Bridging
- **What:** A perceptual exercise for shifting awareness between internal and external focus to reduce PTSD-driven hypervigilance and rumination.
- **How:** Alternate attention between internal body sensations and external environmental details, progressively extending the time horizon of awareness outward. Ruiz also used watching the "Pale Blue Dot" video to gain perspective and step back from the 24-hour ledge.
- **When:** During periods of hyperarousal, dissociation, or intrusive thought loops.
- **Ref:** [02:07:03](transcript.md#020703-massage-perspective-space-time-bridging) — dedicated episode: https://youtu.be/wTBSGgbIvsY?t=8094

### Psychedelic-Assisted Reflection (Advanced — Stabilization Required First)
- **What:** Ibogaine and 5-MeO-DMT used in a structured clinical context to process trauma.
- **How:** Only after life stabilization and with professional clinical supervision. Ruiz describes this as stripping away all coping defenses — which is why prior stability is essential. The 5-MeO-DMT experience was described as "liftoff" — a geometric mosaic in light blue and white, connecting him through time to all warrior cultures. Not appropriate for everyone.
- **When:** Only after baseline stability (sleep, safety, support structure) is established. Not as a first intervention.
- **Ref:** [02:14:10](transcript.md#021410-psychedelics-connection-warrior-culture)
