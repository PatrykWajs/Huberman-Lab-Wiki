## [00:04:12] Should You Vary Wake-Up Time Seasonally?

**Audience:** In Seattle, sunrise varies from 4:30 AM to 9:00 AM depending on season. Are you recommending to vary your wake-up and outside time with the seasons?

**Andrew Huberman:** Somewhat. You don't need to see the sun cross the horizon — that would be great, but not everyone can wake up with the sun. You want to get so-called low solar angle sunlight, because of that yellow-blue contrast. Many people wake up before the sun is out, and if that's the case and you want to be awake, turn on as many bright lights as you can. I'd say that in the winter months up here, many of you feel less well — and the dopamine-melanin pathway is linked: tyrosine is the precursor to dopamine and also to melanin production. So the whole system is linked. I suggest in the winter months getting 30 minutes of sunlight viewing — I know it's a lot, but it's much better than feeling lousy all day. The real key in the winter is to try and catch some sunlight before it goes down, because if you're indoors and it goes down and then you go outside and it's dark, your brain and body don't really know where they are in time. And then you flip on a streaming show and you really don't know where you are in time.

## [00:06:05] Why Is My Drive Depleted Upon Waking-Up?

**Audience:** I go to sleep fired up, ready and excited to do whatever it takes. When I wake up, that drive is depleted. Why, and what can I do?

**Andrew Huberman:** Interesting — I haven't heard that one before. If I were to venture a guess, we didn't spend much time tonight talking about the autonomic nervous system, this kind of seesaw that takes us from very alert to very deep sleep. The parasympathetic nervous system may be overactive relative to the sympathetic — you may be sleeping very, very deeply. When you are in deep rest, the last thing you want to do is get into that forward center-of-mass thinking, planning, and predicting. In sleep, you're in a very deeply parasympathetic state: your eyes are actually closed, and space and time — past, present, and future — are all invited into your thinking. You may be waking up mid-sleep cycle, which makes the transition hard. Many people find it useful to set an alarm so that they wake up at the end of a 90-minute so-called ultradian cycle — rather than sleeping 7 hours, you might be better off sleeping 6 or 7.5 hours, right at the end of one of these cycles. I don't wake up like Jocko Willink at 4:30 AM ready to attack the day. Like Tiger, I want water, I want sunlight, and 90 minutes later I want caffeine.

## [00:12:08] What Excites You About the Future of Mental Health Treatment?

**Audience:** What excites you most about the future research of mental health treatment, particularly anxiety and depression?

**Andrew Huberman:** I'll just reveal my biases: I'm quite pessimistic at the idea that we're going to have better medication soon for most things. Because there are different receptors for all these chemicals all over the brain and body, that side-effect-less drug is unlikely to exist for mental health. What I do think is coming is the combination of maybe some pharmacology, but especially behavioral tools — people actually learning how to drive their nervous system. For conditions like schizophrenia, OCD, and eating disorders — and anorexia is the most lethal of all psychiatric disorders, an amazing and sad fact — I think we're going to see combination behavioral therapy, drug therapy, and brain-machine interface using devices like virtual reality and transcranial magnetic stimulation. I also have to say that I think we're in very exciting times for psychedelic therapies. Four years ago when I started doing public-facing work, I was absolutely terrified to say that word. The clinical data on MDMA and psilocybin are very interesting. The brain can enter a state of heightened learning capacity, but that plasticity needs to be directed toward something. A guest on the podcast, Matthew Johnson at Johns Hopkins, answered my question about microdosing by saying simply: "Macrodose." He explained that the one guided session triggering rewiring plasticity, guided toward a particular direction, is far more useful than nudging the system without any particular goal. Young people and those with preexisting psychiatric issues or addiction propensity should be very cautious, but I'd be lying if I didn't say it's a very exciting time.

## [00:17:25] What Is the Biggest Area For Performance Enhancement?

**Audience:** Where do you see the biggest area for performance enhancement within the elite athletes and operators that already hit marks of proper sleep and nutrition?

**Andrew Huberman:** First of all, very few of them actually hit marks for proper sleep. But for those that do — once sleep and nutrition are dialed in and the motivational component is there — I think the biggest area of untapped potential is what we call creativity: taking preexisting neural maps and starting to combine them in unique ways. Performance in any domain can be summarized as four stages: unskilled, skilled, mastery, and then virtuosity. Virtuosity, by definition, means inviting back in a component of uncertainty — virtuosos actually don't know what they're going to do next. One of the best ways to access creative states is to use your visual system to view things that are highly unstable and uncertain. I don't just love fish tanks; I love staring at videos of aquariums because it's completely unpredictable. Nature is filled with unpredictable visual and auditory stimuli, which is why people have their best ideas in the shower or on a walk — it's that absence of top-down regulation on rules. The liminal state between sleep and waking is also very powerful for accessing creativity. You are able to access combinations of neural maps that are unusual, and you want to find these cognitive states in ways that are non-destructive, rather than using drugs or alcohol.

## [00:22:32] Tips on How to Improve Memory

**Audience:** Do you have any tips on how to improve memory?

**Andrew Huberman:** This is a wild literature and I love it. I thought that to remember things you're supposed to get really excited, really focused, and then remember them — but that's not how you do it. There are data, and stories going back to medieval times when they used to teach kids things and then throw them in the river. There's a beautiful Annual Review of Neuroscience written by the late James McGaugh showing that if you want to remember something, you want to spike adrenaline after you acquired that information. That means the double espresso and the ice bath after you study for math, immediately after. Think about the one-trial learning that nobody wants to experience — a car accident or a traumatic event. You didn't get the spike of adrenaline first; you got it after. If you give people a beta blocker after learning emotional information, they don't learn it as well — incredible data from Larry Cahill at UC Irvine and James McGaugh. It's also true that if you tell yourself something is really important to you, you'll be able to learn it better. When I meet people and they tell me their name and I forget it two seconds later, I now think: what could I do right now to spike my adrenaline a little bit? A cold shower, a sprint, or even some emotional arousal can all help consolidate memory.

## [00:24:54] How Do You Manage Social Media Addiction?

**Audience:** How do you manage social media addiction?

**Andrew Huberman:** We should be careful with the word addiction here — although I think it's entirely appropriate. When you are engaging in a behavior over and over again and you're thinking to yourself, "This isn't even that interesting," you're officially addicted. People talk about the dopamine hits of social media, but those only come at the beginning. When you find yourself scrolling thinking "What am I doing?" — that dopamine wave pool is depleted, at least for that activity. How do you manage it? You have to stop seeking within social media. I've taken on the practice of turning off my phone for a couple of hours each day — it's incredibly hard, and people get really upset because of the tethers they expect. We all have a map of space, time, and closeness to everyone we know, and the phone allows us to tap into that closeness map constantly. You can understand why it's so valuable to people. But those breaks are essential, and I think the data will increasingly bear this out.

## [00:29:10] Is Learning from Failure Equal to Learning from Success?

**Audience:** Is learning from failure equal to learning from success? Is one more efficient than the other?

**Andrew Huberman:** On a trial-by-trial basis, we know that when you fail at an attempt, on the next attempt your forebrain is in a position to engage better — you feel that frustration and you want to get the next one, funneling more neural resources. You have to be mindful of that too, because that focus can narrow so much that you can start to lose access to information that might help you. If you were just to relax a little bit and dilate that focus, you'd often do better. In terms of life experiences, failures keep you humble. I've made a ton of mistakes — some were mistakes of persistence where I kept thinking something would change when it clearly wasn't going to, some were failures of misjudgment about other people, and a lot were just plain failures where the experiment didn't work. You try and reframe those. I do think we owe it to ourselves and to the people we know to try and generate some wins and help other people generate wins too — in running a lab you realize how important it is for your students to publish a paper and feel that success.

## [00:36:29] Advice or Protocols to Improve Learning & Retention

**Audience:** What would be your best one or two pieces of advice or recommended protocol for improving learning and retention for graduate students in science and medicine?

**Andrew Huberman:** I used to teach a course at Cold Spring Harbor on career development for scientists. The two most important things are: first, find non-destructive ways to reset your dopamine and energy levels at least every three days. I used to work really hard on Mondays and Tuesdays, not go in until the afternoon on Wednesdays, go in Thursday and Friday and work really hard, take Saturday off, and maybe do a little from home on Sunday. Those breaks are absolutely key and they're not encouraged in academic or tech cultures. Just do it and let the results define you, not how many hours you're in the lab. Second, Non-Sleep Deep Rest protocols are enormously useful for the cognitive load of graduate work. I know at least two faculty at Stanford — including a Howard Hughes investigator — who take two 20-minute naps per day in their office. When one of them came to visit me at my previous university, he asked if he could nap during the time we were supposed to meet. He then gave a great seminar afterward. The research is pretty clear on the benefits.

## [00:38:42] What Exciting Research/Work are You Doing?

**Audience:** What exciting research or work are you doing?

**Andrew Huberman:** I hope this paper gets accepted soon — it's been out for review forever. We did a very large-scale study during the pandemic: David Spiegel and I, and an amazing PhD named Melis Yilmaz Balban. We essentially equipped people with remote monitoring devices and measured sleep, heart rate variability, stress, and a bunch of other things. We gave them a very brief set of breathing protocols. It turns out that this double inhale followed by a long exhale — the so-called physiological sigh — was the most effective breathing practice for allowing people to control their heart rate variability, reduce overall heart rate, and access better sleep. These were extremely short protocols. I didn't discover physiological sighs, but I love the idea that people can do a very brief protocol once a day — maybe even just while walking down the street — and actually learn to control that autonomic seesaw. We're also gearing up to do studies on people with more severe forms of anxiety and panic attacks, using mainly respiration but also looking at vision-related ways of controlling the nervous system.

## [00:40:22] How Does Dopamine Factor into Neuroplasticity?

**Audience:** How does dopamine factor into neuroplasticity, if at all?

**Andrew Huberman:** It's a very strong trigger of plasticity. There's some work showing that if you stimulate with an electrode the brain area that releases dopamine and pair that with anything — even just an eight kilohertz tone — the brain remaps and treats that tone as highly significant. Remember, dopamine is dumb in a sense: it just marks things as important regardless of whether they actually are. If you trigger dopamine release with Ritalin, Adderall, L-Tyrosine, or other compounds, whatever you're doing seems super interesting — and that's why it's such a slippery slope. That's also why I think if people are going to explore MDMA or other psychedelics, do it as part of one of the university-supported clinical trials. MDMA is a very unusual brain state — high dopamine, high serotonin — and if you're not pushing that toward some therapeutic outcome, everything just feels amazing but you're not directing that plasticity anywhere useful. Dopamine turns us into idiots about what we chase, but that also means it can be harnessed deliberately for learning if we're strategic about it.

## [00:46:47] Is Age 66 Too Old for Neuroplasticity & Learning?

**Audience:** Is age 66 too old to begin learning again?

**Andrew Huberman:** No! Richard Feynman taught himself to draw later in life — and he was also really into flotation tanks and bongo drumming naked on the roof at Caltech. You can absolutely learn at 66 and way beyond. There's an amazing study from Rusty Gage's lab at the Salk Institute showing that even people who are very late in life, terminally ill in fact, are still producing new neurons in the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus. These people were gracious enough to allow researchers to inject them with dyes that would label neurons for analysis postmortem. What's harder with age is focus, and sometimes sleep as well — but the same mechanisms apply. There is no evidence whatsoever that neuroplasticity disappears at any stage.

## [00:48:00] How Do You Read Research Papers?

**Audience:** How do you tackle reading research papers? Do you have a specific strategy?

**Andrew Huberman:** Yes, I take notes on everything. There are four questions I teach students and use myself. First: what's the question they're asking — both major and more specific? Second: what did they do — what methods did they use, are they looking at mice or humans, what conditions? Third: what did they find? Fourth, and most important, write down the answer to this: what did they conclude? Then look back at the first question and ask, did they actually answer that question, or is it something unrelated? Those four questions are essentially the way that I parse each paper. For the podcast, I also use the telephone — I call people and badger them about who's doing the really good work in a given area. And the best way to remember science is to tell someone about it. Before each podcast I'll call someone and say, "Hey, did you know they used to throw kids in the river?" My sister, who is a therapist and does not watch the podcast, once told me about an amazing breathing technique — it was something I'd covered extensively — and I said, "You know, I have a podcast about that." She said, "I don't like your podcast." Older sister.

## [00:50:10] Most Important Takeaway from Your ADHD Research?

**Audience:** What's most important from your ADHD research, for people on medication or not on medication?

**Andrew Huberman:** For people on medication: work with somebody really good who's willing to help you find the minimal effective dose and optimal timing. From waking until about eight or nine hours later — what I've been calling phase one of the day — the systems that release cortisol, dopamine, and epinephrine are more effective at producing those neurochemicals than later in the day. So the important question is: if you're using Ritalin, Adderall, Vyvanse, or Modafinil, when is the optimal time to take it? The numbers of people using these compounds on and off prescription is astronomical — something like 80% of college students use them at some point, because they put you into a narrow tunnel of concentration. For people not on medication: you can train focus, but it feels terrible to do so. There are large-scale studies, including in China, of people literally teaching themselves to focus their vision on a narrow aperture and battling through the agitation and stress that comes with it. Focus will drift — that's normal — and the key is learning to bring it back without self-judgment. The training of focus itself, through that discomfort, is where the real benefit lies.
