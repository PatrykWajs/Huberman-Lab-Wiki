# Episode 354: Transform Pain & Trauma Into Creative Expression | David Choe

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

David Choe — world-renowned artist, muralist, graffiti pioneer, writer, podcaster, and television host — joins Andrew Huberman for an unfiltered, deeply personal conversation recorded in their first in-person meeting. Choe grew up as the middle child of Korean immigrants in Los Angeles, experiencing physical, emotional, and sexual abuse alongside a mother who practiced absolute blind faith — simultaneously calling him the greatest artist alive and raising him in chaos. He channeled that paradox into graffiti vandalism, petty theft, jail time in Japan, pornographic illustration, Vice journalism, and a commission to paint the original Facebook offices that he accepted as equity, which later made him a multimillionaire. The episode is structured around shame as Choe's primary addiction driver — the mechanism behind gambling, workaholism, and every other compulsion — and his decade-long recovery through rehab, plant medicine, men's groups, 12-step programs, and re-learning how to feel like he is enough.

## Key Takeaways

- **Every addiction is a gambling addiction.** Choe frames all compulsive behavior — gambling, sex, food, workaholism, shopping — as variations of gambling: tolerating extreme risk in exchange for a dopamine payoff. The shared root is an inability to sit with oneself. [01:35:11](transcript.md#shame-gambling-addiction-stress)
- **Shame is the drug, not just the wound.** Choe identifies himself as a "shame chaser" — he got high from engineering public humiliation and failure. Posting giant illegal murals hoping people hate them ("not as cool" if everyone called it a masterpiece) was literally more exciting than praise. The shame hit was the point. [01:43:05](transcript.md#sexual-abuse-trauma-shame-addiction)
- **Blind faith and creative conviction share the same mechanism.** Choe's born-again Christian mother modeled absolute belief without evidence — in Jesus, in lying to make a deal, in declaring her son the world's greatest artist when he was 5 years old. This installed the capacity to hold conviction without external proof, which powered his career before anyone agreed he was good. [00:28:45](transcript.md#mother-beliefs-religion-artistic-ability-childhood)
- **Creative explosions happen in the mundane, not the spectacular.** Looking back at his most productive periods, Choe consistently finds they happened in cold, Wi-Fi-free, boring places — San Jose, Palo Alto, Gilroy — not New York City. The myth that you need the right environment to create is just a myth. [00:20:40](transcript.md#childhood-podcasts-mundane-moments--artist-life)
- **You cannot outthink a feeling.** Choe's core critique of intellectuals is applying logic to emotional and spiritual problems. Addiction, faith, shame: none are logic problems. "You're never going to outsmart a feeling." [01:10:57](transcript.md#adapting-creativity)
- **Workaholism is the addiction you can hide in plain sight.** After gambling, jail, sex addiction, and podcast self-destruction, workaholism became Choe's last refuge — socially rewarded, culturally praised, impossible to distinguish from productivity. Society gives you a pat on the back while the underlying escape mechanism is identical to the casino. [01:43:05](transcript.md#sexual-abuse-trauma-shame-addiction)
- **Post-traumatic growth required simultaneous multi-modal intervention.** Recovery required plant medicine, therapy, rehab (45+ days), men's groups, and 12-step all at once — not one modality. Friends staging an intervention was the tipping point. Choe frames it explicitly: "It's either I kill myself or I stop." [02:58:40](transcript.md#rehab-god-purpose-parents--disappointment-the-choe-show-pee-wee-herman)
- **Journaling trained the vulnerability muscle.** Choe's father required movie reviews from his sons as children. When Choe began writing real feelings, having his brothers read and mock the journal became a formative shame event — and also the moment he decided if he could survive that exposure, he could survive any public failure in his art. [00:53:30](transcript.md#santa-belief-journal-vulnerability-heart-break-art)
- **Art immediately after an emotional event is the processing itself.** Choe does not wait until he has processed trauma to make work. The making is the processing. He advocates going directly into the raw state — pick up the brush or pen immediately after a shame event, rejection, or heartbreak. [00:53:30](transcript.md#santa-belief-journal-vulnerability-heart-break-art)
- **"Play the tape out" interrupts in-the-moment addictive logic.** When tempted by a behavior that has always escalated, mentally run the scenario forward: how does this end? Addiction always escalates and loves novelty. Projecting the future breaks the short-circuit of immediate relief. [03:31:15](transcript.md#surviving--thriving-suicide-addiction-play-the-tape-out-fun-feeling-enough)
- **Feeling "enough" is the hardest and most important shift.** Choe writes "I am worthy. I'm enough." in deodorant on his bathroom mirror — a daily counter-program to thousands of ambient "you're not enough" messages. He brainwashed himself into believing he was the best artist alive; the same technique applies to self-worth. [03:31:15](transcript.md#surviving--thriving-suicide-addiction-play-the-tape-out-fun-feeling-enough)
- **Legacy is nothing; presence is everything.** Choe rejects legacy-building as motivation. "You just have this. You just have right now today." He illustrates with an Uber driver who has never heard of Nirvana — the greatest things become invisible within a generation. [03:25:23](transcript.md#legacy-vacation-work-authenticity)

## Tools & Protocols

### Journaling as Unsanitized Emotional Output
- **What:** Write everything — no audience, no self-editing, no polish. Let the worst feelings land on the page.
- **How:** Daily or after emotionally significant events. Goal is full vulnerability, not craft. Choe's father started this practice by requiring movie reviews; Choe expanded it to raw emotional confession.
- **When:** From childhood onward; especially useful during shame, rejection, or heartbreak.
- **Ref:** [00:53:30](transcript.md#santa-belief-journal-vulnerability-heart-break-art)

### Making Art Immediately After an Emotional Event
- **What:** Channel raw emotional states directly into creative work rather than waiting to process first.
- **How:** Pick up the medium and go into the feeling, not around it. The act of making metabolizes the emotion.
- **When:** Immediately after a shame event, rejection, heartbreak, or rage spike.
- **Ref:** [00:53:30](transcript.md#santa-belief-journal-vulnerability-heart-break-art)

### 12-Step Recovery Programs (Multi-Variant)
- **What:** Structured peer-accountability community using the 12-step framework.
- **How:** Choe has attended Gamblers Anonymous, Debtors Anonymous, Overeaters Anonymous, Sex and Love Addicts Anonymous, Workaholics Anonymous, and others. His insight: every addiction is a gambling addiction, so all variants share the same core work.
- **When:** Ongoing; part of a multi-modal recovery stack. Huberman and Choe first met at one of these meetings.
- **Ref:** [01:35:11](transcript.md#shame-gambling-addiction-stress)

### Plant Medicine Therapy
- **What:** Used as part of the recovery stack alongside traditional therapy and 12-step.
- **How:** Integrated into intensive multi-modal approach post-intervention by close friends.
- **When:** During the initial recovery phase.
- **Ref:** [02:58:40](transcript.md#rehab-god-purpose-parents--disappointment-the-choe-show-pee-wee-herman)

### Men's Groups
- **What:** Peer-accountability circles specifically for men dealing with addiction, trauma, and shame cycles.
- **How:** Regular group meetings; Choe names these alongside formal therapy as critical infrastructure for sustained recovery.
- **When:** Ongoing.
- **Ref:** [01:43:05](transcript.md#sexual-abuse-trauma-shame-addiction)

### "Play the Tape Out" Technique
- **What:** When facing a tempting but destructive behavior, mentally project the full downstream scenario.
- **How:** Ask: "How does this end for me?" Addiction always escalates — it does not stay at the comfortable early stage. Running the tape forward breaks the in-the-moment logic of "just this once."
- **When:** At any decision point where immediate relief feels stronger than known consequences.
- **Ref:** [03:31:15](transcript.md#surviving--thriving-suicide-addiction-play-the-tape-out-fun-feeling-enough)

### Positive Affirmation Counter-Programming
- **What:** Writing "I am worthy. I'm enough." in deodorant on the bathroom mirror so it is seen every morning.
- **How:** Treat self-worth like any skill practiced daily. Choe reprogrammed himself into believing he was the best artist through relentless internal repetition — the same technique applies to self-compassion.
- **When:** Every morning as a counter to ambient "you're not enough" messaging.
- **Ref:** [03:31:15](transcript.md#surviving--thriving-suicide-addiction-play-the-tape-out-fun-feeling-enough)

### Electronics Deprivation for Creative Work
- **What:** Social media on a separate phone stored in a lock box; internet blocks during creative sessions.
- **How:** Physical separation of devices. Choe observes his most creative explosions have always happened in cold, Wi-Fi-free, boring environments.
- **When:** During any focused creative or recovery work.
- **Ref:** [03:44:43](transcript.md#hope--faith-electronics-santa-claus)

### Saying No as a Practiced Skill
- **What:** Deliberately declining projects, invitations, and opportunities — especially from a codependent baseline where saying yes was the default survival mode.
- **How:** Treated as an active muscle to build, not a natural state. Choe describes being constitutionally incapable of it for decades.
- **When:** Ongoing in recovery; particularly important for workaholics who receive social approval from overcommitment.
- **Ref:** [02:58:40](transcript.md#rehab-god-purpose-parents--disappointment-the-choe-show-pee-wee-herman)

## Resources Mentioned

- **Vice Media / Vice TV** — Choe contributed illustrations, articles (under multiple fake names to fill pages), and later hosted the "Thumbs Up" hitchhiking series. Never paid for the magazine work. Gavin McInnes and Shane Smith are named as key figures.
- **Facebook (Meta)** — Choe painted the original Facebook offices in Palo Alto circa 2005 at the request of Sean Parker, accepting stock equity instead of a ~$60,000 cash fee. The equity became worth an estimated $200 million+ at IPO.
- **Marvel Comics** — Contacted Choe around 2000 to draw the X-Men; fired him without notice. His rage-response email sent to every Marvel employee became his first viral moment and a defining shame-then-comeback arc.
- **DC Comics / Vertigo imprint** — Axel Alonso brought Choe in after the Marvel incident, providing his mainstream comics breakthrough.
- **Collision Course (Jay-Z & Linkin Park mashup album, 2004)** — Choe created the album cover art for approximately $2,000; Warner Brothers used it in advertising without additional payment, leading to a dispute Sean Parker resolved by killing a major Warner Brothers–Facebook deal.
- **The Choe Show (FX / Hulu)** — Television show Choe created and hosted during his recovery period, conceived as his version of Pee-Wee's Playhouse. FX killed multiple episodes.
- **DVDASA podcast** — Early podcast co-hosted with Asa Akira, Bobby Lee, and Steve Lee; one of the earliest prominent podcasts; Choe describes it as a shame-chasing vehicle that eventually led to public fallout.
- **Comics Top Hits** — Beverly Hills comic book store where Choe worked at age 18 in 1994; location of his encounter with Stan Lee.
- **Giant Robot magazine** — Early outlet where Choe published illustrations and writing; no payment received.
