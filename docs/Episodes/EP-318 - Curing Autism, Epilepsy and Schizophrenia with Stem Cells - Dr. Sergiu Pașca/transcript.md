# Curing Autism, Epilepsy & Schizophrenia with Stem Cells | Dr. Sergiu Pașca

## [00:00:00] Sergiu Pașca

Welcome to the Huberman Lab podcast, where we discuss science and science-based tools for everyday life. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. My guest today is Dr. Sergiu Pașca, MD, professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at Stanford University. Dr. Pașca is an extraordinarily creative and rigorous scientist who has pioneered the use of human stem cells to build living models of the human brain. He developed technologies called organoids and assembloids — three-dimensional, self-organizing structures derived from a patient's own skin cells that can replicate the cellular circuitry underlying autism, epilepsy, schizophrenia, and other brain disorders. Today we discuss the genetics and biology of autism, why diagnoses are increasing, and the remarkable progress Dr. Pașca's lab has made toward treatments and potential cures. We also discuss the ethics of this research, what it means to grow human brain tissue in a dish, and what the first clinical trial built entirely on human stem cell models looks like.

## [00:02:08] Autism Spectrum Disorder, Incidence, Genetics

**Andrew Huberman:** Today we're going to focus on autism — what it is at the neurological and genetic level — and on the stem cell technologies you've built to model and treat it. Before we get into the science, can you give us a clear picture of what autism spectrum disorder actually is?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Autism spectrum disorder is one of the most common neurodevelopmental conditions. Historically, about 1 in 68 children was affected, but more recent estimates put it closer to about 1 in 31, which represents somewhere around 3% of kids. It's much more common in males than females — approximately a 4-to-1 ratio. The condition is defined behaviorally, not biologically. Clinicians use a set of behavioral criteria to make the diagnosis. There is no blood test, no brain scan, no biomarker. Autism is characterized by two main domains: difficulties in social communication and interaction, and restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, and activities. It is called a spectrum because the severity, presentation, and accompanying features — intellectual disability, language delays, epilepsy, sensory sensitivities — vary enormously from person to person. Some individuals are what we call high-functioning or even gifted in specific domains. Others have what is called profound autism, where they cannot communicate, may never live independently, and often require around-the-clock care.

## [00:07:16] Is Autism More Common in Males?

**Andrew Huberman:** The 4-to-1 male-to-female ratio — is that well-established? And do we understand why?

**Sergiu Pașca:** It's well-established. The ratio is roughly 4 males to every 1 female, though in more severe or profound presentations the ratio can be even higher. The explanation is not fully worked out. One influential idea is that females may have what's called a higher threshold for expressing autistic traits — meaning they need a larger genetic or environmental burden to show the same behavioral phenotype. This is sometimes called the female protective effect. It may relate to differences in how male and female brains develop, in hormonal milieu, in social learning strategies, or in some combination. Another factor is that autism in females may present differently and be harder to diagnose — girls may mask or camouflage their difficulties more effectively in social contexts. So part of the apparent ratio may be a genuine biological difference, and part may be a diagnostic bias.

## [00:11:56] Eye Contact in Babies, Fever; Proposed Causes of Autism; Genes

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the early signs, and when do they appear? And what's the role of genetics versus environment in causing autism?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Signs can appear very early in development — sometimes as early as 6 to 12 months of age. One of the most replicated early markers is reduced or atypical eye contact. Typically developing infants show a characteristic trajectory — they orient toward faces, make sustained eye contact, and use gaze to communicate interest. In infants who later receive an autism diagnosis, this trajectory is often flattened. Retrospective video analysis of home movies has been used to identify these early differences, and there are now prospective studies tracking infants with an older sibling with autism. Another early marker that has fascinated researchers is the fever effect — a well-documented phenomenon where some children with autism show temporary but dramatic improvement in social behavior and communication during a fever. This suggests that the brain circuits responsible for social behavior are present but somehow suppressed or dysregulated, and that the metabolic changes associated with fever can temporarily restore more typical function. It's a fascinating clue about the underlying biology.

In terms of causes: genetics plays a very large role. Twin studies, which compare identical and fraternal twins, show that if one identical twin has autism, the other has a very high probability of also having it — heritability estimates typically range from 64 to 91 percent. This established the genetic basis firmly in the 1970s and overturned what had been the dominant hypothesis before that, which we now call the refrigerator mother hypothesis. That was the deeply harmful and completely wrong idea promoted by Bruno Bettelheim that cold, emotionally distant mothering caused autism. Twin studies refuted that conclusively. We now know that autism has a strong genetic basis, though the genetics are extremely heterogeneous — hundreds of different genes are implicated, and for any given individual there may be a combination of many small-effect variants or a single large-effect mutation.

## [00:18:48] Genetic or Idiopathic Autism Diagnoses, Timothy Syndrome

**Andrew Huberman:** What fraction of autism cases have an identifiable genetic cause versus what you'd call idiopathic — meaning we just don't know?

**Sergiu Pașca:** It depends on what you mean by identifiable. If you sequence the genome of a child with autism and their parents, you'll find a clear, high-confidence genetic cause in maybe 10 to 20 percent of cases. These are often what we call de novo mutations — new mutations that arise spontaneously in the child and are not present in either parent. In the remaining cases, you can often find variants that are associated with autism risk statistically, but the causal chain is less clear. In many cases — perhaps 30 to 40 percent — you do find relevant genetic variants, but whether those variants alone cause the autism, or whether they interact with other variants or environmental factors, is hard to say.

The case that really captured our laboratory's attention, and that launched much of our stem cell work, is Timothy syndrome. Timothy syndrome is caused by a single point mutation — one letter change — in the gene CACNA1C, which encodes a calcium channel called CaV1.2. CaV1.2 is a voltage-gated calcium channel that is expressed throughout the body and plays a critical role in cardiac muscle cells and in neurons throughout the brain. The mutation causes the channel to remain open too long after activation, allowing too much calcium to enter the cell. The syndrome is extraordinarily severe. Children with Timothy syndrome have syndactyly — their fingers and toes are fused. They have congenital heart arrhythmias. They have profound intellectual disability. And they have autism and epilepsy. Very few patients survive past childhood because the cardiac involvement is so severe. But Timothy syndrome is what we call monogenic — caused by a single gene mutation — which makes it tractable. You know exactly what went wrong at the molecular level.

## [00:21:37] Rise in Autism Diagnoses

**Andrew Huberman:** Autism diagnoses have increased dramatically over the past few decades. People debate whether this is a real increase or an artifact of better diagnosis, changing criteria, or other factors. What do you think is going on?

**Sergiu Pașca:** It's almost certainly both. The definition of autism has changed substantially. The DSM criteria have been revised multiple times. Historically, you needed fairly severe impairments across multiple domains to receive the diagnosis. Over time, the category expanded — we merged Asperger syndrome and PDD-NOS into the broader autism spectrum — and awareness grew dramatically among clinicians, parents, schools, and the public. Better screening, earlier referrals, and greater willingness to apply the diagnosis all drove the numbers up without any change in underlying biology. But most researchers who look at this carefully believe that the true prevalence has also increased — not just the diagnostic rate. Something has changed over the past several decades that is producing more autism. We don't know precisely what. Potential contributors include changes in prenatal environment, advanced paternal age, immune activation during pregnancy, changes in the gut microbiome, exposure to certain chemicals or toxins, and changes in the complexity of the social and environmental demands children face. But I want to be clear: none of these environmental candidates has been shown conclusively to drive the increase. It remains an open and important question.

## [00:26:46] Cause, Correlation & Neurological Disease; Schizophrenia, Do Vaccines Cause Autism?

**Andrew Huberman:** Vaccines and autism — this is one of the most persistent and damaging scientific controversies of recent decades. What does the science actually say?

**Sergiu Pașca:** The science is unambiguous. Vaccines do not cause autism. This has been studied exhaustively — with millions of children in multiple countries, multiple study designs, multiple research groups. The original claim, published in 1998 in The Lancet by Andrew Wakefield, was not just wrong; it was fraudulent. The data were fabricated. Wakefield lost his medical license. The paper was retracted. Every large-scale study conducted since has found no relationship between vaccines and autism. The MMR vaccine does not cause autism. Thimerosal — the preservative that was once used in some vaccines and has now been removed from childhood vaccines in most countries — does not cause autism. The timing coincidence that led people astray is real: autism is typically diagnosed between 18 and 24 months, and the MMR vaccine is given at 12 to 15 months. So in some children, regression or the appearance of autism symptoms will seem to follow vaccination. But this is a correlation driven by developmental timing, not causation. The tragedy is that the vaccine-autism myth has led to reduced vaccination rates, outbreaks of preventable diseases, and enormous suffering in families who were misled into thinking vaccines harmed their children.

More broadly, the challenge of distinguishing cause from correlation in neurodevelopmental disease is substantial. The brain is developing for years, and the environment is constantly changing. When you see a signal in the data, you need very careful epidemiological methods to know whether the association is causal.

## [00:31:34] Global Increase in Autism; Gene Therapy, CRISPR, Follistatin

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about gene therapy. The idea of correcting the underlying genetic mutation is obviously attractive. Where does that stand for autism?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Gene therapy is advancing rapidly for many diseases, including some neurological ones. The basic strategy is to deliver a functional copy of a gene — or a tool to edit the genome — into cells. The most common delivery vehicle is an adeno-associated virus, or AAV. AAVs can be engineered to preferentially infect neurons. You inject them systemically or directly into the cerebrospinal fluid, and the viral particles infect neurons and deliver their genetic cargo. For monogenic diseases — diseases caused by a single gene — this approach holds real promise.

CRISPR is the other major tool. CRISPR-Cas9 is a molecular scissor system that can make precise cuts in the genome, allowing you to delete, disrupt, or replace specific sequences. Its precision and efficiency have made it transformative. There are now clinical trials using CRISPR for blood disorders, some cancers, and a handful of other conditions. Whether CRISPR will be usable in the brain at scale is still an open question — delivery to the central nervous system is harder, and the safety standards for editing neurons are very high because neurons are largely non-dividing and mistakes cannot be diluted out through cell division.

I should mention that the line between gene therapy and what is sometimes loosely called biohacking is important. There are cases of people obtaining gene therapy vectors — for example, AAVs expressing follistatin, a protein that promotes muscle growth — and self-administering them outside of any clinical or regulatory framework. This is extraordinarily dangerous. Gene therapies can have off-target effects, trigger immune responses, cause insertional mutagenesis, or interact in unpredictable ways with an individual's specific genetic background. The regulatory process exists for a reason. Dramatic claims made in social media settings about self-administered gene therapies should be viewed with great skepticism.

## [00:43:41] Stem Cells, Ethics, Yamanaka Factors, Human Stem Cell Models

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's turn to the technology that is at the center of your work — stem cells. Can you explain what stem cells are, and how the discovery of induced pluripotent stem cells changed what's possible?

**Sergiu Pașca:** A stem cell is a cell that has two defining properties: the ability to self-renew — to divide and produce more of itself — and the ability to differentiate — to produce specialized cell types. Pluripotent stem cells are a special class: they can give rise to any cell type in the body. Embryonic stem cells were the first pluripotent cells studied in humans — derived from early embryos donated from in vitro fertilization clinics. But their use was — and remains — ethically fraught, because obtaining them requires destroying an embryo.

In 2006, Shinya Yamanaka, working in Kyoto, made a discovery that transformed the field. He showed that by introducing just four transcription factors into an adult cell — a skin cell, for instance — you could reprogram that cell backward, stripping away its adult identity and converting it into a pluripotent stem cell. These are called induced pluripotent stem cells, or iPSCs. Yamanaka received the Nobel Prize in 2012 for this discovery. The four reprogramming factors are now commonly called the Yamanaka factors — Oct3/4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc. The ethical problem with embryonic stem cells essentially disappeared overnight, because now you could take a tiny skin biopsy from any patient, convert those cells to iPSCs, and then differentiate them into any cell type you wanted — including neurons. And crucially, those neurons carry the patient's own genome. If the patient has Timothy syndrome, the neurons you derive will carry the Timothy syndrome mutation. You can study the disease in human cells, with the exact genetic background of the patient.

## [00:52:03] Umbilical Stem Cells; Stem Cell Injections & Dangers, Autistic Kids

**Andrew Huberman:** What about umbilical cord stem cells? There are clinics around the world offering stem cell injections to children with autism. How should families evaluate these offerings?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Umbilical cord blood contains hematopoietic stem cells — stem cells that give rise to blood and immune cells. These are real, well-characterized cells with real clinical utility, but their utility is in blood disorders: leukemia, certain anemias, immune deficiency disorders. They are not neurons. They do not become neurons. Banking umbilical cord blood in the hope that it will be useful for treating brain disorders is not supported by the science.

The stem cell injection clinics offering treatments for autism, cerebral palsy, or other neurological conditions are, in virtually all cases, operating outside of any regulatory or scientific framework. These injections are dangerous. Complications include infections, immune reactions, tumor formation, and death. Families desperate for treatments for children with severe autism are an unfortunately predictable target for exploitation. My strong advice to any family is: if a clinic is offering a stem cell treatment that is not part of a regulated clinical trial, do not participate. The risk of serious harm is real, and the probability of benefit is essentially zero.

## [00:59:30] Organoids, Modeling Brain Development, Intrinsic Development Timer

**Andrew Huberman:** So how do you go from a skin cell, to an iPSC, to something resembling brain tissue? What are organoids?

**Sergiu Pașca:** When we differentiate iPSCs into neurons, we expose them to a sequence of signaling molecules that mimic the developmental signals the embryonic brain would normally receive. We can guide them to become cortical neurons — neurons of the cerebral cortex — or interneurons, or many other types. But if you let neurons self-organize in three dimensions rather than growing them flat on a dish, something remarkable happens. The cells form spherical structures — we call them organoids — that spontaneously develop features of brain regions. Neurons stratify into layers. Different cell types appear in the right spatial order. Synapses form. Electrical activity emerges. These are not brains — they lack vascular supply, immune cells, sensory inputs, and many other features. But they capture essential aspects of human neural development that are completely absent from flat cultures.

One of the most striking findings from our lab is that these organoids carry an intrinsic developmental program that is cell-autonomous. The cells know what time it is in development, and they keep time even in a dish. This is not a clock in the sense of circadian rhythms — it's a timer, a program that unfolds on a schedule that is intrinsic to the human genome. We showed this with an elegant experiment. We looked at NMDA receptors in our cortical organoids. In the developing brain, NMDA receptors switch their subunit composition at around the time of birth — specifically, a subunit called NR2B is replaced by NR2A. This switch corresponds to a maturation of synaptic plasticity. We found that this exact switch occurs in our organoids at approximately nine months of culture — matching the developmental stage of birth in vivo. We had not added any external signal to trigger this. The cells simply followed their internal program. This has profound implications: it means organoids can faithfully recapitulate human-specific developmental timings that you cannot study in a mouse, whose gestation and brain development happen on a completely different schedule.

## [01:12:22] Assembloids, Brain Cell Migration & Circuit Formation, Self-Organization

**Andrew Huberman:** Organoids model one brain region. But the brain is a system of interconnected regions. How do you model that?

**Sergiu Pașca:** That's exactly the limitation we confronted. If you want to study how neurons from one brain region migrate to another, or how circuits form between two regions, or how a mutation in one region disrupts connectivity with another — you need something more than a single organoid. That's what led us to develop what we call assembloids. An assembloid is formed by fusing two or more organoids together. The organoids are made separately, each representing a distinct brain region, and then placed in contact. If the cells are healthy and the conditions are right, the organoids fuse, and cells begin to migrate from one to the other, following the same chemical gradients and molecular signals they would follow in a developing brain. The cells self-organize.

Our first assembloid, published in 2015, fused a cortical organoid with a subpallial organoid — one representing the outer cortex, the other representing a deeper structure called the ganglionic eminence, which is the origin of inhibitory interneurons. In the developing brain, inhibitory interneurons are born in the ganglionic eminence and then embark on a long migration to reach the cortex. We saw this migration happen in the assembloid, exactly as it happens in vivo. Interneurons moved from the subpallial organoid into the cortical organoid over days, following chemokine gradients, integrating into the cortical architecture. We could watch it happen under a microscope. And we could compare what happened when we used cells from a healthy control versus cells from a patient with Timothy syndrome. The migration was profoundly disrupted in Timothy syndrome cells. That result told us something important about the circuit-level mechanism of the disease.

We named these structures assembloids, and I'm happy to say that the term was endorsed by our late colleague Ben Barres, who was one of the most rigorous and creative neuroscientists of his generation. He was the one who pushed us to be precise about nomenclature — to call them assembloids and not mini-brains, which would be both scientifically inaccurate and ethically misleading.

## [01:21:22] Four-Part Assembloid, Sensory Assembloid, Pain Conditions

**Andrew Huberman:** How far can the assembloid architecture be extended? What's the most complex version you've built?

**Sergiu Pașca:** We have now built a four-part assembloid that models the sensory pathway from the periphery all the way to the cortex. The components are: sensory neurons of the dorsal root ganglia — the neurons in your body that detect touch, pain, and temperature; spinal cord organoids; thalamic organoids; and cortical organoids. We place them in sequence, allow them to fuse, and observe what happens. Remarkably, axons from the sensory organoids grow into the spinal cord organoid, and then projections from the spinal cord grow into the thalamus, and thalamic axons grow into the cortex. The connectivity mirrors the anatomical connectivity of the real sensory pathway. When we stimulate the sensory neurons, we can record electrical activity propagating through the assembloid all the way to the cortical neurons. We've built a functional sensory circuit in a dish.

This is directly relevant to conditions like pain hypersensitivity disorders. There are patients with mutations in sodium channels — particularly SCN9A, which encodes the channel Nav1.7 — who experience either profound insensitivity to pain or, in other cases, severe chronic pain syndromes. By making assembloids from iPSCs of these patients, we can study how the mutant channel disrupts the entire sensory circuit rather than just a single cell type. And we can test potential therapeutic compounds against the whole circuit rather than against isolated cells.

We have also built what we call corticospinal assembloids, which include cortical organoids fused to spinal cord organoids fused to a muscle component. When we stimulate the cortical neurons electrically, we can watch the signal travel down the corticospinal tract and trigger contraction of the muscle. A human cortex-to-muscle circuit in a dish.

## [01:27:33] Future Medical Therapies, Cell Banking, Immortalize Tissues, Rejuvenate Cells

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the most plausible near-term clinical applications of this technology — not just for modeling disease but for treating it?

**Sergiu Pașca:** I think about several trajectories. The first is cell banking. If you bank iPSCs from a newborn — essentially preserving a library of that individual's cells — you have an indefinitely renewable source of any cell type from that person. If they develop a neurological condition later in life, you can derive neurons matching their exact genetic background and test drugs against those cells. This is personalized medicine at the cellular level. The second trajectory is tissue banking and modeling. Rather than banking undifferentiated cells, you could bank mature cell types and even organized tissue — frozen assembloids representing particular circuits — for future therapeutic use. The third, and most speculative, is direct cell transplantation. Our lab has transplanted human cortical organoids into the brains of newborn rats. The human neurons survive, grow, and integrate into the rat's cortical circuits. They grow six to eight times larger than they do in a dish. When we stimulate the rat's whiskers — which activate the somatosensory cortex through well-known pathways — the human neurons respond. They have become part of the animal's sensory circuit. This opens a path, in principle, toward replacing damaged or missing neurons in the human brain, though enormous challenges of scale, specificity, and immune tolerance remain.

The fourth trajectory involves what some call partial reprogramming — the idea that you can use low doses of Yamanaka factors to rejuvenate aged cells, reversing some epigenetic markers of aging without fully dedifferentiating them back to a pluripotent state. This is very early-stage but fascinating.

## [01:34:56] Assembloids & Ethics, Importance of Nomenclature, Science Collaboration & Self-Correction

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned the importance of not calling these structures mini-brains. Can you talk about the ethical dimensions of this work and why nomenclature matters so much?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Nomenclature is ethics in disguise. If we call an assembloid a mini-brain, we invite the public to imagine that we are growing tiny conscious entities in dishes. That is not what is happening. Our organoids and assembloids do not have eyes, a spine, sensory inputs from the world, or the integrated architecture that supports anything resembling experience. They have organized patterns of electrical activity, but there is no evidence — and no theoretical reason to expect — that they have subjective experience. The term mini-brain is therefore both scientifically inaccurate and ethically misleading. It generates anxiety and opposition that is not warranted by the actual nature of the work, and it potentially derails important research that could benefit millions of patients.

At the same time, we take the ethical questions seriously. We wrote a nomenclature paper in Nature that laid out what these structures are, what they are not, and how they should be described. We also organized what you might call an Asilomar-type conference on the ethics of organoid and assembloid research — modeled on the famous 1975 Asilomar conference on recombinant DNA, where scientists paused, discussed the risks and ethics, and developed guidelines before proceeding. We brought together scientists, ethicists, legal scholars, and patient advocates to develop a shared framework. The scientific community needs to self-regulate, especially at the frontier of what's possible. If scientists don't lead on ethics, the ethics will be imposed by others with less understanding of what the science actually involves.

Science's self-correcting nature is one of its greatest strengths, but it only functions properly when the community is honest about uncertainty, open about methods, and willing to retract and revise when evidence demands it.

## [01:45:38] Cell Transplantation & Ethics, Timing

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned transplanting human cortical organoids into rat brains. That raises obvious ethical questions about creating chimeric animals with human neurons. Where do you stand on that?

**Sergiu Pașca:** It's an important question, and the field has thought hard about it. The experiments we do use neonatal rats — very young animals — because the immature brain is much more plastic and receptive to new cells than an adult brain. The human neurons integrate into sensory circuits that are, in rodents, largely dedicated to processing whisker input. They become part of a somatosensory map, not a cognitive or social circuit. The fraction of human neurons in the rat brain is small. There is no evidence that these animals develop altered behavior, altered cognition, or anything that would suggest the transplanted human neurons are meaningfully changing who or what the animal is.

Timing is critical both in transplantation science and in thinking about treatment windows for neurodevelopmental disorders. The brain's development is highly stage-dependent. There are critical periods when particular circuits are being wired, when intervention can redirect trajectories. Outside those windows, the same intervention may have no effect or even adverse effects. This is one reason why newborn transplantation works better than adult transplantation: you are intervening at the moment when the circuitry is still being assembled. For conditions like Timothy syndrome, the question of whether treatment can be effective only during a narrow developmental window — and what that window is — is one of the most important scientific questions we need to answer.

## [01:55:05] Genetic Testing for Parents, Genetic Penetrance

**Andrew Huberman:** Should parents who have a child with autism, or who know they carry certain genetic variants, seek genetic testing? And how should they think about the results?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Genetic counseling and testing are increasingly available and increasingly informative, but interpretation requires care. For some conditions, the genetics are deterministic: if you have three copies of chromosome 21, you will have Down syndrome, and the presentation is fairly predictable. For other conditions, the same genetic variant produces very different outcomes in different individuals — what geneticists call variable penetrance and variable expressivity. Timothy syndrome is highly penetrant: essentially all individuals who carry the mutation are severely affected. But many autism-associated variants are not. The 22q11.2 deletion — a deletion of 60 genes on chromosome 22, also called DiGeorge syndrome or velocardiofacial syndrome — is the most common microdeletion in humans, occurring in about 1 in 3000 births. It carries a 30% risk of schizophrenia — thirty times higher than the general population — and a 30% risk of autism. But it also carries a 40% chance of minimal neuropsychiatric impact. The same deletion, in different genetic backgrounds, produces radically different outcomes. Why? Probably because each of us carries a unique set of background variants that modulate the severity of any given pathogenic mutation.

This is actually a profound and underappreciated biological question: how does the genetic background of an individual modulate the penetrance of a major mutation? Answering this will require exactly the kind of personalized, patient-specific human cell modeling that assembloids enable. You can take iPSCs from two patients with the same mutation but different outcomes and ask: what is different in the cellular behavior of their neurons?

## [02:02:36] Assembloids, Timothy Syndrome, Epilepsy, Schizophrenia, Dystonia

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's go through the diseases you're modeling with assembloids. Starting with Timothy syndrome — you mentioned a potential therapy that emerged from your work.

**Sergiu Pașca:** Timothy syndrome is the disease we've studied most completely — 2D neurons, 3D organoids, assembloids — over 15 years. At some point, after accumulating so much biological information about exactly how the mutant calcium channel behaves, a therapy became self-evident. We realized that what we needed to do was influence the way the channel is processed in the cell. The CACNA1C gene has two versions of a critical exon — exon 8 and exon 8A. The Timothy syndrome mutation sits in exon 8A. Normal development involves switching expression from exon 8A toward exon 8. If we could manipulate splicing to suppress exon 8A and promote exon 8, we would essentially reduce the amount of the mutant channel the cell produces. We designed a small piece of nucleic acid — an antisense oligonucleotide — that enters cells and redirects this splicing. And it worked. Every single defect we had described over 15 years — the migration deficit, the calcium signaling abnormalities, the altered neuronal activity — was rescued by adding this tiny nucleic acid. It is a gene therapy in principle, but unlike AAV-based approaches, it does not require a viral vector. We've been traveling the world to identify and characterize every Timothy syndrome patient we can find, building a cohort. The drug is now in production, and we are preparing for the first clinical trial. This will be the first psychiatric disease therapeutic developed exclusively using human stem cell models — no animal studies were required to identify the mechanism.

**Andrew Huberman:** What other diseases are you actively modeling?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Intractable epilepsy is a major focus. There are genetic forms of epilepsy where children suffer 60 or more seizures per day — mutations in ion channels or in adhesion molecules that affect how cells stick to each other. These are devastating conditions. We are building assembloid and transplantation models for these epilepsies to understand the circuit dynamics of seizure generation and to test new therapies. Schizophrenia is another major focus. We've been studying 22q11.2 deletion syndrome for over 12 years. It's the highest known genetic risk factor for schizophrenia, and we think it gives us a window into the molecular and cellular underpinnings of psychosis more broadly. We can study any condition that has a strong genetic component — that is the key requirement for our iPSC-based approach. Conditions that are triggered primarily by social environment or social stress — certain anxiety disorders, some forms of depression — are harder for us, because we cannot recreate social experience in a dish. But conditions with a strong genetic signature are now within reach.

Dystonia is also on our list. You mentioned it, and I can confirm we are working on it. Genetic forms of dystonia and dyskinesia — uncontrollable involuntary movements — are devastating for children and families. We know the basal ganglia are critical, and we have now built loop assembloids that include cortex, striatum, thalamus, and midbrain. These circuits show activity. We can induce mutations at specific nodes of the loop and ask where in the circuit the dysfunction originates. If you were designing a gene therapy, you would want to know exactly which cell type in which structure needs to be targeted. Our loop assembloids are designed to answer that question.

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to put in a strong vote for dystonia. I had the experience last year of someone contacting me about a child who had gone from a normal-appearing kid to being essentially unable to move at all — couldn't go to school, couldn't go to camp. It was heartbreaking. And I learned these dystonias are not trivially rare. There are a lot of families living through this, quietly.

**Sergiu Pașca:** You're absolutely right, and it's a condition that is underrepresented in public awareness, partly because of how it presents. Autism gets enormous attention — partly because of its prevalence, and partly because we as a species have a deep affinity for children and naturally want to protect them. Schizophrenia, by contrast, gets far less research funding relative to its burden, partly because the genetic heritability means parents may also be affected and struggle to advocate, and partly because severe schizophrenia can be frightening in a way that creates social distance. Bob Desimone — who was head of the National Institute of Mental Health — told me directly that the disparity in funding between autism and schizophrenia research was at least partly explained by these sociological factors. Dystonia sits in an even more silent corner of public awareness. The science doesn't wait for awareness, but awareness does affect funding, and funding affects timelines.

## [02:14:30] Scientific Career, Walking, Art, Medical School

**Andrew Huberman:** I've known you for years. From the very beginning it was clear you were going to do something important. Without inflating the numbers — what fraction of your waking hours do you spend thinking about this work?

**Sergiu Pașca:** Probably all of it, honestly. I've never experienced this as work. It's the most fascinating thing I can imagine doing. The human brain is extraordinary. The biology of these conditions is endlessly interesting. And as a physician — I trained as a medical doctor in Romania — the knowledge that there are children who cannot communicate, who will never live independently, who suffer from conditions for which there is no treatment — that's a very strong motivator. We've now trained over 350 labs around the world to implement organoid and assembloid technology, through courses we run at Stanford. The technology has been amplified far beyond what our single lab could do.

**Andrew Huberman:** You look younger than when I last saw you. You mentioned before we started that you walk a lot.

**Sergiu Pașca:** I try to do 12,000 to 15,000 steps a day, at least. I walk to work, I walk when I travel — especially in Europe. Walking is the other thing I do besides science. That and art.
