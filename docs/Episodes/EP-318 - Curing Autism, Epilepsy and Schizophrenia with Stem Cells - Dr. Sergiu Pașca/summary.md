# Episode 318: Curing Autism, Epilepsy & Schizophrenia with Stem Cells | Dr. Sergiu Pașca

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Sergiu Pașca, MD — professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at Stanford — for a comprehensive dive into the biology of autism spectrum disorder and the stem-cell technologies Pașca's lab has pioneered to study and treat brain diseases. The episode covers ASD epidemiology (prevalence, genetics, the female protective effect, the fever effect), the rise in autism diagnoses, and the definitive science debunking the vaccine-autism myth. Pașca explains gene therapy approaches — AAV vectors, CRISPR, and the dangers of unregulated self-administration — before turning to how Yamanaka factors allow any patient's skin cells to become neurons carrying their exact disease mutation. The core of the conversation is Pașca's platform: organoids (3D brain-region models with an intrinsic human developmental timer) and assembloids (fused multi-region circuits that model interneuron migration, corticospinal connectivity, sensory pathways, and basal ganglia loops). The episode closes with Pașca describing the world's first clinical trial for a psychiatric disease built entirely on human stem cell models — an antisense oligonucleotide therapy for Timothy syndrome that rescues every known disease phenotype after 15 years of research — alongside active programs in intractable epilepsy, schizophrenia (22q11.2 deletion), and dystonia.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Autism affects ~3% of children (1 in 31) with a strong 4:1 male-to-female ratio** — [00:02:08](transcript.md#000208-autism-spectrum-disorder-incidence-genetics)
   ASD is defined behaviorally, not biologically — no biomarker, no blood test, no brain scan. The male excess may reflect a genuine female protective effect (requiring a greater genetic burden to manifest the same phenotype) and diagnostic bias from better social masking in females. The spectrum spans from profound autism requiring full-time care to high-functioning individuals who are productive and simply different.

2. **Early signs include atypical eye contact from 6–12 months; the fever effect shows social circuits may be suppressed rather than absent** — [00:11:56](transcript.md#001156-eye-contact-in-babies-fever-proposed-causes-of-autism-genes)
   Retrospective home video analysis and prospective sibling-cohort studies identify reduced or atypical eye contact as a replicated early marker. The fever effect — temporary dramatic social improvement during a fever — suggests relevant brain circuits exist but are somehow dysregulated, and that metabolic changes from fever can transiently restore function.

3. **Autism has 64–91% heritability — 1970s twin studies disproved the "refrigerator mother" hypothesis definitively** — [00:11:56](transcript.md#001156-eye-contact-in-babies-fever-proposed-causes-of-autism-genes)
   Twin data overturned Bruno Bettelheim's harmful hypothesis that cold mothering caused autism. Genetics are heterogeneous: hundreds of genes implicated, with de novo mutations accounting for many high-confidence cases (~10–20% of sequenced children). In ~30–40% of remaining cases, relevant variants are found but causal chains are unclear.

4. **Timothy syndrome: a single point mutation in CACNA1C (CaV1.2 calcium channel) causes syndactyly, cardiac arrhythmia, intellectual disability, epilepsy, and autism** — [00:18:48](transcript.md#001848-genetic-or-idiopathic-autism-diagnoses-timothy-syndrome)
   One letter change in CACNA1C keeps the voltage-gated CaV1.2 channel open too long, flooding cells with calcium. Fully penetrant, almost always fatal in childhood from cardiac involvement. Its monogenic tractability made it the anchor for Pașca's 15-year iPSC modeling effort and ultimately the therapy now entering clinical trial.

5. **The rise in autism diagnoses is partly real and partly diagnostic expansion — no single environmental cause has been confirmed** — [00:21:37](transcript.md#002137-rise-in-autism-diagnoses)
   DSM criteria have broadened repeatedly (merging Asperger's and PDD-NOS into ASD), and awareness has grown among clinicians and parents. But most researchers believe true prevalence has also increased. Candidates — advanced paternal age, prenatal immune activation, gut microbiome changes, chemical exposures — remain unconfirmed. The question is open and important.

6. **Vaccines do not cause autism — the 1998 Wakefield Lancet paper was fraudulent, retracted, and Wakefield lost his medical license** — [00:26:46](transcript.md#002646-cause-correlation--neurological-disease-schizophrenia-do-vaccines-cause-autism)
   Millions of children in multiple countries across multiple study designs confirm no link between MMR vaccine or thimerosal and autism. The apparent timing correlation (MMR at 12–15 months; autism typically diagnosed at 18–24 months) is developmental coincidence, not causation. The myth has produced vaccine hesitancy, preventable disease outbreaks, and enormous family suffering.

7. **Gene therapy (AAV vectors, CRISPR) holds promise for monogenic neurological disease — but self-administered gene therapies (e.g., follistatin AAVs) are extremely dangerous** — [00:31:34](transcript.md#003134-global-increase-in-autism-gene-therapy-crispr-follistatin)
   AAVs can deliver functional gene copies or CRISPR machinery to neurons via systemic or CSF injection. CRISPR-Cas9 is entering clinical trials for blood disorders. However, self-administration of gene therapy vectors outside any regulatory framework risks off-target effects, immune reactions, insertional mutagenesis, and death — social media claims about such experiments deserve extreme skepticism.

8. **Yamanaka factors (Oct3/4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc) reprogram adult skin cells into iPSCs, making patient-specific neuron modeling possible** — [00:43:41](transcript.md#004341-stem-cells-ethics-yamanaka-factors-human-stem-cell-models)
   Shinya Yamanaka's 2006 discovery (Nobel 2012) allows any patient's somatic cells to be de-differentiated back to pluripotency and re-differentiated into neurons carrying the patient's exact mutation. This eliminated the main ethical barrier of embryonic stem cell research and enabled disease modeling in human cells with authentic genetic backgrounds.

9. **Umbilical cord stem cell clinics offering autism treatments are dangerous and scientifically baseless — avoid all unregulated stem cell injections** — [00:52:03](transcript.md#005203-umbilical-stem-cells-stem-cell-injections--dangers-autistic-kids)
   Umbilical cord blood contains hematopoietic stem cells useful for blood disorders, not brain conditions. Clinics injecting these for autism, cerebral palsy, or other neurological conditions operate without scientific or regulatory basis. Known complications include infection, immune reactions, tumor formation, and death.

10. **Cortical organoids carry an intrinsic developmental timer: the NMDA receptor NR2B→NR2A subunit switch occurs at ~9 months of culture, matching birth in vivo** — [00:59:30](transcript.md#005930-organoids-modeling-brain-development-intrinsic-development-timer)
    Organoids maintain a cell-autonomous developmental schedule encoded in the human genome — no external trigger required. This recapitulates human-specific developmental timing that mouse models cannot replicate, because human neural development unfolds on a far slower schedule than rodent development.

11. **Assembloids fuse region-specific organoids to model interneuron migration, corticospinal connectivity, sensory circuits, and basal ganglia loops** — [01:12:22](transcript.md#011222-assembloids-brain-cell-migration--circuit-formation-self-organization)
    The first assembloid (2015) fused a cortical + subpallial organoid; inhibitory interneurons migrated from the subpallial region into cortex, replicating in vivo development — and this migration was severely disrupted in Timothy syndrome cells. Later assembloids: corticospinal (cortex + spinal cord + muscle; cortical stimulation → visible muscle contraction); four-part sensory (sensory neurons → spinal cord → thalamus → cortex, modeling Nav1.7 pain channelopathies); loop (cortex + striatum + thalamus + midbrain for dystonia research).

12. **Nomenclature is ethics in disguise — "mini-brain" is inaccurate and misleading; the field held an Asilomar-style ethics conference** — [01:34:56](transcript.md#013456-assembloids--ethics-importance-of-nomenclature-science-collaboration--self-correction)
    Organoids and assembloids lack eyes, spine, sensory inputs, and the integrated architecture that could support subjective experience. "Mini-brain" generates unwarranted public anxiety and is scientifically wrong. Pașca co-authored a Nature nomenclature paper and organized an Asilomar-type conference — scientists, ethicists, legal scholars, and patient advocates — to develop shared guidelines before proceeding.

13. **Transplanted human cortical organoids integrate into neonatal rat brain circuits: human neurons grow 6–8x larger and respond to whisker stimulation** — [01:27:33](transcript.md#012733-future-medical-therapies-cell-banking-immortalize-tissues-rejuvenate-cells)
    Human neurons transplanted into neonatal rat somatosensory cortex survive, extend axons into host circuits, and respond to physiological input via normal thalamocortical relays. They grow far larger than in vitro. Neonatal (not adult) transplantation works because the immature brain is maximally plastic. Proof-of-concept for eventual cell-replacement therapy; scale, specificity, and immune tolerance remain challenges.

14. **22q11.2 deletion syndrome: 60 missing genes, 30x elevated schizophrenia risk, 30% autism risk — yet 40% of carriers have minimal neuropsychiatric impact** — [01:55:05](transcript.md#015505-genetic-testing-for-parents-genetic-penetrance)
    The most common human microdeletion (1 in 3000 births) and the highest single known genetic risk factor for schizophrenia. Variable penetrance — the same large deletion producing radically different outcomes — is driven by individual genetic background. Patient-specific assembloids are exactly the right tool to ask why two people with the same deletion have such different outcomes.

15. **The Timothy syndrome antisense oligonucleotide rescues every documented disease phenotype and is entering the first stem-cell-only psychiatric clinical trial** — [02:02:36](transcript.md#020236-assembloids-timothy-syndrome-epilepsy-schizophrenia-dystonia)
    The mechanism: CACNA1C has two competing exons (8 and 8A); the Timothy mutation sits in exon 8A. The antisense oligonucleotide suppresses exon 8A inclusion and promotes exon 8 inclusion, reducing mutant channel expression — no viral vector needed. Migration deficits, calcium signaling abnormalities, and neuronal activity changes are all rescued. Drug in production; cohort of worldwide Timothy syndrome patients identified.

16. **Pașca trained 350+ labs worldwide in organoid/assembloid technology; walks 12,000–15,000 steps daily; eats one meal a day — a habit from Romanian medical school** — [02:14:30](transcript.md#021430-scientific-career-walking-art-medical-school)
    Pașca trained as a medical doctor in Romania before his Stanford postdoc. The one-meal habit formed during experiments run at dawn and midnight around clinical rotations. Lubert Stryer visited his office ~one month before dying and told him: "You've demystified psychiatric disease — from behavioral definition all the way down to a point mutation, and now on the verge of improving these conditions."

## Tools & Protocols

### Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell (iPSC) Reprogramming

- **What:** Converting an adult somatic cell (typically skin fibroblasts) into a pluripotent stem cell capable of differentiating into any cell type in the body.
- **How:** Four Yamanaka transcription factors (Oct3/4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc) are introduced into adult cells. Over 2–4 weeks the cells lose their adult identity and revert to pluripotency. iPSCs are then differentiated by sequential exposure to signaling molecules that mimic embryonic brain development, guiding them into cortical neurons, interneurons, sensory neurons, or other specified types.
- **When:** Whenever patient-specific disease modeling is needed — especially for monogenic neurological conditions where the exact mutation can be studied in the patient's own genetic background without confounding from animal-model species differences.
- **Ref:** [00:43:41](transcript.md#004341-stem-cells-ethics-yamanaka-factors-human-stem-cell-models)

### Cortical Organoid Culture

- **What:** Three-dimensional, self-organizing neural cultures derived from iPSCs that recapitulate cortical lamination, cell type diversity, synapse formation, spontaneous electrical activity, and human-specific developmental timing.
- **How:** iPSCs are guided through cortical differentiation via patterning signals. Cells are maintained in suspension culture under ultra-low-attachment conditions and can remain viable for 800+ days. An intrinsic developmental timer (e.g., the NR2B→NR2A NMDA receptor subunit switch at ~9 months of culture) unfolds without external instruction.
- **When:** To study human brain development at tissue scale, to model disease phenotypes (migration, synaptic, electrophysiological), to test therapeutic compounds, and to generate tissue for assembloid fusion or in vivo transplantation.
- **Ref:** [00:59:30](transcript.md#005930-organoids-modeling-brain-development-intrinsic-development-timer)

### Assembloid Fusion

- **What:** Combining two or more region-specific organoids to model inter-regional circuit formation, cell migration, axonal connectivity, and multi-region signal propagation in a dish.
- **How:** Region-specific organoids are generated separately (e.g., cortical, subpallial/ganglionic eminence, spinal cord, thalamic, sensory/DRG, striatal, midbrain) and placed in physical contact. Over days to weeks they fuse; cells self-organize by following endogenous chemokine gradients and molecular cues. Circuit function is assessed with calcium imaging, multi-electrode electrophysiology, and optogenetic stimulation.
- **When:** When disease mechanisms involve inter-regional dysfunction — interneuron migration failure (Timothy syndrome), corticospinal tract integrity, sensory pathway pain processing (Nav1.7 channelopathies), or basal ganglia loop disorders (dystonia, dyskinesia).
- **Ref:** [01:12:22](transcript.md#011222-assembloids-brain-cell-migration--circuit-formation-self-organization)

### Antisense Oligonucleotide Splicing Redirect (Timothy Syndrome Therapy)

- **What:** A short synthetic nucleic acid that enters neurons and redirects aberrant pre-mRNA splicing in the CACNA1C gene, reducing expression of the mutant CaV1.2 calcium channel isoform without requiring a viral vector.
- **How:** CACNA1C encodes two competing exon versions (exon 8 and exon 8A). The Timothy syndrome mutation sits in exon 8A. The antisense oligonucleotide suppresses exon 8A inclusion and promotes the normal exon 8, reducing the proportion of mutant channel the cell makes. Every phenotype documented across 15 years of stem cell modeling — interneuron migration deficit, calcium signaling abnormality, altered neuronal activity — is rescued.
- **When:** First-in-class therapy candidate for Timothy syndrome; drug now in production ahead of the first psychiatric disease clinical trial developed exclusively using human stem cell models.
- **Ref:** [02:02:36](transcript.md#020236-assembloids-timothy-syndrome-epilepsy-schizophrenia-dystonia)

### Human Organoid Transplantation into Neonatal Rat Brain

- **What:** Transplanting human cortical organoids into the somatosensory cortex of neonatal rats to enable in vivo maturation, circuit integration, and physiological responsiveness impossible to achieve in culture.
- **How:** Organoids are transplanted into neonatal (not adult) rats because the immature brain is maximally plastic. Human neurons survive, grow 6–8x larger than in vitro, extend axons, and integrate into thalamocortical sensory circuits. When the rat's whiskers are stimulated, human neurons respond via normal anatomical relays.
- **When:** Used as a maturation platform beyond what culture allows, and as proof-of-concept for eventual cell-replacement therapy strategies for conditions involving lost or damaged cortical neurons.
- **Ref:** [01:27:33](transcript.md#012733-future-medical-therapies-cell-banking-immortalize-tissues-rejuvenate-cells)
