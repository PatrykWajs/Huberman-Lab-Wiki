## [00:06:48] Memory: Past, Present & Future; Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** Speaking of memory, we go way back — you were a graduate student when I was first hired as an assistant professor, and now you're a full professor and world expert in memory. My first question is: as we move through our day, how much of our cognition and perception is focused on things happening in the present as opposed to being driven by prior memories? Have studies evaluated how often our brain switches to thoughts about the past?

**Charan Ranganath:** That's a great question to start with, and it's interesting because I actually don't think memory is about the past — I think memory is about the present and the future. It's about taking selectively what you need from the past to make sense of the present and to project to the future. If you look at eye movements, the first time I came into this room I was scanning things I'd never seen before, but now I know what to expect, so I can focus selectively on things that matter. Memory is prediction — your brain is constantly predicting based on stored patterns, and that's why sleep is so crucial: during sleep the brain consolidates memories and uses them to recalibrate its predictive models. When sleep is disrupted, new information can't be effectively encoded or properly integrated with existing knowledge. The hippocampus, which is central to memory, is particularly active during slow-wave and REM sleep to carry out this consolidation process.

## [00:13:23] Self, Memory & Age, Neuroplasticity

**Andrew Huberman:** Most of us have a stable representation of who we are — even people with very severe memory deficits don't wake up wondering who they are. That kind of knowledge might be innate and the representation of self in memory seems very stable. Is that true?

**Charan Ranganath:** It is a really interesting and complex question. If you look at patients with amnesia who can't form new memories, they have a sense of who they are — they know their names and biographies — but what happens is their sense of self doesn't update; it gets kind of stuck. There's this sense of looking and not expecting yourself to be as old as you are because you're stuck in who you were. I think about neuroplasticity as the brain's capacity to update based on experience, and while certain aspects of self are very stable, other aspects — your goals, your values, your understanding of your relationships — are constantly being updated through the memory system. As we age, the rate of this updating tends to slow, which is part of why age-related memory changes can affect identity and flexibility of thought. The good news is that the brain retains more plasticity into late adulthood than we previously thought, especially when curiosity and social engagement remain high.

## [00:18:50] Tool: Curiosity & Dopamine

**Charan Ranganath:** My postdoc Matias Gruber wanted to study curiosity and its effect on memory. I was totally closed-minded and said it was a dumb topic — everyone knows if you're curious about something you'll remember it better just because you're interested. But he said no, this is really interesting, and so we did this experiment that turned out to be beautiful. We gave people trivia questions — like a pub quiz — where sometimes they know the answer, sometimes they get an itch and really need to know it. We scanned people's brains using functional MRI when they got questions, and sometimes they said they were highly curious, sometimes not. What we found was that in the high-curiosity condition people remembered not just the answer but also an incidental face that they were shown during the waiting period — something completely unrelated to the question. The brain in a curious state essentially primes itself for broader encoding: dopamine released in the midbrain and ventral striatum during anticipation of a satisfying answer spreads to the hippocampus and boosts the memory-encoding signal for everything happening in that moment.

**Andrew Huberman:** So curiosity generates a dopamine state that then broadens the aperture of what gets encoded into memory — not just the thing you were curious about but the entire context around it.

**Charan Ranganath:** Exactly. And that's why I think curiosity is one of the most underrated tools for cognitive health. It's not just about remembering more; it's about engaging a neuromodulatory system that makes your brain more plastic and more capable of learning across the board.

## [00:26:55] Dopamine, Forward Movement

**Andrew Huberman:** Esther Perel, one of the world's experts on romantic relationships, said that one of the most sustaining factors for romantic relationships over long periods of time is a sense of curiosity — both about the other person and about where the relationship could go. This word of curiosity seems to be a resounding theme. Dopamine is so important for physical movement in the biological realm — is there a framework for thinking about cognitive forward movement? Taking memories and putting them into the present and future rather than just recycling past ideas?

**Charan Ranganath:** I don't think it's a coincidence that evolution organized dopamine to be involved in both physical movement and what we might call cognitive forward movement. In both cases, dopamine is released in anticipation of a rewarding outcome — it propels you toward something. In the cognitive domain, that means using memory not to replay the past but to simulate possible futures and motivate action toward them. There's fascinating work showing that when people are asked to imagine future events, they activate the same hippocampal-entorhinal circuitry as when they retrieve episodic memories — the brain essentially constructs the future from stored pieces of the past. Dopamine helps prioritize which memories and scenarios get boosted into that constructive process.

## [00:34:22] Dopamine, Learning; Curiosity & Appraisal

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a study at the University of Copenhagen using PET imaging — positron emission tomography — showing that people who do non-sleep deep rest, or NSDR, show significant increases in striatal dopamine compared to traditional meditation. This is why NSDR is useful for restoring mental and physical vigor. When dopamine is released, is it actually facilitating learning, or is it more about motivation and the anticipation of reward?

**Charan Ranganath:** Dopamine does multiple things for learning. It does signal the value of information — the appraisal of whether something is worth remembering. There's a classic view that dopamine signals prediction error: when something is better or worse than expected, dopamine rises or falls, and that signal helps the brain update its models of the world. For memory specifically, dopamine release during learning modulates long-term potentiation in the hippocampus, which is the synaptic mechanism underlying memory consolidation. The curiosity work adds another layer: the appraisal of something as interesting or novel is itself a trigger for dopamine release, independent of external reward, which means cultivating an internally-driven sense of curiosity is one of the most powerful levers we have for enhancing learning.

## [00:40:31] Memory, Hippocampus

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's discuss the circuitry involved in memory. Most people are probably familiar with the hippocampus. If we were to assign a one-sentence functional definition to the hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, and the neuromodulatory systems, what would you say?

**Charan Ranganath:** The hippocampus is controversial — it's arguably the most studied area of the brain — but I believe it's about linking various experiences to a context. Your brain has high-level vision, smell, semantic knowledge, and the hippocampus wiring is really set up not to understand what's going on but to bind disparate elements of an experience together into a unified event representation. The late David Marr, a pioneer in computational neuroscience, proposed that what the hippocampus is about is what he called simple memory — basically saying this set of things happened together at this time and place. The entorhinal cortex funnels information in from all the sensory and association areas, and the hippocampus performs rapid one-shot binding of that information — which is why it's so critical for episodic memory. Without the hippocampus, you can still learn skills and habits through repetition, but you can't form new memories of specific events.

## [00:43:34] Prefrontal Cortex & Memory, Aging

**Charan Ranganath:** The prefrontal cortex is massive — about one-third of the primate brain — and there are a lot of sub-regions that do different things, but globally it serves cognitive control: the ability to regulate movements, perceptions, and thoughts based on higher-order goals. When I tested patients with prefrontal lesions, the hallmark is not a problem holding information in working memory if nothing is distracting them, but as soon as you introduce distraction, their working memory completely falls apart. The prefrontal cortex is what allows you to maintain your goal in mind and resist interference. This is directly relevant to aging: the prefrontal cortex is one of the first areas to show age-related decline, and that's why older adults are more susceptible to distraction and have more difficulty with prospective memory — remembering to do things in the future — compared to younger adults. It's not that the hippocampus has stopped working; it's that the prefrontal guidance of what to encode and what to retrieve becomes less precise.

## [00:50:07] Aging, Prefrontal Cortex & Memory; Depression, Rumination

**Andrew Huberman:** There seem to be parallel processing streams for memory — one form involves the suppression of information, and there's a question of whether curiosity and the ability to integrate new information is diminished in older people. And then there's the issue of rumination and depression.

**Charan Ranganath:** The benefit of curiosity on memory is intact in older people, though I'm less sure whether curiosity itself remains as high. What I can say is that media multitasking — scrolling is the classic example — is clearly bad for memory. The short answer from the research is unambiguous. When people constantly switch between tasks or attention streams, they fail to form deep, contextually rich memories of any single event. For depression and rumination, what's happening is that recalling a negative memory puts you in a negative mood, and that negative mood acts as a context cue that makes it easier to recall more negative memories — you essentially get pulled into a loop. Every time you recall a negative memory in a dysphoric state, it can become more elaborate and more emotionally charged. This is one reason why targeting memory processes — specifically the patterns of recollection — is so important in treating depression.

## [01:00:40] Tool: Lifestyle Factors, Minimizing Age-Related Cognitive Decline

**Andrew Huberman:** At a personal level, what are the sorts of things you do to try and offset cognitive decline? If you had to pick three to five things clearly substantiated by data as supporting the maintenance or enhancement of memory as we get older, what are those?

**Charan Ranganath:** As a memory researcher I almost feel ashamed talking about these things because so many of the most important factors are related to basic health. Sleep is a massive one — there's a beautiful study done with 29,000 subjects in China followed for 10 years. They divided people by sleep quality and quantity, and those with poor sleep showed dramatically accelerated cognitive decline. Beyond sleep: cardiovascular exercise consistently shows some of the strongest effects on brain health in human studies. Social engagement is underrated — isolation is one of the biggest risk factors for cognitive decline. Managing chronic stress matters enormously because cortisol at high chronic levels is neurotoxic to the hippocampus. And curiosity — staying genuinely curious and engaged with novel information — is something I try to practice deliberately every day. Diet and metabolic health also matter; blood glucose dysregulation is strongly associated with accelerated brain aging and Alzheimer's risk.

## [01:09:39] Exercise, Brain Function; ADHD

**Andrew Huberman:** The really impressive effects of exercise on learning capacity and brain health seem to come from cardiovascular exercise — getting blood flow up to the brain and releasing modulators into the brain facilitates learning. Do you make an effort to exercise specifically for brain function?

**Charan Ranganath:** Yes, actually. When I finished my book I limped to the finish line — it probably took some biological years off me — and so I had to rebuild. I discovered I love hiking, and now I exercise regularly, and it has made a real difference in how I feel cognitively. I have ADHD, and I'll say this openly: exercise is one of the most effective interventions I've found for managing attention and focus. The neurobiological reason makes sense — cardiovascular exercise increases BDNF, which supports hippocampal neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity; it also normalizes dopamine and norepinephrine tone in prefrontal circuits, which is essentially what stimulant medications do pharmacologically. For people with ADHD especially, exercise before cognitively demanding work can provide a window of substantially improved focus.

## [01:17:26] Sense of Purpose, Tool: Values, Goals, Navigating ADHD

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned a sense of purpose. Are you linking exercise to purpose — like, I exercise because I want to be able to do my brain work — or are they separate entities?

**Charan Ranganath:** To me the sense of purpose is this existential thing — when I look at my dog moping in the corner, or when I think about my students and what they need from me, I feel like it's my responsibility to be functional. I worked with an ADHD coach, and the first thing she had me do was write down all my values and rank them — I thought it was a waste of time initially, but then I started writing them down and later when I was talking to her about my troubles with motivation, she pointed out that the things I was avoiding were the things I'd ranked as most important. The act of articulating your values makes it possible for the prefrontal cortex to use them as a context for directing attention. When I connect my daily actions — exercise, writing, mentoring — back to those core values, motivation becomes less effortful because the prefrontal-hippocampal system has a meaningful goal to organize around.

## [01:23:31] Forgetting, Intention vs. Attention

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about forgetting and the distinction between intention and attention, because I think many people conflate the two.

**Charan Ranganath:** This is one of the most important distinctions in applied memory science. Attention is what your brain selectively processes from the environment — and a huge amount of attention is automatic, driven by salient stimuli like a phone notification or a loud sound. Intention is directing your attention based on an internal goal — understanding why you are directing perception to particular things. The prefrontal cortex is the engine of intentional attention; it holds your goals in mind and uses them to bias sensory processing toward what matters. Most forgetting that people complain about — where did I put my keys, what did that person just say — is not a failure of memory consolidation; it's a failure to form a memory in the first place because attention was not properly directed. Forgetting is the default; encoding requires intentional effort. Ebbinghaus famously showed that within 20 minutes of learning, about half of newly encoded material is gone, which is why retrieval practice and spaced repetition are so powerful — they force intentional re-engagement with material.

## [01:30:10] Tool: Smartphones, Task-Switching, Forgetfulness

**Charan Ranganath:** One thing that drives forgetting in modern life is the smartphone, because it functions as a walking distraction machine — it has alerts, social media, email, all competing for your attentional resources constantly. Media multitasking — switching between tasks or consuming multiple streams of information simultaneously — is consistently associated with worse memory formation and poorer cognitive performance. The mechanism is clear: every time you switch attention, you fracture the encoding context, and the hippocampus needs a stable, coherent context window to form a proper episodic memory. I've personally split my phone usage so that social media is on a separate device with a timer. That single change had an enormous impact on my ability to drop into deep work, engage fully in conversations, and actually form memories of my experiences.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the recommendation is structural separation — not just willpower — because the mere presence of the phone in your environment activates the habit loop even when you don't check it?

**Charan Ranganath:** Exactly. Even if you have alerts off, if you have the phone nearby and you have a habit of checking it, you're spending cognitive resources suppressing that urge, which is itself a drain on prefrontal executive control. Engineering your environment to remove the cue is far more effective than relying on moment-to-moment willpower.

## [01:36:36] Tool: Pictures, Memories, Intention

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned using a camera intentionally rather than in a spray-and-pray fashion to document experiences. Can you expand on that?

**Charan Ranganath:** When most people take photos, they use the camera as a way of offloading memory — just grab everything and figure it out later — which actually undermines encoding because you're not engaging with what you're seeing, you're just capturing it. What I try to do instead is use the camera as a focusing tool: I pause, I decide what is distinctive or meaningful about this moment, I frame it, and I take the picture mindfully. That deliberate selection process requires the prefrontal cortex to evaluate the scene against my goals and values, which creates a much richer encoding event. My friend John Lisman, who has since passed, used to do this at conferences — he'd take pictures of people randomly laughing or eating, not landmarks or tourist shots, and those pictures became powerful retrieval cues later. When you then see the picture, the key is to use it as a cue to actively recall and reconstruct the event, rather than just passively scrolling past it. That act of recollection itself strengthens and elaborates the memory.

## [01:45:46] Deep Focus, Dopamine

**Andrew Huberman:** You've split your phone so that social media is on a separate device with a timer, which had an enormous impact on productivity and the ability to drop into deep work. What does deep focus actually feel like from a neurobiological standpoint, and what's dopamine's role in it?

**Charan Ranganath:** Deep focus is the state where the prefrontal cortex and its downstream circuits are fully committed to a single goal-directed task, and crucially, the default mode network — which is responsible for mind-wandering and self-referential thought — is suppressed. Dopamine is important for maintaining that state: it sustains the persistent firing of prefrontal neurons that hold the goal in mind and resist distraction. When dopamine tone is too low — which happens in ADHD, chronic stress, or after excessive novelty-seeking — the prefrontal cortex has difficulty maintaining goal representations and is easily hijacked by salient distractors. Engineering your environment to reduce irrelevant novelty — like keeping social media off your main phone — effectively reduces the competition for prefrontal resources and allows dopamine to be spent on sustained, intentional work rather than on suppressing distracting urges.

## [01:49:36] Hearing, Vision, Oral Hygiene, Inflammation, Brain Health, Alzheimer's

**Charan Ranganath:** I mentioned earlier that we'd talk about hearing, vision, and oral hygiene — these sound like unrelated factors but they're all connected through the pathway of inflammation and reduced sensory input to the brain. Hearing loss is now recognized as one of the strongest modifiable risk factors for dementia — the Lancet Commission on dementia prevention identified it as the single largest modifiable risk factor. The reason is partly cognitive load: when you're straining to hear, your working memory resources are being depleted just to process basic speech, leaving less capacity for encoding and higher-level cognition. Hearing aids, when worn consistently by people with measurable hearing loss, have been shown to substantially reduce cognitive decline trajectories. Vision is similar: uncorrected vision problems reduce the richness of environmental input, limiting the novelty and contextual complexity that drives hippocampal encoding. Oral hygiene matters because periodontal bacteria — particularly Porphyromonas gingivalis — have been found in the brains of Alzheimer's patients, and the systemic inflammation driven by chronic periodontal disease appears to cross the blood-brain barrier and accelerate neurodegeneration. General cardiovascular health and keeping inflammation low are thus important not just for the heart but directly for brain aging and Alzheimer's risk.

## [01:59:51] Deja Vu

**Andrew Huberman:** One topic that comes up a lot when people think about memory is deja vu. Do we have any understanding of what deja vu is?

**Charan Ranganath:** It's not fully understood, but here's my best science-based account. Early findings came from Hughlings Jackson, a great 19th-century neurologist who observed that many patients with epilepsy had an aura — an intense feeling of deja vu — right before a temporal lobe seizure. Wilder Penfield, doing brain surgery for epilepsy, would electrically stimulate areas in the temporal lobe and patients would report vivid feelings of familiarity or deja vu. This points to the perirhinal cortex and surrounding medial temporal lobe areas as the substrate — these regions generate familiarity signals, the feeling that something has been encountered before, separately from the hippocampus which generates recollection, the ability to recall specific details. In deja vu, the familiarity signal fires in the absence of a matching recollection — your perirhinal cortex is saying "I've seen this before" but your hippocampus can't find the specific episode to back it up. This mismatch between familiarity and recollection creates the uncanny feeling. It may happen when there is partial pattern-match overlap between the current scene and a stored representation, triggering familiarity without full recollection.

## [02:09:00] Serotonin, Reframing Memories, Trauma

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to talk about the relationship between memory and mental health. In experiments by Karl Deisseroth, a patient with depression who had a vagus nerve stimulator showed complete transformation of narrative in real time as stimulation increased — from suicidal ideation to genuine anticipation of the future. How we cast prior events in memory is so interesting. Can you talk about serotonin and the reframing of memories?

**Charan Ranganath:** There's a paper from Lamberto Maffei's lab using a learned helplessness model — a rodent model of depression — where they showed that by activating serotonin circuits you could essentially rewrite how an animal responds to a previously traumatic context. In humans, serotonin influences the valence and the accessibility of memories — boosting serotonin tends to make positive memories relatively more accessible and negative memories relatively less dominant. This is part of why SSRIs can help with depression, not just by improving mood in the moment but by gradually shifting the balance of what memories get retrieved and how they are appraised. The memory reconsolidation window is also relevant here: every time you retrieve a memory it becomes temporarily labile and can be updated with new contextual information, which is the mechanism by which therapies like EMDR and certain trauma-focused CBT protocols work. The key insight is that memory is not a static recording but a dynamic reconstruction, and the emotional valence attached to a memory can be modified.

## [02:19:05] Psychedelics, Neuroplasticity, Perspective, Group Therapy

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned your neighbor David Olson at UC Davis is studying psychedelic effects on plasticity — the massive increases in neurotrophic factors like BDNF that promote plasticity. Can you put psychedelics into the framework of memory and neuroplasticity?

**Charan Ranganath:** Psychedelics promote a period of heightened neuroplasticity — increased BDNF, synaptic growth, dendritic branching. For people who've taken them, that's what they report: a period of integration afterward where your brain feels like it's changing. But I want to emphasize something critical: plasticity is an opportunity for learning new contingencies, not the learning itself. Taking psychedelics is an experience, but the learning of new contingencies occurs in the integration phase and within the guided session. This is why the clinical trials that showed efficacy used intensive therapist-guided protocols — the plasticity window is not magic, it just makes the brain more capable of updating its models, which means the quality of what you do during and after the session determines whether the plasticity gets used for therapeutic benefit or gets squandered or even makes things worse. Group therapy contexts may be particularly powerful during this window because social learning and perspective-taking activate strong hippocampal encoding signals.

## [02:27:53] Rumination, Trauma, Nostalgia, Narrative

**Andrew Huberman:** We have to be careful not to let narrative make trauma worse — there's a concept of story-fondling where people can go further and further into the description of how terrible something was, reinforced by others, and the memory changes to become worse than the real events were.

**Charan Ranganath:** Yes, retraumatization is so bad precisely because of this memory loop: you recall a negative memory, that gets you in a negative mood because you pull up the context, that makes it easier to recall more negative memories, and every time you recall them they're getting more power because they're associated with these negative states. Recollection is a double-edged blade. Nostalgia is interesting in this context — the original term was coined by a Swiss physician as a disease, a kind of post-traumatic experience that soldiers had where they would be overwhelmed by longing for home. Now we know nostalgia can be therapeutic when it's used to construct a coherent narrative of one's past that integrates positive and negative events into a sense of meaning. The key is whether the narrative is moving toward integration and meaning-making or circling in rumination. This is why skilled trauma therapists are essential — they guide the narrative toward integration rather than amplification.
