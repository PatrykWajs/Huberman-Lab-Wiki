# Episode 286: What Pets Actually Want & Need - Dr. Karolina Westlund

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Karolina Westlund, professor of ethology at Stockholm University and founder of Illis Animal Behaviour Consulting, joins Andrew Huberman to reframe popular assumptions about pets through rigorous ethology and evolutionary biology. The conversation covers how wolf domestication shaped breed-specific behavioral drives, how to read animal emotional states using the core affect space model, and evidence-based protocols for enrichment, petting, litter box management, and cat introductions. They examine the neuroscience of attachment bonds in dogs (secure vs. insecure), the contested science of early spaying and neutering, and the frequently neglected needs of cats as solitary hunters kept in social environments. The episode also addresses horse welfare, zoo conservation tensions, parrot cognition, sexual imprinting vs. attachment, inequity aversion across species, and how human cultural learning perpetuates false beliefs — including the dominance mythology that pervades popular dog training.

## Key Takeaways

- Dog breeds differ in which segment of the wolf predatory sequence they were selectively bred to express — sniffers (hounds), pointers, stalkers/herders (border collies), chasers (greyhounds), grabbers (retrievers), killers (terriers), or guardians — and enrichment should match that breed's position in the sequence. [00:06:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=396s)(transcript.md#000636-dog-breeds--interaction-predatory-sequence-smell-domestication)
- Horses are prey and herd animals that naturally forage up to 16 hours per day; modern husbandry — early maternal separation, single-housing, and rapid scheduled feeding — systematically violates all three of those biological needs and predictably produces problem behavior. [00:02:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=139s)(transcript.md#000219-students--animal-species-horses)
- The "core affect space" model maps emotional states on two axes — valence (pleasant/unpleasant) and arousal — providing a practical framework for moving animals into the calm-safe quadrant (low arousal, positive valence) through enrichment, reduced stressors, and tactile interaction. [00:20:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=1216s)(transcript.md#002016-core-affect-space-petting-tool-consent-test-polyvagal-theory)
- Most dogs dislike having the top of their head patted rapidly; slow stroking — especially in areas the animal cannot self-groom (neck, upper chest) — is preferred and activates polyvagal co-regulation. The consent test (touch briefly, pause, observe if the animal re-initiates) reveals individual preference and should be used before and during every petting session. [00:20:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=1216s)(transcript.md#002016-core-affect-space-petting-tool-consent-test-polyvagal-theory)
- "Dominance" as popularly used (dog walks ahead = leader; dog jumps on you = dominating you) does not map onto the ethological definition, which is solely about priority access to resources. Dogs do not place humans in their dominance hierarchy at all; what looks like dominance is more accurately explained through learning and context-specific expectations. [00:27:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=1673s)(transcript.md#002753-space-dominance-resources-leash-walking-dog-owner-training)
- Tail wag direction carries emotional information: a wag predominantly to the dog's left signals negative emotional states; a wag to the right signals positive states. Body language is generally more reliable than facial expressions for reading dog emotions because dogs use different facial muscles than humans for equivalent emotional states. [00:37:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=2233s)(transcript.md#003713-tail-wagging--interpreting-body-signals-facial-expressions)
- Play in dogs can be distinguished from aggression using the MARS framework: Meta signals (play bow), Activity shifts (non-linear behavior sequence), Role reversals (larger dog lets smaller dog win), and Self-handicapping (larger dog visibly holds back). All four markers together indicate genuine play. [00:43:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=2604s)(transcript.md#004324-play-bow-tool-mars--playing-dogs--empathy)
- Animals — including dogs and primates — demonstrate inequity aversion (sense of fairness), established by Frans de Waal's capuchin monkey experiments and replicated in dogs. The distinction between anthropomorphism (over-attributing human traits) and anthropodenial (denying animals traits they actually share with us) is central to accurate ethological analysis; the Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness (2012) marked the scientific community's formal shift away from denying animal emotional experience. [00:51:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=3106s)(transcript.md#005146-fairness-social-groups-anthropomorphism-vs-anthropodenial)
- Cats are solitary hunters that aggregate in loose social groups and retain the full hunting sequence (unlike most dog breeds). Their behavioral needs — territorial scent marking, hunting expression, isolated feeding preference — are frequently mismatched with how they are kept. Cats brought as prey items back to owners ("gifts") are not giving presents — they simply were not ready to consume the prey and brought it to a safer location. [00:57:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=3465s)(transcript.md#005745-cats-hunting-bring-gifts-interaction--socialization)
- Litter box problems in cats are diagnostic: inappropriate elimination near windows or doors indicates territorial stress; distributed elsewhere indicates pain or litter box aversion. Map elimination sites to distinguish the cause before intervening. [01:03:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=3836s)(transcript.md#010356-scent--territorial-marking-covering-waste-tool-litter-box-placement)
- Urine scent marking in dogs ("pee mail") is a genuine form of social communication conveying gender, reproductive state, and emotional state. Sniff-walks — where the dog sets the pace and direction of sniffing — reduce stress hormones and produce calmer behavior afterward; suppressing sniffing on walks removes a significant source of positive emotional experience. [01:08:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=4097s)(transcript.md#010817-pee-mail--communication-wolves-domestication)
- Parrots have the cognitive complexity of a young child in certain domains, live in flocks in the wild, and require enormous cognitive enrichment, social interaction (ideally a same-species companion), and physical flight space. A single parrot in a small cage constitutes a welfare failure; prospective owners should audit their capacity to meet these needs before acquiring one. [01:20:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=4841s)(transcript.md#012041-stalking-birds-parrots)
- Nose work is one of the most effective interventions for dog behavioral problems: it moves high-arousal anxious dogs toward calm positive valence and re-engages low-arousal or depressed dogs — the same rightward shift in core affect space — across all breeds. Scatter feeding and puzzle feeders are practical daily alternatives that activate the same foraging drive without formal training. [01:25:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=5122s)(transcript.md#012522-nose-work-wildlife-chasing-tool-dog-feeding--challenge)
- Dogs do not sexually imprint on humans; they form attachment bonds — a slower, multi-sense process. Sexual imprinting (learning mate preference from the rearing adult) occurs in certain waterfowl and can misfire in conservation settings, which is why rehabilitators use hand puppets when raising endangered chicks. The Westermarck effect — reduced sexual attraction between individuals raised together from early childhood — is the human parallel to inbreeding avoidance mechanisms found across many species. [01:34:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=5667s)(transcript.md#013427-recognizing-self-vs-other-inbreeding-avoidance-imprinting)
- Dogs form secure or insecure attachment bonds to humans depending on caregiver responsiveness. Early weaning before 8 weeks and unpredictable caregiver responsiveness predict insecure attachment, which manifests as separation anxiety, higher baseline arousal, and reduced trainability. A securely attached dog is more resilient, calms faster after stressors, and is easier to train. [01:40:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=6051s)(transcript.md#014051-imprinting-vs-attachment-bonds-dogs-weaning--secure-attachment)
- The evidence around routine early neutering (especially at 6 months) is increasingly questioned. Early neutering in large-breed dogs is linked to orthopedic issues and certain cancers because sex hormones govern growth plate closure. Reversible chemical castration (deslorelin implant) and ovary-sparing spay are valid alternatives that preserve hormonal function; Scandinavian veterinary practice rarely neuters by default and manages reproduction behaviorally. [01:48:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=6516s)(transcript.md#014836-spaying--neutering-hormones-tool-neutering-alternatives)
- Humans, as animals, have an extraordinary capacity for cultural learning that allows false beliefs about animal behavior (e.g., dominance-based training mythology) to persist across generations even when contradicted by evidence. Many human health problems mirror the behavioral suppression suffered by working dogs kept in apartments — recognizing our own biological drives is as important as recognizing our pets'. [01:57:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=7027s)(transcript.md#015707-humans-as-animals-tools-cultural-learning)

## Tools & Protocols

### Consent Test for Petting
- **What:** Structured check to verify an animal wants tactile interaction rather than having it imposed.
- **How:** Offer slow, gentle contact to a preferred area (neck, upper chest — not top of the head for most dogs) for 3–5 seconds, then remove your hand. If the animal moves toward you or nudges for more, they are consenting. If they move away or become stiff, stop.
- **When:** Before and during any petting interaction, especially with unfamiliar animals or in new contexts.
- **Ref:** [00:20:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=1216s)(transcript.md#002016-core-affect-space-petting-tool-consent-test-polyvagal-theory)

### MARS Framework for Reading Dog Play
- **What:** Four-criteria checklist to distinguish play from aggression.
- **How:** Look for (M) Meta signals such as the play bow, (A) Activity shifts where behaviors change non-linearly, (R) Role reversals where the larger/stronger animal lets the smaller one win, and (S) Self-handicapping where the larger animal visibly holds back strength or speed.
- **When:** Any time two dogs interact — or a dog and human play — and the boundary between play and aggression is unclear.
- **Ref:** [00:43:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=2604s)(transcript.md#004324-play-bow-tool-mars--playing-dogs--empathy)

### Dog Feeding & Challenge (Enrichment Feeding)
- **What:** Replacing bowl feeding with foraging-based feeding to activate the predatory sequence and reduce boredom and anxiety.
- **How:** Use puzzle feeders, Kong-type toys stuffed with food, scatter feeding in grass, or snuffle mats. Match the foraging method to the breed's place in the predatory sequence — nose-heavy breeds (hounds) benefit most from scatter/sniff work; chase breeds benefit from lure toys before meals. Allow sniff-walks where the dog leads the pace and direction of sniffing.
- **When:** Daily. Replace at least one meal with an enrichment feeding method. Nose work sessions (formal scent detection training) can be practiced several times per week.
- **Ref:** [01:25:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=5122s)(transcript.md#012522-nose-work-wildlife-chasing-tool-dog-feeding--challenge)

### Litter Box Placement
- **What:** Diagnostic and preventive approach to litter box problems in cats.
- **How:** Provide one litter box per cat plus one extra. Place boxes in quiet, low-traffic areas where the cat cannot be trapped — not near food or water. Map inappropriate elimination sites: pee near windows/doors = territorial stress; pee elsewhere = investigate pain or litter box aversion. Avoid covered boxes for anxious cats (they limit visibility of threats). Scoop daily; full clean weekly.
- **When:** At baseline setup and whenever a cat begins eliminating outside the box.
- **Ref:** [01:03:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=3836s)(transcript.md#010356-scent--territorial-marking-covering-waste-tool-litter-box-placement)

### Introducing Cats
- **What:** Gradual multi-week protocol for introducing a new cat into a home with existing cats.
- **How:** Confine the new cat to a single room for several days. Allow scent exchange under the door. After 3–5 days, swap bedding between cats. Then allow visual contact through a cracked door, glass door, or baby gate — no physical contact yet. Proceed to supervised physical contact only after stress signals are absent across multiple visual sessions. Full process may take 2–4 weeks. Forcing contact early risks permanently damaging their relationship.
- **When:** Any time a new cat is introduced into a home that already has cats.
- **Ref:** [01:31:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=5461s)(transcript.md#013101-understanding--choosing-dog-for-your-lifestyle-tool-introducing-cats)

### Neutering Alternatives
- **What:** Alternatives to routine surgical castration that preserve hormonal function while addressing specific concerns.
- **How:** For males: chemical castration (deslorelin/Suprelorin implant) is reversible and allows behavioral testing before committing to surgery. For females: ovary-sparing spay (hysterectomy without oophorectomy) eliminates pyometra and pregnancy risk while preserving ovarian hormones. For both sexes: delay surgery until after growth plate closure (12–18 months for medium dogs; 18–24 months for large/giant breeds) if surgery is ultimately chosen.
- **When:** Discuss with a vet before routine neutering, especially for large/giant breeds or dogs showing anxiety-related behavior that may be hormone-dependent.
- **Ref:** [01:48:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=meiU6TxysCg&t=6516s)(transcript.md#014836-spaying--neutering-hormones-tool-neutering-alternatives)
