## [00:06:36] Dog Breeds & Interaction, Predatory Sequence, Smell, Domestication

**Andrew Huberman:** Dogs I know are very smell-oriented. They experience the world largely through their noses. They can sense odor at a distance, they like to get their nose right into things and get deep sniffs. There's a huge range of dog breeds, and I think any discussion about dogs requires that we first kind of separate out some of the major differences — at least in terms of the purebred versions. When I see a mastiff versus a chihuahua versus a scent hound, I'm looking at what appear to be very different animals. Is it true that certain dogs rely on their sense of smell far more than others? And how should we interact with dogs differently depending on what breed they are?

**Karolina Westlund:** I think I can't really answer whether different dog breeds' sensory capabilities differ that much from each other. However, how to interact with different dog breeds — that's really an interesting question. During the process of domestication, in just the last couple of hundred years, we started selecting for different capabilities in different dogs that we needed for different tasks. If we look at a wolf hunting sequence, what they'll do is an orient response — they sniff, they look for prey, then they do some eyeing and stalking behavior, then chasing, then a grab bite, a killing bite, then they dissect and eat the prey. So we have this whole predatory sequence. What happened during domestication was that we selected for certain aspects of that sequence in different breeds. We have the sniffers — the hounds — then the pointers who are selected for that eyeing and pointing behavior. We have the border collies who do some eyeing, stalking, and a little bit of chase but ideally no grabbing. Then we have the pure chasers like greyhounds. Then we have the grabbers, the retrievers. Then we have the killers, the terriers. And then there are the livestock guardians that don't show much of the predatory sequence at all — they still like sniffing, but specific breeds will have one or a few behaviors from the hunting sequence, so we need to understand what purpose they were bred for.

**Andrew Huberman:** Anyone who has seen a Westy — those cute little West Highland Terriers — if one of those hears or senses a rodent in the wall, I've seen one stalk one for several days. The Westy will sense where the rodent is with an absolute fixation, and if there's any way to get into that wall and kill that rodent, it's coming out with that rodent in its mouth. The amount of dedication is just striking.

**Karolina Westlund:** Yes. We systematically and intentionally bred for that just a couple hundred years ago. The working dogs come with this evolutionary genetic backpack that will encourage them to really want to do that work. And then we also have the toy breeds, the lap dogs who are not that interested in any of that working dog behavior. We really need to understand what purpose each breed was bred for.

## [00:20:16] Core Affect Space, Petting, Tool: Consent Test; Polyvagal Theory

**Andrew Huberman:** What does a dog need in order to feel calm and safe?

**Karolina Westlund:** Perhaps we should go to the core affect space — one of the three emotional models that I find very useful in understanding and providing a good environment for animals so that they can thrive. The core affect space is one way of depicting or conceptualizing emotions, where we have valence on the x-axis — how pleasant or unpleasant something is — and arousal on the y-axis. What you're asking is how can we put animals in quadrant two of that core affect space — low arousal and pleasant — so where they're relaxed and feeling safe and sort of engaging socially and being at ease. Some things to consider: the absence of negative emotions helps. If we're in quadrant four — high arousal, unpleasant — we'll find things like fear and aggression. Helping reduce that will sort of automatically help animals move to the right in the matrix. And for animals in lower quadrant three — unpleasant, low arousal, bored or depressed — providing an environment that's stimulating helps them move to the right side of the core affect space. Quadrant one is the high arousal pleasant state — that would be seeking or foraging behavior, exploration, play, sex.

**Karolina Westlund:** To get into quadrant two — feeling safe and that warm fuzzy feeling — one of the things to do is tactile stimulation, which is petting, if the animal enjoys it. Something that might interfere is that we primates are huggers, and we tend to go in and wrap our arms around an animal we really like. To many animals, this is restraint and really scary. So the type of body contact we offer animals — we should consider whether they really enjoy it or tolerate it. One way of doing that is the consent test. You might offer your hand and scratch a little bit, ideally in a place that the animal really enjoys. Most dogs don't enjoy having a hand on top of their head, but rather perhaps on their neck or upper chest. You do that for a few seconds and then you remove your hand to see: does the animal want more? Will they reinitiate that contact, or will they move away? Interestingly, one study described differences between rates of petting and concluded that all dogs are averse to very rapid touch — people tend to pat quickly — but if one deliberately strokes the animal very slowly, the animal's eyelids just start to hood and you basically diffuse the tension very quickly.

**Karolina Westlund:** Also, that nice slow stroke, if you're in a calm emotional state yourself, taps into polyvagal theory and this concept of co-regulation. If you're really calm and relaxed, you're sending out these subtle cues that other individuals — including dogs and horses — are reading and picking up on. Just being calm and relaxed yourself can really help relax the dog.

## [00:27:53] Space, Dominance, Resources, Leash Walking; Dog-Owner Training

**Andrew Huberman:** For dogs, space is a big thing. I've heard that if a dog runs up to you and jumps up on your shin, it's their attempt to dominate you. Or that if the dog moves into your space very quickly, it sees itself as the leader. What are your thoughts on this whole dominance-submission thing on the basis of touch and space?

**Karolina Westlund:** That's Pandora's box right there. As an ethologist, how we define dominance is completely different from how most people define it. The ethological definition of dominance is about priority of access to resources — the dominant individual has priority access to a resource, and the others simply have to wait or look elsewhere. This reduces the risk of confrontation and aggression. It tends to become exacerbated in captivity compared to wild contexts because then animals can't disperse and find alternative resources. I would not label the situations you described as dominance interactions. If the dog backs away when you confront them, I'd rather label that as perhaps a fearful reaction, not submissive as in giving you priority access to a resource. Feral dogs in the wild form linear dominance hierarchies with regards to access to resources, and that can shift depending on what the resource is — it's fluid.

**Karolina Westlund:** Dogs form relationships with us, but from the ethological perspective, we humans have no role in a dominance hierarchy among dogs. They know we are different and they'll learn to expect that in this context that will happen and in that context something else will happen — so we can often reframe what looks like dominance in learning terms. Dog training is really a lot of owner training. There's a lot of learning occurring — you teach the dog that if you stay at my side there won't be any unpleasantness, but if you pull ahead, there'll be an unpleasant consequence to the pulling behavior. I very often think that what we label as dominance can very often be explained in other terms.

## [00:37:13] Tail Wagging & Interpreting Body Signals, Facial Expressions

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you think about animals? What's driving this inquiry into their emotional and cognitive life?

**Karolina Westlund:** First of all, I think that we humans are also an animal species and we tend to put ourselves on a pedestal, thinking we are one thing and animals are this other homogeneous group — which really isn't the case. Each animal species has its own adaptations. The work I do is to try to help animals live better lives with humans, and that very often starts with understanding how that animal species would live in the wild, whether they're a predator or prey animal, how they process the world, the type of information they take in.

**Andrew Huberman:** For instance, we might see a dog who's wagging its tail and think only happy dogs wag their tails, but tail wagging is seen in many different contexts.

**Karolina Westlund:** And we might think of tail wagging as a visual communication thing, but actually it could be that they're dispersing scent. The tail wag will sort of waft scent over to you so you can take in information about their current emotional state. Regarding interpreting tail wags: a dog wagging with a predominant left wag — left for the dog — tends to be associated with negative emotional states, and on the right with positive emotional states. The same goes for cats: they tend to look at the world from the left when in a negative emotional state and from the right when in a positive emotional state. The type of tail wag also matters — whether it's very low and fast or high and stiff communicates different emotional states.

**Karolina Westlund:** Regarding facial expressions: studies have shown that dogs move different facial muscles when making emotional expressions than humans do. When exposed to something like thunder sounds, they'll show one facial configuration; when their owner returns home after several hours, they'll show a different configuration. Some of those facial expressions use different muscles than the corresponding human emotional state, which would bias us toward misreading dog facial expressions. We're typically better at reading gross body language than we are at reading facial expressions.

## [00:43:24] Play Bow, Tool: MARS & Playing; Dogs & Empathy

**Andrew Huberman:** Dogs will do this down-dog type movement — the play bow — with other dogs and with humans. Does it mean the same thing in those two contexts?

**Karolina Westlund:** Most probably does. The play bow is what's referred to as a meta signal for play — it's typically shown in a play context. As far as I know, dogs play a bit differently with humans than they do with other dogs, but they do enjoy playing with humans. Sometimes we humans have a hard time knowing whether what we're seeing is play or aggression, because there will be elements from the aggressive repertoire within a play context. What we can do is look for what's referred to as MARS: M is for meta signals — the play bows or other indicators that say "I want to play." A is for activity shift — you'll see different behaviors like chasing, pouncing, wrestling, biting each other, but these shifts happen not in the same order as they would if they were truly fighting. R is for role reversals — if dogs are of different sizes, they'll take turns winning and losing, because it's not fun playing if you lose all the time, so the bigger dog needs to lose sometimes in order to keep the interaction going. S is for self-handicap — the larger dog will self-handicap, so you might see a big dog just standing there holding a toy while the small dog pulls with all its might.

**Andrew Huberman:** It speaks to a bigger question — do dogs have empathy?

**Karolina Westlund:** I think so. Absolutely. Many dog owners are familiar with how when we're grieving, a dog will often come closer as opposed to moving further away. I think it makes sense from an evolutionary perspective that social animals who live in a cohesive social group are good at reading each other's emotional states and also good at trying to buffer negative emotions if possible. I would expect some type of empathy from any of the more cognitively advanced species.

## [00:51:46] Fairness, Social Groups; Anthropomorphism vs Anthropodenial

**Andrew Huberman:** I've always been curious about the fact that if two animals of the same species both receive food or a treat, they seem to be paying attention to how much treat the other is getting. There was this experiment done on capuchin monkeys by Frans de Waal and his team — two capuchins next to each other, they're asked to hand back a rock and get a reinforcer. The first monkey gets a piece of cucumber and is happy. The second monkey performs the same behavior and gets a grape — which capuchins love much more than cucumber — and the first monkey, watching this, essentially protests and refuses to work for cucumber anymore.

**Karolina Westlund:** Yes. That was a really groundbreaking study. Fairness — or inequity aversion — has been shown in many species now, and in dogs too. So the notion that animals have a sense of fairness is well established.

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the difference between anthropomorphism and anthropodenial?

**Karolina Westlund:** Anthropomorphism is when we attribute human characteristics to animals in a way that isn't really warranted. Anthropodenial is the opposite — it's when we deny animals certain characteristics that they do in fact share with us. Frans de Waal coined that term and it's become increasingly important as we recognize that many animals have rich emotional and social lives. The scientific consensus has shifted away from denying animals emotions; the Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness in 2012 was a major turning point. I tend to look at where the animal is on the phylogenetic tree — if it has a similar structure and similar neural circuitry to a human, I'd expect similar types of emotional experiences. We should be cautious both ways — neither over-attributing nor under-attributing.

## [00:57:45] Cats, Hunting, Bring Gifts?, Interaction & Socialization

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about cats. You own a cat. Tell us about cats from the perspective of an animal ethologist. What are you looking for to tell you something about whether or not a cat is friendly?

**Karolina Westlund:** The common house cat evolved as a solitary hunter that aggregates in loose social groups. As a methodologist, what I tend to do when I look at an animal species is look at three things: their social environment, how they get food, and how they express behavior in general. For cats, they should — if raised well — have had the opportunity to spend enough time with mom, typically up to 14 weeks, which seems to be long enough for the animal to learn how to be a cat and not get too emotionally disturbed by the separation. Cats, as opposed to dogs, typically retain the whole hunting sequence. Sometimes the killing bite isn't quite there, but certainly the grab bite — which is why some cats bring you gifts. They're bringing their prey back, which they do. The prey isn't quite dead, but they'll sometimes consume it later. Some people think the cat is bringing them a gift as such. I think it's just more likely that they simply weren't quite ready to consume the prey at that time, and they're bringing it back somewhere safer.

**Karolina Westlund:** Cats really vary individually in terms of their tolerance for interaction with strangers. If a cat is raised with good early socialization — including with humans — during their sensitive period around 2 to 7 weeks, they're much more likely to be friendly with strangers as adults. The tail up posture in cats — walking toward you with the tail straight up — is a signal that this cat is friendly and interested in interacting. A cat with ears back and hair raised is showing fear or threat.

## [01:03:56] Scent & Territorial Marking; Covering Waste, Tool: Litter Box Placement

**Andrew Huberman:** What is this behavior of bunting — where the cat bumps its head against you or your arm?

**Karolina Westlund:** That's scent marking. I would say when they're scent marking you it's like something you do in your group — you do mutual scent marking, which means that everybody in the group smells more or less the same. It's a way of greeting and incorporating others in the group, so there will be a lot of scent exchange within this type of species living in a group.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is scent marking about territory as well?

**Karolina Westlund:** Yes, but that's a different type of scent marking. Cats have multiple scent glands in the face, and one of them is used to scent mark the inner territory where they feel really safe. Then they usually have urine scent marking which is at the outskirts of the territory. If you have an indoor cat who starts peeing inappropriately, you can create a map of your home and mark where you find the pee — that will give you a lot of information. If it's a territorial issue, it will typically be at the edges — near windows or doors. If it's an elimination problem where the cat has associated the litter box with pain, they will start going outside of the box, but that type of behavior will appear in a different context.

**Karolina Westlund:** For litter box placement: if you have multiple cats, they should have multiple litter boxes because cats are solitary — they prefer privacy for elimination. The rule of thumb is one litter box per cat plus one extra. The placement matters too — not near their food, not in a high-traffic area, and not somewhere the cat feels trapped. Cover on litter boxes can actually cause problems for some cats because they can't see potential threats while eliminating, which is a vulnerable moment for them.

## [01:08:17] "Pee Mail" & Communication; Wolves, Domestication

**Andrew Huberman:** My bulldog would put tremendous determination and effort into peeing on things on walks. I felt like it was one of his great joys in life. He had an endless supply of urine for this apparently — he would go to things and sniff and then pee there.

**Karolina Westlund:** Yeah, he's also reading the pee mail from the other dogs in the neighborhood. The urine tells a lot of information to the other animals — what gender, what reproductive state, perhaps also something about the animal's emotional or mental state. That is how dogs communicate, and they spend a lot of time doing it. They're willing to work to get access to that opportunity, so absolutely I would think that gives them positive emotional experiences. There's some innate drive in dogs to read the emotional and hormonal states of other dogs that have been there before. It's their form of social media — I'm going to post here, and I'm reading what others have posted.

**Andrew Huberman:** The transition from wolves to dogs — domestication took many thousands of years. What's the takeaway there in terms of what dogs are? Are they essentially wolves who have been shaped by humans?

**Karolina Westlund:** They're not wolves. They've diverged significantly. Dogs as a species have adapted to living with humans — they're good at reading human body language and communication in a way that wolves don't spontaneously do. But they carry that evolutionary heritage from wolves, which is why understanding the wolf behavioral sequence is so useful for understanding what drives different dog breeds. The domestication process involved both genetic selection and developmental processes, so dogs today are genuinely a distinct species adapted to cohabitation with humans.

## [01:11:54] Zoos, Conservation; Tigers

**Andrew Huberman:** At a very basic level, are animals looking to optimize food intake and safety? And what does that tell us about zoos? I personally have a pretty strong visceral reaction to zoos that have large carnivores. I realize zoos have positive breeding programs and endangered species protection programs. What's your take?

**Karolina Westlund:** When we talk about zoos, it's good to talk about the evolution of zoos because back in the day — 150 years ago — it was more or less a menagerie. Zoos today tend to have the purpose of doing a lot of conservation work. There's in situ conservation where you work to preserve wild habitat and creating national parks and so on, and ex situ conservation which is housing animal species that are threatened with extinction in an environment that ideally promotes species-typical behavior. I'm conflicted. I think that many zoos are doing a lot of good in this effort and also educating the public — many people who go to zoos might have their interest in animals awakened, which I think is a good thing. But sometimes the housing isn't optimal and some species are a lot more difficult to keep in captivity.

**Karolina Westlund:** Polar bears are really difficult to keep because they're wide-ranging carnivores that cover enormous territories. Tigers are difficult too because they're solitary and highly territorial. The key question is: can you provide the animal with the opportunity to express the full range of behaviors that they need? Modern zoos have moved toward much more enriched environments, naturalistic habitats, behavioral enrichment programs. The best zoos today are genuine conservation centers, not just animal displays. But the fundamental tension between keeping wild animals in captivity and their welfare needs doesn't go away entirely.

## [01:20:41] Stalking; Birds, Parrots

**Andrew Huberman:** Could we talk a little bit about the prey and stalking and capture and killing sequence? When a cat is in its stalking mode, it gets one ballistic strike opportunity before the chase is on. We had a cat when I was a kid that would stalk and then right before it would leap at the prey it would start chattering its teeth. What's going on when an animal does that very deliberate stalking, that calculation, and the teeth chattering?

**Karolina Westlund:** My guess would be perhaps some sort of displacement behavior — there's motivation to move on in the sequence of behaviors to the next behavior, but it's not quite time yet, and so that sort of activation then gets an outlet through that behavior. It's almost like hydraulic pressure building up. I really don't know — I haven't seen this well-discussed in the literature.

**Andrew Huberman:** We've talked about dogs and cats. Let's talk about birds. I had parrots when I was a kid. Parrots are smart. What are parrots thinking about?

**Karolina Westlund:** I think what all animals are thinking about is where their next meal is going to come from, social interactions, and safety. Parrots are cognitively very advanced — they can have the cognitive complexity of a young child in certain domains. They need a lot of social interaction and cognitive stimulation. Parrots in the wild live in flocks and they're highly vocal, so in captivity, having at least one other parrot companion is very important. The problem with parrots as pets is that their needs are often not met — they need huge amounts of cognitive enrichment, social interaction, and physical space to fly. A single parrot in a small cage is typically a welfare nightmare. If people want to have parrots, they should think very carefully about whether they can meet those needs — that means a lot of time, a large flight cage or aviary, and ideally a companion of the same species.

## [01:25:22] Nose Work, Wildlife Chasing, Tool: Dog Feeding & Challenge

**Andrew Huberman:** How would one incorporate natural foraging or prey-seeking behavior into a more pleasant experience for the dog? They need to express these behaviors. If they can't, it's going to come out some other way — maybe destructive to them or the environment.

**Karolina Westlund:** For dog owners, what we can do is try to promote the different aspects of the predatory sequence that that particular dog enjoys doing. I mentioned nose work as being one of the things that many dogs really enjoy. This is just the early days of scientific studies on the effects of nose work, but they're really promising. Essentially, the animal has learned that it needs to find a specific scent in an area — it sniffs the area, roams, follows the scent, and stops and marks when it finds it, and then gets a reward. The effects are remarkable: it seems to help regulate arousal so that animals who are highly strung and have generalized anxiety calm down, and animals that are semi-depressed get more enthusiastic about life. Back in core affect space terms, we have a shift to the right-hand side — positive valence associated with nose work — and it seems really effective across many different breeds and individual dogs.

**Karolina Westlund:** Another approach is feeding challenges — instead of putting food in a bowl, put it in a puzzle feeder or scatter it in the grass so the dog has to forage for it. This is particularly important for working breeds. Also allowing the dog to really follow its nose on walks — sniff-walks rather than exercise walks. Dogs who are allowed to sniff at their own pace during walks show lower stress hormones and calmer behavior afterward. Wildlife chasing can also be redirected — if your dog has a strong chase drive, activities like lure coursing or flirt poles satisfy that drive safely.

## [01:31:01] Understanding & Choosing Dog for Your Lifestyle, Tool: Introducing Cats

**Andrew Huberman:** I think what I'm realizing is that understanding the sequence of natural behaviors — but also where in that sequence a particular breed really leans to — is really the key.

**Karolina Westlund:** Absolutely. Sometimes it can be hard to know where your dog is on that scale, especially if it's a mixed breed. But you can often just look at the conformation of the dog. Very lean dogs with not much muscle and a pointy snout — think greyhound — tend to be the chasers. The ones with more muscles and bigger jaws tend to be the ones that rip things apart — bulldogs, rottweilers, mastiffs. You need to pay a lot of attention to the type of life you're going to offer: whether it's living in an apartment in a busy city or on a farm, which breed of dog is going to adapt best to that lifestyle?

**Andrew Huberman:** Do you think dogs like cities?

**Karolina Westlund:** I think many dogs get very stressed in cities because of the constant bombardment of sensory information — noise, constantly meeting strangers. For many animal species, meeting strangers doesn't happen very often and causes an increase in arousal. For a dog in a busy city street, this can be essentially continuous stress. If you want to have a dog in a city, choosing a breed that's less reactive and more sociable is important, and making sure you can get them to quieter environments regularly.

**Karolina Westlund:** For introducing cats: when you bring a new cat into a home, you should do it extremely gradually. Keep the new cat in a single room initially and allow the cats to smell each other under the door before any visual contact. After several days, you can swap their bedding so they get used to each other's scent completely before meeting. When you finally do visual introductions, use a barrier like a glass door or a baby gate. The whole process can take weeks, and you shouldn't rush it. Forcing cats to meet before they're ready often leads to conflict that can permanently damage their relationship.

## [01:34:27] Recognizing Self vs Other, Inbreeding Avoidance, Imprinting

**Andrew Huberman:** One thing I've always been fascinated by is self versus other species recognition — dogs don't try to mate with cats. Different species of animals seem to know self versus other, and they don't have to learn it. It's innate.

**Karolina Westlund:** Yes, for most species. There are actually a few exceptions — certain waterfowl birds where the male learns through sexual imprinting when they're young to be attracted to females that resemble the female who reared them. In those species the males are typically very ornamented and the females are cryptic — camouflaged — so the males need to learn what mom looks like. When they grow up, they'll start courting females that look like mom. So if you raise such a male with the wrong species, they'll start courting the wrong female.

**Andrew Huberman:** This is why with conservation programs, they use hand puppets so the chicks being raised by humans aren't imprinted on humans.

**Karolina Westlund:** Exactly. And that actually illustrates a broader principle about inbreeding avoidance — some species have specific mechanisms to avoid mating with close kin. The Westermarck effect in humans is one example: children raised together from early childhood rarely develop sexual attraction to each other even if they're not biologically related. Similar mechanisms exist in many animal species. Understanding these natural mechanisms helps us understand both normal behavior and what can go wrong when animals are raised in abnormal conditions.

## [01:40:51] Imprinting vs Attachment Bonds; Dogs, Weaning & Secure Attachment

**Andrew Huberman:** Do dogs imprint on humans?

**Karolina Westlund:** Actually, I would say that dogs don't imprint on humans. They grow attachment bonds to humans. Imprinting is typically a very fast process — it occurs within minutes or hours. Attachment takes longer and involves more senses. Attachment has previously mostly been studied in humans — the bond that grows between caregiver and offspring. What's interesting is that attachment bonds will grow in different ways depending on how the caregiver responds to the young one's needs. You can have a secure attachment bond where the caregiver reliably responds to the needs of the young one, so that if they find themselves alone, they can self-regulate better — their nervous system can more easily calm down again after a stressor. It seems that dogs form, rather than imprinting, a type of attachment bond that can be secure or insecure.

**Andrew Huberman:** This will open up a whole set of ideas for people — this whole notion of secure versus insecure attachment, the Bowlby experiments, disorganized attachment responses — it's thrown around a lot in dating culture and relationship psychology. And now we're saying it's also in your pets.

**Karolina Westlund:** Yes. And the implications are really practical: how you respond to your puppy's distress when you leave them shapes whether they develop a secure or insecure attachment. A securely attached dog is more resilient and can cope better with being alone or with new situations. An insecurely attached dog is more likely to develop separation anxiety and be harder to train because they're chronically in a higher arousal state. Weaning timing also matters — weaning puppies too early can compromise their development. The right timing for separating puppies from their mothers and littermates is around 8 weeks, not earlier. This sensitive period of socialization is critical.

## [01:48:36] Spaying & Neutering, Hormones, Tool: Neutering Alternatives

**Andrew Huberman:** I have a probably controversial question. I opted to neuter my dog when he was about six months old — I did it reluctantly, partly because I'd spent time studying the effects of early androgens on brain development, and you don't have to spend long in the field of hormones and development to know that testosterone and estrogen have a powerful organizing effect on the brain. What's the science say about spaying and neutering?

**Karolina Westlund:** The evidence is pretty mixed. Some studies show that neutering, especially early neutering, can be associated with certain health risks — orthopedic issues, certain types of cancer, and behavioral changes. In females, the evidence around ovarian hormones and health is also complex. The traditional approach of neutering at six months is being questioned now. What seems clearer is that the timing matters a lot, and the risks and benefits vary by breed and size. For large and giant breed dogs, early neutering seems to have more significant negative effects on orthopedic development because sex hormones play a role in the closure of growth plates.

**Karolina Westlund:** Alternatives are available: for males, there are chemical castration options — reversible hormone injections that can be used to test how a male dog behaves without testosterone, before committing to permanent neutering. In Scandinavia, routine neutering of dogs is much less common than in the US — it's not the default, and dogs are expected to be managed behaviorally rather than surgically. For females, ovary-sparing spay is one option where you remove the uterus but keep the ovaries, preserving hormonal function while eliminating the risk of pyometra and unwanted pregnancies. Working with a vet who is up to date on the current evidence is really important here.

## [01:57:07] Humans as Animals, Tools, Cultural Learning

**Andrew Huberman:** We're old world primates. We're the best at technology development among all species, but we're not as good at natural camouflage, catching and killing things with our hands — we need tools. Is there anything in your training as an animal ethologist that causes you to reflect on human beings as particularly spectacular or particularly deficient?

**Karolina Westlund:** One thing that leaps to mind is the extent to which cultural learning occurs in humans. For other animal species, they learn from trial and error — classical conditioning and operant conditioning. They also have social learning — they watch someone else and see what they do. But in humans, cultural learning takes this to a completely different level. We don't just learn from watching; we learn from language, from writing, from passing knowledge across generations in a way no other species can match. This is both our greatest strength and something that can create problems — because we inherit cultural beliefs that may not be based on direct experience or accurate observation, and we don't always question them. The cultural belief that dogs should walk behind you, or that dominance hierarchies explain all dog behavior, or that certain animals feel no pain — these are culturally transmitted ideas that can persist even when the evidence says otherwise.

**Karolina Westlund:** Another thing that strikes me is how humans, compared to other animals, have an enormous capacity to suppress our natural behaviors. We sit in chairs for eight hours a day, we eat on schedules that have nothing to do with hunger, we sleep in ways that are divorced from natural light cycles. Many of the health problems we have — and the recommendations people like you on this podcast make — are essentially about trying to realign our behavior with our biological heritage. In some ways, we're like those working dog breeds in an apartment with no outlet for their predatory sequence. Recognizing our own biological drives and nature as animals is, I think, one of the most useful things we can do for our own well-being.
