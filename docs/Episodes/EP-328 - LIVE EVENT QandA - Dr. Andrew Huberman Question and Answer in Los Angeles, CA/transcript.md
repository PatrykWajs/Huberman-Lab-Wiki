## What Occurs in the Mind/Body When You Have ADHD? Are There Ways to Address It Without Medication? (00:02:23)

Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder used to be called ADD. The hyperactivity part is a little misleading. People with ADHD actually have a tremendous capacity to focus if they like what they are focusing on. Take a kid with ADHD who cannot focus and give them their favorite video game and they are a laser. The threshold to access the dopamine system is higher, and dopamine has this incredible ability to focus the brain and other aspects of the nervous system.

The focus training exercises that have been explored mainly in China do seem to be of benefit. They use them in schools now — literally visual focus exercises. Your mental focus follows your visual focus. There are non medication-based treatments worth knowing about. Supplement-based options that will increase dopamine include L-tyrosine, which is the precursor to dopamine. But if you can rely on behavioral tools first — with sleep and nutrition as prerequisites — do that. And then and only then rely on supplement-based tools or prescription medication. Vyvanse, Adderall, and Ritalin clearly work for ADHD, but some people choose more subtle pharmacologic manipulation like L-tyrosine.

This focusing exercise consists of spending one to three minutes trying to maintain visual focus on a single point. That can increase your ability to focus cognitively. Keep in mind that focusing always involves a refocusing. Research from Wendy Suzuki's lab at NYU found that a roughly 13-minute per day meditation where you just focus on your breathing has been shown to improve focus significantly.

Space-time bridging is a related perceptual exercise. When you close your eyes and focus just behind your forehead, you are focusing on the one location in the body for which there is no sensation — there are no sensory neurons in the brain. There is only thought. That is why in neurosurgeries physicians can poke around with no anesthetic and the patient does not feel it. Space-time bridging starts by closing your eyes and focusing on that location. Then opening your eyes and focusing on a location about the distance of your hand. While attending to your breathing, you focus further out, then further out, and then further out — eventually imagining yourself in the vast landscape of the universe.

When you focus in close, your slicing of time is finer. You notice the subtle fluctuations in your breathing and nearby sensations. When you focus further out, your perception of time changes — which is why panoramic vision produces calm. What you are really doing is changing your time perception by changing your space perception. Space-time bridging is very useful because most people get locked at one station under conditions of stress. It is not a meditation so much as a perceptual exercise.

## As a Teenager, What Are 5 Things You Would Recommend to Physically Feel My Best? (00:11:55)

If you are a 15-year-old surfer who attends high school and plays soccer, the first thing is sleep — lots and lots of it. Tell your parents you need it.

Rather than prescribing a specific meditation protocol, I would encourage a teenager to develop a really keen awareness of what stresses you out, what relaxes you, and what delights you. Those signals are your antennae. At 15 the brain is in an amazing period of heightened neuroplasticity — learn your own nervous system's patterns first, before reaching for any specific tools. Try to detect and learn about what raises your adrenaline, your dopamine, your serotonin. That awareness will be invaluable.

## Should We Wait to Feel the Rise of Adrenaline and the Fall of It Before Bailing From Cold Water? (00:14:42)

Yes — provided it does not kill you. The goal is to reach the point of "I really want to get out of here, but I know I can stay in safely." There is just so much learning in those short moments about where your mind goes. The degree of discomfort — not just physical but mental — is directly predictive of the pain-to-pleasure wave you will experience afterwards.

The surge of dopamine from cold exposure, based on a paper published in the European Journal of Physiology, lasts many hours, and it is a 100 to 200 percent increase in dopamine. It is not a subtle effect — and it is a nice slow rise, a long sustained arc, which is a true antidepressant. Dr. Anna Lembke, head of the Dual Diagnosis Addiction Clinic at Stanford, has written in her book Dopamine Nation about patients who helped themselves out of depressive phases through directed cold water therapy.

An additional question came up about building confidence in moments of feeling intimidated. The key is real-time stress modulation. When we are in a state of fear or stress or anxiety, the rule set and creativity available to us are greatly diminished. This has to do with how the prefrontal cortex interacts with an area of the brain called the insula. The prefrontal cortex is the coach; the rest of the brain are the players. If you get too stressed, the players start to lead the game and the coach drags behind. Raise your stress threshold using behavioral tools and register your wins. The dopamine system is highly subject to interpretation — if you can conceptualize a failure as a partial win, you tap into the dopamine system. Viktor Frankl and Nelson Mandela both found new ways to access the dopamine system even in confinement by focusing on what they could control in their immediate experience.

## What Is the Competing Mechanism Behind Bilateral Eye Movement (EMDR & Walking) That Helps Resolve Psychological Trauma? (00:24:03)

EMDR — moving your eyes from side to side while recounting a trauma — is one of the four approved behavioral treatments for trauma, taken seriously in the psychiatric and psychological community. It tends to work best for single-event traumas as opposed to entire childhoods of repeated harm.

Eye movements from side to side very potently reduce activity in the amygdala, which is the brain's threat detection center. When you walk through space, your eyes generate subtle side-to-side shifts unless you are tracking a fixed target. Research from my lab and others found this leads to potent quieting of the threat detection system. EMDR is essentially pairing that calmer, amygdala-quieted state with recount of something that would normally be triggering — behavioral desensitization in reverse Pavlovian fashion.

Simply taking a walk without looking at your phone, allowing your eyes to move naturally side to side, is a very effective way to shut down the fear and stress system. The future of trauma treatment will likely be combination therapies — pharmacologic manipulations to boost neuroplasticity-supporting systems like dopamine, serotonin, and adrenaline, followed by behavioral interventions like EMDR.

## What New Research or Interventions Are You Most Excited About in the Health & Wellness Realm? (00:28:07)

The key distinction coming in the next few years is between modulation versus mediation. If someone pulls a fire alarm, it modulates your attention — it does not mediate it. Getting good sleep makes you less easily triggered; it modulates stress. But sleep does not directly mediate specific stress responses.

This distinction matters enormously for the gut-brain axis. The gut microbiota create chemical precursors that become serotonin and dopamine in the brain. Consuming low-sugar fermented foods — natto, kimchi, sauerkraut, kombucha, certain yogurts — modulates a huge number of systems. Improving a disrupted microbiome can elevate mood, but it will not cure depression. That is the modulating versus mediating distinction. Sunlight, sleep, healthy gut microbiome, exercise, good nutrition, and social connection all create a general milieu of health — they modulate, they do not mediate.

Palmer cooling is another underused tool. Placing cold water on the palms, soles of the feet, or upper face — at those locations the body skips capillary beds and cools the core much more rapidly. In between sets of exercise or during running or cycling, this can dramatically increase the ability to continue — potentially doubling endurance or the number of sets completed. This is the work of my colleague at Stanford, Dr. Craig Heller. You do not need any special equipment; cold water in a bowl works. The athletes at Stanford and people in the military use it.

## Do You Suppose This Physiological Stress Regulator Transcends Species? (00:42:10)

The double inhale, long exhale — the physiological sigh — absolutely transcends species. My colleague at Stanford, Sue McConnell, is an expert in dog genetics. If you look at dog breeds, some like the bulldog are so parasympathetic-dominant that a nuclear bomb could go off and they might open one eye — getting them into action is more of an effort. Then there are breeds like the whippet or Italian greyhound, always cold, very sympathetic-dominant. Within any breed or species there is a range. Anyone who has had children will tell you they were like this since birth — calm and easygoing, or easily stressed.

There is a concept in autonomic regulation called a hinge. Certain animals have the hinge tightened so the seesaw tilts toward the mellow. Horses are selected not just for physical attributes but for temperament, and they do give you a lot of information about the state of their nervous system. The physiological sigh appears to be a universal autonomous stress regulation mechanism across mammalian species.

## Is There Any Science Behind Staying Motivated or Developing Discipline? (00:47:20)

There is a hybrid fuel model for motivation. The grinding fuel — grit, anger, competitive determination — is real but depletable. It requires significant rest to renew. The more sustaining system is the delight system — dopamine and serotonin activated by genuine interest and love for what you are doing. Try to find the gems within whatever you are working on. When I encountered a topic I hated in college I would tell myself I was going to fall in love with it by finding what was genuinely interesting in it. Sometimes it worked; sometimes it did not. But the attempt to find delight is more sustaining than pure grinding.

Discipline is partly about setting the right context. Sleep, stress baseline, nutrition — getting those right is itself a form of discipline and gives the brain the best chance to generate disciplined effort. These systems are very susceptible to top-down regulation and to context.

## What's Your Opinion on Psilocybin? (00:57:09)

My colleague Nolan Williams, who is triple-board-certified in neurology and psychiatry at Stanford, has done significant work in this area. Psilocybin is not for everybody — people with psychosis or drug addiction issues need to be thoughtful, and it is still illegal, though decriminalized in certain places.

The clinical trials on single macro-dose sessions are quite encouraging. The data for micro-dosing, per Matthew Johnson who has been on the podcast, are not really there. The data on single heroic-dose sessions for depression, PTSD, eating disorders, and end-of-life preparation are compelling — approximately two-thirds of participants achieve lasting relief from one guided session with physicians present. Nolan Williams noted that in studies of lifetime perceived individual and societal risk, psilocybin sits near the bottom of all substances — below caffeine. Alcohol, cocaine, heroin, and methamphetamine sit at the top.

There is a potential hazard with all psychedelics: they alter the chemical landscape such that many things can become attractors in that state. A therapeutic guide steers the session toward meaningful transformative work. The ability to "let go" at the peak of the experience appears to predict a successful outcome — and that is fascinating from a neuroscience standpoint because we do not yet know exactly what that means mechanistically.

## Why Does My Desire to Eat Disappear After I Use the Sauna? (01:01:07)

The sauna, or any deliberate heat exposure that is uncomfortable, releases the molecule dynorphin — the same molecule released during alcohol withdrawal, which produces agitation and a general sense of not feeling well. Dynorphin is an appetite suppressant. After the session, dynorphin drives upregulation of mu opioid receptors, so your endogenous opioids have a more robust effect during the recovery window — which is why the post-sauna period feels good.

Sauna also produces significant increases in growth hormone. Once-weekly sessions — four 20-minute blocks separated by five-minute breaks — produce the largest spikes. More frequent use produces smaller but still significant increases.
