## [00:08:35] Sensory Detection vs. Sensory Perception

**Andrew Huberman:** Charles, thank you so much for joining me today. I want to ask you about many things related to taste and gustatory perception, but maybe to start off — you've worked on a number of different topics in neuroscience, not just taste — how do you think about perception, how it's different from sensation, and what leads to our experience of life in terms of vision, hearing, taste, et cetera?

**Charles Zuker:** The brain is an extraordinary organ that weighs maybe 2% of your body mass, yet it consumes anywhere between 25 to 30% of all of your energy and oxygen. And it gets transformed into a mind, and this mind changes the human condition. It transforms fear into courage, conformity into creativity, sadness into happiness. How the hell does that happen?

The challenge the brain faces is that the world is made of real things — a glass, a cord, a microphone — but the brain is only made of neurons that only understand electrical signals. So how do you transform that reality into nothing but electrical signals that now need to represent the world? That process is what we can operationally define as perception.

In the senses — olfactory, taste, vision — we can very straightforwardly separate detection from perception. Detection is what happens when you take a sugar molecule, put it on your tongue, and a set of specific cells sense that sugar molecule. That's detection. You haven't perceived anything yet. That is just your cells in your tongue interacting with this chemical. But now that cell gets activated and sends a signal to the brain, and now detection gets transformed into perception. Trying to understand how that happens has been the maniacal drive of my entire career in neuroscience — how does the brain ultimately transform detection into perception so that it can guide actions and behaviors?

**Andrew Huberman:** That is a very clear and beautiful description.

## [00:11:48] Individual Variations within Perception, Color

**Andrew Huberman:** A sort of high-level question related to that: I think many people would like to know whether or not my perception of the color of your shirt is the same as your perception of the color of your shirt.

**Charles Zuker:** Excellent question. Given what I told you — that the brain is trying to represent the world based on nothing but the transformation of signals into electrical languages that neurons had to encode and decode — it follows that your brain is different than my brain. And therefore it follows that the way that you are perceiving the world must be different than mine, even when receiving the same sensory cues.

Let me tell you about a simple yet brilliant experiment that demonstrates how we perceive the world differently. In vision, we have three classes of photoreceptor neurons that sense three basic colors: blue, green, and red. These three are sufficient to accommodate the full visible spectrum. I'm going to take three light projectors and project a red light and a green light overlapping on a wide screen — and on the screen, there'll be yellow. Then I'm going to take a third projector with a filter that projects spectrally pure yellow right next to that mixed beam. I'm going to ask you to play with the intensity knobs of the red and green projectors so that you can match the mixed yellow to the spectrally pure yellow next to it. I'll write down the numbers on those two intensity knobs, and then I'll ask the next person to do the same — and we're going to end up with thousands of different number combinations.

So for all of us, yellow is yellow enough that we can use a common language, but for every one of us, that yellow is going to be ever so slightly different. That simple psychological experiment beautifully illustrates how we truly perceive the world differently.

**Andrew Huberman:** I love that example. And yet, in that example, we know the basic elements from which color is created. If we migrate into olfaction — very hard to do an experiment that will allow that degree of granularity and causality. If I give you the smell of a rose, you can describe it to me, and I can describe it also, but I have no way of knowing whether the two of us are experiencing the same thing.

**Charles Zuker:** No question that your experience is different than mine.

## [00:20:19] The 5 Taste Modalities

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about taste. Why did you migrate from studying vision to studying taste?

**Charles Zuker:** My goal has been to understand how the brain does its magic — how do you encode and decode emotions, memories, actions? How do you make decisions? How do you transform detection into perception? One of the key things in science is ensuring that you always ask the right question so that you have a possibility of answering it.

The taste system at the time I began working on it was remarkable in that nothing was known about the molecular basis of taste. We knew we could taste what has been usually defined as the five basic taste qualities: sweet, sour, bitter, salty, and umami. Umami is a Japanese word that means yummy, delicious — and that's nearly every animal species, the taste of amino acids. In humans, it's mostly associated with the taste of MSG, monosodium glutamate. Foods rich in umami include seaweed, tomatoes, cheese — it's a great flavor enhancer that enriches our sensory experience.

The beautiful thing about the system is that the lines of input are limited to five. Each of them has a predetermined meaning — you are born liking sugar and disliking bitter. You have no choice. These are hardwired systems. Of course you can learn to dislike sugar and to like bitters, but in the wild, these are 100% predetermined. Sweet, umami, and low salt are attractive taste qualities — they evoke appetitive responses, the desire to consume them. And bitter and sour are innately predetermined to be aversive.

## [00:26:18] Aversive Taste, Bitter Taste

**Andrew Huberman:** Could I interrupt briefly and ask about that exact point — for something to be appetitive and some other taste to be aversive, and for those to be hardwired, can we assume that the sensation of very bitter or of activation of bitter receptors in the mouth activates a neural circuit that causes closing of the mouth, retraction of the tongue and retraction of the body, and that the taste of something sweet might actually induce more licking?

**Charles Zuker:** 100%, you got it. The activation of the receptors in the tongue that recognize sweet versus the ones that recognize bitter activate an entire behavioral program. In the case of bitter, it's very easy to actually see happening in animals. The first thing you do is you stop licking. Then you make an unhappy face, then you squint your eyes, and then you start gagging. That entire thing happens by the activation of a bitter molecule in a bitter-sensing cell in your tongue. It's incredible — it's the magic of the brain, how it's able to encode and decode these extraordinary actions and behaviors in response to nothing but a simple, very unique sensory stimulus.

## [00:28:00] Survival-Based & Evolutionary Reasons for Taste Modalities, Taste vs. Flavor

**Charles Zuker:** This palette of five basic tastes accommodates all the dietary needs of the organism. Sweet: to ensure that we get the right amount of energy. Umami: to ensure that we get proteins and essential nutrients. Salt: the three appetitive ones to ensure that we maintain our electrolyte balance. Bitter: to prevent the ingestion of toxic, nauseous chemicals — nearly all bitter tasting things out in the wild are bad for you. And sour: most likely to prevent ingestion of spoiled, acidic, fermented foods.

Now, of course, there's a difference between basic taste and flavor. Flavor is the whole experience — the combination of multiple tastes coming together with smell, with texture, with temperature, with the look of the food, that gives you what you and I would call the full sensory experience. But as scientists, we need to reduce the problem into its basic elements so we can begin to break it apart before we put it back together. We look at individual qualities — we give animals sweet, or we give them bitter. We give them sour. We avoid mixes because the first stage of discovery is to have that clarity as to what you're trying to extract.

**Andrew Huberman:** Almost like the primary colors to create the full array of the color spectrum.

**Charles Zuker:** Exactly.

## [00:30:14] Additional Taste Modalities: Fat & Metallic Perception

**Andrew Huberman:** Before I ask you about the first and second and third stages of taste and flavor perception, is there any idea that there may be more than five taste modalities?

**Charles Zuker:** There is. For example, what about fat? There is a compelling argument that a lot of what we call fat taste is really mechanosensory — it's somatosensory cells responding to mechanical stimulation of fat molecules rolling on the tongue that gives you that perception of fat, rather than a dedicated receptor for fat per se.

And another one you could argue is metallic taste. I know exactly what it tastes like. Take a penny, put it in your mouth and you know what it tastes like. Or blood — that's another very good example. And is there really a receptor for metallic taste, or is it nothing but a magical combination of the activation of the existing lines of information? Think of it like the keys of a piano: sweet, sour, bitter, salt, umami. You play a key and you activate a chord. In the case of a piano, a chord leads to a note; in the case of taste, a chord leads to an action and a behavior. But you play many of them together and something emerges that's different than any one of the pieces. It's possible that metallic represents the combination of these other lines activated in just the right ratio.

## [00:34:02] Tongue "Taste Map," Taste Buds & Taste Receptors

**Andrew Huberman:** Could you describe the sequence of neural events leading to a perceptual event of taste? And somewhere in there I'm certain you'll embed an answer to the question of whether or not we indeed have different taste receptors distributed in different locations on our tongue.

**Charles Zuker:** Let's start by debunking that old tale and myth. The most prevalent origin story is that there was an original drawing describing the sensitivity of the tongue to different tastes, and the argument that has emerged is that the data was simply mistranslated as it was being drawn — and of course that led to an entire industry. Things like designing wine vessels so that they act maximally on supposed receptors at particular parts of the tongue.

Now, there is no tongue map. We have taste buds distributed in various parts of the tongue, so there is a map of the distribution of taste buds, but each taste bud has around a hundred taste receptor cells, and those taste receptor cells can be of five types: sweet, sour, bitter, salty, or umami. For the most part, all taste buds have the representation of all five taste qualities. Now, there's no question that there is a slight bias for some tastes — bitter, for instance, is particularly rich at the very back of your tongue, and there's a biological basis for that. That's the last line of defense before you swallow something bad. So having plenty of bitter receptors there means that if they get activated, you can trigger a gagging reflex and get rid of something that might otherwise kill you.

But the notion that all sweet is in the front and salt is on the side is not real. My laboratory identified the receptors for all five basic taste classes — sweet, bitter, salt, umami, and most recently sour, now completing the palette. You can use these receptors to rigorously map where they're found in the tongue. Every taste bud throughout your oral cavity has receptors for all of the basic taste classes.

There are also taste receptors in the pharynx at the very back of the oral cavity, on the tongue, and on the palate. The palate is very rich in sweet receptors.

## [00:39:34] Burning Your Tongue & Perception

**Andrew Huberman:** Many people, including myself, are familiar with the experience of drinking a sip of tea or coffee that is too hot and burning my tongue. When I experience that phenomenon, have I destroyed taste receptors that regenerate, or have I used temperature to distort the function of the circuit?

**Charles Zuker:** Excellent question. The answer is both. It turns out that your taste receptors only live for around two weeks, and this makes sense because here you have an organ — the tongue — that is continuously exposed to everything you could range from the nicest to the most horrible possible things. And so you need to make sure that these cells are always coming back in a way that can reexperience the world in the right way. Other organs have the same underlying logic: your gut, your intestines renew in a very fast cycle. Olfactory neurons in your nose are another example.

So yes, you're burning a lot of your cells and those are done — but the good news is they're going to come back. When you burn yourself with tea, they come back within 20 to 30 minutes to an hour. And in that timeframe, you are also damaging your somatosensory cells — the cells that feel things, not taste things. Then you wait half an hour or so, and then thank God, it's back to normal.

## [00:42:54] The "Meaning" of Taste Stimuli, Sweet vs. Bitter, Valence

**Andrew Huberman:** As a next step along this circuit, let's compare and contrast sweet and bitter as we follow their lines from the tongue to the brain.

**Charles Zuker:** The first thing is that the two evoke diametrically opposed behaviors. If we have to come up with two sensory experiences that represent polar opposites, it will be sweet and bitter. Sweet and bitter are the two opposite ends of the sensory spectrum. Now, a taste can be defined by two features: its quality and its valence. Valence means the value of that experience or stimulus.

Take sweet. Sweet has a quality, an identity — that's what you and I refer to as the taste of sweet. We know exactly what it tastes like. But sweet also has a positive valence, which makes it incredibly attractive and appetitive — and that attractiveness is independent of its identity and quality. In fact, we have been able to engineer animals where we completely remove the valence from the stimulus. These animals can taste sweet, can recognize it as sweet, but it's no longer attractive — it's just one more chemical stimulus. That's because the identity and the valence are encoded in two separate parts of the brain.

In the case of bitter, it has its identity — you know exactly what bitter tastes like — and it also has a valence: a negative valence because it evokes aversive behaviors.

So the signals follow two separate lines, like two separate keys at the two ends of a keyboard. You press the sweet key, and you activate the sweet cells throughout your oral cavity. They all converge into a group of sweet neurons in the next station, which is still outside the brain — one of the taste ganglia. These are the neurons that innervate your tongue and oral cavity, sitting around the lymph node area. And there are two main ganglia that innervate the vast majority of all taste buds in the oral cavity.

From there that sweet signal goes onto the brain stem — the entry of the body into the brain. There's a unique, topographically defined location in the rostral side of the brain stem that receives all of the taste input. From there, the sweet signal goes higher up on the brain stem and then goes through a number of stations — from the brain stem through the thalamus — eventually getting to your cortex. And once it gets to your taste cortex, that's where meaning is imposed into that signal. It's then, and only then, that you can identify this as a sweet stimulus. The whole process takes less than a second.

We can demonstrate this by sticking electrodes at each of these stations and stimulating the tongue — within a fraction of a second you see the response in the following stations. There is an area of your brain that represents the taste of sweet in taste cortex, and a different area that represents the taste of bitter. In a sense, there is a topographic map of these taste qualities inside your brain.

Now, if this group of neurons in your cortex really represents the sense of sweet, and a different group represents bitter, then we should be able to do two things. First, I should be able to go into your brain, silence those neurons — and I can give you all the sweet you want and you'll never know you're tasting sweet. Conversely, I should be able to activate those neurons while giving you absolutely nothing, and you're going to think you are getting the full percept. And that's precisely what we have done in mice — though the same principle applies in humans with zero doubt. When we activate the bitter neurons in the cortex, the animal can start gagging while drinking only water — it thinks it's getting a bitter stimulus.

This substantiates the capacity of the brain to segregate the representation of these two diametrically opposed percepts — sweet versus bitter.

## [00:51:55] Positive vs. Negative Neuronal Activation & Behavior

**Andrew Huberman:** That raises the question of whether those separate sets of neurons are connected to areas of the brain that create the sense of valence, or whether they're simply connected to sets of neurons that evoke distinct behaviors of moving toward and licking, or aversion. Are we essentially interpreting our behavior and our micro-responses, or are our behaviors the consequence of the percept?

**Charles Zuker:** Excellent question. The answer is they go into an area of the brain where valence is imposed — and that area is known as the amygdala. The sweet neurons go to a different area than the bitter neurons. Now let me do a thought experiment. I activate the sweet neurons, and the animal increases licking. That's expected because it's tasting water as if it were sugar. But how do I know that activating them is evoking a positive feeling inside — a goodness, a satisfaction — versus just mechanically forcing the tongue to move faster?

We can actually separate these by doing a place preference test. I take the animal and put them inside a box that has two sides, each with distinct visual features. The animal will explore both sides with equal frequency. Now I activate the sweet neurons every time the animal is on one specific side. If that activation is creating a positive internal state, the animal will want to stay on that side — and that's precisely what you see. And conversely, if I activate the bitter neurons on that same side, the animal will actively want to stay away from that side. And that's precisely what we see.

## [00:56:16] Acquired Tastes, Conditioned Taste Aversion

**Andrew Huberman:** Many people are familiar with the experience of going to a restaurant, eating a variety of foods, and then feeling very sick — and from that point on, having a strong aversion to whatever they think caused it. This is one of the strongest examples of one-trial learning. Do we have any concept of where in this pathway that occurs?

**Charles Zuker:** We do know a significant amount about this at a general level. This is a phenomenon known as conditioned taste aversion. You can pair an attractive stimulus with a really bad one and make an animal begin to vehemently dislike something like sugar — because you've conditioned the animal to now be averse to that otherwise nice taste, because it's been associated with malaise.

When you do that, you can begin to ask what has changed in the signal as it travels from the tongue to the brain in a normal animal versus an animal where you've now transformed sweet from attractive to aversive. This is how we begin to explore how the brain changes the nature, quality, and meaning of a stimulus as a function of its state.

There are events so traumatic that a single occurrence is all it takes — one trial learning. And the taste system is literally at the top of that food chain, likely because the consequences of eating poison can be life or death.

## [00:01:01:44] Olfaction (Smell) vs. Taste, Changing Tastes over One's Lifetime

**Andrew Huberman:** Flavor is distinct from taste because flavor involves smell, texture, temperature, and other features. How much plasticity is there in the taste system over the lifespan? For instance, kids don't seem to like certain vegetables, but eventually grow to incorporate them.

**Charles Zuker:** I want to use the difference between the olfactory system and the taste system to make the point. The taste system has five basic palettes — sweet, sour, bitter, salt, umami — each with a predetermined identity and valence. These are attractive; these are aversive.

In the olfactory system, it's claimed that we can smell millions of different odors, yet for the most part, none of them have an innate predetermined meaning. In the olfactory system, meaning is imposed by learning and experience. There are a handful of odors that could be claimed to universally evoke aversive reactions — sulfur might be a near-universal example — but nearly every other odor is afforded meaning by learning and experience. That's why I like broccoli while you might despise it because your mother forced you to eat it. Same sensory experience, completely different meaning imposed by learning.

This accommodates something important: in the case of taste, you only have neurons for five classes — it's not going to take a lot of your brain. But if we can smell a million odors, and every one of them had to have predetermined meaning, there's not going to be enough brain just to accommodate that one sense. So evolution in its infinite wisdom evolved a system where you have an olfactory cortex with the capacity to associate every odor in a specific context that now gives it meaning.

Back to taste and plasticity. Predetermined and hardwired doesn't mean it's not modulated by learning or experience — it only means you are born liking sweet and disliking bitter. We have many examples of plasticity: beer being one. Why do we learn to love beer and coffee? Because they have an associated gain to the system. That positive valence that emerges out of that negative signal is sufficient to create the positive association. In the case of beer, of course, it's alcohol. In the case of coffee, it's caffeine activating a whole group of neurotransmitter systems that give you that high. So yes, the taste system is changeable, malleable, and subjected to learning and experience.

## [01:09:14] Integration of Odor & Taste, Influence on Behavior & Emotion

**Andrew Huberman:** I've had the experience of the odor of somebody's breath being aversive — not because I could identify it as aversive, but just because I didn't like it. And there are other scents that I can recall — of skin, of foods — that are immensely appetitive. Even mothers describing the smell of their baby's head. What is going on in terms of the combination of odor and taste, given that these two systems are so different?

**Charles Zuker:** They come together ultimately in a place in the brain where they integrate into what we would call the full sensory experience. Here's an experiment that demonstrates this. We know where the olfactory cortex and the taste cortex are in the brain — they're in two different places. We can go to each of these two cortexes, put color traces, and see where those neurons project to in their next targets. If odor and taste come together somewhere in the brain, we should find an area receiving both colors. And we found such an area.

We hypothesized that this area is responsible for multisensory integration of odor and taste. To test this, we trained mice to lick in response to sweet and execute the correct action to get a water reward. We can then do the same experiment but mix taste with odor, training the mice to report when they sense taste alone, odor alone, or the mix. After training, we go to this area of multisensory integration in the brain and silence it. And if that area really represents the integration, the animals should still be able to recognize taste alone and odor alone — but should be incapable of recognizing the mix. And exactly as predicted, that's exactly what you get.

## [01:17:26] Sensitization to Taste, Internal State Modulation, Salt

**Andrew Huberman:** In the visual system, if you look at something long enough, it will actually disappear — a fundamental habituation or desensitization. I'm also familiar with eating something very sweet for the first time and it tasting very sweet, but a few more bites and now it tastes less sweet. Where does that desensitization occur?

**Charles Zuker:** Desensitization is happening at multiple stations. It's happening at the receptor level — the salt in your tongue that senses the sugar gets to a point where it undergoes chemical changes where it now signals far less efficiently, or it even gets removed from the surface of the cell. So the same amount of sugar will trigger far less of a response. And then there's the integrated loss of signaling that happens by continuous activation of the circuit at each of the different neural stations along the pathway.

The reason you need so many stations — from tongue to ganglia, to brain stem, to thalamus, to cortex — is that the taste system has to be subjected to modulation by the internal state. Each of these nodes provides a new site for plasticity and modulation, not necessarily to change the way something tastes, but to ensure that you consume more or less or differently of what you need.

Let me give you one example: salt. Salt is very appetitive at low concentrations because we need it — our electrolyte balance requires it. But at high concentrations — like ocean water — it's incredibly aversive. However, if I salt-deprive you, now that incredibly high concentration of salt becomes amazingly appetitive and attractive. Your tongue is telling you this is horrible, but your brain is telling you, I don't care — you need it. This is what we call the modulation of the taste system by the internal state.

Similarly, if you're thirsty and you have no water, you suppress hunger so that you don't waste water molecules digesting food — because if you're thirsty and have no water, you'll die within a week, whereas you can survive a hunger strike for months as long as you have water. So there are multiple layers at which the taste system — that guides our drive and motivation to consume the nutrients we need — must be modulated in response to the internal state.

## [01:24:05] Taste & Saliva: The Absence of Taste

**Andrew Huberman:** I have a question about saliva. Is there any kind of signal for the absence of taste despite having something in the mouth? And could the composition of saliva in a fed state versus an unfed state modulate taste sensitivity?

**Charles Zuker:** The experiment has actually been done. It turns out the answer is no — and you can do this experiment using artificial saliva. We know the composition of saliva, so you can make such a thing in the laboratory and apply it to taste cells in culture or on a washed tongue. What happens is that the system is engineered to desensitize to saliva — to become agnostic to it, to become invisible to it. And there is no difference based on the state of the animal.

**Andrew Huberman:** Curiosity kills the cat, but saves the career of a scientist every single time.

**Charles Zuker:** Absolutely, every single time.

## [01:28:10] Sugar & Reward Pleasure Centers; Gut-Brain Axis, Anticipatory Response

**Andrew Huberman:** Why do we crave sugar when we're stressed? And can you describe the gut-brain axis and the aspects of gut-brain signaling that drive or change our perceptions and behaviors that are completely beneath our awareness?

**Charles Zuker:** Sugar activates what I'll generically refer to as reward-pleasure centers in a way that dramatically changes our internal state. This is why we eat a gallon of ice cream when we're very depressed — it's soothing.

Let me start by stating that the brain needs to monitor the state of every one of our organs — it has to. This is the only way the brain can ensure that every one of those organs are working together in a way that we have healthy physiology. What hadn't been fully appreciated until recently is that this is a two-way highway where the brain is not only monitoring, but also modulating back what the body needs to do. That covers everything from monitoring the frequency of heartbeats and the breathing cycle, to what happens when you ingest sugar and fat.

Let me give an example of how the brain can take contextual associations and transform them into incredible changes in physiology and metabolism. Remember Pavlov? In his classical conditioning experiments, he would ring a bell every time he was going to feed a dog. Eventually the dog learned to associate the ringing of the bell with food coming — and the first incredible finding was that the dog would start to salivate in the presence of the bell alone. Neurons in the brain that formed that association now represent "food is coming" and send a signal to motor neurons to squeeze the salivary glands so you release saliva.

But what's even more remarkable is that those animals are also releasing insulin in response to the bell alone. This illustrates the two-way highway going down: somehow the brain created these associations, and neurons now know food is coming and send a signal all the way down to the pancreas to release insulin because sugar is coming.

Over the past dozen years, and with great force over the last five years, the main highway communicating the state of the body with the brain has been uncovered as what we now refer to as the gut-brain axis. That highway is a specific bundle of nerves — the vagus nerve, emerging from the vagal ganglia, the nodose ganglia — that innervates the majority of the organs in your body, monitoring their function and sending a signal to the brain, and the brain going back down and saying this is going alright, do this, or this is not going too well, do that.

I don't think obesity is a disease of metabolism. I believe obesity is a disease of brain circuits. The brain ultimately appears to be the conductor of this orchestra of physiology and metabolism.

## [01:36:23] Vagus Nerve

**Andrew Huberman:** The vagus nerve has in popular culture been kind of converted into a single meaning of calming pathways, mostly because the yogic community was among the first to talk about vagal stimulation. But experimentally in the laboratory, many neuroscientists stimulate the vagus to create states of alertness and arousal.

**Charles Zuker:** You're right. The vagus nerve is made out of many thousands of individual fibers that make this gigantic bundle. And it's likely that each of these fibers carries a slightly different meaning. This group of fibers is telling the brain about the state of your heart. This group is telling the brain about the state of your gut. This group tells the brain about its nutritional state. This your pancreas, this your lungs. They are, again, like the keys of a piano.

There is a lot of data showing that activating the entire vagal bundle has very meaningful effects in a wide range of conditions — in fact, it's being used to treat intractable depression and epileptic seizures. But activating the bundle is like turning on all the lights in a stadium because you need to illuminate where you lost your keys under your seat. You've just turned on 10,000 bulbs of a thousand watts each. Only one of these happens to be pointing to where you lost your keys. So while we're lucky enough that some of those fibers do something meaningful for depression or epilepsy, it should not be misconstrued as specific or selective activation.

The goal — along with many other great scientists, including Steven Liberles at Harvard, who started a lot of this molecular dissection of vagal gut-brain communication — is to uncover what each of those lines is doing. What are each of those keys of the piano playing? They are finding multiple parallel pathways: some that control heartbeat, some that control the respiratory cycle, some involved in the feeling of satiety when your stomach gets distended. The textbooks will soon change on the basis of this work.

## [01:43:09] Insatiable Sugar Appetite, Liking vs. Wanting, Gut-Brain Axis

**Charles Zuker:** Let me tell you about the gut-brain axis and our insatiable appetite for sugar and fat. This is a story about the fundamental difference between liking and wanting. Liking sugar is the function of the taste system — and it's not really liking sugar, it's liking sweet. Wanting sugar — our never-ending appetite for sugar — is the story of the gut-brain axis.

This work began in my own laboratory long ago when we discovered the sweet receptors and could engineer mice that lack these receptors — animals unable to taste sweet. If you give a normal mouse a bottle containing sweet (either sugar or an artificial sweetener — they both activate the same receptor, trigger the same signal, and the mouse can't distinguish between them), the mouse will drink 10 to 1 from the sweet bottle versus water. But if you knock out the sweet receptors, the mouse can no longer tell them apart and will drink equally from both — because that sweet bottle tastes just like water.

Now here's the key experiment. We give the sweet-receptor-knockout mouse sugar water versus plain water. Initially, as expected, it drinks equally from both. But if I keep that mouse in the cage for the next 48 hours, something extraordinary happens: when I come back, that mouse is drinking almost exclusively from the sugar bottle. How could this be? It cannot taste it.

During those 48 hours, the mouse learned that there is something in that bottle that makes it feel good. And that is the bottle it wants to consume. The mouse identifies that bottle by using other sensory features — the smell, the texture (sugar at high concentrations is kind of goopy), the side of the cage the bottle is on. It doesn't matter what. The mouse realizes there is something there that makes me feel good, and that's what I want. That is the fundamental basis of our unquenchable craving for sugar, and it is mediated by the gut-brain axis.

The first clue that this is post-ingestive is that it takes a long time to develop. So we reasoned: if this is true and the gut-brain axis is driving sugar preference, there should be a group of neurons in the brain that respond to post-ingestive sugar. And indeed, we identified a group of neurons in the brain that does this, receiving their input directly from the gut-brain axis.

What's happening is this: sugar is recognized normally by the tongue, activating an appetitive response. Now you ingest it, and it activates a selective group of cells in your intestines that send a signal to the brain via the vagal ganglia saying, "I got what I need." The tongue only knows you tasted it; the gut knows it got to the point where it's going to be used and absorbed. The gut sends the signal to reinforce the consumption of this thing, because this is the one that was needed — sugar, source of energy.

These signals are processed in the intestines because that's where all the absorption happens. You want the brain to know that you had successful ingestion and breakdown of what you consumed into the building blocks of life — glucose, amino acids, fat — once they are in the form that the intestines can absorb.

## [01:52:03] Tool: Sugar vs. Artificial Sweeteners, Curbing Appetite

**Charles Zuker:** This now-sugar molecule activates this unique gut-brain circuit that drives the development of our preference for sugar. A key element of this circuit is that the sensors in the gut that recognize the sugar do not recognize artificial sweeteners at all. It's because it's a very different type of receptor — it's not the tongue receptors being used in the gut. It's a completely different molecule that only recognizes the glucose molecule, not artificial sweeteners.

This has a profound impact: since artificial sweeteners don't activate the gut-brain axis, they'll never satisfy the craving for sugar like sugar does. I believe that artificial sweeteners have failed in the market to curb our appetite for sugar because they beautifully work on the tongue — they activate the liking pathway, recognizing sweet versus non-sweet — but they fail to activate the key sensors in the gut that inform the brain, "you got sugar, no need to crave anymore."

## [01:54:06] Cravings & Gut-Brain Axis

**Andrew Huberman:** Can we relate wanting to a particular set of neurochemicals? The pathway is: glucose activates cells in the gut, which through the vagus, through the nodose ganglia, up into the brain stem — and from there, where does it go? What is the substrate of wanting?

**Charles Zuker:** Molecules like dopamine — a very diabolical molecule because it evokes both a sense of pleasure and also a sense of desiring more, of craving — are centrally involved. Artificial sweeteners are failing as a means to satisfy sugar craving at the level of nutrient sensing. And yet if we trigger this true sugar-evoked wanting pathway too much, we find ourselves wanting more and more sugar.

We need to come up with strategies that can meaningfully change the activation of these circuits that control our wanting — particularly for populations at risk. This gut-brain circuit that informs the brain about the presence of intestinal sugar is a very important target. Can we modulate these circuits? Can we make your brain think you got satisfied with sugar even though I'm not giving you sugar? That raises the question of whether the receptors for glucose in these gut cells are susceptible to other things that are healthier for us — and I think that's an important goal to pursue.

## [02:01:53] Fast vs. Slow Signaling & Reinforcement, Highly Processed Foods

**Andrew Huberman:** This effect took a couple of days in mice, while the perception of taste is immediate. The brain is able to couple the taste of a sweet drink with the experience of nutrient extraction in the gut — under a context where the mouse is also ingesting other things and smelling other mice — and that coupling takes sustained exposure. **Charles Zuker:** You have to think of it not as humans, but as animals in the wild — which is easier to comprehend the logic. Why should this take a long time, given that taste is immediate? Because you want to make sure that this source of sugar is the best, the richest — the one where you get the most energy for the least amount of work. You want to identify rich sources of sugar. If the system simply responded immediately to the first sugar that got to your gut, you'd form associations with food sources that are not the ones you should be eating from. Don't fall in love with the first person you encounter.

And so evolutionarily, by having the taste system give you immediate recognition, but then forcing the gut-brain axis to reinforce it only when sustained repeated exposure has informed the brain — this ensures you don't form the association prematurely. When we remove it from the context of foraging in the wild, and instead go to the supermarket — highly processed foods are hijacking, co-opting these circuits in a way that would never have happened in nature. But when you take the highly processed extracted tangerine product, everything's already been broken down for you, and your system has no work — you are co-opting and hijacking the circuits by activating them at a time scale that would never normally happen in nature.

The lessons emerging from understanding how these circuits operate can ultimately inform how we deal with our diets in a way that we avoid what we're facing now as society. I also think the training of people thinking about metabolic science and metabolic disease is largely divorced from the training of neuroscientists.
