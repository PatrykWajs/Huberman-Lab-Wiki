## [00:00:00] Pain & Pleasure

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today, we continue our discussion of the senses, and the senses we are going to discuss are pain and pleasure. Pain and pleasure reflect two opposite ends of a continuum, a continuum that involves detection of things in our skin, and the perception, the understanding of what those events are.

## [00:00:39] Skin, Appetitive vs Aversive Behaviors

Our skin is our largest sensory organ and our largest organ indeed — much larger than any of the other organs in our body. It acts as a barrier between our organs and the outside world, harbors neurons that detect light touch, temperature, or pressure, and it's an organ that allows us to experience either great pain or great pleasure. I think we all intuitively understand what pleasure and pain are. Pleasure generally is a sensation in the body and in the mind that leads us to pursue more of whatever is bringing about that sensation, while pain leads us to want to withdraw or move away from some activity or interaction. Scientists call these appetitive behaviors — those that create an appetite for more — and aversive behaviors, those that make us want to move away from something.

## [00:02:10] Skin, Neurons & Brain

Throughout the skin, we have neurons — little nerve cells. The cell body, where the DNA and the central factory of the cell resides, actually sits right outside your spinal cord in structures called DRGs, or dorsal root ganglia. These DRGs are remarkable because they send one branch — an axon — out to our skin, and another wire from the same cell body goes in the opposite direction up to our brain, creating connections within the brain stem. These wires in the skin respond to mechanical forces: some respond only to light touch, others to coarse or hard pressure, others to temperature — heat or cold — and still others to certain chemicals on the skin. What's incredible is that the language all these neurons use is exactly the same: electrical signals. Yet your brain distinguishes cold from heat from touch, because there must be another element in the equation — your brain — that interprets these signals. The brain takes these electrical signals and interprets them, partially based on experience, but there are also innate, hardwired aspects of pain and pleasure sensing that require no experience whatsoever.

## [00:04:46] Brain Interpretation, Homunculus, Two-Point Discrimination Test

What parts of the brain are responsible? Mainly, the somatosensory cortex — the portion of the neocortex on the outside of our brain. In your somatosensory cortex, you have a map of your entire body surface called a homunculus — your representation of touch, including pleasure and pain. It is highly organized in a very particular way: the areas of your skin with the highest density of sensory receptors are magnified in your brain. Those areas are the lips, the face, the tips of the fingers, the feet, and the genitals, because the innervation — the number of wires — going into those regions far exceeds that going to other areas.

You can experience this in real time by doing a simple experiment called two-point discrimination. If a person took two pens about a centimeter apart and pressed them to the top of your hand while your eyes were closed, you would be able to perceive two distinct points of pressure. However, if they did this to the middle of your back, you would experience them as a single point of pressure. Two-point discrimination is higher in areas of your body with many more sensory receptors. Most of us don't really appreciate the profound influence this change in density of receptors across our body's surface has on our experience of the world.

## [00:07:43] Pain & Pleasure, Subjective Interpretation

You've got sensors in the skin, and you've got a brain that's going to interpret what's going on with those sensors. Your subjective interpretation of what's happening has a profound influence on your experience of pleasure or pain. There are several main categories that impact these experiences: expectation, anxiety and arousal, how well you've slept, where you are in the 24-hour circadian cycle, and your genes. Our ability to tolerate pain changes dramatically across the 24-hour cycle — during daylight waking hours we are more resilient to pain and better able to experience pleasure, while at night the threshold for pain is much lower, especially in the hours between 2:00 AM and 5:00 AM. Pain threshold and how long a pain response lasts is also in part dictated by our genes. These factors — expectation, anxiety, sleep, circadian time, and genetics — together powerfully modulate our experience of both pleasure and pain in ways that allow us to dial up pleasure and dial down pain.

## [00:09:53] Tool: Pain & Expectation

There are now a number of solid experiments that point to the fact that if we know a painful stimulus is coming, we can better prepare for it mentally and therefore buffer or reduce the pain response. If subjects are warned that a painful stimulus is coming, their subjective experience of that pain is vastly reduced. However, if they are warned just two seconds before the pain arrives, it does not help — it actually makes it worse — because they can't do anything mentally to prepare in that brief window. Similarly, if warned two minutes before, it also makes it worse because expectation ramps up autonomic arousal, with all attention funneled toward the negative incoming experience.

So how soon before a painful stimulus should we know about it if the goal is to reduce pain? The answer is somewhere between 20 seconds and 40 seconds is about right. This illustrates clearly that it absolutely cannot be just the pattern of signals arriving from the skin — there has to be a subjective interpretation component, because we are all different in terms of our pain threshold.

## [00:11:31] Pain Threshold

Pain threshold has two dimensions. The first is the amount of mechanical, chemical, or thermal stimulation that it takes for you to say "I can't take that anymore." The second is how long the pain persists. Experiments at Stanford School of Medicine and elsewhere had subjects put their hand into a very cold vat of water, measuring how long they kept it in and then rating the pain on a scale of one to ten. That simple experiment revealed that people experience the same cold stimulus vastly differently — some rated it a ten out of ten extreme pain, others barely painful at all like a one, and others a three or a five. In fact, there is no objective measure of pain. Similarly, pleasure is something we all talk about, but we have no way of gauging what other people are experiencing except through what they report through language.

## [00:13:09] Heat & Cold, Tool: Moving into Cold or Hot Environments

We do have sensors in our skin that respond to heat and cold, and one of the best tests of how somebody can handle pain is to ask them to get into an ice bath. Some do it quickly, some slowly, and others find the experience of cold so aversive that they cannot get themselves in. The neurons that sense cold respond to relative drops in temperature — it's not about the absolute temperature of the water but about the relative change. Therefore, you can bypass these signals going up to the brain with each degree of change by simply getting in all at once.

Getting into cold water up to your neck is actually much more comfortable than being halfway in and halfway out, because of the difference in signals from the cold receptors on your upper torso versus your lower torso. Provided it is safe, getting into cold water is always going to be easier done quickly and up to your neck. Heat is the opposite — heat is measured in absolute terms by the neurons, so gradually moving into heat makes sense, finding the threshold that is safe and comfortable or at least resides within that realm of safety.

## [00:15:00] Subjective Pain, Psychosomatic, Fibromyalgia, Whole Body Pain, Acetyl-L-carnitine

Our experience of pain and the degree of damage to our body are not always correlated. A classic example published in the British Journal of Medicine involved a construction worker who fell and a nail went through his boot. He looked down, saw the nail going through his boot, and was in absolute excruciating pain. When they cut away the boot at the hospital, they revealed that the nail had passed between two of his toes — it had failed to impale his body in any way. The view and perception of that nail entering and exiting the boot was sufficient to create the experience of a nail through his foot, and the moment he realized it had not gone through his foot, the pain completely evaporated.

The so-called psychosomatic phenomenon is often misunderstood as meaning "all in one's head," but everything is neural. Whether pain comes from a gaping wound or cannot be explained by any injury — it's all neural. Fibromyalgia, or whole body pain, was written off for a long time, but there is now firm understanding of at least one of its bases: activation of a particular cell type called glia via the Toll-4 receptor. Treatments exist, including the prescription drug naltrexone at low doses, which can block Toll-4 receptors on glia. Another approach is acetyl-L-carnitine, which has evidence showing it can reduce symptoms of chronic whole-body pain and other forms of acute pain at dosages of one to four grams per day, taken orally in 500 mg capsules, with studies available on PubMed and examine.com. L-carnitine appears to impact multiple processes affecting pain and perhaps accelerating wound healing as well.

## [00:19:17] Acupuncture, Electroacupuncture, Pain Management

A completely non-drug, non-supplement approach to dealing with pain that has existed for thousands of years is acupuncture, and only recently has the Western scientific community started paying serious attention to it. Dr. Sean Mackey, director of the pain division at Stanford School of Medicine, noted that a fraction of people experience tremendous pain relief from acupuncture while others experience none at all. Qiufu Ma's laboratory at Harvard Medical School explored electroacupuncture — needles with electrical current passing through them.

What they found was that if electroacupuncture is provided to the abdomen, it creates activation of the sympathetic ganglia involving noradrenaline and neuropeptide Y, and this stimulation can be either anti-inflammatory or pro-inflammatory depending on whether the intensity is low or high — making it a precarious technique that may explain why some report relief and others do not. However, stimulation of the legs with electroacupuncture activated a neural circuit going from the legs up to an area at the base of the brain called the DMV, which activated the adrenal glands atop the kidneys, causing a release of catecholamines that were strongly anti-inflammatory. Electroacupuncture of the legs and feet, done correctly, can be anti-inflammatory, reduce symptoms of pain, and perhaps accelerate wound healing through activation of catecholaminergic pathways.

## [00:22:07] Red Heads & Pain Threshold, Endorphins

Redheads have a higher pain threshold than other individuals on average, and there is now a study examining this mechanistically. There is a gene called the MC1R gene that encodes for a number of different proteins, including those related to the production of melanin — which is why redheads often are fair-skinned, sometimes have freckles, and of course have red hair. This gene is associated with a pathway involving POMC (proopiomelanocortin), which is cleaved into different hormones: one that enhances pain perception — melanocyte-stimulating hormone — and one that blocks pain, beta endorphin.

Endorphins are endogenously made opioids that numb or reduce our perception of pain. We all have beta endorphins and POMC, but redheads make more of these endogenous endorphins. This does not mean redheads should be subjected to more pain — all it means is that their threshold for pain on average is shifted higher than that of other individuals.

## [00:24:02] Improving Pain Threshold, Dopamine

Pain threshold is something that can be built up, and certain patterns of thinking can allow us to buffer ourselves against the pain response. Certain forms of thinking are associated with the release of particular neuromodulators, in particular dopamine. Dopamine is a molecule associated with novelty, expectation, motivation, and reward. The ways in which dopamine can modulate pain are not mysterious — it is really through the activation of brain stem neurons that communicate with areas of our body that deploy immune cells. Neurons in our brain stem can be modulated by the release of dopamine, and those neurons control the release of immune cells from organs like the spleen. Those immune cells can then go combat infection.

When we're happy, we're better able to combat infection, deal with pain, and deal with all sorts of things — dopamine affects particular circuits in a very neurobiological and biochemical way, and tells those cells and circuits that conditions are good, allowing for more resilience.

## [00:25:30] Pleasure, Dopamine, Serotonin; Depression, Anti-depressants

With all the machinery related to pain, you might think our entire touch system is designed to detect pain and avoid tissue damage. But a good percentage of it is devoted to pleasure. Pleasure serves an adaptive role related to every species' primary goal: reproduction. That process is closely associated with the sensation and perception of pleasure, and it is no surprise that the highest density of sensory receptors is in and around the genitalia.

The currency of pleasure exists in multiple chemical systems, but the primary ones are the dopamine system — which handles the anticipation of pleasure and the work required to achieve it — and the serotonin system, which is more closely related to the immediate experience of pleasure. From dopamine and serotonin stem other hormones and molecules like oxytocin, associated with pair bonding and closely linked to the serotonin system. Those chemicals together create sensations of warmth, wellbeing, and safety. Dopamine is more closely associated with testosterone and other molecules involved with pursuit and further effort to get more of whatever causes more dopamine release.

If levels of serotonin and dopamine are too low, it becomes almost impossible to experience pleasure — a state called anhedonia, also described as depression. Antidepressants like Wellbutrin (bupropion) or SSRIs like Prozac and Zoloft raise dopamine and serotonin respectively, but not by increasing the acute peaks of those molecules — rather, they raise the overall tonic baseline levels. Think of it as a tide: your pleasure is like a boat, and it can't get out to sea unless the tide is high enough. These antidepressants have saved millions of lives, though they also have side effects and must be determined right for each individual — side effects can include reduced interest in food because why seek more if serotonin is already elevated.

## [00:29:41] Pleasure & Pain Balance, Dopamine, Addiction

You might want to be wary of any experience — chemical, physical, emotional, or some combination — that lets your dopamine go too high, and certainly you want to avoid it going too low. Every time the pleasure system is kicked into high gear — an absolutely spectacular event — there is a mirror-symmetric activation of the pain system. This might seem like an evil curse of biology, but it is actually a way to protect the whole system of reward and motivation. With repeated exposure to high levels of chemically induced dopamine peaks, those peaks start to go down and down in response to the same experience — we habituate or attenuate — and yet the pain side increases in size. This has a preservative function in keeping us safe.

What was just described is actually the basis of most, if not all, forms of addiction — something to be addressed in a future episode. The pleasure and pain systems are inextricably linked, and understanding their balance is key to maintaining healthy motivation, resilience, and wellbeing over the long term.

## [00:31:37] Recap & Key Takeaways

Today we talked about the pathways in the skin and in the brain, and elsewhere in the body that control our sense of pleasure and pain. We described a number of different tools ranging from different supplements to electroacupuncture and various other tools that one could use to modulate your sense of pleasure or pain. In thinking about pleasure, we have to think about the dopamine system and the serotonin system, and some of the related chemical systems. Understanding the general principles of how pleasure and pain work, how they interact, and the various cells and systems within the brain and body that allow them to occur and modulate them is the most important takeaway from this episode, as well as your subjective experience of pleasure and pain.
