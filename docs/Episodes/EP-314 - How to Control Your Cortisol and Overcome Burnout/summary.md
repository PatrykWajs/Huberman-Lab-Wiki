# Episode 314: How to Control Your Cortisol & Overcome Burnout

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman reframes cortisol not as a "stress hormone" but as an energy-deployment hormone — its primary role is releasing blood glucose to fuel the brain and body whenever energy is required, whether for stress, exercise, focus, or simply waking up. He walks through the full 24-hour cortisol rhythm (four distinct phases tied to sleep and waking), the HPA axis and SCN mechanisms that generate and regulate that rhythm, and a dense, actionable toolkit of behavioral, nutritional, and supplement-based protocols to keep cortisol high in the morning and low by evening. The episode maps two distinct burnout patterns — early-phase (high morning cortisol/anxiety, afternoon crash) and late-phase (low morning cortisol/brain fog, wired at night) — and prescribes opposite interventions for each. It closes with how cortisol rhythms flatten with age, menopause, and chronic illness, and why that flattening predicts reduced lifespan, cancer susceptibility, hippocampal degeneration, and impaired cognition.

## Key Takeaways

- **Cortisol is an energy-deployment hormone, not merely a stress hormone.** It releases glucose from liver and muscles into the bloodstream to fuel the brain and body; its benefits or harms depend almost entirely on *when* it is high or low across the 24-hour cycle. [00:03:16](transcript.md#000316-cortisol--directing-energy-glucose-adrenals)

- **The single most important rule: cortisol high shortly after waking, low in the hours before and during sleep.** Getting this rhythm correct reinforces every other health and performance metric. [00:01:29](transcript.md#000129-stress-tool-daily-cortisol-rhythm)

- **Cortisol follows four distinct phases across the 24-hour day.** Phase 1: minimal secretion from 4 h before until 2 h after lights-out. Phase 2: slight rise in hours 3–5 of sleep. Phase 3: rapid "main secretory" rise in hours 6–8 of sleep (only fully achieved with 8+ hours of sleep). Phase 4: the cortisol awakening response — a sharp rise starting before waking, with a 1–2 hour window to amplify it further. [00:10:14](transcript.md#001014-daily-cortisol-phases--rhythm-waking-up--cortisol)

- **The HPA axis governs cortisol synthesis via a negative feedback loop.** Hypothalamic PVN neurons release CRH → pituitary releases ACTH → adrenals synthesize and release cortisol → rising cortisol shuts down CRH production, preventing runaway levels. This same feedback explains why a high nighttime cortisol spike (from late exercise, stress, or bright light) blunts the next morning's cortisol peak. [00:17:55](transcript.md#001755-cortisol-release--regulation-hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal-hpa-axis)

- **Morning bright-light exposure is the single most powerful morning cortisol anchor.** The SCN uses a parallel splanchnic-nerve pathway to drive cortisol release in the first 1–3 hours after waking; sunlight or a 10,000-lux lamp can boost cortisol by up to 50% and is clinically equivalent to light therapy used for seasonal affective disorder. [00:28:42](transcript.md#002842-bright-light--cortisol-release-tool-increase-morning-cortisol--sunlight)

- **Delaying caffeine 60–90 minutes after waking reduces afternoon energy crashes.** Chronic caffeine users get little cortisol spike from caffeine, but caffeine extends cortisol's active duration. Taking it later flattens the mid-morning cortisol decline and prevents a steep afternoon drop. Caffeine cutoff should be around 2 p.m. for most people. [00:41:44](transcript.md#004144-increase-morning-cortisol-tools-hydration-delaying-caffeine-intake)

- **Exercise timing acts as a powerful cortisol entrainment cue via the SCN.** Exercising within the same 2–3 hour window each day conditions an anticipatory cortisol rise (Pavlovian-style). Morning exercise is ideal; high-intensity exercise after sundown can triple or quadruple cortisol at exactly the wrong time, blunting next-morning cortisol via negative feedback. [00:49:30](transcript.md#004930-exercise-entrainment-cues--cortisol-rhythm-tool-boost-energy--exercise-schedule)

- **Deliberate cold exposure raises catecholamines but does NOT chronically elevate cortisol when done regularly.** Used occasionally (≤2×/week), it produces a meaningful cortisol spike; done habitually, the cortisol effect disappears while the long-arc dopamine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine benefits persist. Evidence does NOT support the claim that cold exposure is differentially harmful for women vs. men. [00:57:52](transcript.md#005752-does-deliberate-cold-exposure-increase-cortisol-energy--mood)

- **Grapefruit and black licorice extend cortisol life through enzyme inhibition — morning-only tools.** Grapefruit compounds inhibit CYP3A4, extending cortisol duration by 25–50%. Glycyrrhizin in black licorice inhibits 11β-HSD2, preventing cortisol-to-cortisone conversion. Both also extend caffeine duration. Avoid both in the evening. Contraindicated with hypertension or pregnancy. [01:02:51](transcript.md#010251-increase-morning-cortisol--nutrition-grapefruit-black-licorice)

- **Evening bright artificial light (especially short-wavelength blue/white LEDs) causes large cortisol spikes.** Because basal evening cortisol is very low, the negative feedback loop is primed — even moderate bright light can trigger a large relative increase, explaining why people are more emotionally reactive at night. Dim, warm-colored (amber/red) lighting prevents this. [01:14:30](transcript.md#011430-lower-evening-cortisol-tools-dim-lights-light-color)

- **Evening starchy carbohydrates lower cortisol by elevating blood glucose.** Cortisol's job is to mobilize glucose; when blood glucose is elevated post-meal, cortisol release is suppressed via hypothalamic glucose sensing. Low-carb dieters see a transient cortisol rise for the first ~3 weeks that then normalizes. [01:20:54](transcript.md#012054-lower-evening-cortisol-tools-caffeine-timing-stress-response--exhales-starchy-carbohydrates)

- **Ashwagandha (300–600 mg evening) reduces cortisol 11–29%; apigenin (50 mg) and magnesium threonate (300–400 mg) augment it.** Together they form Huberman's personal sleep stack. Ashwagandha must NOT be taken at high doses in the morning — it will suppress the necessary morning cortisol peak. Supplements should augment, not replace, behavioral tools. [01:44:32](transcript.md#014432-supplements-to-reduce-cortisol-ashwagandha-apigenin-magnesium)

- **Burnout presents in two distinct cortisol-driven patterns requiring opposite interventions.** Pattern 1 (early-phase): cortisol rises too fast → morning anxiety/waking too early, afternoon crash. Pattern 2 (late-phase): blunted morning cortisol → brain fog/low energy all day, wired and unable to sleep at night. Cushing's and Addison's disease are separate medical conditions requiring physician evaluation. [01:50:57](transcript.md#015057-burnout-cushings--addisons-2-burnout-patterns)

- **NSDR/Yoga Nidra (10–30 min) is the primary early-phase burnout tool; physiological sighs (3–5 min) are the fastest real-time reset.** NSDR slows the steepness of the morning cortisol rise and shifts the acrophase later. Physiological sighs (clinically validated by Huberman and David Spiegel at Stanford) lower heart rate, raise heart rate variability, and improve sleep quality. [01:55:23](transcript.md#015523-early-phase-burnout-tools-nsdryoga-nidra-boost-morning-cortisol-caffeine)

- **After age 40, cortisol rhythms flatten in both sexes; this flattening predicts reduced lifespan and cancer survivability.** The bigger the morning peak and the steeper the afternoon decline, the better outcomes in cancer patients. Elevated evening cortisol also causes hippocampal neuron degeneration — impairing memory and the hippocampus's own role in contextual stress regulation, creating a vicious cycle. [02:08:02](transcript.md#020802-age-male-vs-females-lifespan-cancer-menopause-brain-health)

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight / Bright Light Exposure
- **What:** View outdoor sunlight or a 10,000-lux light box within 30 minutes of waking.
- **How:** Go outside (even on cloudy days — more important on cloudy days due to lower overall luminance); take off sunglasses (eyeglasses/contacts are fine). Look toward the direction of the sun; blink as needed. If indoors, use a ~$100 10,000-lux lamp at eye level.
- **When:** As early as possible after waking; the first 1–2 hours are the only window where the SCN can use bright light to drive additional cortisol release. This is the foundational protocol.
- **Ref:** [00:28:42](transcript.md#002842-bright-light--cortisol-release-tool-increase-morning-cortisol--sunlight)

### Hydration Upon Waking
- **What:** Drink 16–32 oz of water immediately after waking; can include electrolytes.
- **How:** Plain water or electrolyte mix (Huberman uses LMNT); do this before or alongside morning light exposure.
- **When:** First thing in the morning. Acts on adrenals to modestly increase pulsatile cortisol and epinephrine release. Even mild dehydration impairs cognitive and physical function.
- **Ref:** [00:41:44](transcript.md#004144-increase-morning-cortisol-tools-hydration-delaying-caffeine-intake)

### Caffeine Delay (60–90 Minutes After Waking)
- **What:** Postpone first coffee or tea until 60–90 minutes after waking.
- **How:** Let the cortisol awakening response peak first. Caffeine then flattens the cortisol decline slope into early afternoon, reducing the crash. Cut off caffeine around 2 p.m. (adjust earlier if sleep-sensitive).
- **When:** Daily for habitual caffeine users experiencing afternoon energy crashes. Those who must have caffeine immediately should stack other morning cortisol tools aggressively to compensate.
- **Ref:** [00:41:44](transcript.md#004144-increase-morning-cortisol-tools-hydration-delaying-caffeine-intake)

### Morning Exercise Timing
- **What:** Perform resistance or cardiovascular exercise within the first 3 hours of waking.
- **How:** Any modality at familiar intensity. Timing regularity matters more than modality — train within the same 2–3 hour window each day to build an SCN-driven anticipatory cortisol rise. Avoid high-intensity exercise after sundown.
- **When:** 4–6 days per week; at least one rest day. Afternoon or early evening exercise is acceptable if followed by a post-workout cortisol cooldown protocol.
- **Ref:** [00:49:30](transcript.md#004930-exercise-entrainment-cues--cortisol-rhythm-tool-boost-energy--exercise-schedule)

### Deliberate Cold Exposure (Occasional Use)
- **What:** Cold shower or cold plunge to boost epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine, with a transient cortisol spike.
- **How:** 1–5 minutes in uncomfortably cold but safe water. Morning use preferred.
- **When:** No more than 1–2 times per week if the goal is cortisol elevation; habitual use (>2×/week) eliminates the cortisol effect while preserving the long-arc dopamine and catecholamine benefits.
- **Ref:** [00:57:52](transcript.md#005752-does-deliberate-cold-exposure-increase-cortisol-energy--mood)

### Grapefruit / Grapefruit Juice (Morning Cortisol Extender)
- **What:** Inhibits CYP3A4 enzyme that breaks down cortisol, extending active duration by 25–50%. Also extends caffeine duration via the same pathway.
- **How:** Eat one whole grapefruit (pink or yellow) or drink 6–8 oz of grapefruit juice in the morning.
- **When:** Morning only. Avoid in the evening. Significant interactions with medications metabolized by CYP3A4; avoid if on corticosteroid medications or if chronically elevated cortisol is a concern.
- **Ref:** [01:02:51](transcript.md#010251-increase-morning-cortisol--nutrition-grapefruit-black-licorice)

### Black Licorice / Glycyrrhizin (Morning Cortisol Booster)
- **What:** Glycyrrhizin inhibits 11β-HSD2, preventing cortisol conversion to inactive cortisone, potently increasing active circulating cortisol.
- **How:** Small amounts of real black licorice candy or low-dose licorice root extract (start at the lowest available dose). Must be black licorice — red licorice does not contain glycyrrhizin.
- **When:** Morning only; useful on days when exercise or sunlight is not accessible. Avoid with hypertension, pregnancy, or breastfeeding due to potent blood pressure effects.
- **Ref:** [01:02:51](transcript.md#010251-increase-morning-cortisol--nutrition-grapefruit-black-licorice)

### Dim Lights & Warm Light Color in the Evening
- **What:** Reduce overhead/bright lighting and avoid short-wavelength (blue/white LED) light starting ~2 hours before bed.
- **How:** Turn off overhead lights; use floor or table lamps. Switch to amber or red-spectrum bulbs. Dim phone and computer screens; use Apple triple-click shortcut to enable red-screen mode. Use blue-light-blocking glasses (Huberman uses Roka Wndow glasses) when lighting environment cannot be controlled.
- **When:** Starting at or before sundown; critical from ~2 hours after sundown onward. Viewing actual sunset outdoors can calibrate retinal sensitivity so indoor artificial light has less impact on melatonin suppression.
- **Ref:** [01:14:30](transcript.md#011430-lower-evening-cortisol-tools-dim-lights-light-color)

### Physiological Sigh (Real-Time Stress & Cortisol Reset)
- **What:** Double inhale through the nose followed by a long, complete exhale through the mouth — fastest known tool to reduce sympathetic activation in real time.
- **How:** Full inhale through nose → second short sharp sniff through nose to maximally inflate all lung alveoli → long slow exhale through mouth until lungs are completely empty. Repeat 1–3 times for acute stress; do continuously for 3–5 minutes as a deliberate evening protocol for late-phase burnout. Validated in a clinical trial with David Spiegel (Stanford) to lower heart rate, raise HRV, and improve mood and sleep.
- **When:** Any moment of acute stress; evening and nighttime especially important. For late-phase burnout, use as a daily 3–5 minute protocol.
- **Ref:** [01:20:54](transcript.md#012054-lower-evening-cortisol-tools-caffeine-timing-stress-response--exhales-starchy-carbohydrates)

### Evening Starchy Carbohydrates
- **What:** Eat starchy carbohydrates (rice, potatoes, yams, pasta, oatmeal) with the evening meal to suppress cortisol release via elevated blood glucose.
- **How:** Include a moderate serving at dinner. Avoid eating within 2 hours of sleep ideally (impairs growth hormone release in early slow-wave sleep). A small starchy snack closer to bed is preferable to gnawing hunger that prevents sleep.
- **When:** Last meal of the day, ideally 2–4 hours before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:20:54](transcript.md#012054-lower-evening-cortisol-tools-caffeine-timing-stress-response--exhales-starchy-carbohydrates)

### Post-Late-Workout Cortisol Cooldown
- **What:** Structured protocol to bring cortisol back down after evening exercise (which can triple or quadruple cortisol at a time when basal levels are very low).
- **How:** After workout: 2–3 minutes of long-exhale breathing in the car or at home. Hot shower or sauna — not cold shower (cold spikes cortisol further). Eat starchy carbohydrates plus quality protein. Dim all lights immediately on arriving home. Avoid phone/screen use before bed.
- **When:** Any time high-intensity exercise occurs after sundown or within ~4 hours of intended sleep time.
- **Ref:** [01:35:30](transcript.md#013530-evening-exercise--cortisol-tool-spike-your-morning-cortisol)

### Ashwagandha (Evening Cortisol Reduction)
- **What:** Adaptogenic herb shown in clinical trials to reduce cortisol by 11–29%.
- **How:** 300–600 mg extract (KSM-66 or similar) in the evening — with or after dinner. Do NOT take ≥300 mg in the morning; it will suppress the necessary morning cortisol peak. Low doses (<50–100 mg) found in morning supplement blends are too small to have significant cortisol impact.
- **When:** Evening/nighttime only. Use only after behavioral tools (light, exercise, nutrition, breathing) are already in place.
- **Ref:** [01:44:32](transcript.md#014432-supplements-to-reduce-cortisol-ashwagandha-apigenin-magnesium)

### Apigenin (Evening Cortisol & Sleep)
- **What:** Flavonoid derived from chamomile; acts on GABA and chloride-channel pathways to reduce cortisol and promote sleep. Also a mild aromatase inhibitor.
- **How:** 50 mg capsule in the evening, ~30–60 minutes before intended sleep. Part of Huberman's personal sleep stack (apigenin + magnesium threonate + theanine), taken nightly for 8+ years.
- **When:** 30–60 minutes before sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:44:32](transcript.md#014432-supplements-to-reduce-cortisol-ashwagandha-apigenin-magnesium)

### Magnesium Threonate or Bisglycinate (Evening)
- **What:** Magnesium supports sleep quality and may indirectly activate pathways that further suppress cortisol. Threonate has better CNS penetration; bisglycinate is interchangeable for sleep.
- **How:** 300–400 mg magnesium threonate or bisglycinate, ~30–60 minutes before sleep.
- **When:** Evening only, alongside apigenin.
- **Ref:** [01:44:32](transcript.md#014432-supplements-to-reduce-cortisol-ashwagandha-apigenin-magnesium)

### NSDR / Yoga Nidra (Early-Phase Burnout Recovery)
- **What:** Non-sleep deep rest — guided body-scan relaxation that reduces cortisol, shifts the morning cortisol acrophase later, and trains voluntary parasympathetic engagement. Studies using 11-minute sessions show significant cortisol reduction.
- **How:** Use a 10–30 minute NSDR or Yoga Nidra audio track (free on YouTube/Spotify/Apple; Huberman provides his own and recommends Kelly Boy's recordings). Lie still and follow the guided script. Set an alarm if doing it immediately upon waking to avoid oversleeping. Falling back asleep is acceptable.
- **When:** Immediately upon waking for Pattern 1 burnout (to reduce the excessively steep cortisol rise). Midday as a general recovery and cortisol-modulating tool.
- **Ref:** [01:55:23](transcript.md#015523-early-phase-burnout-tools-nsdryoga-nidra-boost-morning-cortisol-caffeine)

### Late-Phase Burnout Protocol
- **What:** Multi-tool intervention for Pattern 2 burnout (blunted morning cortisol, wired and sleepless at night). The core fix is lowering evening cortisol so morning levels can recover.
- **How:** In the 6 hours before intended sleep: avoid caffeine; dim all lights and avoid bright screens; take a relaxing (not cold) shower or use sauna if available; do 3–5 minutes of physiological sighs; eat starchy carbohydrates with dinner; take ashwagandha (300–600 mg) and apigenin (50 mg) in the evening. View late-day sunlight (sunset walk) to calibrate retinal sensitivity. On the following morning, aggressively stack all morning cortisol boosters.
- **When:** Sustained effort over days to weeks required to reset the rhythm. Start where pain is greatest (evening vs. morning) and expand outward.
- **Ref:** [02:01:35](transcript.md#020135-late-phase-burnout-tools-reduce-evening-cortisol)
