## [00:02:50] The Power of Mindset on Stress

The first audience question asks about the latest findings on the physiological mechanisms behind stress's impact on the body and brain, and practical tools or techniques for managing stress effectively.

I think one of the most interesting findings in the field of stress in the last five years — maybe even three years — is the work from my colleague Ali Crum at Stanford. She works on mindsets. Stanford students come into the laboratory and view a roughly five-minute movie about how awful stress is for the mind and body — all the things it does like deplete your immune system, make you miserable, deplete certain aspects of the reproductive axis, and on and on. Then a separate group comes in and watches a video — also five minutes, also factually true — about all the things that stress can do to enhance performance both cognitively and physically, like additional energy, additional cognitive power, access to certain memory sets albeit narrow memory sets, and so on. What you find is that the results point directly to the fact that whatever you believe about stress, provided the information you have is true, is what happens. So if I tell you that stress improves your memory, focus, and attention, one observes that. If I tell you that stress depletes your immune system, one observes that.

This is something that we don't quite yet understand as neuroscientists — the psychology of it makes more sense frankly than the mechanisms — but it's becoming very clear that what we believe about a given phenomenon strongly impacts how it shapes our response to that. You can't simply delete information about stress being bad for you. But if you want stress to be enhancing — there's literally now a concept called the stress-is-enhancing mindset — the thing you can do is to learn more about how stress can be enhancing. We're not talking about lying, we're not talking about placebo effect; we're talking about real knowledge based in fact that one can absorb. I find it amazing and wonderful that the mere learning of something can actually change how we respond to something at a core physiological level.

## [00:05:23] David Goggins: A Case Study in Resilience

The second important set of findings on stress relates to a structure that I've talked about recently on the podcast and that I talked about with the one and only David Goggins. That guy is every bit as intense as he comes across. I met him for the first time in 2016 at a gathering in Silicon Valley. At the end of the day he leaves for a minute, changes into his shorts and shirt, and says he's going running to the airport. We were about 14 miles from the airport — and he had his bags. I thought: this guy is nuts, and I love him. I think one reason we love Rick Rubin and David Goggins is they truly are different — they just do what they're going to do and trust that they're doing right for themselves and for the people around them.

There's this structure in our brain — and these are recent discoveries, not from my lab — made by a colleague of mine at Stanford, Joe Parvizi, who's in the department of neurosurgery. He's made beautiful discoveries about the anterior mid-cingulate cortex. When Joe put a little stimulating electrode into this area — because he had patients who needed neurosurgery — and they probe around asking questions like "what do you feel?" and "what are you going to do?", sometimes they hit an area and the person says they feel like they're about to go into a rage — and they back off. But stimulate the anterior mid-cingulate cortex and the patient says they feel like they're heading into a storm — and they say "no but I'm ready, I'm leaning in." Another patient, stimulate their anterior mid-cingulate cortex, and they say they feel like they're going to get up out of their chair and do something really, really difficult.

Across multiple people you're seeing the same general kind of forward, center-of-mass response — leaning into challenge. And there are now scores of studies in the last three to five years showing that people who successfully overcome a challenge of any kind — fitness challenge, cognitive challenge — their anterior mid-cingulate cortex expands or at least increases its baseline levels of activity. People who fail to meet that challenge show less anterior mid-cingulate cortex activity. There's bidirectionality of the response. It seems that doing things that are difficult — that we don't enjoy, or that we have to push ourselves to do — grow and enhance the activity within this anterior mid-cingulate cortex. And the beauty of it is that it generalizes: the growth of the anterior mid-cingulate cortex can be directed towards lots of different things.

So I think this is a call to seek pleasure, seek comfort, seek relaxation, seek sleep every night, seek sunlight in the morning — but also deliberately seek out challenges. The importance of doing hard things in a psychologically and physically safe manner is truly beneficial toward our ability to manage ourselves in what would otherwise be called stress.

## [00:09:59] Exploring Time Perception & Frame Rate

Time perception is something I'm as obsessed by as I am by many other topics. I've always been struck by the observation — certainly not uniquely mine — that if you're sitting waiting for an appointment at the doctor's office, time goes by really slowly. Whereas if you have a really full day with lots of activities, time seems to go by really fast. Which means that our frame rate on life is highly dynamic, and in fact it is set by our visual system — at least for sighted folks.

It is a fact that when we focus on things up close — think of a watchmaker, think about looking at your phone — our perception of time is more fine-grained. Our frame rate is higher: more frames per second than when we view things at a distance. When we think about time-space coding in the brain, we need to anchor ourselves to something. Our visual system is the way we anchor ourselves in time. We have knowledge about past, present, and future, but at an unconscious level we need to set our frame rate.

If you go down to Bondi and you lie back and you look up at the clouds moving in an unpredictable way — whenever we're looking at a landscape with some lack of predictable features like waves or rustling trees — you're not really in a mode of trying to anticipate just how far the next thing will be. Your level of autonomic arousal goes down and your frame rate expands. Your perception of the passage of time changes. This is why I love aquariums: you zone out, the fish and the kelp move slowly, and every once in a while a whale shark goes through and snaps you to attention and then disappears. It's immensely relaxing. It slows your frame rate down and resets you.

Whereas when we're texting, typing, scrolling social media — our frame rate is high. Social media is tuned to a frame rate that keeps us engaged just up to the point where we want to swipe to the next thing. The algorithms are designed that way.

Frame rate is also set by where you're looking. The further out you're looking, the larger the time bins you're capturing — less resolution. Closer in, and the more you're trying to predict the next outcome — what we call duration, path, and outcome — what's going to happen, for how long, and what follows — the higher the frame rate goes. Hitchcock understood this: there's a movie of his in which the sun rises and sets in the background and by the end of the 90-minute film you feel as though a full 24 hours has passed, because of the way frame rate is manipulated throughout.

Psychedelics will distort time perception mainly through the deployment of large amounts of the neuromodulator serotonin, which is intimately involved in clock perception mechanisms. The 40 Hz tones that can be valuable for cognitive work tend to entrain certain circuits within the brain for the kinds of tasks we typically call "work" — logical, sequential thinking — very different from writing music or coming up with poetry, where a lower frame rate, broader time bins, and more expansive gaze might actually be more useful.

## [00:18:20] Jet Lag Protocol: Adjusting to New Time Zones

An audience member asks about the jet lag protocol I followed when arriving in Sydney. This was actually a little bit easier for me because it's just a full day ahead from California. Nonetheless I suffered tremendously from jet lag earlier in my life. Once in 2017 I went to Abu Dhabi — a 12-hour flip from the Bay Area — and I was a wreck. I barely made it to the meeting. I slept great the first night and then just didn't sleep for two days.

Here are a few key points. The key is to find your temperature minimum. Ask yourself what time you normally wake up without an alarm. Let's say for you it's 7 a.m. Subtract two hours from that: your temperature minimum is approximately 5 a.m. This is the single most important reference point for shifting your clock.

If you expose your eyes — not your skin, but your eyes — to bright light in the two to three hours prior to your temperature minimum, you phase-delay your clock: you push back your wake time and sleep time by about an hour. If you view bright light in the two to three hours after your temperature minimum, you phase-advance your clock: you wake up earlier and go to sleep earlier by about an hour.

You might wonder: if I get morning sunlight every day, why don't I keep waking up earlier and earlier? Because in the afternoon when you get sunlight in your eyes, you phase-delay your clock a little bit, and as a consequence you wake up and go to sleep at more or less the same time every day. It's an amazing balancing mechanism. Getting sunlight in the middle of the day doesn't shift your circadian clock at all — that period is called the circadian dead zone.

So when you land in a new location, ask yourself what your body thinks the time is back home. If it's before your temperature minimum by home reckoning, don't get bright light — you'll delay your clock in the wrong direction. If it's after your temperature minimum, go outside and get light in your eyes — you'll advance your clock and get onto local time faster. Sometimes you'll want to wear sunglasses and a brimmed hat for the first few hours after landing to avoid accidentally shifting in the wrong direction. Use this strategy for the first three days, and you can shift very fast.

It also helps to eat on the local schedule — food is another zeitgeber, a timekeeper for the circadian clock. Social rhythms and physical activity also help. But the temperature minimum and the role of light before or after it is the heavy hammer in this whole process.

In the evenings I also do a lot of red light — just an inexpensive party-style red light bulb. Viewing red or dim light in the hour or two before sleep is known to reduce cortisol levels, compared to standard white or blue-white lighting. Rick Rubin actually has his house on all red lights at night — no artificial white lighting past sundown.

## [00:26:44] The Science of Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the nervous system's ability to change itself in response to experience — and it's something all of us retain throughout the lifespan. The key thing to understand is that the signal for neuroplasticity — the signal that tells the brain rewiring is needed — is not focus itself, but rather the chemical release that accompanies the experience of agitation and frustration during a challenging task. Adrenaline and norepinephrine released during struggle flag the nervous system that something important is happening and that rewiring should occur. The actual rewiring happens during sleep and rest.

## [00:26:49] The Transformative Power of Psychedelics

Just a few years ago I was too frightened to talk about psychedelics publicly. These are still scheduled drugs in the United States, although they are being explored for therapeutic reasons — mainly for the treatment of severe depression, smoking cessation, and eating disorders. Anorexia nervosa still has the highest morbidity of any psychiatric challenge, and there's a real need for treatments that work.

Things like psilocybin, LSD to some extent, and MDMA — which technically is an empathogen rather than a classic psychedelic — have been explored quite extensively in the last few years, and I've completely ramped my stance on them for a couple of reasons. As a teenager I explored these things and I don't recommend that at all. The adolescent brain is highly plastic — being a teenager is already a psychedelic experience and you do not need to add pharmacology on top of it. That said, these compounds clearly have their role.

A couple of things have changed my stance. First, there are a lot of federally funded studies taking place at Stanford and elsewhere. Second, psychedelics are no longer associated with counterculture the way they once were — they are now heavily associated with veterans groups using them for PTSD, including ibogaine, which is a 22-hour psychedelic journey where you close your eyes and get real-life recollections of your experiences with agency inside those experiences. That work is being led by my colleague Nolan Williams at Stanford.

## [00:29:26] Exploring Psilocybin & MDMA: Personal Experiences & Insights

Psilocybin, if you look at it chemically, looks a lot like serotonin — it binds nearly selectively to a specific serotonin receptor and seems to create more resting-state lateral connectivity, meaning more brain areas talking to other brain areas after the psilocybin journey compared to before.

I have done psilocybin as an adult — as part of a clinical trial — and participated in an MDMA trial as well. These journeys can be terrifying while they're happening. The second time around I thought: okay, I've done this, this time is going to be better. It was still terrifying. But I learned a lot. There does seem to be an anti-depressant effect.

What seems to happen with these compounds is that they allow us to see relationships between events of past and present and anticipate certain changes into the future, while experiencing the fullness of the emotionality of those experiences in real time. In talk therapy, unless you happen to be speaking to your therapist in a moment when something is really acute and hitting you hard, it's difficult to experience the fullness of an issue while simultaneously parsing it cognitively. Psychedelics and MDMA seem to allow people to get into the full amplitude — maybe even an enhanced amplitude — emotionality of an experience while simultaneously reflecting on it. With the help of a therapist or guide, this leads to specific actionable insights and outcomes.

MDMA dramatically increases serotonin and dopamine, but it seems to be the serotonergic effect that is responsible for most of its therapeutic effect. MDMA — methylene dioxy methamphetamine — is not necessarily bad just because of that name. And contrary to what was once claimed, it does not appear to be neurotoxic in appropriate dosage ranges. The paper claiming neurotoxicity was retracted — the researchers had accidentally been giving subjects methamphetamine instead of MDMA. Nobody talks about that retraction, but it is in the literature.

MDMA's empathogenic component is particularly interesting for PTSD because ultimately PTSD is really about developing empathy for oneself and resolving a core confusion in the nervous system about responsibility. At an unconscious level, trauma seems to scramble the sense of who is responsible for what happened — even when someone knows intellectually that they were the victim. MDMA seems to be able to short-circuit that confusion through a pathway of self-empathy. Self-empathy deserves far more exploration in the years to come.

## [00:36:12] The Science of Sleep: How Temperature Affects It

An audience member mentions getting into the sauna two hours before going to sleep really improves their sleep quality, and asks what's going on.

The relationship between temperature and sleep is well established. To fall asleep you need to cool down by 1 to 3 degrees; to wake up you need to heat up by about 1 to 3 degrees. When you get into a sauna or take a hot bath, or even wash your hands and face with warm water in the evening, you actually end up cooling yourself off — because of the way the body thermoregulates. The brain area that controls thermoregulation is the medial preoptic area, which operates like a thermostat. If you warm the external portion of the body, the brain then has to cool your core body temperature. That doesn't happen right away, but it happens as you get out of the sauna — you've warmed up, which allows you to cool down internally, and then you're able to fall asleep and stay more deeply asleep.

Matt Walker put it well: "Warm up to cool down to fall asleep. Stay cool to stay asleep. Warm up to wake up." The same mechanism works with a cold plunge — the surface of your body gets cold, the thermal receptors transmit information to the medial preoptic area, and your core body temperature eventually increases. All roads lead to the same thermoregulatory mechanism.

## [00:39:38] Understanding Stress Response & Habituation

An audience member asks: if we expose ourselves to the same stress over and over again, do we release the same amount of adrenaline, and does the body become less responsive to it?

Typically you'd release less and less adrenaline with repeated exposure — this is habituation. This relates to an important fact about the amygdala, which means "almond" — it happens to be shaped like one. People associate the amygdala with threat detection, but it's actually more of a novelty detector. Anytime we experience something novel, we have an elevated level of autonomic arousal. Like earlier tonight before the show, there was a repeating sound. The first time I heard it I was startled — by the fifth time I was barely registering it. If we had recorded from my amygdalae, you'd find that the first time there was a big spike in activity, then lesser and lesser with each repetition. That's habituation.

However, if with each subsequent exposure the stressor is decreasing your life satisfaction or increasing your psychological stress — something you can't stand or that genuinely threatens you — the response might go in the opposite direction. Context and personal relevance determine whether you habituate or sensitize.

When your activation state is too high — you're primed to go like a sprinter at the blocks — you can dispel some of that energy by tapping your foot or doing some other rhythmic activity with another part of your body. That clears the excess anticipatory drive and lets you do fine work.

## [00:47:00] Finding Your Passion: Advice for the Youth

A 17-year-old audience member asks for advice on finding their passion.

I think your passion is rooted in a feeling state that you've already accessed — hopefully many times, but at least once — earlier in your life. At some point, for whatever reason or circumstances, you weren't thinking about what your parents wanted you to do, or what was cool or not cool in school. You were in a pure feeling state of "yum — that's really cool" — a kind of beholding. I can't answer the question of what that thing is for you, but I'll tell you: if you spend some time in your memory banks, you'll be able to remember a feeling. Maybe the feeling was about a board game you played, or something you observed, or maybe it just arose through some activity entirely unrelated to the thing that actually generated it.

Here's where it gets tricky: sometimes we think it's the activity, but it's not the activity. What we're really chasing is a feeling of delight in something that is very personal — in fact, very unique to you — to the extent that I believe it's not capable of being created by anybody else. Feedback from other people about what we should do or what we're good at is merely a calibration point on a compass. Someone says "maybe you should try this" and you go "hmm" — or you go "yuck" — those are calibration signals pointing you back toward that feeling state.

The good news is that you're 17, your brain is still highly plastic. The good news for all of us is that we are capable of neuroplasticity and introspection throughout the lifespan. Even if you can't remember, you can sense — and what you're sensing is how physical information in your environment converts into electrical and chemical signals inside you. Something about the way you are wired leads you to say "yes, that, more of that." If you can sense into what it is that gets you going in that direction, I really believe you can find your unique gifts.
