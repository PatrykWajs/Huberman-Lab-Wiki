**Ari Wallach:** It's a great series of questions. One of the things that Homo sapiens do extremely well is what we call mental time travel. We're able to actually take ourselves in the current moment and project out. In fact, Marty Seligman — kind of the father of positive psychology — put forth this idea in his great book *Homo Prospectus* that what separates us out from almost every other species is that we do two things extremely well: we can do mental time travel toward the future — we can think about different possible outcomes and different possible scenarios — and we can collaborate to make the ones that we want to see manifest. That involves language, that involves social interaction, a whole bunch of other things. But at the end of the day, what we do extremely well — and as far as we know we're the only ones who do it — is to project out into futures that we want.

We know where this comes from; it's coming from the hippocampus. One thing about the hippocampus that's amazing is that it's almost atemporal — it doesn't actually have a time stamp. And so what it does is it takes snapshots of episodic memories that have happened in the past, reassembles them so that we can mentally time travel, and then figures out these different future scenarios of what might happen.

So if we take Ari and Andy 150,000 years ago — he calls me Andy, folks — if Ari and Andy are out on the Serengeti 150,000 years ago, we Homo sapiens about 200,000 years ago were kind of starting to spread out of the rift valley into Africa. We were now within a small tribal structure, wanting to hunt larger and larger game. We were no longer reactive — if we wanted to go after that game, it's not a foregone conclusion that it would do what we wanted it to do. We had to start thinking about different scenarios. That first kind of mental time travel is really coming from our desire for more protein, to exist and to grow the group — to feed the super energy-intensive thing called the human brain.

Now fast forward: 20,000 years ago our ancestors crossed Beringia — now the Bering Strait — and we're in North America. Fast forward to right now: on my way in here I get a notification on my phone — *ding* — and I immediately pick up the phone to see what that new information is. So we're working on two- to three-hundred-thousand-year-old hardware while at the same time we have a cultural substrate that has, for lack of better words, hacked into that older part of us to make us want immediate gratification and force us to react in a way where that mental time travel has closed the temporal horizon. We're no longer training ourselves to think about the far future — we're actually training ourselves to think about the immediate present. And I don't mean present in a Buddhist way; I mean presentism as in a hall of mirrors: there is no past, there is no future, there's only this moment.

## [00:22:00] Tool: Empathy for Others

## [00:26:09] Empathy for Future Generations, Emotion & Logic

## [01:09:23] Longpath, Telos, Time Perception

**Ari Wallach:** The work that I do — the mindset I am advocating for — I call Longpath. Longpath sits on three pillars. One: *transgenerational empathy* — empathy with yourself, empathy with the past, and empathy with the future. The second pillar: *futures thinking* — you'll notice it's "futures" with an S, as opposed to the singular future, because we often think of the future as a noun, something out there, as opposed to what the future really is, which is a verb, something that we do. The final pillar — and the most difficult for us to wrap our head around — is this idea of *telos* — ultimate aim, ultimate goal. What are we here for?

We all suffer from what I call lifespan bias. The most important unit of time to Andrew Huberman is from your birth to your death. We're all wired that way. But the reality is that for our species, going back hundreds of thousands of years, these things actually overlap — I come from my parents, I am here, and now I have children. These are not distinct units; there are massive overlaps in terms of the culture, the emotional, the psychology of what I got from them and what I'm giving to my kids.

What ends up happening in a lifespan-biased society — the one we exist in right now — is we have lost the telos. We have lost the ultimate aim or goal or purpose for our species, for our civilization on this planet. When you don't have that, and God is no longer in the picture and religion is no longer in the picture, we flounder. We're looking for metrics to judge: am I doing the right thing? Do I matter? Will people know who I am 200 years from now? Is my sense of purpose connected to anything larger?

Most mammals have about a million years from when they rise up to when they go extinct. We're in the first third of this ball game. That's reassuring.

**Andrew Huberman:** You're one of the first people to really call attention to the fact that the human brain can expand or contract its time perception — and we do this all day long. High stress, high excitement: life and thinking shrinks the aperture, makes us very good at dealing with things in the present, get to the next day or the next hour. This is the opposite of what the Buddhists traditionally said, which was to be present in order to see the timelessness. When we are stressed, the horizon gets right up close. Is there a way to leverage the immediacy of our experience to actually create useful tools for the future?

## [01:23:02] Tool: Visualizing Future You

**Andrew Huberman:** The problem with charts or photos is they're not in the forefront of our consciousness throughout the day. **Ari Wallach:** One of the people on my Advisory Board is Hal Hershfield — a smart, great guy at UCLA — who does a lot of future-self work. He put a bunch of people into an fMRI to see where the flow is and asked a series of questions: think about yourself right now — one part of your brain lit up. Think about a celebrity — another part lit up. Think about yourself 10 years from now — and the part of the brain that lit up was the same part as for the celebrity. You had a vague idea of who future-you was, but you weren't connected to them. They were like a stranger.

He took one group and put them in a 3D virtual reality: as you walk across a room, you look in a mirror and see a photo of you aged 10 years. **Andrew Huberman:** This notion of protocol for imagining future self — not as a way to scare yourself into better health habits, although if it works, great — but as a way to get your mind into the reality that if you survive you're going to get older by definition, and that person needs care. ## [01:30:17] Death, Western Society

**Ari Wallach:** Let's talk about death. One of the things that keeps us from thinking about the far future and acting to alter it for the better is the fact that to truly think and feel yourself into the far future means you're going to have to think about a moment where you no longer exist.

In 1972, Ernest Becker wrote *The Denial of Death* — he won the Pulitzer Prize for it. Becker's contention was that we are the only species that, at a very early age, recognizes that we are here for a short period of time, and that at one point we will cease to exist. And his further contention was that everything — religion, culture, laptops, convertibles — everything we create is our way of pushing back the understanding that we will cease to exist. It horrifies us.

I would argue that every addiction is based in a fear of death and an attempt to shorten the time scale of thinking, shorten the time scale of rewards, to avoid that reality. In America especially, in the Western world, we push back from death in every way — we put older people in old age homes; it used to be we lived together in multigenerational homes because older people, I would argue, remind us of our own mortality.

Until we can reconcile ourselves — truly, at an individual and maybe even collective level — that we will cease to exist, it becomes extremely difficult to "future" in the way I'm advocating for: to be a great ancestor to future descendants and generations.

In the show, I go to the high mountain desert outside Tucson and I sit with a death doula — a Lua Arthur. What a death doula does is help us and our loved ones exit this world. She does something called a death meditation. You literally go through a guided meditation where you go from breathing to sensation of breath to literally just becoming one with the soil.

I went through a version of the death meditation when I was 18 years old. I literally picked up the phone at 2 a.m. and it was a charge nurse. She said, "I want to bring you up to speed. Your father is not responding. We've been doing CPR. There are no orders on what to do — what do you want us to do?" And I made that call. That was my way of confronting the salience of his mortality and my own mortality very abruptly.

There is a certain level of emancipation when you've come close to death. You don't want to wish it on anyone, but when you have come close, you all of a sudden become free from the burdens that society places on you in the Beckerian way of trying to push back mortality. As a society in the West we do the exact opposite — we inject things into our bodies and do everything we can to push it back for quantity, but we don't think about the quality of the life we want now.

Japan, notably: 90% of the companies over a thousand years old on planet Earth are in Japan. They understand they don't need to exist within their own lifespan bias — they're part of a chain, a great chain of being. That larger purpose, that larger telos, is what's missing right now in Western society. Not just so that we're grounded and happy, but because we need to be able to confront climate change, synthetic biology, and artificial intelligence from a place of wisdom rather than just smartness.

## [01:47:03] Traditional Institutions, Family, Future Consciousness; "Protopia"

**Ari Wallach:** The idea of sleep training — putting children in another room — is relatively new. That comes from the Victorian era. If you go back to most indigenous cultures, everyone slept together. Most cultures for thousands of years wore their babies during what we call the fourth trimester — the mother would have a wrap and the baby would be held close, skin to skin. There's a reason for everything. For a human baby to come out as cognitively, intellectually, and physically ready as a baby chimpanzee would take 18 months of gestation, but we only do nine — because when we went from walking on all fours to being bipedal the female pelvis closes, and there's only so much room for the baby to come out.

So much of what we do we don't critically examine. You're talking about the breakdown of the family structure — I would argue that breakdown isn't happening now. It happened when we decided to move from tribes and clans raising children and moved into a Victorian-era mindset. Why does the female of the species go through menopause and then continue to live? Basically elephants, whales, and humans. Why? Because those are the species where you need elders to help care for the young. Maybe it's also the propagation of story, as you said earlier.

We need to critically examine: why do we do what we do? What are the things we want to keep and what do we want to let go of? The incentive structure is that we actually care about where we take our species. I'm often asked, "How is it that we've gotten so messed up?" And I always say: we didn't mess up. We're doing much better. I walk into my daughters' rooms and look at their bookshelf — 15-year-old twin daughters — and every piece of fiction that takes place in the future is dystopian: Hunger Games, Maze Runner, a world that has gone bad. These are the best sellers. We're attracted to that in the same way we're attracted to a dumpster fire.

Dystopian stories can act as an early warning system — if you keep doing this one thing and extrapolate out a few decades it'll look like this. But what we're missing are the stories about what if we get it right. What I call *protopia* — this idea put forth by Kevin Kelly — a better tomorrow, not perfect, but one where we're making progress. It's unbelievably important that we start setting stories in protopia, in better tomorrows, where not everything is perfect but where we have made significant progress.
