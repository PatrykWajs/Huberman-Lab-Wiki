# Episode 233: Create Your Ideal Future Using Science-Based Protocols — Ari Wallach

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman hosts Ari Wallach — futurist, Columbia University professor, and founder of Longpath Labs — for a deep discussion on why modern life has collapsed the human temporal horizon into chronic short-term thinking and what each person can do about it. Wallach introduces his three-pillar Longpath framework — transgenerational empathy, futures thinking, and telos — alongside a suite of concrete, science-backed protocols ranging from visualizing your aged self to writing letters to your future self. The conversation covers how the hippocampus enables mental time travel, how Antonio Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis explains why emotions (not cognition alone) drive behavior change, and why confronting mortality is a prerequisite for genuine long-term thinking. Wallach argues that egoic legacy fades while modeled behavior endures across generations, and that the antidote to dystopian short-termism is cultivating "protopia" — a directionally positive future — through daily, embodied practice. By episode's end listeners have a complete toolkit for anchoring long-term values into present-day decisions and becoming, as Wallach puts it, "the great ancestors the future needs us to be."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Mental time travel is humanity's superpower — and we are squandering it.** The hippocampus is nearly atemporal and reassembles episodic memories to simulate futures. Modern notification culture has hacked this system into "presentism" — a hall of mirrors with no past and no future. [00:06:13](transcript.md#000613-mental-time-travel-technology--present)

2. **Transgenerational empathy has three layers.** Self-compassion must come first; then empathy for ancestors; then empathy for future generations. Skipping self-compassion makes the other layers collapse. [00:15:46](transcript.md#001546-technology-tools-transgenerational-empathy-bettering-today)

3. **Empathy for others starts cognitive but must become felt.** Mirror neurons and social contagion mean that genuine felt empathy — not just intellectual acknowledgment — is the mechanism through which caring spreads and culture changes. [00:22:00](transcript.md#002200-tool-empathy-for-others)

4. **Empathy for future generations must be felt, not just thought.** Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis shows that purely cognitive visions of the future fail to change behavior. Connecting emotionally to how you want future generations to *feel* acts as a kedge anchor that pulls present behavior toward that future. [00:26:09](transcript.md#002609-empathy-for-future-generations-emotion--logic)

5. **Emotions are forward-pointing, not backward-looking.** Contrary to Freudian framing, Seligman argues emotions are tools for making better future decisions. The pain of a breakup is your body telling you: don't do that again. Pro-social emotions — awe, compassion, love — can be deliberately cultivated to drive long-term action. [00:31:48](transcript.md#003148-tool-emotion-to-guide-action)

6. **Cathedral thinking: plant the carob tree.** Build things whose fruits you will not live to see. The architect of the Blue Mosque did not live to see it completed; that sense of awe the structure still produces is the outcome of cathedral thinking. Your behaviors today — however small — compose your cathedral. [00:38:02](transcript.md#003802-tools-perfect-day-exercise-cathedral-thinking-awe--future-generations)

7. **Egoic legacy fades; modeled behavior endures.** Building names on buildings matters less than how you interact with your children, your barista, your partner. In 250 years, your 50,000 descendants will not know your name — but they may carry how you treated strangers. Susan Blackmore's meme theory: cultural units transmit longitudinally through modeled behavior. [00:43:52](transcript.md#004352-egoic-legacy-modeling-behavior)

8. **Lifespan bias is the enemy of telos.** We are wired to treat birth-to-death as the primary unit of time. Longpath challenges this: individual lives overlap massively with the lives before and after them. Without telos — ultimate aim — we flounder and fill the void with short-term metrics. [01:09:23](transcript.md#010923-longpath-telos-time-perception)

9. **We are in the first third of our species' existence.** Most mammal species last about a million years. Homo sapiens at ~300,000 years old is in the bottom of the third inning — a deeply reassuring frame for long-term thinking. [01:09:23](transcript.md#010923-longpath-telos-time-perception)

10. **Confronting death is prerequisite to long-term thinking.** Ernest Becker's *The Denial of Death* argues that every human institution is a way of pushing back the terror of non-existence. Until we can reconcile our own mortality, we cannot properly "future." Japan's thousand-year-old companies exist because their culture integrates death and elder wisdom rather than hiding both. [01:30:17](transcript.md#013017-death-western-society)

11. **Protopia over dystopia.** Most fiction set in the future is dystopian (Hunger Games, Maze Runner) because negativity bias makes it compelling. What is missing — and urgently needed — are stories about "better tomorrows," not perfect ones. Kevin Kelly's concept of protopia: imperfect but directionally positive progress. [01:47:03](transcript.md#014703-traditional-institutions-family-future-consciousness-protopia)

12. **AI is ingesting how we model discourse now.** Large language models trained on current public content will carry forward the quality of human interaction found there. How we speak to each other on podcasts and social media literally teaches machines what it means to be human. [01:58:48](transcript.md#015848-tool-behavior--modeling-for-the-future)

13. **"Why do we vote on Tuesdays?"** Americans vote on Tuesdays because 200 years ago that was the travel window between Sunday church and Wednesday market day. This metaphor for unexamined inherited stories is at the heart of the "why Tuesdays?" self-examination practice. [02:08:11](transcript.md#020811-tool-why-tuesdays-examining-self)

14. **Social media is a hall of mirrors, not a time capsule.** Cave paintings 40,000–50,000 years old transmitted survival knowledge across generations. By contrast, most social media content has a half-life of hours. The real time capsules are modeled behaviors, storytelling, and deliberate acts of transgenerational record-keeping. [00:51:13](transcript.md#005113-social-media-time-capsule-storytelling)

15. **Science tells us how we got here; telos tells us where to go.** The Enlightenment replaced the Church but left a vacuum of purpose. Science can describe the origin of the universe but cannot prescribe where humanity should aim. That is the role of telos — and its absence drives short-termism. [01:01:18](transcript.md#010118-short-term-thinking-life-purpose-science--religion)

## Tools & Protocols

### Transgenerational Empathy Practice

- **What:** A three-step empathy expansion — from self, to ancestors, to future generations.
- **How:** (1) Self: extend genuine self-compassion — recognize you were doing the best you could with what you had at the time. (2) Ancestors: read biographies or study family history to build felt empathy for those who came before. (3) Future generations: when thinking about descendants, don't just list what you want for them — close your eyes and feel how you want them to feel.
- **When:** Ongoing orientation; revisit whenever making a significant life or business decision.
- **Ref:** [00:15:46](transcript.md#001546-technology-tools-transgenerational-empathy-bettering-today)

### Emotion as Kedge Anchor

- **What:** Using pro-social emotions as a physical, felt anchor toward desired futures rather than purely intellectual goal-setting.
- **How:** When thinking about a future you want to create — for yourself or for future generations — don't just list it. Close your eyes and feel what it would feel like. How do you want your great-grandkids to feel? Let that emotion settle in your body. Emotions do not know the clock or calendar — they pull behavior forward toward the state they represent, just as a kedge anchor pulls a ship toward where it was thrown.
- **When:** Before significant decisions; during planning exercises; when motivation for long-term goals is flagging.
- **Ref:** [00:31:48](transcript.md#003148-tool-emotion-to-guide-action)

### Perfect Day Visualization

- **What:** A two-part body-based visualization to surface your actual values and aspirations.
- **How:** (1) Eyes closed, vividly imagine a terrible day — let the sensations settle in your body. (2) After a break, imagine your perfect day with no rules, no constraints, anywhere in the world — notice what appears. Write down the seeds that emerge. They tend to be realistic and bear out over time.
- **When:** Periodically, especially when feeling directionless or before setting long-term goals.
- **Ref:** [00:38:02](transcript.md#003802-tools-perfect-day-exercise-cathedral-thinking-awe--future-generations)

### Empty Photo Frame (Future Generations Anchor)

- **What:** A blank picture frame placed among family photos as a physical reminder of future generations.
- **How:** Place a shelf of family photos from grandparents through children. Beside the last filled frame, put a visibly blank frame representing grandchildren or future generations you may never meet. When facing a decision or a reactive moment, look at that blank frame and ask: "What is this actually all about? How will descendants look back on this?" Use the phrase "long path" as a mantra to return to the bigger arc.
- **When:** Daily — visible in your home. Invoke when about to react short-term.
- **Ref:** [01:15:19](transcript.md#011519-tools-photo-frames-behavior--legacy-life-in-weeks)

### Life in Weeks Chart

- **What:** A visual grid of your entire life divided into weeks, filled in as they pass.
- **How:** Find or create a grid with one square per week of your expected lifespan. Mark your birthday, set your predicted lifespan (e.g., 100 years = 5,200 squares), and fill in the squares that have passed. The visual representation of where you are can galvanize better decision-making.
- **When:** Review periodically — Wallach does it mornings. Useful at life transitions.
- **Ref:** [01:15:19](transcript.md#011519-tools-photo-frames-behavior--legacy-life-in-weeks)

### Visualizing Future You (Aged Photo)

- **What:** A printed or digital photo of your aged self placed where you see it daily.
- **How:** Use Snapchat or an aging app to generate a realistic older version of your face (10–20 years). Print it and place it on your bathroom mirror or desk. Spend 2–3 seconds looking at it each morning and connecting emotionally with that future person. Based on Hal Hershfield's fMRI research: when you feel disconnected from your future self (your brain treats future-you as a stranger), present behavior becomes short-sighted. The visual intervention changes that.
- **When:** Daily. Especially effective for decisions about health, savings, and habits.
- **Ref:** [01:23:02](transcript.md#012302-tool-visualizing-future-you)

### Death Meditation

- **What:** A guided meditation on mortality to emancipate long-term thinking from fear of death.
- **How:** Follow a guided death meditation (available online; Wallach references death doula Lua Arthur near Tucson). The meditation moves from breath awareness to sensation to becoming one with the soil. The goal is reconciliation with the reality of non-existence — which, per Becker, is the prerequisite for truly caring about the future.
- **When:** Once or periodically; especially useful if short-term focus is driven by mortality anxiety.
- **Ref:** [01:30:17](transcript.md#013017-death-western-society)

### Writing a Letter to Your Future Self

- **What:** A private letter addressed to yourself, to be delivered 5–10 years from now.
- **How:** Write a letter to future-you. Include your hopes, dreams, desires, fears, and what you want to see happen. The transformation happens in the writing, not the receiving — it forces you to think concretely about who you want future-you to be. Use longpath.org or futureme.org to schedule delivery. Think of it as passing the baton in a relay: you're carrying something that was handed to you, and you are handing it forward.
- **When:** Any significant life moment — annually, at milestones, whenever motivation for the future is low.
- **Ref:** [01:36:20](transcript.md#013620-tool-writing-letter-to-future-self)

### "Why Tuesdays?" Self-Examination

- **What:** A CBT-inspired audit of inherited behavioral stories you've never questioned.
- **How:** Identify one habitual behavior or belief you've simply always done or held. Ask: where did this come from? Is it serving me? Is it serving the future I want to build? Americans vote on Tuesdays because of 200-year-old horse-and-buggy logistics — not because it's optimal. Once you identify a story that no longer serves you, consciously write a replacement story.
- **When:** Ongoing; journaling practice. Particularly useful during periods of behavioral change.
- **Ref:** [02:08:11](transcript.md#020811-tool-why-tuesdays-examining-self)
