## [00:00:21] What is Trauma?, Guilt & Shame

**Andrew Huberman:** We could just start off very basic and just get everyone oriented. How should we define trauma?

**Paul Conti:** I think we have to look at trauma as not anything negative that happens to us, but something that overwhelms our coping skills and then leaves us different as we move forward. So it changes the way that our brains function, and then that change is evident in us as we move forward through life. We can see it in mood, anxiety, behavior, sleep, physical health — we can identify it, and we can also see it in brain changes.

If trauma rises to the level of changing the functioning of our brains, then there's almost always a reflex of guilt and shame around the trauma that can lead us — and often leads us — to bury it, to avoid it, which is exactly the opposite of what needs to be done. We need to communicate and put words to what's gone on inside of us. Very often a person knows, but they're not admitting it to themselves because they're afraid of it — they don't know what to do. But if they start talking, they'll talk about the event or the situation. It could be something acute or it could be something chronic that really has been harmful to them, and then they feel different afterwards.

But that doesn't always happen. Sometimes it's a process of exploration through dialogue — whether written or spoken — of the person exploring the changes inside of themselves, maybe changes to their self-talk, changes to their thoughts about the world and whether they can navigate safely and readily in it. As an anchor, I'll use the example of my own life: when I was much younger in my early 20s, my younger brother took his life by suicide. The response of guilt and shame and hiding all of it inside of me — I wasn't acknowledging it because I didn't know what to do about it. I felt guilty, I felt responsible, and I felt ashamed.

There was an avoidance inside of me, so I didn't see that the change was in me, but I was taking care of myself poorly. There was enough going on that was unhealthy that I couldn't avoid the realization that things were different — my reflex to "can I make my way in the world, can I have a good life, can I be happy" were all coming through the lens of heightened anxiety, heightened vigilance, a sense of guilt, a sense of shame, and a sense of non-belonging. It was ultimately good and helpful people around me, and my own realization that things were not going well, that led me to get some help and to be able to talk about it and realize, oh my gosh, I need to face these things that are going on inside of me.

## [00:03:20] Evolutionary Context of Trauma, Shame & Guilt

**Andrew Huberman:** Why is it that when we experience trauma, these things that we call guilt and shame surface? Those emotions must exist in us for some reason, but in this case, it seems like they don't serve us well. So why is it that we seem to be reflexively wired to feel guilty and feel ashamed when that's the exact opposite of what we need to do in the case of trauma?

**Paul Conti:** There's something adaptive that has happened in us through evolution that now becomes maladaptive in the way we live in the modern world. Through most of human development, people weren't living that long, and the idea was to survive and reproduce. So traumatic things that happened to us — it would make sense for them to stay with us. If you ate a new food and got really sick, you better remember that. If someone from a nearby group attacks you, you better remember that. So the traumatic things that are emblazed in our brain are built to last. Things that are positive will generate some emotion inside of us, but things that are profoundly negative are much more likely to stay with us, and that was adaptive when survival was everything.

The same thing is true with shame. The limbic system — the system often called the emotion system in our brains — has aroused affect: a response created in us without our choice. If we're walking down the road and someone jumps in front of us, there's a response of fear and anger, the heart starts beating faster, more blood goes to the muscles, we're getting ready to fight or run. Shame is an aroused affect — it can be raised in us without our choice, and it's very powerful. Imagine a tribe sheltered together, and someone eats half the food at night. There's a very negative response, and that person feels shame because shame is so powerful to control behavior. This is evolutionarily coherent when you're fighting for survival as an elder statesman at age 20.

But it doesn't make sense in a world where we live much longer and navigate in all sorts of different ways. Guilt becomes what's called "feeling technically" — where we relate the aroused affect to ourselves. Shame and guilt are such profound behavioral deterrents that they made evolutionary sense for short, survival-driven lives. In the modern world, where traumas can change our brain functioning and entire orientation for years and years, those same mechanisms become deeply harmful. This leads us to the Freudian concept of the repetition compulsion.

## [00:07:18] Repetition Compulsion, Repeating Trauma

**Andrew Huberman:** My understanding of the repetition compulsion is that we all want to solve our traumas and it allows us to put ourselves into micro or macro versions of that over and over again — we get to run the experiment again and again in an attempt to solve it. Why is it that somebody who is in an abusive relationship goes on to have a second and third or fourth verbally or physically abusive relationship? On the surface of it, it makes no sense.

**Paul Conti:** Then if we think about how our brains actually function — we're trained in Western society to think of ourselves as logical creatures, but that's completely not true. The limbic system, the emotion system inside of us, always trumps logic. Does it ever make sense to run into a burning building? Logic says no. But if someone you love is in there, people run right in, because the limbic system says yes. When logic and emotion come head-to-head, emotion wins all the time. And the limbic system does not care about the clock or the calendar.

How I'd relate that to the repetition compulsion: when people are repeating, what they're trying to do is make things right — with the idea that if we can repeat the situation and make it right, it will fix everything. That makes perfect sense if we remember where that concept is coming from: the emotional part of the brain that wants relief from the suffering of the trauma and does not understand the clock or the calendar. If I can solve something now, I will also solve something in the past.

That's why I can't tell you how many times I've sat with someone starting therapy, and a person will say their last seven relationships have been abusive. What I often point out is: you haven't had seven different abusive relationships — you've had one that you've repeated seven times. The light bulb goes off: now we start getting to what's really going on and what needs to happen. That person needs to face what happened in the original abusive relationship.

It always comes down to the person feeling terrified while the abuse was going on, feeling guilty, feeling ashamed, feeling like they brought it on themselves and don't deserve anything better. If that's bundled up inside of someone like a medical abscess — a walled-off infection inside the body — then the limbic system is going to want to fix that by trying to recreate that situation and make it right this time. I see that play out clinically over and over again. And why do things get better? Because we go to the trauma and unlock it. It's not hidden inside where it can control things — we bring it to the surface and take away its power.

## [00:11:08] Processing Trauma in Therapy or On Your Own, Grieving

**Andrew Huberman:** Thinking about the thing — the event or events plural — evokes this arousal, this internal state that makes some people feel sleepy and exhausted, others feel really anxious, others feel angry. It's clear we need to confront these things. How do we deal with that internal arousal, and does that have to be done in the presence of a skilled trained therapist?

**Paul Conti:** We so often try to change the trauma of the past in order to control the future, and what that really adds up to is the trauma of the past dominating our present. We're not really living in the present — we're trying to control the future. In the moment, if I need to fall asleep, I might try to put that out of my mind, try thought redirection. There are short-term strategies that can let us be functional, but the answer is to go look directly at that trauma, explore it. That can be done with a professional, but not always — sometimes it can be done by talking to another person, writing it down, looking at what's going on inside.

We're so afraid of looking at the trauma that has changed us that we'll look anywhere but at that. What ends up happening is when the person puts words to it — in writing, talking to a trusted other, or with a therapist — things start to change. Just the fact that you can talk about it and other people don't recoil. Take the example of a person who was abused by a coach when they were a child: once they start talking about it, they start seeing themselves as they would see someone else. If you ask someone what they think of a 10- or 11-year-old who was abused by an adult, they say, "Oh my goodness, I feel compassion for that person." But if it's us, it's guilt and shame and we have to hide it away.

When the person starts looking at it from the outside, it starts to take the energy out of it. All the guilt and shame gets juxtaposed against what really happened, and they can say: "I was a terrified child, I didn't understand at all" — and they can come to a place of compassion. And if the person cries about it, that's great. Crying is one of the best coping mechanisms we have — it doesn't hurt us, and it lets us grieve. We can't grieve if there's guilt and shame inside of us; it just blocks grief. There has to be a clean slate in a sense in order to feel sadness. What you see is a shift from anxiety, anger, and frustration directed toward the self — toward being able to process it, bring compassion to bear, and direct the negative emotions where they're warranted. It's remarkable how just one hour of talking can leave a person feeling immensely better.

## [00:15:08] Introspection, Tool: Processing Trauma Through Words

**Andrew Huberman:** How do we do that in a way that isn't retraumatizing ourselves?

**Paul Conti:** It starts with real introspection. When things are bouncing around in our minds, often it's very non-productive — it's the same thing over and over again, and that's not helpful. If we're just thinking about it in the same way we always think about it, all we're doing is reinforcing the trauma. But if we can distance enough, we can think in ways that allow us to have new thoughts. And if we speak or write, there are even more mechanisms that come online in our brains — monitoring mechanisms. We think in a different way if we're using words, and we're better able to bring in that observing ego: what's going on inside of me?

So it can be very helpful to think, to talk to a trusted other — a friend, family member, clergy — or to write. These are things that can be done without expending any resources. Sometimes, if the symptoms are significant enough, we really do need to talk to a professional who can help us get to the root of the trauma. What are the characteristics to look for in a therapist?

## [00:16:23] Finding a Therapist, Rapport; Duration of Therapy

**Paul Conti:** If you look at the top 10 important factors to find in a therapist, just repeat "rapport" 10 times. It's trust. It's a back and forth. It's like: even though I'm doing something difficult, I'm doing it with someone who's really helping me, someone who's in it with me, someone who's really paying attention and wants me to be better — that's indispensable.

Good therapists are not pigeonholed by a certain modality. They may come at the world largely through a psychodynamic, CBT, or DBT lens — there are lots of different ways to do therapy — but when you really talk to really good, experienced therapists, it's all coming through the vehicle of rapport, with practical shifting to what the person needs. People should perhaps try a few therapists and have a session or two or three to see if rapport feels like it's taking root. Word of mouth is important — if someone you trust tells you a person is good, that says a lot and makes the pre-test probability quite high.

**Andrew Huberman:** How does one gauge how much therapy they ought to be doing, and should it always be on the therapist to decide?

**Paul Conti:** I think a lot of times it would be the therapist to say: more work, more intensive work can make a difference. But the person also needs to take ownership. If the therapy is going well, you'll often know it. You'll feel differently and think differently. And the duration really does depend on what's going on. Some people can get to a place of relief and resolution fairly quickly — and it can be maintenance from there — while others with longstanding, deep trauma need more time. The key is: if the person and the therapist are in a good working relationship with a shared sense of the goals, that will determine how long things need to go.

## [00:18:37] Prescriptions, Depression, Treating Core Issues

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you think about prescription drugs in the context of treating trauma and other conditions?

**Paul Conti:** I think that we tend to overutilize medicines in this country because we have a health care system so based on throughput that we want to polish the hood when there's a problem in the engine. We overutilize medicines often as an end point — "oh, we're going to make that person's depression better with an antidepressant" — when most of the time, for that depression to really get better and stay better, the person needs to unravel what's driving it. The first branch point has to be: what is the diagnosis, what is the level of severity?

The vast majority of people who are helped by antidepressants don't have clinically severe depression. Those medicines create more distress tolerance. They can take away what clinically is called rumination — not the standard meaning, but the clinical meaning: distress centers in the brain that are overactive, getting stuck in maladaptive negative pathways where we think about something over and over with no real chance of solving it. Medicines can help that, but we have to have flexibility around their conception.

The modern medical system of 15-minute psychiatric visits is very concerning. We use approximately five times as much medicine as say the Dutch population, who have a health care and cultural system more rooted in personal responsibility. If a person comes in with high cholesterol, the first order of business is better self-care. But in our system, we tend to jump to prescriptions and shift people through the system and out the door. I see patients on seven medicines to treat seven different symptoms, with side effects from all seven — maybe two of them to treat the side effects from the other five. That's bad. Medicines get overused for systemic and categorical reasons instead of asking what's really going on in this person.

## [00:21:16] Psychedelics & Overcoming Trauma, Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love to talk about psychedelics, with the preface that we're talking about this in a legal clinical setting. What are your thoughts on these drugs for therapeutic potential and potential hazards?

**Paul Conti:** The data coming from the labs and academic centers is so powerfully positive. These are used in professional hands with the right kind of guidance — extremely powerful tools when used in the right way. What happens is we see less communication or less chatter in the outer parts of the brain, the outer cortex — where language is, where vision is, where executive function is, so planning and task execution. So much of that is about making our way in the world around us.

When we reduce neurotransmission in those places and seat it in a part of the brain like the insular cortex — the parts of the brain that are in the middle — which I believe is where our humanness really is — the psychedelics create less chatter in the outer brain, and we become seated in the part of the brain most about our experience of true humanness. That's why people can see with clarity: "That trauma is not my fault." We feel a sense of compassion for ourselves. We release ourselves from guilt. It can do what we are trying to get at in good therapy, but catalyze it dramatically.

I think the medicinal value is putting us in that part of the brain where a person can really find truth. That's why this field has come so far in just a few years — it is very clinically evident. We're going to see more and more the value of what the psychedelics do, and how they become a heuristic for understanding how our brains really function. The deep parts of the brain — the insular cortex and areas around it — light up when a person has an experience of spiritual ecstasy or connection with another person. That tells us something very important and very special is going on there.

When patients come back online in a normal cognitive way, they realize: "Wow, something different happened there." Of course these are powerful tools — misused, very bad things can happen — but the clinical utility, and what it means that so many people change for the healthier, tells us they are powerful anti-trauma mechanisms when used clinically in the right hands.

## [00:25:05] MDMA, Overcoming Fear

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to talk about MDMA. What sorts of states do you think MDMA is creating that can explain why it's a useful therapeutic tool in some cases, and what sorts of cases those might be?

**Paul Conti:** This is very different from psychedelics, which are seating our consciousness in these deep centers of the brain. What MDMA is doing is flooding certain parts of the brain with positive neurotransmitters. And I think what that creates is a greater permissiveness inside to entertain or approach different things. When those systems are flooded, it's more permissive to think about difficult material without all the chatter — "that's your fault," "you're never going to get anywhere because of that" — those voices can kind of go away. Then we can think about the traumatic material in a way that isn't through the lens of fear.

I think that's the power of MDMA: it's permissive of approaching something, contemplating something, a de novo approach. That's also why the experience can vary — if there's no clinical guidance to it, a person could be in a state of "I just feel good" without doing problem-solving work. The clinical guidance says: let's take that state and do something with it. Now that you're in this state, let's approach the things that are traumatic and come at them from a de novo perspective. We're coming to understand that these substances have immense potential to help us, but that also increases our respect for these modalities — and what negative things can happen if we're not respectful.

## [00:27:01] Talking about Trauma, Language

**Andrew Huberman:** In your book, you talk about how we need to be careful about the use of language around trauma and problem describing in general. How should we think about language in parsing trauma, and how do we talk about these things in a way that doesn't diminish them?

**Paul Conti:** We just have to be very careful about what we're saying and what we're communicating. This doesn't mean controlling language too much — I think there's a phenomenon now where people are trying to control language in ways that can be psychologically or clinically unhelpful. But I think the specificity of language matters: what are we trying to say, how are we defining it? Even the word "trauma" — it doesn't just mean anything kind of negative, because then it's diluted to meaning nothing. But it also doesn't just mean injury in combat. We have to talk about what it actually is.

I think anchoring it to something that rises to the magnitude of overwhelming our coping skills and changing us — at least if I define it that way and communicate it to you, we can understand what we're talking about. There's a phenomenon where people casually say "I'm traumatized" or "I'm depressed" when they mean they're having a hard day, and that can dilute the clinical seriousness of actual trauma or clinical depression. At the same time, over-controlling what people say doesn't help either. The goal is precision and compassion simultaneously.

## [00:28:54] Taking Care of Oneself, Tool: Self-Care Basics

**Andrew Huberman:** We hear about this concept of taking care of oneself and at a surface level it can sound a little bit light, but to me it's a deep and powerful concept. How should we think about taking care of oneself?

**Paul Conti:** I see a very fascinating dichotomy. Our brains and psyches are extraordinarily complex — so much complexity in our unconscious minds. But on the other hand, psychological concepts consistent with health are often very simple — and by simple I don't mean light, I mean straightforward. Self-care is absolutely one of them. How much is talked about how to take care of oneself that just skips over the basics that are necessary as a building block for all else? It doesn't matter how many chefs or vacations a person has if the basics of self-care aren't squared away.

Are you sleeping enough? Are you eating well? Are you getting natural light? Are you interacting with people who are good to interact with? Are you accepting negative interactions in your life? Are you living in circumstances that make you feel okay? They're very very basic premises, but so often we're not looking at them at all — either because in some automatic way that is sometimes trauma-driven we're not going to look at that, or because our sense of power is tied to not taking care of ourselves.

I'll give you an example: I tend to do reasonably well with very poor self-care, and that was very adaptive when I was in medical training. I could eat a lot today, not eat the next day, sleep two hours, sleep eight. But overall, that's not good, and it hasn't been good as I've aged. At some point I realized: I'm doing all these things to make myself healthier, but I'm ignoring the basics. Why? Because somewhere inside of me is still the idea that my ability to be really functional, to generate success in the world, is tied to my ability to push through without proper self-care — that if I start eating and sleeping regularly, I'll lose some edge.

That's worth examining deeply. The basics of diet, exercise, sleep, meaningful social connection, living circumstances, leisure activities, and sunlight are immensely important and dramatically undervalued. They are the foundation on which all other psychological work rests.
