# Episode 315: Essentials - Increase Strength & Endurance with Cooling Protocols - Dr. Craig Heller

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Craig Heller, a professor of biology at Stanford and world expert on temperature regulation, explains why the body's natural heat portals — the palms, soles of the feet, and upper face — are far more effective cooling sites than the neck, torso, or armpits. The conversation covers how local muscle overheating causes anaerobic failure, why cold showers before aerobic exercise improve endurance, why ice water and ice packs are counterproductive, and how palmar cooling during rest periods between sets can double or triple an athlete's total work output.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cold showers produce an adrenaline shock but don't meaningfully improve performance on their own.** The "benefit" people feel is largely an adrenal response rather than a physiological performance enhancement. ([transcript](transcript.md#deliberate-cold-exposure-cold-showers-ice-baths-vasoconstriction))

2. **Pre-aerobic cold exposure extends endurance by increasing your body's heat buffer.** A cool shower before a long run lowers starting core temperature, giving the body more capacity to absorb exercise-generated heat before reaching the impairing threshold. Pacers will go farther; forcers will go faster. ([transcript](transcript.md#cold-shower-vs-cold-immersion-boundary-layer-tool-improve-aerobic-exercise-performance))

3. **Anaerobic muscle failure is driven largely by local overheating.** Muscle metabolism rises 50–60× during anaerobic effort, but blood flow cannot keep pace. A temperature-sensitive enzyme critical for ATP production shuts off above ~39–39.5°C — this is the physiological basis of hitting failure. ([transcript](transcript.md#anaerobic-exercise--overheating-muscle-failure-muscle-fatigue))

4. **Cooling the skin surface over a hot muscle does not cool the muscle.** The skin, fascia, and tissue are strong insulators. Heat only exits muscle through the blood. Cold towels, fans, and ice packs applied over working muscles are largely ineffective at lowering muscle temperature. ([transcript](transcript.md#anaerobic-exercise-heat-cool-down-with-ice-water-or-cold-towel))

5. **Ice packs to the neck and ice vests are counterproductive during exercise.** They trick the brain's thermostat (preoptic anterior hypothalamus) into thinking the body is cool, causing vasoconstriction of the true heat-loss portals — actually sealing heat inside the body. Core temperature continues rising even as the person feels relief. ([transcript](transcript.md#should-you-cool-bodyhead-to-lower-body-temperature-hyperthermia-heat-stroke))

6. **Heat stroke is difficult to self-detect.** As core temperature approaches danger, athletes vasoconstrict and stop sweating (paradoxically), experience cardiac drift (rising heart rate), and feel exhausted but can be overridden by motivation. Multiple high-profile athletic deaths have occurred during practice, not competition. ([transcript](transcript.md#should-you-cool-bodyhead-to-lower-body-temperature-hyperthermia-heat-stroke))

7. **Glabrous skin (palms, soles, upper face) contains arteriovenous anastomoses — high-flow shunts that bypass capillaries.** These are the body's primary radiators, evolved in mammals to enable heat loss through the small fur-free skin surfaces. The palms are visibly redder than the backs of the hands due to these vessels. Tight grip or gloves close these shunts. ([transcript](transcript.md#body-sites-for-quick-cooling-palms-soles--upper-face-glabrous-surfaces))

8. **Loose grip during cardio improves performance.** Gripping handlebars, a phone, or any object tightly squeezes shut the palmar heat portals. Athletes cycling, running, or training should keep hands relaxed when possible. Gloves and socks meaningfully impede heat loss. ([transcript](transcript.md#tool-loosen-grip--performance-gloves--socks))

9. **Pouring water on the face/head cools the brain via reversed venous flow.** The veins draining glabrous upper-face skin pass through the skull and, when overheated, reverse flow direction — delivering cooled blood directly to the brain. This is distinct from and more effective than cooling the neck. Its potential role in offsetting concussion-related neuronal damage is theorized but not yet established. ([transcript](transcript.md#cooling-brain-via-upper-face-offset-concussion))

10. **Palmar cooling between sets can dramatically increase anaerobic work volume.** Greg Clark, a 49ers tight end, went from 5 sets of dips (totaling ~120 reps) to 300 reps within weeks when cooling his palms for 3 minutes between each set. This was a true conditioning effect — gains were retained even without the device. Similar results have been replicated with bench press and push-ups. ([transcript](transcript.md#enhance-anaerobic-performance--cooling-palms-heat-loss))

11. **Ice water is too cold for palmar cooling — it causes vasoconstriction.** The optimal temperature is cool but not ice-cold. Sticking hands into a bucket of ice water reflexively vasoconstricts the very portals you're trying to use, sealing heat inside the body. The sweet spot keeps the portal open (palm remains warm to another person's touch after contact). ([transcript](transcript.md#coolmitt-ice-cold-is-too-cold))

12. **Cooling glabrous surfaces is twice as effective as standard medical hyperthermia treatment.** Published research from Heller's lab showed that cooling the palms, soles, and upper face cooled hyperthermic subjects at twice the rate of the medically recommended protocol (ice packs to armpits, groin, and neck). ([transcript](transcript.md#tool-use-palmer-cooling-to-enhance-performance-cooling-palms-soles--face))

13. **Palmar cooling also doubles aerobic endurance.** In a treadmill study with ~18 subjects walking uphill in a 40°C room, continuous palmar cooling during exercise doubled total endurance time compared to no cooling in a single trial. ([transcript](transcript.md#improve-aerobic-endurance--cooling-palms))

## Tools & Protocols

### Palmer Cooling for Anaerobic Training
- **When:** During rest periods between sets (3-minute rest standardized in studies)
- **How:** Place palms on a cool (not ice-cold) surface for 2–3 minutes per rest interval — this captures the steepest part of the exponentially declining heat-loss curve
- **DIY method:** Hold a bag of frozen peas or blueberries, alternating hands; after contact, have someone feel your palm — if warm, the portal is open and working; if cold, you vasoconstricted and need a warmer object
- **Device:** CoolMitt by Arteria (www.coolmitt.com) — provides cool (not ice-cold) surface with convective flow to prevent boundary layer buildup; used by NFL teams, college teams, Olympic athletes, Navy SEALs, MLB, NBA, and the National Tennis Association
- **Expected effect:** Doubles or triples total work volume over weeks of consistent use; gains persist as a true conditioning adaptation even without the device

### Pre-Aerobic Cooling
- **When:** Before a long run or endurance workout on a warm day
- **How:** Take a cool (not ice-cold) shower to lower starting core temperature
- **Effect:** Delays onset of heat-impairing threshold; translates to greater distance (pacers) or greater speed (forcers)

### Grip and Apparel Optimization
- **Keep grip loose** on handlebars, bars, and equipment whenever safe — tight grip shuts palmar portals
- **Minimize gloves** during training in warm conditions; gloves insulate and impede heat loss from the hands
- **Minimize thick socks** when practical; soles of feet are heat loss portals and heavy socks impair them
- **Avoid running with a gripped phone** — hold loosely or use an armband

### Continuous Palmar Cooling During Aerobic Exercise
- **When:** Throughout endurance sessions, especially in hot environments
- **How:** Hold cool devices continuously while exercising (laboratory setup suspends devices from ceiling; practical portable options in development)
- **Effect:** Doubled treadmill endurance time in ~18 subjects walking uphill at 40°C ambient temperature in a single trial with vs. without cooling

### Brain Cooling via Upper Face
- Pouring cool water on the scalp/face during high-intensity or hot-environment exercise provides legitimate brain cooling via reversed venous flow through the skull
- More effective than applying cold to the back of the neck, which primarily deceives the brain's thermostat
- Theorized (not yet established) to offset neuronal swelling from concussion by reducing inflammation and blood flow

### What Not to Do
- Do not apply ice packs or ice vests to the torso, neck, or behind the neck during exercise — causes vasoconstriction of heat portals and masks rising core temperature
- Do not use ice water for palmar cooling — triggers reflex vasoconstriction of the portal
- Do not rely on cold towels applied over muscles to reduce muscle temperature — skin is a strong insulator; heat only exits muscle through the blood
