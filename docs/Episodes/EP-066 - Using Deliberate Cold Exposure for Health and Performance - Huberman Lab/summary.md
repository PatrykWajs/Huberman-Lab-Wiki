# Episode 66: Using Deliberate Cold Exposure for Health and Performance

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman provides a comprehensive, mechanistically grounded guide to deliberate cold exposure — covering how cold affects the brain and body via catecholamines (norepinephrine, epinephrine, dopamine), metabolism, and thermoregulation. He details specific protocols for improving mental resilience, mood, focus, metabolism, and athletic performance, and introduces the concept of glabrous skin cooling as a powerful and underutilized performance tool. The episode also addresses the ranking of cold modalities, eustress vs. distress, testosterone effects, and the importance of correct timing around training and sleep.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cold is a reliable, non-negotiable catecholamine trigger.** Deliberate cold exposure causes robust, dose-dependent increases in norepinephrine, epinephrine, and dopamine via cold receptors in the skin triggering release from the adrenals, the locus coeruleus, and other sites — making it one of the most controllable stress-response tools available. [00:38:27](transcript.md#003827-protocols-for-cold-exposure)

2. **Cold creates eustress, not distress — no cortisol spike.** The Sramek study showed cold immersion produces large increases in norepinephrine (~530%) and dopamine (~250%) with no significant rise in cortisol, placing deliberate cold in Hans Selye's category of eustress — stress with health-promoting rather than health-degrading neurochemical effects. [01:00:22](transcript.md#010022-cold-exposure-for-dopamine-mood--focus)

3. **The Søberg Principle: 11 minutes per week is the minimum effective dose for metabolic benefit.** Split across 2–4 sessions, cold water immersion to the neck for a cumulative 11 min/week increases brown fat thermogenesis and core metabolism. [01:12:55](transcript.md#011255-cold-exposure--metabolism-brown-fat)

4. **Cold converts white fat to beige/brown fat via norepinephrine → UCP1.** Norepinephrine released during cold binds receptors on white fat cells and activates UCP1 (uncoupling protein 1), PGC1-alpha, and PPAR-gamma pathways — increasing mitochondrial density and driving gene expression changes that convert energy-storing white fat into thermogenic beige/brown fat. [01:12:55](transcript.md#011255-cold-exposure--metabolism-brown-fat)

5. **Neurons release norepinephrine directly into fat tissue.** Beyond circulating plasma norepinephrine, skin cold-sensing neurons communicate via other neurons directly to fat cells, releasing norepinephrine locally into fat stores — an often-overlooked mechanism that compounds the metabolic conversion effect independent of circulating levels. [01:34:15](transcript.md#013415-norepinephrine--fat-cells)

6. **Dopamine from cold is long-lasting and real.** In the Sramek study, cold immersion (14°C / 57.2°F for one hour) produced a 250% increase in plasma dopamine that persisted for the full two-hour post-exposure measurement window, supporting sustained mood, motivation, and focus far beyond the cold bout itself. [01:00:22](transcript.md#010022-cold-exposure-for-dopamine-mood--focus)

7. **Do not use cold immersion within 4 hours of strength/hypertrophy training.** Cold water immersion (up to the neck) immediately post-resistance training blunts mTOR-driven hypertrophy and strength signaling. Cold showers are less studied but likely less problematic; if maximizing muscle growth, separate cold by 4+ hours or move it to a different day entirely. [01:36:22](transcript.md#013622-cold-physical-performance-inflammation)

8. **Cold water immersion is the most potent modality; hierarchy matters.** Ranked by effectiveness: (1) full cold water immersion to neck with hands and feet submerged — most effective; (2) cold shower; (3) cold outdoor exposure with minimal clothing. Heat transfer from body into water is ~4× greater than into air, making immersion far more efficient per minute of exposure. [00:34:53](transcript.md#003453-cold-showers-vs-cold-water-immersion)

9. **Glabrous skin surfaces (palms, soles, upper face) are the body's primary heat-dump portals.** Cooling these surfaces — not the torso or head — efficiently lowers core temperature without triggering the hypothalamic thermostat (medial preoptic area) to increase body temperature. Cooling the torso or head tricks that thermostat and can paradoxically raise core temperature further. [00:18:41](transcript.md#001841-tool-quickly-decrease-core-body-temperature-glabrous-skin)

10. **Palmar cooling works by protecting pyruvate kinase from heat-induced failure.** Muscle contractions depend on pyruvate kinase, which functions only in a narrow temperature range. Exercise heats muscles locally, eventually short-circuiting contraction ability. Cooling the palms between sets extracts heat through glabrous-skin arterio-venous anastomoses, lowers muscle temperature, and restores pyruvate kinase function — allowing 2–3× more training volume and eliminating delayed onset muscle soreness. [01:53:27](transcript.md#015327-tool-palmar-cooling--endurance)

11. **Shivering is a powerful thermogenic signal — do not suppress it.** Shivering releases succinate from muscles (documented in a 2018 Nature study), which activates brown fat thermogenesis. Warming up by movement after cold (not via sauna or hot shower) preserves this metabolic signal per the Søberg Principle. [01:29:14](transcript.md#012914-tools-increasing-metabolism-wcold--the-søberg-principle-shivering)

12. **Cold exposure likely increases testosterone via dopamine → luteinizing hormone.** The 250% dopamine increase from cold immersion can stimulate luteinizing hormone, which drives testosterone (and estrogen) production. No controlled study has confirmed this directly, but the pathway is biologically plausible. Direct groin cooling adds a vasoconstriction-then-rebound-vasodilation mechanism for local perfusion, though no peer-reviewed evidence confirms benefit over standard immersion. [02:03:18](transcript.md#020318-cold-exposure-to-groin-increasing-testosterone)

13. **Cold exposure in the morning boosts alertness; late-night cold can disrupt sleep.** Core body temperature rises after cold exposure via thermogenesis. Done early, this reinforces the circadian temperature rise supporting wakefulness. Done too late (evening/night), it can impair sleep onset by opposing the 1–3°C temperature drop required for deep sleep. [02:07:50](transcript.md#020750-tool-optimal-timing-for-daily-cold-exposure)

14. **Mindset during cold exposure is trainable and transferable.** Deliberately entering cold and maintaining cognitive clarity while stressed trains top-down prefrontal control over limbic/hypothalamic escape reflexes — the same neural pathway activated by real-life stressors. Grounding tactics: controlled breathing (double inhale through nose, extended exhale through mouth), mental math, or deliberate sentence construction while in cold. [00:50:57](transcript.md#005057-optimal-mindsets-during-cold-exposure)

15. **Caffeine 60–120 minutes before cold amplifies dopamine signaling.** 300 mg caffeine upregulates striatal dopamine D2/D3 receptor density and efficacy, so more dopamine released by cold can bind and exert its mood/focus effect. Combining fasted state + caffeine + cold layers multiple dopamine-enhancing mechanisms. [01:25:55](transcript.md#012555-tool-caffeine-dopamine--cold-exposure)

16. **Moderate exercise (~15 min, zone 2) enhances cognitive performance.** A 101-person RCT showed 15 minutes of moderate jogging elevated energy and significantly outperformed mindfulness meditation on perceptual speed and visual attentional control tasks; the mediating variable was subjective energy, likely driven by catecholamine release. [00:04:23](transcript.md#000423-tool-moderate-exercise--cognitive-work)

## Tools & Protocols

### Mental Resilience via Cold (Walls Method)
- **What:** Cold water immersion or shower used to train prefrontal top-down control over stress reflexes.
- **How:** Set a number of "walls" (surges of adrenaline/desire to exit) to traverse per session rather than a fixed time or temperature. One wall = the moment you feel you want to get out but stay in for at least 10 more seconds. Count 3–5 walls per session. Vary temperature, duration, and wall count across sessions to avoid adaptation. Optionally: engage in cognitive tasks (math, deliberate sentence construction) while in cold to train the prefrontal cortex to stay online under stress.
- **When:** Morning or midday. 3×/week minimum. Adjust based on daily resilience level.
- **Ref:** [00:38:27](transcript.md#003827-protocols-for-cold-exposure), [00:50:57](transcript.md#005057-optimal-mindsets-during-cold-exposure)

### Søberg Protocol (Metabolic / Brown Fat Activation)
- **What:** Cold water immersion (to the neck) for a cumulative minimum of 11 minutes per week.
- **How:** Split into 2–4 sessions at whatever temperature feels genuinely cold. After exiting, do NOT rewarm via hot shower or sauna — allow the body to rewarm naturally through shivering and movement. Try to induce shivering: exit the cold, stand with arms extended (do not hug yourself), allow evaporative cooling, then re-enter if needed. Shivering releases succinate which activates brown fat thermogenesis.
- **When:** Morning or midday. Avoid late evening.
- **Ref:** [01:12:55](transcript.md#011255-cold-exposure--metabolism-brown-fat), [01:29:14](transcript.md#012914-tools-increasing-metabolism-wcold--the-søberg-principle-shivering)

### Cold Exposure for Dopamine & Mood
- **What:** Cold shower or immersion to trigger sustained dopamine elevation lasting hours.
- **How:** Enter cold water at a temperature that feels very cold. Optional enhancement: ingest 100–400 mg caffeine 60–120 minutes prior to upregulate dopamine receptor availability. Fasted state further amplifies norepinephrine release. Expect stress during exposure; the mood benefit is primarily post-exposure and persists for hours.
- **When:** Morning preferred. Effects last hours post-exposure.
- **Ref:** [01:00:22](transcript.md#010022-cold-exposure-for-dopamine-mood--focus), [01:25:55](transcript.md#012555-tool-caffeine-dopamine--cold-exposure)

### Glabrous Skin Cooling (Rapid Core Temperature Reduction)
- **What:** Applying cold to the palms, soles of feet, and/or upper face to rapidly lower core body temperature.
- **How:** Hold cool (not freezing) objects in the palms, immerse feet in cool water, or apply a cool cloth to the upper face/forehead. The cooling surface must maintain vasodilation — ice directly on glabrous skin causes vasoconstriction, collapsing the arterio-venous anastomoses and blocking heat exchange. Use frozen blueberry packs or water bottles with a few ice cubes rather than bare ice. Commercial option: CoolMitt (coolmitt.com).
- **When:** During overheating, exercise-induced hyperthermia, or between exercise sets to extend performance. Also for treating hyperthermia emergencies (palms + soles + upper face simultaneously for fastest core temperature reduction).
- **Ref:** [00:18:41](transcript.md#001841-tool-quickly-decrease-core-body-temperature-glabrous-skin), [01:47:36](transcript.md#014736-hyperthermia--glabrous-skin-cooling)

### Palmar Cooling for Endurance & Strength Volume
- **What:** Cooling the palms between exercise sets to dramatically increase total training volume by protecting pyruvate kinase function in muscles.
- **How:** Between sets, hold a cool (not freezing) surface against the palms for ~2 minutes. Use frozen blueberry packs, water bottles with a few ice cubes, or the CoolMitt device. Not bare ice — vasoconstriction blocks heat extraction. Do NOT combine with full cold immersion (to neck) within 4 hours of hypertrophy/strength training.
- **When:** Between sets during resistance training or continuously during endurance sessions. Most impactful for breaking through plateaus; not required every session.
- **Ref:** [01:53:27](transcript.md#015327-tool-palmar-cooling--endurance)

### Movement During Cold Exposure
- **What:** Moving limbs while in cold water to intensify the cold stimulus without lowering temperature.
- **How:** While submerged to the neck, actively move arms and legs (pedal feet, move knees). Movement disrupts the thin warm thermal boundary layer that forms around the body in still water, continuously exposing skin to colder water. Keep limbs submerged — removing glabrous surfaces (hands and feet) from the water reduces the stimulus.
- **When:** Any cold immersion session where greater stimulus is desired at a fixed temperature.
- **Ref:** [00:55:26](transcript.md#005526-tool-using-movement-during-cold-exposure)

### Optimal Timing for Daily Cold Exposure
- **What:** Strategic placement of cold exposure in the day to support circadian alertness and sleep.
- **How:** Do cold exposure in the morning through mid-afternoon. Avoid cold exposure within 3–4 hours of intended sleep, as thermogenesis raises core temperature — which opposes the 1–3°C temperature drop needed for deep sleep onset and maintenance.
- **When:** Morning to ~4–5 PM for most people. Highly individual; test and adjust.
- **Ref:** [02:07:50](transcript.md#020750-tool-optimal-timing-for-daily-cold-exposure)

### Moderate Exercise for Cognitive Enhancement
- **What:** ~15 minutes of zone 2 cardio to boost attention, perceptual speed, and energy before cognitive work.
- **How:** Jog or cycle at a pace where you can hold a conversation but feel mild effort (zone 2). 15 minutes produces measurable cognitive benefits on trail-making tests (perceptual speed, visual attentional control) and working memory tasks. The key variable is increased energy via catecholamine release.
- **When:** Before cognitively demanding work. Morning preferred.
- **Ref:** [00:04:23](transcript.md#000423-tool-moderate-exercise--cognitive-work)
