**Matt Walker:** Welcome back, Dr. Huberman, an absolute pleasure. Let's talk about the different types of sleep, because I think most people think of sleep as just one thing. Most people sleep at night; some people also nap — a topic we'll also discuss today. But it turns out there are a lot of different types of sleep. What are the different types of sleep and what do they do for us? I guess everyone's probably wondering already — and I certainly am — what types of sleep are we already engaging in, meaning am I having multiple types of sleep each night?

**Andrew Huberman:** This is a fascinating question and it comes back to something we've discussed in a previous episode — the different stages of sleep and how they unfold. What you're asking, though, is an incredibly subtle but relevant question: how should I be sleeping in terms of the phases of sleep? Should I have one phase, should I have two phases of sleep, or should I have many phases of sleep? In some ways you can answer that question on the basis of the lifespan, because how we sleep in terms of those chunking sessions changes as we develop.

**Matt Walker:** To be clear in nomenclature: monophasic simply means a single phase — a single bout of sleep within a 24-hour period. Biphasic means that within that 24-hour period you are having two bouts of sleep. How do these different phases change across the lifespan? When you're an infant and you're first born, within the first year of life you are incredibly polyphasic, going through wake–sleep phases every two hours. Why? At least two reasons: first, an infant needs to feed every two hours, so their energy needs dictate they can't sleep for very long. Within probably the first six months things start to change. The second reason is developmental — vast amounts of brain wiring are occurring that seem to require these alternating bouts of sleep and wakefulness.

## [00:20:19] Adults & Biphasic Sleep, Modern Society

**Matt Walker:** Once we're adults we become monophasic to a degree, but there is some contention about the way we sleep in modernity — that we may not be sleeping in the way we were designed to sleep. This brings us back to biphasic sleep. In the first episode we spoke about this strange afternoon dip in alertness called the postprandial dip, happening somewhere between 1:00 and 4:00 p.m. It's measurable and it seems to be biologically wired into us.

If you look at certain cultures not touched by modernity — hunter-gatherer tribes — they don't sleep the way we do for at least two reasons. First, they will often have a Siesta-like pattern especially in the hot dry season. Second, the timing of their sleep is different. They don't go to sleep as the sun goes down. On average they go to sleep about two hours after sundown and wake up just before sunrise — not because of light, but because temperature changes first. The thing that changes before the sun truly rises is temperature, and temperature is a very strong predictor that forces them awake.

Think about the term "midnight" — it refers to the middle of the night, but for most of us in the modern world, midnight is when we're thinking about sending our last email or posting to social media. Midnight is no longer midnight for modern society. The extreme early riser who likes to go to bed around 8:00 p.m. and wake up around 4:00 a.m. — most people hear 4:00 a.m. and think that's early. Jocko Willink is famous for posting images of his digital watch at 4:30 a.m. But if he's going to bed at 8:00 p.m., midnight is genuinely the middle of his night.

## [00:25:14] Chronotype, Circadian Rhythms & Biological Flexibility

**Matt Walker:** Why do we have chronotypes? Why is there such variability in when people prefer to sleep? Wouldn't it just be easier if biology designed us all to be asleep at the same time? Not so. We mentioned in the first episode that sleep is truly idiotic in the sense that you're not protecting yourself or the people that you care about. If everyone slept at the same moment in time, you as a collective and as an individual would be vulnerable for an 8-hour period. But by way of this wonderful injection of variability in preferences for when people sleep — some going to bed at 8:00 p.m. and waking at 4:00 a.m., others going to bed at midnight and waking at 8:00 a.m. — you've ensured there will always be someone awake. As a clan you've reduced your collective vulnerability down by roughly 50%. Mother Nature injected variability by way of genetics of chronotype to distribute and lessen the burden.

**Andrew Huberman:** That reminds me of how the circadian rhythm is about 24 hours but not exactly 24 hours. The rhythm of the suprachiasmatic nucleus neurons is rarely exactly 24 hours — it's 24.2 or 24.4 — and the idea is that this variation allows for entrainment to the outside light-dark cycle, which changes across the year. You don't want it rigidly 24 hours because there is variation in light-dark cycles, especially as you move away from the equator. So my SCN might be 24.2, yours 24.6, someone else's 24.1, and that allows malleability in matching the circadian rhythm to outside light-dark rhythms.

**Matt Walker:** A beautiful demonstration that there is always some wiggle room in how biology is programmed. Some degree of noise can be very beneficial and much more predictive of the way the world works. It's much more adaptive for a species to embrace that variability.

Does that necessarily mean it is directly inherited from mom and/or dad? If your parents are both extreme early morning types, will you grow up to be an extreme early morning type?

**Matt Walker:** It's very unlikely to find anyone whose parents were both extreme morning types who is a neutral or evening type, and vice versa. My guess is that people who know their biological parents and their rhythms will find it highly likely that they will at some point acquiesce to being very similar to them. There are certain life conditions and contexts where you can fight that — if you're in a punk rock band and touring all the time, even though your mom and dad may be morning types, you're playing gigs and there's no chance you'll be on that schedule. But at some point, say you retire, and you give yourself the opportunity to express your natural rhythm, you will go back to it.

Yes, it's highly genetic, though not entirely so — there is some degree of modification based on context. Also your exposure to light matters. You can be someone who is a neutral like me, but if you're constantly invaded by electric light at night, drinking too much caffeine, on your laptop and computer and phone, always activated by social media, it's very easy to drift and become a 1:00 a.m. to 9:00 a.m. person. That's not my natural chronotype, but context and the environment can shift me.

## [00:32:55] Biphasic Sleep, Adults; Body Position & Sleepiness

**Matt Walker:** Coming back to biphasic sleep, I described one version for adults: the Siesta-like notion — one long bout at night, short bout during the day, hitting that sweet spot of the postprandial dip between 1:00 and 4:00 p.m. There is also a different version of biphasic sleep for adults described in the literature, called first sleep and second sleep. Here you fall asleep for about 4 hours, then wake up, are awake for several hours, then go back to sleep for another 3 or 4 hours. If you look at the historical record, particularly European cultures — Great Britain in particular — there is good evidence that somewhere between about the 15th to 19th century, people were describing this behavior. They would wake up in the middle of the night, make food, play music, write, make love. It clearly did happen.

But is it the way we were designed to sleep biologically? I don't think so. There is no good collection of evidence from the biology of our human rhythms arguing that there is a magical period of wakefulness that should occur right in the middle of the night. There is one paper often cited for this, but that paper says nothing about first sleep/second sleep — it doesn't speak about biphasic sleep at all. That paper has at least three problems as a justification: the artificial nature of the study (participants were forced into bed for 14 hours rather than a standard 8), the fact that the so-called awakening was a probability distribution rather than a clear universal event, and the timing was highly variable across individuals. The body of sleep science does not support this as a biological design.

On body position: we know that lying down increases sleepiness. The act of going supine reduces the effort required to keep blood flowing to the brain — you're no longer fighting gravity. Core body temperature also shifts slightly when you lie down, which nudges you toward sleep. This is why lying down is so closely associated with rest states and why even lying down without sleeping can provide restoration — a point relevant to our later discussion of non-sleep deep rest.

## [00:40:09] Naps, Positive Benefits, Nighttime Insomnia

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about naps. Are naps good for us? Should we nap? What if we don't like naps? Why do we sometimes wake up from naps feeling groggy, and other times refreshed?

**Matt Walker:** Naps are both good and bad depending on the situation — they can be a double-edged sword. We and others have done lots of studies on naps and the benefits are fascinating. In one study, we had participants assigned to one of two groups. At midday they all learned a whole list of new facts. One group then took a 90-minute sleep opportunity centered right around that postprandial drop in alertness. The other group remained awake lying on a bed watching a nature documentary. Then 5 hours later we had them do another learning session. The group that did not nap showed a gradual decline in learning capacity across the day. The nap group was able to sustain their learning and, if anything, improve it. The difference between those two groups at 5:00 p.m. was about 20%. That's certainly non-trivial.

We did another study where we looked at emotional processing — showing people different types of emotional expressions and having them rate them before and after a nap versus the same period without a nap. The group that did not nap rated fearful faces and angry faces as much more fearful and angry by 5:00 p.m. The group that napped showed a different pattern — they actually lessened the response to fear and blunted the normal increase in anger sensitivity across the day. The nap seemed to boost the ability to respond positively to positive emotional expressions as well.

So naps clearly have benefits: improved learning capacity, enhanced emotional regulation, improved alertness and concentration. The other side of the double-edged sword is nighttime sleep. If you are someone who struggles with insomnia or has fragile nighttime sleep, napping during the day can rob you of that sleep pressure — that accumulated adenosine — that you need to fall asleep and stay asleep at night.

## [00:49:38] Tool: Optimal Nap: Duration & Timing; Grogginess

**Matt Walker:** How to optimally nap — and the word "optimal" is interesting because the question "how long should I nap?" really requires the follow-up question: what are you trying to optimize?

I mentioned the study about emotional faces for a specific reason: the benefit there came by way of REM sleep, not non-REM sleep. In our first episode we said that when you go through these roughly 90-minute cycles, you get most of your non-REM sleep first and then your bout of REM sleep at the end. To get REM sleep in a nap, you have to nap for a longer period of time — you have to get through the non-REM sleep first.

But for most people when they speak about naps, they want the quick reboot — improved alertness and concentration, that slight boost in brain energy, the motivation to keep going. For those basic goals, aim for a 20-minute nap. Here's why: if you look at the dose-response curve — 5 minutes, 10 minutes, 15 minutes, 20 minutes, 30 minutes, 45 minutes, 90 minutes — after 5 or 10 minutes you don't get very much. Once you get past about 15 to 17 minutes, things start to change meaningfully in terms of what non-REM sleep can provide. At 20 minutes you get solid light non-REM sleep benefit without the risk of dropping into deep slow-wave sleep.

The problem with sleeping longer — say 30 to 45 minutes — is that you risk entering deep slow-wave sleep. When you then wake up out of deep sleep, you experience what's called sleep inertia: that grogginess, the crumpled face, the post-nap fog. It can take 20 to 30 minutes to shake off. So the sweet spot for most people seeking alertness and cognitive restoration is 20 minutes. If you want to capture REM sleep benefits (mood reset, emotional recalibration), you need the full 90-minute cycle — effectively a short sleep period placed at the right time.

## [00:58:15] Nap Capacity, "Liminal" States & NSDR

**Andrew Huberman:** Many people think of nappers as lazy, but I have a colleague at Stanford — a Howard Hughes investigator, member of the National Academy — who graduated high school at 15, near-superhuman output, who is so religious about napping that when he travels to give seminars he insists they schedule a nap time for him after lunch. He attributes much of that output to the nap: not just the post-nap work he's able to perform, but his ability to manage so many ideas. A capacity to nap is something worth considering.

Many people listening are thinking: I can't nap — should I? Can one teach themselves to nap? And I'd also like to discuss the pseudo-nap states that I find intriguing — lying down and doing a progressive body relaxation, things like Yoga Nidra, non-sleep deep rest. Does it have to be a nap? If we're not good nappers, should we try? And if so, how?

**Matt Walker:** Three questions. First: if you are not a natural napper, don't necessarily force yourself — as long as you're getting the sleep you need at night and you feel refreshed during the day and you don't have that postprandial drop to the point of thinking "I almost need to nap," there is no pressure based on anything I've been telling you to start napping.

But if you want to try: mimic nighttime as best you can. Shut off the lights, use blackout curtains or an eye mask, block out noise with earplugs or a sound machine. Lie down — lying down reduces core body temperature and increases the likelihood of sleep onset, even if that sleep is very light. Consistency also helps: if you practice having a rest period at the same time each day, your brain and body begin to expect and prepare for that transition.

On the liminal states — what you call NSDR (non-sleep deep rest), also known as Yoga Nidra: these states are fascinating and probably do offer real neurological and restorative benefit, even if the person never fully crosses into sleep. My hypothesis is that these states put the brain into something close to slow-wave activity, which means parts of the brain become metabolically less active and get the chance to dissipate some of the accumulated adenosine. We should do a study together — EEG and imaging — to look at what the brain is actually doing in these liminal states.

## [01:07:37] NASA Nap Culture, Power Naps

**Matt Walker:** NASA figured out the value of naps back in the 1980s. When you are up in orbit, depending on your orbit, you may be rotating around the planet 10 to 20 times per 24 hours — you're seeing 10 to 20 sunsets and sunrises — so your sleep is a total mess. NASA identified that the weak link in a space mission is the human being; that's where errors typically happen. They found that short naps produced almost a 20% boost in alertness and almost a 50% boost in task productivity. It was so powerful it translated to terrestrial NASA employees on the ground, and became known as the NASA nap culture.

From there came what we call "power naps." The name has a specific backstory: David Dinges and Mark Rosekind were looking at how to use naps strategically for pilots on long-haul flights, where the most dangerous moment is landing. They asked: if pilots can nap for only a certain period, when should they place that nap — at the start, middle, or end of the flight? Counterintuitively, they found that napping early in the long-haul flight was most optimal — it sustained performance throughout the rest of the flight rather than just providing a boost at the end.

When they took their findings to the FAA (who funded the work) and proposed calling the practice "prophylactic napping," there were many chuckles in the room — an alpha-male pilot culture was not going to adopt that term. So they looked around and decided: power naps. That is where the term comes from — not just because it powers you back up, but because the team needed a name the culture would actually embrace.

## [01:12:50] Tools: Nap Timing, "Fragile" Nighttime Sleep; On-Off-On Protocol

**Andrew Huberman:** I've become a real fan and practitioner of Yoga Nidra — lying down for 30 to 60 minutes, progressive relaxation. But sometimes the names are a separator. People hear "Yoga Nidra" and think it must involve yoga movement, or must have some mystical component. That's why I coined the phrase "non-sleep deep rest" (NSDR) — it maintains the critical components of Yoga Nidra without necessarily including intentions, and has shorter 10 or 20-minute protocols.

What I'm integrating from today's conversation is that there is pretty good reason to explore biphasic sleep — for non-nappers to think about whether they'd like to explore napping. For people already napping, to really think about the placement and duration of that nap. What you told us suggests I should consider shifting naps earlier — finishing lunch and lying down for 10 to 15 minutes rather than waiting until 2:00 or 3:00 p.m. Is that right?

**Matt Walker:** I think it could make a meaningful difference, and it again depends on how much of a struggle sleep becomes in the evening. The later you nap, the more likely it becomes that your nighttime sleep is affected — and not just in terms of difficulty falling asleep. The other consequence, which is non-mutually exclusive, is that your sleep may not be as deep, as your nighttime sleep becomes more fragile. The probability that you will wake up in the middle of the night is higher. And when you do wake up — say to use the restroom — the speed with which you fall back asleep is compromised, because you've jettisoned some of that sleepiness via the nap and there isn't as much to take you back down.

The On-Off-On protocol is worth mentioning here. If you find that a full nap is making your nighttime sleep fragile, you can try an abbreviated approach: lie down, close your eyes, allow yourself to drift toward the edge of sleep — the hypnagogic state — without necessarily committing to a full nap. Ten minutes of this kind of hovering at the threshold of sleep provides meaningful rest and alertness restoration without substantially depleting adenosine. Think of it as the minimum effective dose.

## [01:18:57] Avoiding Naps: Insomnia, Aging & Sleep Quality Decline

**Matt Walker:** There are some people who should absolutely avoid napping. The strongest evidence comes from insomnia. The recommendation there is: avoid naps. I know how desperate you are for sleep when you're suffering from insomnia, and the temptation to nap is that much higher, making the advice that much harder to adopt. But avoiding naps is one of the components in cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I). Trust me on that.

The evidence for aging is a little less causal, so you have to be more cautious. But the data has now become quite strong: when you get past about 65 years old, large epidemiological studies show that napping in older adults is predictive of worse health outcomes and a higher likelihood of early mortality. This seems contradictory to everything I've been saying. The resolution: it's probably not that napping during the day is bad for older adults per se. It's that the naps reflect a problem with nighttime sleep. The naps are a symptom, not the cause.

What happens to sleep as we age? As we go into our teenage years, our chronotype shifts — we want to go to bed later and wake up later. Something also happens with deep sleep: it starts to perform a different function. As an infant, huge amounts of REM sleep were wiring up new synaptic territories, running fiber-optic cable to new neighborhoods. As a teenager, once the experiment of running that bandwidth everywhere has run for years, the brain goes through synaptic pruning — calling back connections from parts of the brain that aren't using their bandwidth very much, and redirecting it to regions that are working hard. This final cortical maturation is performed in part by changes in slow-wave sleep during adolescence.

As we get into older years, the amount of slow-wave sleep declines significantly — sometimes by as much as 80 to 90% compared to young adulthood. The brain's ability to generate deep sleep diminishes. That means less overnight clearing of adenosine, less consolidation of memories, and a more fragile, disrupted sleep architecture. When older adults nap, it's often because the quality and depth of their nighttime sleep has dropped so severely that they need to compensate. Addressing the root cause — the nighttime sleep quality — is the real intervention.

## [01:28:20] Caffeine, "Nappuccino"; Hot Drinks

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about caffeine. I've heard the term "nappuccino" — referring to the practice of drinking caffeine then laying down for a nap, and supposedly waking up feeling more refreshed. My understanding is that caffeine is effectively an adenosine antagonist — a competitive occupier of adenosine receptors — and napping removes some sleep pressure by allowing some adenosine clearance. Both sound great, but the warning label is that having sufficient adenosine built up is one of the ways you feel sleepy at night and fall asleep. What's the story?

**Matt Walker:** Caffeine is a very interesting compound. It's no coincidence that "caffeine" and "adenosine" sound similar — caffeine targets the adenosine receptors in your brain. You might think: if caffeine works on those same receptors, shouldn't it increase sleepiness? No, because when caffeine binds to those receptors it doesn't deactivate or activate them — it simply blocks them. Think of it like a room full of chairs. Adenosine molecules are trying to sit down on those seats (the adenosine receptors), and as they do, sleepiness builds. Caffeine races into the room and hijacks the seats — it sits down on the receptors, so adenosine can't find a seat. Your brain is still flooding the room with adenosine, but caffeine has hit the mute button on your sleepiness signal.

Then at some point the caffeine wears off. Not only do you go back to the same level of adenosine you had two hours ago — you now have that plus the additional two hours of adenosine that has been building up while caffeine was blocking the receptors. You experience a caffeine crash, and now you need even more caffeine to recover from the crash and go further. That's the vicious cycle.

Caffeine has an onset of action around 12 to 17 minutes. When you grab your first cup of coffee and within four or five minutes you say "I already feel better" — that has nothing to do with the caffeine. Peak plasma concentration won't arrive for 12 to 17 minutes. What you're feeling is partly placebo, and partly a Pavlovian conditioned response — the smell of coffee, the sound of the machine, the ritual itself creates an anticipatory arousal. The alertness is coming, and in that anticipation there's its own form of wakefulness.

The nappuccino — also known as the caffeine nap — works because of timing. You drink your caffeine, then immediately lie down for a 20-minute nap. By the time you wake up, the caffeine is reaching peak plasma concentration and is starting to block adenosine receptors — at exactly the moment when the nap has naturally cleared some adenosine. The two mechanisms stack: a cleaner adenosine landscape plus the caffeine blocking further accumulation. You wake up with substantially improved alertness compared to either the nap or the caffeine alone.

## [01:38:28] Adenosine Clearance, Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there anything besides caffeine and sleep that can clear adenosine? Can exercise clear adenosine, can a cold shower clear adenosine?

**Matt Walker:** I've heard a great quote from a former member of the National Academy of Sciences: "A drug is a substance that, when injected into an animal or a human, produces a scientific publication." Almost any substance alters some feature of sleep or wakefulness if you look with a fine enough instrument — and that includes aspirin, caffeine, exercise, light.

For the postprandial drop in alertness, one recommendation is to get outside and walk around. Some of that is because you'll get daylight, which is a stimulator of alertness. Physical activity also increases endorphins, which are wake-promoting. But none of those things are really altering adenosine — they're overriding it through other mechanisms. The adenosine is still building up; you're just feeling better despite it.

It really does seem that for the most part, only sleep — particularly non-REM sleep — gives the brain the chance to remove adenosine. It's not that there's some special pulsing cleansing mechanism for adenosine during deep non-REM sleep. The glymphatic system does remove toxic metabolic byproducts of wakefulness, but the adenosine clearance works differently: during deep sleep, your brain is metabolically less active and therefore not producing as much adenosine. The natural mechanisms that are always occurring in the background to clear and degrade adenosine get the chance to work, because you're no longer working against the opposite tide of growing adenosine. It dissipates.

There is an interesting possibility regarding anesthesia — my guess is that you probably do jettison some sleep pressure when you are under anesthesia, since your brain is metabolically suppressed. Similarly, I think these liminal states — NSDR, Yoga Nidra — could be fascinating territory, because if they put you into something close to slow-wave activity patterns, those territories of the brain are metabolically less active, which might allow the brain to dissipate adenosine. Anything that mimics a less metabolically active brain could produce adenosine dissipation benefits.

## [01:43:10] Tool: Delaying Caffeine, Afternoon Crash, Sleep Quality

**Andrew Huberman:** I've been touting the benefits of delaying caffeine intake by 90 to 120 minutes after waking. There's been a misconception that I was mandating this for everyone — that's not the case. I actually wake up and drink caffeine close to waking if I'm going to exercise soon after. But I've heard from people who experience that if they delay their caffeine intake by 90 to 120 minutes, they can offset the afternoon crash. Part of that may be offsetting the afternoon need for more caffeine, because by delaying the morning caffeine there's less incentive to drink caffeine in the afternoon, which dominoes to better sleep at night.

My question: given that adenosine clearance is happening during sleep, one could imagine that upon waking there is a transition period. If someone wakes up groggy — in a pseudo-sleep state at 5:45 a.m. — it stands to reason they're still clearing adenosine. If they drink caffeine right away, they'll block those adenosine receptors and there will be continued buildup of adenosine rather than clearance. Does that framework make logical sense?

**Matt Walker:** I think it is good advice for people to test, for two reasons. First, by taking caffeine early and masking the adenosine, you make your brain more metabolically active, which means you build up more adenosine during the day. Sleepiness arrives earlier and perhaps that postprandial drop is harsher, and you perhaps then self-medicate with more caffeine — and so goes the vicious cycle.

The second reason: if you've been using caffeine this way for a long period of time, you may be masking the quality of your sleep. You wake up, immediately medicate with caffeine, feel alert, and think "there's nothing wrong with my sleep." But maybe there is. The test: abstain from caffeine for about two weeks, get through the detox period, and then — taking caffeine at 11:00 after waking at 7:00 — ask yourself whether in that clean 7:00 to 11:00 window, absent caffeine, you feel rested, restored, and refreshed. Can you operate with cognitive acumen? If not, that's a signal worth investigating with a closer look at your sleep.

## [01:49:06] Caffeine, Health, Antioxidants; Caffeine Tolerance & Alcohol

**Matt Walker:** I've changed my mind on caffeine. When I first published my book I was very binary and dictatorial about it — sleep is absolute, this has to be this way, and I was not in favor of caffeine. I've changed my mind for two reasons.

First, that's not how society works or how people live. Second, if you look at the data on caffeine and health, it's stunning. On almost every metric we can measure, drinking some degree of caffeine is beneficial — though there is a U-shaped function: once you get past roughly three or four cups of coffee, things start going in the downward direction.

The apparent contradiction: I was telling people caffeine is not good for your sleep, and sleep is wonderful for health. But then caffeine also transacts many of the same health benefits as sleep. How? When you look at the data carefully, it's not the caffeine that's carrying those health benefits — it's the coffee itself. The coffee bean is packed full not just of caffeine but of antioxidants. Because of our deficient Western diets, so absent of antioxidants, the humble cup of coffee has been asked to carry the Herculean weight of our antioxidant needs on its shoulders. Case in point: decaffeinated coffee still provides those antioxidants without the caffeine, and lo and behold, you get many of the same health benefits. It's not the caffeine — it's the coffee itself.

A good rule on caffeine timing: aim to step away from caffeine somewhere between 10 to 12 hours before you expect to go to bed, depending on your sensitivity. That's genetic — there is a specific polymorphism in a gene that predicts whether you are very sensitive to caffeine or not, based on how quickly you can metabolically remove it from your system. If you're very sensitive, try to stay clear 12 to 14 hours before bed; if less sensitive, maybe 8 hours.

The danger for people who say they can have an espresso with dinner and fall asleep fine: that may be true, but studies show that even if you can fall asleep and stay asleep, the amount of deep sleep can drop by up to 20% after that late caffeine. You wake up not remembering having any problems with sleep — no awareness of how much deep sleep you had — but you find yourself reaching for three cups of coffee rather than two. And so goes the cycle.

We also see an interesting relationship between caffeine and alcohol. People will use alcohol to help them come down and fall asleep. But alcohol fragments and disrupts sleep, reducing REM sleep particularly. The next day, feeling unrefreshed, they reach for more caffeine. The caffeine then makes it harder to fall asleep, so more alcohol is needed. Caffeine and alcohol can become locked in a mutually reinforcing dependency that degrades sleep quality in both directions.

## [01:56:54] Tool: Nap "Enhancements", Caffeine, Light & Face Washing

**Matt Walker:** There was a brilliant investigation from a great sleep research group in Japan. They asked: the nap is good, the caffeine nap may be better, but can we go further? They designed a study with five different experimental groups: a non-nap control group; a nap group; a nap plus caffeine group; a nap plus cold face and cold handwashing immediately after waking; and a nap plus bright light (2000 lux immediately afterwards).

The cold hands and face washing works because of thermoregulation. I told you the three-part story: warm up to cool down to fall asleep, stay cool to stay asleep, warm up to wake up. In the morning you reverse-engineer the evening: instead of warming the periphery to release blood from the core (to cool the core for sleep), you hit the face and hands — vascular surfaces where we can modulate temperature quickly — with cold water. The cold causes vasoconstriction, forcing blood back to the core, which warms the core and signals wakefulness. You also get a mild adrenaline shot from the cold-water stimulus.

Results: compared to the no-nap group, the nap group showed wonderful benefits in alertness, cognitive performance, and reduced sleepiness. The nap plus caffeine added further benefit. The nap plus cold face/hand washing added something more. The nap plus bright light also added something more. They didn't test the full stack — nap plus caffeine plus cold hands/face plus bright light — but if you want to build it, here is the protocol: get into bed, drink your espresso shot before you turn off the light, lie down, set your alarm for 20 minutes. When you wake up, the caffeine is kicking in. You go straight to cold water on hands and face, then get immediate daylight for 5 to 10 minutes outside. At that point you're in a supercharged state — nap plus caffeine plus cold water plus light all stacking additively.

## [02:04:33] Polyphasic Sleep, Adverse Effects

**Andrew Huberman:** Several times we've talked about polyphasic sleep. What are some of the more high-performance polyphasic strategies that people have tried?

**Matt Walker:** Polyphasic sleep — having multiple phases of sleep within a 24-hour period — emerged in the biohacker movement around the late 1990s and 2000s. The idea is to take the 24-hour pie chart, slice it into multiple thin sleep phases, leaving large thick slices of wakefulness in between. The goal: increase the amount of time awake and thereby increase productivity.

There's the Uberman schedule, the Everyman schedule, the triphasic schedule, and various other flavors — all differing in how the pie chart is split. The first description I can find in the human record comes from Time Magazine in 1943, describing the schedule of designer Buckminster Fuller. He called it the Dymaxion schedule of polyphasic sleep — scaled from his Dymaxion design principle. He saw sleep as a waste of time and tried to extract maximum efficiency by inserting tiny slivers of sleep around the clock.

What does the literature say? A group of scientists at Harvard looked at all available studies testing polyphasic sleep claims. The first finding: there is no credible peer-reviewed evidence that polyphasic sleep improves cognition, mood, productivity, or health in healthy adults above what equivalent total sleep time in a conventional schedule provides. The second finding: many polyphasic schedules result in significant total sleep restriction — participants are simply sleeping less overall. And when you restrict sleep, you accumulate cognitive impairment even if you feel fine subjectively.

The National Sleep Foundation has released a consensus statement explicitly cautioning against polyphasic sleep schedules due to adverse health effects. The polyphasic society website makes impressive claims, but it is not a ratified scientific or medical body — it is a community that lives online.

## [02:12:43] Sleep Deprivation & Car Crashes; Polyphasic Sleep

**Matt Walker:** There is an important public safety dimension here. When you're not getting sufficient sleep, road traffic accidents become a real risk. We know about micro-sleeps — involuntary brief losses of consciousness that can occur during wakefulness when sleep-deprived. Car accidents caused by sleepiness can be catastrophic because the driver is not braking, not steering — there's no reaction at all.

One study put people getting less than 6 hours of sleep for several nights into a driving simulator. Sleeping less than 6 hours a night resulted in a 30% increase in crash or off-road events. AAA data on real-world crashes shows that at 5 hours of sleep, there is roughly a 2-3x higher likelihood of an accident. At 4 hours of sleep, it's close to a 10-fold greater risk. The increase is not linear — it's exponential.

I bring this back to polyphasic sleep because many polyphasic schedules result in less than 6 hours of total sleep time. That 30% study — not even the extreme case — should give you pause. If I called two taxis and told you one has a 30% higher likelihood of getting in a crash: which would you put your family in? You wouldn't choose it. That's the only cautionary note I'd close on — not judgment of personal choice, but awareness of the potential harm to others.
