## Key Findings

- AMH (anti-müllerian hormone) testing is crucial for all women who may want children someday—it measures egg quantity, not quality, and provides essential data for family planning decisions. — *[00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-slug)*

- Fertility is a vital health marker that extends far beyond pregnancy; infertility correlates with increased rates of metabolic syndrome, cancer, heart attack, stroke, and early death, making it a window into chronic inflammation and metabolic dysfunction. — *[00:04:42](transcript.md#000442-slug)*

- Women with infertility have higher rates of chronic inflammation and insulin resistance—the underlying drivers are often detectable and treatable before they progress to serious disease. — *[00:05:03](transcript.md#000503-slug)*

- Menstrual cycle tracking remains informative through perimenopause and can reveal hormonal dysfunction even when pregnancy is not the goal; the strict "12 months without a period" definition of menopause delays necessary treatment. — *[00:06:00](transcript.md#000600-slug)*

- Hormone replacement therapy can begin in the perimenopausal period and does not require waiting until complete ovarian failure; early intervention offers cardioprotective, neuroprotective (Alzheimer's risk reduction), and bone-protective benefits. — *[00:07:35](transcript.md#000735-slug)*

- Current medical nomenclature conflates hormone replacement (for deficient levels) with hormone augmentation (optimizing within normal range); clearer language would improve patient autonomy and appropriate treatment access. — *[00:09:56](transcript.md#000956-slug)*

- The fecundability (monthly pregnancy probability) drops significantly with age: 20% at age 30, 11–12% at 35–36, 5% at 38, and 3% at 40+; however, women who have previously had a live birth maintain approximately 18–20% monthly probability until age 37. — *[00:23:47](transcript.md#002347-slug)*

- Secondary infertility is real and emotionally distinct; women who conceived before may face unexpected difficulty conceiving again, compounded by age-related declines in egg and sperm quality and increased prevalence of conditions like endometriosis and adenomyosis. — *[00:26:06](transcript.md#002606-slug)*

- After two pregnancy losses, evaluation is warranted; 80% of recurrent pregnancy loss cases have normal testing, but 20% reveal treatable causes (medication or procedures) that can markedly improve outcomes. — *[00:34:50](transcript.md#003450-slug)*

- The current medical model requiring failure (12 months of trying) before investigation is counterproductive; proactive screening—semen analysis, blood work, tubal/uterine evaluation—should be offered earlier, especially for women 35+. — *[00:25:25](transcript.md#002525-slug)*

- Chronic inflammation, autoimmune disease, untreated endometriosis, and toxin exposure (including microplastics accumulating in ovaries) accelerate ovarian aging; lifestyle modifications targeting these factors can extend reproductive lifespan. — *[00:18:11](transcript.md#001811-slug)*

- Microplastics accumulate in ovarian tissue and endocrine-disrupting chemicals from plastics correlate with worse IVF outcomes and longer time-to-pregnancy; toxin avoidance should be pragmatic rather than all-or-nothing. — *[00:19:26](transcript.md#001926-slug)*

- Egg quality is determined by genetics and cellular competency (not testable in individual eggs); it declines with age largely due to metabolic health changes, not aging itself, making metabolic optimization actionable. — *[00:41:43](transcript.md#004143-slug)*

- Women deserve agency over their health through education about normal cycle parameters, red-flag symptoms (painful periods, irregular cycles, spotting), and access to data-driven decisions rather than dismissal or forced failure before intervention. — *[00:15:51](transcript.md#001551-slug)*

## Tools & Protocols

### AMH Testing (Anti-Müllerian Hormone)
- **What:** A blood test measuring egg quantity (ovarian reserve), not egg quality; standard marker for fertility assessment.
- **How:** Single blood draw; can be done at any point in the menstrual cycle; results inform family planning timelines.
- **When:** Recommended for all women considering future pregnancy, ideally before age 35; useful baseline throughout reproductive years.
- **Ref:** [00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-slug)

### Menstrual Cycle Tracking for Health Monitoring
- **What:** Recording cycle length, ovulation date, period flow, pain, and mood to identify hormonal patterns and red flags.
- **How:** Use a calendar, app, or journal; mark period start, track cervical mucus and basal body temperature if desired; note symptoms.
- **When:** Throughout reproductive years, including perimenopause; continues to reveal hormonal dysfunction regardless of pregnancy intent.
- **Ref:** [00:06:00](transcript.md#000600-slug), [00:15:51](transcript.md#001551-slug)

### Proactive Fertility Screening (Non-Failure-Based)
- **What:** Early evaluation (rather than after 12 months of trying) for women 35+, including semen analysis, blood work, and tubal/uterine imaging.
- **How:** Schedule with fertility specialist or informed OB-GYN; obtain baseline data on egg count, sperm quality, anatomy, and hormone levels.
- **When:** Women 35+ considering pregnancy, or any woman with concerning symptoms (irregular cycles, painful periods, spotting, prior loss).
- **Ref:** [00:25:25](transcript.md#002525-slug)

### Hormone Replacement/Augmentation Therapy
- **What:** Estrogen (patch, pill, vaginal insert, cream) and/or progesterone supplementation during perimenopause and menopause.
- **How:** Dosing individualized by symptom assessment; can start in early perimenopausal period (not waiting for 12 months amenorrhea).
- **When:** When experiencing symptoms or upon entering perimenopause, after discussion of risks/benefits and personal health context.
- **Ref:** [00:07:35](transcript.md#000735-slug), [00:13:34](transcript.md#001334-slug)

### Inflammatory Burden Reduction
- **What:** Systematic lifestyle approach targeting modifiable factors that damage ovaries: toxin exposure, ultra-processed foods, stress, inadequate sleep, misaligned exercise.
- **How:** Reduce plastic bottle use; prioritize whole foods; manage stress and sleep; choose anti-inflammatory exercise; avoid smoking and excessive nicotine.
- **When:** Throughout life; especially before and during fertility window; cumulative daily choices matter more than single interventions.
- **Ref:** [00:18:11](transcript.md#001811-slug), [00:19:26](transcript.md#001926-slug), [00:21:20](transcript.md#002120-slug)

### Recurrent Pregnancy Loss Evaluation
- **What:** Standardized testing after two losses (or one if D&C performed or periods changed): blood work, semen analysis, sperm fragmentation, uterine and tubal imaging.
- **How:** Coordinate with fertility specialist; testing identifies treatable causes in ~20% of cases (medication or procedural).
- **When:** After second pregnancy loss (can accelerate to first in certain circumstances); do not delay further testing.
- **Ref:** [00:34:50](transcript.md#003450-slug)

## Resources Mentioned

### Studies
- **Time to Conceive (Natural Fertility Cohort):** Large population study (Dr. Crawford participated in fellowship research) establishing age-related fecundability data: 20% monthly probability at age 30, declining to 3% at age 40+; protective effect of prior live birth documented.
- **Microplastics and Endocrine Disruptors (Cohort Studies):** Population-based research linking plastic exposure and endocrine-disrupting chemicals to worse IVF outcomes, lower live birth rates, and longer time-to-pregnancy.

### Supplements / Compounds
- **Estrogen (multiple formats):** Patches, pills, vaginal inserts, vaginal creams; used in hormone replacement/augmentation therapy for menopausal and perimenopausal symptoms.
- **Progesterone:** Alone or in combination with estrogen; particularly important when ovulation is irregular or absent; perimenopausal women sometimes respond well to progesterone monotherapy.
- **Testosterone (low-dose):** Added last to hormone regimen; necessary only in select cases; estrogen can convert to testosterone in some women.

### Books
- **The Fertility Formula: Take Control of Your Reproductive Future** by Dr. Natalie Crawford — Comprehensive guide covering reproductive health, hormone health, and actionable lifestyle factors for all women regardless of pregnancy goals; includes personal fertility journey narrative.

### Online Resources
- **Mail-In Sperm Analysis (CLIA-Certified Labs):** At-home collection kit sent to accredited laboratory; provides full sperm parameters (count, motility, morphology) without in-office visit; valid alternative to fertility clinic testing.
