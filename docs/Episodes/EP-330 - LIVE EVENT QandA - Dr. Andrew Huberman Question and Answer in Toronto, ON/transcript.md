## [00:33:02] What Does Your Morning Meditation Consist Of?

My morning meditation is not really a meditation, it's a perceptual exercise. And that perceptual exercise has a weird name because I gave it a weird name. I call it space time bridging, but it's not that. To me, one of the most interesting things about the nervous system is our ability to orient in different time domains.

We know from states of high stress that we start fine slicing time. The world becomes like a slow motion video because frame rate has increased. As a vision neuroscientist, in my laboratory, we were doing studies with virtual reality where we can crank up people's level of stress by giving them certain visual stimuli, and their ability to parse information is clearly increasing in the time domain — they're fine slicing, much in the same way that when you look at a slow motion video of somebody dunking a basketball, it's because the frame rate went up. So when we are in high alertness states, our frame rate increases. When we're very relaxed, our frame rate decreases.

It's also true that your visual system and your perception of time are inextricably linked. If you close your eyes and you focus on your internal state, you are fine slicing time. The metronome is your breathing and your heart rate combined. When you open your eyes and you look at something in your immediate environment, when you move from interoception to exteroception, your perception of time shifts fairly dramatically. And then as you look out further on to, say, the horizon, you extend the time domain even more. If you then imagine yourself on the whole globe, you extend your time domain even more.

So my morning meditation — it's more of a perceptual exercise — is to step through these different time domains: close my eyes and focus on my internal state, open my eyes and focus on something close by, look a little bit further, look a bit further, think about myself on the globe, the whole world moving. You're really extending your space domain, and then the time domain expands with it.

This perceptual exercise is a way of training my nervous system to shift deliberately between these different time domains. And for me, it's been very useful for improving task switching, something that, as you probably have noticed, I'm not very good at — I go into the trench, and I don't leave the trench very easily.
