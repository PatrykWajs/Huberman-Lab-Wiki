# Episode 93: Psychedelics & Neurostimulation for Brain Rewiring | Dr. Nolan Williams

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Nolan Williams — triple-board-certified psychiatrist, neurologist, and director of the Stanford Brain Stimulation Lab — joins Andrew Huberman for a comprehensive exploration of the neural circuitry of depression, trauma, and mood regulation. The conversation spans the full landscape of modern depression treatments: transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), psychedelics (psilocybin, MDMA, ketamine, ibogaine, ayahuasca), SSRIs, cannabis, and behavioral interventions including sleep deprivation and light therapy. A central theme is that depression is best understood as a circuit-level dysfunction rather than a chemical imbalance — the left DLPFC fails to govern the overactive subgenual cingulate, and effective treatments (TMS, psychedelics, chronotherapy) all converge on restoring that temporal hierarchy. Williams describes the landmark Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT) protocol, which achieved 80%+ remission in treatment-resistant depression in five days using individualized fMRI targeting and space learning theory. The episode also covers the neurobiological basis of each psychedelic compound, the opioid-system mechanism of ketamine, the THC/CBD distinction in cannabis, and closes with David Nutt's Lancet drug-harm analysis, which ranks alcohol as more dangerous than heroin when personal and societal harm are combined.

## Guest: Dr. Nolan Williams

**Dr. Nolan Williams, M.D.** is a triple-board-certified psychiatrist and neurologist and a professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at Stanford University School of Medicine. He is the director of the [Stanford Brain Stimulation Lab](https://bsl.stanford.edu), where his research focuses on using transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) — combined with psychedelics and learning protocols — to treat depression, PTSD, and other mood disorders. He is the principal investigator of the landmark SAINT/SNT study, which demonstrated 80%+ remission rates in treatment-resistant depression using an accelerated TMS protocol.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Depression is the most disabling condition worldwide and is now a major cardiac risk factor** — the American Heart Association lists it as the fourth risk factor for coronary artery disease alongside hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes. Moderate depression is as disabling as an acute heart attack; severe depression rivals untreated terminal cancer. The field is shifting from the flawed "chemical imbalance" model toward a circuit-based "Psychiatry 3.0" understanding — depression as a fixable arrhythmia of the brain. ([00:09:16](transcript.md#000916-depression-risk-factors-emergency-psychiatric-treatments))

2. **The brain-heart connection is a direct, measurable neural tract** — TMS over the left DLPFC propagates through the anterior cingulate, insula, amygdala, nucleus tractus solitarius, and into the vagus nerve, decelerating heart rate by ~10 bpm per stimulation train. Depressed individuals show elevated resting heart rates; vagus nerve stimulation (from the neck upward) also alleviates depression via the same tract. ([00:15:11](transcript.md#001511-the-brain-heart-connection-vagus-nerve-prefrontal-cortex))

3. **Right vs. left hemisphere asymmetry acts as a biological mood dial** — lesions connected to the left DLPFC on the human connectome cause depression; lesions connected to the right cause mania. Exciting the left DLPFC or inhibiting the right is antidepressant; exciting the right is anti-manic. This hemispheric balance is empirically confirmed with TMS perturbation studies but its developmental mechanism is not yet resolved. ([00:17:51](transcript.md#001751-right-vs-left-brain-hemispheres--mood-balance-connectome))

4. **In depression the cingulate leads the DLPFC; effective treatment reverses this timing** — healthy brains show DLPFC activation preceding the anterior cingulate; in treatment-responsive depressed patients pre-SNT, the cingulate fires first, generating semi-volitional negative content. SNT re-establishes DLPFC temporal dominance, and the degree of re-timing correlates directly with antidepressant outcome. ([00:39:00](transcript.md#003900-beliefidentity-rules-re-scripting-tms--talk-therapy))

5. **Precision fMRI targeting, not skull-landmark TMS, is essential for SNT efficacy** — each patient's DLPFC stimulation coordinate is the specific sub-region most anti-correlated with the subgenual anterior cingulate (sgACC) in resting-state fMRI. Generic targeting misses this circuit. The same sgACC–default mode network connectivity change is produced by both effective SNT and post-psilocybin treatment, revealing a convergent mechanism across modalities. ([00:45:49](transcript.md#004549-dorsolateral-prefrontal-cortex-tms--depression-treatment))

6. **Ketamine's antidepressant effect requires opioid receptor engagement, not just dissociation** — a crossover study with 50 mg naltrexone completely blocked ketamine's antidepressant effect while leaving the dissociative experience unchanged. This refutes the "experience is sufficient" hypothesis. Antidepressant duration from a single infusion averages ~10 days, far shorter than psilocybin or MDMA effects. ([00:54:27](transcript.md#005427-ketamine-the-opioid-system--depression-psychedelic-experience-or-biology))

7. **SSRIs work — but not by correcting a serotonin deficit** — multiple meta-analyses confirm efficacy in subpopulations for depression, OCD, GAD, and panic. The delay in onset (weeks) suggests the mechanism is downstream plasticity (BDNF upregulation, circuit recalibration) rather than acute serotonin elevation. SSRIs are not recommended for children due to risk of increasing suicidal ideation. Williams argues TMS and psychedelics are "Psychiatry 3.0" because they directly recalibrate circuits without systemic neurochemical flooding. ([01:03:42](transcript.md#010342-ssris-serotonin--depression-childhood-chemical-imbalance-or-circuit))

8. **Psilocybin dissolves rigid cognitive "rules" by globally increasing brain connectivity and entropy** — David Nutt and Robin Carhart-Harris found psilocybin paradoxically decreases overall brain activity while increasing global functional connectivity, loosening stuck sgACC–DMN coupling that anchors depressive self-representation. The therapeutic mechanism overlaps with exposure-response-prevention therapy: tolerating internal experience without compulsive suppression. ([01:13:58](transcript.md#011358-memories--rule-creation-psilocybin--rule-resolution))

9. **MDMA achieves ~2/3 PTSD remission in 1–3 sessions with years-long durability** — it suppresses amygdala reactivity while elevating serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine, opening a window for traumatic memory reconsolidation without overwhelming fear. The 1990s Science neurotoxicity claim was retracted after the lab discovered it had injected methamphetamine, not MDMA. Controlled studies in MDMA-only users and clinical trial patients show no neurocognitive damage. ([01:21:00](transcript.md#012100-mdma--post-traumatic-stress-disorder-ptsd-treatment-psilocybin--depression-treatment))

10. **"Letting go" during psychedelic sessions is a clinical instruction, not a metaphor — it mirrors ERP therapy** — exposure-response-prevention therapy for OCD works by having patients tolerate triggering stimuli without performing compulsions; the same letting-go of internal experience during psilocybin or MDMA sessions appears to drive therapeutic reconsolidation. Resistance during the session correlates with worse outcomes. ([01:34:53](transcript.md#013453-exposure-response-prevention-letting-go--depression-treatment))

11. **Ibogaine produces an 18–36 hour third-person life review with dramatic PTSD and addiction results** — patients re-experience autobiographical memories from a detached, empathic vantage, frequently reporting self-forgiveness of moral injuries. Williams' team conducted the first full neurobiological study in former Navy SEALs and Army Rangers, documenting pre-post improvements in depression, PTSD scales, and neurocognitive batteries. Primary safety concern is cardiac QT prolongation; EKG screening substantially reduces this risk. Schedule I in the US; legal in Mexico. ([01:45:35](transcript.md#014535-ibogaine--life-review-ptsd-depression--clinical-trials))

12. **Ayahuasca (DMT + reversible MAOI) reduces recidivism and shows antidepressant effects** — a Brazilian prison study found statistically significant reduction in return-to-prison rates in ayahuasca-exposed inmates vs. controls. The brew's precise pharmacological window (reversible MAOI prevents GI breakdown of DMT without inducing serotonin syndrome) was somehow discovered across Amazonian cultures among 10,000–20,000 plant species. Neurocognitively safe in adults and children at ceremonial doses. ([02:01:59](transcript.md#020159-ayahuasca-brazilian-prisoner-study))

13. **THC is pro-psychotic; CBD is antipsychotic and antiepileptic — modern cannabis is the opposite of natural** — selective breeding has maximized THC and eliminated CBD, inverting the probable original ratio. High-dose THC can precipitate psychosis that resolves upon clearance; high-dose CBD reduces symptoms in established schizophrenia and controls seizures in Dravet and Lennox-Gastaut syndromes. Adolescent exposure to high-THC cannabis (pre-prefrontal maturation, ~age 25) is associated with a ~4× increase in later psychosis. ([02:06:55](transcript.md#020655-cannabis-thc-cbd--psychosis-clinical-uses))

14. **Alcohol is the most dangerous drug by combined personal and societal harm — above heroin and cocaine** — David Nutt's Lancet analysis ranks alcohol first when personal health risk and societal harm (accidents, assault, cancer, drunk driving deaths) are combined. Psilocybin sits at the opposite end. Yet alcohol is served at medical school events and university graduations while far less harmful substances remain Schedule I, a double standard Williams and Huberman predict will be viewed like 1950s doctors smoking — as historical folly. ([02:14:52](transcript.md#021452-personal-relative-drug-risk--alcohol))

15. **Total sleep deprivation rapidly relieves depression but requires circadian anchoring to last** — a single sleepless night produces antidepressant effects in ~50% of depressed patients; recovery sleep immediately reverses the effect. Triple chronotherapy (sleep deprivation + sleep phase advance + bright light) retrains the dysregulated circadian system underlying many depressions, producing durable relief. Pro-anxiety in non-depressed individuals; must be medically supervised. ([02:20:42](transcript.md#022042-circadian-reset-for-depression-sleep-deprivation-light))

16. **SNT compresses 7.5 months of standard TMS into 5 days using space learning theory** — hippocampal theta-burst stimulation extends cortical plasticity for ~1 hour; spacing sessions ~60 minutes apart exploits the same memory-consolidation spacing effect as studying flashcards in rounds. Combining this with 5× the normal dose and individualized fMRI targeting yields 60–90% remission rates in treatment-resistant depression. Patients report no numbness or sexual side effects — they simply feel "back to normal." ([02:34:25](transcript.md#023425-space-learning-theory--tms-stimulation))

## Tools & Protocols

### Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)
- **What:** Non-invasive brain stimulation using focused magnetic pulses to activate or inhibit specific cortical regions via Faraday induction.
- **How:** A coil placed over the scalp delivers theta-burst stimulation (TBS) pulses to the left DLPFC; the precise target is identified using resting-state fMRI functional connectivity maps (site most anti-correlated with the subgenual anterior cingulate cortex, sgACC). TBS mimics hippocampal rhythms to extend plasticity for ~1 hour per session.
- **When:** Standard clinical protocol: 30 sessions over 6 weeks. Accelerated SNT protocol: 50 sessions over 5 days.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:22:34](transcript.md#002234-heart-rate--depression-behavioral-interventions-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation-tms), [transcript 00:45:49](transcript.md#004549-dorsolateral-prefrontal-cortex-tms--depression-treatment)

### Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT / SAINT)
- **What:** Accelerated, individualized TMS protocol for treatment-resistant depression achieving 60–90% remission.
- **How:** 10 TMS sessions per day for 5 days; each session ~9 minutes of TBS with ~50-minute inter-session breaks exploiting space learning theory; target is the DLPFC sub-region most anti-correlated with sgACC via pre-treatment resting-state fMRI scan.
- **When:** Treatment-resistant depression (failed multiple antidepressants). Administered in a clinical setting; commercial device available via Magnus Medical with FDA clearance.
- **Ref:** [transcript 02:28:43](transcript.md#022843-stanford-neuromodulation-therapy-snt-study); device: [https://www.magnusmed.com](https://www.magnusmed.com)

### Triple Chronotherapy
- **What:** Three-part behavioral intervention for rapidly resetting the circadian system and durably relieving depression.
- **How:** (1) One full night of total sleep deprivation; (2) sleep phase advance (progressively earlier bedtimes across several nights); (3) morning bright light therapy (10,000 lux light box, 30–60 min upon waking).
- **When:** Supervised inpatient or outpatient setting; useful for patients requiring rapid antidepressant relief including those with suicidal ideation. Not suitable for highly anxious patients — sleep deprivation exacerbates anxiety.
- **Ref:** [transcript 02:20:42](transcript.md#022042-circadian-reset-for-depression-sleep-deprivation-light); paper: [https://bit.ly/3CJCWiv](https://bit.ly/3CJCWiv)

### Psilocybin-Assisted Therapy
- **What:** High-dose psilocybin (5-HT2A agonist) in a supervised clinical setting to treat depression by disrupting the sgACC–default mode network coupling that anchors depressive self-representation.
- **How:** 25 mg synthetic psilocybin with trained therapists present; patient is instructed to "let go" — observe and release thoughts, emotions, and perceptions rather than resist them. Preparatory sessions (set/setting) and integration sessions surround the drug session.
- **When:** Research settings; ~1/3 achieve separation from placebo in blinded trials; ~1/2–2/3 in open-label. Effects last months from 1–2 sessions.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:13:58](transcript.md#011358-memories--rule-creation-psilocybin--rule-resolution), [transcript 01:30:38](transcript.md#013038-psilocybin-brain-connectivity--depression-treatment)

### MDMA-Assisted Therapy for PTSD
- **What:** Pharmaceutical-grade MDMA in a therapeutic context to allow traumatic memory reconsolidation.
- **How:** 1–3 sessions (75–125 mg MDMA) combined with extended psychotherapy; MDMA suppresses amygdala reactivity while elevating serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine, creating a window for fear extinction without overwhelming re-traumatization.
- **When:** MAPS Phase 3 trials; ~2/3 of participants show clinically significant PTSD improvement; durability in the years range reported in earlier follow-up studies.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:21:00](transcript.md#012100-mdma--post-traumatic-stress-disorder-ptsd-treatment-psilocybin--depression-treatment)

### Ketamine Treatment
- **What:** Dissociative anesthetic used for treatment-resistant depression (IV ketamine off-label; intranasal esketamine/Spravato FDA-approved).
- **How:** IV infusion (0.5 mg/kg over 40 min) or intranasal esketamine; antidepressant effects require opioid receptor engagement (blocked by 50 mg naltrexone) and promote dendritic spine enlargement. Dissociation is necessary but not sufficient for the antidepressant effect.
- **When:** Treatment-resistant depression; rapid effect (hours) but typically short-lived (~10 days per infusion) without maintenance dosing.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:54:27](transcript.md#005427-ketamine-the-opioid-system--depression-psychedelic-experience-or-biology)

### Ibogaine Treatment
- **What:** Long-acting psychedelic alkaloid from iboga tree root bark (Gabon, Africa) for PTSD, depression, and opioid addiction.
- **How:** Single oral dose (~15–20 mg/kg) in supervised setting after mandatory cardiac EKG screening; 18–36 hour closed-eye experience of third-person autobiographical life review with preparatory and integration psychotherapy.
- **When:** Currently Schedule I in US; legal in Mexico (MAPS trials ongoing). Cardiac screening required — QT prolongation is the primary safety risk.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:45:35](transcript.md#014535-ibogaine--life-review-ptsd-depression--clinical-trials)

### Ayahuasca
- **What:** Two-plant psychedelic brew (DMT + reversible MAOI) used in clinical research and sacramentally across South America.
- **How:** Oral ingestion in ceremonial or clinical setting; the reversible MAOI plant prevents GI breakdown of DMT and allows brain penetration without triggering serotonin syndrome. 4–6 hour experience. Must use a reversible MAOI — irreversible MAOI would be dangerous.
- **When:** Research contexts; sacramentally legal in Brazil and parts of South America; some US religious exemptions.
- **Ref:** [transcript 02:01:59](transcript.md#020159-ayahuasca-brazilian-prisoner-study)

### Exposure Response Prevention (ERP) / "Letting Go"
- **What:** Behavioral technique for OCD and compulsive behaviors; also the key therapeutic directive during psychedelic sessions.
- **How:** Patient observes distressing thoughts or urges without performing the compulsive response. During psilocybin or MDMA sessions: therapists instruct patients to observe and release internal experiences rather than resist or suppress them. The same tolerance-of-experience mechanism underlies both approaches.
- **When:** OCD treatment (standard outpatient); within-session instruction during psilocybin or MDMA therapy.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:34:53](transcript.md#013453-exposure-response-prevention-letting-go--depression-treatment)

### Behavioral Heart Rate Modulation (Physiological Sigh)
- **What:** Exhale-emphasized breathing to rapidly downshift heart rate and prefrontal-limbic tone via the vagal-DLPFC tract.
- **How:** Double inhale through the nose (reinflates collapsed alveoli, maximizing lung volume) followed by a long, slow exhale through the mouth. Engages the vagus-to-brain pathway in the bottom-up direction as a complement to top-down TMS.
- **When:** Daily; especially during anxious-depressive states or acute stress. Most effective in mild depression where volitional capacity remains intact.
- **Ref:** [transcript 00:22:34](transcript.md#002234-heart-rate--depression-behavioral-interventions-transcranial-magnetic-stimulation-tms)
