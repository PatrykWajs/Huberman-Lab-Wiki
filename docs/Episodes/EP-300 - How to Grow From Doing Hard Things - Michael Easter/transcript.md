## Discomforts, Modern vs Ancient Life

**Michael Easter:** You've changed my life, actually. You've changed my behavior on a daily basis. An ex-girlfriend of mine who lives in Colorado had just listened to your book, The Comfort Crisis, and she was saying that she believes it explains why people in her hometown are so mentally robust into their 70s, 80s, even 90s. She grew up in the mountains, and her mom would put her in a basket and send her down the river to a friend's house. She grew up in cold water, in the outdoors almost all the time, doing hard things — and she was absolutely convinced that the bodily expectation of daily activity was determined by that early upbringing.

**Andrew Huberman:** So I read the book and started doing hard things on a regular basis, mostly rucking. I make it a point each week to write down one thing I'm going to do that is truly uncomfortable. It has transformed my mental health, and I was already feeling really good. So let's talk about modern life versus ancient nervous systems. What do you think the human brain and nervous system were designed to do, and how does that fit into modern life?

**Michael Easter:** I think we evolved in a context where we had to do hard things all the time. Life was uncomfortable — you spent 100% of your time outdoors, it was often too hot or too cold, you were physically active all day, walking something like 20,000 steps a day on average, usually carrying something heavy. You had long periods of unstimulated downtime where you would talk to other people face to face. Life was effortful, challenging, and you had to go through those discomforts in order to survive.

**Michael Easter:** The promise and peril of modern life is that we no longer have to do these hard things to survive. If you want food, you can go to the gas station on the corner. You don't need to move much — you could do Uber Eats, work behind a screen, and essentially never leave your house. We've removed physical discomfort from our lives, and even boredom, which is an uncomfortable thing, now has an easy effortless escape in the form of a phone. The temperature stays at 72 degrees. This list goes on and on.

**Michael Easter:** Now, let me be clear — this is a good shift in the grand scheme of time and space. But it does come with problems, because we evolved in environments of discomfort. Humans are wired to do the next easiest, most comfortable thing because that would have served us in the past: conserve calories, eat more food when available, don't spend energy unnecessarily. Now those same instincts backfire. Call this an evolutionary mismatch.

## Modern Problems, Exercise, Trail vs Treadmill Running, Optic Flow, Hunting

**Andrew Huberman:** As a neurobiologist, when I step back, it seems like what we were trying to optimize for throughout human history was to limit discomfort, ensure the species persists, and care for our offspring over a long developmental period. So if our goal in human evolution is to rid ourselves of discomfort and make things easier, why at some point is more comfort bad for us?

**Michael Easter:** There are side effects. Most of the diseases that kill us today result from overconsumption of food and insufficient movement — a lot is tied to metabolic health. I like to say these are good problems to have in the grand scheme of time and space. I'd rather my problem be that I have to go exercise than that I have to go hunt every single day. But they are problems we need to solve. And you're right — a lot of what exists now in health and wellness is just trying to bring the outdoors indoors.

**Michael Easter:** Exercise is a great example. No one "exercised" in the past — exercise is something we made up basically after the industrial revolution. We got sedentary jobs, realized the people sitting all day were developing strange new health problems while those still moving around weren't, so we told people to just go move around for the sake of it. That becomes this strange idea of movement for its own sake. But it makes sense when the average American is walking 4,000 to 6,000 steps a day.

**Michael Easter:** And when we try to solve for this problem, the way we do it is interesting. We take a belt, put a motor on it, put the person in an air-conditioned building, and blast CNN at them the entire time. But when a person runs outdoors on a trail, so much more is happening. The trail is unpredictable — you have to navigate rocks and ruts, think about the weather, deal with temperature changes. You run through trees and open spaces, which has a real emotional and spiritual benefit. You might see a random coyote or bighorn sheep. You encounter the unexpected.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's also optic flow — what you get when you're hiking, walking, cycling, or doing other outdoor movement. We know it has a powerful effect in suppressing some of the brain areas involved in fear. Francine Shapiro, who developed EMDR therapy for trauma, came up with those lateral eye movements on a walk and was essentially trying to bring the walk into her clinic. For years I thought EMDR was complete garbage, but multiple papers and animal studies showed that this lateralized repetitive eye movement suppresses the amygdala. Forward ambulation — walking and running — suppresses fear circuits in the brain.

**Michael Easter:** That makes evolutionary sense. Think about persistence hunting — a dangerous act you had to do every single day to survive. If you had fear suppression during an act that is somewhat perilous, that would give you a huge advantage in actually taking down that animal. And it's also a great metaphor for life: just keep moving forward, focus on the goal, don't hesitate — you can get pretty far.

## Risk & Rewards, Intellectual vs Experiential Understanding

**Andrew Huberman:** Were you the kid who would hold the firecracker to the very last second, or were you more the writer kid?

**Michael Easter:** I was not the kid holding the firecracker to the last second. I'm still not that person. But if I have a good reason — if there's a greater reward at the end — I'm perfectly willing to accept risk. Think about skateboarding: the point wasn't to fall, the point was to become a better skateboarder, and in the process you might get banged up. I've always oriented that way. The focus is always on the overall goal.

**Michael Easter:** As a journalist I read a lot of studies and speak to experts, but I also find that I get the best information — and can better communicate it so someone might actually take action — when I go to the source and have a story around it. That has taken me to war zones, the middle of the Amazon jungle, the Arctic for 30 days, and recently a long hike through southern Utah and the Grand Canyon. On those trips, you peel back the deeper layers of whatever idea you're trying to communicate.

**Michael Easter:** There's a big difference between intellectual understanding and experiential understanding, and it's that experiential understanding I want to get to the heart of. I'm not suggesting everyone has to go to the jungle or the Arctic. Start where you're at, do something that's a little out of your comfort zone, see what happens. You didn't die — great. Try it again the next day. By continuously pushing that edge, you find that you don't fall off it; the edge tends to expand. And as it expands, you end up a better person.

## Modern Luxuries, First-World Problems, Gratitude, Tool: Volunteer

**Andrew Huberman:** Here's a question: if humans have introduced so many comforts that small things feel uncomfortable — for me, airports are annoying, I hate being secondary-screened even though it's such a trivial thing — and yet for many people life already feels hard. Work is hard, things are expensive, it's hard to get enough sleep, the world seems unstable. So if someone wants to start leaning into productive discomfort, how should they start?

**Michael Easter:** Let me use myself as an example. When I fly up to the Arctic — from Las Vegas to Anchorage, then to Kotzebue above the Arctic Circle, then a tiny plane that drops me 100 miles out — on the way there I'm like everyone else complaining about the flight. Seats too small, cramped, plane too hot, movies are bad, the coffee's not very good, there's a baby crying.

**Michael Easter:** Then I spend 33 days in the Arctic. If I want water, I hike down to a stream and carry heavy water bags back up. I'm freezing the entire time. If I need to use the bathroom, I hike out on the tundra with a rifle because of grizzly bears. I'm starving the whole time. Everything is hard and uncomfortable for more than a month.

**Michael Easter:** On the flight back to Las Vegas? It was pure luxury. That chair — I hadn't sat in a real chair for a month. The coffee was hot, it was the best thing I'd ever drunk. The crying baby: hand him over, I got him. The movie in the seatback: Fast and the Furious 79, greatest thing I've ever seen. And when I go to the bathroom, I hit this little red button and hot running water comes out of a faucet. I hadn't had hot running water on my hands for more than a month. And I thought: this is a tube of steel hurtling through the air at 600 miles per hour, 35,000 feet above sea level, and it's one of the most amazing things ever.

**Michael Easter:** What did it take for me to realize that flight is a miracle instead of a personal injustice? I had to go out and reset that goalpost in a totally different world. There's a psychologist — David Levari, I believe now at Brown — who did a study published in Science called "prevalence-induced concept change." They showed 800 faces, asking people to judge threatening versus non-threatening. Midway through, they started showing fewer truly threatening faces. What happened? People started judging borderline faces as threatening — they said "threatening" just as many times as before.

**Michael Easter:** The same pattern appeared with research proposals judged as ethical or unethical. As fewer truly unethical proposals appeared, people got more nitpicky. The finding: as people experience fewer and fewer problems, we don't become more satisfied. We simply lower our threshold for what we consider a problem. As the world has become more comfortable and we encounter fewer real traumas, we don't stop and think "this is amazing." We broaden our definition of what a problem is. The same number of problems, but they've become progressively more hollow.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's the science of first-world problems — a moving goalpost.

**Michael Easter:** Exactly. When I got back from the Arctic, nothing rattled me for about a month. But I can't go to the Arctic every month. So what can I do in daily life to push that goalpost back? Volunteering is one example — if you live a decent life, go help people whose lives are harder and you'll gain perspective. That takes an hour a week. Recovery meetings are another: you hear stories from people at rock bottom, and you walk out realizing what a real problem actually is. You need these moments that put things in context, and you need the narrative you tell yourself around them — that framing is a really important part.

## Rites of Passage, Tool: Challenge, Narrative & Purpose; Embracing Discomfort

**Andrew Huberman:** When you think about rites of passage — these arose spontaneously in tribes around the world — the point was to take someone from point A to point B where they're more capable, more confident, more competent. You sent them out to do something challenging. They would struggle, face problems, figure things out, come back changed. And then people would gather around and ask: what did you learn? What story are you telling yourself about that?

**Michael Easter:** Shaping the narrative around a life event becomes critically important for mental health. Think about event centrality: how central is an event going to be to your life, and what story will you tell around it? People who take something bad that happened and make it the central component of their personality tend to have worse mental health. People who take it and ask "what can I learn from it, how can I grow, where might it take me?" have completely different trajectories. The narrative you tell yourself really matters.

**Andrew Huberman:** I started keeping a folder where I look back at different phases of life and list out the bullet-point events, with no particular endpoint in mind. What I find is that the things that felt the worst at the time often turned out to be the best things ever, and the big wins were almost always the outgrowth of prior negative experiences. It keeps coming back to this theme: discomfort seems to be a prerequisite for truly feeling good about oneself. They can't exist separately.

**Michael Easter:** The vast majority of things that are good for us and help us grow are going to be hard. Exercise sucks when you're doing it, but you get the long-term benefit. A salad is less delicious than a Dorito — and anyone who argues has been eating way too many salads. About 18% of Americans actually meet the federal exercise guidelines of 150 minutes of moderate to vigorous activity per week with strength training twice a week. Why? Because for most people it's uncomfortable — we have internal levers that dissuade extra movement. But on the other side of that discomfort is improved health, improved mental well-being. Doing the uncomfortable thing is essentially the buy-in to a better life.

## Choice, 2% Study, Silence, Tools: Do Slightly Harder Things; Notice Resistance

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some difficult things we can introduce to daily routine that have been shown to make us feel better, besides exercise, sunlight, and cold water?

**Michael Easter:** My Substack is called the 2% Newsletter, and here's why. There's a study that found only 2% of people take the stairs when an escalator is available. 2%. But 100% of people know that taking the stairs would be better for them in the long run. This tells me we're wired to do the next easiest thing, but living better in modern life often requires doing slightly uncomfortable things that are obvious and right in front of us. Do the slightly harder thing that you know will give a long-term return — take that.

**Michael Easter:** Examples: if you have a phone call, pop in your headphones and walk while you take it. Carry your groceries rather than getting a cart. Park in the farthest spot. These things sound obvious, but no one actually does them. Non-exercise activity thermogenesis — NEAT — is all the movement in a person's life that isn't dedicated exercise, and it often outweighs the benefits of exercise by calories burned. Mayo Clinic data suggests people who move around a lot more in daily life can burn 800 extra calories just from incidental movement.

**Michael Easter:** Another area: we have increased the world's loudness fourfold as human beings. Silence is actually pretty good for us in this context of noise. People feel weird at first in silence, a little unsettled, but as time goes on they tend to calm down and reset. Most people blast music the moment they sit down, or keep the TV on all day not because they're watching it but because they need noise. Cutting that out, even though it's a little hard at first, will improve you over the long run. I wrote a post called the "2% Manifesto" on my Substack that lists many different ways to apply this — it's a mindset shift: how can I take something I have to do and make it a little harder to get a benefit?

**Andrew Huberman:** What you said reminds me of noticing the feeling of resistance. The strawberry hulls I leave sitting there rather than picking them up — it's less about the hulls themselves and more about noticing how trivial that resistance is, yet how pervasive it is. The things we resist doing erode some circuit in the brain. And there's something about the contrast between prior discomfort and current experience — the more uncomfortable our prior experience, the better the next phase of life feels.

## Cognitive Challenges, Walking, Screens, Tool: Sitting with Boredom

**Andrew Huberman:** In the cognitive and emotional domain — things like reading a difficult book, having a hard conversation with your partner — do you intentionally do those things in all areas of your life?

**Michael Easter:** My wife and I go on very long walks and that's where all the magic happens. We'll do 8 to 12 miles on a Saturday — that's four hours together. The first hour is small talk. By hour two, we're getting into the deep and gritty stuff. There's something about forward ambulation with other people that is really life-giving, something almost spiritual about the connection that comes from it. Those conversations wouldn't happen if we were sitting on the couch watching Netflix.

**Michael Easter:** There's also the value of boredom, which I've written about in The Comfort Crisis and Scarcity Brain. Boredom is an evolutionary discomfort that tells us: whatever you're doing right now, the return on your time invested is running thin — go do something else. In the past, if we were foraging in an area and finding nothing, boredom would kick in and prompt us to try somewhere else. In modern life, when that discomfort kicks in, the "something else" is just effortless escape through a phone or Instagram. Sitting with boredom and leveraging it to see where else it takes you beyond a screen can be really valuable.

**Michael Easter:** There's also an important point that a lot of media around screens misses. If we take two hours off our phone screen time, people often get bored and turn on Netflix — not much different. You're still just receiving information being beamed into you rather than seeing what the world can offer you. Rather than focusing on less phone, I like to think: more boredom. Get yourself in a space where boredom kicks on. It'll be uncomfortable, your mind will wander, and you might find some good ideas. The best ideas come when we've removed ourselves from outside stimulation. It's why people have that cliché of getting their best ideas in the shower — you're removed from the noise, you're just kind of there, and bam.

**Andrew Huberman:** Throughout history, people get ideas from dreams during nighttime sleep, or from the liminal states between waking and sleep — times of inactivity with no sensory input, when the brain processes things. It makes perfect sense that in daydreaming or boredom, new ideas would surface. But you've got to write them down — a good idea forgotten is gone.

## Capturing Ideas, Attractor States, Tool: Being in Nature

**Michael Easter:** I usually have a notebook on me. Much like a nighttime dream where you think you'll remember it and then you won't — same thing happens during the day. Joe Strummer of the Clash believed that if you have an idea, you have to write it down not just because you'll forget it, but because even if you remember it, you can't capture the essence of the inspiration unless you write it at that moment.

**Michael Easter:** On my most recent 40-day hike through southern Utah and the Grand Canyon, I couldn't stop to write — we had too much mileage to cover. So I used voice notes on my phone, and came back with about 500 of them, some 10 seconds, some six minutes. I realized I should have started doing that on day one, not day three or four, because some of the best thoughts from the early canyons just evaporated.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a neurobiologist at Caltech named David Anderson who has done interesting work on attractor states — the idea that the brain doesn't just switch on demand into deep focus. You warm up, ratchet into it, and over time it's like a ball bearing on a surface that becomes more concave until you're in a deep trench of focus. We can entrain these states, link them to specific cues like making coffee at 9 a.m. But if you look at most people's daily activity through the lens of attractor states and ask what are you training your brain to do, the answer is often: scrolling, reading comments, texting, noise. We've created these attractor states of busyness and distraction, and then the end of the day comes and nothing got done.

**Andrew Huberman:** Getting out into nature and removing that overload forces you into a different mental direction — it's the overload principle applied to the mind. Which is one of the reasons I love these practices so much.

## 2% Rule, Rites of Passage, Tool: Misogi Challenge

**Andrew Huberman:** So there's the 2% rule — taking on the identity of doing the harder thing that gives long-term benefit. And then at the other end of the spectrum is the Misogi concept. Could you explain Misogi?

**Michael Easter:** The cliff notes: Misogi is a modern rite of passage designed to teach people what they're capable of and give them an experience that really changes them. I heard about this from a guy named Marcus Elliott. He has an MD from Harvard but decided to get into sports science, runs a facility called P3 in Santa Barbara, and works with NBA and NFL athletes. He realized that the big changes that force a player to be better — that get them to a better state when a game is on the line — can't be measured. And he does this practice he calls Misogi.

**Michael Easter:** The idea: once a year you go out and do something really, really hard. He defines "really hard" as having a 50/50 shot at completing whatever your Misogi task is. Second rule: you can't die. The implication is do something pretty hard but survivable. Even when we take on challenges, we usually know we're going to complete them — people don't run marathons genuinely unsure if they'll finish. The Misogi should have genuine uncertainty. And what tends to happen when you go out and do something truly challenging, truly unsure if you'll finish, is you get to a moment where you think you've hit your edge. All is lost. But if you keep going, one foot in front of the other, you get to another moment where you look back and realize: I thought my edge was back there, but I'm clearly past it. I sold myself short. And then the question becomes: if I sold myself short here, where else in my life am I selling myself short? That's where the big changes happen.

**Michael Easter:** If you look back in history, we had things like this in the form of rites of passage. Doing something that truly thrusts you beyond what you thought you were capable of — where you have to figure things out, really doubt yourself, and overcome — becomes a great teacher for the human spirit. And the Misogi doesn't have to be extreme. Someone once came up to me after a talk and said her Misogi was trying sushi. She'd always been afraid of it, forced herself to do it, didn't love it but didn't hate it — and more importantly, it taught her to ask what other fears she had about things that are probably totally fine. That opened a door to try flying alone, visiting a friend. It can be anything. Just push up against your edge, wherever your edge is.

**Andrew Huberman:** How important is it to keep the Misogi quiet versus sharing it?

**Michael Easter:** Better to keep it to yourself. We live in a world where people do things for external validation — likes on social media, being seen as the neighborhood badass. If you can just do something only for you, that makes it more valuable. The moment you decide to do it in 59 minutes because someone else did it in an hour, you've put a ceiling on yourself. Do it for yourself and you might do it in 55. And there's something powerful about having a lever only you know about that you can pull when you need to.

## Phones, Sharing with Others, Social Media, Tool: Reflection vs Screen Time

**Andrew Huberman:** What if we ran an online experiment where people refrained from smartphone use for a certain number of hours per day, and instead of posting their sleep score, they posted the hours they managed to be completely offline? Would the community element detract from the experience of being away from the phone?

**Michael Easter:** It's complicated. There would probably be some upsides to the sharing — the community element and competition might reinforce people's behavior in ways they wouldn't have if alone. But there would probably be individual differences. Some people don't do well with large social elements to things — personally, I could just go run 26 miles on my own without paying an entry fee and wearing a bib. For others the community is the whole point.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think we should do the experiment. Build a community of people who use social media for actual learning and connection, but aren't leaning on it for the mental chewing gum version all day long. One app I've found useful is called ClearSpace — when you go to open a selected app like Instagram, it shows you an inspiring quote and makes you wait 10 seconds, then asks how much time you actually want to spend. It gets you intentional rather than just reflexively opening apps.

**Michael Easter:** My mom had cancer about 10 years ago — she's fine now — and she was in the waiting room in her gown for one of her annual checkups. As the nurse left, she reached for her phone and realized she'd left it in the car. She had to sit and feel the anxiety and vulnerability. By being forced into that moment, she started asking: why don't I want to feel this? Because I value being alive. Why do I value being alive? All these reasons came up: I appreciate this thing, this other thing. That insight taught her a lot about how she wanted to spend her time. Her behavior did change afterward. Having moments where we don't immediately go for the easy comfortable thing can lead to real insights.

**Andrew Huberman:** She sounds like an amazing woman. I'm really happy she's healthy.

**Michael Easter:** She is. She's a single mom, I'm an only child, she got sober when I was about a month old, was sober for 40 years. The cards were stacked against us, but she worked her ass off and built us a pretty good life.

## Dopamine, Spending vs Investing, Guilt

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to talk about dopamine as currency. I've long believed dopamine is the universal currency of motivation — it literally controls movement in the body, which is why people with Parkinson's who lose dopamine neurons can't move. And I was thinking about the idea of spending versus investing our dopamine. Scrolling is spending — and it's the kind of spending we don't even notice we're doing. We're leaking dopamine. It's not transformative like coming back from a Misogi thinking "I lived." It keeps you in the rut of looking for more because it's like mental chewing gum.

**Michael Easter:** That's exactly right. And the investing is usually things that are going to be a little more challenging, not as hyper-stimulating — things you maybe wouldn't want to do at first, but once you've done them enough you realize "this has really changed me." Hopefully you start to crave them. That's where we want people to get with all sorts of things that can enhance their lives.

**Andrew Huberman:** And reflection — in states of boredom, meditation, prayer, whatever the orientation — is another form of investing dopamine. When I look at the neurobiological literature, most of us are spending out during the day, resetting with sleep, spending again, resetting again — a life that can become kind of meaningless. Your work in The Comfort Crisis and Scarcity Brain is really about how to invest dopamine in effort and reflection, as a way to capture more capability and lean into things.

**Michael Easter:** I love the language of spending versus investing. And I'll add: people who pile up money and invest endlessly without ever spending, maybe you also need to learn to spend it sometimes. I used to beat myself up for going on Instagram and looking at nonsense. Then I realized — you wrote for five hours, you got a workout in, you took the dog for a walk, you helped around the house. Dude, watch a dog video for 20 minutes. It's fine. You've invested a bunch. Buy the thing you don't necessarily need. I stopped having guilt around that — I could actually appreciate it more because I'd earned it.

## Relaxation, Shared Identities & Community, Music, Tool: In-Person Meeting

**Andrew Huberman:** We don't all have to become self-punishment machines in service of building dopamine reserves. Being able to relax, enjoy things, and savor them is another source of investment. Walking with your wife — real, in-person connection — makes us feel so many things. We evolved to connect to other humans. Netflix and chill can be a great thing if it's not the only thing.

**Michael Easter:** Exactly. And the internet has allowed interesting new ways to find tribes to belong to. I'm into the Grateful Dead — when I got into them, I found Reddit threads discussing live shows, like the difference in how Jerry played a certain song in 1978 versus 1979. You end up becoming friends with Deadhead778 who you've never met, and then eventually you're at a show connecting with people you'd never otherwise meet — the hippie with two bucks in a dream to get to the next show, and the hippie with a Rolex taking his private jet, all in it together for this shared thing.

**Michael Easter:** On my 40-day hike, we had to hitchhike to resupply towns. In a gear store in Escalante, Utah, I picked up something with a Deadhead logo, and the woman working there — who'd seen the Dead 500 to a thousand times — immediately connected with me and offered us a ride 40 miles back to the trailhead. That shared identity instantly allowed a real connection with a total stranger.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about recovery groups? That's the power of in-person community — shared identity, people keeping tabs on each other, sharing stories people might not know anywhere else. If Bill Wilson had founded AA online on AOL Messenger, it would have helped some people, but it would not have the power of getting people together in person to converse.

**Michael Easter:** Today, I wrote a post on 2% about the value of gathering. I spoke to a researcher in Oregon who said the internet when used appropriately can be a great community builder — and the best next step is when those online communities figure out ways to meet in person. I do events I call the "Don't Die" event — different from the Bryan Johnson Don't Die event, this is the original. It's me and my friend Mike Moreno, a former CIA case officer. We teach people travel and wilderness survival skills over two days. Most attendees come from my Substack and already know each other from the comments. They show up, they know each other from the internet, and then they hang out and do awesome stuff together. That's the perfect model.

## Loss of Gathering Places, Internet & Distorted Views, Hitchhiking

**Michael Easter:** The Don't Die events get people in person, and I think we need to fight for that. You hear a lot about how people spend less time together — partly because of the decline of organized religion, which used to be the hub of sociality in towns. There's also a loss of places like old pubs that served as community pillars. A journalist named Gwendalyn Bounds wrote a book called Little Chapel on the River about how after 9/11 she moved to a town called Garrison, New York, where the heart of the community was an old Irish pub by the train station. People across the political spectrum would come, give each other grief but all in fun, and it was like the heart of community gathering. Those places are being replaced by chains with no unique identity — all predetermined by someone in a corporate office 3,000 miles away.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a case for finding those places that still exist and hanging out there. You don't even have to drink at a pub — I don't drink and I still love bars.

**Michael Easter:** The internet also dehumanizes people. It's easy to yell and scream at an icon the size of a thumbtack who said one thing, but if that same person was sitting across the bar from you, you might not even bring up politics — you might actually think they're a great person. Another example from the hike: we hitched a ride with a couple from China, both academics, right in the middle of the trade war. We got in the car, and nobody gave a damn about any of it. Two Americans, one of whom worked in government for 20 years, and two people from China, just connecting and talking about what it's like to live in our respective countries. These people were fantastic. It takes those face-to-face experiences to realize that most people are far more kind, happy, and willing to help than you'd ever expect. If you're just existing behind a screen where people can shout, you get a distorted view of the world.

## Misogi & Entry Points; Daily Schedule, Caffeine Intake

## Outdoor Adventures, Backpacking & Nutrition

**Andrew Huberman:** When you're out on these adventures, do you maintain your routines — writing early, sleeping early? And what do you eat?

**Michael Easter:** On outdoor adventures I'm usually up pretty early with the sun. I sleep a lot better and longer when I'm out in the wilderness. I bring instant coffee — terrible stuff, but it tastes incredible out there because that's what you've got. I don't bring a stove, both because of the extra weight and because of horror stories of people boiling water on awkward stoves and then knocking it into their lap in the middle of nowhere. I'll just mix instant coffee with whatever temperature the water is.

**Michael Easter:** For food, my four rules on the trail: one, it's got to be good on my stomach — if something upsets your stomach and you have to hike 25 miles, you're having a really bad day; two, it has to be calorie-dense — two pounds of peanut butter has way more calories than two pounds of apples and takes less space; three, it has to taste good or you won't eat it; four, look at nutritional composition, though when you're eating 4,000-plus calories a day it's hard not to get enough protein. I found these Met-Rx Big 100 Bars in a small gas station — 400 calories, 30 grams of protein, all sorts of vitamins and minerals, tastes like a candy bar. Magic on the trail.

**Andrew Huberman:** Very different from what you'd recommend people eat at home.

**Michael Easter:** Exact opposite. At home: eat more foods that are ingredients rather than have ingredients. On the trail: you're going to eat a lot of crap for 40 days, and when you get home, lean into salads. On that last 40-day hike we averaged 20 to 25 miles a day carrying full packs. I was trying to eat 4,000 to 5,000 calories a day and still lost about 13 pounds. Herman Pontzer at Duke did some back-of-the-hand math and estimated I was probably burning about 6,300 calories a day.

## Camping & Sleeping, Nature, Three-Day Effect

**Andrew Huberman:** Some people will hear "40 days" and feel like that's not accessible to them. But you mentioned something important: even camping for just two or three nights can provide incredible reset and benefits. Kenneth Wright at the University of Colorado Boulder has done beautiful experiments taking students camping — no alarm clocks, sleeping after sunset, waking around sunrise. Just two nights of camping in the Colorado mountains was able to reset circadian rhythms for melatonin and cortisol, the hormones that bookend our days. So while there are many things one can do — cold showers, exercise, forced early hours, dimming lights — getting out into nature and camping for a couple of nights has a lasting effect on circadian rhythms.

**Michael Easter:** Totally. And we have so much incredible public land in the United States. Only about 3 to 4% of the country is developed — the rest is farmland and open land. The best stuff often isn't in national parks but in these middle-of-nowhere places. Even car camping counts. Any amount of time outdoors, especially a little more rugged and off the grid, has so many benefits.

**Michael Easter:** There's a researcher at the University of Utah named David Strayer who has done work on what he calls the "three-day effect." After three days in nature, really beneficial things happen — people come back calmer, more collected, more reset, more aligned. He started studying it because a guy named Ken Sanders — who owns a famous rare book shop in Salt Lake City and was a friend of the environmental writer Edward Abbey — had been calling this phenomenon "the three-day effect" among his friends for years. Strayer thought: I've felt that, but I've never heard anyone put a term on it. And so he started doing research into it.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think it takes time for us to drop into these different ways of being — and that's also neural. Our nervous system shapes itself around our interactions. The attractor states I mentioned earlier: it takes a few days to wash away the attractor states of scrolling, notifications, busyness, and build new ones. That's the three-day effect from a neural standpoint.

## Sea Squirts; Misogi Adventures & Cognitive Vigor, Writing, Happiness

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to share an idea I've been wrestling with. Sea squirts — tunicates — live two lives. They're mobile, have a nervous system, swim for part of their life. Then at some point they descend onto a rock, fix themselves to it, and live the rest of their life fixed there. And when they land, they eat their own nervous system — specifically the parts that are no longer required for moving around. Here's the idea: perhaps the areas of the brain that atrophy first in humans — the neural pathways that deteriorate first — are the areas involved with jumping, landing, throwing, navigating uneven surfaces, encountering lack of familiarity. And perhaps the deterioration of those pathways sets in motion a cascade that causes the loss of neurons in other areas. Maybe we're a lot like the sea squirt, just starting earlier nowadays.

**Michael Easter:** I like this idea. Everything I've found suggests that we need to do the things we could easily offload onto devices or other people — but if we do that, we lose something. Does my cognitive vigor seem to be increasing? Yes — definitely. Confounding variable is I stopped drinking at 28, so up to 28 I was a damn idiot. But I've become a sharper writer and sharper thinker over time.

**Michael Easter:** From a writer's perspective, you will write better and have more material if you go out and do things. Go to the source. Report it. Even just talking to another human being — not just reading studies behind a screen — gives you so much more to work with. And for the average person who isn't a writer: that's a story. You want to die with a lot of badass stories. That's a life well lived. Happiness isn't an endpoint — it's a rolling average of your behaviors. What do your behaviors look like? More awesome behaviors or more crappy behaviors? Awesome behaviors probably involve other people, doing things that push you a little, teaching you something about yourself, getting out of your office and away from the screen. That's it.

**Andrew Huberman:** The word "happiness" is a slippery slope — you can chase feeling states endlessly. What we're really describing is the feeling of dopamine being trickled out in response to effort and then getting the rewards of that effort, repeated. Those rewards include the non-effort states of being able to lean into social things with more ease and richness, because you put in a great day. That's deep pleasure. That can only be built through this connection between the gears we've been talking about.

## Effort & Rewards, Addiction, Dopamine, Catecholamines

**Michael Easter:** A lot of behaviors that hurt people in the long run have a similar structure to behaviors that help them. Gambling has a random reward schedule. But my job as a journalist also has a random reward structure — I go into a situation, I don't know what I'm going to find, the reels of the slot machine are spinning, the dice are falling. Same exact circuitry, but channeled into something that becomes more rewarding over the long run.

**Andrew Huberman:** The circuitry is exactly the same for gambling and going out to find a great story. My graduate adviser taught me: the fun was doing the experiments. You can celebrate wins, but you can't let yourself internalize wins more than the effort to get there. My father, when I published my first paper in Science, didn't say congratulations. **Michael Easter:** Same circuit — dopamine, and that's really a proxy for norepinephrine and epinephrine. Norepinephrine operates in the brain to make you feel alert, adrenaline in the body. ## Humans, Running & Carrying Weight, Fat Loss, Tool: How to Start Rucking

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us why rucking and carrying weight are so valuable and distinctly different from just hitting the gym.

**Michael Easter:** When we were hunting caribou in the Arctic, I was thinking about the research that humans evolved to run long distances to chase down animals in the heat — persistence hunting. We won the thermoregulation game. We sweat, we don't have much fur, and other animals can't manage their heat. Dan Lieberman at Harvard had a 2004 paper on this. But I thought: what happens after you actually kill an animal? You have to carry it back to camp. And humans are unique among mammals — no other mammal can pick up weight and carry it a long distance. It absolutely shaped us into who we are, allowed us to conquer the globe, carry our tools into the unknown on two legs with hands freed up.

**Michael Easter:** So rucking is essentially what the military calls walking with weight in a backpack. I've started shifting my language to "weighted walking" or "walking with weight" — if I tell my 75-year-old mother to "ruck," she Googles it and gets confused by military content. Weighted walking: you get cardio stimulus from covering ground plus strength work from loading your skeletal and muscular system. Two-in-one. It generally burns more calories per mile than walking or running because of the added weight.

**Michael Easter:** A small study on backcountry hunters in Alaska — only 12 people because they could only find 12 crazy enough — found that these guys lost a significant amount of weight, but it was all from fat. They actually gained a very small amount of muscle, which shouldn't happen when you're losing weight. I think it's this amazing activity we've engineered out of our lives. Humans evolved to carry — babies, meat from hunts, gathered food. Then we got cars, grocery carts, furniture dollies.

**Andrew Huberman:** How should someone start?

**Michael Easter:** Start light. After The Comfort Crisis came out with a whole chapter on rucking, I got destroyed by military guys saying I was going too easy. But they started with 100 pounds — of course that would injure a beginner. I tell women to start with 5 to 20 pounds, men 10 to 30, depending on fitness level. Ease in. You can build up over time. My go-to is 35 to 40 pounds — uncomfortable and challenging, but not so soul-crushing I have to stop. Don't go over 50 pounds as a firm number, or over a third of your body weight. Military research supports that upper limit, and the injury rate within reasonable weights is exceedingly low — not much higher than plain walking.

## Motivation; Slot Machines, Loss Disguised as a Win, Speed

**Michael Easter:** A lot of the "failure to launch" young people aren't accessing that circuit in a generative way. Anna Lembke — author of Dopamine Nation — says when people ask what to do if they haven't found their passion, she says "mow the lawn." People think that sounds like a mom telling them what to do, but she understands: you do something you don't want to do, you complete it, job well done, and you're learning the process. Then you superimpose that understanding onto things that are hopefully meaningful.

**Michael Easter:** In Scarcity Brain I got into slot machines — I live in Las Vegas and you see people playing them all day long. Everyone knows the house always wins, and yet people do it. I ended up at a casino used entirely for gambling behavior research, funded by Caesars and some tech companies. It's a metaphor for the world we live in: because things can be tracked and digitized, the saw can continuously be sharpened. Until about 1980 nobody really played slot machines, then a guy named Cy Redd noticed his grandkids would play Atari all day. He applied that to slot machines: make them screen-based. Suddenly you could program the odds rather than being constrained by physical spinning reels.

**Michael Easter:** With screen-based slots, you can bet on 40 different lines. And this is where "loss disguised as a win" comes in — you bet a dollar and win 40 cents. Technically you lost, but something exciting happened, the machine lights up, ding-ding-ding. The machine can now have exciting events happen far more frequently while you're just slowly losing your money instead of quickly. People weren't playing physical slot machines much before 1980 because with physical reels you might play 20 games and nothing happens — that behavior extinguishes fast. But if something happens every second or third pull, suddenly behavior locks in.

**Michael Easter:** Then there's speed. With the old physical handle, the average slot player played 400 games per hour. Redd made it a button. The average shot up to 900 games per hour. Swipe right, swipe left, infinite scroll, instant page loads. No friction. The faster you can do a behavior, the more likely you are to do it. And all of this got figured out in the strange, heartless, but freaking beautiful town I live in called Las Vegas.

## Tool: Avoid Frictionless Foraging; Sports Betting, Speed; Junk Food, Three V's

**Andrew Huberman:** You talked about the speed of the slot machine, and I want to reframe the dopamine concept in a way that's more intuitive: anytime we find ourselves in frictionless or low-friction foraging, we're in serious trouble. That's the red flag. The moment you're in frictionless foraging mode, your baseline is dropping and you don't even realize it.

**Michael Easter:** Exactly. A great example of how this played out recently is sports betting. It used to require driving to the casino, placing a bet on a game hours away, waiting three hours to watch it, then cashing in or taking the loss. Once it got legalized in many states and moved to cell phones, you don't have to drive anywhere. Two buttons. Then the gambling industry realized: if speed increases gambling rates, what can we do about the fact that a game is three hours long? What if you could bet on a play? How many plays are in a game — hundreds. So now you have live in-game prop bets, parlays with 12 teams, bonus things thrown in. It's a train wreck for younger men especially.

**Andrew Huberman:** And frictionless foraging plus online pornography for younger men — numerous examples of this same pattern everywhere.

**Michael Easter:** The Three V's from the junk food industry illustrate the same principle. In the 1970s, the food industry wanted people to eat more. They invented snacking as a category. A junk food consultant said any food that's going to take off needs: value (cheap), variety (lots of options — look at the 10 flavors of Doritos), and velocity (quick and easy to eat, you can pound a bunch of calories and want to keep going). Once they locked that formula in, you start to see obesity take off in the US in the 1970s. And mental obesity follows the same formula with TikTok. The parallels between physical and cognitive — the same slot machine logic applied to what you put in your body and your mind.
