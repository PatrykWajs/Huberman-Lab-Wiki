# Episode 110: How to Access Your Creativity | Rick Rubin

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman sits down with legendary music producer Rick Rubin to explore the nature of creativity — what it is, where it comes from, and how anyone can access it. Rubin draws on decades of work with artists across virtually every genre to argue that creativity is not a personal trait but a relationship with a universal source, and that the artist's job is to tune their perception, remain open, and follow feeling as a guiding signal. The conversation covers Rubin's four-phase creative framework, the role of the subconscious, the power of nature and sleep in resetting creative capacity, and the surprising importance of professional wrestling in Rubin's own creative life.

## Guest: Rick Rubin

**Rick Rubin** is one of the most celebrated music producers of all time, having worked with artists spanning heavy metal, hip-hop, country, rock, and pop — including Run DMC, Beastie Boys, Public Enemy, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Metallica, Jay-Z, Johnny Cash, Adele, Tom Petty, System of a Down, LL Cool J, Kanye West, and many more. He co-founded Def Jam Recordings in 1984 and later served as co-president of Columbia Records. In 2023 he published *The Creative Act: A Way of Being*, a book exploring the universal principles behind creative work, applicable to any domain.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Creativity flows from a universal source, not from the individual.** Rubin frames creative ideas as existing in a kind of "cloud" that anyone can tune into — the artist's role is to receive and refine, not to manufacture. ([00:08:23](transcript.md#000823-creativity--ideas-cloud-analogy))

2. **Feelings are the primary compass for creative work.** When something resonates, a feeling arises before language can describe it — Rubin describes this as a "surge of energy" first experienced hearing the Beatles at age three or four. Learning to trust and follow that feeling rather than intellectualizing it is the core skill of a creative person. ([00:17:36](transcript.md#001736-feelings--creative-ideas))

3. **Personal taste is more important than external opinions.** Art made to please others is a commercial endeavor, not an artistic one. Rubin consistently advises artists to return to their own honest response to the work as the only reliable metric. His analogy: tasting two dishes and knowing which one you want to finish eating — that directness of feeling is what an artist must protect against the noise of external consensus. ([00:22:01](transcript.md#002201-rules-choice--art-personal-taste--others-opinions))

4. **The creative process has four distinct phases: seed collecting, experimentation, crafting, and completion.** Each phase requires a different mindset. Deadlines are harmful in the seed and experimentation phases but helpful in the crafting and completion phases. Rubin discovered this by reverse-engineering instinctive decisions he had made across decades — the book is a collection of these reverse-engineered principles. ([02:08:16](transcript.md#020816-creative-work-phases-structure--deadlines))

5. **Disengagement from conscious focus unlocks subconscious problem-solving.** When stuck, deliberately stepping away — walking, sleeping, showering — allows the subconscious to work. Rubin treats distraction and rest as active creative tools, not failures. He works on multiple projects simultaneously: leaving Project A means fully engaging Project B or C, so the subconscious continues processing A while the conscious mind is occupied elsewhere. ([01:04:14](transcript.md#010414-focus-disengaging--subconscious-anxiety))

6. **Self-doubt is not an obstacle to eliminate — it is a signal to be used.** Self-doubt lives in everyone and exists to serve the work. The goal is not to silence it but to dance with it productively. ([02:15:32](transcript.md#021532-self-doubt--performance))

7. **Nature is the most reliable reset for creative perception.** Spending time in nature — oceans, walks, sunlight — strips away conditioned filters and returns access to raw, unmediated experience. Rubin notes that even a familiar beach can reveal itself as a completely different place after six months away, illustrating how perception is always filtered and always changing. He calls nature "the only truth." ([01:52:40](transcript.md#015240-resetting-energy--nature-nostalgia))

8. **Morning liminal states are prime creative windows.** The slow transition out of sleep — before the structured conscious mind fully activates — is one of the best times for ideas to surface. Rubin falls asleep to a lecture or audiobook to stop self-talk from preventing sleep, then wakes slowly (about one hour) and goes outside into sunlight before engaging with anything structured. Protecting the in-between state is as deliberate as his waking routine. ([02:01:56](transcript.md#020156-sleep-waking-up--sunlight-capturing-ideas))

9. **Perception is always filtered and therefore always incomplete.** Everything the brain registers is an abstraction — a principle Rubin links to Nobel laureate Richard Axel's view. Pareidolia (the brain's tendency to see faces in clouds, or animals in wood grain) reveals how relentlessly the mind imposes symbolic pattern onto raw sensory data. Recognizing that our view of reality is partial — and deliberately seeking contrast, novelty, and fresh vantage points — expands creative range. ([00:47:40](transcript.md#004740-perception-filters-contrast--novelty))

10. **The brain is a confabulation machine — we live inside stories we unknowingly invent.** Rubin observes that when something unexpected happens, the mind instantly generates the most plausible explanation available and then treats that invented story as what actually occurred. This is the normal mode of human cognition, not a pathology. Recognizing it is essential for creative and intellectual freedom — and it's why wrestling (where everyone agrees the story is made up) can feel more honest than "real" events. ([01:18:26](transcript.md#011826-process--cloud-perception--storytelling))

11. **"No input, no output" (Joe Strummer's Law) is a core creative principle.** You cannot generate interesting output without feeding yourself interesting input. Rubin actively seeks music, experiences, and ideas he does not yet know to maintain creative aliveness. ([02:39:44](transcript.md#023944-look-for-clues-belief-effects))

12. **The ability to sustain focused attention — not happiness or talent — is the most important creative capacity.** Whether someone is happy or unhappy matters less than whether they can remain present enough to feel what the work is doing. Success can become its own distraction when external rewards begin competing for attention with the work itself. ([02:46:25](transcript.md#024625-attention-emotion--art))

13. **Belief shapes reality in measurable ways.** Rubin and Huberman discuss how belief effects operate in art, science, nutrition, and medicine — and how holding open, non-dogmatic views preserves creative and intellectual freedom. Rubin applies this empirically: he tries fringe interventions and keeps whatever works, without attachment to the source. ([02:29:42](transcript.md#022942-public-opinion--science-light-acupuncture--nutrition))

## Tools & Protocols

### Morning Sunlight Exposure

**What:** Spending time in direct sunlight shortly after waking, ideally outside.
**How:** Rubin aims to get into sunlight as soon as possible after waking, then walks on the beach for one to two hours.
**When:** First thing in the morning, before engaging with structured work or phone.
**Ref:** [02:01:56](transcript.md#020156-sleep-waking-up--sunlight-capturing-ideas)

### Beach / Nature Walk (Daily Creative Reset)

**What:** Long, solitary walk in nature — specifically on the beach — without phone interaction (audio only: lectures, audiobooks, podcasts).
**How:** Walk alone for 1–1.5 hours each morning while listening passively to audio content. Use voice memos to capture ideas that arise.
**When:** Morning, after sunlight exposure, before structured work begins.
**Ref:** [02:01:56](transcript.md#020156-sleep-waking-up--sunlight-capturing-ideas), [01:52:40](transcript.md#015240-resetting-energy--nature-nostalgia)

### Voice Memo Idea Capture

**What:** Recording emerging ideas verbally into a phone rather than typing.
**How:** Speak ideas aloud into the voice memo app during walks or whenever ideas arise. Use transcription services (e.g., rev.com) to convert to text. Rubin mentions wanting to move to audio methods after learning that Richard Axel — a Nobel laureate — writes manuscripts by pacing his office and dictating into his phone.
**When:** During walks, in liminal states (waking/pre-sleep), whenever an idea surfaces.
**Ref:** [02:01:56](transcript.md#020156-sleep-waking-up--sunlight-capturing-ideas)

### Mantra Meditation (Transcendental Meditation style)

**What:** Repeating a mantra or sound internally to override the conscious talking mind and create a trance-like state that quiets self-talk.
**How:** Rubin learned TM at age 14. Typically practiced first thing after waking — go outside, close eyes, meditate. If doing twice daily: second session before dinner. On long flights: sustain for up to an hour or more. Rubin once meditated the entire New York to LA flight, losing track of time entirely.
**When:** Morning (primary); optional second session pre-dinner; opportunistically during travel.
**Ref:** [02:48:07](transcript.md#024807-mantra-meditation-awareness-meditation)

### Awareness (Open Monitoring) Meditation

**What:** Eyes closed (or open), simply noticing whatever sensory experience arises — sounds, physical sensations, temperature, feelings — without labeling, interpreting, or storytelling.
**How:** Sit quietly, inventory whatever arises in real time (sounds, body sensations, warmth, etc.). No judgment, no narrative. Can be done eyes open or closed.
**When:** Anytime; particularly useful as a complement to mantra meditation.
**Ref:** [02:48:07](transcript.md#024807-mantra-meditation-awareness-meditation)

### Monroe Institute Surgical Support Series (Pre/Post-Surgery)

**What:** Audio recordings designed to promote healing, reduce surgical trauma, and support recovery before and after medical procedures.
**How:** Listen with headphones before being put under anesthesia and during recovery. Rubin used this before a surgery and reported accelerated healing and reduced trauma response. The medical staff mistakenly believed he had already been given a sedative, based solely on his physiological state — he had not.
**When:** Before surgery, during anesthesia induction, during recovery.
**Ref:** [02:48:07](transcript.md#024807-mantra-meditation-awareness-meditation) | External: [Monroe Institute Surgical Support Series](https://www.monroeinstitute.org/products/the-surgical-support-series)

### Pre-Sleep Wind-Down (Low-Stimulation Audio)

**What:** Falling asleep to a spoken-word lecture or audio content to prevent self-talk loops; avoiding violent or high-arousal content before sleep.
**How:** Play a lecture, podcast, or audiobook at low volume to occupy just enough of the conscious mind to prevent rumination. Avoid horror, violent content, or anything emotionally destabilizing.
**When:** Nightly, as part of the pre-sleep routine.
**Ref:** [02:01:56](transcript.md#020156-sleep-waking-up--sunlight-capturing-ideas)

### Yoga Nidra (Pre-Sleep)

**What:** A guided relaxation practice used to clear the mind and prepare for sleep.
**How:** Rubin mentions yoga nidra alongside pro wrestling viewing as acceptable pre-sleep wind-down practices.
**When:** Before sleep, as an alternative or complement to passive audio content.
**Ref:** [02:01:56](transcript.md#020156-sleep-waking-up--sunlight-capturing-ideas)

### Four-Phase Creative Framework

**What:** A structured way of understanding where a project is in its lifecycle, so the correct mode of engagement can be applied.
**How:**
- **Phase 1 — Seed Collecting:** Ongoing, always active. No deadline. Collect anything interesting without expectation of use.
- **Phase 2 — Experimentation:** Plant seeds, create conditions, observe what grows. No deadline. Set the stage but do not dictate outcomes.
- **Phase 3 — Crafting:** Engage as craftsman — trim, combine, shape. Internal deadlines become acceptable.
- **Phase 4 — Completion:** Final edit, shareable version. External deadlines are appropriate here.

Phases are not strictly sequential — movement between them is fluid and continuous.
**When:** Applied to any creative project from inception to release.
**Ref:** [02:08:16](transcript.md#020816-creative-work-phases-structure--deadlines)

### Deliberate Disengagement (Subconscious Activation)

**What:** Intentionally stepping away from a creative problem to allow the subconscious to process it.
**How:** When stuck, switch to a completely different activity (walk, sleep, shower, watch wrestling). Do not force conscious analysis. Return when the subconscious has surfaced something.
**When:** Whenever conscious effort produces diminishing returns or a sense of being blocked.
**Ref:** [01:04:14](transcript.md#010414-focus-disengaging--subconscious-anxiety)

### Novelty Seeking (Unfamiliar Music and Experiences)

**What:** Actively consuming art, music, and experiences you do not already know, particularly outside your primary domain.
**How:** Rubin listens extensively to classical music, old jazz, and music he has never heard. Uses Shazam to identify songs that surprise him. Seeks unpredictable inputs to maintain fresh perception.
**When:** Ongoing; integrated into daily listening habits.
**Ref:** [02:19:13](transcript.md#021913-predictability--surprise-authenticity)

### Wrestling as Creative Palate Cleanser

**What:** Watching professional wrestling (approximately 11 hours per week for Rubin) as a deliberate pre-sleep and creative reset activity.
**How:** Watch wrestling before sleep. Rubin describes it as one of the few things that completely relaxes him. Its unpredictability, honest theatricality ("we know it's made up"), and variable reward schedule (you never know what will happen) make it an effective tool for resetting perception and dopamine. The key property is low stakes — "I don't really care what happens, which feels good."
**When:** Pre-sleep; as a buffer between structured creative work and rest.
**Ref:** [01:35:38](transcript.md#013538-wrestling-energy--reality-dopamine), [01:52:40](transcript.md#015240-resetting-energy--nature-nostalgia)

### Considering the Inverse / Opposites Exercise

**What:** When facing a creative problem or stuck point, deliberately construct the opposite of what you are attempting — use it as a contrast tool to clarify or find a new path.
**How:** Ask: what is the opposite of what I am trying to do? What would it look like if everything about this were reversed? Use the contrast to reveal assumptions. Rubin notes that this mirrors the structure of perception itself: blue only exists in relation to yellow; night only exists because there is day. Contrast is not a trick — it is how reality is built.
**When:** During experimentation or crafting phases when the direction feels unclear.
**Ref:** [01:29:13](transcript.md#012913-limited-resolution-considering-the-inverse)
