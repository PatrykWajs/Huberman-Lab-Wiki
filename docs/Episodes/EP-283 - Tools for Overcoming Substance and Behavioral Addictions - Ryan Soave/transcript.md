## [00:01:32] Addiction, Addictive Behaviors, Relief

**Andrew Huberman:** Ryan Soave, welcome. You're the guy that people call, reach out to, cry to when everything comes crashing down. And you have this incredible gift really to orient people in time and space when that sort of thing is happening. You do this for men, women, teens, kids, adults, families. I would love for you to explain to people listening what addiction is and how you see it show up in people's lives.

**Ryan Soave:** First, I'd like to make a little bit of a distinction. When we look at the DSM, the diagnostic manual that we use for diagnosing psychological disorders, not really addiction as a diagnosis. We can look at people and look at biological, psychological and social factors and make a diagnosis — a substance use disorder, an alcohol use disorder, whether it's moderate or severe or they're dependent on it. Not all people that show up with an alcohol use disorder for a point in time are necessarily an addict. It might have been a life circumstance that brought them there.

**Ryan Soave:** I like to broaden that definition. The question I'll ask people is: does it have you or do you have it? Is it driving you and your behaviors? Because I don't really see addiction as the problem — addiction is the solution. Whatever they're addicted to is the solution to some underlying stressor. As humans, when we're uncomfortable, when we're experiencing pain, our immediate reaction is to get out of that. And when that stress becomes really big, we're going to look for the things that are going to impact us a lot quicker — taking a drink, using a drug. Once people start doing that, depending on what it is, they could become physically addicted very quickly.

**Ryan Soave:** One of the doctors who initially supported the founders of Alcoholics Anonymous, his name was Dr. Silkworth, and he wrote this opinion: men and women drink essentially because they like the effect produced by alcohol. I believe that effect is relief. People are looking for some sort of relief, and the question has to be: what is it that they're looking for relief from?

## [00:06:10] Rehab, Detox, Physical & Emotional Stability

**Andrew Huberman:** When somebody comes to you, or in some cases is brought to you, who's in the throes of addiction — what's your first line of attack? Are you focused on what the underlying stressor is, or are you trying to stabilize them first?

**Ryan Soave:** I serve as Chief Clinical Officer for Guardian Recovery. We do treatment in different states. We serve thousands of people a year, and we have medical detoxes that we start with. If somebody needs to be detoxed from alcohol or drugs, we're not going to start asking questions about the underlying stressor. We're going to do medical assessments, nursing assessments. Sometimes we have to send them to the ER if they're in dire need or so acute they can't even be in the facility.

**Ryan Soave:** We're going to get them physically stabilized. The thing with addiction is a lot of people just say stop. But for people that just stop — assuming they're not in danger — if you just stop alcohol, you could have seizures and die. Even if they went to a detox and were able to stop, their life doesn't generally get better right away. In fact, a lot of times it gets worse. They've been using this substance as a medicator. Now all of a sudden the medicine's gone and they've got to deal with life.

**Ryan Soave:** First, we understand what's going on with them biologically so we can make sure they're safe. As we support them over the days that they start becoming more comfortable, their physical conditions start to stabilize. Then we can start to understand what's going on with them in their environment — their pattern of use, what effects it's having in their life. We want to understand how it's playing out biologically, psychologically, and socially, and really get a clear picture of who this person is.

**Ryan Soave:** We try to create a microcosm of their social universe so they can experience some stressors. We're having them communicate with their families, face some of the things they don't want to face. We have a saying: treatment can't really begin till the crisis occurs. And what that means is more of the internal or existential crisis. If someone's coming in going "well I'm great, everything's great," we're never able to really get to the causes and conditions of it.

**Ryan Soave:** What I really do with my clients is help them learn how to feel bad. We don't put that on the website because no one's going to come to us saying "how can I feel really bad?" But I think that's at the core of it. How can we build a distress tolerance so that they can face discomfort, face pain, and not choose the thing that gives immediate short-term relief that's going to end up hurting them and those around them? When people are able to be available for the depths of discomfort, then they really become more available for the heights of joy and satisfaction in life.

**Ryan Soave:** We start to build that capacity so we can start looking at the beliefs — the limiting beliefs — that have been driving them, that are often set from earlier on in their life. That's often what we talk about as trauma. Not always a big event that happened on a single day, but the shapings that people had in their life that were adaptive. People are very adaptive. Kids in their formative years will develop strategies to live that adapt them in a way they can survive the environments they're in. But if that adaptation which was appropriate in their family, their school, their environment — if it was something toward a survival response, a fight-or-flight type response — and that's never resolved, later in life they're still applying these adaptive strategies in situations where it's not appropriate.

## [00:46:10] Creating a Life Post-Addiction, Jellinek Curve

**Andrew Huberman:** Something I've observed is that sometimes behaviors provide energy that feeds a bigger ecosystem — gives a sense of purpose, great relationships, and to remove that thing would disrupt the whole web. That's sometimes what scares people about removing the addiction.

**Ryan Soave:** This gets to what we would call in Maslow's hierarchy of needs the self-actualization: they're able to build a life they never thought they could have before. You'll hear people say "a life beyond my wildest dreams." For a lot of those people, that's just being able to meet anything with some sense of peace.

**Ryan Soave:** There was a biophysicist — I think his name was Jellinek — at Stanford at the end of his career, and he wrote a book in the 50s or 60s called The Disease Concept of Alcoholism. Out of that came what's called the Jellinek Curve. It starts at the top and it's for alcoholism — where people start drinking and their tolerance increases. But they start to experience some problems — hung over, late for work, arguments. As they keep drinking and descending down this curve, they enter what I think is called the critical phase. That's where actually tolerance decreases. They're dependent on it. They need it just to get by. They can't keep the job, can't keep their relationships, their physical body is starting to fall apart.

**Ryan Soave:** When those people are able to get treatment and find recovery, the curve starts to go back up the recovery side. In the beginning they're not feeling great — they've lost the thing that helps them get through life. But if they can stay in a contained environment, whether a treatment center or a 12-step program community, eventually they start seeing that they can build a life, finding pleasure from life rather than from the substance. Their tolerance for stress starts to build, and they move toward self-actualization.

**Ryan Soave:** Recovery is not about finding a sense of peace that you stay in. Balance is not standing still on both feet — balance is dynamic. With the recovery process, you're able to recognize when you're out of balance sooner and return to it quicker, and have the tools to know what the emotional conditions are that you might be facing every day.

## [00:55:30] Tool: Emotional Weather Forecast, Gratitude, Plan, Strivings

**Ryan Soave:** Something I give to a lot of my clients is what we call the Emotional Weather Forecast. The first part is they've got to really know themselves — identifying their character defects and their virtues, just like a balance sheet where you want to know your liabilities and your assets. In the 12-step world, there's a line from Bill Wilson's AA literature: humility is an honest recognition of who and what we've become followed by a deep desire to become who we can be. If I don't know where I'm starting, I can't know how to get somewhere.

**Ryan Soave:** So once they understand these assets and liabilities, to look at each day ahead. We start off with gratitude. Gratitude isn't just about being thankful for things — I really believe gratitude is meeting what is as it is. I try to be grateful for things I'm challenged by as well. Then laying out your plans for the day — not a detailed calendar, but just knowing what's happening. Then looking at what your emotional state is currently: fears, resentments, anger, guilt, shame.

**Ryan Soave:** When I can take what's going to happen today and what I'm already experiencing, I can look at what I'm doing and think: there are some character liabilities or defects that might come up. If I have to travel today with three flights, I can predict what emotional disturbances can happen, coupled with the state I'm already in. Much like going on a trip where I would look and see what the weather's going to be — if it's going to be rainy, I pack a raincoat. I won't avoid getting wet, but once I start getting wet I can get my raincoat and put it on.

**Ryan Soave:** The last part is to look for what I'm going to watch for and what do I want to strive for. I'm going to strive for being patient, tolerant, kind. Just because I write this down doesn't mean I won't experience impatient moments. But when I get impatient, I can remember this is what I want to watch out for today, and use one of the tools I've laid out — maybe just walk away, take a breath, remind myself what I'm striving for today.

## [00:01:12:03] Tolerating Stress, Sailing Analogy, Discomfort

**Ryan Soave:** I believe one of our biggest challenges as human beings is that we confuse discomfort with threat and respond to discomfort as if it's threat. ## [00:01:16:03] Building Distress Tolerance, Proactive Behaviors, Cold Plunge

**Andrew Huberman:** I think you know the adrenaline response is fast. It's great training: this feels uncomfortable, I want to get out, but I'm going to learn to control my thinking in this uncomfortable circumstance. What it comes down to is sensations in our body that we're experiencing that feel really uncomfortable — and if we have some training, we can sense them a little earlier.

## [00:01:23:55] Stilling Leads to Seeing, Tool: Recognizing Stress Response; Relationships

**Andrew Huberman:** There are sensations in the body that come about very fast because the adrenaline response is fast. **Ryan Soave:** Someone gets a text message that says "we need to talk later" — they immediately go into their perception or their narrative from the past about what that means. But it's just words on a screen and we don't even know what it means yet. Now I'm oriented to the past rather than the present. It creates an automatic reaction. The solution is to first become aware that this is happening, then find a way to stop and intervene — maybe those 20 seconds, taking a few breaths, walking away.

## [00:02:08:58] Transmuting Energy, Running, Dopamine, Feelings

**Andrew Huberman:** Foraging physically through space is the same from the perspective of the dopamine system as foraging mentally. The dopamine system evolved for this — any animal searching for water, searching for food, searching for mates. It's the movement forward through space that satisfies the dopaminergic system. And the most impressive study in my mind on NSDR shows these big increases in dopamine from resting — a 65% increase in endogenous dopamine or something like that. There's something about still body, active mind that ramps up dopamine stores.

## [00:02:15:41] Cocaine, Amphetamine, Stimulant Addiction

**Andrew Huberman:** Things that really hit the dopamine system hard — cocaine, amphetamine, Adderall, other stimulants. Is there something unique to those addictions?

**Ryan Soave:** There's not a physical detox from stimulants like amphetamines or cocaine the way there is from heroin, opioids, or alcohol and benzodiazepines. Coming off stimulants is really hard — it's a psychological addiction. There's an obsession of the mind that gets triggered. There isn't really a physical detox, but they can't seem to get out of that obsession. Smoking crack, aside from shooting cocaine intravenously, leads to the fastest increase in dopamine — it's the rate of increase that's just as important as the peak.

**Ryan Soave:** In working with those, there's a lot of energy that goes into just having them become still. I see it as: stimulants help the physical body kind of match the internal state of the nervous system. That's one of the reasons we see stimulants work for ADHD. I've seen people that when they take stimulants, they get really calm.

## [00:02:18:21] Overcoming Porn Addiction, Shame

**Andrew Huberman:** These days I hear a ton of desperate stories, mostly from young guys — thousands of young men — about porn addiction. They're telling me they can't stop watching porn. What do I tell them?

**Ryan Soave:** Porn addiction at the time, specifically related to video pornography, was having the same impact on the brain in young men as crack cocaine. It's extremely powerful. It has a lot of other effects in that it sets up a very unrealistic perspective of what sex and intimacy is. It can also set their sexual template — they can quickly escalate from what might seem like normal sex to violent sex, bringing a lot of shame. It can escalate quickly and impact their real-life relationships.

**Ryan Soave:** Stopping completely is the answer. It can be similar to other addictions — first the admission that it's a problem, then being able to talk to somebody, start taking some accountability around it. Identify the behaviors that start leading to that. It can be done in therapy, or there are 12-step groups for that — SA or SLA — to identify what their sobriety would look like and how they're going to be held accountable to that. There's also software available to block access, or an accountability partner who monitors the websites you go to.

**Andrew Huberman:** One thing that makes porn addiction different is the extra layer of shame associated with it. Reducing the shame around it is key to helping them recover. If they're holding it in, not sharing it, there's no place for it to go but shame. And shame separates us. Separate, we're not connected. Not connected, we're alone. And alone, we keep carrying that forward.

**Ryan Soave:** Secrets keep us sick. They're going to keep it secret, and that means they won't seek help in person. They might reach out to someone like you on a podcast, which is a more anonymous way. Direct them back to: treat this like any other addiction — you've got to talk about it, you've got to have some sort of admission, find a community around it, most likely work with a therapist to understand what's driving the behavior.

## [00:02:30:03] Struggle, "Discomfort Appetite"

**Andrew Huberman:** Beware the things that come easily and quickly. If it's pornography and you can get the illusion of pseudo-sex. If it's relaxation from a drink. If it's fast money. All these positive reward signals are masking what will soon happen — that thing is going to make you lopsided internally and make you crave it more. Things that come easy are traps.

**Ryan Soave:** Struggle is distress, and as long as we're struggling, we're in the distress tolerance. I remember in graduate school, I had a paper accepted by the journal Science. I was over the moon. My graduate adviser said "the pleasure was the work of doing the paper." That taught me to repeat that process. The reward is in the process — the effort is the reward.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can we put on the shelf that perhaps we have an appetite for discomfort that we can either satisfy through healthy things or unhealthy things?

**Ryan Soave:** I think that's a good way to put it. We can call it the Soave-Huberman discomfort appetite. We're built to expend energy and then sleep and then wake up and expend energy again. If we're not expending the energy, how can we sleep? And then we wake up with more stored stress and carry the stressors from the day before. One of the beauties of recovery is they talk about one day at a time — just getting through this day.

## [00:02:36:09] Addiction Treatment, Detox, Rehab Centers, 12-Step Meetings, AA

**Andrew Huberman:** If people out there are in a place where they know something's got them as opposed to them having it, what do you suggest?

**Ryan Soave:** There are a lot of options. In our centers, we strive to create access — insurance, Medicaid coverage in different states. But another great thing is to just go straight into a 12-step meeting. They're everywhere. How do people find those meetings? Go online, Google "12-step in your area." Narcotics Anonymous, Alcoholics Anonymous, Cocaine Anonymous, Overeaters Anonymous, Gamblers Anonymous — every one of them has a central office. There are also online meetings, 24 hours a day, anywhere in the world.

**Ryan Soave:** I always tell people to try six different ones. They're free. You'll find your group. The organizational structure of Alcoholics Anonymous is really incredible — there are no leaders, it's a very flat organization, politically unaffiliated. They have a vow of poverty; if someone died and donated a million dollars, they can't take more than $10,000. It's been like this for 80-some years.

**Ryan Soave:** The idea of 12-step was discovered by Bill Wilson and Dr. Bob Smith, who co-founded Alcoholics Anonymous. Bill Wilson had been white-knuckling sobriety, going to the hospital and helping others. He found that by trying to help others, he stayed sober. He was on a business trip in Akron, Ohio, where Dr. Bob Smith lived. He came out of a business meeting and was at the hotel, hearing the bar, had some change in his pocket, was pulled between the bar and the phones — and for whatever reason he went to the phones. He called and asked if there were any drunks he could help. They directed him to this local doctor Bob Smith, also an alcoholic. They met, started talking, started helping each other, then went and helped another guy and then another. One man's decision — instead of going to the bar, going to the phone — transformed millions of lives.

**Ryan Soave:** For people who don't like the 12-step spirituality, there are other recovery programs like Smart Recovery or Refuge Recovery. Go out and try different things. Even the act of trying different things is going to help them move out of being stuck. For families, there's Al-Anon, Codependents Anonymous, Families Anonymous, Adult Children of Alcoholics — there's stuff out there for everyone.

## [00:02:46:54] Is Addiction the Problem?, Trauma, Stress & Addiction Cycle

**Andrew Huberman:** Is addiction the problem, or is addiction an attempted solution to a different but less obvious problem?

**Ryan Soave:** The short answer is both. The addiction is really the solution to some sort of discomfort or stress they're experiencing, but then the addiction itself becomes problematic. It's the person's attempt to find relief from whatever stressor they're experiencing — but it's a solution that becomes problematic. It takes on a life of its own. It becomes its own trauma. Drinking alcoholically and blacking out is a trauma to your body, to your brain.

**Ryan Soave:** There's this cycle I always use: trauma, stress, and addiction. Expanding the definition of trauma to wound — it starts with a wound, and then stress is the relationship to that wound, much like a limp after a broken leg. When stress gets big enough, people look for relief through behaviors or substances. Then the addiction itself creates more trauma, which then leads to more stress, which then leads to more addiction — a perpetual motion machine. The medicine becomes the source of stress.

## [00:02:49:05] GLP-1 Agonists & Addiction Treatment?, HALT; Addicted to Stress?

**Andrew Huberman:** Are GLP-1 agonists like Ozempic and Wegovy being used for addiction outside the context of weight loss?

**Ryan Soave:** I believe there's some initial research going on that GLP-1s can help with cravings and addiction in general. There's an acronym called HALT — Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired — which seems so simple, but it's so true. People tend to be at higher risk for relapse when they're hungry, angry, lonely, or tired. If you're hungry, you're looking to elevate your blood sugar, and anecdotally, diabetics who maintained their blood sugar properly had less chance of relapse.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can somebody be addicted to stress?

**Ryan Soave:** I think people can have their nervous system set in a way that they need that level of stress or activation just to get to baseline, just to feel alive. I've seen this especially with special forces combat veterans coming home — a lot of the mundane stuff almost didn't feel like they felt alive. They needed really risky behaviors, really high-adrenaline behaviors, just to get to what might feel normal for them. It's a drive — an intensity addiction.

## [00:02:52:44] Sugar Addiction, Caffeine; Ibogaine, Psilocybin & Considerations

**Andrew Huberman:** A lot of questions about ibogaine, probably because of the incredible work of my colleague Nolan Williams at Stanford with single ibogaine trials for people getting over alcoholism — mostly in veterans, covered on the Rogan podcast. What are your thoughts on ibogaine and other psychedelics like psilocybin for addiction?

**Ryan Soave:** I've seen some pretty incredible things happen with people. The rates of remission from alcohol use disorder with one or two ibogaine trials is somewhere between 40 and in some cases 80% — with therapeutic support, other things happening, and evidence of brain plasticity in a number of brain areas.

**Ryan Soave:** My general take on psychedelics: if I look at PTSD, I really see that as a state disorder — people get stuck in a certain state, the survival state. Things like psychedelics can help people have an experience where they recognize another state, that they have access to something other than survival. Those touchstone experiences can really give them something to build on.

**Ryan Soave:** What I'm most concerned about is the ethics we develop around psychedelics — who's delivering it, what are the rules. There are people with psychological and psychiatric disorders that it can really impact in another way — schizophrenia, bipolar. I think like any other miraculous-seeming approach to medicine, they're a great window into how we can be and an experience of who this person I can be — but I don't think there's going to be some immediate structural change that lasts on its own. The danger is then people start using them over and over and lean on them as a crutch. It's like a trampoline that lets you jump up and see the top of the building, but you haven't built the staircase or the elevator yet.

## [00:02:59:56] Helping Someone With Addiction

**Andrew Huberman:** If we know somebody who is clearly suffering from addiction and they're not yet in a medical crisis — what can and should we do?

**Ryan Soave:** One of the biggest things we can do is talk to them, but without shaming them. If you don't know how to talk to them, reach out for some help yourself — there are family support meetings like Al-Anon. Have an open conversation without shaming them, approaching it with the idea that this is a disease, that we're talking to a sick friend, not a bad person.

**Andrew Huberman:** If a 12-step meeting is an open meeting, you could offer to go with them. You don't have to say anything — you just say "pass." You could take them to a meeting or even go and just observe. Some non-alcoholics have come to open meetings to learn more about it and then be able to help somebody they know.

**Ryan Soave:** Reach out and find a therapist that can do an assessment. In our mission to create access, we've been doing innovative stuff around virtual care and in-home care. When people are resistant and maybe need to go to a residential treatment center, we can offer different services at much lower cost covered by insurance that can build a relationship with them, help them witness their failures, and get them to the correct level of care.
