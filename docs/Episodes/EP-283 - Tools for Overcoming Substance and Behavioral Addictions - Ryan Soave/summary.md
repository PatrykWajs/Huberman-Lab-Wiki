# Episode 283: Tools for Overcoming Substance & Behavioral Addictions - Ryan Soave

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Ryan Soave, Chief Clinical Officer at Guardian Recovery, breaks down the full recovery process from detox through building a life post-addiction, sharing concrete zero-cost protocols applicable to every type of substance and behavioral addiction. The conversation covers the trauma-stress-addiction cycle — how a wound (trauma) creates ongoing stress, which drives the search for relief through substances or behavior, which then generates new trauma in a self-perpetuating loop. Soave and Huberman discuss specific addiction types including alcohol, cocaine and stimulants, gambling, video games, and pornography, as well as emerging treatments such as ibogaine and psilocybin. A suite of practical daily tools is presented — the Emotional Weather Forecast/Map, Yoga Nidra/NSDR, deliberate cold exposure, seven-breath breathwork, and 12-step community engagement — all aimed at building distress tolerance: the core skill that makes lasting recovery possible. The episode closes with guidance on how to help someone who is suffering from addiction and resources for both addicts and their families.

## Key Takeaways

- **Addiction is the solution, not the problem** — it is an attempt to find relief from underlying stress or trauma; the substance or behavior becomes problematic secondarily. [00:01:32](transcript.md#000132-addiction-addictive-behaviors-relief)

- **Physical stabilization comes first** — before addressing trauma or psychology, detox and biological safety must be established; abrupt alcohol cessation can cause seizures and death — this is medically distinct from other substances. - **We confuse discomfort with threat** — the autonomic nervous system treats perceived social threat as physical danger; the 20-second adrenaline window shuts down the forebrain; building the gap between stimulus and response is the central recovery skill. ## Tools & Protocols

### Emotional Weather Forecast / Map
- **What:** A structured daily morning practice to anticipate the emotional landscape of the day and orient toward intentional behavior — a road map for navigating the experience of being human.
- **How:** Write five components each morning: (1) 3–5 gratitudes, including at least one challenge — "meeting what is as it is"; (2) high-level plan for the day; (3) current emotional state — fears, resentments, anger, guilt, shame; (4) character liabilities or defects likely to arise given today's plans and emotional state; (5) strivings — ways of being, not outcomes (e.g., patient, tolerant, kind). Share the list with at least one trusted person daily to externalize and anchor intentions. Optionally review at night as a daily inventory.
- **When:** Every morning. The brain at 4 PM is not the brain at 8 AM; a reminder of your aspirational self is essential because spontaneous self-regulation fails without it.
- **Ref:** [Tool: Emotional Weather Forecast, Gratitude, Plan, Strivings](transcript.md#005530-tool-emotional-weather-forecast-gratitude-plan-strivings)

### 30-Day Abstinence Test
- **What:** A self-diagnostic tool to determine whether a behavior or substance "has you" or whether you have it.
- **How:** Commit to 30 complete days without the substance or behavior. Monitor: whether you think about it constantly when abstaining, whether you feel trapped or obsessive, and what areas of life it is impacting. Discuss honestly with a therapist or trusted person — "if I'm sitting in a business meeting completely dissociated because I'm focused on not gambling, that's a pretty good indication it has me."
- **When:** Anytime someone suspects a behavior or substance may be addictive; especially useful before seeking formal treatment to clarify severity.
- **Ref:** [Tool: 30 Days Abstinence & Addiction; Impact](transcript.md#004313-tool-30-days-abstinence--addiction-impact)

### Yoga Nidra / Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR) for Recovery
- **What:** A guided lying-down meditation practice that induces sleep-like brain states while maintaining wakefulness; the first daily activity at Guardian Recovery for addiction and trauma recovery. Huberman learned this practice directly from Soave.
- **How:** Lie on your back in a comfortable position. Follow a guided audio track (20–35 minutes). Practice includes: relaxing body movements (tensing/releasing muscles, gentle face massage), long-exhale breathing to activate the parasympathetic nervous system, body scanning (e.g., the 61-point technique moving awareness from forehead to throat to fingers and toes), and optionally setting an intention (e.g., "I am at peace with myself as I am and the world as it is"). Mind stays active; body becomes still. Falling asleep is acceptable but not the goal.
- **When:** Ideally morning and late afternoon; second best in the evening; third best anytime possible. Schedule proactively regardless of whether you feel you need it — the best time to build capacity is when not in crisis.
- **Ref:** [Yoga Nidra, Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR)](transcript.md#013056-yoga-nidra-non-sleep-deep-rest-ndsr) | [Yoga Nidra Timing, Regulation of Autonomic Nervous System; Breathwork](transcript.md#014545-yoga-nidra-timing-regulation-of-autonomic-nervous-system-breathwork)

### Cold Plunge for Distress Tolerance Training
- **What:** Deliberate cold water immersion as a proactive training tool for building distress tolerance; teaches control of thinking under uncomfortable physiological conditions that mirror real-life stress triggers.
- **How:** Enter cold water (cold plunge, cold bath, or cold shower). Stay in for the discomfort rather than immediately escaping. Practice controlling thoughts during the 20-second forebrain-offline window that follows the adrenaline response. Cold water is a highly reliable way to elicit an adrenaline response — the training transfers to real-life triggers.
- **When:** Scheduled proactively, not only in crisis. The adrenaline state from cold water mirrors the adrenaline state from stressful triggers, building transferable skill for real-life distress.
- **Ref:** [Building Distress Tolerance, Proactive Behaviors, Cold Plunge](transcript.md#011603-building-distress-tolerance-proactive-behaviors-cold-plunge)

### Seven-Breath Parasympathetic Reset
- **What:** A rapid, in-the-moment intervention to activate the parasympathetic nervous system during a stress response; the fastest available tool for shifting out of sympathetic dominance.
- **How:** Take 7 intentional breaths with long exhales — the long exhale directly activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Can be deployed before getting out of the car, before lunch, after a stressful meeting. First ask: "Am I or is anyone around me in immediate physical danger?" — if you can ask, the answer is almost certainly no. Scheduled anchor points throughout the day compound over time.
- **When:** In any triggering moment, and proactively as scheduled anchor points throughout the day.
- **Ref:** [Yoga Nidra Timing, Regulation of Autonomic Nervous System; Breathwork](transcript.md#014545-yoga-nidra-timing-regulation-of-autonomic-nervous-system-breathwork) | [Stilling Leads to Seeing, Tool: Recognizing Stress Response; Relationships](transcript.md#012355-stilling-leads-to-seeing-tool-recognizing-stress-response-relationships)

### HALT Relapse Prevention Check
- **What:** A simple daily self-assessment acronym for monitoring the four physiological/emotional states that most reliably increase relapse risk.
- **How:** Ask: Am I Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired? Address whichever state is present before entering high-risk situations or making important decisions. Blood sugar stability (avoiding hunger) may be especially impactful — anecdotally, diabetics who maintained blood sugar had lower relapse rates; GLP-1 research is exploring this mechanism.
- **When:** Before high-risk situations, daily as a check-in, and whenever a craving arises.
- **Ref:** [GLP-1 Agonists & Addiction Treatment?, HALT; Addicted to Stress?](transcript.md#024905-glp-1-agonists--addiction-treatment-halt-addicted-to-stress)

### 12-Step Community Engagement
- **What:** Peer-based recovery community using a structured 12-step program as a "design for living"; available free, worldwide, for virtually every type of addiction. Alcohol is mentioned only in step one — everything else is about how to live.
- **How:** Search online for your local group (AA, NA, CA, GA, SA, OA, etc.) or go to aa.org, na.org, etc. Try at least six different meetings before deciding — every group has a different culture. Open meetings welcome non-addicts and family members. Online meetings are available 24/7 globally. For family members: Al-Anon, Codependents Anonymous, Families Anonymous, Adult Children of Alcoholics. Alternatives for those who prefer secular or non-spiritual approaches: SMART Recovery, Refuge Recovery.
- **When:** As soon as someone recognizes they may have a problem; does not require hitting bottom or formal diagnosis. Can begin before or alongside formal treatment.
- **Ref:** [Addiction Treatment, Detox, Rehab Centers, 12-Step Meetings, AA](transcript.md#023609-addiction-treatment-detox-rehab-centers-12-step-meetings-aa)

## Resources Mentioned

- **Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)** — [aa.org](https://www.aa.org)
- **Narcotics Anonymous (NA)** — [na.org](https://www.na.org)
- **SMART Recovery** — secular, science-based alternative to 12-step — [smartrecovery.org](https://www.smartrecovery.org)
- **Refuge Recovery** — Buddhist-inspired addiction recovery — [refugerecovery.org](https://refugerecovery.org)
- **Guardian Recovery** — Ryan Soave's organization; multi-state addiction treatment centers — [guardianrecovery.com](https://guardianrecovery.com)
- **Al-Anon** — support for families of alcoholics and addicts — [al-anon.org](https://al-anon.org)
- **The Jellinek Curve** — visual model of alcoholism progression and recovery; from E.M. Jellinek's *The Disease Concept of Alcoholism* (1960); Jellinek was a biophysicist at Stanford
- **Dopamine Nation** (book) — Anna Lembke; explains dopamine dynamics, the pleasure-pain balance, and addiction science
- **Man's Search for Meaning** (book) — Viktor Frankl; referenced for the concept of the gap between stimulus and response
- **EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)** — therapy modality discovered by Francine Shapiro while going for a walk; bilateral eye movement during forward movement through space processes distress
- **Ibogaine for addiction** — research by Nolan Williams at Stanford; 40–80% remission rates from alcohol use disorder; treatment conducted in Mexico with pre/post brain scans at Stanford
- **Psilocybin for addiction** — studies ongoing in the US
- **GLP-1 Agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy)** — early research suggests potential utility for addiction cravings; currently off-label use only
- **Waking Up App** — meditation app with guided yoga nidra and NSDR sessions — [wakingup.com](https://www.wakingup.com)
