# Episode 40: Using Temperature for Performance, Brain & Body Health | Dr. Craig Heller

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Craig Heller, Professor of Biology and Neurosciences at Stanford, joins Andrew Huberman to explain the science of thermoregulation and how controlling body temperature is one of the most powerful levers for optimizing physical and cognitive performance. The central discovery from Heller's lab: cooling "glabrous" skin surfaces — the palms, foot pads, and upper face — via arteriovenous anastomoses (AVAs) can triple anaerobic work volume, eliminate delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), and dramatically extend endurance, while most popular cooling methods (ice packs to the neck, cold towels on muscles) are counterproductive because they trigger vasoconstriction and impair the body's natural heat-loss portals. The episode covers the pyruvate kinase mechanism of muscular fatigue, the CoolMitt technology and low-cost alternatives, and how hyperthermia tricks the brain into feeling cool while core temperature climbs toward dangerous levels. Additional topics include brown fat activation by mild cold, the adenosine-caffeine interaction during exercise, non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT), brain freeze physiology, hibernation biology in bears and ground squirrels, the thermal basis of sleep hygiene, and the correct re-warming strategy for hypothermia.

## Guest: Dr. Craig Heller

**Dr. H. Craig Heller** is a Professor of Biology and Neurosciences at Stanford University, former Chair of the Biology Department and Director of the Human Biology Program. His laboratory studies thermoregulation, circadian rhythms, sleep, and Down syndrome. He is the scientist behind the palmar cooling technology commercialized as **CoolMitt**, and has collaborated with NFL teams (San Francisco 49ers), the US Navy SEALs, Olympic athletes, and the Department of Defense. He also led long-term field research implanting EEG/EKG/temperature sensors in hibernating black bears in Alaska.

- Stanford profile: [https://profiles.stanford.edu/h-craig-heller](https://profiles.stanford.edu/h-craig-heller)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Heat — not glycogen depletion — is the primary and fastest cause of muscular failure.** Pyruvate kinase, the enzyme gating fuel entry into the mitochondria, shuts off when local muscle temperature exceeds ~39–39.5 °C. Glycogen is still present; that is why three minutes of rest restores the ability to do another set. [00:16:45](transcript.md#001645-temperature-gates-our-energy-use)

2. **Core temperature rise impairs cognition as well as muscle.** At ~39 °C core temperature, simple arithmetic becomes impossible. Post-exercise brain fog may be a direct consequence of residual brain heat. The phrase "cool, calm and collected" describes a literal physiological state. [00:19:00](transcript.md#001900-local-versus-systemic-fatigue-heat-is-why-we-fail)

3. **Most popular cooling methods are counterproductive.** Ice packs on the neck or quads, cold towels on the torso, and cold sponges to the head cool the hypothalamic thermostat — making the athlete feel better while core temperature continues to rise. This mismatch is why heat stroke deaths occur during practice, not competition. [00:22:10](transcript.md#002210-cooling-off-most-methods-are-counterproductive)

4. **Glabrous skin surfaces are the body's radiators.** The hairless palms, foot pads, and upper face are underlain by AVAs — direct artery-to-vein shunts that bypass high-resistance capillaries and allow massive, variable blood flow for heat exchange. Squeezing these surfaces (gripping handlebars, a barbell, or a phone) collapses the AVAs and defeats the radiator. [00:31:50](transcript.md#003150-best-body-sites-for-cooling-palms-foot-pads-upper-face)

5. **Pouring cool water on the upper face actively cools the brain.** Emissary veins in the skull normally drain brain blood outward; when the body is overheated, flow reverses — cool venous blood from facial glabrous skin flows back into the cranial circulation, acting as a selective brain cooler. [00:38:00](transcript.md#003800-cooling-your-brain-via-the-upper-face-concussion)

6. **Palmar cooling between sets can triple anaerobic work volume.** 49ers tight end Greg Clark went from ~180 dips across five sets to 300+ dips over four weeks of twice-weekly training with inter-set palmar cooling. Female Stanford students reached over 800 total pushups per session using the same protocol. [00:41:25](transcript.md#004125-extraordinary-tripling-performance-by-cooling-the-palms)

7. **Intra-workout palmar cooling eliminates DOMS.** Athletes consistently expect soreness after cooling-enhanced sessions and never experience it. A controlled study confirmed no significant delayed onset muscle soreness in the cooled group despite far greater total work volume. [00:45:35](transcript.md#004535-enhancing-recovery-eliminating-soreness-wintra-workout-cooling)

8. **Ice-cold is too cold; ~16–20 °C is the optimal palmar cooling range.** Temperatures below ~10 °C cause reflex vasoconstriction of AVAs, sealing heat inside. Suitable low-cost implements: a slightly cool (not frozen) water bottle or loosely held partial-thaw frozen peas, held for ~3 minutes. Verify effectiveness by checking that the palm feels warm (not cold) to a third party after contact. [00:53:00](transcript.md#005300-cool-mitt-ice-cold-is-too-cold-3-minutes-cooling)

9. **Cooling gains are permanent.** Increased work volume produces real structural adaptations — more sarcomeres, capillaries, and mitochondria. Athletes retain elevated performance baselines even after stopping palmar cooling. [01:17:35](transcript.md#011735-cooling-enhanced-performance-is-permanent)

10. **Palmar cooling outperforms anabolic steroids in work-volume gains.** A literature review of rigorous steroid studies showed ~1% per week improvement in bench press. Heller's cooling protocol produced ~300% improvement over one month. The effect is equivalent in female athletes, with no androgenic side effects or legal issues. [01:19:55](transcript.md#011955-anabolic-steroids-versus-palmer-cooling)

11. **Multiple sclerosis patients are exquisitely heat-sensitive.** Even modest temperature rises impair neural conduction in demyelinated axons. CoolMitt has allowed MS patients who could no longer tolerate summer heat to return to outdoor activities such as golf. [00:50:00](transcript.md#005000-multiple-sclerosis-heat-sensitivity--amelioration-with-cooling)

12. **Glabrous skin cooling is twice as fast as standard hyperthermia first aid.** Heller's lab compared cold packs on axilla/groin/neck (current field protocol) versus cold packs on palms/soles/face: cooling rate doubled on the glabrous sites. The same reversal applies to hypothermia re-warming: target glabrous surfaces and major arterial sites, not the torso or limbs. [01:04:40](transcript.md#010440-hypothermia-story-ideal-re-heating-strategy)

13. **For sleep, core body temperature must fall.** A warm bath 1–2 hours before bed vasodilates AVAs, dumps heat rapidly, and produces a post-bath temperature drop that signals the hypothalamic thermostat to lower its set point and initiate sleep. Sleeping in a cool room allows glabrous surfaces (extended hand, face) to passively fine-tune heat loss throughout the night. [01:43:42](transcript.md#014342-sleep-cold-warm-baths-screens--socks)

14. **Brown fat in humans is distributed, not discrete.** Unlike rodents with discrete interscapular brown fat pads, human brown fat is scattered. Mild cold that lowers core temperature triggers sympathetic activation of brown fat thermogenesis; shivering is a coarser backup mechanism that can only raise metabolism ~3–4× versus ~10× for exercise. [01:26:55](transcript.md#012655-studies-of-bears--hibernation-brown-fat), [01:31:10](transcript.md#013110-brown-fat-distribution--activation-in-humans)

15. **Caffeine may impair muscular performance via adenosine blockade.** Hypoxic working muscle releases adenosine, which dilates local blood vessels to increase oxygen delivery. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, potentially reducing blood flow and oxygen supply to working muscles — the opposite of what most pre-workout consumers intend. [01:39:44](transcript.md#013944-how-pre-workout-drinks--caffeine-may-inhibit-performance)

16. **NEAT (non-exercise activity thermogenesis) can account for large caloric differences between individuals.** Any muscle activity — leg bouncing, fidgeting — produces heat at roughly 5× its mechanical work output (80% of energy becomes heat). Hyperthyroid individuals are a clinical extreme of elevated NEAT; hypothyroid is the opposite. [01:37:50](transcript.md#013750-fidgeters-non-exercise-induced-thermogenesis)

## Tools & Protocols

### Pre-Workout Cold Shower / Bath (Aerobic Performance)

- **What:** Take a cool/cold shower or brief cold immersion before an aerobic workout to pre-load the body's heat-absorption capacity.
- **How:** A few minutes of cool water is sufficient. Full immersion beats showers because showers create an insulating boundary layer of warmed water; moving around during immersion breaks the boundary layer and increases heat exchange.
- **When:** Immediately before aerobic exercise (running, cycling, rowing, etc.).
- **Ref:** [00:11:55](transcript.md#001155-cooling-before-aerobic-activity-to-enhance-performance)

---

### Palmar Cooling Between Sets (Anaerobic Performance)

- **What:** Cool the palms between resistance-training sets to remove accumulated local muscle heat, restore pyruvate kinase function, and enable more total work volume with no DOMS.
- **How:** Hold a cool (not ice cold, ~16–20 °C / 60–68 °F) object loosely in both hands for ~3 minutes between sets. Suitable objects: a slightly cool water bottle, loosely held partially-thawed frozen peas, or a cool (not frozen) juice can alternated between hands. Do not squeeze tightly — compression collapses AVAs. Verify: palms should feel warm (not cold) to a third party after contact; cold palms indicate vasoconstriction. Optimal device: CoolMitt (coolmitt.com), which maintains the correct temperature range with a mild vacuum.
- **When:** During every inter-set rest period; 3 minutes captures the steepest part of the exponential heat-removal curve.
- **Ref:** [00:53:00](transcript.md#005300-cool-mitt-ice-cold-is-too-cold-3-minutes-cooling), [00:58:20](transcript.md#005820-how-you-can-use-palmer-cooling-to-enhance-performance)

---

### Avoid Ice Packs / Cold Towels to Neck, Quads, or Torso During Exercise

- **What:** Do NOT apply ice packs or cold towels to the neck, head, quads, or torso to cool down during exercise.
- **How:** Omit these common sideline methods. They trigger defensive vasoconstriction, shut down AVA heat-loss portals, and cool the hypothalamic thermostat — making athletes feel cooler while core temperature continues rising.
- **When:** Any time tempted to use these methods during or between exercise bouts.
- **Ref:** [00:22:10](transcript.md#002210-cooling-off-most-methods-are-counterproductive)

---

### Loose Grip / Open Hands During Aerobic Exercise

- **What:** Keep hands relaxed and open during running, cycling, or other aerobic work to maximize continuous passive palmar heat loss through AVAs.
- **How:** Run with loose, open fingers rather than a clenched fist or phone grip. On a bicycle, periodically release grip pressure. Avoid gloves where safe to do so.
- **When:** Throughout any sustained aerobic activity.
- **Ref:** [00:31:50](transcript.md#003150-best-body-sites-for-cooling-palms-foot-pads-upper-face)

---

### Paw-Pad Cooling for Overheating Dogs

- **What:** Cool an overheating dog via its paw pads — the canine equivalent of human glabrous skin.
- **How:** Apply cool (not ice cold) water to the dog's paw pads. Especially important for brachycephalic breeds (bulldogs, pugs) with impaired thermoregulation.
- **When:** Whenever a dog shows signs of overheating; proactively during hot-weather exercise.
- **Ref:** [01:11:40](transcript.md#011140-paw-lmer-cooling-for-dog-health--performance)

---

### Warm Bath Before Bed (Sleep Onset)

- **What:** Take a warm (not hot) bath 1–2 hours before sleep to accelerate sleep onset and improve sleep quality.
- **How:** A warm soak vasodilates AVAs in hands and feet, causing rapid heat dumping. After exiting, core temperature drops faster than it would naturally, triggering the hypothalamic set-point descent that initiates sleep.
- **When:** 60–90 minutes before intended sleep time.
- **Ref:** [01:43:42](transcript.md#014342-sleep-cold-warm-baths-screens--socks)

---

### Sleep in a Cool Room with Glabrous Surfaces Exposed

- **What:** Keep the bedroom cool and sleep with at least one hand or leg outside the covers to passively regulate nighttime temperature via AVA heat loss.
- **How:** Set room temperature to the cool end of comfort. Extend a hand, arm, or leg outside the covers as needed — these are the body's passive radiators. Avoid thick gloves or tight socks unless you run cold; feet and hands are the primary nighttime heat-loss surfaces.
- **When:** Every night as a sleep-hygiene baseline.
- **Ref:** [01:43:42](transcript.md#014342-sleep-cold-warm-baths-screens--socks)

---

### Mild Cold Exposure for Brown Fat Activation

- **What:** Regular mild cold exposure to activate brown adipose tissue thermogenesis and support baseline metabolic rate.
- **How:** Cool showers, cool ambient environments, or brief cold exposure — stopping short of intense shivering. Brown fat thermogenesis is a sympathetic nervous system response to core temperature dropping; moderate cold is the trigger. Intense cold primarily triggers shivering (muscle-based).
- **When:** Regular mild cold exposure as a lifestyle habit.
- **Ref:** [01:26:55](transcript.md#012655-studies-of-bears--hibernation-brown-fat), [01:31:10](transcript.md#013110-brown-fat-distribution--activation-in-humans)

---

### Hypothermia Re-Warming: Target Glabrous Surfaces and Major Arteries

- **What:** When re-warming a hypothermic person, apply heat to glabrous skin surfaces (palms, soles) and major arterial sites (axilla, groin, neck) — not the torso or limbs.
- **How:** Use warm packs or circulating-water pads at these sites. Avoid rubbing limbs — this drives cold peripheral blood back to the core. In hospital settings, warm circulating pads applied to the feet accelerate post-surgical hypothermia recovery dramatically.
- **When:** Emergency re-warming of a hypothermic person; post-surgical recovery room.
- **Ref:** [01:04:40](transcript.md#010440-hypothermia-story-ideal-re-heating-strategy)

---

### Consistent Sleep Schedule (Sleep Hygiene Foundation)

- **What:** Maintain consistent bed and wake times as the foundational sleep intervention, prioritized above pharmacology.
- **How:** Set a fixed wake time and bedtime. Avoid bright/blue-spectrum screen light within 1–2 hours of sleep (pushes circadian phase). Keep the bedroom cool and dark. Avoid caffeine and thermogenic stimulants in hours before sleep.
- **When:** Every night, as an ongoing lifestyle practice.
- **Ref:** [01:43:42](transcript.md#014342-sleep-cold-warm-baths-screens--socks)
