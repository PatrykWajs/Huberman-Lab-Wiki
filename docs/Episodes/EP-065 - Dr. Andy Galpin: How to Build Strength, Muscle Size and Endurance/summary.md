# Episode 65: Dr. Andy Galpin: How to Build Strength, Muscle Size & Endurance

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Andy Galpin, tenured professor of kinesiology at Cal State Fullerton and world expert in exercise science, delivers a comprehensive masterclass covering nine distinct exercise adaptations, the modifiable variables governing strength, hypertrophy, and all categories of endurance, and the full suite of practical protocols — including the 3-by-5 concept, mind-muscle connection, breathing tools, muscular endurance, hydration equations, cold/heat strategies, and supplementation — applicable from beginner to elite athlete. The episode spans approximately 211 minutes and covers every major lever for improving physical performance and body composition. Galpin also previews his cutting-edge "Absolute Rest" project, a diagnostic sleep and recovery platform developed with NASA-affiliated researchers.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Nine exercise adaptations, not one.** Galpin defines nine distinct outcomes from training: skill/technique, speed, power, strength, hypertrophy, muscular endurance, anaerobic capacity, VO2 max/anaerobic threshold, and long-duration endurance. Fat loss is a byproduct, not an adaptation. Training for the wrong adaptation with the wrong variables is why most people stagnate. [00:08:20](transcript.md#000820-adaptations-of-exercise-progressive-overload)

2. **Progressive overload has many levers beyond adding weight.** Modifiable variables include exercise choice, exercise order, volume (sets × reps), intensity (% of 1RM), tempo, rest periods, and frequency. Changing any one of these constitutes progressive overload. The most common mistake is varying the wrong variable — or changing all simultaneously. [00:14:40](transcript.md#001440-modifiable-variables-one-rep-max-muscle-soreness)

3. **The 3-by-5 concept for strength and power.** Use 3–5 exercises, 3–5 sets, 3–5 reps, 3–5 minutes rest, 3–5 days per week. For strength, use 85%+ of 1RM. For power, lower intensity to 40–70% of 1RM to prioritize bar velocity. This framework is scalable, forgiving, and hard to do wrong. [01:08:50](transcript.md#010850-tools-protocols-for-strength-training-the-3-by-5-concept)

4. **Hypertrophy works across a very wide rep range.** Five to 30 reps per set produces roughly equivalent hypertrophy. The real driver is proximity to muscular failure and total weekly volume — minimum 10 working sets per muscle group per week, with 15–20 optimal and 20–25 for advanced trainees. Blood flow restriction (BFR) at 20–40% 1RM also produces meaningful hypertrophy for injured or deconditioned individuals. [00:58:45](transcript.md#005845-hypertrophy-training-repetition-ranges-blood-flow-restriction)

5. **Mind-muscle connection is mechanistically real and goal-dependent.** For strength, focus on moving the weight with intent. For hypertrophy, focus on contracting the target muscle. This mental shift changes motor unit recruitment patterns, confirmed by EMG research on internal vs. external attentional focus. Flexing the muscle between sets (as bodybuilders do) may also enhance hypertrophic response. [01:10:48](transcript.md#011048-mind-muscle-connection)

6. **Training frequency differs between strength and hypertrophy.** Strength can be trained daily on the same muscle — the primary driver is neural, not damaging. For hypertrophy, twice per week per muscle group is the minimum effective dose; every 48–72 hours allows protein synthesis to complete before the next stimulus. Missing the window means lost opportunity, not lost gains. [00:43:50](transcript.md#004350-how-to-select-training-frequency-strength-vs-hypertrophy)

7. **Muscular endurance is essential for joint health and injury prevention.** Slow-twitch postural muscles (spinal erectors, soleus, rotator cuff stabilizers) must maintain endurance so fast-twitch muscles can express force safely. Loss of muscular endurance in stabilizers is a leading cause of chronic joint pain and acute injuries during heavy or explosive movements — and can often be resolved by training just below the pain threshold rather than resting completely. [02:08:15](transcript.md#020815-muscular-endurance-fast-vs-slow-twitch-muscle)

8. **Combining endurance and strength requires smart sequencing and modality selection.** The interference effect is real but greatly overblown. Zone 2 cardio has almost no ability to block hypertrophy. High-eccentric modalities (running) create more interference than low-eccentric ones (cycling, rowing, swimming). Adding strength work to an endurance program almost always benefits the endurance athlete with very little detriment. [01:37:25](transcript.md#013725-endurance-training-combining-with-strength)

9. **The Galpin Equation for hydration.** Daily baseline: bodyweight in pounds ÷ 2 = ounces of fluid per day. During exercise: bodyweight in pounds ÷ 30 = ounces every 15–20 minutes. Add 500 mg sodium before and after training. If weight loss during a session exceeds 1% of bodyweight, a specific strategy is required. The daily baseline must be met first — you cannot catch up in-session if you start dehydrated. [02:16:35](transcript.md#021635-hydration-the-galpin-equation-sodium-fasting)

10. **Cold exposure timing matters: detrimental for hypertrophy, neutral to beneficial for endurance.** Ice bath immediately after strength/hypertrophy disrupts the inflammatory signaling needed for muscle growth — Galpin estimates roughly 10% attenuation. Wait at least 4 hours post-lifting, or do cold on off days or before training. After endurance training, cold is neutral to potentially beneficial (may enhance mitochondrial biogenesis). Morning cold is Galpin's preferred default. [02:35:57](transcript.md#023557-cold-exposure-training)

11. **Sodium bicarbonate is one of the best-evidenced performance supplements.** By buffering intramuscular pH, baking soda delays acid-mediated fatigue. Dose: start with ½ teaspoon (approx. 0.2–0.3 g/kg bodyweight), taken 45–90 minutes before training with food to reduce GI distress. Most effective for high-intensity efforts lasting 1–8 minutes. Lactate is not the cause of fatigue — it is actually a buffer that prevents it; the real culprit is hydrogen ion (acid) accumulation. [03:04:02](transcript.md#030402-tool-sodium-bicarbonate)

12. **Creatine monohydrate is Galpin's "Michael Jordan" of supplements.** The most studied, safest, most cost-effective supplement with effects spanning muscular power, strength, and recovery, as well as emerging neurological, mood, and metabolic health data. Standard dose: 5 g/day. No loading required. The majority of current creatine research is in clinical populations studying the brain and mood — not sport performance. [03:17:26](transcript.md#031726-tool-creatine-monohydrate)

## Tools & Protocols

### 3-by-5 Strength/Power Protocol
- **What:** A simple, scalable framework for strength and power training.
- **How:** Choose 3–5 exercises covering push/pull and upper/lower patterns. Do 3–5 sets of 3–5 reps. Rest 3–5 minutes between sets. Train 3–5 days per week. For **strength**: 85%+ of 1RM. For **power**: 40–70% of 1RM with maximal bar velocity intent.
- **When:** Any strength or power training phase; applicable beginner to advanced.
- **Ref:** [01:08:50](transcript.md#010850-tools-protocols-for-strength-training-the-3-by-5-concept)

### Progressive Overload Variable Management
- **What:** Systematically manipulating training inputs to drive continued adaptation.
- **How:** Modifiable variables: exercise choice, exercise order, volume (sets × reps), intensity (% 1RM), tempo, rest interval, frequency. Change one or two variables per block. Do not vary all simultaneously. Progression can also mean moving from a single-joint to a multi-joint exercise.
- **When:** Every training block transition; primary tool against plateau.
- **Ref:** [00:14:40](transcript.md#001440-modifiable-variables-one-rep-max-muscle-soreness)

### 1RM Estimation Without Maximum Testing
- **What:** Safe method to estimate true 1RM without injury risk of a maximal lift.
- **How:** Select a weight you can lift for a known rep count at near-max effort. Use published conversion charts (e.g., 10RM ≈ 75% of 1RM, 5RM ≈ 87% of 1RM) to back-calculate. Alternatively, use feel: if you could have done 1–2 more reps, that load approximates your rep-max. Reassess every 4–6 weeks.
- **When:** Before setting intensity percentages; ideal for beginners and those without coaching.
- **Ref:** [00:14:40](transcript.md#001440-modifiable-variables-one-rep-max-muscle-soreness)

### Hypertrophy Training: The 3-Signal Check
- **What:** A practical framework (from Mike Israetel) to verify a muscle is being effectively stimulated for growth.
- **How:** After a set, check three signals: (1) Did you feel the target muscle contracting during the set? (2) Did you get a pump in that muscle during or after? (3) Is there mild soreness there the next day? Aim for a 3-out-of-10 or higher on at least one or two signals. Absence of all three strongly predicts no growth.
- **When:** Every hypertrophy session, especially for lagging muscle groups.
- **Ref:** [00:58:45](transcript.md#005845-hypertrophy-training-repetition-ranges-blood-flow-restriction)

### Mind-Muscle Connection for Hypertrophy
- **What:** Internal attentional focus on the target muscle during each rep.
- **How:** During hypertrophy sets, focus on squeezing and contracting the target muscle rather than on moving the weight. Between sets, actively flex the target muscle. For strength sets, shift focus to moving the weight with maximal intent and speed. For hard-to-activate muscles, use tactile cues (someone touching the muscle) or visualization.
- **When:** Hypertrophy training sessions; any time a muscle under-responds despite volume.
- **Ref:** [01:10:48](transcript.md#011048-mind-muscle-connection)

### Eccentric-Only Training for Muscle Activation
- **What:** Using the lowering (eccentric) phase only to recruit difficult-to-activate muscle groups.
- **How:** Start from the top position of a movement (e.g., top of a pull-up on a box) and lower under control, focusing entirely on the target muscle. Eliminates the need to generate concentric force. Progress over 6–12 weeks: eccentric → isometric → full concentric-eccentric.
- **When:** When a muscle group fails to respond to standard training; rehabilitation; learning motor patterns for compound movements.
- **Ref:** [01:16:16](transcript.md#011616-mental-awareness)

### Supersets for Time-Efficient Training
- **What:** Pairing non-competing exercises during rest periods to increase training density.
- **How:** Pair push/pull or unrelated muscle groups. Perform the second exercise during the rest period of the first. Preserves adequate rest quality for each movement while compressing session length. Supersets cause a small measurable reduction in maximum strength gains — acceptable for most people; avoid for elite strength athletes.
- **When:** Hypertrophy or general fitness blocks when time is limited. Avoid during max-strength work requiring full CNS recovery.
- **Ref:** [00:27:30](transcript.md#002730-modifiable-variables-of-strength-training-supersets)

### Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Training
- **What:** Low-load training with venous occlusion that drives hypertrophy via metabolic stress.
- **How:** Apply a proper BFR cuff (not a tourniquet — risk of blood clot) to the proximal limb. Use 20–40% of 1RM. Standard protocol: 4 sets (30-15-15-15 reps) with 30–45 seconds rest. The occlusion creates metabolic stress and recruits high-threshold fibers despite low loads.
- **When:** Injury rehabilitation, deload phases, travel, or when heavy loads are unavailable.
- **Ref:** [00:58:45](transcript.md#005845-hypertrophy-training-repetition-ranges-blood-flow-restriction)

### Breathing During Resistance Training
- **What:** Strategy for managing intra-abdominal pressure and breathing during sets.
- **How:** Brace the core cylindrically (front, sides, and back) at ~20% activation. For low-rep strength work (1–3 reps), omit breathing entirely during the rep. For higher-rep sets (3–8+), hold breath and brace during the eccentric/most dangerous phase, then exhale during the concentric. For long sets (10+ reps), exhale on every third rep or as needed. Separate breath from brace — you should be able to talk while braced at 20%.
- **When:** Every resistance training session; especially important on heavy compound movements.
- **Ref:** [01:17:57](transcript.md#011757-breathing-tools-for-resistance-training-post-training)

### Post-Training Downregulation Protocol
- **What:** A deliberate breathing and environment protocol to shift the nervous system from sympathetic to parasympathetic after training.
- **How:** Immediately after training, sit or lie down. Breathe with extended exhale: 4-second inhale, 8-second exhale (2:1 ratio), or box breathing (equal inhale, hold, exhale, hold). Cover eyes or control light. Minimum 3 minutes; 5 minutes is optimal. Can be done in a shower if necessary. Prevents the adrenaline crash that occurs 3–4 hours post-training.
- **When:** After every training session. Prevents energy crashes and accelerates workout-to-workout recovery.
- **Ref:** [01:27:57](transcript.md#012757-breathing-tools-for-resistance-training-post-training)

### Zone 2 Cardio (Long-Duration Endurance Base)
- **What:** Sustained aerobic exercise at conversational pace for cardiovascular and metabolic health.
- **How:** 150–180 minutes per week of low-intensity steady-state cardio (cycling, walking, rowing, swimming). Should allow full conversation throughout. Choose low-eccentric modalities first if new to endurance. Practical tip: take phone calls while walking to accumulate this without extra time blocks. Does not meaningfully interfere with hypertrophy at true zone 2 intensity.
- **When:** Non-negotiable foundation for all endurance and health goals; recommended regardless of other training modalities.
- **Ref:** [01:51:20](transcript.md#015120-tools-protocols-for-endurance-training)

### VO2 Max / Anaerobic Capacity Intervals
- **What:** Short bursts near maximum heart rate to build VO2 max and all cardiovascular adaptations.
- **How:** Once per week (minimum every other week), reach near-maximum heart rate. Ideal: 4–8 bouts of 30 seconds to 6 minutes at maximal non-sustainable effort, with equal rest (work:rest 1:1). Maximum heart rate rough estimate: 220 minus age (highly individual — measure directly). Use low-eccentric modalities (bike, sled, uphill running) when combining with strength training.
- **When:** After establishing a zone 2 base; once per week or at minimum twice per month. Must actually approach max heart rate — 70–80% is insufficient.
- **Ref:** [01:51:20](transcript.md#015120-tools-protocols-for-endurance-training)

### Sustained High-Output Intervals (4–12 Minutes)
- **What:** Middle-intensity sustained work bouts for maximum cardiovascular adaptation.
- **How:** 2–6 minute hard efforts at ~80% max heart rate, with equal rest (e.g., mile repeats at sustainable-but-hard pace with equal rest). Repeat 1–6+ times depending on capacity. Use sled pushes, kettlebell circuits, rowing, or running. Nasal breathing preferred where possible; mouth acceptable when intensity demands it.
- **When:** Once per week as a separate session from zone 2 and max-effort intervals; arguably the highest cardiovascular benefit per unit of time.
- **Ref:** [01:51:20](transcript.md#015120-tools-protocols-for-endurance-training)

### Muscular Endurance Training
- **What:** Training the slow-twitch postural muscles to maintain joint stability under sustained load.
- **How:** Wall sits, planks, farmer's carries, isometric holds, and similar exercises. Train just below the threshold of pain or excessive discomfort — this desensitizes pain signals and builds tissue tolerance. Do not simply rest an injured area; train it at sub-threshold intensity. Progress volume gradually.
- **When:** In every training program as a foundation for joint health; especially important for chronic low back, shoulder, or knee pain. Starting point when returning from injury.
- **Ref:** [02:08:15](transcript.md#020815-muscular-endurance-fast-vs-slow-twitch-muscle)

### The Galpin Equation (Hydration During Exercise)
- **What:** Practical formula for daily and in-session hydration.
- **How:** **Daily baseline:** bodyweight in pounds ÷ 2 = total ounces of fluid per day. **During exercise:** bodyweight in pounds ÷ 30 = ounces every 15–20 minutes. **Sodium:** 500 mg before and after training; higher-sodium sweaters go to 750 mg. If session is over 60 minutes in heat, add 300 mg sodium during. To determine sweat sodium content: use Levelen sweat patch ($150) or Gatorade consumer patch ($25). Visual shortcut: check hat/headband for white salt rings after training.
- **When:** Every exercise session; must arrive well-hydrated — you cannot catch up in-session.
- **Ref:** [02:16:35](transcript.md#021635-hydration-the-galpin-equation-sodium-fasting)

### Cold Exposure Timing Protocol
- **What:** Using cold immersion for recovery while protecting hypertrophy adaptations.
- **How:** Avoid cold water immersion (ice bath, not shower) immediately after strength/hypertrophy — wait at least 4 hours. Cold showers are far less disruptive. After endurance training, cold is neutral to potentially beneficial (may enhance mitochondrial biogenesis). Morning cold is Galpin's preferred default: HRV drops acutely then recovers to above-baseline over 2–3 hours. Moving in cold water (breaking the thermal layer) intensifies the stimulus more than sitting still. Distinguish adaptation phase (limit post-lifting cold) from optimization/in-season phase (recovery priority).
- **When:** Whenever adding deliberate cold exposure to a training program.
- **Ref:** [02:35:57](transcript.md#023557-cold-exposure-training)

### Heat Exposure (Sauna) Protocol
- **What:** High-temperature sauna or hot water immersion for cardiovascular, hormonal, and recovery benefits.
- **How:** 180–220°F dry or wet sauna. Some studies suggest post-training sauna may augment hypertrophy via blood flow, metabolic stress, and heat shock proteins (not yet definitive). Huberman's practice: three 20-minute sessions per week (60 min total). Rehydrate aggressively afterward — expect 2–3 lbs of fluid loss at ~225 lbs. Infrared sauna generally does not reach sufficient temperatures. Sauna is not a substitute for exercise.
- **When:** Post-training or on rest/recovery days.
- **Ref:** [02:43:15](transcript.md#024315-heat-exposure-training)

### Recovery Readiness Markers
- **What:** Low-cost daily tests to assess nervous system recovery before training.
- **How:** (1) **Grip strength on waking:** dynamometer or subjective quality; drop from baseline = incomplete recovery. (2) **CO2 tolerance test:** 4 rhythmic deep breaths in/out through nose; on the 4th, full inhale, then exhale as slowly as possible through nose; time the exhale — longer = better parasympathetic tone. (3) **Finger tap test:** tap a single finger as fast as possible for a set interval; lower count than baseline = CNS fatigue. (4) **Vertical jump:** daily force plate or subjective assessment. Take all metrics under identical morning conditions.
- **When:** Every morning, especially during high-volume phases or before deciding whether to train hard.
- **Ref:** [02:53:47](transcript.md#025347-recovery)

### Sodium Bicarbonate (Baking Soda) Supplementation
- **What:** An oral pH buffer that delays acid-mediated fatigue during high-intensity exercise.
- **How:** Start with ½ teaspoon (approx. 2–3 g; 0.2–0.3 g/kg bodyweight) taken 45–90 minutes before training with food. Timing is individual — peak benefit 20–90 minutes post-ingestion. Titrate up to manage GI side effects. Most effective for 1–8 minute high-intensity efforts.
- **When:** Before high-intensity training or competition. Not necessary for pure low-rep strength work or zone 2 sessions.
- **Ref:** [03:04:02](transcript.md#030402-tool-sodium-bicarbonate)

### Beta-Alanine Supplementation
- **What:** An amino acid precursor to intramuscular carnosine that buffers intracellular acid accumulation.
- **How:** 3.2–6.4 g/day in divided doses to reduce tingling paresthesia. Can be stacked with sodium bicarbonate. Benefits accumulate over 4+ weeks of consistent use; not an acute booster.
- **When:** For muscular endurance work (20+ rep sets, circuits, events lasting 1–4 minutes).
- **Ref:** [03:17:26](transcript.md#031726-tool-creatine-monohydrate)

### Creatine Monohydrate Supplementation
- **What:** The highest-evidence, broadest-benefit performance supplement available.
- **How:** 5 g/day of creatine monohydrate, taken consistently (with or without food, on training and non-training days). Loading (20 g/day in 4 divided doses for 5–7 days) saturates stores faster but is optional. No need to cycle.
- **When:** Daily, indefinitely. Effects on strength and power emerge after 2–4 weeks. Neurological and metabolic benefits require longer-term use.
- **Ref:** [03:17:26](transcript.md#031726-tool-creatine-monohydrate)

### Absolute Rest Platform
- **What:** A diagnostic sleep and recovery system identifying mechanistic causes of poor sleep with targeted interventions.
- **How:** Built via first-principles thinking by Galpin, Cody Burkhart (NASA), computer scientists, and an MD. Assesses four nodes: (1) psychology, (2) physiology (cortisol, DHEA, melatonin, dopamine, serotonin — tracked day, pre-sleep, and next morning), (3) pathology (full PSG polysomnography for sleep disorders), (4) environment (temperature, humidity, VOCs, allergens, CO2 cloud near face). Example: one athlete had 280+ sleep disruption episodes per night — all positional — solved with a $20 back pillow; testosterone tripled in 3 months.
- **When:** High-performance recovery context; individuals who have optimized basic sleep hygiene.
- **Ref:** [03:20:08](transcript.md#032008-absolute-rest)
