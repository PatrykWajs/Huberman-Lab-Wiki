# Episode 170: Improving Male Sexual Health, Function & Fertility | Dr. Michael Eisenberg

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Michael Eisenberg — urologist, clinician, and research scientist at Stanford who has published over 300 peer-reviewed articles — joins Andrew Huberman for a comprehensive review of male reproductive and sexual health. They cover global trends in sperm quality and testosterone decline, the outsized role male factors play in couple infertility, and how lifestyle variables (heat, sleep, obesity, exercise, alcohol, cannabis, smoking) affect testicular function. The conversation examines prostate health, urinary symptoms, and the full spectrum of erectile dysfunction causes and treatments — from PDE5 inhibitors and penile injections to inflatable implants — as well as pelvic floor dysfunction and when Kegel exercises can backfire. Eisenberg describes his meta-analysis showing penile length has been increasing over decades (the opposite of sperm count trends), discusses the real risks of finasteride/dutasteride including post-finasteride syndrome, and explains clinical tools — clomiphene, hCG, FSH therapy, varicocele repair — that can raise testosterone or restore fertility without suppressing sperm production. The episode closes with practical guidance on semen analysis as a general health biomarker, paternal age risks, sperm banking, testicular cancer self-exam, and how to access testing through insurance.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Sperm quality decline is real but contested** — A landmark 1990s Danish paper showed a 50-year decline; a recent global meta-analysis of tens of thousands of men (including African and Asian data) supports continued decline. Geographic variation is significant: in a multi-center US study of fertile fathers, men from rural Midwest settings showed lower sperm parameters than men from urban East Coast centers — possibly pesticide exposure, possibly population differences. Obesity explains only ~10% of the purported 50% decline, leaving endocrine disruptors and other factors as likely contributors. [00:04:20](transcript.md#000420-sperm-quality-geographic--environmental-factors)

2. **Male factors cause ~50% of couple infertility, yet men are bypassed 1/3 of the time** — When couples seek fertility care, the male is skipped in about a third of cases despite contributing to half of infertility diagnoses. IVF/ICSI requiring only a single sperm has reduced clinical incentive to investigate and optimize male factors. [00:12:00](transcript.md#001200-fertility--sperm-quality-testosterone-cell-phones--heat)

3. **Testosterone levels are declining across decades even after adjusting for age and BMI** — CDC NHANES longitudinal data and multiple cohort studies show men recruited in earlier decades had higher testosterone than age-matched men recruited later. Obesity-driven aromatization (fat converts testosterone to estrogen), insulation of the testes by larger thighs, and likely prenatal or adult endocrine disruptor exposure are the leading mechanisms. [00:19:26](transcript.md#001926-testosterone-age-obesity)

4. **Exogenous testosterone shuts down sperm production via LH/FSH suppression** — Intratesticular testosterone (needed for spermatogenesis) is ~10x higher than serum levels; exogenous testosterone eliminates endogenous LH/FSH drive, collapsing both. Approximately 1 in 20 infertile men presenting to Eisenberg's clinic are infertile because of testosterone therapy they started without knowing this risk. hCG (500–1,000 IU every other day) can maintain sperm production while on testosterone by acting as an LH mimic. [00:26:49](transcript.md#002649-tool-optimize-sperm-quality-exogenous-testosterone-hcg)

5. **Heat is one of the most evidence-based threats to sperm quality** — The testes must be ~2–4°C below core body temperature. Hot tubs, prolonged sitting, seat warmers, cycling >5 hours/week, and laptops in the lap all raise scrotal temperature. Scrotal cooling packs worn inside shorts in saunas can partially offset heat exposure; studies support their benefit. Testosterone production is far less sensitive to heat than spermatogenesis. [00:26:49](transcript.md#002649-tool-optimize-sperm-quality-exogenous-testosterone-hcg)

6. **Lifestyle factors with clearest evidence: smoking is definitively harmful; alcohol matters at high volumes** — Smoking is bad across all semen parameters and should be avoided entirely. Alcohol shows effects mainly above ~20 drinks/week; daily cannabis use in a 1,200-man landmark study significantly lowered concentration, motility, and morphology, though lower-frequency use data are less definitive. Opioids and benzodiazepines are assumed harmful. Current OTC NSAIDs (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) appear safe based on current data. [00:36:57](transcript.md#003657-tool-lifestyle-factors--sperm-quality-alcohol)

7. **Step count independently predicts testosterone across BMI categories** — Walking more is associated with higher testosterone in large national datasets even when controlling for BMI — a direct incentive independent of weight loss. Cycling merits specific caution: >5 hours/week correlates with lower sperm counts; prolonged saddle pressure on the pudendal neurovascular bundle can cause numbness and erectile dysfunction independent of heat. Standing up in the saddle periodically helps dissipate heat. [00:49:55](transcript.md#004955-bicycling-numbness--sexual-dysfunction-walking--testosterone)

8. **Semen quality is the "sixth vital sign" — a barometer of whole-body health and longevity** — Men with higher semen quality have lower rates of cancer, cardiovascular disease, and all-cause mortality independent of fertility goals. ~10% of the male genome is devoted to reproduction, so reproductive failure can signal broader systemic dysfunction. About 15% of men have sub-normal semen quality; ~1% have no sperm in the ejaculate and are unaware. [01:03:12](transcript.md#010312-sleep--semen-quality-overall-health)

9. **Sleep follows a U-shaped relationship with semen quality — 7–9 hours is optimal** — Both insufficient and excessive sleep correlate with lower semen quality. Nocturnal penile tumescence is linked to REM sleep cycles; consistently waking without morning erections can indicate vascular or neural issues warranting evaluation. [01:03:12](transcript.md#010312-sleep--semen-quality-overall-health)

10. **Paternal age increases mutation load and child health risks** — Average US paternal age rose from ~27.5 to ~31 over 40 years. Each year adds ~2 new mutations to sperm DNA. Older fathers have higher rates of children with achondroplasia (via selfish spermatogonial selection), autism, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, leukemia, CNS cancers, and smaller neonatal brain volume on MRI. Sperm banking before 40 and earlier conception are the main mitigation strategies. [01:13:21](transcript.md#011321-paternal-age--puberty-trends-older-fathers--child-health-risk)

11. **Erectile dysfunction is primarily vascular/neural — endocrine causes are <10%** — High blood pressure, diabetes, atherosclerosis, and obesity are the dominant causes. The penile arteries (~1 mm diameter) occlude before coronary or cerebral vessels, making ED an early warning sign of systemic vascular disease. Men diagnosed with ED should be screened for cardiovascular risk factors. After lifestyle optimization, PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil, avanafil) work in ~60–70% of men; urethral suppositories and intracavernosal injections (80–90% efficacy) are next; inflatable penile implants are the most invasive but highly effective last option. [01:38:21](transcript.md#013821-blood-flow--erectile-dysfunction-medication-cardiovascular-health)

12. **Pelvic floor dysfunction is bidirectional — Kegels are not universally beneficial** — Weak pelvic floor causes urinary leakage and contributes to ED; hypertonic (overly tight) pelvic floor causes urgency, pain, split urine stream, and delayed ejaculation. Kegel exercises strengthen but can worsen hypertonic cases. Pelvic floor PT with biofeedback and relaxation techniques benefits both subtypes; also appropriate post-prostatectomy. [01:52:36](transcript.md#015236-pelvic-floor-health-urology--physical-therapy-split-stream-urination)

13. **Penile length has increased over 30+ years globally — likely prenatal androgen disruption** — A meta-analysis of 55,000 men found increasing erect penile length (average ~5–6 inches erect) over several decades — the opposite direction from sperm count trends. Leading hypotheses: prenatal endocrine disruptors acting during the male androgen programming window in utero, or earlier puberty onset leading to longer DHT exposure. Post-pubertal supplementation with DHT analogues (e.g., oxandrolone) to increase penile length is physiologically unsupported. [01:59:03](transcript.md#015903-penile-length--trends-dihydrotestosterone-dht-puberty)

14. **Finasteride/dutasteride carry real, potentially permanent sexual side effect risks** — These 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors block testosterone-to-DHT conversion. Clinical trial participants all had normal baseline function, masking individual sensitivity variation. In post-marketing experience, men in their 20s–40s taking these for hair loss can develop dramatically lower sperm counts (usually recover in 3–6 months after stopping), ED, and low libido. Post-finasteride syndrome — persistent sexual dysfunction and cognitive issues after discontinuation — is real for a subset, may be treatment-refractory, and has no known predictive biomarker. [02:09:01](transcript.md#020901-hair-loss-dutasteride-finasteride--sexual-health-post-finasteride-syndrome)

15. **Clomiphene raises testosterone without suppressing sperm; FSH is more logical but cost-prohibitive** — Clomiphene (selective estrogen receptor modulator) blocks hypothalamic estrogen receptors, increasing GnRH → LH/FSH → endogenous testosterone while preserving sperm production. It partially blunts estrogen signaling (important for libido and bone health), so men often prefer the feel of testosterone therapy. FSH injections directly stimulate spermatogenesis and outperform hCG in randomized trials, but cost $2,000–$3,000/month vs ~$300–$500 for hCG, limiting their use despite superior logic. [02:16:11](transcript.md#021611-clomiphene-testosterone--estrogen-signaling)

16. **Varicocele affects 15% of all men and 30–40% of infertile men; Peyronie's disease affects 5–10%** — Dilated scrotal veins warm the testicle and impair sperm quality; they can also cause testicular shrinkage or stunted growth in adolescents and are considered progressive — longer duration means more damage. Surgical varicocelectomy or radiological embolization can restore function. Peyronie's disease (penile scarring causing curvature/deformity) now has an FDA-approved collagenase treatment (Xiaflex) plus traction devices and surgical options. [02:24:15](transcript.md#022415-varicocele-peyronies-disease)

## Tools & Protocols

### Avoid Scrotal Heat Exposure

- **What:** Minimize external heat to the scrotum to protect spermatogenesis; testes must be ~2–4°C below core body temperature.
- **How:** Avoid prolonged hot tub use during active fertility concern; use a desk or lap pad instead of placing a laptop on the lap; stand up in the bicycle saddle periodically on long rides; limit cycling to under ~5 hours/week if sperm quality is a priority. In saunas, use a commercially available insulated scrotal cooling pack (thin barrier layer prevents frostbite) worn inside shorts.
- **When:** Ongoing; especially during fertility planning or after abnormal semen analysis.
- **Ref:** [00:26:49](transcript.md#002649-tool-optimize-sperm-quality-exogenous-testosterone-hcg)

### Baseline Semen Analysis

- **What:** Formal semen analysis measuring ejaculate volume, sperm concentration, motility, and morphology — treated as a general health biomarker ("sixth vital sign"), not only a fertility test. DNA fragmentation is an optional add-on.
- **How:** Order through a urologist, fertility clinic, or commercial home kit (note: home kits cannot reliably assess motility). DNA fragmentation testing adds cost (low thousands) but is valuable for men over 35 or with other risk factors. Insurance increasingly covers testing for appropriate indications; primary care physicians can order it.
- **When:** Recommended baseline for all men in their 20s–30s; consider sperm banking before age 40 or before starting testosterone therapy.
- **Ref:** [01:09:19](transcript.md#010919-tool-sperm-analysis--overall-health-sperm-banking)

### hCG Co-Administration with Testosterone Therapy

- **What:** Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) acts as an LH mimic, stimulating Leydig cells to maintain intratesticular testosterone and sustaining spermatogenesis during exogenous testosterone use.
- **How:** Prescribed alongside testosterone therapy; typical dose is 500–1,000 IU subcutaneously every other day. Requires physician prescription and periodic semen analysis to verify efficacy.
- **When:** Any man on testosterone therapy who wants to preserve fertility or testicular size.
- **Ref:** [00:26:49](transcript.md#002649-tool-optimize-sperm-quality-exogenous-testosterone-hcg)

### Optimize Lifestyle Foundations First

- **What:** Behavioral inputs with clearest evidence for testosterone and sperm quality: avoid all smoking, maintain healthy body weight, exercise regularly (including walking — step count correlates with testosterone independent of BMI), get 7–9 hours of sleep, and limit alcohol. These should be in place before any pharmacological testosterone support.
- **How:** Track step count as a proxy for overall activity; avoid all tobacco products; keep alcohol moderate; prioritize sleep duration and quality. Eisenberg explicitly notes testosterone therapy is commonly initiated before patients have addressed these basics.
- **When:** Ongoing lifestyle foundation.
- **Ref:** [00:36:57](transcript.md#003657-tool-lifestyle-factors--sperm-quality-alcohol), [00:49:55](transcript.md#004955-bicycling-numbness--sexual-dysfunction-walking--testosterone), [01:03:12](transcript.md#010312-sleep--semen-quality-overall-health)

### Tadalafil (Cialis) for Prostate and Urinary Health

- **What:** Low-dose daily tadalafil (2.5–5 mg) is FDA-approved for benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) and lower urinary tract symptoms, in addition to erectile dysfunction. It relaxes smooth muscle in the prostate and bladder neck.
- **How:** Taken daily at 2.5–5 mg. Simultaneously supports erectile function even at these low doses. Separate from on-demand higher doses used for acute ED.
- **When:** Men with urinary symptoms (nocturia, urgency, weak stream) or ED seeking dual benefit.
- **Ref:** [01:26:42](transcript.md#012642-tool-prostate-health-urination-tadalafil-cialis)

### PDE5 Inhibitors for Erectile Dysfunction

- **What:** Phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors (sildenafil/Viagra, tadalafil/Cialis, vardenafil/Levitra, avanafil/Stendra) enhance nitric oxide-mediated vasodilation, increasing penile blood flow when sexually stimulated. Effective in ~60–70% of men. Originally developed as blood pressure medications; ED was an off-target finding.
- **How:** On-demand (sildenafil 30–60 min before activity; tadalafil's ~20-hour half-life allows a "weekend pill" approach) or daily low-dose tadalafil (5 mg). Dose titration is standard. Common side effects: headache, facial flushing, back ache, leg cramps, nasal congestion.
- **When:** First-line pharmacological treatment for ED after lifestyle optimization. Men with ED should also be screened for cardiovascular risk (BP, lipids, fasting glucose) given the vascular overlap.
- **Ref:** [01:38:21](transcript.md#013821-blood-flow--erectile-dysfunction-medication-cardiovascular-health)

### Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

- **What:** Evaluation and targeted treatment of pelvic floor dysfunction — either weak (causing urinary leakage, post-prostatectomy incontinence) or hypertonic (causing urgency, pain, split-stream urination, delayed ejaculation, ED). Pelvic floor PTs are typically sex-agnostic in training.
- **How:** Assessment by a pelvic floor PT using biofeedback, manual therapy, relaxation techniques, or strengthening exercises as indicated. Kegel exercises are appropriate post-prostatectomy or for weak pelvic floor; they are contraindicated and potentially harmful for men with hypertonic pelvic floor.
- **When:** Men with urinary leakage, urgency, split urine stream, unexplained scrotal/perineal pain, delayed ejaculation, ED, or post-prostatectomy symptoms. Referral via urologist.
- **Ref:** [01:52:36](transcript.md#015236-pelvic-floor-health-urology--physical-therapy-split-stream-urination)

### Clomiphene for Testosterone Support Without Fertility Suppression

- **What:** Clomiphene citrate (Clomid) is a selective estrogen receptor modulator that blocks hypothalamic estrogen feedback, increasing GnRH → LH/FSH → endogenous testosterone while preserving sperm production. Used off-label for men.
- **How:** Typical off-label dosing: 25–50 mg every other day or daily, prescribed by a urologist or reproductive endocrinologist. Testosterone rises; estrogen signaling is partially blunted — a limitation for libido and bone health.
- **When:** Men with symptomatic low testosterone who still want to preserve fertility; an alternative to exogenous testosterone therapy for reproductive-age men.
- **Ref:** [02:16:11](transcript.md#021611-clomiphene-testosterone--estrogen-signaling)

### Varicocele Evaluation and Treatment

- **What:** Clinical grading (Grade 1–3) of dilated scrotal veins by physical exam and ultrasound. Surgical varicocelectomy (outpatient ligation) or radiological embolization to restore normal testicular temperature and blood flow.
- **How:** Evaluation by a urologist. Treatment indicated when varicocele accompanies fertility concerns, abnormal semen analysis, low testosterone, scrotal discomfort, or stunted testicular growth in adolescents. Considered a progressive lesion — earlier treatment is generally better.
- **When:** Any man with fertility concerns, unexplained low testosterone or low sperm parameters, or persistent scrotal discomfort. Present in ~15% of all men and ~30–40% of infertile men.
- **Ref:** [02:24:15](transcript.md#022415-varicocele-peyronies-disease)
