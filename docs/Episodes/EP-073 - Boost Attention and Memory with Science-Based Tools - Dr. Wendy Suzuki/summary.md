# Episode 73: Boost Attention & Memory with Science-Based Tools | Dr. Wendy Suzuki

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

In this episode, Andrew Huberman hosts Dr. Wendy Suzuki, Professor of Neural Science and Psychology at New York University and incoming Dean of the College of Arts and Science at NYU, to discuss the neuroscience of memory, attention, and brain plasticity. They cover how the hippocampus encodes, stores, and imagines events across past, present, and future — and why losing hippocampal function (as in the famous patient HM) erases the ability to form new memories entirely. Dr. Suzuki shares the personal story of how gaining 25 lbs during her tenure push led her to exercise, which transformed both her grant writing ability and her research program. The conversation explores two molecular pathways by which cardiovascular exercise elevates BDNF — a myokine from striated muscle and beta-hydroxybutyrate (a ketone) from the liver — and how this grows a "big, fat, fluffy hippocampus." Practical protocols are provided for exercise frequency and timing, 12-minute daily body-scan meditation, positive affirmations via the IntenSati method, and behavioral strategies to protect sustained attention.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Memory has four core drivers: novelty, repetition, association, and emotional resonance.** These are the conditions under which the hippocampus commits experiences to long-term memory. The amygdala processes emotional salience and amplifies hippocampal encoding for charged events. [00:07:27](transcript.md#000727-how-memories-form)

2. **The hippocampus is the organ of context across time — not just memory.** It associates things together for past, present, and future; without it, imagination of novel futures is also lost, not only recall of past events. Patient HM's bilateral hippocampectomy in 1954 remains the clearest evidence for this role. [00:10:14](transcript.md#001014-hippocampus-memory-association--imagination)

3. **Memory storage involves both hippocampus and cortex.** Long-term memories appear to ultimately consolidate in the cortex, but the hippocampus acts as a long-term intermediate storage area — the boundary between the two is not clean. HM retained memories from before surgery partly because his posterior hippocampus was intact. [00:16:20](transcript.md#001620-encoding-long-term-memory)

4. **One-trial learning is the brain's survival fast-path.** Extraordinarily emotional or threatening events encode instantly via amygdala-hippocampus interaction, bypassing the normal repetition requirement. This is an evolutionary protective feature, biased toward negative events. [00:18:48](transcript.md#001848-one-trial-memory)

5. **Dr. Suzuki's personal morning protocol: tea meditation → 30-min cardio+weights → hot-cold shower → 7.5–8 hours sleep.** She performs this 7 days a week. The cold blast produces epinephrine/norepinephrine from locus coeruleus and a sustained dopamine rise (~2.5x, lasting 4–5 hours). Skipping even one day is noticeable. [00:21:56](transcript.md#002156-tool-foundational-habits-to-enhance-brain-performance)

6. **Cardiovascular exercise produces BDNF via two distinct molecular pathways.** Pathway 1: striated muscles release a myokine that crosses the blood-brain barrier and stimulates BDNF in the hippocampus. Pathway 2: the liver releases the ketone beta-hydroxybutyrate during exercise-induced stress, which also crosses the blood-brain barrier and stimulates BDNF. Neither pathway requires a ketogenic diet. [00:39:35](transcript.md#003935-cardiovascular-exercise-bdnf-brain-derived-neurotrophic-factor)

7. **BDNF is "Miracle-Gro for the brain," building a "big, fat, fluffy hippocampus."** It supports neurogenesis in the dentate gyrus and synaptogenesis broadly. Even sedentary people retain the capacity to grow new hippocampal neurons — exercise is the signal that triggers it. Dr. Suzuki's personal motivation for daily exercise is building hippocampal reserve against the Alzheimer's her father developed. [00:39:35](transcript.md#003935-cardiovascular-exercise-bdnf-brain-derived-neurotrophic-factor)

8. **Adult neurogenesis in humans continues into the ninth decade of life.** More recent studies with improved techniques beyond the original 1998 Rusty Gage / Peter Eriksson *Nature Medicine* paper confirm new neuron birth in adult human hippocampi across the entire lifespan, not just in young adults. [00:48:48](transcript.md#004848-neurogenesis-new-neuron-production-in-adults)

9. **Acute exercise (30–45 min cardio) improves mood, prefrontal attention, and reaction time — effects persist for at least 2 hours post-workout.** Dr. Suzuki's published and unpublished studies show significant improvements on Stroop and Eriksen flanker tasks, plus reduced anxiety, depression, and hostility across all ages (20s through 90s). Older adults showed even greater cognitive gains than younger adults. [00:51:50](transcript.md#005150-effects-of-exercise-on-memory)

10. **Exercise timing: do it just before your most cognitively demanding work.** Morning is best for most people because it aligns with peak cortisol and primes prefrontal function. Late-day high-intensity cardio can elevate cortisol at night and impair sleep-dependent memory consolidation. Start cognitive work immediately after — no waiting required. [00:56:31](transcript.md#005631-tool-timing-daily-exercise-cortisol)

11. **Two to three 45-minute cardio sessions per week produces measurable cognitive gains in sedentary adults.** Dr. Suzuki's study on low-fit 30–50-year-olds (spin class 2–3x/week for 3 months) found improved Stroop performance, recognition memory, and spatial episodic memory (virtual maze task). In mid-fit people, every additional session per week linearly improved mood and hippocampal memory — "every drop of sweat counts." [01:05:33](transcript.md#010533-tool-exercise-protocol-for-improving-cognition)

12. **A 44-year longitudinal Swedish study showed high-fit women gained 9 extra years of good cognition.** 300 Swedish women classified as low/mid/high-fit in their 40s were followed into their 80s. High-fit women maintained measurably better cognition for nine additional years compared to low-fit peers. This is correlational, not causal, but aligns with all mechanistic data. [01:00:02](transcript.md#010002-age-related-memory-loss-daily-exercise)

13. **Positive affirmations via IntenSati combine spoken statements with physical movement.** Developed by Patricia Moreno, IntenSati pairs affirmations ("I am strong now") with kickboxing/dance/yoga moves. Self-affirmation research shows activation of the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (valuation, self-relevance), improved mood, and reduction of the chronic negative self-talk documented by Ethan Kross in *Chatter*. [01:20:58](transcript.md#012058-positive-affirmations--mood)

14. **12 minutes of daily body-scan meditation for 8 weeks significantly improved stress response, mood, and cognitive performance.** Dr. Suzuki's study achieved the highest participant retention she had ever seen — adherence to 12-minute daily meditation exceeded adherence to daily podcast listening. The mechanism is repeated practice at returning to the present moment — training the attentional system like a muscle. [01:27:28](transcript.md#012728-meditation--cognitive-performance)

15. **The top three zero-cost tools for attention are exercise, meditation, and sleep.** These independently and synergistically improve prefrontal function, reduce anxiety, and support memory consolidation. Stimulants (Adderall, Ritalin, Modafinil) are widely used as substitutes at universities but do not address the root deficit created by insufficient movement, poor sleep, and chronic distraction from smartphones. [01:37:14](transcript.md#013714-tool-strategies-to-increase-attention)

## Tools & Protocols

### Foundational Brain Health Habits
- **What:** Four daily behaviors that form the cognitive performance base: quality sleep (7–9 hrs), daily cardiovascular movement, social engagement, and mindfulness practice.
- **How:** Prioritize all four daily. Dr. Suzuki's personal implementation: 45-minute tea meditation → 30-min cardio+weights → hot-cold contrast shower → 7.5–8 hours sleep. Seven days a week, including yoga or abbreviated sessions on busy days.
- **When:** Daily; consistent timing (same time each day) leverages circadian anticipation to make habits self-reinforcing.
- **Ref:** [00:21:56](transcript.md#002156-tool-foundational-habits-to-enhance-brain-performance)

### Cardiovascular Exercise for Memory & Attention
- **What:** Aerobic exercise that elevates heart rate to clearly above resting — any modality (spin class, running, cycling, kickboxing, dance, power walk).
- **How:** 45-minute sessions; 2–3x/week produces measurable cognitive gains in sedentary adults. Increasing to daily produces proportionally greater mood and hippocampal memory benefits ("every drop of sweat counts"). Even a 10-minute walk provides an acute mood boost via dopamine, serotonin, and noradrenaline.
- **When:** Preferably in the morning, immediately before the day's most cognitively demanding work. Begin cognitive work right after — no waiting window required.
- **Ref:** [01:05:33](transcript.md#010533-tool-exercise-protocol-for-improving-cognition)

### Timing Daily Exercise Around Cortisol
- **What:** Schedule exercise early to leverage natural morning cortisol for alertness and avoid disrupting sleep with late-day cortisol spikes.
- **How:** Exercise within the first 1–3 hours after waking. Avoid high-intensity cardio within 3–4 hours of sleep. Two hours of cognitive benefit follow a 30-45 min cardio session — confirmed by Dr. Suzuki's published data.
- **When:** Daily, morning preferred. Those who can only exercise later still obtain benefits; timing optimizes but does not gatekeep them.
- **Ref:** [00:56:31](transcript.md#005631-tool-timing-daily-exercise-cortisol)

### 12-Minute Daily Body-Scan Meditation
- **What:** Short, consistent guided body-scan meditation to train the attentional system and reduce stress reactivity.
- **How:** Log in to a guided meditation platform (or follow any body-scan script). Eyes closed, bring attention progressively through the body. When the mind wanders, notice and gently return — each redirection is one repetition of attention training. Same time each day aids habit formation.
- **When:** Daily, 12 minutes. Dr. Suzuki's study used 8 consecutive weeks; consistent daily practice, not occasional long sessions, drove the results.
- **Ref:** [01:27:28](transcript.md#012728-meditation--cognitive-performance)

### IntenSati: Positive Affirmations with Physical Movement
- **What:** Exercise format developed by Patricia Moreno combining spoken positive affirmations with choreographed movement from kickboxing, dance, yoga, and martial arts.
- **How:** During exercise movements, vocalize affirmations ("I am strong now," "I believe I will succeed"). Begin quietly if needed; aim to say them aloud at full voice. Standalone affirmations (written or spoken) also work — ground them in genuine personal values, not generic platitudes.
- **When:** During aerobic exercise sessions or independently in the morning. Specificity and personal relevance matter more than timing.
- **Ref:** [01:20:58](transcript.md#012058-positive-affirmations--mood)

### Strategies to Increase Attention
- **What:** Behavioral and environmental tactics to protect and build sustained attentional capacity.
- **How:** (1) Exercise before demanding cognitive work for an acute prefrontal boost lasting ≥2 hours. (2) Practice 12-min daily body-scan meditation to train present-moment focus. (3) Prioritize 7–9 hours of quality sleep — the most physiologically foundational of the three pillars. (4) Eliminate phone/notification interruptions during focused work blocks. (5) Reduce chronic phone exposure — constant connectivity is a primary driver of attentional fragmentation in modern life.
- **When:** Daily; preventive habits (sleep, exercise, meditation) are upstream. Environmental controls (phone-free blocks) apply during active work sessions.
- **Ref:** [01:37:14](transcript.md#013714-tool-strategies-to-increase-attention)
