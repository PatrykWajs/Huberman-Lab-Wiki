# Episode 113: Dr. Andy Galpin: Optimal Protocols to Build Strength & Grow Muscles | Huberman Lab Guest Series

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

This is episode 2 of a 6-part guest series with Dr. Andy Galpin, a Professor of Kinesiology at Cal State University Fullerton. The episode systematically covers the science and practical protocols for building strength, power, and muscle hypertrophy, distinguishing these as separate physiological adaptations that require different programming approaches. Galpin walks through all modifiable training variables — exercise choice, order, volume, intensity, repetition cadence, breathing, rest, and frequency — and explains the underlying mechanisms (neural, structural, and energetic) behind each adaptation. He provides complete, immediately applicable training templates for power, strength, and hypertrophy, and closes with guidance on nutrition timing, creatine supplementation, deliberate cold exposure timing, and managing recovery. The central theme is that the concepts driving adaptation are few, the methods are infinite, and adherence over time beats any single protocol.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Strength training is the only tool that combats neuromuscular aging.** Loss of muscle power runs at 8–10% per year after ~40, far outpacing strength loss (2–4%/year) and size loss (1%/year). Motor unit count drops 30–40% in older individuals. One study showed 30–170% strength/hypertrophy gains in subjects over age 90 within 12 weeks — it is never too late to start. [00:00:00](transcript.md#000000-benefits-of-strength--hypertrophy-training-aging)

2. **Strength and hypertrophy are distinct adaptations with different drivers.** Strength is a functional output (force production) driven primarily by neural changes — motor unit recruitment, firing rate, acetylcholine recycling, calcium affinity, and improved mechanics. Hypertrophy is purely structural — increased contractile protein (myosin/actin) and/or sarcoplasmic fluid volume. The two overlap heavily in beginners but decouple at intermediate/advanced levels. [00:17:48](transcript.md#001748-strength-vs-hypertrophy-training-adaptations)

3. **Connective tissue adapts slower than muscle — plan around this lag.** Tendons and ligaments respond to resistance training but on a longer timeline. Returning to previous loads after a layoff carries high injury risk because connective tissue tolerance drops faster than perceived strength. Bone density responds strongly to axial (vertical) loading, especially in teens and 20s, but positive changes are possible at any age; women with bone mineral density issues need blood work and possibly hormonal assessment alongside training. [00:22:42](transcript.md#002242-ligaments-tendons--resistance-training)

4. **The four non-negotiables of any training program: adherence, progressive overload, individualization, appropriate target.** Adherence is the single top predictor of program effectiveness in the literature. Progressive overload (~3–5% load increase per week) is the most commonly violated principle and explains most plateaus. Without it, training can still produce mood, cognition, and flexibility benefits but not strength or hypertrophy gains. [01:04:00](transcript.md#010400-non-negotiables--modifiable-variables-of-exercise-training)

5. **The "3 to 5" rule is the foundational template for power and strength.** 3–5 exercises, 3–5 sets, 3–5 reps per set, 3–5 minutes rest, 3–5 days per week. For power: 30–70% of 1RM with maximal intent. For strength: 70–100% of 1RM. Power is non-fatiguing and can be trained almost daily; strength requires more recovery. Any hypertrophy or accessory work should come after — not before — this training. [01:12:53](transcript.md#011253-tool-speed--power-training-3-to-5-approach-periodization-planning)

6. **Repetition cadence is goal-specific and should be selected deliberately.** For strength: maximize concentric intent regardless of bar speed; 3-1-1 cadence (eccentric-pause-concentric) is the default. For hypertrophy: 3-1-1 or 3-1-2 works well; slowing the eccentric to 5 seconds or adding isometric pauses builds time-under-tension and can substitute for heavier loads when equipment is limited. Triphasic training formalizes this by cycling eccentric-only, isometric-only, and concentric-only emphasis across training blocks. [01:30:55](transcript.md#013055-strength-vs-hypertrophy-repetition-cadence-triphasic-training)

7. **The Valsalva maneuver is the correct breathing strategy for heavy lifting.** Inhale into the belly (clavicles should not rise), brace all four trunk quadrants, hold through the sticking point, exhale at the top or locked-out position. Blood pressure can spike to 450/350 mmHg during a heavy compound set — the breath hold balances spinal stability against blackout risk. For multi-rep sets: plan breath clusters in advance (e.g., hold for reps 1–10, exhale at lockout, then increase breathing frequency as reps approach failure). [01:44:03](transcript.md#014403-tool-breathing--training-valsalva-technique)

8. **Training to failure is not required and is often counterproductive.** Stopping 1–2 reps short (reps in reserve) produces equivalent hypertrophy with faster recovery and less injury risk. Galpin recommends taking failure on safer single-joint or machine exercises, or on the final exercise of a session. For heavy compound barbell lifts, stop at technical failure (form breakdown). Beginners benefit from occasionally going to true failure just to understand what 100% effort feels like. [02:02:35](transcript.md#020235-training-to-failure-exercise-selection--recovery-standardization)

9. **Hypertrophy requires 10–20 working sets per muscle group per week; 15–20 is the practical sweet spot.** Minimum effective dose: ~10 sets/week/muscle. Advanced trainees may push toward 25. Volume is the primary hypertrophy driver. Rep range: 4–30 all work, with 8–15 as the most reliable sweet spot. The 5–8 rep range simultaneously builds strength and hypertrophy. Frequency matters less than total weekly volume — 3 full-body sessions per week is more resilient to schedule disruption than body-part splits. [03:09:04](transcript.md#030904-tools-hypertrophy-training--modifiable-variables-splits)

10. **Three independent mechanisms drive hypertrophy — only one is needed.** Mechanical tension (heavy load), metabolic disturbance (high reps/short rest/pump), and muscle damage. No single mechanism is required, and muscle damage is not necessary for growth. Blood flow restriction training at 20–30% 1RM achieves hypertrophy via metabolic disturbance alone. This explains why so many different training styles produce results. [02:47:29](transcript.md#024729-hypertrophy-training-program-muscle-growth--signaling)

11. **Cold water immersion after training blunts hypertrophy signaling.** Ice baths post-training block the mTOR/satellite cell cascade that triggers muscle protein synthesis. Avoid cold immersion before, after, or on the same day as hypertrophy training during growth-focused blocks. Cold showers (brief) are acceptable — not cold enough to meaningfully blunt signaling. Cold immersion is compatible with strength and endurance goals, and is appropriate for in-season recovery when recovery speed outweighs growth optimization. [04:21:09](transcript.md#042109-deliberate-cold-exposure--hypertrophy-vs-strength)

12. **Creatine monohydrate is the most evidence-backed supplement for strength, power, and hypertrophy.** Dose: 3–6g/day (5g is the standard; scale to 7–10g for athletes 275+ lbs). Timing is irrelevant. Benefits extend beyond muscle: bone mineral density, cognitive function, memory, and potential neurological disorder applications. Loading phases are unnecessary. Creatine monohydrate has the largest evidence base; other forms offer no demonstrated advantage. [04:26:41](transcript.md#042641-nutrition-timing--strengthhypertrophy-creatine)

13. **Total daily protein dominates timing for hypertrophy.** Target ~1g per pound of bodyweight (2.2g/kg). Once total intake is sufficient, protein timing and source type matter far less. Post-workout nutrition ratios: 1:1 protein-to-carbohydrate for strength work; 2:1 to 4:1 carbohydrate-to-protein for conditioning-heavy sessions. Carbohydrate timing matters more than protein timing for glycogen replenishment. Protein synthesis increases from eating protein alone (independent of exercise) — both signals are additive when combined. [04:26:41](transcript.md#042641-nutrition-timing--strengthhypertrophy-creatine)

14. **"Chaos management" — handling disrupted sessions — should be planned by training goal.** When time is short: for hypertrophy, preserve volume (drop load and rest); for strength, preserve intensity (cut volume, keep load high). Hidden stressors — poor sleep, life stress, under-nutrition — suppress recovery and act as a "foot on the brake." Addressing these often produces more progress than changing the training program itself. [03:27:06](transcript.md#032706-hypertrophy-repetition--rest-ranges-muscle-failure-chaos-management))

15. **Aerobic fitness enhances hypertrophy capacity, not just endurance.** A 6-week endurance-only conditioning block before a hypertrophy phase produced more muscle growth than jumping straight into hypertrophy training. Cardio interferes with hypertrophy primarily when: (1) total caloric intake is too low, or (2) the endurance modality involves heavy eccentric loading (running > cycling). Moderate cardio before or after lifting is fine; separating sessions into AM/PM is slightly better; separate days is ideal. [04:01:15](transcript.md#040115-combining-cardiovascular--hypertrophy-training-interference-effect)

16. **Rear deltoids and neck are high-impact, commonly neglected muscle groups.** Both directly support shoulder health, posture, and injury prevention. Rear delts: reverse flies, face pulls, band pull-aparts (15–30 reps, high frequency). Neck: isometric holds in flexion, extension, and lateral positions — avoid neck bridges (disc risk). Both recover rapidly and can be trained 3–5x/week. Eric Cressey is the recommended resource for shoulder girdle mechanics and programming. [04:12:06](transcript.md#041206-tool-neck--rear-deltoid-exercises-stabilization--hypertrophy)

## Tools & Protocols

### The "3 to 5" Framework for Power & Strength

- **What:** A compact programming rule that spans speed, power, and strength development.
- **How:** Pick 3–5 compound exercises organized by movement patterns (push, pull, hinge, rotation). Perform 3–5 sets of 3–5 reps. Rest 3–5 minutes between sets. Train 3–5 days per week. For power: 30–70% of 1RM, moving with maximum concentric intent. For strength: 70–100% of 1RM. Increase load by ~3–5% per week. Run for 5–8 weeks, then deload. Add hypertrophy or accessory work only after this training — never before.
- **When:** When power, speed, or maximal strength is the primary goal. Power work can be done almost daily; true strength work requires more recovery.
- **Ref:** [01:12:53](transcript.md#011253-tool-speed--power-training-3-to-5-approach-periodization-planning)

### Repetition Cadence Protocol (3-1-1 Default)

- **What:** Standardized rep tempo that controls eccentric phase and maximizes concentric intent.
- **How:** 3 seconds eccentric (lowering/lengthening phase), 1-second pause at the bottom, 1-second concentric (as fast as controlled allows). For hypertrophy with limited load: extend eccentric to 5–10 seconds and add a 2–3 second isometric hold to generate sufficient time under tension. Note: in exercises starting concentric (e.g., row), the 3-second phase is the return/lowering.
- **When:** Default for all resistance training. Adjust emphasis based on goal — for strength, prioritize concentric speed; for hypertrophy, emphasize time under tension.
- **Ref:** [01:30:55](transcript.md#013055-strength-vs-hypertrophy-repetition-cadence-triphasic-training)

### Triphasic Training

- **What:** A multi-block protocol isolating eccentric, isometric, and concentric phases separately to maximize muscle tension across the full range of motion.
- **How:** Phase 1 (several weeks): eccentric-only emphasis — controlled 3–5 sec lowering, return assisted or light. Phase 2: isometric emphasis — hold at most challenging position for 2–3 sec. Phase 3: concentric emphasis — explosive but controlled. Load: 60–80% 1RM, 8–15 reps. Applicable to compound and isolation movements.
- **When:** Hypertrophy-focused training phases, especially for lagging muscle groups or limited-equipment environments.
- **Ref:** [01:30:55](transcript.md#013055-strength-vs-hypertrophy-repetition-cadence-triphasic-training)

### Valsalva Breathing Technique

- **What:** Intra-abdominal pressure technique for spinal protection and force transfer during heavy compound lifts.
- **How:** Inhale into the belly (not chest; clavicles should not rise). Brace all four trunk quadrants simultaneously (front, sides, back). Hold breath through the sticking point. Exhale only after clearing the hardest portion or at the top/lockout. For multi-rep sets: plan breath clusters — hold for first cluster of reps, exhale at a safe locked-out position, re-brace, increase breathing frequency as reps approach failure.
- **When:** All heavy compound movements: squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press. At near-max loads (1–3 reps), hold for the entire rep. Not required for light isolation work.
- **Ref:** [01:44:03](transcript.md#014403-tool-breathing--training-valsalva-technique)

### Prilepin's Chart for Load Prescription

- **What:** Soviet weightlifting guideline mapping intensity zones to optimal rep volumes for strength training.
- **How:** At 55–65% 1RM: 3–6 reps/set, ~24 total reps optimal. At 70–75%: 3–6 reps/set, ~18 total. At 80–85%: 2–4 reps/set, ~15 total. At 90%+: 1–2 reps/set, ~4–10 total reps. Even pure strength athletes spend ~35% of training in the 55–65% zone for skill accumulation and tissue tolerance. Use to auto-regulate volume across a block without exceeding recovery capacity.
- **When:** Periodization planning for strength and power blocks. Useful for assessing whether a failed rep or off-day should alter planned volume.
- **Ref:** [01:53:22](transcript.md#015322-tool-training-auto-regulation-specificity-vs-variation-prilepins-chart), https://bit.ly/3XD9Nxw

### Cluster Sets for Strength

- **What:** Breaking a set into individual-rep clusters with brief intra-set rest to maintain maximal force output across all reps.
- **How:** Perform 1 rep, rack or set down the implement, rest 5–20 seconds, perform the next rep, repeat until set volume is complete. Example: 5 singles with 10-second pauses instead of 5 continuous reps. Best suited to exercises with easy reset: deadlifts, trap bar, box jumps. More awkward for bench press.
- **When:** Training at 85–100% 1RM when maintaining peak force quality on every rep is the priority. Can be the primary strategy for compound movements or applied only to the session's first exercise.
- **Ref:** [02:30:16](transcript.md#023016-tools-strength-training--modifiable-variables-cluster-sets-dynamic-variable-sets)

### Dynamic Variable Resistance (Bands & Chains)

- **What:** Accommodating resistance that increases load as you move into a stronger position, training the full human strength curve.
- **How:** Attach elastic bands or chains to the barbell. As you stand/press/squat, resistance increases (bands stretch, chains lift off floor) — heaviest where you are mechanically strongest. Start with a lower base barbell weight to account for added band/chain tension. Requires stable setup and controlled execution.
- **When:** Intermediate-to-advanced strength training, especially for breaking through sticking points. Commonly applied to deadlift, squat, and bench press.
- **Ref:** [02:30:16](transcript.md#023016-tools-strength-training--modifiable-variables-cluster-sets-dynamic-variable-sets)

### Hypertrophy Training Weekly Template

- **What:** Weekly volume and structure guidelines for reliable muscle growth without excessive complexity.
- **How:** Target 10–20 working sets per muscle group per week (15–20 is the practical sweet spot). Distribute across 2–4 sessions per muscle. Per session: 2–5 exercises per muscle group, 3–5 sets each. Reps: 8–15 for most sets; some sets at 4–8 (strength-hypertrophy overlap) and some at 15–30 (metabolic stress). Rest: 60–90 seconds for metabolic emphasis, 2–3 minutes for heavier sets. Include both bilateral and unilateral movements. Ensure hamstrings, rear delts, and neck receive direct work — commonly under-hit.
- **When:** Any block where hypertrophy is the primary goal. Can be run as full-body 3x/week, upper/lower, push/pull/legs, or body-part splits — frequency matters less than achieving total weekly volume.
- **Ref:** [03:09:04](transcript.md#030904-tools-hypertrophy-training--modifiable-variables-splits)

### Cold Exposure Timing Protocol

- **What:** Guideline for using deliberate cold exposure without blunting hypertrophy signaling.
- **How:** Avoid cold water immersion before, immediately after, or on the same day as hypertrophy training. If cold is desired during a hypertrophy block, restrict it to full rest days only. Cold showers (brief, not full immersion) are acceptable — insufficient cooling to meaningfully blunt signaling. During non-hypertrophy phases (strength, endurance, in-season), cold immersion post-training is appropriate.
- **When:** Any time deliberate cold exposure co-exists with resistance training. Match cold use to the current phase goal.
- **Ref:** [04:21:09](transcript.md#042109-deliberate-cold-exposure--hypertrophy-vs-strength)

### Neck & Rear Deltoid Training for Stabilization & Hypertrophy

- **What:** Direct training for two commonly neglected muscle groups with outsized impact on posture, shoulder health, and injury prevention.
- **How:** Rear delts: lying reverse flies (scapula down and back cue), face pulls, band pull-aparts. 15–30 reps, 3–4 sets, 2–4x/week. Neck: isometric holds in flexion, extension, and lateral positions; avoid neck bridges (disc injury risk). Head-harness devices acceptable. 2–3 sets of isometrics 3–5x/week. Both muscle groups recover quickly.
- **When:** End of upper body sessions or as standalone activation work. Prioritize if doing bench-heavy or push-dominant programming to prevent anterior/posterior imbalance.
- **Ref:** [04:12:06](transcript.md#041206-tool-neck--rear-deltoid-exercises-stabilization--hypertrophy)

### Post-Workout Nutrition Protocol

- **What:** Macronutrient ratio guidelines for fueling recovery and maximizing protein synthesis around training.
- **How:** Strength-focused training: 1:1 protein-to-carbohydrate (e.g., 35g protein / 35g carbs). Conditioning/endurance-heavy training: 3:1 to 4:1 carb-to-protein (e.g., 35g protein / 100–140g carbs). Mixed strength + conditioning: 2:1 carb-to-protein (e.g., 35g protein / 60–70g carbs). Can be consumed pre-, mid-, or post-workout. If training fasted, prioritize consuming within 1–2 hours post-session. Daily protein target: ~1g/lb bodyweight.
- **When:** Around every training session. Carbohydrate timing is more critical than protein timing for glycogen replenishment.
- **Ref:** [04:26:41](transcript.md#042641-nutrition-timing--strengthhypertrophy-creatine)
