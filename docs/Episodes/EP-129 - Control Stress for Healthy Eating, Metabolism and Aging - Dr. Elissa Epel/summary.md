# Episode 129: Control Stress for Healthy Eating, Metabolism & Aging | Dr. Elissa Epel

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Elissa Epel, professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at UCSF and director of the Center on Aging, Metabolism and Emotions, joins Andrew Huberman to examine how stress — in its many forms — shapes mood, eating behavior, metabolic health, and cellular aging. The conversation covers why thoughts and rumination are the dominant modern stressor, how chronic perceived helplessness shortens telomeres while hormetic (beneficial) stress promotes hippocampal growth and resilience, and how stress drives compulsive overeating through insulin resistance and the opioid reward system. Dr. Epel presents evidence-based tools — stress shields, mindful eating, body scans, radical acceptance, expressive journaling, and breathwork — and shares preliminary findings from her UCSF study comparing low-arousal relaxation methods to high-arousal approaches including the Wim Hof method, revealing that all paths reduce anxiety and depression while the Wim Hof group shows a selective boost in daily positive affect. The episode also covers how a two-month mindfulness intervention during pregnancy improved insulin sensitivity in mothers and produced healthier metabolic profiles in their children, why mitochondrial health is the cellular bridge between chronic stress and depleted mood, and why the "skillful surfing" framework — knowing when to lean in versus when to accept — is the master skill of stress resilience.

## Guest: Dr. Elissa Epel

Dr. Elissa Epel is a professor and vice chair of the Department of Psychiatry at the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF) and director of the Aging, Metabolism and Emotion (AMEC) Center. She is a leading researcher on stress, telomere biology, and metabolic health. She is co-author (with Dr. Elizabeth Blackburn) of "The Telomere Effect" and author of "The Stress Prescription." Her lab has pioneered research showing how perceived stress shortens telomeres, how stress drives compulsive eating and insulin resistance, and how mindfulness and breathwork interventions produce lasting biological benefits.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Thoughts are the primary stressor.** Stressors themselves are inevitable; the stress response is what matters. Rumination — mentally replaying stressful events — is the most common and damaging form of modern stress. The breathing strategies are the most direct and fast path to reducing stress in the body. [00:06:18](transcript.md#000618-stress-effects-on-body--mind)

2. **Three bins of stress-mitigation tools.** Top-down strategies (mindset shifts, reframing, narrative), body-based strategies (breathwork, exercise, cold exposure), and environmental shifts (safe spaces, safety signals like pets, photos, music). All three are needed; different situations call for different tools. [00:12:50](transcript.md#001250-tools-overthinking--stress)

3. **Some stress is essential for optimal aging.** Underexposure to stress leads to faster cognitive and cellular aging than moderate, controlled stress. The Baltimore Experience Corps study showed hippocampal growth in elderly volunteers who took on the meaningful challenge of tutoring at-risk youth. Dave Almeida's national daily-stressor data found that people who report no stressors have significantly lower cognitive health. [00:21:23](transcript.md#002123-stress-benefits-aging--cognition-stress-challenge-response)

4. **Chronic perceived helplessness shortens telomeres.** Caregivers of chronically ill relatives show significantly shorter telomeres than controls. The mindset with which stress is viewed — threat versus challenge — predicts telomere length even under the same objective stress load. [00:21:23](transcript.md#002123-stress-benefits-aging--cognition-stress-challenge-response)

5. **Shifting to a challenge response is achievable and measurable.** Personalized "stress shields" — self-statements like recalling past successes, distancing ("this won't matter in 10 years"), and reframing physiological arousal as helpful — shift physiology toward vasodilation, better cardiovascular output, and improved cognitive performance. [00:32:19](transcript.md#003219-tool-shifting-stress-to-challenge-response-stress-shields)

6. **Stress drives overeating through insulin resistance and the opioid reward system.** Roughly 50% of people with obesity have a compulsive eating phenotype where stress triggers craving and inability to feel full. The more insulin-resistant a person is, the more their reward center activates during stress, creating a vicious cycle. Sugary beverages compound the problem by bypassing satiety signals on top of a cortisol-elevated glucose state. [00:37:40](transcript.md#003740-stress-overeating-craving--opioid-system)

7. **Mindful eating + HIIT can break the stress-eating cycle.** In clinical trials, mindful eating improved insulin resistance and long-term weight management specifically in people with compulsive eating patterns. High-intensity exercise metabolizes the stress response — cortisol, adrenaline, elevated blood glucose — that would otherwise drive craving. Cravings are like waves; they peak and pass. [00:48:55](transcript.md#004855-tools-breaking-overeating-cycles-mindfulness)

8. **Mindfulness during pregnancy improves insulin sensitivity in mothers and metabolic health in offspring.** A two-month mindful eating and stress-reduction intervention in pregnant women significantly improved insulin sensitivity within the first month. At long-term follow-up, mothers showed lower depression and their children showed healthier metabolic profiles — suggesting epigenetic effects on the developing fetus. [01:06:47](transcript.md#010647-tools-mindfulness-pregnancy--metabolic-health)

9. **A brief body scan reduces food cravings.** Craving is a state where internal reward circuitry is locked to an external cue. A body scan intentionally shifts attention inward — to skin, heartbeat, breath — unhooking that lock. Attention is a lever for craving control; the fact that something as brief as a body scan can meaningfully reduce craving underscores how powerful directed attention is. [01:14:11](transcript.md#011411-body-scan--cravings)

10. **Meditation slows biological aging.** Cross-sectional studies show longer telomeres in long-term meditators. One-week Transcendental Meditation retreats dampen inflammatory gene expression pathways dramatically; machine learning identified retreat vs. non-retreat participants over 90% of the time. Reduced depression persisted 10 months later, especially in people with early-life adversity. [01:17:28](transcript.md#011728-tool-meditation--aging-meditation-retreats)

11. **Mitochondrial health mediates the link between chronic stress and mood.** Caregiving mothers show significantly lower mitochondrial enzyme activity (less ATP production) than non-caregiving mothers. Within that group, those with better mitochondrial function experience more positive emotion daily. Burnout is literally in the cells. [01:26:02](transcript.md#012602-mitochondrial-health-stress--mood)

12. **Radical acceptance preserves biological resources.** Attempting to control genuinely uncontrollable stressors ("brick walls") consumes enormous psychobiological resources. People who score high on equanimity show flatter cortisol curves, less inflammatory gene expression, and longer telomeres. The practice: ask "Is this a brick wall?" — if yes, drop the rope and redirect energy toward the actual sphere of influence. [01:29:49](transcript.md#012949-chronic-stress--radical-acceptance-brick-wall)

13. **Uncertainty tolerance is trainable.** Intolerance of uncertainty — preferring a bad certain outcome over an uncertain potentially good one — is a major driver of anxiety and rumination. Mindfulness practice over time increases people's ability to sit with "I don't know" without triggering the full alarm response. [01:37:57](transcript.md#013757-tool-control-uncertainty)

14. **"Skillful surfing" requires two modes.** The challenge-response/lean-in approach is energetically expensive but appropriate for acute, bounded, navigable challenges. The receptive/acceptance mode is better for chronic brick-wall stressors. Applying the wrong mode is exhausting. Each successful stress navigation — by either mode — raises the stress threshold via stress inoculation. [01:45:25](transcript.md#014525-stress-management-skillful-surfing)

15. **Expressive writing builds narrative coherence and improves biological outcomes.** Journaling about deepest thoughts and feelings around a stressor — over several sessions — produces measurable psychological and physical health improvements. People under chronic stress who can narrate their experience (what it means, how they're coping) show better biological outcomes than equally stressed people who cannot make sense of it. [01:50:25](transcript.md#015025-narrative-purpose--stress)

16. **The Wim Hof method produces selective gains in daily positive emotion.** In Dr. Epel's UCSF study comparing mindfulness/slow breathing to exercise/Wim Hof method, all groups showed dramatically reduced anxiety and depression after three weeks of daily practice. The Wim Hof group showed a particularly selective daily increase in positive affect — mirroring Huberman Lab's cyclic sighing study — while physiological profiles diverged significantly across groups. [01:52:49](transcript.md#015249-breathwork-wim-hof-method-positivity--cellular-aging)

## Tools & Protocols

### Stress Shields (Challenge-Response Reframing)

- **What:** Personalized self-statements used before or during a stressful event to shift the body from a threat response (vasoconstriction, inflammation) to a challenge response (vasodilation, increased cardiac output, better cognition).
- **How:** Choose statements that feel authentic: recall a past success ("I've handled harder things before"), activate resources ("I can call X"), use temporal distancing ("this won't matter in 5 years"), reframe arousal ("my heart racing means my body is ready — this is helpful"), or use direct affirmation ("I got this"). Write, say aloud, or think them — all modalities work.
- **When:** Before entering a stressful situation (preparation) or during it (real-time intervention). Practice during low-stakes daily stressors to build the habit.
- **Ref:** [00:32:19](transcript.md#003219-tool-shifting-stress-to-challenge-response-stress-shields) | Chapter 3 of "The Stress Prescription" by Dr. Elissa Epel

### Mindful Eating for Stress-Induced Overeating

- **What:** A structured practice of checking in with hunger signals, slowing down eating, and increasing interoceptive awareness before and during meals — targeting the compulsive/craving-driven eating pattern triggered by stress.
- **How:** Before eating, pause and scan the body: Am I physically hungry or emotionally triggered? Eat slowly, put utensils down between bites, notice texture and taste without judgment. The goal is not calorie restriction but re-regulation of the hunger/satiety system.
- **When:** At every meal, with particular importance at times of elevated stress. Most benefit for people who identify with stress-triggered craving or inability to feel full.
- **Ref:** [00:48:55](transcript.md#004855-tools-breaking-overeating-cycles-mindfulness) | UCSF Mindful MOMS trial

### Body Scan for Craving Interruption

- **What:** A brief (2–5 minute) systematic shift of attention from the external environment to internal body sensations to interrupt the exteroceptive-interoceptive lock driving a food craving.
- **How:** Close eyes or soften gaze. Begin at the top of the head or soles of the feet. Slowly move attention through each body part, noticing sensation — warmth, tension, tingling, numbness — without trying to change anything. When a craving arises, note it without acting, then return attention to the body.
- **When:** When a craving appears, before deciding whether to eat. Also usable as a daily standalone 5-minute practice to build interoceptive skill.
- **Ref:** [01:14:11](transcript.md#011411-body-scan--cravings)

### High-Intensity Exercise for Stress Metabolization

- **What:** Short, high-intensity exercise bouts (HIIT or brisk walks) used specifically to metabolize the stress response in the body and reduce cravings — working through the "positive stress" pathway.
- **How:** Any form that elevates heart rate significantly for 10–20 minutes: sprint intervals, HIIT circuits, brisk uphill walking, cycling at high resistance. Goal is to burn off elevated cortisol, adrenaline, and blood glucose that would otherwise drive craving.
- **When:** As a direct intervention when stressed and feeling cravings, before defaulting to comfort eating. Also effective as a daily practice to reduce baseline stress-driven craving frequency.
- **Ref:** [00:48:55](transcript.md#004855-tools-breaking-overeating-cycles-mindfulness)

### Radical Acceptance Practice ("Drop the Rope")

- **What:** A cognitive-behavioral practice of distinguishing controllable from uncontrollable stressors and consciously withdrawing effort from genuinely immovable situations ("brick walls") to conserve psychobiological resources.
- **How:** (1) Ask — "Is this actually a brick wall? Can I change this?" (2) If no, practice the internal gesture of "dropping the rope" — notice the sensation of resistance and deliberately release it. (3) Redirect freed energy toward your actual sphere of influence. (4) When the urge to pull resurfaces, recognize it without judgment and drop the rope again. Journaling supports this process.
- **When:** Daily practice for people under chronic, ongoing stress (caregiving, chronic illness, unresolvable work or relationship stress). Requires consistent cultivation; does not happen automatically.
- **Ref:** [01:29:49](transcript.md#012949-chronic-stress--radical-acceptance-brick-wall) | "The Stress Prescription," chapter on radical acceptance

### Expressive Writing / Narrative Journaling

- **What:** Structured journaling sessions writing about deepest thoughts and feelings around a stressor to build narrative coherence — transforming chaotic stress experience into something with shape, meaning, and edges to work with.
- **How:** Write about what you're facing, why it's hard, what it means to you, and how you're coping. No editing for grammar or polish. A few sessions (3–5) over days or weeks. The coherence-building function — not just the emotional release — is the active ingredient.
- **When:** During periods of chronic or non-negotiable stress. Particularly valuable for situations with no clear resolution, where meaning-making is the primary lever available.
- **Ref:** [01:50:25](transcript.md#015025-narrative-purpose--stress) | James Pennebaker's expressive writing research

### Wim Hof Breathing (Positive Stress / Hormetic Breathwork)

- **What:** A high-arousal breathwork method involving cycles of 30–40 deep, forceful inhales and passive exhales followed by a breath hold on exhale, then a recovery breath with a short hold. Creates deliberate physiological stress in a controlled, short-duration burst — the "hormetic stress" pathway to resilience.
- **How:** Standard protocol: 30–40 rounds of deep belly-to-chest inhale (through nose or mouth) followed by a relaxed, passive exhale. After the last exhale, hold breath (on empty) as long as comfortable. Then take one large inhale, hold 15 seconds, exhale. That is one round; typically 3–4 rounds per session. Practice lying down in a safe environment, never in or near water.
- **When:** Morning practice is common. In Dr. Epel's UCSF study, participants practiced daily for three weeks; significant reductions in anxiety and depression were observed alongside selective daily increases in positive affect.
- **Ref:** [01:52:49](transcript.md#015249-breathwork-wim-hof-method-positivity--cellular-aging) | Ongoing UCSF study (Epel, Mendes, Prather); John W. Brick Foundation funding

### Daily Meditation Practice

- **What:** A consistent, brief daily meditation practice — focused-attention or mantra-based — as a long-term investment in slower biological aging, reduced inflammatory gene expression, and boosted telomerase activity.
- **How:** 10–20 minutes daily. Focused-attention: choose an anchor (breath, mantra, body sensation) and return attention to it each time it wanders. Transcendental Meditation uses a personalized mantra. The specific form matters less than consistency. Extended retreats (1 week or more) produce larger acute shifts in gene expression and mood.
- **When:** Daily. Retreats are most effective after establishing a daily practice baseline; effects on depression can persist 10+ months.
- **Ref:** [01:17:28](transcript.md#011728-tool-meditation--aging-meditation-retreats) | UCSF Retreat Study; Deepak Chopra retreat collaboration
