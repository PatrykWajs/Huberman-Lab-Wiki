## [00:06:18] Stress; Effects on Body & Mind

**Andrew Huberman:** So great to have you here. We have colleagues in common and topics of interest related to our laboratories in common, so we have a lot of questions today. I'd love to just kick off by you explaining a little bit about the different forms of stress. We hear "stress is bad," "stress can kill us," "no one likes to feel stressed," and so on — but as you and I both know, that's not the entire picture. I'd love for you to educate us on what stress is and what it isn't, where it can be problematic, and where perhaps it can even be beneficial.

**Elissa Epel:** So as a stress scientist, it is a word I use a lot, but it has to be broken down because it has so many different kinds of dimensions and meaning. There's good and bad stress, there's acute and chronic stress. Technically it just means any time we feel overwhelmed, that we feel like the demands are too much for our resources. That's a very technical way to put it. But really so much of life is about meeting challenges, and we're never going to get rid of different stressful situations in life — if anything, they are increasing. So it really comes down to not the stressors, what happens to us, but really how we respond — the stress response. That's a distinction that we're still trying to get the field to talk about in a more specific way so that we can think about what situations are in your life. They might be difficult ongoing situations like caregiving, or work stress, or worrying about health — your own or someone's. And then there's how are you coping with it. So when something happens we mount a stress response and we recover, and that's beautiful — no harm done. We need that. That's why we're here still alive. It's that survival response. The problem these days is that we just keep it alive in our head. We keep it alive with our thoughts. Our thoughts are the most common form of stress.

**Andrew Huberman:** Even though I expected we would get into tools to combat stress a little bit later, since you have now told us that our thoughts are the biggest propagator of internal stress — what, to your knowledge, is the best way, or what are the best ways for us to manage overthinking and ruminating on stressful topics? I certainly experience stress and when I do I have tools related to breathwork, running, exercise, sleep, non-sleep deep rest. But when we succumb to stress and the thinking patterns take over and the gears are turning and they won't stop turning — what are the tools that are most efficient for dealing with that overthinking or ruminating?

## [00:12:50] Tools: Overthinking & Stress

**Elissa Epel:** When people just can't seem to let go of the thing that's the stressor — thinking about not the stress in their body but the thing that caused the stress, the difficult conversation, the thing that irked them on social media or in their personal or professional life — I wish I had one answer, but I'm going to say lots of strategies tackle that. So in those three bins: one, our top-down strategies of awareness and things that we can say to ourselves — since our beliefs and mindsets can really help us release stress and view stress more positively. The second bucket is not that the mind changes the body, but the body changes the mind. Those are the set of strategies that you tend to use the most, right? We're working stress out of the body, we're metabolizing it, we're burning it up, and we get relief. It changes our amygdala activity and moves us to more of an experience state where we're more in our somatosensory cortex. And then the third bucket is — change the scene. Just getting away from all the stress triggers that we have in our office or in the city and being in an environment that we find calming. It might even just be a corner of the house. But implanting what I call safety signals — we're just these animals that are conditioned to signals whether we're aware of it or not. So having things like comforting pets, pictures, smells, music — why not? They help, they add up.

**Andrew Huberman:** I like the idea of having a small physical space — or a large physical space, but for most people a small predesignated physical space — that represents a safe zone, and populating that safe zone with things. As a visual neuroscientist originally, we know that photographs are extremely powerful cues for the memory system, especially actual physical photographs. There is some work showing that if people keep a photograph of something that draws positive memories, that photograph — kept with them — can actually be a positive cue for alleviating stress and enhancing mood. This is probably done less so nowadays because everyone keeps things on their phones, but it's worth considering.

## [00:15:37] Acute, Moderate & Chronic Stress, Breathing

**Andrew Huberman:** Stress — it's clear that there's short-term, medium-term, and long-term stress. You've studied all these different forms. If you would be so kind as to just give us an overview of the different forms of stress, how we can learn to recognize those, and then I'd love to transition from there into talking about some of the work that you've been doing on stress-related eating and stress as it relates to aging in particular.

**Elissa Epel:** When we think about stress we usually think about feeling stressed — reporting stress — and that's important. What our bodies are doing is also important, and it's not always related to what our minds are reporting. Measuring levels of the nervous system and how vigilant we are is another way that we can understand stress. That's particularly important because that's how stress gets under the skin. We might not be aware — we might report low stress — but we're still holding tension and being much more sympathetically dominated, meaning our body is vigilant and scanning for cues and we don't feel safe. So we're mobilizing a lot more energy than we need to. Stress is so expensive to the body. The stress response uses a tremendous amount of energy, ATP that's made by your mitochondria. If we have that kind of vigilant stress response on all day, we're just going to feel exhausted.

The pandemic's long shadow left many of us not good at turning the stress response off — and that's what we want to really focus on. We need to mount a big stress response to cope with things when we need extra energy, but then we can actually let our body relax and turn it off. That's where the rumination comes in. We want to catch ourselves rehearsing and reliving stress or worrying about the next thing, saying, "Right now, I'm safe." And the breathing strategies are the most direct and fast path to reducing stress in the body, period.

**Andrew Huberman:** Our colleague David Spiegel, associate chair of psychiatry at Stanford, has said it best: breathing is unique among the functions of the brain because it really originates as a brain function and then extends, of course, to the body as a mechanical function. And for that reason we have a kind of direct lever to the brain through breathing. And so whereas with heart rate we can't just decide to slow our heart rate, we can slow our breathing and that will directly impact our heart rate and our mind. Not all the time can we override a stress response with breathing, but we can certainly modulate it.

## [00:21:23] Stress Benefits, Aging & Cognition; Stress Challenge Response

**Andrew Huberman:** You distinguish between optimal aging, typical aging, and accelerated aging. What's interesting is that while toxic, chronically unmitigated stress clearly accelerates aging, it turns out that underexposure to stress leads to more rapid aging than ideal amounts of stress. In other words, no stress is not the answer — rather, having some stress is ideal if you want optimal aging. Could you explain the mechanisms behind that and tell us about your telomere work?

**Elissa Epel:** Having no stress means we're not really living — we're not engaging in the gifts of life, which inevitably have some challenge and risk. Let me give you an example. One study took elderly people who had retired, and society labels them as done with their meaningful work in life. This program brought them to work in schools and tutor young at-risk students. What happened is they went from feeling safe and under-stressed to feeling challenged but generative. They were feeling more purpose, like they were growing, their day had more meaning, they had more relationships and caring relationships with the students. The students had all sorts of issues — drugs, poverty — and so they felt the stress of that, but they also saw how much they could help. In the study, they took images of the hippocampus, and those who engaged in the program — particularly the men — actually had growth of their hippocampus during this program. So at any stage in life we can be growing and challenging ourselves even in our much later years and growing our brain.

**Andrew Huberman:** It's interesting because the hippocampus is so plastic, so amenable to the addition of new memories. The most striking study to me is the one by Rusty Gage at the Salk Institute in the early 2000s. They took terminally ill people who agreed to have their bodies injected with a dye that would label new neurons. After they died, their brains were processed, and what they discovered was that even in terminally ill, sometimes quite old individuals, those people were still generating new neurons, especially in the context of still trying to learn and acquire new information.

**Elissa Epel:** Dave Almeida measures daily stressful events in huge national populations and a small percentage of people report no stressors. What he found is that their level of cognitive health was significantly lower. You can imagine — the hippocampal neuroprogenitor cells are just not being stimulated. I wasn't aware of that result, so I appreciate you sharing it. I almost have to wonder if it's like exercise — everyone knows some exercise is better than no exercise for heart health and the brain, but no one thinks of challenging oneself cognitively or a little bit of acute stress as being a beneficial form of health practice. Perhaps we need to start thinking of it in those terms.

The telomere story connects to this. Telomeres are the caps at the ends of our chromosomes — they protect our DNA like the plastic tips on shoelaces. Each time a cell divides, telomeres shorten a little bit. When they get too short, cells can no longer divide and they age or die. We've found that chronic psychological stress — particularly the kind that involves feeling out of control and helpless — is associated with shorter telomeres and lower levels of telomerase, the enzyme that rebuilds telomeres. Caregivers, for instance, of chronically ill relatives show significantly shortened telomeres compared to controls. But here's the exciting part: the mindset with which you view that stress matters enormously. People who view their stressful caregiving as threatening versus those who view it as a meaningful challenge show different telomere lengths, even under the same objective stress load.

## [00:32:19] Tool: Shifting Stress to Challenge Response, "Stress Shields"

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there specific mental scripts that people can use to shift from a threat response to a challenge response? And are we all able to follow those scripts?

**Elissa Epel:** Yes, to some extent we control the script. We can use it to prepare ourselves going into a stressful situation and we can use it at any point during the stressor. Some of us are just wired to have a big threat response — maybe from epigenetics we've inherited, maybe from early trauma that has shaped us to have this exaggerated emotional response. And yes, we and others have found that trauma sensitizes our emotional stress response so that we are feeling more threatened. But that's okay because that's the part we can't control, and we just have to have a lot of self-compassion and awareness. But what happens next — that's when we can start to use those statements: self-comforting, self-compassion, distancing. There are all sorts of statements that allow us to recover more quickly.

I call them "stress shields." The whole trick here is that people need to find the strength statements that fit them — that feel right and that they believe. I list a bunch of options in chapter three of my book, which is called "Be the Lion Instead of the Gazelle." The lion and the gazelle are both experiencing high stress — the lion is chasing the gazelle — but the gazelle is having a total threat response because she might die, while the lion is having a challenge response because it's excited to get dinner. We get to shape which one we are. Some of those statements: remind yourself of past successes, remind yourself of someone you can call or text, remind yourself that this outcome is not going to affect your life in 10 years — that's a distancing kind of perspective-taking. Telling yourself "I got this," "I can do it," "I have what it takes" — those are all good shields.

Another set: some of us feel really stressed out by stress — once we feel our heart racing, that leads to "oh no, this is bad for me." Rather than getting stressed by stress, we actually want to remind ourselves that this stress response is empowering, it's going to help me cope, my body is excited, my body is doing just what it should right now. In studies by Wendy Mendes and others who do this reappraisal research, they've basically trained people to view stress as positive during stressful lab situations. People do better, they perform better, they feel more positive emotion, they problem-solve better, they recover more quickly.

## [00:37:40] Stress, Overeating, Craving & Opioid System

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love to talk about the relationship between stress and eating, and this is also a great opportunity to talk about the opioid system. Most people are familiar with the opioid epidemic — but what we're about to discuss is the fact that we have an endogenous opioid system within us, neurons and other cell types that can release substances into our brain and body that make us feel less pain and feel sedated at a healthy level. Food can activate this opioid system. What is the relationship between stress and eating?

**Elissa Epel:** Stress and eating is an interesting one. Most people when they feel stressed either eat more or less. You've told me you eat less. And that's consistent with your high sympathetic temperament — when you have a big stress response, your digestion is pretty much shut down. But that's not what most people complain about. Most people complain about overeating or binge eating when they're emotional or stressed. What that looks like is that the stress response is driving cravings, and also a kind of high insulin or insulin-resistant state. And what goes along with that is tending to be overweight or have obesity.

We've found that maybe 50% of people with obesity have the compulsive eating pattern. When they're stressed they feel like they can't control their eating, they don't get full, they think about food a lot. Stress exacerbates that tendency. And lean people — less than 20% — have that same compulsive tendency. But what the lean people also have is this tremendous dietary restraint, a control over their eating, so they don't overeat even though they're thinking about food a lot.

This makes it really hard to eat well, because when you're stressed you're craving the comfort food — high fat, high sugar, high salt, depending on your temperament. With repeated bouts of stress you're just going to be gaining weight, particularly in the intra-abdominal area. We've seen it cross-sectionally, in rat and mouse studies, and in human studies over about ten years I studied this. If you stress mice out and give them Oreos, the mice develop binge eating and a terrible metabolic health profile — metabolic syndrome. That's because if the body thinks we're under chronic stress, it's going to store stress fat or abdominal fat so it can mobilize that energy in a second.

The opioid system is certainly involved. In studies with lean people and people with obesity, my colleague Rajita Sinha has basically found that when you stress them out, people with obesity have a different reward response — the more insulin resistant they are, the more their reward center lights up during stress. And the naloxone-Wellbutrin combination — that's one of the very few drug combinations that has been used for binge eating, and early trials showed that it really does dampen the compulsive eating. But ultimately, improved nutrition is the only way to solve it as a public health problem. My colleague Robert Lustig has been the biggest proponent of helping people understand that the root of the problem is in processed food and sugar, and that drugs don't touch that — we just override the effects of any drugs with our diet.

## [00:48:55] Tools: Breaking Overeating Cycles, Mindfulness

**Andrew Huberman:** What can somebody do who finds themselves in that category — binge eating or heading towards binge eating, using food to comfort or alleviate stress?

**Elissa Epel:** In our weight loss trials and healthy mindful eating trials, we find that mindful eating is not going to cause a lot of weight loss by itself — but the people who benefit most from learning this kind of calm self-regulation, where you check in with your hunger, you slow down, you increase your awareness of your body — that interoceptive awareness skill is really critical for people with compulsive eating. In our trials, people with compulsive eating who get randomized to mindful eating do better in terms of their insulin resistance, their glucose, and their long-term weight loss. That's one good clue.

Another is that the positive stress pathway — high-intensity interval training, or other ways to increase beneficial stress in short-term bursts to metabolize stress in the body — can help with the cravings. What that would look like: if someone finds themselves reaching for snack food when stressed, using all three bins of tools — top-down strategies like mindfulness, changing the body through exercise or breathwork, and changing the scene. The craving is like a wave — it does peak and then come down and pass. You don't have to ride it to its full conclusion, eating everything in sight. If you can use a combination of techniques to ride that wave down, you can break the cycle.

## [00:54:44] Soda & Sugary Drinks

**Andrew Huberman:** Drinking sugar in the form of liquid is one of the worst things we can do in terms of our body's regulation of insulin and glucose — it's not just empty calories, it's a particularly fast and disruptive form of glucose delivery to the bloodstream.

**Elissa Epel:** Right. Soda is a fascinating public health case because it's so woven into the toxic food environment — it's just right there, easily accessible. And the thing about liquid sugar is that it doesn't trigger the same satiety signals as eating something solid, so people end up consuming enormous amounts of glucose without feeling full. The brain's reward circuitry gets hijacked by the combination of sugar and the carbonation and the flavor — it's a very engineered product. I think the most honest framing is that sugary beverages are a direct pathway to insulin dysregulation, particularly in people who are already stressed or insulin-resistant, because stress itself is already raising cortisol and cortisol is already raising blood glucose. So you're pouring liquid sugar on top of an already elevated glucose state. That's a compounding insult to metabolic health.

## [01:00:51] Smoking, Processed Food & Rebellion

**Andrew Huberman:** One of the most effective messaging systems in the battle against smoking was to get young people to stop not by telling them it was bad for them, but by showing them videos of rich executives cackling about how much money they were making off of others' health problems — making being a non-smoker anti-establishment. The same might work for soda and processed food.

**Elissa Epel:** Absolutely. It's like pulling the blinders off — letting people know that we're vulnerable to all the marketing and that there's real suppression of data behind a lot of it. And it's happening with eating disorders too. Eric Stice, who's at Stanford with you, has been doing work with Erica Garber on a dissonance-based intervention for eating disorders, and it's the same principle — by taking on this awareness that the culture is shaping unhealthy eating and that you are going to be the rebel, you're going to fight against it and take control. That gives people a sense of agency and motivation to change. And when people feel in control of their food choices for their own reasons, rather than being told what to do, that's when lasting behavioral change happens.

## [01:06:47] Tools: Mindfulness, Pregnancy & Metabolic Health

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love for you to share your findings around the study you did of pregnant women — how stress and pregnancy and different patterns of eating and physiological changes that people experience during pregnancy could be managed. I think they're relevant not just to people who are pregnant but to everybody.

**Elissa Epel:** With overweight and obesity, we know we can't just change calories — the next stressful event will come along and people will go back to what their brain is driving them to do, which is binge on comfort food. So we've done these interventions with men and women showing that we can help them regulate using mindful eating strategies. We wanted to do this with pregnant women because when you have excess weight and you're pregnant, you're really vulnerable to gaining excessive weight during pregnancy, which is not healthy for the mom or the offspring.

This study took us probably ten years total to get the grant and recruit groups of women who were pregnant at the same stage and give them this training in mindful eating, mindful nutrition, and stress reduction. My colleague Nicki Buss has been following the babies for almost ten years. First, we couldn't stop excess weight gain — about 60% of women in both the control group and the mindful group gained excess weight during pregnancy. So you'd stop there and say it failed. But there have been so many beautiful developments.

Within the first month of the intervention, all women got an oral glucose tolerance test. We found that the women in the training group had significantly improved insulin sensitivity — their bodies were metabolizing glucose more efficiently. That's a pretty striking result from two months of mindfulness training. When we followed the children, we found that the offspring of the mothers who had received the mindfulness training had healthier metabolic profiles in childhood. The mothers' reduced stress response during pregnancy seemed to have epigenetic effects on the developing fetus. And then looking at the mothers themselves at long-term follow-up — years later, the mindfulness group had lower levels of depression. So a two-month intervention during a critical period had effects that rippled forward for years in both the mothers and their children.

## [01:14:11] Body Scan & Cravings

**Andrew Huberman:** What is a body scan doing at a mechanistic level in terms of reducing cravings? Is craving a heightened sense of interoception or of exteroception?

**Elissa Epel:** That's a great question. Interoception is essentially the sensory awareness of our own internal organs and our own skin. A craving like seeing a donut in front of you — you're in almost complete exteroception, but you're tethered to it, like the external object is in control of you. It's hijacked your prefrontal cortex, it's hijacked everything. Then if you do a body scan, you're shifting more towards interoception — you're focusing on your skin, your heartbeat, your breath, all the internal signals. So I could see how that would draw your attention off the external stimulus and reduce the craving. It suggests that craving is actually a form where interoception is exquisitely locked to exteroception — the internal reward circuitry is firing in response to an external cue. By intentionally shifting attention inward with a body scan, you're essentially unhooking that lock. The craving doesn't disappear, but its grip weakens because your attentional resources are now directed elsewhere. This is a remarkable result — it is very hard to stop cravings. There are people who get deep brain stimulation for severe binge eating disorders. So the idea that a brief body scan can meaningfully reduce craving is extraordinary, and it points to just how powerful directed attention is as a tool.

## [01:17:28] Tool: Meditation & Aging; Meditation Retreats

**Andrew Huberman:** Have there been any long-term studies of stress intervention? What can we learn about the long-term outcomes of things like body scans, meditation, and breathwork?

**Elissa Epel:** There haven't been that many long-term studies of stress interventions. The meditation studies are probably the best example. There are studies that have followed people who have taken up meditation, or cross-sectional studies where you compare a long-term meditator to someone who's never meditated. We have found that there is slower biological aging. Other people have found that short-term meditation interventions dampen the inflammatory pathways of gene expression, and cross-sectionally Elizabeth Hoge and others have found longer telomeres in meditators versus controls. We haven't found telomere lengthening in our short-term meditation studies, but we do find boosts in telomerase activity — the enzyme that protects cell aging and slows cell aging, rebuilding the telomere. Those studies suggest that if someone were to continue meditating, they might keep up that slower rate of aging.

We did one study at a retreat center where Deepak Chopra leads this Transcendental Meditation retreat — people got a mantra and they were focusing for probably eight hours a day on different yoga, meditation, and reflective exercises. Half the group just walked around the resort and heard some boring health talks — that was our control group. The gene expression pathways were like night and day from day one to the last day. Our machine learning model was able to identify people over 90% of the time as being on day one versus day seven. We followed them about ten months later and found that the group who learned meditation still had lower depression, while the control group bounced right back up. People with early adversity benefited the most from the meditation condition.

## [01:23:35] Meditation, Psychedelics & Neuroplasticity

**Andrew Huberman:** What was the meditation condition — how long per day?

**Elissa Epel:** They learned Transcendental Meditation / primordial sound meditation, where you have focused attention on a word over and over. There's also more awareness of the body. They did it on and off during the day. One week total.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've never done one of these extended meditation retreats. Various people in my life have told me I needed to go do a silent meditation. I do a daily meditation practice, but it's relatively brief. I tend to focus more on deliberate cold exposure and breathwork and exercise and sunlight. Are all of these TM meditations silent?

**Elissa Epel:** Yes, they range from half a day to two weeks. The longest I've ever done is a two-week silent meditation retreat, and that was after ten years of doing yearly shorter retreats. I think it would be too hard and stressful if you haven't been able to work up to it. I recommend them — they're amazing ways to get to the mind and to calm the body in ways that you don't get from daily short belts of meditation. It's like a quantum shift in your level of stress. There's a parallel with psychedelics here — both seem to create this window of heightened neuroplasticity, where synaptic connections are more malleable. With psychedelics there's this long, perhaps weeks- or months-long tail of plasticity where most of the rewiring is happening. That's not unlike meditation where one bout might cause some adjustment, but repeated short meditations or longer retreats induce this big-time brain plasticity. The book "Altered Traits" covers this beautifully.

## [01:26:02] Mitochondrial Health, Stress & Mood

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm particularly intrigued by a graph in one of your papers — the Picard 2018 paper — describing the relationship between mitochondrial health and mood in the context of people with different mood tendencies. Could you describe that study?

**Elissa Epel:** We've done in-depth studies looking at people under a lot of daily demand — caregivers — and then at normal parents of neurotypical children who still have a lot of stress. We ask whether people under chronic stress have accelerated aging. We look at telomeres, epigenetics, mitochondrial health, and then what explains those who look really good — who look resilient and don't look vulnerable. Martin Picard, my colleague who has been obsessed with mitochondrial health, has developed a way to measure mitochondrial health in humans — we can measure a bunch of enzymes and adjust it for how many mitochondria we have, giving us a nice index from the blood.

In this study of young mothers who had either typical children or children with autism, the caregiving moms had significantly lower or dampened mitochondrial activity. What that means is they can't produce as much energy, so if they're feeling more exhausted from the chronic stress, we know why. Some of those low levels even looked like people with genetic reasons to have low mitochondrial activity.

But here's the beauty of the study: we then looked within their day at their mood and asked what about the caregivers who had really great mitochondrial enzymes — and thus should be making a lot of ATP. They had more positive emotions, both waking up and in the evening, but especially in the morning. There's this beautiful relationship between energy production at the cellular level and the experience of positivity and resilience in daily life. It points to the idea that when we're burned out from chronic stress, it's not just in our heads — it's literally in our cells, in our mitochondria. And it opens up the question: can we improve mitochondrial health through behavioral interventions, and would that then boost our mood and resilience?

## [01:29:49] Chronic Stress & Radical Acceptance, "Brick Wall"

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to drill into "radical acceptance" — a theme now in the formal psychology literature, associated with dialectical behavior therapy. I think a lot of people hear those words and think, "Oh, that means you just have to accept what is and deal with it." But what you're talking about is chronic stress of the sort where there is unlikely to be a simple solution — like we're just going to be grappling with this thing. Perhaps a very close relative dealing with a lifelong condition, or a situation we can't extract ourselves from. Radical acceptance of that fact can ratchet into an understanding of, "Okay, and yet there are tools that we can use to not just offset the negative health effects but maybe even thrive." What are the data around the practices that can help make that conversion possible?

**Elissa Epel:** It's not a quick answer. It's partly how we view life and our own purpose. Even just the idea that bad things shouldn't happen sets us up for a perpetual state of resistance. When we accept that life will always bring these challenges — that some walls are brick walls you cannot change — then we can drop the rope. The practice is: when you notice yourself pulling and straining against something immovable, ask yourself, "Is this a brick wall? Can I change this?" And if the answer is no, then the practice is to drop the rope. What does that feel like? It feels like relief, ultimately — though getting there can feel like grief, like loss of control. Because giving up control is deeply uncomfortable for humans, especially in a culture that prizes agency and willpower.

The data suggest that radical acceptance practiced regularly shifts us toward what we'd call psychological equanimity — a kind of stable, resilient baseline that doesn't swing as dramatically in response to stress. People who score high on measures of equanimity show flatter cortisol curves, less inflammatory gene expression, and — in our studies — even longer telomeres. The mechanism seems to be that not fighting reality conserves enormous psychobiological resources. All that energy that was going into resistance — into trying to will away a brick wall — can now go toward actual coping, toward relationships, toward meaning-making.

## [01:37:57] Tool: Control, Uncertainty

**Andrew Huberman:** This tendency for us to try and pull on brick walls — our colleague Dr. Paul Conti, a psychiatrist who wrote a book on trauma, talked about how the limbic system that engages these fight-or-flight responses has no sense of time, and that's why developmental scripts get reactivated. The limbic system and the stress system, when activated, distort our perception of time. This is what leads to what's sometimes called the repetition compulsion — people will place themselves into mildly to severely traumatic circumstances over and over again. The idea is that these circuits when activated engage entire cognitive scripts that make it very hard to escape, pulling you into a story that is exquisitely difficult to get away from.

**Elissa Epel:** That's so insightful. And it brings up the parallel with the control dimension of stress. We know from decades of research that perceived lack of control is one of the most potent stressors there is. People will endure a lot of pain or difficulty if they feel like they have some agency over it. The problem is that our stress systems evolved to try to maximize control, because historically unpredictability was often life-threatening. So we have this deeply wired drive to understand, predict, and control our environment — and in modern life, with all of its genuine uncertainty, that drive is constantly being frustrated.

The practice I've found most useful — both from the research and personally — is what I'd call "expanding the sphere of acceptance" while "clarifying the sphere of influence." You make a clear-eyed list: what actually can I affect here? And then you put your energy there. Everything outside that sphere, you practice — with real practice, it doesn't happen automatically — you practice releasing. It's not that you stop caring. Radical acceptance is not resignation. It's more like: I care deeply, and I'm choosing to direct my caring toward what I can actually change. That shift — from trying to control the uncontrollable to focusing your energy on your actual sphere of influence — is one of the most liberating and health-protective things a person can do under chronic stress.

Another dimension of this is uncertainty tolerance. Some people have extremely low tolerance for uncertainty — they'd rather have a bad certain outcome than an uncertain potentially good one. That intolerance of uncertainty is itself a major driver of anxiety and rumination, and it's trainable. Mindfulness practice, over time, increases people's ability to sit with "I don't know" — to hold uncertainty without it triggering the full alarm response.

## [01:45:25] Stress Management, "Skillful Surfing"

**Andrew Huberman:** About half of the messages we get related to stress and mind-body interventions relate to adopting this forward center of mass — stress can make us more resilient, the mindset matters. And then on the other hand there are the stress mitigation techniques that are more of a receptive mode, an awareness mode. Do you think it's fair for us to adopt a general framework — having both of these in one's kit of tools could be valuable?

**Elissa Epel:** Yes, I think having both is essential. I call the overall practice "skillful surfing." Waves of stress will come — that's non-negotiable. What we're developing is the skill to recognize the wave before it's already crashed over us, to decide in real time whether this is a wave we want to ride hard — meaning lean in, use the forward center of mass approach, activate the challenge response — or whether this is a wave we want to let pass, letting it wash through us without resistance. The skill is in the discernment.

The leaning-in approach — the challenge response, the "be the lion" mindset — is energetically expensive but offers tremendous returns when the situation actually calls for it. It's appropriate for acute, bounded, navigable challenges. The receptive mode — mindfulness, acceptance, equanimity — is less energetically demanding, and it's particularly suited for the chronic, non-negotiable stressors, the brick walls. The danger is when people apply the wrong tool. If you try to muscle your way through a brick wall, you exhaust yourself. If you sit in receptive mode when you actually need to act, you miss opportunities.

Both approaches, practiced regularly, also build something that might be the most important stress-resilience capacity of all — what researchers sometimes call "stress inoculation." Each time you successfully navigate a stress — whether through active engagement or through acceptance — you slightly raise your stress threshold. The next wave doesn't hit as hard. Your nervous system learns that stress is survivable, and over time, manageable. That's the cumulative gift of consistent practice: not the elimination of stress, but an expanding capacity to meet it.

## [01:50:25] Narrative, Purpose & Stress

**Andrew Huberman:** For people dealing with non-negotiable stressors — the kind that will go on for years — is spending some time creating a written or spoken narrative helpful? Are there data supporting the use of journaling?

**Elissa Epel:** Definitely. Creating a coherent narrative is critical to our ability to make sense of things, find meaning, find resolution, and have a social identity around our lived experience. In stress research, it's not what happens to us — it's how we're interpreting it and how we're responding to it. A narrative of purpose — filling in the blank about what's meaningful to you — is why we're different from the rats and monkeys we study. They have these amazing stress responses, we have them too, and we can't always control that. But we have the ability to do this projection to the future, to ask what is our purpose in life, to know that we are going to die and we can have some control over how we live and how we want to be remembered. That is beautiful. That helps us rise above just being animals in clothes.

For journaling specifically — and there's a body of research from James Pennebaker on expressive writing — writing about your deepest thoughts and feelings around a stressor, over a few sessions, produces measurable improvements in psychological and physical health. The coherence-building function of narrative is protective. It transforms raw, chaotic stress experience into something with shape, meaning, and edges you can work with. Even people under chronic, non-negotiable stress who can narrate it — this is what I'm going through, this is why it's hard, this is what it means to me, this is how I'm coping — show better biological outcomes than people who are equally stressed but can't make sense of it.

## [01:52:49] Breathwork, Wim Hof Method, Positivity & Cellular Aging
