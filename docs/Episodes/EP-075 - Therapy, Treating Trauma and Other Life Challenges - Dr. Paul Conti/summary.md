# Episode 75: Therapy, Treating Trauma & Other Life Challenges | Dr. Paul Conti

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Paul Conti — psychiatrist, trauma expert, and author — joins Andrew Huberman for a comprehensive discussion on what trauma is, how it changes the brain, and how to heal from it. They cover the full landscape of treatment tools: talk therapy, journaling, prescription medications, alcohol, cannabis, and the emerging clinical use of psychedelics and MDMA. The episode is equally valuable for people actively processing trauma, those seeking therapy, and anyone wanting to understand how maladaptive emotional patterns form and resolve.

## Guest: Dr. Paul Conti

**Dr. Paul Conti, M.D.** is a psychiatrist specializing in trauma, personality disorders, addiction, and complex psychiatric challenges. He received his M.D. from Stanford University School of Medicine and completed his residency (serving as chief resident) at Harvard Medical School. He is the founder and director of the **Pacific Premier Group** — a multidisciplinary clinical practice in Portland, Oregon and beyond. He is the author of *Trauma: The Invisible Epidemic* (2021).

## Key Takeaways

1. **Trauma is defined by functional brain change, not just negative experience.** Trauma is something that overwhelms coping skills and leaves the brain functioning differently — identifiable through shifts in mood, behavior, sleep, anxiety, and hypervigilance. Not every hard experience qualifies. [00:07:00](transcript.md#000700-defining-trauma)

2. **Guilt and shame are evolved mechanisms that become maladaptive.** The limbic system embeds negative experiences deeply for survival purposes. Shame is aroused affect — it arises without choice. In modern life these mechanisms prevent people from processing trauma by driving avoidance instead of confrontation. Vicarious experiences — such as prolonged news exposure — can also cause trauma-like changes in brain function. [00:14:05](transcript.md#001405-guilt--shame-origins-of-negative-emotions)

3. **The repetition compulsion is real and rooted in limbic logic.** People repeatedly recreate the emotional conditions of their trauma because the limbic system — which doesn't understand time — believes that solving a similar situation now will also resolve the original wound. A person doesn't have seven different abusive relationships; they have the same relationship seven times. [00:21:38](transcript.md#002138-repeating-trauma-the-repetition-compulsion)

4. **Trauma dominates the present by disguising itself as an attempt to control the future.** The energy spent trying to change the past — through avoidance, rumination, or repetition — prevents people from truly living in the present. The answer is to go look directly at the thing and put words to it. Putting words to trauma — even to a single trusted other — can produce immediate relief because other people not recoiling is itself deeply therapeutic. [00:28:23](transcript.md#002823-how-to-deal-with-trauma--negative-emotionsarousal)

5. **Negative fantasies serve punishment, avoidance, and illusory control — not resolution.** When people catastrophize or replay imagined terrible outcomes, three mechanisms are at work: self-punishment ("see, you deserve that"), distraction from deeper affect, and a false sense of control. Like opioid use, it soothes in the short term while worsening the long-term problem. These patterns do not change on their own without new perspective. [00:45:30](transcript.md#004530-internal-self-talk-punishing-narratives--negative-fantasies)

6. **Sublimation is functional but limiting.** Channeling trauma's arousal energy into productivity (working harder, caring for others) is a real adaptive strategy. But it keeps a person seeing through the lens of trauma and limits their full potential. Conti has never seen a single patient who addressed their trauma and became less functional — it is always either the same functionality with greater happiness, or expanded capability. [00:57:00](transcript.md#005700-sublimation-of-traumatic-experiences)

7. **Rapport is the most important factor in finding a good therapist.** Research consistently shows rapport — trust, attentiveness, genuine partnership — outranks any specific therapeutic modality. Good therapists are versatile and not rigidly pigeonholed in one approach. Word-of-mouth referrals are the most reliable signal. Therapy should be experienced as difficult, not always pleasant — the discomfort is the work. [01:02:34](transcript.md#010234-tool-finding-a-good-therapist)

8. **Medicines are tools, not fixes — and are vastly over-prescribed in the US.** The American healthcare system's throughput model leads to patients on seven medicines who aren't improving. Antidepressants are better understood as distress-tolerance enhancers. They can be used short-term during intensive therapy work. The US uses approximately five times more psychiatric medication than the Netherlands, which has a culture more rooted in personal responsibility. [01:19:00](transcript.md#011900-prescription-drugs--treating-trauma-antidepressants-treating-core-issues)

9. **All attention deficit is not ADHD.** Anxiety, depression, trauma, poor sleep, poor diet, and stress all impair attention. Stimulants prescribed for non-existent ADHD carry serious risks: impaired judgment, increased anxiety, more impulsivity, paranoia, and in extreme cases psychosis — often without the person realizing their behavior has changed. [01:32:18](transcript.md#013218-attention-deficit-hyperactivity-disorder-adhd--prescription-drugs)

10. **Alcohol is almost never a good coping tool; cannabis is more nuanced.** Conti states the number of times alcohol has been a good coping mechanism "approaches zero," with additional risk for genetic profiles (more prevalent in Northern European populations heading west) that produce euphoric responses to alcohol. Cannabis narrows attentional focus — useful for gating out intrusive thoughts around sleep, but at high distress it can narrow focus onto the negative thing. It is not a treatment for depression, anxiety, or trauma. [01:40:37](transcript.md#014037-alcohol-cannabis--positive--negative-effects)

11. **Psilocybin and LSD work by reducing cortical chatter and seating consciousness in deeper brain structures.** Less communication in the outer cortex allows people to access truth about their trauma without the guilt-shame-avoidance machinery. Consciousness is re-seated in areas like the insular cortex — the same areas that light up during spiritual ecstasy and genuine human connection. Hallucinations may carry symbolic value but are not the core mechanism of therapeutic benefit. [01:44:53](transcript.md#014453-psychedelics-psilocybin--lsd-therapeutic-uses-trauma-recovery)

12. **MDMA floods the brain with positive neurotransmitters, creating permissiveness for approaching trauma.** Unlike true psychedelics, MDMA doesn't re-seat consciousness in deeper brain areas — it reduces the reflexive guilt, shame, and hypervigilance that block processing. Clinical guidance during the session is essential to direct that state productively; without it, the permissive state may be wasted on pleasant but non-therapeutic tangents. [02:00:01](transcript.md#020001-mdma-therapeutic-uses)

## Tools & Protocols

### Journaling for Trauma Processing

- **What:** Writing about trauma, internal narratives, and repetitive thoughts as a self-directed therapeutic tool.
- **How:** Bring curiosity rather than automaticity. Ask: "Why am I thinking about this? When did this start? Why is this in me?" Use the journal at night, or carry it for unpredictable moments when difficult thoughts arise. Read what you write — seeing it externalized creates distance, integrates compassion and logic, and can produce genuine revelations. Do not try to parse out every moment of a traumatic event — acknowledge what happened and ask what it has done to you.
- **When:** Particularly useful for those without access to professional therapy; as a complement to therapy; in small bite-size pieces.
- **Ref:** [00:53:22](transcript.md#005322-tools-processing-trauma-on-your-own-journaling)

---

### Talking to a Trusted Other

- **What:** Verbally sharing trauma or difficult internal narratives with a trusted friend, family member, or clergy member.
- **How:** The act of putting words to experience — even with a non-professional — brings additional monitoring mechanisms online in the brain and creates the same external perspective as journaling. The person needs to be trustworthy and willing to listen without recoiling. Biting off small pieces is key — do not attempt to re-live the full trauma chronologically.
- **When:** For anyone without access to professional therapy. Warning sign requiring professional help: suicidal thoughts or thoughts of not wanting to be alive.
- **Ref:** [01:16:35](transcript.md#011635-self-talk--journaling-talking-to-trusted-individuals)

---

### The Observing Ego Practice

- **What:** Developing the ability to stop and observe what is going on inside oneself — to watch one's own thoughts rather than being immersed in them.
- **How:** When a thought or narrative is bouncing repetitively, shift from first-person immersion to a curious observer stance. Ask: "When did I start thinking this way? What does this trace to?" The goal is not to ruminate but to bring a fresh, de novo perspective. Writing or speaking accelerates this shift because language engages additional monitoring mechanisms in the brain.
- **When:** At any moment when repetitive negative thoughts arise — in the car, during a walk, before sleep.
- **Ref:** [00:37:17](transcript.md#003717-processing-trauma-do-you-always-need-a-therapist)

---

### Choosing and Evaluating a Therapist

- **What:** A framework for selecting a therapist and monitoring whether the work is effective.
- **How:** Prioritize rapport above all else — trust, attentiveness, genuine back-and-forth. Use word-of-mouth referrals when possible. Treat the first sessions as a job interview. Expect the work to be difficult, not always pleasant. A good therapist adapts their approach and is not rigidly pigeonholed. If you feel you're not being helped, discuss it — either the approach or frequency changes, or you find someone else. Watch for the pattern of being "happy to not feel helped" because avoidance is comfortable.
- **When:** Before entering therapy; after several sessions if progress feels stalled; whenever you suspect you might be using the structure of therapy to avoid the actual work.
- **Ref:** [01:02:34](transcript.md#010234-tool-finding-a-good-therapist)

---

### Therapy Frequency and Intensity

- **What:** Calibrating how often and how intensively to engage in therapy.
- **How:** Below once per week, sessions are largely spent catching up. Once a week for an hour is the practical minimum for forward progress. When there is something significant to process, turn up the intensity — more sessions per week, or concentrated multi-day intensive formats (e.g., 30 clinical hours over one week with five or six different clinicians) which produce disproportionately large gains relative to equivalent hours spread over time.
- **When:** Evaluate continuously. Crisis points (e.g., after a suicide attempt, repeated rehab stays) or a strong personal desire for change warrant maximally intensive formats. Stable maintenance phases can be less frequent.
- **Ref:** [01:07:20](transcript.md#010720-optimizing-the-therapy-process-frequency-intensity)

---

### Short-Term Therapeutic Use of Prescription Medications

- **What:** Using antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs like fluoxetine/Prozac) as distress-tolerance scaffolding during a defined period of therapeutic work, rather than as long-term fixes.
- **How:** Reframe antidepressants not as "cures for depression" but as tools that increase serotonin in circuits governing mood regulation, anxiety regulation, and distress tolerance. Work with a psychiatrist to define a time-limited use with a plan to taper off. Medicines should accompany, not substitute for, human-to-human therapy.
- **When:** When distress tolerance is the limiting factor preventing engagement in therapy; when depression or anxiety are severe enough to make processing very difficult.
- **Ref:** [01:28:35](transcript.md#012835-short-term-vs-long-term-use-of-prescription-drugs-antidepressants)

---

### Self-Care Foundations

- **What:** The non-negotiable behavioral baseline on which all other therapeutic work rests.
- **How:** Consistently audit the fundamentals: adequate sleep, nutritious diet, regular exercise, natural light exposure (especially morning sunlight), reducing negative social interactions, and avoiding persistently distressing circumstances. Recognize when skipping self-care is itself trauma-driven (e.g., the belief that suffering or pushing through without basics equals productivity or edge).
- **When:** Continuously — especially before adding more complex interventions. These must be addressed first.
- **Ref:** [02:15:09](transcript.md#021509-defining-taking-care-of-oneself)

---

### Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy (Clinical Setting Only)

- **What:** The emerging clinical use of psilocybin, LSD, and MDMA as catalysts for trauma processing in therapeutic settings.
- **How:** Currently available only through clinical trials (search clinicaltrials.gov). These compounds require professional clinical guidance before, during, and after sessions. Psilocybin/LSD reduce cortical chatter and seat consciousness in deeper brain structures where trauma can be seen without the guilt-shame overlay. MDMA floods the brain with dopamine and serotonin, creating permissiveness to approach difficult material without defensive reflexes. Clinical guidance during the session prevents aimless states and directs the experience therapeutically.
- **When:** Only in legal clinical trial settings with professional oversight. Not for recreational or unsupervised use.
- **Ref:** [01:44:53](transcript.md#014453-psychedelics-psilocybin--lsd-therapeutic-uses-trauma-recovery), [02:00:01](transcript.md#020001-mdma-therapeutic-uses)
