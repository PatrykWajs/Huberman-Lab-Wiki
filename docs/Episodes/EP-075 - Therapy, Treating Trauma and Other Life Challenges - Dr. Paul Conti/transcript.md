## [00:07:00] Defining Trauma

**Andrew Huberman:** Paul, thank you so much for being here today.

**Paul Conti:** Thank you so much for having me.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've been looking forward to this, and I've received a ton of questions about trauma, about therapy, about how to assess where one is in their own arc of problems and addressing familial issues and relationship issues and so forth. If we could just start off very basic and get everyone oriented — how should we define trauma? We all have hard experiences. Some of them we might ruminate on more than others, but what is trauma?

**Paul Conti:** To make the definition relevant, I think we have to look at trauma as not anything negative that happens to us, but something that overwhelms our coping skills and then leaves us different as we move forward. So it changes the way that our brains function, and then that change is evident in us as we move forward through life.

**Andrew Huberman:** So how do we know if we have trauma or not? I've heard that everyone has trauma — for instance, that if we are a child and we request love or attention from a parent, and they dismiss us, that's a microtrauma. Is that overstating the issue? Do we all have trauma? What are micro traumas, and what are macro traumas?

**Paul Conti:** I think traumas that we might categorize as disappointments, or things that are negative but not deeply impactful, I think that's not a helpful definition. The helpful definition is something that rises to the magnitude of really changing us — something we can see in how we behave, in mood, anxiety, behavior, sleep, and physical health. So we can identify it, and we can also see it in brain changes. The fact that we become more hypervigilant, and we can see that different parts of the brain are more active — that definition captures how trauma, if it rises to a certain level, leaves us different. I think that's the helpful definition because it's a clinical definition: it's changes in us as people, and we can map those changes to identifiable shifts in our brain function.

**Andrew Huberman:** So how do we know if we've been changed by something? I can think back to childhood events where some kid on the playground said something negative about me. Clearly my brain has been changed by that event in that I remember it, but how do we know if something has actually changed the way that we are? Because we don't know how we would be otherwise.

**Paul Conti:** That's difficult — it's doable, but it's difficult. Because if the trauma rises to the level of changing the functioning of our brains, then there's almost always a reflex of guilt and shame around the trauma that can lead us — and often leads us — to bury it, to avoid it. People tend to avoid looking at the change in them, which is exactly the opposite of what needs to be done. In a viral pandemic, we want to isolate, but with the trauma epidemic we need to communicate with other people. We need to put words to what's going on inside of us.

Very often a person knows what's happened — I've done about 20 years of clinical work focused on trauma, and a lot of the time the person knows but they're not admitting it to themselves because they're afraid. But if they start talking, they'll talk about the event or situation — it could be something acute or something chronic. And then they feel different afterwards.

To anchor this with an example from my own life: when I was much younger, in my early twenties, my younger brother took his life by suicide. The response of guilt, shame, and hiding all of it inside me was very dramatic — I wasn't acknowledging it, because I didn't know what to do and I felt guilty and ashamed. I was like 24 years old. My reflex toward the world — can I make my way, can I have a good life, can I be happy — all came through the lens of heightened anxiety, heightened vigilance, guilt, shame, and a sense of non-belonging. It was ultimately good people around me and my own realization that things were not going well that led me to get some help, to be able to talk about it, and to realize: I need to face these things inside of me.

## [00:14:05] Guilt & Shame, Origins of Negative Emotions

**Andrew Huberman:** Why do you think that when we experience trauma, guilt and shame surface? Everything you're telling me is that in the end, that's not adaptive. Why would we be built that way? And what is guilt, really? What is shame, really?

**Paul Conti:** I don't think anyone knows the answers for sure, but my read is that there's something adaptive that happened through evolution that now becomes maladaptive in the modern world. Through most of human development, people weren't living that long, and the idea was to survive and reproduce. Traumatic things that happened to us making sense to stay with us — if you ate a new food and got really sick, you better remember that. The traumatic things emblazoned in our brain are built to last.

And shame — I think it helps to talk about the limbic system, often called the emotion system. One aspect of it is affect, meaning states that are aroused in us without our choice. If we're walking down the road and someone jumps in front of us, there's a response of fear and anger — heart starts beating faster, more blood to the muscles. And shame is also an aroused affect. It can be raised in us without our choice, and it's very powerful — which makes it an extremely strong behavioral deterrent. So if someone in a tribe did something wrong, shame is a very powerful mechanism to control behavior and keep the group cohesive. Guilt is the next step — when shame gets related to self. These are such profound behavioral interventions and deterrents that you can see how evolutionarily they all make sense if we're fighting for survival and we might be an elder statesman if we make it to 20. But it doesn't make sense in a world where we live much longer and navigate in all sorts of different ways.

There's so much coming at us now that can be traumatizing. How many times have I written a prescription that says "no more news"? People are clicking and clicking, enthralled in a frightening way with the horrors in front of them. And vicarious experiences can cause trauma too. We know this is not theoretical — the changes in the brain can come from vicarious experiences, which is why people glued to the news and feeling like, "oh my goodness, what is happening?" can develop trauma-like changes in brain function.

I feel sure that the majority of addiction I treat arises, ultimately, from trauma — from trying to soothe something that's stuck inside that the person isn't letting outside because of guilt and shame, but which is running around in their head tormenting them. The opiates given after a minor surgery help the physical pain, but they're really helping the pain inside. And that very quickly turns into addiction.

## [00:21:38] Repeating Trauma, the Repetition Compulsion

**Andrew Huberman:** This brings up the Freudian concept of a repetition compulsion — the idea that instead of acknowledging trauma and trying to distance from it, there's a reflex of shame and guilt, and people then repeat behaviors that stuff the trauma away. Or, incredibly, they return over and over again to the kinds of emotional states or scenarios that look just like the trauma. Is the repetition compulsion a real thing? And why would we be wired that way?

**Paul Conti:** The first thing I would say is: strong yes, absolutely. We see that over and over, not necessarily in everyone, but in a lot of people who have suffered trauma. On the surface it seems like it makes no sense. But if we think about how our brains actually function — we're trained in Western society to think of ourselves as logical creatures, but the limbic system always trumps logic. If logic and emotion come head to head, emotion wins, if that emotion is powerful enough. And the limbic system does not care about the clock or the calendar.

So when people are repeating, what they're trying to do is make things right — with the idea that if we can repeat the situation and make it right, it will fix everything. Which makes perfect sense if we consider where that idea is coming from: the emotional part of the brain wants relief from the suffering of the trauma and does not understand time. If I can solve something now, I will also solve something in the past.

I can't tell you how many times I've sat with someone who says, "I know you just can't help me — my last seven relationships have been abusive." And I'll say: if you think about this, you haven't had seven different abusive relationships. You've had the same relationship seven times. That's always been the case. And now we start getting to what's really going on — the person needs to face what happened in that original abusive relationship. The person felt terrified while the abuse was going on, felt guilty, felt ashamed, felt like they brought it on themselves. And if that is bundled up inside someone — like a medical abscess inside the body, a walled-off infection — of course the limbic system is going to want to fix that by trying to recreate the situation and make it right this time.

And then the eighth relationship can be entirely different than the first seven. Things get better because we go to the trauma and unlock it. We bring it to the surface and take away its power.

## [00:28:23] How to Deal with Trauma & Negative Emotions/Arousal

**Andrew Huberman:** So much of our reflexive response to trauma is about some very deep attempt to change the past. And that really struck me — the number of behaviors and thoughts and avoidances that human beings engage in trying to change the past. It doesn't work, and yet it dominates the present. So what does one do? I'm feeling upset about something. My options seem to be healthy catharsis — I could tell the story, feel it. Or pack it down. Or tell the story, but then the telling itself re-evokes the arousal. How do we deal with that internal arousal in a moment?

**Paul Conti:** We so often try to change the trauma of the past in order to control the future, and what that really adds up to is the trauma of the past dominates our present. And we don't have to live that way. In the moment — if we need to fall asleep, we might try to put something out of mind, redirect a thought. There are short-term strategies that let us be functional. But the real answer is to go look directly at that thing, to explore the trauma.

We're so afraid, so often, of looking at the trauma that has changed us that we'll look anywhere but that. It's like it's hidden in a closet and we'll shine the light everywhere else but we're not going to open that door. People say, "If I talk about that I'm going to start crying and never stop, I'm going to fall apart." Which is never what happens. What ends up happening is when the person puts words to it — in writing, talking to a trusted other, or with a therapist — things start to change. Just the fact that you can talk about it and other people don't recoil from you — I can't tell you how many times someone has told me about their trauma with such an anxious look, as if I'm going to recoil from them. And then there's a sense of surprise when I don't.

The key shift is that when someone starts talking about their trauma, they can look at themselves from the outside — the way they would look at someone else. If you ask someone what they think of a child who was abused and manipulated by an adult, they feel compassion. But if it's the person themselves, it's guilt and shame. When the person starts looking at their own experience from that outside perspective, it starts to take the energy out of it. Who should feel guilty about that? And now the conceptions come together. They say: I was a terrified child. And they can come to a place of compassion. Crying is one of the best coping mechanisms we have — it doesn't hurt us, and it lets us grieve. But we can't grieve if there's guilt and shame blocking the grief. Once there's a clean slate, sadness replaces anxiety and anger, and the changes happen. It's remarkable how just getting it out there and having one hour of talking like that can leave a person feeling immensely better.

## [00:37:17] Processing Trauma, Do You Always Need a Therapist?

**Andrew Huberman:** What I'm hearing is: when traumas happen, what we need most is to confront them and the emotions around them directly. But instead, our system defaults to guilt and shame, trying to hide it. The whole system seems completely backwards. And it raises an important question: do we need to be in the presence of a therapist to do this work? Is there a benefit to taking a walk and thinking about these things, breaking down and crying if that's necessary? And how do we do it in a way that isn't retraumatizing?

**Paul Conti:** When things are bouncing around in our minds, often it's very nonproductive — it's the same thing over and over again, and that's not helpful. There's an idea sometimes called an "observing ego" — the ability to stop and look at what's going on inside ourselves. If we're just thinking about it in the same way we always think about it, all we're doing is reinforcing the trauma. But if we can distance enough to think: "Hmm, I'm interested in what's going on inside of me," then we can bring new thoughts.

I think of a certain person who loved music. At some point I learned she was taking long drives with the radio off. When I asked why, it turned out she was running over and over in her head: "I'm a loser, I'm a loser." She didn't want the music on because it would drown out what she felt she had to say to herself. And her ability to observe that — to think, "why am I doing this, when did this start?" — allowed us to eventually get to what needed to be talked about.

Speaking or writing brings additional mechanisms online in our brains — monitoring mechanisms. We think differently when we're using words, and we're better able to bring in that observing ego. So it can be very helpful to talk to a trusted friend, family member, or clergy member, or to write. These are things that can be done without expending any resources. And sometimes it's not necessary to have a formal therapist, though sometimes the symptoms are significant enough that we really do need to talk to someone professional. I've seen people who have had five residential stays for mental health or substance reasons, and no one ever took a trauma history. Then when you take a trauma history, you can see: that's obviously where this is all coming from.

## [00:45:30] Internal Self-talk, Punishing Narratives & Negative Fantasies

**Andrew Huberman:** Can people start to have negative fantasies? I'll give a little personal disclosure here. I've felt several times in my life that I will start to create a narrative about something that hasn't happened — about something terrible that somebody is going to do. I wonder why I'm doing this. One idea is that I was attempting to avoid thinking about other things — anger has a gravitational force to it. Another idea is that imagining worst outcomes makes reality seem better by contrast. And the third possibility is I have no idea why, but it seemed like a reflex. Why would somebody have a default narrative when driving or walking of imagining how terrible something is going to turn out?

**Paul Conti:** I think there are three factors there, and they're all bad. I would say: punishment, avoidance, and control. The trauma inside of us, that makes guilt and shame, so often leads to a desire to punish oneself. If we tell ourselves we're a loser or this awful thing is going to happen — part of what we're doing is saying to ourselves, "see, you deserve that." That's a very negative way that the brain tries to make us do better by hurting us more for things that we couldn't and shouldn't have been expected to control in the first place. The second factor is distraction — as you said, anger and negative fantasy can distract us from affect and emotion that could be much more painful. And the third is a sense of control — if you think ahead to something awful that you imagine is going to happen, maybe that will let you avoid it.

You can see how the brain is in a sense really confused. Part of it wants to punish, part of it doesn't want to think about the underlying issue at all, and part of it wants to make it better. And all of that resolves into negative fantasies of the future which may help us feel better about something in the present, but don't help us make anything better. This is where trauma and all the reflexive responses after trauma ultimately lead us. There is no natural end to any of this unless we look at it in a different way and bring new perspective. It doesn't change on its own.

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm struck by your statement that these thoughts or behaviors can make us feel better, but they don't actually make anything better. In that way, this mode of imagining terrible outcomes is like taking opioids — you feel better in the moment, but it probably makes things worse. So it just answers a lot of questions about what seems like a totally maladaptive wiring diagram.

## [00:51:10] Short-Term Coping Mechanisms vs. Long-Term Change

**Andrew Huberman:** We need to confront the thing, but we don't want to go into the repetition compulsion — it's a knife edge. Working with a very skilled clinician is the ideal circumstance. But for those who can't access support: you've talked about journaling as a useful tool. Could you highlight some of the other things people can do on their own?

**Paul Conti:** If I could add something first — we have these short-term coping mechanisms in us, and in a way it makes sense. If we find ourselves in terrible situations, a short-term coping mechanism can get us through them. In the modern world, whether it's food, drugs, sex, alcohol, or negative thoughts — this is short-term soothing. Even the negative thoughts. Anger is short-term soothing at the expense of long-term change. That's where addictive pathways can come into play. We're not adapted to this way of living — we haven't lived this way throughout 99.9% of human history. So I want to make a point of that: short-term soothing at the expense of long-term change.

## [00:53:22] Tools: Processing Trauma on Your Own, Journaling

**Paul Conti:** The hallmark of self-directed work has to be bringing new eyes to it — thinking about oneself with curiosity instead of automaticity or repetition. "Why am I thinking about this? When did this start? Why is this in me?" Whether it's words or writing, that's so important.

For journaling, it depends on the person. We don't want somebody carrying a journal all day if that creates a compulsion to write about everything. It might be good to write a little bit at night. Or for someone whose thoughts come in a strong and unpredictable way, having the journal handy makes sense. Because then putting words to it and being able to read those words — when people read what they've journaled, like "I thought again about how I'm a terrible person who can't have a good life, because I was in such a bad car accident" — actually seeing that written out gives enough distance that we can start to integrate both compassion and logic. "Okay, I feel a sense of compassion — what does this mean? What really happened here?" Those are the kinds of revelations we can have through the written or spoken word.

Our brains don't automatically say, "Hey, wait — I've been spinning wheels here for a long time. Was there another way to look at this?" We need something from outside, which can just be knowledge. That's part of why I wrote my book — the realization that some huge percentage of everything I'm treating is rooted in trauma and the opacity of trauma. We don't see that the depression, the panic attacks, the addiction, the maladaptive choices — all of it coming from trauma — because trauma hides itself in that opacity.

## [00:57:00] Sublimation of Traumatic Experiences

**Andrew Huberman:** Something came up in the course of your answer. Some people funnel their maladaptive thoughts or behaviors — the things that generate repetition of anger or arousal — into functionality in the world. For instance, in thinking about things that upset me in the past and imagining bad outcomes, there's a certain internal state of arousal. For many years I was able to use that to work an extra three hours a day, or to pack my schedule with work and social engagement, so I could show up as a very good brother to my sister. It was a transformation of something negative into a functionality in the world that was actually rewarding. And yet describing it, I can see how it would be wonderful to source from something else. But I can also imagine how terrified one would be of giving up that fuel.

**Paul Conti:** What you're describing maps to what's called sublimation — there's something negative inside of us, but we transfer that energy into something adaptive or positive. The anger becomes harder work, or being kinder to someone close. There's validity to that. But it can become self-justifying: "Look at what this is doing for me — I wouldn't work as hard without it." And then we start to become attached to the trauma.

Just because we can sublimate some of the negative affect from trauma into something productive doesn't mean that's best. We can get to our destination by a very circuitous route — we might waste an hour, but we get there. That doesn't mean it's optimal. And it doesn't look at all the negative — the wasted fuel, the wasted time. I have yet to see one person who has addressed their trauma and become less functional. It's always either they're just as functional but happier, or more functional — because the sublimation limits us. It limits our perspective to only what we can see and do through the lens of the trauma. And that is never better than the alternative.

## [01:02:34] Tool: Finding a Good Therapist

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's discuss how one could or should go about finding a really good therapist. Typically this is done by word of mouth. But what are some of the characteristics one should look for?

**Paul Conti:** There's a lot of data about this. If you look at what are the top 10 important factors to find in a therapist, the answer is essentially: repeat "rapport" ten times. That's the key. And if you think about that, it's pretty amazing — because therapeutic modalities can be so different. What that's telling us is something very obvious: rapport means somebody who is paying attention, trust, a back and forth. Even though I'm doing something difficult, I'm doing it with someone who's really helping me, someone who's in it with me, who's really paying attention and wants me to be better. That's indispensable. A therapist not making eye contact, or who insists you have to fit into the box of the way they do it — that is not going to be helpful.

Good therapists are not pigeonholed by a certain modality. They may come at the world largely through a psychodynamic or CBT or DBT lens — there are lots of different ways to do therapy — but really good, experienced therapists are practically shifting to what the person needs. If you have that versatility coming through the vehicle of rapport, you've got a winning combination.

Word of mouth is important — if someone you trust tells you this person is good, that already makes the probability quite high. And people should look at a therapist the way they'd look at anyone they're interviewing for a job. If you don't feel helped enough, talk to the therapist about it, because maybe that therapist isn't a match, or maybe they can change their approach, or increase the frequency. The key is to go in knowing: this isn't going to be easy. I've got to talk about difficult things. I want someone who's really looking at what's going on inside of me, even if it's not always pleasant and enjoyable — because it has to be hard sometimes.

## [01:07:20] Optimizing the Therapy Process, Frequency, Intensity

**Andrew Huberman:** Maybe we could drill a little deeper into the mechanics of therapy. I got hundreds of responses from the audience asking: how should I show up to therapy? Should people take a five-minute meditative drop-in before, or just show up cold? During therapy, is it good to take notes or not? And post-therapy, how should they process the information?

**Paul Conti:** I think it varies so much by person. For how to show up — the answer would be: whatever lets you be fully present when you're in therapy. For some people that's showing up early, sitting, meditating a little. For others, they just walk into the room and they're present. And the same thing applies on the other end. There are people who are really well served by going for a walk afterwards, or sitting quietly and putting things in order — otherwise they lose the ahas. Maybe that involves taking notes during therapy. For other people, they need to do the exact opposite: leave, not think about it at all, then reflect later. Being present when it's happening, then being able to consolidate and retain what's been gained — that's most important.

As for frequency — if it's less than once a week, it's hard to retain progress. We spend a lot of time catching up. Once a week for an hour is really the minimum if we're going to get somewhere, not just maintain something. But more intensive work is not linear — it's exponential. We've found tremendous benefit from doing 30 clinical hours with someone over the course of a week, with five or six different clinicians. That kind of intensity is worth probably twice as many hours done in a more spread-out way, because it's in the person in an active way.

What brings someone to that level of intensive work? It's usually a person who is really distressed by something, or someone who comes to the realization: "I can't take this anymore, I'm sick of the cyclical depression, I need help." Sometimes it's a crisis point — after a suicide attempt, or returning to rehab for the third or fourth time. And sometimes it's a person realizing they just want to understand themselves better.

## [01:14:51] Tool: Self-Awareness of Therapy Needs, Mismatch of Needs

**Paul Conti:** I think a lot of times it would be the therapist to say that more intensive work could make a difference. But the person also needs to take ownership of their own therapy and say: if I don't feel helped enough, I have to think about that and talk to the therapist about it — maybe that therapist isn't a match, or maybe the approach or frequency needs to change. People can get into a rhythm of therapy where it's really not helping them, but they either feel nihilistic about it — "oh, I'm no better and I'm going to therapy" — or there's a sense of checking a box. And the part of the brain controlled by guilt, shame, and avoidance thinks that's a great idea. Am I happy that I'm not feeling helped, because I don't have to face this thing I don't want to face? The answer comes down to observing ourselves and taking ownership of what's going on in us, and then that commitment to self-care to say: I need to go change this.

## [01:16:35] Self-talk & Journaling, Talking to Trusted Individuals

**Andrew Huberman:** And for those who maybe don't have the means or insurance for weekly therapy — could one take an entire day to do a lot of journaling, thinking, and walking? A self-generated intensive?

**Paul Conti:** There could be utility to that, but it depends on the person — there could also be something negative about it if the person isn't ready. We don't come directly at the trauma immediately, at least most of the time. We don't need to explore every moment of the trauma in depth. More often the person has to acknowledge what happened — "I was sexually abused" — and then ask: what has that done to me? That doesn't mean parsing out every moment of the terror. That can lead people to a worse place.

I think the idea is biting off small pieces — writing, talking — and importantly, communicating with another trusted person. If there's not someone in one's personal life, there are clergy members, even if one isn't affiliated with an organized religion. You could probably find people who want to help. If a person is having suicidal thoughts, or thoughts of not wanting to be alive — those are warning signs for really getting help. Don't try to do that on your own. Go find a resource.

## [01:19:00] Prescription Drugs & Treating Trauma, Antidepressants, Treating Core Issues

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to talk a little bit about chemistry — drugs. So maybe first we could talk about prescription drugs. How does one decide when medication is useful?

**Paul Conti:** There aren't really tests for these things — blood tests for serotonin metabolites, by the time what we're talking about has been metabolized significantly and left the brain, we really don't learn much from that. I think we tend to over-utilize medicines in this country because we have a healthcare system that's so based on throughput — we want to polish the hood when there's a problem in the engine. We overutilize medicines often as an end point. "We're going to make that person's depression better with an antidepressant." Well, maybe — but most of the time for depression to really get better and stay better, they need to unravel what's driving it.

The first assessment step is: is there a diagnosis that warrants medicine? Bipolar disorder, OCD, ADD — these are diagnoses where we understand more about what's going on in the brain and how medicines can treat or stabilize them. It doesn't mean the medicine substitutes for therapy, but sometimes medicine and therapy can go hand in hand.

And here's where I think things get more interesting — using medicines to help the person engage in therapy as productively as possible. Antidepressants: the vast majority of people helped by them don't have clinically severe depression. Those medicines create more distress tolerance in us. If you're going to do something difficult — bringing trauma to the surface, processing, making life change — if we can create more distress tolerance in a person, that can be immensely helpful.

I've seen one person on seven medicines to treat seven different symptoms, with side effects from all seven — maybe two of them to treat the side effects from the other five. If you really get at what's going on, they might end up on two medicines and doing much better. I think medicines get overused in large part for systemic reasons. A healthcare system with 15-minute psychiatry visits spaced weeks apart — I don't understand how that goes well the vast majority of the time. Think about how we use approximately five times as much medicine as the Dutch population. They have a cultural system more rooted in taking responsibility for oneself.

## [01:28:35] Short-term vs. Long-Term Use of Prescription Drugs, Antidepressants

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to pick up on one theme: the short-term versus long-term use of these drugs. Is there anything to say that someone couldn't take a properly prescribed medication for the first three months of therapy — just for higher distress tolerance during that work — and then come off it? Does life have to follow the binary model of "on forever or not at all"?

**Paul Conti:** Absolutely yes to short-term use. In American medicine we are so much better at starting medicines than we are at taking them away, and part of that is driven by such a strong presence of the pharmaceutical industry. If we look at medicines like Prozac as tools — they seem to make there be more serotonin in certain circuits important for mood regulation, anxiety regulation, and distress tolerance. We can deploy them sometimes for the longer term because sometimes that's necessary. But absolutely for the shorter term too. When we think about them that way — as tools, not fixes — we don't see them as substitutes for the human-to-human work that needs to be done.

## [01:32:18] Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) & Prescription Drugs

**Andrew Huberman:** ADHD is the topic that comes up most in audience questions. A huge number of students and working professionals are taking Ritalin, Adderall, Modafinil, Vyvanse, because they struggle to focus without it, or simply like how well they can focus when they do take it. Should we be concerned about this?

**Paul Conti:** Medication for ADHD can be extremely effective, and the studies show us that — if there is actually ADD, then medication is very, very helpful. But all attention deficit is not Attention Deficit Disorder. Attention deficit can come from many places: anxiety, depression, poor sleep, poor diet, stress, trauma. There's a "rock in the shoe" analogy — if someone comes in and every time they step down the rock hurts and they can't maintain attention, the answer isn't ADD medication. The answer is to remove the rock. But physicians are so programmed to think about medicines that they see ADD and reach for Adderall.

When people have ADD, they generally tolerate stimulants very well, without the other problems that can come of stimulants. But when people are treated for ADD and they don't have ADD — sometimes they know they don't have ADD, but the stimulants make them function better, so they get them anyway — that's not a good thing to do. Stimulants when not needed over time do affect physical function, judgment, and vigilance. They can affect the vigilance inside of us. We see the same thing with bipolar disorder — true bipolar disorder is extremely important to treat with medicines effectively, but how many people are diagnosed with bipolar disorder who absolutely don't have it?

## [01:37:31] Negative Effects of ADHD Prescription Drugs

**Andrew Huberman:** What constitutes negative effects of stimulants? I've heard that upward of 75% of college students use these drugs semi-regularly, off-prescription, to study.

**Paul Conti:** The touchstone running through our conversation is prioritizing short-term benefit over solving a long-term problem. Short-term use of stimulants: sure, people are more alert, can stay awake more, study more intensely and longer. But there's so much risk to that. How many times have I seen someone who was "just doing that to study" and now they're addicted to the amphetamines, and their behavior has changed and they don't know it. We see judgment impairment, heightened levels of anxiety, more impulsivity in decision-making, and sometimes even frank psychosis — which is not common, but it does happen.

The same principle applies: it changes our brains and we don't know it. The same often happens with amphetamines used inappropriately. We don't realize that we're a little more impulsive in our decision-making, a little less trusting. These are significant negative things that, if we're not aware of them, the person will just say: "Oh, I'm just using it to study, to work more." That's not without a high level of risk.

## [01:40:37] Alcohol, Cannabis — Positive & Negative Effects

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on cannabis for anxiety management, depression, and maybe even ADHD?

**Paul Conti:** If I could make an alcohol comment first — the number of times I've seen alcohol having been a good idea for coping with something approaches zero. The alcohol for coping is just never good. And there's an additional risk factor: certain genetic profiles cause people to respond very strongly to alcohol, with not just a little short-term relief but a euphoric response. Those genetics seem to be more prevalent in Northern European populations heading west. And there's a tremendous risk when a person responds so strongly to alcohol or habituates coping to alcohol.

Cannabis is a little bit of a different story. From my observation, what it seems to do is narrow our attentional perspective — that's why people want to use it before watching a movie with friends. Our cognitive spectrum narrows and instead of worrying about that thing at work or that relationship issue, one can just be present. It gates out other attentional intrusions. In some ways I've absolutely seen it be helpful — around sleep, for instance, because a person can gate out intrusive thoughts and relax. But at higher levels of distress and tension, cannabis can narrow the focus of cognition to the thing that is negative. The idea that it's a treatment for depression, anxiety, or trauma is not true. Can it be helpful under certain circumstances? Yes. Can it also be harmful? Yes. Do I think it's safer than alcohol? Yes. Does that mean it's uniformly safe? No. We want to be respectful of anything that can change how our brain is working — and that certainly includes cannabis.

## [01:44:53] Psychedelics: Psilocybin & LSD, Therapeutic Uses, Trauma Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love to talk about psychedelics — these are controlled substances illegal to possess, sell, or use in most of the country, though there are areas where they're decriminalized. Whereas five years ago I was afraid to say the word "psychedelics" in any public venue, there are now laboratories at Stanford working on ketamine, psilocybin, MDMA — there's terrific work at Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine in Matthew Johnson's Lab. So I'd love to approach this from a place of true exploration, in the context of legal clinical settings. What are your thoughts on psilocybin and LSD for therapeutic potential?

**Paul Conti:** If we look at the true psychedelics — psilocybin and LSD, as distinct from ketamine and MDMA — the reason they've gained so much momentum is because the data coming from academic centers is so powerfully positive. And the trials are bearing that out.

My read of the neuroscience is this: what we see is less communication, or less chatter, in the outer parts of the brain — the outer parts of the cortex. As human beings we tend to glorify those parts because that's where language is, vision, executive function — so much of what helps us make our way in the world. But I believe that we get lost in living in the cortex. So much of what's going on there is about survival. And then we're thinking all the time: "What would I do if there were a war, if the economy collapses, if someone gets sick?" — all future projection coming from a place of fear.

When psychedelics take the neurotransmission out of those places and seat consciousness in parts like the insular cortex — the middle parts of the brain — I believe that's where our true humanness really lies. That's where people can see with clarity: "That trauma — that thing is not my fault." They feel a sense of compassion for themselves. They release themselves from guilt. It can do what we try to get at in good therapy, but it can really catalyze that — putting a person in the part of the brain that can see things for what they are, without all that cortical chatter.

I think the medicinal value of psychedelics is in seating a person in the deeper part of the brain where they can really find truth. We're going to see more and more of the value of that, and how what psychedelics do can become a heuristic for understanding how our brains really function and what parts really matter to our experience of being human. Those deep brain areas and the insular cortex — they light up during spiritual ecstasy and during genuine connection with another person. We've been attracted to studying the outer parts of the brain partly because they're easier to study, but I think that has been misguided. And we're learning, through these novel modalities.

## [01:54:32] Sentience, Language, Animals

**Andrew Huberman:** I also wonder whether other animals experience life more from that orientation — from that middle brain structure — with less chatter. Being in the presence of animals that seem to just be present in their immediate environment, not too much anticipation.

**Paul Conti:** Through sentience — that's exactly what you're talking about. Sentience is extremely important. If we're going to overvalue language, then I think we undervalue sentience. Which is why we tend to undervalue animals and their suffering. We say, "Well, they're not saying anything about it, they're not writing about it, so it's easy to ignore." And think about the hubris of that — because we can think and talk and write, we must be feeling more than species that don't do that. I think we're going to understand more about sentience in other species, and that it is at the core of existence. My hope would be that we value humans and animals more through the evolution of that understanding.

## [01:55:48] Psychedelic Hallucinations, Trauma Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** Nothing in your answer about psilocybin and LSD focused on hallucinations per se. Do you think the hallucinations are valuable for anything?

**Paul Conti:** I think we're really getting into the philosophical, the ontological. I don't claim to know the answer. I think at times the hallucinations have a metaphorical or symbolic way of being helpful — people come to understand things they hold dear and true after the experience, often through the lens of the hallucinations. Are the hallucinations necessary? Are they sometimes important, sometimes not? We don't understand that, and we want to be respectful of the mystery of that.

What I think is fascinating is what we see with psychedelic medicines that's so different from substances of abuse. With substances of abuse, a person has experiences and conceptions of self that without the substance they know are wrong — "liquid courage." But with psychedelic medicines, people are having experiences so delinked from normal reality, and then when they come back to normal cognition, they realize: "Wow, what I experienced is true." People say that all the time, which tells me something different is going on. People will understand that the trauma wasn't their fault, that they're not less than because of their sexuality or gender identity. They feel differently about themselves, guilt and shame are impacted. These are likely powerful anti-trauma mechanisms, used clinically in the right hands.

## [02:07:28] Language, Processing Trauma, Social Media, Societal Divisions

**Andrew Huberman:** In your book you talk about how we need to be careful about the use of language around trauma. At one extreme, we hear that your brain and body hear every word you say. On the other hand, I can say "I love you" with a tone of hatred and "I hate you" with a tone of love. How should we think about language in parsing trauma? And you give cautionary notes about talking about depression, trauma, and PTSD in terms that might diminish their real severity.

**Paul Conti:** Language is wonderful, but it leads us astray often too. Someone says a word — does that person know what that word means? What nuance are they taking from it? We have to be careful about what we're saying and communicating. There's a phenomenon now where people are trying to control language too much — you can't say anything someone else might find hurtful, or you have to refer to people in ways that might be psychologically or clinically unhelpful. I think the over-control of language is not good. But the specificity of language — what are we trying to say, how are we defining it? The word "trauma" — it doesn't just mean anything kind of negative, because then it dilutes down to meaning nothing. It also doesn't just mean injury and combat. I think anchoring it to something that rises to the magnitude of overwhelming our coping skills and changing us — then at least I can define it that way and communicate it and we can understand each other.

Another aspect of language: we're so much less distanced from each other through social media. Social media can do very good things, but it can also be used to harm people from a distance. How much hatefulness is there out there, I think coming from anger and frustration in people, back to trauma — where people just want to be angry. And the words people use are sometimes so awful that someone reading that, if they're in the demographic being targeted, feels beleaguered and vulnerable. There was some civility and decorum in our world not that long ago. I remember a time in political discourse when people were civil to one another. Now there's an acceptance of things that are bombastic — it's a circus side show of people being just angry and aggressive, not really linked to anything. Other people's anger attaches to it, and it's not about what it's alleged to be about, but about aligning with the anger.

Should it really be okay that people are communicating in ways that would get them suspended from middle school? Or do we need to take a stand for rational use of language? I don't want my use of language to be over-controlled by someone who thinks they know better than everyone else. But I also don't want language so angry and aggressive that it perpetuates and spreads vulnerability, and facilitates trauma. I feel that a lot — I see things play out and I think: we're somehow okay with this. It doesn't matter which side of the political spectrum it's coming from. And I think that's an indicator that what we're doing is really hurtful to us. People become more angry, more beleaguered, there are more divisions between us.

## [02:15:09] Defining "Taking Care of Oneself"

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to talk about the concept of taking care of oneself. This comes up in your book, and to me it's a deep and powerful concept. **Paul Conti:** I see a very fascinating dichotomy here — how complex our brains and psyches are, and yet psychological concepts consistent with health are often very simple.
