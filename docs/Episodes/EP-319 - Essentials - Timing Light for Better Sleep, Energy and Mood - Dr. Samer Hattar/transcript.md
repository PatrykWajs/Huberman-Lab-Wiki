## Light, Circadian Clock vs Solar Day, Sleep-Wake Cycle

**Andrew Huberman:** You are best known in scientific circles for your work on how light impacts mood, learning, feeding, hunger, sleep, and these sorts of topics. So maybe you could just ease us into what the relationship is between light and these things like mood and hunger, etc.

**Samer Hattar:** Sure. You do appreciate the effect of light for vision. When you wake up in a beautiful area, a beautiful ocean, light is essential — the sunrise, the sunset. That's your conscious perception of light. But light has a completely different aspect that is independent of conscious vision, and that's how it regulates many important functions in your body. I think the best-studied and best-known is your circadian clock. The word circadian comes from the word "circa," which means approximate, and "dian" from day. So it's an approximate day. Why is it an approximate day? Because if I put you or any other human being who has a normal circadian clock in constant conditions — with no information about feeding time, sleep time, or what time it is outside — you still have a daily rhythm, but it's not exactly 24 hours. It will shift out of the solar day because it's not exactly 24 hours, and hence the name circadian.

**Andrew Huberman:** How does that rhythm show up in the tissues of our body?

**Samer Hattar:** It shows up at every level we have studied. It shows up at the level of the cell, at the level of the tissue, and at your behavior. The most obvious for you is your sleep-wake cycle. You sleep and you're awake in 24-hour rhythms. The period length of the sleep rhythm on average is 24.2 hours. So you'll be drifting 0.2 hours every day out of the solar day if you don't get the sunlight. The sunlight adjusts that approximate day to an exact day. So now your behavior is adjusted to the light-dark environment, or the solar day. It's part of the brain that is not consciously driven. You do not know when it happens or when it doesn't happen. And that's what we'll get into when I tell you why light affects your mood and why sometimes people don't know how to deal with light to improve their mood, for example.

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the relevance? Why should we care about that short difference?

**Samer Hattar:** So let's do the math. If you shift out 0.2 hours a day, in 5 days you're shifting out 1 hour. So you're literally 1 hour off in your social behavior in 5 days. In 10 days you're 2 hours off. And if you're an organism that is living in the wild, shifting out of the right phase of the cycle, you could either miss food or you could become food. So it's really essential for survival. I think it's one of the strongest aspects of survival for animals to have the anticipation and the adjustment to the solar cycle.

## Eyes, Photoreceptors & Light Entrainment, Blindness, Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the machinery that allows that to happen and how does that machinery work?

**Samer Hattar:** We knew that in mammals, including humans, the eyes are required for this function. If humans are born without eyes or the optic nerves are damaged, humans are not able to adjust to the solar cycle. So we know that the eyes are required. In the human retinas there are two types of photoreceptors. They are called rods and cones because of their shapes. These rods and cones simply take the photon energy — which light is made of — and change it into an electrical signal that allows us to build the image of the environment in our cortices. However, people have found — including me, with the work of David Buren and Ignacio Provencio — that there is a subset of ganglion cells. The ganglion cells are the cells that leave the retina; their axons leave the retina and project to the brain. These were thought to only relay rod and cone information from the light environment to the brain. We found that a small subset of these ganglion cells are themselves photoreceptors that were completely missed in the retina. And these are the photoreceptors that relay the light environment subconsciously to the areas in the brain that house the circadian clock or the circadian pacemaker, which adjusts all the clocks in our bodies to the central brain clock and allows them to entrain to the 24-hour light-dark cycle.

**Andrew Huberman:** So these are cells that connect the eye to the brain that behave like photoreceptors essentially. I think it's worth mentioning now that people who are pattern-vision blind — people who cannot see but have eyes — many of them still have these cells, these melanopsin intrinsically photosensitive cells, and can essentially match or entrain to the light-dark cycle.

**Samer Hattar:** In fact they possibly have no problems in circadian photo entrainment. They'll have enormous sleep-wake cycle issues, but they are totally image-blind. And what's really interesting — I heard this story from Chuck Czeisler so I'll give him credit — some of these people who are image-blind usually get dry eyes and doctors used to think, since they are image-blind and getting dry eyes, why not just remove their eyes since they're not using them anymore? And the minute they would remove their eyes, they start having cyclical sleep problems, indicating that now they are not entraining to the light-dark cycle and are having cyclical jet lags when their clock shifts through the light-dark cycle.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's really interesting. I hear from a number of blind people that a lot of them have issues with sleep. I think in part because they don't realize that they too need to see light at particular times of day or night in order to match their schedule.

**Samer Hattar:** Absolutely.

## Morning Light, Artificial Lights, Tool: Morning Sunlight Exposure

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the proper way to interact with light in the first part of the day?

**Samer Hattar:** Honestly, I think the easiest thing is waking up and getting as much light as you can into your eyes. Your system is primed. If you're entrained, it's primed to get light. The sun should be out. Even when it's cloudy, you're going to get enough intensity to help you adjust your cycle to the daylight cycle.

**Andrew Huberman:** These are general rules of thumb, but how long do you recommend people go outside?

**Samer Hattar:** So if you do it daily, I would say 15 minutes. If you don't do it daily, you may want to increase it. You do it more, it doesn't hurt. And if you're sensitive, you don't even have to go in the sun — you could be in the shade. There's going to be so many photons out there in the shade. It's going to be perfect.

**Andrew Huberman:** Okay. And if for some reason one finds themselves very far north and it's very dense cloud cover, how long and at what point should somebody consider using an artificial light source to mimic the sunlight?

**Samer Hattar:** Honestly, this is where we don't have a lot of information still, because this is where we'll discuss in more detail — if you put humans in artificial conditions, the circadian system is very sensitive to light. But in reality, in the real environment, light is also affecting other aspects that are independent of the setting of the circadian pacemaker. We call those the direct effects of light on mood, for example. That is very hard to figure out what intensity you need to use, and we haven't done enough experiments because the system has been discovered just recently. But if you're that far north and you're in the winter and you want to make sure you don't go without — I would suggest using light boxes personally.

**Andrew Huberman:** Okay. So I think we've nailed down that first part of the day. Basically, it's get 10 to 30 minutes depending on how bright it is and try to do that as often as possible to give the system a regular signal.

**Samer Hattar:** Daily is the best. This system is really about multiple days — even for the effect on depression. So you don't have to worry if you missed it one day. Stay longer if you want, but if you're in a hurry and you want to do other stuff, that's a great recommendation.

**Samer Hattar:** Absolutely.

**Andrew Huberman:** Simply by staying in, being on your phone too much, not getting the sunlight.

**Samer Hattar:** And you saw this during the pandemic. A lot of people mentioned that their sleep-wake cycles suffered a lot. Because if you're not going out and you're staying at home and you don't have big windows and you're waking up late — and then you're using very bright light till late at night — your body is going to shift. Now your day is going to start, instead of when the sun comes up at 6:00 in the morning, your day is going to start at 11:00 in the morning. That's what your body's going to think is the beginning of the day. So then you're not going to be able to sleep at 10 o'clock at night because now that's really, for your body, a completely different timing.

## Chronotypes, Intrinsic Circadian Rhythms

**Andrew Huberman:** There is this idea of chronotypes — that we each intrinsically have a best rhythm of either being a morning person or a night owl or more of a standard kind of person, to bed around 10:30, up around 7. And I think there are now good data, correct me if I'm wrong, from the National Institutes of Mental Health and elsewhere showing that the more we deviate from that intrinsic rhythm, the more mental health issues and physical health issues start to crop up.

**Samer Hattar:** So there is great data on this and there's a couple of things that complicate this. The first is that people who are usually late — who go to sleep late and wake up late — they have an overwhelmingly higher level of depression. People who go to sleep early and wake up early do better in life, they perform better. But the question is: is that intrinsic to the system, or is that society? Because society generally starts things early. That's a hard question. We discriminate against late risers. And I'm not so sure that the circadian system is that variable in the human population. Clearly there are maybe some genetic factors that make a small percentage different, like everything with a bell-shaped curve, but I think most of the time the light environment may play a role. Once you get into a rhythm it's hard to break out of it, because if you start sleeping late and waking up late you're not getting the morning sunlight, and so you're just going to stay late.

**Andrew Huberman:** It seems to me that the only way to really know if you're meant to be an early bird or a late person or somewhere in between is to get morning sunlight and figure out whether or not that makes you feel better.

**Andrew Huberman:** So, what should people do in the afternoon and evening time in terms of their light-viewing behavior?

**Samer Hattar:** I mean, the best thing to do is to let the natural light creep into darkness — that would be the best, but clearly that would be inefficient. You want to go home, you want to read, you want to talk to your kids, talk to your family. So I think it's nice to extend the day. I don't think that's wrong, if you somehow can block that light from affecting your circadian clock.

**Samer Hattar:** I am an extreme, but I measured it for myself and I asked Reie, my wife, if she's okay with it. She also liked the dimness. Both of us can see well in dim conditions. But I think you have to measure it for yourself. It's a very simple experiment — just try to dim the light as much as you can. I call it the minimum amount of light you require to see comfortably. You can use red light that is very dim if you want to keep the room for sleeping. Red light that is very dim has a very small effect on the circadian clock, and below 10 lux of red light literally doesn't affect sleep at all. So there are ways to do it.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've seen you check your phone after dark — once or twice. And you did it by sort of pointing your phone away from you, right? It actually makes sense that if you shine a flashlight in your eye it's much brighter than if you shine it to the side. **Samer Hattar:** And even when I check sometimes, I check it so fast and switch it off so fast. So ideally I should not check iPhones and iPads. I don't use my iPad at night because it's hard to lower it enough — it's huge. But even my iPhone, I try not to use it at night.

## Light Exposure & Effects on Stress, Mood & Learning, Tripartite Model

**Andrew Huberman:** You had what I consider an absolutely landmark, beautiful paper published in Nature a few years ago, showing that if you disrupt the exposure to light — or the timing of the exposure to light — there are dramatic effects on the stress system and on the learning and memory system. So if I interpret that correctly, that could mean that when we view light and how much light we get could make us feel happier or less happy, or even depressed, stressed, affect learning, etc.

**Samer Hattar:** Bingo. Even if we're sleeping and waking up at the appropriate times. Because we're talking about the whole system. Eventually, when you start having the other problems, you also develop sleep problems. But you're absolutely right. And in fact, now research from Diego Fernandez in the lab has found that these effects actually require different brain regions. So we don't only have a theory, we don't only have a light environment that showed they can be dissociated — we know that they use completely different brain regions. The SCN that I told you about earlier — the place where the central pacemaker is, the one that receives direct input from the retina through the ipRGCs to adjust your circadian clock — is not the area that receives the light input for mood regulation. It's a completely different brain region. And what's really amazing, this region also receives direct input from the ipRGCs but projects to areas in the brain that are known to regulate mood, including the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, which has been studied for many years to be impacted in human depression. So just by this amazing serendipity, to find that a region so deep in the advanced brain — like the prefrontal cortex, your executive brain, one of the most elaborated in humans — receives input from these ancient photoreceptors was stunning to us.

**Andrew Huberman:** How does that finding inform daily protocols for you or for other people?

**Samer Hattar:** That's why we came up with the tripartite model, because as a circadian biologist I only thought of light through the circadian clock affecting behavior. As a sleep biologist they only thought of the homeostatic drive affecting sleep affecting behavior. And for people who study light for vision and other functions, they thought only of the environmental input. But now if you put them all together, you get the tripartite model — where it's really mind-boggling and it makes so much sense. The organism doesn't want to depend on a single component. But if you could incorporate these three together, you could have a beautiful system that is well adapted.

So let me tell you — the sleep-wake cycle. We know there is a homeostatic drive to affect sleep. The longer you're awake, the more you want to be asleep. That's your homeostatic drive. We've talked about the circadian influence on sleep and the fact that the light-dark cycle affects the circadian system, which eventually affects sleep. So these two components are well understood. Now the third factor is your direct light or environmental input. How much light you get from the environment can also highly impact sleep. So even if you have a good circadian system and homeostatic drive, if you're getting light at the wrong time of the day or if you're being stressed, then your sleep is going to suffer. So you have to think of the three together to have a beautiful sleep-wake cycle.

## Light & Appetite, Tool: Regular Meal Times

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about food and eating and appetite. You had yet another incredible discovery showing that there are direct effects of light on appetite and feeding behavior. So for somebody who's interested in affecting their eating behavior, how should they use light in order to adjust their eating behavior?

**Samer Hattar:** Right. Now that I've told you about all these interactions between the different inputs to the circadian clock, just think about it as an engineer — what would be the best thing? The best thing is to know when your food times happen in the day. When should you get light, and where is your circadian clock in your system? So if you eat at very specific times of the day, that's another signal that is telling your body and your clock what time of the day it is. If you're having lunch at the correct time every day and you're getting bright light, now you have two systems that are informing your clock and your clock is going to be better. So regular mealtimes, regular mealtimes that fit your circadian clock.

And in fact, when I started doing this and it helped me lose weight — I'm exposing myself to the right amount of light-dark cycle, I'm eating at regular times. It is amazing. You will not be hungry, let's say, until 11:45 if you eat at noon, and then all of a sudden the hunger jumps. This is clearly not an energy issue because it could not be that drastic. No, the desire to eat is mainly driven by these hormone cues that are very exquisitely timed to exactly the sleep-wake cycle, but also to light.

**Andrew Huberman:** How regular are you, or do you recommend people be about meal times? Are we talking about down to the minute?

**Samer Hattar:** Plus or minus about half an hour. So 12 noon is my normal lunch — plus or minus half an hour. And it depends if you also do multiple meals. Remember, three meals — that's a decision that somebody came up with, I don't know why. Given our friend Sachin Panda's work, you could have two meals, or you could have multiple meals that are distributed across your active time.

**Andrew Huberman:** Some people are not hungry early in the day. They might be late-shifted people, in which case eating later in the day will work well for them.

**Samer Hattar:** As long as they don't eat early in the morning. You just have to work with your schedule, with your active schedule.

**Andrew Huberman:** What we're talking about really is finding your ideal sleep schedule.

**Samer Hattar:** Exactly.

**Andrew Huberman:** And finding your ideal eating schedule.

**Samer Hattar:** Exactly. And understanding how those two things interact. The nice thing is, finding them out is going to help you understand how they interact, because from the tripartite model we know that they are all interconnected. And for each person they're going to be interconnected differently.

**Andrew Huberman:** It's striking to me that in all animals besides humans, if they deviate too much from the appropriate exposure to light and the light-dark cycle, they essentially don't mate and/or die and/or get killed off. But in humans, we are able to override that, at least to some extent. But the ways in which we suffer appear to be things like obesity, metabolic syndromes, reproductive syndromes, endocrine syndromes, mood and depressive disorders.

## Jet Lag, Tools: Temperature Minimum; Eat on Local Schedule, Avoid Mismatched Light Exposure

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about jet lag. What are the two or three things that people can do to adjust their schedule quickly? Like, let's say fall classes are starting, you start a new job, or you have a baby or a puppy. What is the best way to shift the clock quickly?

**Samer Hattar:** So it's very simple. Imagine you're outside with no industrial light. If your body thinks you're in early evening and you see a bright light, what does this tell you? Oh, wait — this is not early evening yet. It's still late afternoon. So I have to delay my clock back to late afternoon. If you get light early in the evening, it delays your clock — meaning it makes you want to go to sleep later. Whereas later in the night, you get a temperature nadir — a low temperature in your body — and after that, light starts advancing your clock.

**Andrew Huberman:** If I understand correctly, if your typical wake-up time is say 7:00 a.m., then your low point in temperature probably occurs somewhere around 5:00 a.m.

**Samer Hattar:** Yeah.

**Andrew Huberman:** And if you view light right around then, it's going to essentially advance your clock.

**Samer Hattar:** Yeah. Because then your body thinks, "Oh, it's 7:00, so advance your clock by 1 to 2 hours." But if I were to view light at 3:00 a.m., it would probably delay my clock.

**Andrew Huberman:** Okay. So let's say I land in a new schedule — I land in a new time zone. I was told that one of the ways to help shift the clock and to avoid gastrointestinal issues is to eat on the local schedule.

**Samer Hattar:** Absolutely. But you have to remember the light. Let me take a very simple example — New York to Italy, six-hour difference. Let's say you fly from New York at night and reach Italy at 8:00 in the morning. What is the New York time? It's 2:00 a.m. So when you land in Italy, you want to avoid light like the plague. You could eat, but you really don't want to get bright light, because otherwise it's going to delay you. It's going to send you to California instead of sending you to Italy.

**Andrew Huberman:** What Samer is saying is so crucial. Just because getting bright light in your eyes early in the day is really beneficial when you're at home — when you travel to a new time zone, you have to take into account where your body thinks you are. And so if you're looking at the Italian sunrise, having just flown from New York to Italy without preparing by waking up a little bit earlier in anticipation over multiple days, and you view light at 6:00 or 7 a.m. Italian time — beautiful Italian sunrise — you are going to delay your clock. You're going to throw yourself back to California, but you are in Italy. You're going to be miserable.

**Samer Hattar:** It's very important to avoid getting the wrong light information when you're trying to adjust your body, because otherwise it shifts you to the other side. Absolutely right.

**Andrew Huberman:** You are one of those people that has such vigor. I think a lot of your ability to work hard and focus and really do so many things at an impressive level is because you think about these issues — you think about when you're going to be optimal for focus, when you're going to be optimal for exercise, and the "when" is the key.

**Samer Hattar:** And I think a lot of people live in the landscape of feeling like there's something broken inside them. ## Seasonality, Seasonal Depression; Daylight Savings Time
