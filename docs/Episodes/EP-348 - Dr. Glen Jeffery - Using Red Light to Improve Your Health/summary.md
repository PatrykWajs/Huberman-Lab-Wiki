# Episode 348: Dr. Glen Jeffery: Using Red Light to Improve Your Health & the Mitochondria Connection

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Glen Jeffery, PhD — professor of neuroscience at University College London and leading researcher on light and retinal health — explains how long-wavelength light (red, near-infrared, and infrared) improves health by reducing the viscosity of "nano water" inside mitochondria, speeding up ATP production and triggering synthesis of more electron-transport-chain proteins. The conversation covers why modern LED lighting is a serious public health threat comparable to asbestos, how 3 minutes of 670 nm red light improves vision for 5 days, why incandescent/halogen bulbs produce stronger and longer-lasting benefits than targeted red-light devices, and zero-to-low-cost strategies anyone can implement. Dr. Jeffery also presents evidence on blood glucose regulation, Parkinson's disease, macular degeneration, myopia in children, mitochondrial disease, and the correlation between LED adoption and the flattening of Western lifespan gains since ~2010.

## Key Takeaways

- **Mitochondria run on nano water, and long-wavelength light changes that water.** Mitochondria spin ATP-producing pumps surrounded by viscous "nano water." Long-wavelength (red/IR) light reduces that viscosity, increasing spin rate — an immediate effect. Longer term, the body synthesizes more electron-transport-chain proteins: "the train runs faster, then more tracks are laid." The mechanism is water absorption, not direct absorption by mitochondria themselves. The discovery was pioneered by Tina Karu in Russia and largely ignored for decades. [00:17:58](transcript.md#light-impacts-mitochondria-function--structure-long-wavelength-light-redir-nano-water)

- **Long-wavelength light passes through clothing, skin, bone, and skull.** Demonstrated by placing a spectrometer on subjects' backs in direct sunlight: a few percent exits the back, meaning the vast majority is absorbed and scattered throughout the body. Bob Fosbury (ESA astrophysicist) confirmed it passes through six layers of T-shirt regardless of color. It passes through the skull — now used clinically at UCL by biomedical engineer Ilas Takanides to monitor mitochondrial function in neonates after stroke. [00:25:00](transcript.md#long-wavelength-light-passes-through-clothing--body-tissue-scattering)

- **3 minutes of 670 nm red light improves color vision by ~20% and the effect lasts exactly 5 days.** The response is a binary switch, not a dose-response curve — sufficient energy triggers it, then it resets after 5 days. Effect is conserved across flies, mice, and humans. Eyes open or closed does not matter; eyelids pass long-wavelength light. Effect is more pronounced in people over ~40 because aging mitochondria have more room to improve. [00:51:04](transcript.md#offsetting-retinal-aging-improve-vision--long-wavelength-light)

- **Morning is the optimal time for red-light exposure.** Mitochondrial protein composition and ATP production peak in the morning (sunrise to ~11:00 am) and shift to maintenance-mode tasks in the afternoon. The circadian gating of the vision-improvement effect is consistent across flies, mice, and humans — morning gives the biggest response. [01:03:50](transcript.md#mitochondrial-theory-of-aging-circadian-rhythm--mitochondria)

- **Incandescent/halogen bulbs produce stronger and longer-lasting vision improvements than targeted red-light devices.** A UCL study placed 40W incandescent desk lamps in a windowless, LED-lit workspace for two weeks. Both blue and red color perception improved equally and significantly — better than any red-LED experiment to date — and the improvement persisted for at least one month (vs. the 5-day reset from a 670 nm flashlight). Smooth full-spectrum light beats a narrow-band device. [01:39:07](transcript.md#incandescent--halogen-bulbs-mitochondria--built-environments)

- **Red light on a small area of skin (~4×6 in on the back) reduces post-meal blood glucose spikes by >20%.** Mitochondria boosted by light consume more glucose to produce ATP, lowering circulating blood sugar systemically. Bumblebee pilot experiments confirmed the direction before human trials. Blue/short-wavelength light has the opposite effect. Subjects felt no heat, ruling out placebo. [00:30:08](transcript.md#long-wavelength-light--blood-glucose-mitochondria)

- **LED lighting is a public health issue on the scale of asbestos.** LED white light carries a heavy blue/violet spike — the dangerous range is 420–440 nm, not the broader "blue light" commonly discussed — and essentially no long-wavelength output. Real-time imaging shows mouse retinal mitochondria declining under domestic LED illumination. Mice under LED-only environments gain weight, develop fatty livers, smaller hearts and kidneys, abnormal sperm, and altered anxiety behavior — all on the same diet as full-spectrum controls. [01:20:56](transcript.md#short-wavelength-light-led-light-mitochondria--serious-health-detriments)

- **Western European lifespan gains plateaued around 2010, coinciding with mass LED adoption.** The correlation is under active investigation by multiple independent labs. Dr. Jeffery does not yet claim causation but considers it a serious working hypothesis. Sunlight (balanced spectrum) is associated with lower all-cause mortality — including reductions in cardiovascular disease and cancer — in large Swedish and University of East Anglia epidemiological datasets (research led by dermatologist Richard Weller). [01:28:39](transcript.md#lifespan-led-lights-sunlight--balanced-wavelengths-sunlike-marketing)

- **Red light reduces the pace of retinal cell death and slows neurodegeneration.** Aging mice given daily red-light bursts retain significantly more rod photoreceptors at old age. John Mitrofanis showed red light shone on the abdomen of primates with drug-induced Parkinson's disease slowed dopamine-neuron death in the substantia nigra. The mechanism is mitochondria-mediated: healthy membrane potential reduces apoptotic "eat me" signaling. [00:36:19](transcript.md#redIR-light-parkinsons-disease-cell-death-eye-rods--aging-mitochondria-community)

- **Early intervention is critical; red light cannot rescue end-stage disease.** Dr. Jeffery's initial macular degeneration trial failed because patients were too advanced. Ophthalmologist Ben Burton replicated it with earlier-stage patients and obtained significant vision improvements. The same principle applies to rheumatoid arthritis — intervene at the first ache, not after the hands are already twisted. [01:10:44](transcript.md#macular-degeneration-rescuing-vision-early-intervention)

- **Mitochondria communicate as a body-wide community.** Stimulating a small skin patch produces systemic blood glucose effects — locally within 1–2 hours, distally within ~24 hours. Signaling candidates include cytokines (low-level increase = protective immune priming) and microvesicles (carrying mitochondrial cargo in serum). Cells can physically donate mitochondria to sick neighbors. [01:13:59](transcript.md#light-effects-at-local-vs-distant-tissues-immune-system-body-communication)

- **The built environment creates a double insult.** Commercial buildings use cheap restricted-spectrum LEDs (budget overruns cut the lighting last) AND infrared-blocking thermally insulating glass. Workers are simultaneously deprived of long-wavelength light from both artificial and natural sources. Plants reflect infrared back into rooms; planting trees around buildings measurably reduces systemic inflammation biomarkers in occupants. [01:39:07](transcript.md#incandescent--halogen-bulbs-mitochondria--built-environments)

- **Never use lasers for photobiomodulation.** Laser light forms "caustics" — hot spots where energy triples or quadruples — unlike the uniform scattering of LED light. Chinese myopia studies using low-power lasers found focal retinal damage despite stated low energy levels. No living entity has evolved under monochromatic light. Lasers are for trained medical use only. [01:45:19](transcript.md#windows-light--office-work-screens-kids--myopia-tools-plants-lighting)

## Tools & Protocols

### Red/Long-Wavelength Light for Vision Improvement
- **What:** 670 nm (or any wavelength above 670 nm; efficacy drops below ~650 nm) from an inexpensive LED flashlight.
- **How:** Hold at comfortable distance — start close, back off just below the discomfort-brightness threshold. Eyes open or closed both work.
- **Duration:** 3 minutes per session triggers the effect.
- **Frequency:** Once every 5 days maintains the benefit.
- **Timing:** Morning (sunrise to ~11:00 am) for maximum effect; circadian gating is real.
- **Energy:** Dim to moderately bright is fine; experiments worked at ~1 mW/cm² (near-dead batteries). Bright is not required.
- **Ref:** [00:59:28](transcript.md#tool-long-wavelength-light--preserve-retinal-mitochondria-sunlight) | [01:07:57](transcript.md#tool-improve-vision-with-long-wavelength-light)

### Incandescent / Halogen Bulbs for Indoor Lighting
- **What:** 40W incandescent desk lamp or halogen bulb (classic incandescents are banned in North America; halogens remain available and are functionally identical).
- **How:** Place near workspace, light directed down — you do not need to look at it. Ambient full-spectrum light in the environment is what matters.
- **When:** Morning most impactful; dimmed halogen in the evening does not suppress melatonin.
- **Why better than red-light devices:** Smooth full-spectrum emission; UCL study produced equal improvement in both blue and red color perception, persisting for at least one month.
- **Dimming tip:** Dimming a halogen shifts output further toward infrared and dramatically extends bulb life.
- **Ref:** [01:39:07](transcript.md#incandescent--halogen-bulbs-mitochondria--built-environments) | [02:00:35](transcript.md#tool-candlelight-dim-halogen-lamps)

### Candlelight (Zero-Cost Evening Option)
- **What:** Odorless pure beeswax candle (avoid scented paraffin — volatile compounds are a separate concern).
- **How:** Use at desk or nightstand while reading in the evening. Candlelight is almost entirely long-wavelength.
- **When:** Evening. Does not suppress melatonin. Safe for circadian rhythm.
- **Ref:** [02:00:35](transcript.md#tool-candlelight-dim-halogen-lamps)

### Red Light for Blood Glucose Management
- **What:** Any long-wavelength light source applied to exposed skin.
- **How:** Illuminate a ~4×6 inch area of skin (back, arm) before or around mealtimes.
- **Effect:** Reduces post-meal blood glucose spike by >20% via mitochondrial community signaling.
- **Ref:** [00:30:08](transcript.md#long-wavelength-light--blood-glucose-mitochondria)

### Improving Indoor Environment (Zero to Low Cost)
- **Get outside:** Even 10–20 minutes outdoors provides long-wavelength light through clothing; use lunch breaks, phone calls, dog walks.
- **Add plants:** All plant matter reflects infrared light. Plants on windowsills or near light sources return infrared into the room.
- **Avoid infrared-blocking glass:** If you have a choice, use standard glass rather than thermally insulating IR-blocking glass.
- **Dim LEDs in the evening:** Reduces total short-wavelength load even if spectrum is not changed.
- **Ref:** [01:45:19](transcript.md#windows-light--office-work-screens-kids--myopia-tools-plants-lighting) | [01:55:56](transcript.md#bring-the-outdoors-indoors)

### Red/Near-IR Panels (Commercial Devices)
- **What:** Full-body red/near-IR panels (e.g., Joovv).
- **How:** Andrew Huberman's personal protocol: 3–4 sessions/week, 5–10 minutes per session.
- **Caveats:** Many consumer devices are not calibrated to stated wavelength, and quality degrades over time. Medical-grade differs meaningfully from consumer-grade.
- **Ref:** [01:34:45](transcript.md#fires-incandescent-lights-vs-led-lights-lasers-long-wavelength-devices)
