## [00:00:00] Workplace Optimization for Performance

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today, we're going to talk all about how to optimize your workspace for maximum productivity. Indeed, that means to heighten levels of focus, to increase levels of creativity, to improve your ability to task switch. And this could be for sake of school or for work, creative endeavors, personal endeavors — this really extends to everybody.

## [00:00:36] Clutter, Variables for Workspace Optimization

This is a topic that's intrigued me for a very long time because my undergraduate adviser, my graduate adviser, and my postdoc adviser had many things in common, including being great scientists, being kind people, and terrific mentors. But they had another thing in common which always perplexed me — their offices were a complete disaster. They had mountains of books, mountains of papers, mountains of all sorts of stuff, and yet all of them were extremely productive and could remain extremely focused in that incredibly cluttered environment. Now I'm somebody who doesn't like clutter — I find it very hard to focus in cluttered environments. And indeed, there's tremendous variation among people as to whether or not they can remain focused or whether or not they struggle to focus in physically cluttered environments.

There's no right or wrong to this, but the question we should ask ourselves is why were they all able to be so focused? It turns out that the reason they were able to be so focused is that they all captured one single and yet fundamental variable of workspace optimization. We're going to talk about what all the variables of optimizing a workspace are — things like vision, light, noise in the room, whether or not you listen to music or not, whether or not you use noise-cancelling headphones or not. We're going to do that in a way that you can optimize your workspace regardless of whether you are at home or on the road. The last thing I would ever want to do is create a situation where you find the optimal workspace and then become a slave to it — that's just not the way the world works.

What you want is a short checklist of things that you can look to anytime you sit down to do work, so you can think about the underlying variables that impact your brain and body and allow them to get into the optimal state in order to learn, be productive, and move through your workday in a relaxed and pleasurable way while maintaining focus.

## [00:02:41] Vision & Light, Tool: Mornings & Bright Light; Windows

The first variable to think about in terms of workspace optimization is vision and light. From the time you wake up in the morning until about 6, 7, 8, or sometimes 9 hours later, your brain is in a unique state — high levels of dopamine, a neuromodulator, and high levels of epinephrine, as well as hormones like cortisol. That early part of the day is a time in which being in a brightly lit environment can lend itself to optimal work throughout the day, not just during that early phase. One of the things I've done for my workspace is to make sure that when I wake up in the morning I do go get my sunlight, and if the sun isn't out, I turn on as many bright artificial lights as I can manage or tolerate before going to get sunlight exposure. Once I set out to do work, all the overhead lights in the room are on as well as lights in front of me — this is again to stimulate heightened levels of focus and further release of these neuromodulators: dopamine, norepinephrine, and epinephrine.

One very low-cost approach is a desk lamp plus overhead lights. Ring lights can be pretty cost-effective and yet very bright, with the sort of bright blue light that will optimally stimulate those melanopsin ganglion cells. I use a light pad placed on the desk in front of me, turned on throughout phase one of the day. For those of you who can place your desk near a window — and even better, open the window — that would be fantastic. Sunlight is going to be the best stimulus for waking up your brain and body through the melanopsin-to-hypothalamus system. Looking at sunlight through a window is 50 times less effective than through an open window, mostly because windows filter out a lot of the wavelengths of blue light essential for stimulating the wakeup signal.

## [00:04:45] Afternoon, Evenings & Light; Tool: Dimming Lights

In the afternoon, starting at about 9 hours after waking and continuing until about 16 hours after waking, you want to start dimming the lights in the environment. In this so-called phase two of the 24-hour cycle — from about 9 to 16 hours after waking — you want to bring the level of lights down a bit. Having lights in front of you is fine, but overhead lights at that time are not going to be optimal for the sorts of neurochemical states that your brain wants to be in. The states I'm referring to are a shift from the dopamine and norepinephrine that are highest early in the day to increases in things like serotonin and other neuromodulators that put your brain into a state better for creative endeavors or more abstract thinking.

What I personally do is turn off overhead lights in the afternoon — it's not completely dim or dark, but I start to reduce overhead light and just keep the light pad on and whatever other lamps I'm using. Somewhere around 4 or 5 p.m., about 12 to 14 hours after waking, I turn off the light pad and start to transition the lights in my environment to more yellows and reds. For people who are working in the middle of the night — phase three, about 17 to 24 hours after waking — you really want to limit the amount of bright light getting into your eyes to just what allows you to do the work. Getting brighter light than that will severely deplete your melatonin levels, shift your circadian clock, and is effectively like traveling to another time zone. If you stay up from 2 to 4 a.m. or 3 to 6 a.m. working on a paper with bright light in your eyes, your body registers it as flying 6 hours into a different time zone, throwing off sleep, metabolism, and more. The exception: if you really want to stay awake, turning on all the lights can help — but you have to weigh that against the circadian shift.

## [00:07:13] Alertness & Visual Focus, Tool: Screen Placement

There's another aspect of vision critically important for how alert we are going to be and how well we can maintain that alertness — where our visual focus is in a given environment. We have clusters of neurons in our brain stem that control our eyelid muscles and our eye movements up and down and to the sides. When we are looking down toward the ground or anywhere below basically the central region of our face, the neurons that control that eye movement are intimately related to areas of the brain stem that release certain neuromodulators and activate areas of the brain associated with calm and even sleepiness. The opposite is also true: neurons that place our eyes into an upward gaze — above the level of our nose and literally looking up while keeping the head stationary — trigger activation of brain circuits associated with alertness.

This has obvious implications. Contrary to what most people do — looking down at their laptop, tablet, or phone — if you want to be alert and maintain maximum focus, you want that screen or whatever you're looking at to be at least at eye level and ideally slightly above it. This is a simple, underappreciated tool that can make a significant difference in your sustained attention throughout a work session.

## [00:09:05] Vergence Eye Movement & Alertness, Tool: Visual Breaks

Our visual pathways have two major channels. The first is the parvocellular channel, involved in looking at things at specific points in space and at high resolution or detail. The second is the magnocellular channel, involved in looking at big swaths of visual space at lower resolution. You don't have to remember the names — what you do have to remember is that you create maximum alertness and maximum ability to focus when your system is in that parvocellular mode. When you bring your eyes to a common point — what we call a vergence eye movement — you create a narrower aperture of your visual window, meaning your visual world actually shrinks perceptually.

The caveat is that if you look at a narrow space — a book, laptop, tablet, or phone — for any extended period of time, those vergence eye movements not only create alertness but also require energy and can fatigue the eyes through a process called accommodation, where the shape of the eye literally has to change so the lens can focus at that location. Accommodation is demanding, which is why your eyes get tired when you focus on something for too long. The principle extracted from ophthalmology and neuroscience is: for every 45 minutes in which you are focusing on a phone, tablet, book page, or computer, you want to shift into panoramic vision for at least 5 minutes. The best way to do this is to take a walk outside — look off into the distance, at a horizon — which will automatically trigger panoramic gaze, very relaxing to the eyes, allowing you to return to a focused work bout. The one thing you absolutely do not want to do is go outside and check your phone, because you're still in vergence eye movement and not giving the system the break it needs.

## [00:11:50] Cathedral Effect, Creative vs Analytical Thinking, Tool: Ceiling Height by Task

There is an aspect of workspace optimization that can bias whether your brain and nervous system are better suited for detailed analytic work or more abstract work. This is called the cathedral effect. Formally discussed since the early 2000s, the cathedral effect holds that people in high-ceiling environments shift their thinking toward more abstract and creative, lofty-type ideas — hence the phrase referring to cathedrals. This was observed in the language people used as well as the sorts of ideas they would generate. Conversely, people in lower-ceiling environments tend to be more oriented toward restricted, detailed, analytic language about things in their immediate space.

Most of us have a fixed ceiling height at home, but you may have rooms with higher ceilings and rooms with lower ceilings. If you want to do creative work during phase two (9 to 16 hours after waking), do it in the high-ceiling room, or even outdoors — because the highest ceiling is of course the sky. Lower ceilings tend to orient thinking toward detailed, correct-answer-type work. Higher ceilings or no ceiling orient the brain toward abstract reasoning, brainstorming, and pulling from broader swaths of memory — because abstract reasoning is taking existing elements and arranging them in novel ways. If you don't have access to a different room, you could use a brimmed hat or hoodie to lower your visual environment for analytic work, or remove it for creative work. The cathedral effect can be leveraged even within a single space.

## [00:14:50] Background Noise to Avoid; White Noise, Pink Noise

There is a lot of quality scientific data on whether listening to particular sounds can enhance cognition — and the answer is yes, but with very particular types of sounds under very particular conditions. You can find studies supporting any type of environment, from complete silence to white noise to binaural beats to classical music. However, there are a few types of environments to avoid and a few types of sounds that really can enhance cognition and the ability to focus, and that seem to work for all people.

Background noise to avoid: there is a paper by Jordan Love and Alexander Francis on psychophysiological responses to HVAC noise during mentally demanding work. It identifies a large dataset showing that loud, incessant workplace noise — the humming of air conditioners or heaters — can really increase mental fatigue and vastly decrease cognitive performance. We've all experienced the enormous relief when an ongoing background noise stops. Does that mean we shouldn't listen to white noise, pink noise, or brown noise? These are just different constellations of auditory frequencies played together — brown noise has different frequencies included at higher amplitude — and you can find them easily on YouTube. There is some evidence that white, pink, or brown noise can facilitate cognition mainly through an increase in overall alertness via areas like the locus coeruleus associated with autonomic arousal. However, there is really no reason to suspect that those particular patterns of noise will optimize particular mental functions in a targeted way.

## [00:17:38] Sound Patterns to Improve Concentration

There are particular patterns of sounds that have been shown in peer-reviewed studies to optimize certain types of mental processing. If you search for apps or go online for sounds that improve thinking, you'll generally find three types. First are isochronic tones — tones usually of a common frequency, like a beep, then a pause, then a beep of the same frequency. Contrast those with monaural beats — repetitive, almost percussive beats delivered to just one ear. Then there are binaural beats, which as the name suggests are beats delivered to the two ears — one pattern of percussive beat to one ear and a different pattern, or at least one that's out of phase and not synchronized, delivered to the other ear.

What happens is that because of the way the auditory system converges in the brain stem and generates what are called interaural time differences, the difference between the two patterns of beats heard by each ear leads to a third pattern that the brain entrains to and maps onto, generating particular types of brain waves. Interaural time differences are the ways in which, if you were to hear something off to your right, the signal arrives in your right ear before it arrives in your left ear — and there is a brain stem area where signals from both ears converge and literally do the math: this signal arrived before the other, and the difference between those signals is the interaural time difference. Binaural beats have been generated in ways that create particular patterns of interaural time differences that cascade up to the rest of the brain and put the forebrain and other areas involved in cognition and action into a particular rhythm — including alpha waves, theta waves, and gamma waves. Looking across studies of binaural beats, we can ask what frequencies appear to be useful for enhancing particular kinds of brain function.

## [00:20:54] Tool: Binaural Beat Frequency for Focus; Dopamine

The frequency of binaural beats that appears to bring about improved cognitive functioning — at the level of memory, reaction times, and verbal recall — seems to be 40 Hz. You might try listening to binaural beats for about 30 minutes while doing something else, like eating lunch or taking a walk, and then going into your work session, because the brain doesn't immediately switch into a particular pattern of oscillation — it takes some time. 40 Hz binaural beats are available through many apps and YouTube channels, hopefully at zero cost. Some mechanistic context: this 40 Hz binaural beat pattern seems to have an effect on what's called striatal dopamine. That dopamine release leads to heightened levels of motivation and focus. Why motivation and focus? Dopamine is actually the substrate by which epinephrine is made — the dopamine molecule is converted into epinephrine, adrenaline — and they work together like close cousins to put us on a path of mental movement toward a goal. That is part of the reason why 40 Hz binaural beats can enhance focus, reduce reaction times, and improve learning and memory.

## [00:22:27] Tool: Managing Interruptions

There is a simple method for managing interruptions that I learned from my graduate adviser and that works very, very well. What she would do was if I came by and asked a question, or if anyone came by and asked a question, she would acknowledge their presence but would not shift her body toward them. She purposely did not position her computer facing the door — which I think is a deadly-to-focus way of positioning your workspace. Her computer was facing the wall, with the door perpendicular to that. I would come by and say "I have a question," and she would say "Yes," acknowledging my presence but never actually orienting her body toward me — which told me this conversation was not going to last very long. No matter how long I stood there or what I asked, she would never orient toward me, which generally kept these conversations very short.

Another approach that some colleagues have used is to simply say no to everything when someone comes by — just shout "no" through the door, or say "no" when someone asks "Can I bother you for a second?" — until the person goes away. Some of the most productive people I know used this method. Not always the kindest, but some of them were very kind. The key principle is to use body orientation and physical signals to communicate that a focused work session is in progress.

## [00:23:51] Sit or Stand for Work?, Tool: Sit-Stand Desk

Is it better to sit or to stand when doing work, at least as it relates to focus and productivity? The answer is both. There have been a number of systematic studies exploring sit-stand desks — desks that can be set to a height for standing and then lowered for sitting. Just sitting is terrible for us — an enormous number of studies point to the fact that people who sit for five, six, or seven hours a day doing work have all sorts of issues related to sleep, neck pain, cognitive performance, cardiovascular effects, even digestion, and potentially pressure effects on the pelvic floor depending on the chair used. People who stand are in a slightly better situation where many of those health metrics improve. But people who do a combination of sitting and standing at the same desk throughout the day — or move from one desk to another — will experience the best outcomes.

People who decreased their sitting time by about half each day showed incredibly significant effects on reduced neck and shoulder pain, increased subjective health and vitality in work-related environments, and — perhaps most importantly — improvement in cognitive conditioning and the ability to embrace new tasks. What happens if you just stand all day? That can generate postural issues in terms of stabilization and fatigue. That said, most people in the US are not getting sufficient movement throughout the day, and standing at one's desk can improve some health metrics and productivity, probably because of the postural effects discussed earlier. After about 10 years of working at a sit-stand desk, I find I can't sit for too long before wanting to stand. My standing stints can be anywhere from 30 minutes to 2 hours — though 2 hours starts to feel long. The literature supports standing, but ideally with variation, not leaning on the desk for extended periods.

## [00:26:21] Key Takeaways, Flexibility & Changing Locations

Let's review some of the basic elements of what we've covered today. In the first part of your day — zero to nine hours after waking — you want bright lights, especially overhead lights, as bright as you can keep them without feeling uncomfortable. Bright lights will create the maximum state of alertness. Try to place whatever you're focusing on at least at nose level or above, avoid reclining, and try to stand for at least half of your workday — that's a good goal that may take time to work up to. If you're going to use sound to increase focus and alertness, try to avoid exposure to white, pink, or brown noise for extended periods of more than an hour or so — it may actually be damaging to the auditory system and at the very least creates a background level of anxiety and stress that is not going to serve you well.

If you're going to pursue particular types of sound frequencies, consider using 40 Hz binaural beats — not monaural beats — done during a particular task or for 30 minutes prior. Do not rely on binaural beats all the time every day, as that could cause them to lose potency due to the way the auditory system attenuates. For the cathedral effect: detailed analytic work belongs in low-ceiling environments; for brainstorming and creative work, use a high-ceiling or no-ceiling environment. If you don't have that flexibility, a brimmed hat or hoodie can lower your visual environment for analytic work, and removing it opens things up for creative work. Finally, there's nothing that says you have to always work in the same location — you can move from home to cafe, from office to home, or between different locations within your home. Thank you for joining me for this discussion about the science and peer-reviewed literature on workspace optimization. I hope some if not all of the tools will be beneficial for you. And as always, thank you for your interest in science.
