# Episode 144: How to Improve Your Eye Health & Offset Vision Loss | Dr. Jeffrey Goldberg

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman sits down with Dr. Jeffrey Goldberg — chair of the Department of Ophthalmology at the Byers Eye Institute, Stanford University — for a comprehensive discussion on visual health across the entire lifespan. The conversation covers newborn red-reflex checks, childhood amblyopia and myopia prevention, eye safety and hygiene, adult corrective lens options (glasses, contacts, LASIK), presbyopia, dry eye biology and management, and the major blinding diseases of older age (cataracts, glaucoma, age-related macular degeneration, diabetic retinopathy, and retinitis pigmentosa). Dr. Goldberg shares behavioral protocols, rigorous clinical-trial evidence for supplements (AREDS2, nicotinamide), and emerging research on red-light photobiomodulation and high-resolution retinal imaging for detecting Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and multiple sclerosis before neurological symptoms appear. The episode includes personal anecdotes from Huberman about his own visual history and a discussion of supranormal visual performance training at Stanford's new Vision Performance Center.

## Guest: Dr. Jeffrey Goldberg

Dr. Jeffrey Goldberg is a clinician-scientist (MD, PhD) and Professor and Chair of the Department of Ophthalmology at the Byers Eye Institute at Stanford University School of Medicine. His laboratory focuses on retinal ganglion cell biology, optic nerve regeneration, stem cell therapies, and nanotechnology-based treatments for blindness. He sees patients weekly while simultaneously directing a research program targeting cures for glaucoma, retinitis pigmentosa, and macular degeneration. ## Key Takeaways

1. **Newborn red-reflex check is the first eye exam.** Every newborn receives a penlight red-reflex check in the nursery. A normal retina returns a red reflex; a whitish or absent reflex can signal retinoblastoma or other serious pediatric eye disease. Beyond that, additional exams are triggered by nystagmus, eye misalignment, failure to make eye contact, or parental suspicion in the toddler years. Routine elementary-school screening covers amblyopia and strabismus. [00:06:29](transcript.md#000629-childhood--eye-exams)

2. **Amblyopia (lazy eye) must be caught and treated during development — but the window extends into the early teens.** If one eye is misaligned or has a very different refractive error, the brain stops developing strong connections to that eye. Older thresholds ("fully correctable before age 3, partly before age 6") have been revised upward: even children in their tween and early teen years can recover meaningful vision with patching of the strong eye and/or muscle realignment. Depth perception (stereopsis) is the hardest function to recover and may remain permanently impaired even after central visual acuity improves. [00:11:36](transcript.md#001136-eye-misalignment--recovery)

3. **Outdoor time — not just reduced near work — is the key driver of myopia prevention in children.** RCTs show children who spend more time outdoors in natural daylight progress more slowly in near-sightedness. The mechanism is full-spectrum bright outdoor light triggering retinal dopamine release that suppresses axial elongation of the eyeball. Screens and near-work alone are not the primary driver; it is indoor vs. outdoor light environment that matters most. There is likely dose-dependent benefit; the optimal number of outdoor hours per day has not been definitively established. [00:20:38](transcript.md#002038-myopia-near-sightedness-children--sunlight)

4. **Eye safety and physical protection are underemphasized.** The cornea is less than a millimeter thick and highly vulnerable; the retina is neural tissue subject to injury and inflammation. Dr. Goldberg sees one or two open-globe injuries per week in the emergency room. Wearing glasses, goggles, or safety eyewear during gardening, metal grinding, woodworking, or any activity involving projectiles prevents a significant fraction of unnecessary permanent eye injury. [00:31:18](transcript.md#003118-eye-safety--protection-eye-hygiene)

5. **Eyelid hygiene (blepharitis scrubs) keeps meibomian glands functioning and reduces dry eye.** Eyelid margins can accumulate scurf (dead skin) and develop blepharitis (inflammation), especially with age. A daily routine — diluted No-More-Tears baby shampoo applied with a fingertip to gently closed eyelids — clears debris, keeps oil glands patent, and reduces dry eye symptoms. Dedicated eyelid scrub pads are also available over the counter. [00:31:18](transcript.md#003118-eye-safety--protection-eye-hygiene)

6. **Reading glasses do not weaken the eyes — use them freely.** A persistent myth holds that relying on readers accelerates presbyopia. Dr. Goldberg's recommendation: wear the correction that provides the sharpest comfortable vision. The crystalline lens stiffens with age regardless of glasses use. While slightly undercorrecting might exercise focusing muscles, it also deprives the retina and brain of sharp signals. Practical advice: enjoy your best vision. [00:54:54](transcript.md#005454-reading-glasses-use--recommendations)

7. **Latent hyperopia can explain night driving difficulty and the benefit of readers at distance.** Some people's optical system is tuned to focus beyond infinity, requiring constant muscular effort to see clearly at distance. When fatigued — or after alcohol — this latent farsightedness can surface. A small plus-lens correction can relieve the strain and sharpen distance vision at night. [00:58:34](transcript.md#005834-night-vision--glasses)

8. **Near-far (pencil pushup) exercises have genuine clinical evidence.** Convergence insufficiency — difficulty maintaining binocular alignment on a near target — responds well to near-far exercises. The same exercises are used in concussion rehabilitation to restore smooth-pursuit eye movements, which become choppy after traumatic brain injury. Smooth-pursuit exercises are also being studied as part of supranormal visual performance training. [01:09:52](transcript.md#010952-near-far-exercise--presbyopia-smooth-pursuit-exercise--concussion)

9. **2020 vision is average, not perfect — fighter pilots and athletes commonly achieve 20/10.** 20/20 means reading at 20 feet what the average healthy adult reads at 20 feet. Legal blindness in the US is 20/200 or worse. LASIK and other procedures frequently produce 20/15 or 20/10 vision. Raptors and hawks naturally possess ~20/8 or better. Visual acuity is highest in the center (fovea) and falls off markedly toward the periphery — the visual system essentially operates two cameras simultaneously. [01:19:11](transcript.md#011911-2020-vision-visual-acuity)

10. **Contact lenses carry real infection risk; hygiene is non-negotiable.** Sleeping in lenses dramatically increases bacterial and fungal keratitis risk; corneal scarring can leave permanent blurring even after the infection clears. Daily disposables carry the lowest risk. Never rinse lenses or cases with tap water. Tolerance shortens with age as tear film reserve declines. [01:24:51](transcript.md#012451-contact-lenses-use-risks--aging)

11. **UV light — not blue light — is the primary optical hazard causing cataracts.** Cumulative UV-A and UV-B exposure oxidatively clouds the crystalline lens over decades. Virtually all modern plastic eyeglass lenses already block UV. Blue-blocking lenses have no proven daytime benefit and may interfere with circadian entrainment. Wide-brimmed hats and UV-blocking sunglasses are the relevant protective tools. Transition lenses darken based on UV, not visible light — which is why they stay clear inside cars (car glass blocks UV). [01:31:34](transcript.md#013134-uv-protection--cataracts-blue-blockers)

12. **Light-colored eyes show greater light sensitivity and longer pupil dilation — a real biological difference.** Dilation drops last 4–8 hours in blue/hazel-eyed people vs. 1–4 hours in brown-eyed people. This likely explains why lighter-eyed individuals find bright light more uncomfortable and squint more readily. [01:38:20](transcript.md#013820-light-sensitivity--eye-color)

13. **Dry eye is most commonly driven by meibomian gland (oil-layer) dysfunction, not just tear deficiency.** Tears consist of a saltwater layer (lacrimal gland) and an essential oil layer (meibomian glands in eyelids). Both quantity and quality of each layer decline with age. Screen use reduces blink rate, impairing tear redistribution. The industry has solved for the aqueous layer (artificial tears) but not the oil layer. Serum tears — prepared from the patient's own blood serum diluted with saline — deliver growth factors that support ocular surface repair in resistant cases. Preservative-free drops can be used as often as needed. [01:46:26](transcript.md#014626-dry-eye-tears--age)

14. **Glaucoma is the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide — and is silent until late.** Glaucoma is an optic-nerve neurodegenerative disease driven primarily by elevated intraocular pressure. Patients have no symptoms until peripheral vision is severely compromised. Regular IOP measurement and optic nerve imaging after age 40 (or earlier with family history) enable early detection. Lowering IOP with drops, selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT), or surgery slows progression whether pressure starts high or in the normal range. The large LIGHT trial showed SLT was at least as effective as first-line drops and preferred by patients for adherence. Even with full treatment, 10–20% of patients lose meaningful functional vision. [02:22:17](transcript.md#022217-glaucoma-screening--treatment)

15. **Smoking is the most powerful modifiable risk factor for macular degeneration — and also harms glaucoma.** AMD's two major risk factors are age and smoking (including secondhand smoke). Smoking also worsens glaucoma by impairing blood supply to the optic nerve's watershed zone. Cannabis transiently lowers IOP but only for the duration of intoxication — too brief and impractical for sustained therapy; smoking cannabis additionally causes ocular surface inflammation and dries the eyes. Cardiovascular health behaviors (no smoking, blood pressure control, exercise, healthy diet) directly benefit the optic nerve. [02:28:07](transcript.md#002807-smoking-vaping--vision-diseases-cannabis--eye-pressure)

16. **Eye pressure follows a circadian rhythm, peaking at night — elevated sleep position can help severe glaucoma.** IOP is highest in the early morning hours and lowest during the early day. For patients with severe or treatment-resistant glaucoma, sleeping at roughly 30 degrees of head elevation can reduce nocturnal pressure spikes. Which eye faces down does not appear to matter — people naturally shift sides throughout the night. [02:35:13](transcript.md#023513-eye-pressure--sleep-position)

17. **Red-light therapy (670 nm) shows promise for offsetting age-related retinal decline.** Two published human studies from Glenn Jeffery's lab at University College London show that 2 minutes of low-intensity 670 nm red-light exposure a few times per week — done in the morning — improves color contrast sensitivity and inner retinal function in adults over 40. The mechanism is photobiomodulation of mitochondria in energy-hungry photoreceptors. Both red and violet light have also shown early evidence of slowing myopia progression in school-age children. Crucially, uncomfortably bright light of any wavelength can damage the eye — established safe protocols are essential. [02:37:48](transcript.md#023748-macular-degeneration-optic-neuropathies--red-light-therapy)

18. **AREDS2 supplements reduce AMD progression by ~25% in intermediate-to-advanced patients.** The AREDS2 formula (vitamin C 500 mg, vitamin E 400 IU, lutein 10 mg, zeaxanthin 2 mg, zinc 80 mg, copper 2 mg) outperformed the original AREDS formula (which included beta-carotene) and avoids a small elevated lung cancer risk in smokers. Not indicated as a general preventive supplement for people without AMD. [02:48:10](transcript.md#024810-areds2-supplementation--age-related-macular-degeneration)

19. **Vitamin B3 (nicotinamide) is an emerging neuroprotective treatment for glaucoma.** Two small but controlled RCTs showed high-dose nicotinamide (not nicotinic acid, to avoid flushing) is safe and improves inner retinal function measured by visual fields and electroretinogram in glaucoma patients. The mechanism is NAD+ pathway support in retinal ganglion cell metabolism. Large phase-3 trials are now underway worldwide. Dr. Goldberg is already recommending it for patients with glaucoma refractory to standard pressure-lowering treatments. [02:53:39](transcript.md#025339-glaucoma--vitamin-b3-supplementation)

20. **The retina is a non-invasive window into brain neurodegeneration.** Because the retina is CNS tissue, retinal thinning and optic nerve changes visible with standard clinical imaging correlate with Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and MS. Dr. Alfredo Dubra's adaptive optics retinal imaging achieves sub-cellular resolution. Collaborative work between Dubra and neuro-ophthalmologist Dr. Heather Moss has identified novel retinal structures specific to MS patients — a potential biomarker for distinguishing neurological diseases without brain imaging. [02:58:42](transcript.md#025842-retinal-imaging--neurodegeneration-screening-multiple-sclerosis)

## Tools & Protocols

### Outdoor Time for Myopia Prevention (Children)

- **What:** Substantial daily time outdoors in natural full-spectrum daylight to slow myopic progression.
- **How:** Aim for at least 1–2 hours per day outside. Outdoor light intensity — many times higher than indoor artificial lighting — triggers retinal dopamine release that suppresses axial elongation. Children can read outdoors; the key variable is the lighting environment, not the visual task alone.
- **When:** Throughout childhood and adolescence; most critical during elementary-school years when myopia initiates and progresses fastest.
- **Ref:** [00:20:38](transcript.md#002038-myopia-near-sightedness-children--sunlight)

### Eyelid Hygiene (Blepharitis / Dry Eye Prevention)

- **What:** Daily cleaning of eyelid margins to remove debris, reduce blepharitis, and maintain meibomian oil gland function.
- **How:** Add a small amount of No-More-Tears baby shampoo to palm, dilute with a little water, and with eyes gently (not tightly) closed, lightly rub the eyelashes and lash roots with a fingertip or the edge of a washcloth. Do not scrub. Over-the-counter eyelid scrub pads are an alternative.
- **When:** Once or twice daily, particularly if experiencing dry eye, gritty sensations, or eyelid irritation.
- **Ref:** [00:31:18](transcript.md#003118-eye-safety--protection-eye-hygiene)

### Near-Far (Pencil Pushup) Exercise

- **What:** A convergence exercise that trains eye muscles to maintain near focus and transition between near and far targets.
- **How:** Hold a pencil or finger at arm's length, focus on the tip, slowly move it toward the nose while maintaining single clear focus, then move it back out. Repeat for 10–25 repetitions. Alternatively, alternate fixation between a near target (~15 cm) and a far target across the room.
- **When:** Daily or several times per week. Specifically indicated for diagnosed convergence insufficiency and post-concussion visual rehabilitation; safe for general use.
- **Ref:** [01:09:52](transcript.md#010952-near-far-exercise--presbyopia-smooth-pursuit-exercise--concussion) | Video: https://youtu.be/ObtW353d5i0?t=4130

### Smooth Pursuit Eye Exercises

- **What:** Structured training of smooth, coordinated eye tracking used in concussion rehab and general visual performance training.
- **How:** Follow a moving target (ball, screen-based stimulus) with both eyes while keeping the head still. Dedicated YouTube channels provide standardized routines.
- **When:** Several times per week; especially important post-concussion or for athletes seeking improved visual performance. Not harmful for healthy eyes.
- **Ref:** [01:09:52](transcript.md#010952-near-far-exercise--presbyopia-smooth-pursuit-exercise--concussion) | Resource: https://www.youtube.com/c/VisualExercises/videos

### Contact Lens Hygiene Protocol

- **What:** Strict hygiene regimen to minimize infection risk from contact lens wear.
- **How:** Never sleep in lenses unless explicitly approved for extended-wear by your ophthalmologist. Never rinse lenses or cases with tap water. Use only sterile saline or approved lens solution. Replace lens cases regularly. Prefer daily disposables for the lowest infection risk.
- **When:** Every day of lens wear.
- **Ref:** [01:24:51](transcript.md#012451-contact-lenses-use-risks--aging)

### UV-Blocking Eyewear

- **What:** Sunglasses with UV-A and UV-B blocking to protect the crystalline lens and reduce long-term cataract risk.
- **How:** Wear UV-blocking sunglasses in significant outdoor sunlight — especially at high elevation or near reflective surfaces (snow, water). Look for lenses labeled UV400 or 100% UV protection. Wide-brimmed hats also reduce UV to the eyes and periocular skin. Note: most modern plastic eyeglass lenses already block UV by default.
- **When:** Lifelong habit; cumulative UV exposure matters. Most important during peak sun hours.
- **Ref:** [01:31:34](transcript.md#013134-uv-protection--cataracts-blue-blockers)

### Dry Eye Management — Warm Compresses + Preservative-Free Drops

- **What:** Treating meibomian gland dysfunction (most common cause of dry eye) with heat and appropriate lubrication.
- **How:** Apply a warm compress (clean warm washcloth or heated eye mask) to closed eyelids for 5–10 minutes to liquefy thickened meibomian gland secretions. Follow with preservative-free artificial tears — not multi-dose drops with preservatives, which can inflame the ocular surface with frequent use. Preservative-free drops come in single-use strips and can be used as often as needed throughout the day.
- **When:** Morning or evening daily; preservative-free drops throughout the day as needed.
- **Ref:** [01:53:24](transcript.md#015324-dry-eye-serum-tears--preservative-free-artificial-tears-prp)

### Elevated Sleep Position for Glaucoma

- **What:** Sleeping with the head slightly elevated to reduce nocturnal intraocular pressure spikes.
- **How:** Use a wedge pillow or adjustable bed to achieve roughly a 30-degree head-up angle. Which eye faces down does not matter clinically — people shift sides through the night. Prioritize sleep quality over position; if elevation disrupts sleep, the trade-off may not be worthwhile.
- **When:** Every night, for individuals with severe or treatment-resistant glaucoma.
- **Ref:** [02:35:13](transcript.md#023513-eye-pressure--sleep-position)

### Morning Red-Light Therapy (670 nm) for Retinal Mitochondrial Support

- **What:** Brief low-intensity 670 nm (deep red) light exposure to the eyes to boost mitochondrial ATP production in photoreceptors, potentially offsetting age-related retinal decline.
- **How:** Use a 670 nm red-light source positioned approximately 2 feet (60 cm) from the eyes. Look toward (not directly into) the light for approximately 2 minutes. Light intensity must not be uncomfortably bright.
- **When:** Morning, a few times per week. Avoid late-day exposure as photoreceptors are more sensitive then. Most relevant for adults over 40.
- **Ref:** [02:37:48](transcript.md#023748-macular-degeneration-optic-neuropathies--red-light-therapy) | Study: https://go.nature.com/46jzN5P

### AREDS2 Supplementation for AMD

- **What:** The Age-Related Eye Disease Study 2 (AREDS2) formula, clinically proven to reduce AMD progression.
- **How:** Take the standard AREDS2 formula daily: Vitamin C (500 mg), Vitamin E (400 IU), Lutein (10 mg), Zeaxanthin (2 mg), Zinc (80 mg), Copper (2 mg). Use formulations that substitute lutein/zeaxanthin for beta-carotene (original AREDS) to avoid elevated lung cancer risk in smokers.
- **When:** Daily, ongoing. Indicated for those diagnosed with intermediate or advanced AMD in one eye — not a general preventive supplement for people without AMD.
- **Ref:** [02:48:10](transcript.md#024810-areds2-supplementation--age-related-macular-degeneration) | Info: https://bit.ly/3NKIFdC

### Nicotinamide (Vitamin B3) for Glaucoma Neuroprotection

- **What:** Oral nicotinamide (niacinamide) supplementation to support NAD+ pathway and protect retinal ganglion cells in glaucoma.
- **How:** Use nicotinamide (not nicotinic acid/niacin) to avoid flushing side effects. Dosing is under active clinical investigation — discuss with your ophthalmologist.
- **When:** Daily, under ophthalmologist guidance, for those with glaucoma or optic neuropathy — particularly when standard pressure-lowering treatments are insufficient.
- **Ref:** [02:53:39](transcript.md#025339-glaucoma--vitamin-b3-supplementation) | Trial: https://bit.ly/3r3bGbB | Study: https://bit.ly/3peYWOB
