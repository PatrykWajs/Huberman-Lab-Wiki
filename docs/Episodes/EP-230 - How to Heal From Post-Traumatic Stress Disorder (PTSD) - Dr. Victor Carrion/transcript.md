## [00:06:19] Stress, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), Avoidance

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to talk today about PTSD — post-traumatic stress disorder — in particular in young people but also in adults. Before we do that, can you educate us on the definition of stress, maybe distinguish between short-term and long-term stress, and then perhaps we can segue into PTSD?

**Victor Carrion:** That's a very good way of starting, because in reality my main interest was the role of stress and stressors and how stressors would activate the gene makeup and make us vulnerable to things we might be vulnerable to. But at the time when I was training, psychiatry as a field was very diagnosis-based — so you needed an anchor, and hence I use PTSD to communicate what I was really referring to.

The reality is that the experience of stress, as we now know, is a spectrum from beneficial to not beneficial to traumatic. Stress operates in our lives as an inverted U-shaped curve. The more stress we have, the better we perform — up to a point. If we don't care about that exam we're going to have tomorrow, we'll probably fail. Vaccines are a stress in the system. I'm very concerned also about the overprotection of kids from any type of stress, because it is through the experience of early stress that we develop our problem-solving abilities and become aware of our coping mechanisms.

We can manage stress because, in the same way that through homeostasis we have a range of temperatures in which we can live, the same thing with stress — we can actually cope up to a certain point. After a certain point it's not homeostasis anymore; it turns into what we call allostasis, when it really starts having a physiological cost to the body. In that inverted U-shaped curve there's an optimal point where your health, happiness, and performance are better because of the stress you've been having. But after that optimal point, all of those outcomes start to decline — and it is in that second part of the curve where we find traumatic stress.

Traumatic stress is a type of stress that not only is something you have to cope with but actually puts your physical integrity in jeopardy. When you experience traumatic stress, many outcomes are possible. One is that you're resilient. Another is that you may develop symptoms of post-traumatic stress disorder. And of course we can come out of PTSD and return to that optimal point — we don't want to get rid of stress, we just want to return to that optimal point.

Treatment is available and people can recover from PTSD. But there's one thing that really gets in the way — something that in my team we say: "PTSD feeds on avoidance." If we pretend that something didn't happen, if we pretend it will go away, if we pretend that treatment is not necessary — that's when it gets complicated. It gets complicated with substance abuse, it gets complicated with self-injurious behaviors, and then at that point it becomes harder to treat.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is it also possible that PTSD gets worse if we tend to look at it over and over again — ruminate on it — in the absence of any structured clinical support? If people perseverate on their traumas, can the negative impact of those traumas actually root deeper?

**Victor Carrion:** It's interesting that you use the word perseverate, because one of the characteristics of trauma when it affects children is that it robs them from play. Play is something that's essential in development — it's how we grow socially, emotionally, physically. But when play becomes traumatic play, it becomes non-joyful but it becomes perseverative and repetitive. This is the attempt of the individual to try to make sense of what happened.

The reason why it's not good to be alone with it and perseverate on it by oneself is that we're probably not looking at the right insult. In our experience, usually PTSD doesn't result from that one traumatic event. We all carry a backpack and we can all carry all the stressors that come our way, but if you're five, six, or seven years old and that backpack gets really heavy, you can fall backwards — and that's because you don't have the tools to carry it. It is the accumulation of stressors, some of which may be traumatic, that causes the symptoms of PTSD.

## [00:11:41] Stressors, Perseverate; Children & PTSD

**Andrew Huberman:** I get the impression that PTSD can be caused by a single event or trauma but that there's a cumulative aspect to it. Is it the case that in children, because their brain is far more plastic, it takes far fewer or less intense negative experiences in order to create PTSD?

**Victor Carrion:** Epidemiological studies confirm that assertion. Children are more vulnerable. A line that I really don't like is "children are resilient" — because children really are not. They're more vulnerable. They have the opportunity to become resilient if we help them and tell them what tools to use and how to develop, but they are more vulnerable to PTSD. Part of it might be that neuroplasticity — and this is why we care for them, why we protect them.

By the same token, that neuroplasticity can work both ways. Because if PTSD is teaching us that the environment can have an impact on biology — that's the only lesson — and in PTSD it is a negative impact because of a negative stressor or accumulation of stressors. But that also means that if the impact is positive, as in a good supportive system or psychotherapy, recovery can actually happen in an easier way.

## [00:16:13] Transgenerational Trauma

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a topic that comes up a lot anytime the letters PTSD are stated in that order — transgenerational trauma. I can imagine at least two forms: one is a narrative passage where discussion of trauma passes through generations and impacts children; the other is this idea that somehow the genome is modified by the trauma such that even if kids are raised by parents who adopted them and have no contact with grandparents who experienced the trauma, they are somehow more vulnerable. What are your thoughts about the transgenerational passage of trauma, both forms?

**Victor Carrion:** The jury is still out on whether genomic changes that result as a consequence of stress can be passed from one generation to the other. But certainly the genes that made one generation vulnerable are being passed to the next generation as well — that we know. There's also the impact of learning. I have treated kids who come to me with all of the symptoms of PTSD and there's no trauma I can find — and the parent cannot find the trauma, and the kid doesn't report trauma. But when I'm talking with the parent, the trauma becomes evident in the history of the parent. The parent has developed PTSD and behaves in a way that has been learned by the new generation — ways like avoidance, re-experience, hypervigilance, or lack of trust.

We know that the battle between nature and nurture is pretty much over — they both influence vulnerability and they both interact. Stress as both a response within the brain and a response within the body: adrenaline and epinephrine are released from the adrenals but also from areas of the brain like the locus coeruleus. There's a parallel effect of elevated states of mind — more alert, more focused — and the body is also prepared for action.

## [00:19:20] Post-Traumatic Stress Injury (PTSI); Children, Dissociation & Cortisol

**Andrew Huberman:** With PTSD, I often hear that some of the symptoms are more of the opposite end of the spectrum in terms of autonomic arousal — things like dissociation, fatigue, checking out. Can both sets of phenotypes exist in the same person?

**Victor Carrion:** A lot of people call post-traumatic stress disorder "post-traumatic stress injury" — not considering it a disorder but considering it something where our fight-or-flight mechanism, the autonomic nervous system, has been injured and we need to regulate it again. It's going to hurt, it's going to be painful — just like when you break your arm and go to the emergency room. A lot of people visualize it as an injury rather than a disorder.

What happens to a young kid is that they're very little and they cannot fight, and they're also very dependent and they cannot flee. So they're stuck. They freeze, and that's dissociation. During development, dissociation is actually a healthy defense mechanism — but very much like a white blood cell, if you have too much of it you can develop dissociative disorders. It does help children cope with some situations.

Because this arousal system is so key in the development of these issues in children, I thought we should look at the hormone cortisol in kids. When I was a fellow doing my child psychiatry fellowship, I was seeing all types of kids with all kinds of issues — some had ADHD, some had OCD, some had PTSD symptoms. But I was getting a lot of kids with notes from school saying "this kid has ADHD, please place on Ritalin." In most cases, what happened was that the hypervigilance was being misinterpreted as hyperactivity, and the dissociation was being misinterpreted as inattentiveness.

## [00:27:17] Cortisol & Brain, Post-Traumatic Stress Symptoms

**Andrew Huberman:** Cortisol starts to rise just before we wake up in the morning, assuming a good night's sleep, and peaks maybe 30 to 90 minutes after waking. The pattern then is that it rises through mid-morning, into early afternoon, then starts to taper off. In kids with PTSD, cortisol doesn't come down to low levels as much as it does in healthy individuals in the evening and nighttime?

**Victor Carrion:** That's correct. We did a number of studies demonstrating that the normal circadian rhythmicity of cortisol was there — it was higher early in the morning, and as the day progresses it decreases. But the bedtime level wouldn't come as low as the healthy controls — it would remain high. This was important clinically because many of the symptoms these kids were having were happening at night: bed-wetting, nightmares, not sleeping deep enough, not sleeping long enough.

We were concerned about the work by Sapolsky, Bruce McEwen, and his mentor demonstrating the neurotoxicity that glucocorticoids can have in key areas of the brain — areas in the limbic system and the cortical system, which interestingly enough have a lot of glucocorticoid receptors. The glucocorticoid receptors and cortisol are more common in areas like the hippocampus and the prefrontal cortex. These areas relate to the symptoms that many individuals with PTSD have — memory, anticipation of the future, problem solving, context-dependent problem solving, and even those attention issues that make them overlap with kids who have ADHD.

## [00:33:19] PTSD, Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

**Andrew Huberman:** What percentage of pure ADHD diagnoses would you like to see explored for the possibility of a PTSD influence?

**Victor Carrion:** I firmly believe that ADHD does exist. Let me say two facts that we know in the field: one is that kids are getting overmedicated — they're getting more medications than they need for ADHD and for anything in general. But within those kids, those that truly have attention deficit hyperactivity disorder are getting undermedicated because of access issues. Most of them we're not identifying.

The first line of intervention for ADHD is stimulant treatment — it works very well for children who have the correct diagnosis. But the first line of intervention for children that have a history of PTSD — be it acute or chronic — is psychosocial. It's a psychosocial intervention. If you give a kid who has PTSD and no ADHD a stimulant medication, not only is it not taking care of ADHD because they don't have it, but it adds to that hyperarousability.

## [00:40:17] PTSD & ADHD; Identifying Cues, Triggers & Interventions

**Victor Carrion:** There are clinical ways of separating hyperactivity from hyperarousability and hypervigilance. Hyperactivity, if you see a kid that is not medicated and has ADHD with hyperactive symptoms, they're going to be hyperactive for most of the time you're with them. The kid who has hyperarousability — it will be more of an on-and-off phenomenon. The hypervigilance and hyperarousability come more when they're presented with a cue that consciously or unconsciously reminds their body of the traumatic event or experiences.

The problem is not the hypervigilance; the problem is knowing when to turn it on and when to turn it off — having the cognitive flexibility to say, yes, this is a dangerous situation and I better respond this way. We need to help kids develop new competitive responses so that the experience of the other responses then extinguishes the response that was adaptive at one point but now it's maladaptive.

Cues are usually neutral and they're usually related to our senses — what we see, what we hear. All of these things — the senses are really the window to the central nervous system. In this state of hyperarousability, when something traumatizing is happening, our senses are really acutely aware of what's going on, registering everything related to that moment. So these cues usually are neutral — not a gun, which is a threat, but a color, a sound. It was raining the day that happened — so rain may be a trigger.

Identifying those cues is important because they let you know when your symptoms are coming. They let you know that symptoms are not coming out of nowhere, that you're not a problem or crazy or bad — which is sometimes the message kids get when they go to the principal's office. They let you know this is a normal response: I've learned through my psychosocial intervention that this is a cue that triggers a response that was helpful at one time and through classical conditioning has become conditioned.

## [00:47:49] PTSI, Autonomic Nervous System Seesaw; Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** I like the term post-traumatic stress injury — PTSI — because if we understand that the autonomic nervous system, the seesaw back and forth between the sympathetic fight-or-flight and parasympathetic rest-and-digest systems, are always at play in us — then PTSI liberates us a bit. The injury is a tightening of the hinge with the seesaw tilted too much to one or the other side.

**Victor Carrion:** Yes — and I like the visualization of your seesaw and the hinge. It reminds me of that cognitive flexibility — it's not there, it's kind of stuck, it's too tight. And in some individuals they just experience the dissociation — they're like stuck on the bottom of the seesaw — whereas for other individuals they're hyperaroused all the time. A good night's sleep allows some recalibration of the tightness of that hinge. Anytime we don't sleep well or long enough, we're not good psychologically.

I really have to tip my hat to Dr. Matthew Walker from UC Berkeley for writing the book "Why We Sleep" — he had to come out with kind of the downer message: "Listen, you know, this is serious stuff, you better sleep." But I think we're there now in 2024 — people understand.

## [00:53:11] PTSD, Brain Development & Kids; Cue-Centered Therapy

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about some of the treatments you use and have developed for PTSD in young people.

**Victor Carrion:** In pediatric psychiatry we have three different populations: the preschoolers — zero to about five or six years old — the school-age kids from about six until end of adolescence, and then the teenagers. The projects I'm describing happen mostly with the school-age children.

When we started doing magnetic resonance imaging to look at the impact of cortisol, we had a number of studies demonstrating that kids with higher levels of cortisol had less volume of the hippocampus. The first cross-sectional study showed no difference — which gave me a lot of hope that there would be a window of opportunity to intervene. We also followed a small sample longitudinally, and there we saw a correlation between that higher pre-bedtime cortisol and smaller hippocampal volume. In a functional imaging study, we saw that healthy kids were activating more voxels of the hippocampus; there was concern that neuroplasticity was really affecting brain development.

In the prefrontal cortex, we saw something in the ventral medial area. The amygdala becomes active very quickly when you present emotional faces to young kids — and that hyperactive amygdala needs a brake of some sort. That brake comes from the prefrontal cortex. But if you have a prefrontal cortex that's not working well, then your brake is not working.

So the issue came: this is important information to know what to target with treatment. Can we target this with psychosocial interventions? We decided to begin with the cues — teaching kids to understand what cues are, what classical conditioning is, talking to them about the impact of trauma, talking to them about how recovery is possible. And something that I never thought I would end up doing was developing a treatment. Most treatments out there for trauma were targeting one traumatic event and not targeting that backpack — that allostatic load. They were also requiring that the parents were involved in treatment. I can see where that might be problematic when the parents are perhaps the source of the trauma, or when there's avoidance, or practical limitations like loss of work.

So how can we devise a treatment that is hybrid and multimodal — not only cognitive behavioral therapy, but that brings other elements like self-efficacy, empowerment, insight-oriented work, and gives it a structure that can be tested? That's how we created cue-centered therapy — CCT — for kids who have PTSD. We've had a number of trials, and it helps decrease symptoms of anxiety, depression, and PTSD — as scored not only by the student but also by observers, by the parents. In one trial where we measured how the parents were doing, parents who were not participating in treatment — their own anxiety was decreasing as well.

We brought in functional near-infrared spectroscopy — it's cheaper than MRI, more portable, and easier to administer. The prefrontal cortical areas were helping predict which kids would do better — only for those kids having cue-centered therapy compared to treatment as usual.

## [01:04:25] Limbic Pathway, Inner Dialogue, Therapy Toolbox

**Andrew Huberman:** You said the frontolimbic pathway is important. The limbic pathway creates a response state — alertness or relaxation — and translates information into a level of reactivity. The frontal cortex is the pathway by which an internal dialogue could be delivered to quiet that limbic pathway. The message that would trigger it would be the kid recognizing — because they learned through therapy — "this is okay, I've had this happen before, it passes, or I'm supported." These internal dialogues need to be practiced?

**Victor Carrion:** All of those examples are positive thoughts — and they're not automatic thoughts, they are thoughts that need to be practiced. Negative thoughts that reside in our reptile brain are automatic. The hyper-danger type of response, when we evolved, is responsible for our survival. Only 50 million years ago, when we developed the frontal cortex more, positive thoughts came into the picture. They're very helpful, but they're not automatic like the negative ones are. Hopefully they will become so.

What I tell the kids: if I give you a guitar right now, would you be able to play me a song? Absolutely not. But if you had guitar lessons and practiced, you probably will be able to play a song a year from now. We have a slogan: "Practice positive thoughts." In cue-centered therapy, one of the lessons is that they have an empty toolbox, and this toolbox gets filled with tools that they learn — practicing positive thoughts, deep breathing, mindfulness, muscle relaxation. But they decide what the cues are; they decide what tools they're going to put into their toolbox. By far, whatever tools they develop that have not been taught by me or anybody else work better — when they develop it themselves.

I had a case where a girl came in the next week and was much better. I was very proud — I thought, oh, she's been practicing the tools we discussed. She said: "No, I actually don't remember anything you said last time, but I came up with this thing: when I feel bad I'm drinking a glass of orange juice." At that moment I could go both ways — I could say no, you must practice my tools, or I could say, how wonderful, you've identified a tool that helps you. Obviously I did the latter. What I think is happening with the orange juice is nothing about the glass or the vitamin C — it's about the fact that she has sent a message to herself: "I can take care of myself." The best tool that she has is herself. Whatever these kids go in the future, there's something that's always going to be there with them — which is themselves.

## [01:12:34] Agency & Control, Deliberate Cold Exposure, Narrative

**Andrew Huberman:** There's the classic anecdote about the patient who has anxiety attacks whose psychiatrist gives them a couple of pills. They decide to keep those pills in their pocket, and knowing they have those pills allows them to control their anxiety.

**Victor Carrion:** It gives them a sense of control. And that decision they're making is what gives them a sense of control — that's important.

**Victor Carrion:** I like that you're getting to the hinge of that seesaw. The cold shower or deliberate cold exposure probably loosens the hinge. ## [01:18:11] Custom Toolbox Development; Energy

**Andrew Huberman:** The toolbox is very effective, can be customized, maybe even covert — we can have these tools inside us and don't need to share them with anybody. That seems like a compelling reason for exploring the toolbox approach.

**Victor Carrion:** The exercise of the events — a lifeline — is done separate from the toolbox. We actually work on the toolbox first to identify coping mechanisms and tools. So what would that look like? The first thing I ask: when you're feeling agitated, anxious, nervous — is there anything that makes you feel better? Talking to friends, planning a sleepover, listening to music.

Also, very simple yoga exercises — things like the mountain pose can be quite helpful for some kids. It helps them reassess the moment and stop, breaking that chain of negative thoughts that happen one after another.

## [01:26:32] Tool: 4-Corner Square Response, Understanding Cues

**Andrew Huberman:** You described the four-quadrant system — could you share with us this four-corner system?

**Victor Carrion:** We also add in cue-centered therapy an examination of a response by looking at a square composed of four corners. The four corners are: what you're thinking (the cognitive side), what you're feeling emotionally, what you're feeling physically (the somatic component), and what your action is. This is your classical triangle of cognitive behavioral therapy — what you're thinking, what you're doing, how you're feeling — but we felt it was important to add that somatic, physiological component. Many children don't have the vocabulary to talk about all of this; they just tell you, "I have a headache" or "I have a stomachache."

Depending on the kid that comes, you're going to start examining their response through one of those corners. If the kid is really brainy and likes to think, you start in the cognitive corner. Other kids are very attentive to their body and say, "I feel my heart racing when I engage in this response." You start with that corner. The beauty is that most of the time you don't have to work in all of the corners — by just working in one corner, all the other corners change and a new response develops. If the kid is thinking, "I'm not in danger," maybe they don't need to leave running — maybe they can just tell the teacher they're distressed by the amount of noise. The kid has created a new square. Hopefully we take that one response as a square and build a cube of many potential responses. If I'm too distressed to think which response to use, I can bring myself there by using my toolbox. It all starts tying together.

## [01:32:59] Tool: "Creating Space," Feelings Thermometer, Analyzing 4-Corners

**Andrew Huberman:** What you're describing is the ability to become responsive as opposed to reactive — responsive implies some optionality. This four-corner system gives us an anchor point to start from. It describes what the mindfulness arena calls "creating space" — creating space within us to choose better options.

**Victor Carrion:** And I like that you're not calling it nervous energy — it is just energy. It's extra energy that needs to be placed somewhere. Some kids that are not psychologically minded don't even want to engage with this — they say, okay, what is it that I'm doing? I'll do something different. They'll immediately develop the next square, changing the action first, which changes all the others.

We use the feelings thermometer. If the kid is at 10 on the thermometer at that moment, the best thing is to use a tool from the toolbox — not to engage on the square at that moment, until they come down a little bit. Then when they're at maybe 5, 4, 3, they can engage with the square exercise. The toolbox should be used under essentially any conditions. The square can be used when they are at a slightly lower level of stress because it requires a certain level of cognitive intervention.

## [01:46:07] School, Yoga & Mindfulness Curriculum

**Andrew Huberman:** You've been developing yoga and mindfulness curricula for schools.

**Victor Carrion:** Yes. Our work in prevention started because we wanted to see if we could build resilience before PTSD happened, and we were interested in testing mindfulness in kids. There is evidence that mindfulness can be helpful in kids. We started developing a curriculum that combines yoga and mindfulness and takes it to classrooms. When we talk about yoga, we're talking about very simple poses like the mountain pose — things that even I can do. When we talk about mindfulness, we're talking about very short exercises, maybe one to two minutes, that kids can do in the classroom.

The curriculum has shown promising results in terms of improving stress resilience, mood, and sleep. We've been particularly interested in taking this to under-resourced schools, where kids have been exposed to significant stressors and don't have access to the same resources. We have developed modules for different grades, and we've trained teachers and other school personnel to administer them because we know that access to mental health services for kids is very limited.

The sleep component has been particularly interesting. We know that poor sleep exacerbates PTSD symptoms. When we teach these kids these simple mindfulness exercises before bedtime, we're seeing improvements in sleep quality. The data also suggest that these interventions can impact the stress biomarkers we were discussing earlier — cortisol levels.

## [01:55:31] Implementing School Mindfulness Programs, Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you actually implement these programs in schools? What does a teacher need to do?

**Victor Carrion:** We've been training teachers extensively. The curriculum is manual-based so that it can be implemented with fidelity. Teachers receive training, they receive support, and they have a manual that guides them through the sessions. We have a consultant who can be reached if they have questions. We've also developed videos that demonstrate the yoga poses and mindfulness exercises. The goal is to make it as easy as possible for the teacher to implement.

We've also been thinking about technology and how to use apps to deliver some of these interventions. The short mindfulness exercises that we have are perfectly suited for apps. Sleep is a big component of this — we are doing some work now where we're looking at sleep as a mechanism by which the intervention is working. When kids sleep better, they do better at school, they have better mood, they are more attentive, and they're less symptomatic.

## [02:00:52] Barriers to School Programs

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the barriers to implementing these programs more broadly?

**Victor Carrion:** The barriers are many. First, there's the stigma around mental health in schools. Second, there's the concern from teachers about adding more to their plate — they're already overwhelmed. Third, there's the issue of buy-in from administrators and parents. Fourth, there's the financial barrier — these programs need funding. Fifth, there's the need for training. And sixth, there's the problem of sustainability — how do we make sure these programs are maintained over time and not just a one-time thing?

We've been trying to address these barriers by making the programs as easy to implement as possible, by providing ongoing support, and by demonstrating the effectiveness of these programs with data. We've also been working with local communities and organizations to find funding. And we've been partnering with schools that are committed to this work and understand the importance of mental health in education.

## [02:06:08] Redefining Success, Identity

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you think about the relationship between PTSD and identity — how a child defines themselves?

**Victor Carrion:** This is a very important issue. Many of the kids that I see have incorporated the trauma into their identity. They see themselves as victims, as broken, as damaged goods. Part of the work of cue-centered therapy is to help them redefine their identity — not as victims but as survivors. Not as broken but as resilient individuals who have been through something very difficult and have found ways to cope.

We also work on redefining success. Success is not just about academic achievement — it's about developing coping skills, building relationships, finding ways to contribute to your community. When kids see themselves in a broader context and understand that their experiences, while painful, have also given them strengths and insights, they begin to see themselves differently.

## [02:10:33] Resilience & Adaptation; Organoids, Epigenetic Treatment Response

**Andrew Huberman:** You've been doing some fascinating work with organoids. Can you explain what organoids are and how they relate to PTSD research?

**Victor Carrion:** Organoids are three-dimensional structures derived from stem cells that can mimic the architecture and function of real organs — in our case, brain organoids. What's fascinating is that we can take cells from individuals who have PTSD, reprogram them into stem cells, and then grow them into brain organoids. This allows us to study the biology of PTSD in a way that wasn't possible before.

What we're finding is that these organoids from individuals with PTSD behave differently from organoids from healthy controls — they show differences in gene expression, in the way cells migrate and organize, in the way they respond to stressors. And what's really exciting is that we're starting to see that when we expose these organoids to the equivalent of psychotherapy — the molecules that are released during effective therapy — they respond in ways that parallel what we see clinically.

This is important because it suggests that psychotherapy is not just changing thoughts and behaviors — it's actually changing biology. And because we can look at the organoid from each individual person, we're beginning to understand why some people respond to one treatment and not another. This is the future of precision medicine in psychiatry — matching treatments to the individual biology of the patient.

We're also looking at epigenetic changes — changes in how genes are expressed without changing the underlying DNA sequence. We're finding that trauma leaves epigenetic marks, and that effective treatment can reverse some of these marks. This is one of the most exciting areas of research in the field.

## [02:21:42] Listening to Kids & Adults

**Andrew Huberman:** What advice would you give to parents, teachers, and clinicians about listening to kids who may be experiencing PTSD?

**Victor Carrion:** The most important thing is to listen without judgment and without an agenda. Kids know when adults are listening to hear what they want to hear versus actually listening to understand them. Create a safe space where the child knows they can say whatever they're feeling without being punished or dismissed.

Validate their experience. You don't have to agree with everything they say, but you can acknowledge that their experience is real and their feelings are valid. Ask open-ended questions — "tell me more about that" — rather than yes-or-no questions. And most importantly, let them lead. Children and adults alike are much more likely to engage in treatment and to recover when they feel that they have agency, that they have a voice, that their perspective is being taken seriously.

The same advice applies to adults. Too often we dismiss what adults are feeling as weakness or as an inability to cope. But if we understood that they are responding to a physiological injury — that their autonomic system has been injured — we would treat them with much more compassion and much more effectively.
