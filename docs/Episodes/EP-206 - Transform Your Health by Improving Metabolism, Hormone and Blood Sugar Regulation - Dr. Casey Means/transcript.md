## [00:14:17] Trifecta of Bad Energy

**Andrew Huberman:** Most people when they hear metabolism think of energy, and when they think of energy they think about mitochondria. You've talked about the trifecta — the big three: mitochondria, inflammation, and oxidative stress — as three critical factors underlying metabolic dysfunction. Could you get us all on the same page about what mitochondria are and what they do, why they're so important, and then we can turn to inflammation and oxidative stress?

**Casey Means:** Mitochondria are structures within the cells — we have 40 trillion cells and each cell might have a handful or many thousands of mitochondria. They are the magical part of the cell that does that conversion process: breaking down food and then converting it to a currency of energy our body can recognize. What's currently happening is that our environment — across every single facet of our life over the past 50 to 75 years — has changed rapidly. How our food system has changed from whole real food in good soil to industrial food. Our sleep habits have changed — we're sleeping less, it's very fragmented. Our movement patterns have changed — we're sitting 80% of the waking day. Our time in nature has changed — Americans are indoors 93% of a 24-hour period. We have 80,000 synthetic toxins in our food, water, air, personal care products, and home care products. We live in a thermoneutral existence now with our thermostats. Our emotional health is very different — we are exposed to low-grade chronic stress triggers.

**Casey Means:** The unique thing about all of these changes in our environment is that each one of those pillars synergistically and directly hurts the mitochondria through different mechanisms. The chronic low-grade stress, the sitting, the ultra-processed nutrient-devoid food, even blue light at nighttime — all of it through different core biologic mechanisms synergistically hits this part of the cell, the mitochondria. So we have the food coming in but we're not doing a good job of converting it to an energy form we can use. We have these underpowered, dysfunctional cells because of mitochondrial dysfunction caused by our environment. And of course, if you've got energy substrate that you can't process, it's going to be stored — that's why we have an obesity and overweight epidemic affecting close to 80% of Americans. Obesity is one branch of a tree that's rooted in this mitochondrial dysfunction that's caused by our environment.

**Casey Means:** I had trained as an ENT surgeon at Stanford Medical School and I was so fascinated by this idea of how we're defining the diseases we're treating in these specialties. I saw sinusitis day in and day out — we'd have these patients on the table that we'd be literally drilling into their skulls to suck out sinus pus. When you actually go to the science and look at what is actually happening to create sinusitis, you find a lot of papers about mitochondrial dysfunction. Sinusitis is a chronic inflammatory condition where the cells are essentially sensing some threat and then mounting an immune response that creates swelling and pus buildup. But we confuse the pus buildup with the disease, which is actually happening inside the cells.

**Casey Means:** This gets to your question about chronic inflammation and oxidative stress. When you've got a dysfunctional mitochondria — let's say in nasal mucosal tissue — that is a cell that can't do its job, that is an underpowered cell. Those cells will initiate a process called the cell danger response — work done by Robert Naviaux at UCSD — where the mitochondria understands it's not working properly and the cell releases extracellular ATP, which is not really supposed to go outside the cell. That creates a massive innate immune response saying "I'm underpowered, I need help, my mitochondria is broken." But the immune system comes and there's not much it can do, because the problem is not something the immune system can help with — the problem is outside the body, it's the environment. You end up with this tornado of dysfunction: mitochondrial dysfunction caused by the environment, leading to the cell danger response, which leads to innate immunity, and then on top of this you get oxidative stress — the third piece of the trifecta — which is essentially these mitochondria trying so hard to do their work but creating damaging metabolic byproducts called free radicals that cause more damage.

**Casey Means:** To really make it simple: the trifecta of bad energy underlying metabolic dysfunction — chronic inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and oxidative stress — is like chronic inflammation is biochemical fear, it's a response to a threat; mitochondrial dysfunction is like rolling blackouts, not enough power; and oxidative stress is like wildfires. What's funny is that what's happening inside the cell is almost like what's happening in our society — it's like living in California: blackouts, fires, fear. And the optimistic news is that we actually have tons of tools and tests that can actually help us understand our level of metabolic health and it's very easy to improve if we know what we're actually focused on.

## [00:27:57] Insulin Resistance, Tool: Mitochondrial Capacity & Exercise

**Andrew Huberman:** You've talked about underpowered cells, yet most people are consuming enough and probably excess amounts of caloric energy. The analogy I come up with is a power plant that can convert incoming power-line energy to power that can be distributed to a community — there's plenty of potential energy, but something about the power plant is inefficient or dysfunctional. It's not a lack of input — it's a lack of conversion and output. What is it that would improve access to energy and energy utilization in cells? Is it simply getting the mitochondria to function better, or can one actually create more mitochondria?

**Casey Means:** This is the key question for how we can get healthy — it's really about creating capacity in the body to turn more potential energy to usable energy, as opposed to taking that potential energy and shunting it to a storage form. Fundamentally, this is the cause of insulin resistance. When the body senses that the mitochondria can't do that conversion process, the cell basically says: because I can't convert this, I'm going to take the substrates and block them — turn them to basically a storage form, which inside the cell could be toxic fats like ceramides and diacylglycerol. I'm also going to block the cell from taking any more substrates because it can't do anything with them. That fundamentally is a root of insulin resistance. The body says we're going to block the ability of the insulin receptor to transduce its intracellular signaling pathway, so you don't get the GLUT4 receptors on the cell membrane to allow the glucose to come in. So insulin resistance is the cell compensating for the mitochondria being broken.

**Casey Means:** To answer your question: what we really want to do to increase our metabolic capacity from a first-principles perspective is to make more mitochondria, get each mitochondria to be more functional, and have each more functional mitochondria processing more energy substrates. This means promoting mitophagy — the recycling of old mitochondria to new mitochondria — it means promoting mitochondrial biogenesis, increasing the oxidative capacity of individual mitochondria, and improving mitochondrial fusion, which is when mitochondria come together to form long chains that are more efficient. For mitophagy, you can focus on various types of endurance exercise and high-intensity interval exercise. For improving oxidative capacity, this is things like sprint workouts. To build more mitochondria, resistance training literally tells the body to make more. And then you look at the data on walking — people who walk more than 7,000 steps a day have a 50 to 65% lower chance of dying in 10-year follow-ups — because walking is literally a glucose disposal signal.

## [00:35:03] Tools: Walking & Glucose; Frequent Movement

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's touch on some lifestyle factors since we talked about dysfunctional dark side for a bit. Walking first — you said 7,000 steps a day. What are we really talking about there?

**Casey Means:** If walking were a pill, it would be the most impactful pill we've ever had in all of modern medicine. There was a paper in JAMA — 6,300 participants followed for 10 to 11 years — where people who simply walked 7,000 steps per day compared to less than that had up to a 70% lower risk of all-cause mortality in the follow-up period. Follow-up research with slightly different numbers showed that 8,000 to 12,000 steps per day was associated with a 50 to 65% lower all-cause mortality. This has been played out in many many studies showing about a 50% reduction in Alzheimer's, dementia, obesity, type 2 diabetes, depression, cancer, gastric reflux — just all across the board. And I think the key thing is that it's not about the steps, it's about the fact that muscle contraction is medicine. When we contract our muscles even in a very low-grade way like walking or doing a couple of air squats, we're activating AMPK and essentially causing that cell to have a stimulus to push glucose channels to the cell membrane. Most of the time the glucose channels are in little vesicles inside the cells — they're not on the cell membrane — so of course that's going to keep glucose in the bloodstream, not being processed by the mitochondria.

**Casey Means:** So let's take two people: a person walking for one to two minutes every 30 minutes throughout the day — that person is stimulating glucose channels to be at the membrane all day. Now let's take another person who works out really hard for one hour but then sits the entire rest of the day. Yes, they've gotten the benefits from the exercise, but for a lot of that day those glucose channels are inside the cell not doing the work they could be doing. There have been studies that took three groups — walking 20 minutes before each meal, 20 minutes after each meal, or two to three minutes every 30 minutes throughout the day — all adding up to 60 minutes of walking. The groups that do the short movement regularly throughout the day, even though the total time is the same, have significantly lower 24-hour glucose level averages and 24-hour insulin level averages. They are metabolically healthier. I think the concept of exercise that we're very wedded to in our Western culture, looking at blue zones and centenarians — they're kind of moving in ways built into their everyday life.

**Andrew Huberman:** I have heard that a short walk after a meal will reduce blood glucose in a really dramatic way.

**Casey Means:** Huge — 30, 35%. Just taking a walk around the block after a meal is definitely a prescription because the research is so strong on it. Simply a 10-minute walk or a dance party in the kitchen — moving your muscles for 10 minutes after a meal — can drastically reduce your glucose response because you're just bringing all those channels to the membrane. You're taking up the glucose, you're using it. It's a whole different physiology than sitting on the couch after a meal. That's very high impact, very high leverage if it's done after a meal. Our gym memberships in the US have doubled since the year 2000 and obesity has gone up in the same period — there's some mismatch between our obsession with exercise and our actual outcomes, and I think it's because we have not actually rebuilt constitutive movement into our daily lives.

## [00:58:21] Tool: Blood Test Biomarkers, Vital Signs & Mitochondrial Function

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about blood tests. What do you think are the three to five things that basically everybody, if they can, should know about what's circulating in their blood?

**Casey Means:** Every single person listening I hope will after this episode go to their health record or send their doctor a message and at least get the following tests. The first seven that I'll mention are very basic and literally define metabolic syndrome: fasting glucose, fasting triglycerides, HDL cholesterol, hemoglobin A1c, total cholesterol, waist circumference, and blood pressure. The two studies over the past five years that showed us in large populations that the vast majority of American adults are metabolically dysfunctional use those biomarkers. One from UNC in 2018 showed that based on those biomarkers, 88% of American adults have suboptimal metabolism. A follow-up study from the Journal of the American College of Cardiology showed that number has gone to 93.2% of American adults are suboptimal in their metabolism. If you had to pay out of pocket, these tests would be less than $100.

**Casey Means:** Let me explain what each test tells us about what's happening inside the cell. Fasting glucose: to be optimally metabolically healthy, we want fasting glucose less than 100. Triglycerides less than 150. HDL above 40 for men or 50 for women. Hemoglobin A1c less than 5.7%. Total cholesterol-to-HDL ratio less than 3.5 to 1. Waist circumference less than 35 inches for women or 40 inches for men. Blood pressure less than 120 over 80. If those things were all in those ranges and you weren't on medication, you were considered optimally metabolically healthy — that's now only 6.8% of Americans. All of these biomarkers are easy to change in one to two months with simple lifestyle habits.

**Casey Means:** Triglycerides are a storage form of excess carbohydrates in the blood — that's key to understand, we don't want to confuse triglycerides with eating more fat. If the body's mitochondrial capacity is low, it can't process glucose or fatty acids to ATP very well, so it blocks their entry into the cell. Now you've got glucose rising in the bloodstream. The body does not want lots of glucose floating around because it can independently cause endothelial dysfunction, it can cause oxidative stress in the bloodstream, it can cause glycation — sugar literally sticking to things. So the body converts excess glucose to triglycerides to be stored. A picture of both glucose being high and triglycerides being high in your blood should signal to everyone that there's probably something going on inside the cell that's blocking the cell from being able to use and process energy.

**Casey Means:** Hemoglobin A1c is looking at how many of the hemoglobin molecules in red blood cells have sugar stuck to them — that's glycation. If the concentration of glucose is higher over time, more glucose is going to stick to red blood cells, creating a higher percentage of glycated hemoglobin. Because blood cells last for about 90 to 120 days, hemoglobin A1c gives us a snapshot of average blood sugar levels over that period. Blood pressure relates to mitochondrial function through insulin: when the cell becomes insulin resistant — which again is a compensatory mechanism for mitochondrial dysfunction — insulin rises in the blood. Insulin is one of the key activators of nitric oxide, which is the molecule in the blood that dilates and relaxes blood vessels. So when we become insulin resistant and not responding to that insulin signal, we end up getting less nitric oxide activity, which is how blood pressure rises.

**Andrew Huberman:** My optimal ranges — what would those look like?

**Casey Means:** My ranges for optimal are tighter than what those studies used. I'd much rather see fasting glucose at 70 to 85 and triglycerides at 50 — which to me would say the cell is processing energy great, things are moving through, we're not backing up in the bloodstream. A patient might go into the doctor with a fasting glucose of 99 — one point under the normal range — and triglycerides of 149 — one point under — and the doctor might say the patient is totally fine. But they're on the upper end of normal for both, which would say to me as someone thinking about the mitochondria: this person is definitely metabolically dysfunctional.

## [02:24:34] Tool: CGMs, Glycemic Variability, Dawn Effect, Individuality
