# Episode 206: Transform Your Health by Improving Metabolism, Hormone & Blood Sugar Regulation | Dr. Casey Means

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Casey Means, MD — physician, Stanford-trained expert in metabolic health, and author of *Good Energy* — joins Andrew Huberman to break down how metabolic dysfunction underlies nearly every major chronic disease. They cover the root cause trifecta (mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic inflammation, oxidative stress) and provide a comprehensive toolkit of low- and zero-cost interventions — from walking patterns, blood biomarker testing, and food quality, to continuous glucose monitoring, fasting, cold exposure, and mindset — that anyone can use to restore cellular energy production and long-term health.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Metabolic dysfunction underlies 9 of the 10 leading causes of death in the US** — not separate diseases but a single failure of cellular energy conversion. The latest data shows 93% of American adults have suboptimal metabolism; only 6.8% meet all seven basic metabolic health criteria. [00:06:32](transcript.md#000632-metabolism-metabolic-dysfunction-medicinal-blindspot)

2. **The trifecta of bad energy — mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and oxidative stress — acts like rolling blackouts, biochemical fear, and wildfires inside cells.** Every pillar of modern life (ultra-processed food, sedentary behavior, artificial light, 80,000 synthetic toxins, thermoneutral living, chronic stress) synergistically damages mitochondria through different mechanisms simultaneously. [00:14:17](transcript.md#001417-trifecta-of-bad-energy)

3. **Insulin resistance is the cell compensating for broken mitochondria, not a primary disease.** When mitochondria can't process substrates, the cell blocks insulin signaling and prevents further glucose entry — GLUT4 receptors stay inside vesicles rather than reaching the cell membrane. Fixing mitochondria, not just controlling blood sugar, is the real lever. [00:27:57](transcript.md#002757-insulin-resistance-tool-mitochondrial-capacity--exercise)

4. **Walking 7,000–12,000 steps per day is associated with 50–70% lower all-cause mortality** across multiple large studies. The mechanism is muscle contraction activating AMPK and translocating GLUT4 glucose channels to the cell membrane throughout the day — something a single gym session cannot replicate across the full waking day. [00:35:03](transcript.md#003503-tools-walking--glucose-frequent-movement)

5. **Two minutes of movement every 30 minutes produces better 24-hour glucose and insulin profiles than one continuous workout.** Three groups walking 20 min before meals, 20 min after meals, or 2–3 min every 30 min (same total time) showed that frequent short breaks yielded significantly lower 24-hour glucose and insulin averages. A 10-minute walk after a meal reduces postprandial blood glucose by approximately 30–35%. [00:35:03](transcript.md#003503-tools-walking--glucose-frequent-movement)

6. **Seven basic blood biomarkers define metabolic health and should be tracked 3–4x per year:** fasting glucose (optimal 70–85), fasting triglycerides (optimal ~50), HDL (>40 M / >50 F), hemoglobin A1c (<5.7%), total cholesterol-to-HDL ratio (<3.5), waist circumference (<40" M / <35" F), and blood pressure (<120/80). Direct-to-consumer testing (Function Health, InsideTracker) removes the barrier when physicians resist ordering these tests. [00:58:21](transcript.md#005821-tool-blood-test-biomarkers-vital-signs--mitochondrial-function)

7. **Ultra-processed food (60–75% of American calories) acts as cellular polypharmacology** — creating mass confusion in satiety circuits, reward pathways, and nutrient-sensing systems. A Kevin Hall NIH inpatient study showed people ate 500 more calories/day ad libitum on ultra-processed versus unprocessed food and gained weight; the unprocessed group lost weight on the same protocol. Industrial agriculture has depleted soil micronutrients by up to 70% in some crops. [01:21:58](transcript.md#012158-tool-ultra-processed-vs-real-food-obesity-soil--micronutrients)

8. **The body can produce its own GLP-1 naturally by growing more L cells, stimulating GLP-1 secretion, and inhibiting DPP-4** — the enzyme that rapidly degrades GLP-1. Key strategies: eat ~100g spinach/day for thylakoids (2–3x GLP-1 increase over 12 weeks); fiber and fermented foods grow L cells via short-chain fatty acids; protein rich in valine and glutamine plus green tea EGCG stimulate L cells; DPP-4 inhibitors include black beans, oregano, rosemary, guava, and myricetin-rich berries and peppers. [01:39:10](transcript.md#013910-tools-control-cravings-glp-1-production-microbiome-support)

9. **Ozempic and GLP-1 analogs do not address the toxic environment damaging mitochondria** — they are a Band-Aid for a root cause that isn't changing. People commonly regain all weight or more after stopping. Lean mass loss is a documented concern. The history of weight-loss pharmacology has not reduced chronic disease rates; more SSRIs correlate with more depression, more metformin with rising type 2 diabetes rates. [01:51:42](transcript.md#015142-ozempic-glp-1-analogs-root-cause--medicine)

10. **Compressing eating into a 6–10 hour daytime window significantly lowers 24-hour glucose and insulin** without reducing calories. The same meal at 9:30 a.m. produces significantly lower glucose and insulin than at 8:30 p.m. because of chronobiology and melatonin's transient impairment of insulin sensitivity near sleep. The average American eats 11 times per day over a 15-hour window, rarely tapping fat stores. [02:07:27](transcript.md#020727-tool-intermittent-fasting--metabolic-flexibility-insulin-sensitivity)

11. **CGMs reveal three early-warning insulin resistance signals invisible to standard annual testing:** slow glucose clearance (high area under the curve; goal: back to baseline within 90–120 min of last bite); glycemic variability patterns (spikier non-diabetics have worse metabolic biomarkers across the board per the Snyder glucotypes paper); and dawn effect magnitude (healthy: <10-point rise). Post-meal glucose dips (reactive hypoglycemia) directly predict 24-hour carbohydrate cravings. [02:17:03](transcript.md#021703-tool-continuous-glucose-monitors-cgms--awareness-glucose-spikes)

12. **Deliberate cold exposure stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis and increases metabolically active brown adipose tissue; sauna activates heat shock proteins that upregulate antioxidant defense.** The body makes ~88 lbs of ATP per day; average body temperature has dropped ~2% over the last 100 years, a sign of declining mitochondrial output. Both thermal tools are supplementary — food, sleep, and movement come first. [02:00:54](transcript.md#020054-tool-deliberate-cold--heat-exposure-brown-fat)

13. **Sleep is a metabolic necessity:** even dim light during sleep alters morning blood glucose; four to five hours of sleep alters resting blood glucose. The sleeping brain cycles through glucose-dominant and ketone-dominant metabolic phases throughout the night — truncating sleep disrupts daytime fuel regulation. [02:33:10](transcript.md#023310-sleep-continuous-monitoring--biomarkers)

14. **Chronic psychological fear triggers the cell danger response, diverting mitochondrial energy from repair toward threat defense.** Stress visibly raises blood glucose on CGMs. Americans spend 93.7% of time indoors, often streaming fear-inducing media. Time in nature induces awe — the fundamental antidote to chronic fear — and nature-connected people are measurably metabolically healthier for pleiotropic reasons. [02:44:04](transcript.md#024404-tool-being-in-nature-sunlight-fear)

## Tools & Protocols

### Walking Protocol

- **What:** Accumulate 7,000–12,000 steps per day through frequent movement breaks distributed across the day, not just one exercise session.
- **How:** Two minutes of light movement (walking, air squats, any muscle contraction) every 30 minutes throughout the waking day. Add a 10-minute walk immediately after each meal. Under-desk treadmill at ~1 mph for 2–3 hours of the workday yields 6,000–8,000 steps with minimal disruption to knowledge work.
- **When:** Throughout the day continuously; post-meal walks within 10–15 minutes of the last bite for maximal glucose-lowering effect.
- **Ref:** [00:35:03](transcript.md#003503-tools-walking--glucose-frequent-movement)

### Exercise for Mitochondrial Capacity

- **What:** Multi-modal exercise combining zone 2 endurance (mitochondrial biogenesis), HIIT (mitochondrial fusion), and resistance training (muscle hypertrophy adds more mitochondria per unit volume).
- **How:** Resistance training targeting all major muscle groups 2–3x/week. 75 minutes of strenuous or 150 minutes of moderate cardio per week (basic government guidelines that 80% of Americans fail to meet). At least 7,000 steps/day on top of structured exercise. Soleus push-ups (seated heel-raise) and general fidgeting (NEAT) add constitutive glucose clearance throughout the day.
- **When:** Consistent weekly schedule.
- **Ref:** [00:44:25](transcript.md#004425-tools-exercises-to-improve-mitochondrial-capacity-desk-treadmill)

### Blood Biomarker Monitoring

- **What:** Seven core metabolic biomarkers plus additional advanced markers to track at least 3–4x per year.
- **How:** Request from physician at annual physical or use direct-to-consumer services (Function Health 110 biomarkers <$500 twice yearly; InsideTracker; Next Health). Optimal ranges: fasting glucose 70–85, triglycerides ~50, HbA1c <5.7%, HDL >50 F/>40 M, BP <120/80, waist <35" F/<40" M. Additional markers when possible: fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, hsCRP, apoB, uric acid, GGT, liver function tests. Use biomarker trends as objective feedback rather than relying on subjective feel.
- **When:** 3–4x per year.
- **Ref:** [00:58:21](transcript.md#005821-tool-blood-test-biomarkers-vital-signs--mitochondrial-function)

### Real Food Foundation

- **What:** Build diet around fiber, omega-3s, adequate healthy protein, probiotics, and high-antioxidant foods; eliminate or drastically reduce ultra-processed foods (Nova 4 classification).
- **How:** Prioritize single-ingredient whole foods from good soil. Aim for multiple fiber sources (vegetables, legumes, basil seeds, chia seeds, hemp seeds, flax seeds added to meals). 1–3 servings low-sugar fermented foods daily (sauerkraut, kimchi, Greek yogurt, kefir, miso). Colorful fruits and vegetables for polyphenols. Quality protein ~1g/lb body weight/day. Add fat and fiber to every meal to blunt glucose spikes.
- **When:** Every meal, every day.
- **Ref:** [01:21:58](transcript.md#012158-tool-ultra-processed-vs-real-food-obesity-soil--micronutrients)

### Natural GLP-1 Production

- **What:** Stimulate endogenous GLP-1 secretion via three pathways — grow more L cells, increase per-cell GLP-1 output, and inhibit DPP-4 (the enzyme that degrades GLP-1) — to reduce cravings and improve satiety without pharmaceutical analogs.
- **How:** L cell growth: eat more fiber (prebiotics), polyphenols (colorful produce, spices, teas, cocoa), and fermented foods; short-chain fatty acids from microbial fermentation stimulate L cell differentiation. GLP-1 secretion: eat ~100g raw spinach/day for thylakoids (2–3x boost over 12 weeks); high-protein foods rich in valine and glutamine; green tea with EGCG; curcumin. DPP-4 inhibition: black beans, Mexican oregano, rosemary, guava, myricetin-rich berries, peppers, Swiss chard.
- **When:** Daily, integrated into regular meals.
- **Ref:** [01:39:10](transcript.md#013910-tools-control-cravings-glp-1-production-microbiome-support)

### Compressed Eating Window (Time-Restricted Eating)

- **What:** Compress daily caloric intake into a 6–10 hour daytime window to reduce 24-hour glucose and insulin exposure without reducing calories.
- **How:** Stop eating 2–3 hours before sleep. Move first meal to late morning. Frontload carbohydrates earlier in the day when insulin sensitivity is highest (morning produces a lower glucose and insulin response than evening for the same meal). Ease in gradually if currently eating over 12–15 hours per day.
- **When:** Daily; a 6–10 hour window is the target; 8–10 hours is practical for most people.
- **Ref:** [02:07:27](transcript.md#020727-tool-intermittent-fasting--metabolic-flexibility-insulin-sensitivity)

### Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) Experimentation

- **What:** Wear a CGM periodically to understand personal glucose responses to food, lifestyle, stress, and sleep — using curiosity rather than trying to "game" a flat line.
- **How:** Monitor: glucose spike magnitude and time-to-return (healthy: back to baseline within 90–120 min of last bite; area under the curve should be low); glycemic variability (spikier = worse metabolic biomarkers); dawn effect (<10-point rise is healthy); post-meal dips (reactive hypoglycemia predicts 24-hour carb cravings). Experiment with food order, fat/fiber addition, pre- and post-meal walks, cold exposure, and stress management. OTC option: Dexcom Stelo (no prescription). App platform: Levels.
- **When:** At least one multi-week wear per year; more frequently when testing dietary or lifestyle changes.
- **Ref:** [02:17:03](transcript.md#021703-tool-continuous-glucose-monitors-cgms--awareness-glucose-spikes)

### Deliberate Cold and Heat Exposure

- **What:** Brief cold exposure and sauna to stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis, brown fat activation, and antioxidant defense systems.
- **How:** Cold shower or plunge for several minutes on days separate from (or 6+ hours after) resistance training to avoid blunting hypertrophy adaptations. Sauna at high temperature 15–20 minutes, 3–4x/week to activate heat shock proteins.
- **When:** Supplementary — added after food, sleep, and movement are consistently addressed.
- **Ref:** [02:00:54](transcript.md#020054-tool-deliberate-cold--heat-exposure-brown-fat)

### Time in Nature and Mindset Safety

- **What:** Dramatically increase time spent outdoors and reduce inputs that activate the cell danger response (fear-based media, chronic stress) so mitochondria allocate energy toward repair and homeostasis rather than threat defense.
- **How:** Work outdoors when possible — take walking meetings, eat outside, open mail outdoors, chop vegetables on a balcony. Set hard boundaries on fear-inducing media. Address unresolved psychological stressors. Morning sunlight outdoors is especially important for circadian entrainment and glucose regulation. Target reducing indoor-only time from ~94% toward 50% or less.
- **When:** Daily habit.
- **Ref:** [02:44:04](transcript.md#024404-tool-being-in-nature-sunlight-fear)
