# Episode 334: Protect & Improve Your Hearing & Brain Health | Dr. Konstantina Stankovic

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Konstantina Stankovic — otolaryngologist, neuroscientist, and Chair of Otolaryngology at Stanford School of Medicine — joins Andrew Huberman for a comprehensive discussion on auditory health and its profound connections to brain function, cognitive decline, and overall well-being. Hearing loss currently affects 1.5 billion people and disables 500 million of them; the WHO projects another billion will be affected by 2050. The conversation covers the full lifespan of hearing — from in-utero sound detection in the second trimester through old age — and provides actionable, science-backed protocols to protect hearing at every stage.

A central theme is that even subtle hearing loss, invisible on standard audiograms ("hidden hearing loss"), impairs focus, strains cognition, and meaningfully increases dementia risk. Unlike vision loss, hearing loss is chronically underappreciated by the public and by medicine, yet it sits at the intersection of mental health, social development, nutrition, environmental toxicology, and cancer biology.

## Key Takeaways

- **Hearing loss is a massive and growing public health crisis.** 1.5 billion people are currently affected; 500 million are disabled by it; 1 billion more will be affected by 2050. Age-related assumptions are outdated — progressive hearing loss is now occurring in children and young adults due to noise exposure and headphone use.

- **Hidden hearing loss is real and underdiagnosed.** Standard audiograms can appear normal while synaptic connections between cochlear hair cells and auditory neurons are already damaged by loud sound. Symptoms: difficulty understanding speech in noisy environments, tinnitus, or muffled sound after noise exposure. This damage has permanent anatomical correlates even when audiometric thresholds recover.

- **The cochlea is tonotopically organized.** High-frequency sounds (consonants, critical for speech intelligibility) are processed at the base; low-frequency sounds at the apex. Noise-induced damage preferentially hits the high-frequency base first, explaining why people with early hearing loss hear vowels fine but miss consonants and struggle with speech clarity in noise.

- **Sound and emotion are deeply coupled.** The auditory system has direct connections to limbic and emotional centers. Low-frequency sounds (infrasound) can induce fear or unease without conscious awareness. Music triggers emotional responses partly through these hardwired pathways, beyond learned associations.

- **Tinnitus is a phantom sound generated by the brain.** When the brain is deprived of normal auditory input, it upregulates its own gain, producing perceived sound. Hyperactivity in the inferior colliculus is measurable via imaging. Thinking about tinnitus reinforces its neural circuits and worsens it; engagement in other activities quiets it.

- **Tinnitus treatment has only two evidence-backed interventions.** The American Academy of Otolaryngology–Head and Neck Surgery endorses: (1) amplification via hearing aids (for those with measurable hearing loss) and (2) cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT). No supplement — including melatonin or magnesium — has shown benefit in systematic reviews or meta-analyses, though subtyping limitations make this conclusion uncertain.

- **Hyperacusis** (pathologically lowered pain threshold for sound) and **phonophobia** (fear of sound) are distinct conditions that can co-occur with tinnitus. Both require specialist evaluation.

- **Hearing loss is a modifiable dementia risk factor.** The brain must allocate additional cognitive resources to decode degraded auditory input, reducing capacity for memory and executive function. Social withdrawal from hearing difficulty also accelerates cognitive decline. Treating hearing loss with hearing aids is associated with reduced dementia risk.

- **Estrogen protects hearing.** Women tend to maintain better high-frequency hearing than men until menopause. Post-menopause, estrogen loss accelerates auditory decline. Men lose high-frequency hearing faster earlier in life, partly from greater noise exposure. Hormonal milestones (puberty, pregnancy, menopause) each have auditory correlates.

- **NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin at high doses) are ototoxic.** Regular or high-dose NSAID use damages hair cells. This is dose-dependent and compounded by concurrent noise exposure. Acetaminophen is also implicated.

- **Environmental toxins impair hearing.** Heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium), microplastics, and industrial chemicals are ototoxic. Plastics heated in microwaves or dishwashers leach ototoxic compounds.

- **Birds can regenerate cochlear hair cells; mammals cannot.** Chickens and other birds regenerate hair cells after damage. Humans cannot. Gene therapy and stem cell approaches to hair cell regeneration are in early development and represent a major research frontier.

- **In-utero hearing begins in the second trimester.** The fetus detects the mother's voice and heartbeat from around 18–20 weeks. Post-birth, the auditory environment profoundly shapes language acquisition and social-cognitive development during the critical period of the first years of life.

- **Cochlear implants are transformative but require early intervention.** In children with profound hearing loss, cochlear implants placed before 18 months of age leverage auditory critical periods for optimal speech and language outcomes.

- **Superior semicircular canal dehiscence (SSCD)** is a condition where a bony gap in the inner ear causes the person to hear internal body sounds (footsteps, heartbeat, eye movements) loudly and abnormally. It is surgically correctable.

- **Adult auditory plasticity exists.** Learning an instrument or a second language reshapes auditory cortex even in adults. Musicians have measurably better auditory discrimination and hearing preservation into old age.

- **Hearing aids and AI-enhanced devices are rapidly improving.** Modern hearing aids use AI to selectively amplify speech while suppressing noise. OTC hearing aids have been FDA-approved since 2022.

## Tools & Protocols

- **Safe sound threshold: 85 dB for no more than 8 hours; every 3 dB increase halves the safe exposure time.** Normal conversation (~60 dB) is safe indefinitely. A loud concert (110–120 dB) causes damage within minutes. Use a free decibel meter app to monitor environments.

- **Wear earplugs at concerts and loud venues.** High-fidelity musician's earplugs (Etymotic, Eargasm) reduce volume while preserving sound quality. Standard foam plugs also work. Wearing them for part of an event is better than nothing.

- **Take a mid-event quiet break.** Step outside or to a quieter area for 10–15 minutes during a loud event to give the auditory system a recovery window.

- **Maximize distance from speakers.** Sound intensity drops with the inverse square of distance. Standing 10 feet vs. 1 foot from a speaker provides substantial protection.

- **Magnesium before anticipated noise exposure.** Animal and some human studies suggest magnesium (magnesium threonate for CNS penetration, or magnesium glycinate) may reduce noise-induced hearing loss via antioxidant and vasodilatory mechanisms. Not proven to reverse existing damage. Dietary sources: nuts, seeds, leafy greens, dark chocolate, legumes.

- **Slow speech and face the listener when communicating with someone hard of hearing.** Speak slower, not louder. Enable lip reading. Eliminate competing background noise.

- **Use earplugs for sleep in noisy environments.** Nocturnal noise adds to cumulative hearing damage and degrades sleep quality.

- **Avoid chronic/regular NSAID use.** Especially avoid combining NSAIDs with high noise exposure. Discuss alternatives with a physician.

- **Avoid heating food in plastic containers.** Use glass or ceramic in microwaves and dishwashers to reduce ototoxic chemical leaching.

- **Get a hearing test (audiogram) routinely.** Many adults have undiagnosed hearing loss. Early detection allows early intervention. If asymmetric hearing loss is found, imaging (MRI) is warranted to rule out acoustic neuroma.

- **Pursue CBT for tinnitus.** Cognitive behavioral therapy is one of the only interventions with strong evidence. It restructures the emotional and attentional relationship to the phantom sound.

- **Use background noise to manage tinnitus.** White noise machines, fans, or ambient sound reduce the perceived loudness of tinnitus by reducing the signal-to-noise advantage of the phantom sound in silence.

- **Play a musical instrument.** Even in adulthood, playing music reshapes auditory cortex and preserves hearing acuity. Playing with others adds social-cognitive benefit.

- **Learn a second language.** Language learning drives auditory plasticity and strengthens the neural architecture of hearing into adulthood.
