# Episode 45: How to Exercise for Strength Gains & Hormone Optimization | Dr. Duncan French

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Andrew Huberman interviews Dr. Duncan French, Vice President of Performance at the UFC Performance Institute, about the science and practice of optimizing resistance training for hormone output — specifically testosterone and growth hormone. They cover specific protocols for load, volume, rest, and training frequency, as well as how cold and heat exposure, catecholamines, nutrition strategy, and skill development all interact with performance and hormone optimization.

## Guest: Dr. Duncan French

**Dr. Duncan French, Ph.D.** is the Vice President of Performance at the [UFC Performance Institute](https://www.ufc.com/ufc-performance-institute). He has over 20 years of experience working with elite, professional, and Olympic athletes across three full Olympic cycles within the British Olympic system, including GB boxing (Beijing cycle), GB basketball (London cycle), and GB Tae Kwon Do (Rio cycle). He also spent approximately three years working with Newcastle United in the English Premier League. Prior to joining the UFC, he served as Director of Performance Sciences at the University of Notre Dame. He completed his PhD under Dr. William Kramer at Ball State University and UConn, focusing on neuroendocrinology and hormonal responses to resistance training. His research explores how training order (endurance vs. resistance), hormonal responses to exercise (testosterone, estrogen, cortisol), and neurotransmitter interactions (dopamine, epinephrine) influence performance outcomes.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Testosterone is driven by both intensity (mechanical load) and volume (metabolic load); growth hormone is primarily driven by intensity alone.** This distinction is commonly misunderstood — the claim that growth hormone is volume-driven is one of those cases where internet advice has it backwards. [00:16:22](transcript.md#001622-what-kind-of-training-increases-testosterone-growth-hormone)

2. **The primary research protocol for testosterone-stimulating resistance training is 6 sets of 10 reps at ~80% of 1RM with 2-minute rest intervals, using compound multi-joint movements (e.g., back squat).** Both the intensity (weight on the bar) and the volume (60 total reps per exercise) components are essential — neither alone is sufficient. [00:16:22](transcript.md#001622-what-kind-of-training-increases-testosterone-growth-hormone)

3. **The margin between a productive and counterproductive training volume is narrower than most assume: moving from 6 sets to 10 sets (e.g., German Volume Training / 10×10) causes significant load drop-offs that undermine the intensity signal.** Maintaining the load across all sets is essential; if load must be dropped to complete reps, the protocol is already counterproductive. [00:20:19](transcript.md#002019-intensity-mechanical-load-volume-metabolic-load-inter-set-rest-periods)

4. **Rest interval length is as important a programming variable as load or volume, and it is the most overlooked.** Shorter rest (60–90 sec) preserves metabolic/lactate stress (the volume signal, best for hypertrophy); longer rest (2–3 min) preserves mechanical load (the intensity signal). In a direct comparison: Athlete A doing 6×10 with 2-min rest will gain more muscle than Athlete B doing the same sets with 3-min rest. [00:20:19](transcript.md#002019-intensity-mechanical-load-volume-metabolic-load-inter-set-rest-periods)

5. **For non-drug-assisted individuals, approximately 2 intensive sessions per week using the 6×10 protocol is appropriate, supplemented by other sessions with higher rep ranges (12–20) or lower intensity.** The high-volume, high-intensity approach is only sustainable on a daily basis for bodybuilders whose sole training goal is hypertrophy. [00:25:25](transcript.md#002525-training-frequency-combining-workout-goals)

6. **Acute, brief catecholamine surges (epinephrine/norepinephrine) transiently increase testosterone and directly correlate with sustained force output during training.** French's PhD research — motivated by studies of parachute jumpers before combat — showed that epinephrine begins rising 15 minutes before a challenging workout, and athletes with the highest epinephrine response also sustain the highest force output throughout the session. Chronic, uncontrolled stress has the opposite effect. [00:29:35](transcript.md#002935-how-stress-can-increase-or-decrease-testosterone)

7. **Cold exposure immediately after resistance training blunts the mTOR/hypertrophic signaling pathway and should not be used when the goal is muscle growth.** Cold is best reserved for competition-prep phases (when physical adaptation is already banked and recovery quality is the priority), or on dedicated recovery days for mindset and inflammation management. [00:36:55](transcript.md#003655-using-cold-exposure-for-mindset-anti-inflammation-muscle-growth)

8. **Skill acquisition is quality-driven, not volume-driven: a 90-minute focused session beats a 3-hour fatigued one.** As soon as fatigue distorts movement mechanics, motor learning stops — the athlete is grooving inaccurate patterns. French cites Dr. Gabriele Wulf at UNLV as a leading authority on motor learning and skill acquisition. [00:46:55](transcript.md#004655-skill-development)

9. **Glycogen depletion in the brain — not just the muscles — explains post-workout cognitive fatigue ("brain fog").** Even lower-intensity skill/drill sessions carry significant cognitive load and require nutritional refueling; athletes often under-fuel these sessions because they were not physically brutal. [00:50:05](transcript.md#005005-why-hard-exercise-creates-brain-fog-role-of-nutrition)

10. **"Metabolic efficiency" (concept from Bob Seebohar, formerly of USA Triathlon) means training the body to use fat at low intensities and carbohydrate at high intensities — rather than defaulting to carbohydrates across all effort levels.** The UFC uses largely ketogenic-style baseline eating with precisely timed carbohydrate windows immediately pre-, during, and post-training sessions. [00:59:13](transcript.md#005913-metabolic-efficiency-matching-nutrition-to-training-needs-based-eating)

11. **Exogenous ketones are used at the UFC Performance Institute specifically post-fight for brain health after potential cranial trauma — not primarily as a performance fuel.** The goal is to maintain energy delivery to potentially compromised brain tissue. [00:56:15](transcript.md#005615-ketones-brain-energy-offsetting-brain-injury-spiking-glucose-during-ketosis)

12. **MMA fighters demonstrate an exceptional and trainable ability to toggle between extreme high-arousal and calm states — a skill that distinguishes elite performers from typical people who remain locked in stress states for days.** This is developed through repeated exposure to extreme stress in training combined with structured recovery and management of the training process. [01:12:40](transcript.md#011240-voluntarily-switching-between-different-states-of-arousal)

## Tools & Protocols

### Resistance Training for Testosterone & Growth Hormone Optimization

- **What:** Compound, multi-joint resistance training using moderate-to-heavy loads (back squat is the primary research example; applies broadly to deadlifts, presses, rows).
- **How:** 6 sets of 10 repetitions at approximately 80% of 1-rep maximum. If load must be dropped before completing 10 reps, unload the bar and finish the set — maintaining intensity across all reps is the key. Do not extend to 10+ sets of the same pattern; the resulting load drop-off negates the hormonal stimulus.
- **When:** Approximately 2 times per week for non-drug-assisted individuals. Supplement with 1–2 additional sessions using different rep ranges (12–20 reps) or lower intensity to drive different training stimuli.
- **Ref:** [00:16:22](transcript.md#001622-what-kind-of-training-increases-testosterone-growth-hormone)

---

### Rest Interval Manipulation

- **What:** Deliberate control of inter-set rest period to bias toward metabolic or mechanical stress on a given day.
- **How:** 60–90-second rest intervals = greater metabolic/lactate stimulus (superior for hypertrophy). 2–3-minute rest intervals = greater mechanical load preservation (superior for strength and hormonal output). Do not extend rest simply to lift more weight — choose the interval that matches the session goal.
- **When:** Applied within each workout session based on the primary training goal for that day.
- **Ref:** [00:20:19](transcript.md#002019-intensity-mechanical-load-volume-metabolic-load-inter-set-rest-periods)

---

### Pre-Workout Catecholamine Priming

- **What:** Brief, controlled acute stress exposure to transiently elevate epinephrine and testosterone before a training session.
- **How:** Psychologically frame the upcoming workout as a meaningful, difficult challenge. French's research showed epinephrine begins rising ~15 minutes before a challenging workout during mental preparation. Music, competitive context, or deliberately engaging with the difficulty of what's ahead are all valid approaches.
- **When:** 15–30 minutes pre-workout. Keep the stressor short and controllable. Do not let stress extend into the post-workout recovery window.
- **Ref:** [00:29:35](transcript.md#002935-how-stress-can-increase-or-decrease-testosterone)

---

### Cold Exposure — Periodized, Not Reflexive

- **What:** Ice bath, cold shower, or cold plunge used strategically based on the current training goal — not reflexively after every session.
- **How:** Avoid immediate post-resistance-training cold immersion when the goal is muscle growth, as it blunts the mTOR/hypertrophic signaling pathway. Use cold during competition-prep phases (when physical adaptation is already banked and recovery is the priority), or on separate recovery days for mindset and inflammation management.
- **When:** Competition-prep and recovery phases. Not during general preparatory phases where hypertrophy is the training goal.
- **Ref:** [00:36:55](transcript.md#003655-using-cold-exposure-for-mindset-anti-inflammation-muscle-growth)

---

### Heat Acclimation / Sauna Protocol

- **What:** Deliberate heat exposure via sauna (~200°F / ~93°C) to improve sweating efficiency, promote heat shock protein expression, and stimulate growth hormone release.
- **How:** Begin with 15 minutes of exposure (or 3 × 5-minute efforts for those with very low heat tolerance). Progress toward 30–45 minutes of continuous exposure over time. Approximately 14 sauna sessions are needed before meaningful heat acclimation adaptations (improved sweat rate, cardiovascular tolerance) appear. Must begin 8–10 weeks before any competition or event requiring heat tolerance.
- **When:** Dedicated recovery sessions. Not immediately before high-intensity training. Begin the acclimation protocol well in advance — not during fight week.
- **Ref:** [01:14:30](transcript.md#011430-heat-getting-better-at-sweating-heat-shock-proteins-sauna)

---

### Metabolic Efficiency / Needs-Based Eating

- **What:** Matching macronutrient intake to the metabolic demands of each training session and training period rather than applying a fixed daily ratio.
- **How:** At low intensities (daily living, low-intensity cardio): allow the body to oxidize fats — minimize unnecessary carbohydrate intake. At high intensities (resistance training, sprints, skill work): supply carbohydrates immediately pre-, during, and immediately post-session. The UFC uses largely ketogenic baseline eating with targeted carbohydrate windows around sessions. For brain trauma recovery: use exogenous ketones post-event to maintain brain energy supply, not as a performance enhancer.
- **When:** Ongoing nutritional strategy. Carbohydrate intake is tactical, timed to session demands — not habitual. Regularly assess whether current training load justifies current carbohydrate intake.
- **Ref:** [00:59:13](transcript.md#005913-metabolic-efficiency-matching-nutrition-to-training-needs-based-eating)

---

### 12-Week Rotating Training Blocks with Dual Data Logging

- **What:** Structured periodization in 12-week blocks with both objective performance metrics and subjective well-being data logged throughout.
- **How:** Rotate training emphasis every 12 weeks (e.g., strength phase → power phase → conditioning phase). Log objective data (1RM, sprint times, HRV) alongside subjective data (mood, perceived effort, sleep quality, energy). Use both datasets to drive programming adjustments — subjective data often captures adaptation quality that objective metrics miss. Recognize that 15 athletes doing the same workout will have 15 different responses.
- **When:** Year-round periodization framework. 12 weeks is sufficient for 99% of physiological adaptations to a new training stimulus to become visible.
- **Ref:** [01:20:12](transcript.md#012012-using-rotating-12-week-training-programs-logging-objective-subjective-data)

---

### Arousal State Toggling Practice

- **What:** Training the ability to voluntarily shift between high-arousal (sympathetic) and low-arousal (parasympathetic) states on demand.
- **How:** Repeated exposure to high-stress training environments (e.g., combat sports, competitive contexts, high-intensity intervals) followed by deliberate relaxation and recovery. Practice both transitions: calm to high-arousal pre-training, and high-arousal back to calm post-training. MMA training is cited as an exceptional context for developing this skill due to its extreme arousal demands.
- **When:** Embedded into regular training. Specific cool-down protocols after intense sessions reinforce the parasympathetic shift.
- **Ref:** [01:12:40](transcript.md#011240-voluntarily-switching-between-different-states-of-arousal)
