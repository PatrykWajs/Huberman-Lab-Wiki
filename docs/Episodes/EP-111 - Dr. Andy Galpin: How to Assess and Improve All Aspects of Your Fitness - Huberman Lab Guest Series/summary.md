# Episode 111: Dr. Andy Galpin: How to Assess & Improve All Aspects of Your Fitness | Huberman Lab Guest Series

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

This is episode 1 of a 6-part special series with Dr. Andy Galpin. The episode establishes a comprehensive framework for assessing physical fitness across nine distinct exercise-induced adaptations — from skill and speed through strength, hypertrophy, and multiple tiers of endurance. Dr. Galpin provides both gold-standard laboratory tests and free, equipment-minimal field tests for each adaptation category, enabling anyone to identify their performance gaps and prioritize their training accordingly.

## Guest: Dr. Andy Galpin

**Dr. Andy Galpin** is a professor of kinesiology at California State University, Fullerton and one of the foremost world experts on exercise science. His research spans the full spectrum of human performance — from elite professional athletes (including Cy Young winners, MVPs, and Olympic champions) to recreational exercisers. Galpin completed his PhD in the lab of legendary exercise physiologist Dave Costill at Ball State University. He is known for bridging rigorous exercise physiology research with practical, real-world application. His work includes foundational studies on lifelong exercisers, muscle fiber composition, and the genetic vs. lifestyle determinants of fitness.

## Key Takeaways

1. **There are nine distinct exercise-induced adaptations**, not just a binary of "cardio vs. weights." They are: skill/technique, speed, power, strength, hypertrophy, muscular endurance, anaerobic capacity, aerobic capacity (VO2 max), and long-duration endurance. Fat loss and general health are byproducts of these nine, not separate training categories. [00:05:40](transcript.md#000540-9-exercise-induced-adaptations)

2. **Fitness assessment should cover all nine categories.** Most people over-index on one or two areas and leave severe deficits in others. The goal is to eliminate catastrophic "performance anchors" — one severely weak category limits the performance and health of the entire system. [00:02:04](transcript.md#000204-assessing-fitness)

3. **Lifelong endurance training preserves VO2 max into old age but does not preserve lower-body strength.** Galpin's research on lifelong competitive cross-country skiers in their 80s–90s at the Karolinska Institute in Stockholm showed VO2 max equivalent to sedentary 40-year-old men (group average 35–38 mL/kg/min), yet leg strength was no better than age-matched non-exercisers. Combined training is required to preserve both. [00:17:20](transcript.md#001720-lifetime-endurance-training-vo2-max--other-health-metrics)

4. **Genetics set a starting place, but lifestyle drives the bulk of adaptation.** A study of monozygotic (identical) twins with 35 years of discordant exercise habits showed the exercising twin had dramatically superior VO2 max, blood pressure, lipid panel, and resting heart rate — but nearly identical total muscle mass and inferior strength scores. Muscle fiber composition in the quad: non-exerciser ~50% slow-twitch; exerciser ~95% slow-twitch. Same DNA, completely different physiology. [00:26:10](transcript.md#002610-genetics-vs-lifestyle-endurance-training--identical-twins)

5. **Resting heart rate above 60 bpm is a red flag.** Galpin disagrees with the textbook "60–80 is normal" range. He treats any resting heart rate above 60 as a signal of deconditioning or underlying dysfunction. Values above 75 warrant investigation. [00:17:20](transcript.md#001720-lifetime-endurance-training-vo2-max--other-health-metrics)

6. **Modern gym culture is built on bodybuilding assumptions that do not apply to most people's goals.** The dominance of bodybuilding from the late 1970s onward embedded body-part splits and isolation movements that are suboptimal for general health and performance. Arnold Schwarzenegger's films (*Pumping Iron*, *Conan*, *The Terminator*) and Joe Weider's publishing empire drove mainstream adoption of a paradigm that was never designed for general fitness. Understanding this history explains most common training misconceptions. [00:40:51](transcript.md#004051-exercise-physiology-history-strength-training-popularity)

7. **The standing broad jump is the best free power test.** No run-up. Swing arms and jump forward maximally from a standstill. Measure from starting toe to worst (back) heel of landing. Men: target ≥ own height. Women: target ~85% of own height (~15% reduction applies across all power tests). [01:12:38](transcript.md#011238-speed-test-power-test)

8. **Grip strength is a critical health marker with specific asymmetry standards.** A hand grip dynamometer ($20–$100) is one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality in the literature. Men: minimum 40 kg, target 60+ kg. Women: minimum 35 kg, target 50+ kg. Left-to-right asymmetry should be no more than 10% — the non-dominant hand is often equally or more strong than the dominant. [01:18:42](transcript.md#011842-strength-test)

9. **The leg extension test is preferred over back squat for population-wide strength assessment** because it removes technique variability. Target: body weight on the machine for one rep. Past age 40, subtract ~10% per decade and still be in a solid position. Alternative: goblet/front squat hold with half body weight at the bottom for 45 seconds (entry level: one-third body weight for 30 seconds). [01:18:42](transcript.md#011842-strength-test)

10. **Hypertrophy is assessed by fat-free mass index (FFMI), not aesthetics alone.** Use DEXA scan or body composition test; enter weight, body fat %, and height into a standard online FFMI calculator. Men: target ≥20, severe physiological deficit <17. Women: target ≥18, severe deficit <15. Assumes body fat sub-30% (men) and sub-35% (women). [01:27:16](transcript.md#012716-hypertrophy-test)

11. **Muscular endurance push-up benchmarks are gender-specific with clear severity tiers.** Men: ≥25 reps = good; 10–25 = needs work; sub-10 = severe red flag. Women: ≥15 reps = good; 5–15 = OK; sub-5 = concern; 0 full push-ups = a strength deficit, not endurance — train absolute strength first. Any pause during the set = failed test. [01:29:38](transcript.md#012938-muscular-endurance-test-push-up)

12. **VO2 max targets: men ideally above 55 mL/kg/min (minimum 35); women ideally above 50 mL/kg/min (minimum 30).** A VO2 max below 18 is the "line of independence" — below this level, independent living becomes very difficult. Dave Costill's standard: "There is no human excuse to be below 60." The Cooper 12-Minute Test (run as far as possible in 12 minutes) is the best free field estimate. Test your worst category most frequently; your best category once a year. [01:52:44](transcript.md#015244-vo2-max-measurements)

## Tools & Protocols

### Movement / Skill Screen
- **What:** Joint-by-joint functional movement assessment across major patterns.
- **How:** Ideal: qualified physical therapist or movement specialist. Self-assess: record video from front and side view, 3–10 reps of an upper-body press (push-up), upper-body pull (row or pull-up), lower-body press (squat), and lower-body pull (deadlift). At each joint, assess four things: (1) symmetry left-to-right and front-to-back, (2) stability — no shaking or drifting under a 3–5 second pause at end range, (3) awareness — do you know what your limbs are doing, (4) full range of motion. Scoring: 0 = do not load this exercise (acute injury risk); 1 = minor flaw / proceed with caution; 3 = clear to train.
- **When:** Once per year or at the start of any new training cycle. Address deficits before loading.
- **Ref:** [01:07:18](transcript.md#010718-movementskill-test)

### Broad Jump (Power Test)
- **What:** Standing broad jump — equipment-free lower-body power assessment.
- **How:** Stand behind a line, no run-up. Swing arms and jump forward maximally. Measure from starting toe to worst (back) heel of landing. Men: target ≥ own height. Women: target ~85% of own height (~15% reduction applies across all power tests). NFL athletes at 6 ft typically jump 9–11 ft — the standard here is clearing your own height as a green flag, not optimization.
- **When:** Annually or at start/end of a programming block.
- **Ref:** [01:12:38](transcript.md#011238-speed-test-power-test)

### Vertical Jump (Power Test — Alternative)
- **What:** Two-handed vertical jump — equipment-free lower-body power test.
- **How:** Measure two-handed standing reach (both middle fingers touching, arms fully overhead, mark wall with a highlighter). Jump and touch as high as possible with both hands simultaneously — two-handed removes shoulder mobility as a variable. Record distance between standing reach and jump-touch. Target: ≥24 inches; past age 50, ≥20 inches; women scale ~15% lower. Optional upgrade: force plate for rate of force development data.
- **When:** Interchangeable with broad jump.
- **Ref:** [01:12:38](transcript.md#011238-speed-test-power-test)

### Grip Strength Test
- **What:** Hand grip dynamometer — proxy for total-body strength and all-cause mortality predictor.
- **How:** Test both dominant and non-dominant hand. Men: minimum 40 kg, target 60+ kg. Women: minimum 35 kg, target 50+ kg. Left-to-right asymmetry should be no more than 10% — the non-dominant hand is often equally or more strong. Devices cost $20–$100.
- **When:** Each comprehensive testing block.
- **Ref:** [01:18:42](transcript.md#011842-strength-test)

### Dead Hang (Grip / Shoulder Girdle Endurance)
- **What:** Timed body-weight hang from a standard pull-up bar.
- **How:** Use a thin bar (full hand wrap). Hang for time. Minimum: 30 seconds. Good: 30–50 seconds. Target: 60+ seconds. Women tend to perform well here relative to men because of favorable grip-to-body-weight ratio — female standards are similar. Scales imperfectly at body-weight extremes (very heavy or very light individuals).
- **When:** Same day as strength tests.
- **Ref:** [01:18:42](transcript.md#011842-strength-test)

### Leg Extension Strength Test
- **What:** Machine-based bilateral leg extension — standardized lower-body strength assessment.
- **How:** Target: 1× body weight for one rep. Use a sub-5-rep set and convert via an online 1RM calculator (accuracy degrades past 5 reps). Age adjustment: subtract ~10% per decade past age 40 and still be in a solid position. Only perform if pain-free and technically proficient.
- **When:** Annually.
- **Ref:** [01:18:42](transcript.md#011842-strength-test)

### Goblet / Front Squat Hold (Strength Alternative)
- **What:** Isometric squat hold with load — tests lower-body strength, core stability, and positional control simultaneously.
- **How:** Hold a kettlebell or dumbbell at chest (goblet position). Entry level: one-third body weight for 30 seconds. Solid: half body weight for 45 seconds. Descend to full squat bottom and hold. Holding position with no technical breakdown is the primary pass criterion; returning to standing is a bonus.
- **When:** As an alternative or complement to the leg extension test.
- **Ref:** [01:18:42](transcript.md#011842-strength-test)

### FFMI Body Composition Test (Hypertrophy Assessment)
- **What:** Fat-free mass index — quantifies whether muscle mass is sufficient for health, independent of aesthetics.
- **How:** Get a DEXA scan (gold standard) or use bioelectrical impedance. Enter total body weight, body fat %, and height into any standard online FFMI calculator. Men: FFMI ≥20 = sufficient; FFMI <17 = severe physiological deficit. Women: FFMI ≥18 = sufficient; FFMI <15 = severe deficit. Thresholds assume body fat sub-30% (men) and sub-35% (women).
- **When:** At the start of any testing block; 48 hours after last hard exercise to avoid inflammation/water-storage artifacts.
- **Ref:** [01:27:16](transcript.md#012716-hypertrophy-test)

### Push-Up Muscular Endurance Test
- **What:** Max consecutive full push-ups — upper-body muscular endurance.
- **How:** Full ROM (complete elbow lockout at top, chest near floor at bottom). Continuous movement only — any pause fails the test. Men: ≥25 reps = good; 10–25 = needs work; sub-10 = severe red flag. Women: ≥15 reps = good; 5–15 = OK; sub-5 = concern; 0 full reps = strength deficit (train absolute strength first, not endurance).
- **When:** Annually or at start/end of programming blocks.
- **Ref:** [01:29:38](transcript.md#012938-muscular-endurance-test-push-up)

### Plank Hold (Muscular Endurance Alternative)
- **What:** Static plank — core and upper-body muscular endurance.
- **How:** Front plank: hold 60 seconds. Side plank: hold 45 seconds. Pass/fail at those times.
- **When:** Same day as push-up test; useful when push-up mechanics are compromised.
- **Ref:** [01:29:38](transcript.md#012938-muscular-endurance-test-push-up)

### 75% Load Muscular Endurance Test
- **What:** Max reps at 75% of 1RM — direct relative muscular endurance measure, and diagnostic for whether a deficit is in endurance or absolute strength.
- **How:** After the strength test (e.g., leg extension 1RM = 200 lb → load 150 lb → max reps). Target: 8–12 reps. Below 8 = muscular endurance deficit. Well above 12 = peak strength may be the limiting factor. Key principle: if someone cannot perform even one rep of a movement (e.g., a pull-up), they have a strength deficit — muscular endurance training is irrelevant until absolute strength is established.
- **When:** Immediately after the strength test on the same day.
- **Ref:** [01:29:38](transcript.md#012938-muscular-endurance-test-push-up)

### Anaerobic Capacity Test (30-Second Sprint / Wingate / Bosco)
- **What:** All-out maximal effort — measures peak anaerobic power and cardiovascular recovery capacity.
- **How:** Lab standards: Wingate test (30-second all-out cycle ergometer) or Bosco Protocol (max vertical jumps as fast and high as possible for 60 seconds on a force plate — subjects are destroyed by second 45). Field version: 30-second all-out effort on an air bike, rower, or sprint track. Record total work or distance. Primary metrics: (1) can you approach predicted max heart rate (220 − age); (2) heart rate recovery — target ~30 bpm drop in first 60 seconds (~0.5 bpm/sec), ~60 bpm total by 2 minutes, roughly half again by 3 minutes. Recovery worse than this signals a deficit.
- **When:** Annually. Do not combine with the VO2 max test on the same day.
- **Ref:** [01:36:23](transcript.md#013623-anaerobic-capacity-test-heart-rate)

### Cooper 12-Minute Run Test (VO2 Max Estimate)
- **What:** Maximize distance covered in 12 minutes — best free field estimate of VO2 max.
- **How:** Run as far as possible on a flat, measurable course in exactly 12 minutes. Enter distance into a Cooper Test calculator for estimated VO2 max in mL/kg/min. This test is extremely uncomfortable — do not start too fast; there is no hiding from it. Men targets: ideally ≥55 mL/kg/min, minimum 35. Women targets: ideally ≥50 mL/kg/min, minimum 30. Below 18 = "line of independence."
- **When:** Annually. Do not pair with other maximal tests on the same day.
- **Ref:** [01:39:29](transcript.md#013929-maximal-heart-rate-test-vo2-max)

### One-Mile Walk Test (Submaximal VO2 Max Estimate)
- **What:** Walk one mile as fast as possible — submaximal VO2 max estimate for those who cannot run.
- **How:** Requires a heart rate monitor. Walk one mile at fastest sustainable pace; record finish time and immediate post-walk heart rate. Enter into a one-mile walk test calculator. Standard equations are universal — the source of the calculator does not affect results.
- **When:** For those unable to run; replaces the Cooper Test.
- **Ref:** [01:39:29](transcript.md#013929-maximal-heart-rate-test-vo2-max)

### Long-Duration Steady-State Test
- **What:** Sustained aerobic work over 20+ minutes — tests aerobic base maintenance.
- **How:** Choose a familiar activity (loop around home, bike, treadmill). Maintain consistent non-walking pace without any stop or break for 20+ minutes. Pass/fail: can you sustain pace continuously? Optional added criterion: nasal breathing only throughout — sustaining 30 minutes with nasal breathing only is a strong positive indicator.
- **When:** Annually; informally trackable via perceived exertion during regular training.
- **Ref:** [01:42:42](transcript.md#014242-long-duration-steady-state-exercise-test)

### Heart Rate Recovery Test
- **What:** Rate of heart rate drop in 1–3 minutes post-maximal effort — autonomic and cardiovascular efficiency marker.
- **How:** After any maximal effort, stop completely. Record heart rate at 1, 2, and 3 minutes. Targets: ~30 bpm drop by minute 1; ~60 bpm drop by minute 2; roughly half again by minute 3. Recovery worse than these benchmarks signals a deficiency in anaerobic or cardiovascular capacity.
- **When:** Bolt onto the end of any maximal test (Cooper Test, sprint test, etc.).
- **Ref:** [01:44:00](transcript.md#014400-fitness-testing-frequency--testing-order)

### Annual Comprehensive Fitness Testing Block
- **What:** Full-battery fitness assessment covering all nine adaptation categories.
- **How:** Recommended 3-day split (more days = better data, but 3 is practical). Body composition (FFMI/DEXA) always first — requires 48 hours with no hard exercise beforehand. Movement/skill screen next when freshest. Day A: power tests (broad jump / vertical jump) → strength (leg extension 1RM + grip + dead hang) → muscular endurance (75% load test + push-ups + plank), all in one session. Day B: anaerobic capacity (30-second sprint) + heart rate recovery tacked on. Day C: VO2 max (Cooper 12-minute run or 1-mile walk) + long-duration steady-state; heart rate recovery can also bolt onto the VO2 max effort. Re-test the worst category most frequently; best category once a year only.
- **When:** Once per year as a comprehensive baseline; every 6 months is ideal.
- **Ref:** [01:44:00](transcript.md#014400-fitness-testing-frequency--testing-order)
