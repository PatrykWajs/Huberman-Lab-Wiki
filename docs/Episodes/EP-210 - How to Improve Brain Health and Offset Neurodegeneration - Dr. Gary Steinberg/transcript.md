## [00:06:16] Stroke, Hemorrhage & Blood Clot

**Andrew Huberman:** I have a lot of questions. I know people are interested in keeping their brains healthy and sadly things happen to the brain — sometimes as a consequence of aging, sometimes as a consequence of certain activities. Maybe you could explain right off the bat what is a stroke, what is an aneurysm, what is a hemorrhage, where do these terms overlap, how are they different?

**Gary Steinberg:** Sure. So a stroke is like a heart attack of the brain. It involves disruption of blood flow to the brain — either in the form of a blocked vessel or, less likely, a hemorrhage. About 87% of strokes are due to a clot — either forming in the brain artery itself or forming closer to the heart or in the carotid artery and dislodging and blocking blood flow to the brain. About 13% are caused by a hemorrhage — bursting of a blood vessel — and that results in lack of oxygen and glucose being delivered to the brain cells, which ultimately causes death of tissue and disruption of neurologic function. That's what a stroke is.

**Andrew Huberman:** How do we know if we have clots residing in our body that could be dislodged? I know that some people when they fly wear compression socks. I know that some people have genetic mutations that affect clotting. I'll raise my hand — I did some genetic testing, I may be a heterozygote for Factor V Leiden, which is a clotting factor. Heterozygote means I have one mutant copy. I don't suffer from excessive bleeding or clotting, but there are lifestyle factors that can exacerbate an existing mutation like that.

**Gary Steinberg:** Well, you might not know in many cases. You can have a predisposition due to certain genes that are mutated that predispose to clots. Those clots can occur on the arterial side or the venous side. The arterial side is what generally causes an ischemic stroke. On the venous side, you can develop deep vein thrombosis — which can travel to the lung and cause a pulmonary embolism — though that generally does not go to the brain. You can develop venous problems in the brain which can cause a venous-type stroke, but that's much less common. That causes a stroke not by lack of blood flow delivered to the brain, but by having a clot in an important vein so the blood can't get out of the brain — it backs up and causes swelling or edema.

## [00:10:25] Blood Clots & Risk Factors, Medications, Smoking, Cholesterol

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some things that impact clotting or excessive bleeding? My understanding is Factor V Leiden mutations are one example. Others include blood-thinning agents like baby aspirin, fish oil, oral contraceptives for women — those can all affect clotting. Tell me if I'm wrong about any of those and if any of these things predispose people to more stroke or hemorrhage.

**Gary Steinberg:** Different kinds of drugs thin the blood and can predispose you to having a larger hemorrhage if something bursts or if you have some traumatic injury. Aspirin is a type of antiplatelet agent that thins the blood — there are many types of antiplatelet agents and they're very useful for treating people who have a predisposition to develop clots. Anticoagulants are another type — coumadin, warfarin, Eliquis — lots of new agents. They'd put someone at somewhat increased risk for hemorrhage.

As far as oral contraceptives — if you go back to the 1970s, the first generation were heavily estrogen-dominated rather than progesterone, and they did increase the risk of developing clots. Women who took oral contraceptives and smoked had a very high incidence of developing clots and ischemic strokes. The newer generations are much safer, but for my patients who have had strokes or are at risk for stroke, we recommend that women do not take oral contraceptives — that they use some other form, like an IUD.

Smoking is a very high risk factor for developing clots, which can lead to strokes, heart attacks, peripheral vascular disease. High lipids — high bad cholesterol LDL — when people can't reduce it with diet it's recommended they take a statin. The statins are very effective in lowering bad cholesterol and preventing strokes and heart attacks. Interestingly, the statins have also been shown to be highly beneficial for blood vessel integrity even if you don't have high LDL — so for my patients I often recommend they take a statin even if they don't have high cholesterol. And then hypertension is another risk factor for developing clots and arterial disease.

**Andrew Huberman:** When you say smoking dramatically increases the risk of stroke, is that because of nicotine per se, is it the vasoconstriction and blood pressure elevation from nicotine itself, or is there something about smoking — maybe even vaping — the contaminants, the other chemicals in cigarettes?

**Gary Steinberg:** It's not just nicotine. Nicotine is one of the factors, but it's the other products produced by smoking that can have an effect. So given that so many fewer people are smoking — are we seeing less stroke?

**Gary Steinberg:** Yes, the incidence of stroke is actually decreasing. It may be in part due to decreased smoking, but it's also in part due to other modifiable factors — hypertension is much better treated now, people exercise more, there's a lower incidence in some subgroups of obesity.

## [00:16:19] Heart & Brain Health; Neurosurgery & Brain Function

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the relationship between heart health and brain health as it relates to stroke? I would imagine that anything that's good for our heart is probably good for our brain, given the enormous amounts of blood and glucose that the brain requires to function normally.

**Gary Steinberg:** It's a good point. In general, the things that are good for the heart are good for the brain. There are differences between the heart and the brain, but they both depend very much on blood flow. The brain is unique, though — it represents only 2% of the body weight yet draws 15% of the total blood flow, and remarkably it consumes 20% of the body's oxygen. The brain is the most important organ — not the heart, not the kidneys. But I'm biased, of course.

**Andrew Huberman:** Of all the tissues in the body, if you had to pick one tissue — one cubic millimeter — to remove, your brain and probably the neural retina would be your least favorite choice, just given the deficits that can result.

**Gary Steinberg:** Right. Speaking of which — your specialty, neurosurgery — of all the years of doing brain surgery, can you recall one of the most incredible moments or days that allowed for some insight into how the brain works?

**Gary Steinberg:** I mean every every patient is different, so I'm always learning. A couple weeks ago I had a patient who had a vascular malformation located right in her speech area. In order to operate safely, first we did a functional MRI scan before surgery — which gives us some idea of where the speech area is, mapped out by having the patient awake and talking during the scan, because there's a coupling between blood flow and neuronal activity. The most accurate way of determining the speech location is to operate on the patient while she's awake. We sedate the patient, numb up the scalp, take off a piece of bone after cutting the scalp open, the membrane covering the brain — called the dura — and then we allow the patient to wake up from the sedation. I can stimulate areas of her cortex with her awake, and quite surprisingly there was no speech activity in the corridor I chose, so we found a safe pathway. She talked to us the entire case — told us about her daughter who was involved in debate — while I was taking out this vascular malformation under 20x magnification with a laser whose fiber optic tip is 0.5 millimeters.

Other times I've been amazed operating deep in the brain — in the brainstem, which is a small area that connects the thalamus. Signals coming from the cortex go through the thalamus to get down to the face, arm, and leg to move the muscles. When I trained in the '80s we never operated in that area because we couldn't do it safely. With developments in computer technology, imaging, and anesthesia, we can now find safe corridors to get into the brainstem. This last week I took out two vascular malformations measuring between 8 mm and a centimeter, and I'm amazed that patients are hardly set back in some cases — because in the past we would have clobbered them doing that.

## [00:23:27] Current Technology & Neurosurgery, Minimally Invasive Techniques

**Andrew Huberman:** How much of neurosurgery nowadays is actually burrowing down through the brain to a given location versus using laser or fiber optic approaches?

**Gary Steinberg:** Neurosurgery is becoming much less invasive. Minimally invasive techniques include operating through the vessels — so colleagues can go through the groin in the femoral artery or through the radial artery, thread a catheter backwards into the brain from the groin, go up into the aorta and into the carotid artery, from there into the brain arteries — the middle cerebral artery — and they can treat hemorrhagic problems like aneurysms by deploying thrombogenic coils or new devices. They can pull clots out if there's an acute stroke from a clot in an artery in the brain.

We've also developed techniques to use focused radiation on the brain — called radiosurgery. Examples are gamma knife and CyberKnife, which was invented at Stanford. Gamma knife uses a cobalt source; the CyberKnife uses x-rays. These can be focused on a very small area and you can eliminate vascular malformations, tumors, even some pain conditions like trigeminal neuralgia. It's not risk-free because radiation is still a form of energy that's damaging, but it's much easier and safer than some of the invasive techniques. We now operate through tiny openings — when I take out vascular malformations in the brainstem, for instance, I sometimes operate through openings of 2 to 3 millimeters.

Another form of non-invasive treatment is focused ultrasound — you don't have to open the skull; it focuses sound waves on areas of the brain. We're using that to treat essential tremor and Parkinson's disease, and it's starting to be used for treating tumors. Deep brain stimulation — putting in an electrode and stimulating the brain — was first used for treating Parkinson's disease, is used to treat chronic pain, recently shown to be beneficial for epilepsy, and now is even being used to treat psychiatric disorders like depression and OCD.

## [00:28:13] Transient Ischemic Attacks (TIA); Spinal Cord Strokes

**Andrew Huberman:** TIA — transient ischemic attacks — what are they, what are the symptoms, and is there anything people can take for them?

**Gary Steinberg:** A transient ischemic attack or TIA is a reversible stroke. It results in a temporary loss of function — such as inability to move, partial or complete paralysis, inability to speak, visual problems, double vision, blurred vision, loss of vision, slurred speech, difficulty understanding language, imbalance, problems walking, even cognitive problems. It depends on what part of the brain it affects.

In the past, a TIA was defined as a neurologic deficit due to lack of blood flow lasting less than 24 hours. But now with sophisticated imaging like MRI, some patients who would have been considered to have a TIA — lasting minutes to up to 24 hours — are shown on MRI to have had a little stroke. So now if an MRI shows a new abnormality — a new stroke — it's called a stroke rather than a TIA.

**Andrew Huberman:** My understanding is that people can also have strokes in their spinal cord, since spinal cord tissue is central nervous system tissue.

**Gary Steinberg:** Yes, it's much less common than a stroke involving the brain, probably because there's less tissue involved. The spinal cord is supplied by an anterior spinal artery on the stomach/front side, and by two posterior spinal arteries on the back side. If there's an interruption to blood flow in any of those arteries, it can cause death of tissue in the spinal cord, resulting in a neurologic deficit — depending on where it is: paralysis of both legs, or sensory problems, or loss of proprioception.

## [00:33:23] Stroke Risk: Alcohol, Cocaine & Other Drugs

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there any relationship between alcohol intake and the propensity for stroke or hemorrhage?

**Gary Steinberg:** Yes. People who overindulge are at risk for developing stroke problems — it can promote problems with the blood vessels, clots, but also hemorrhage — it can make the blood vessels more fragile. Another factor I see commonly in patients who develop aneurysms — those blisters on the blood vessels in the brain — is that patients I see are not just smokers but indulge in other drugs. Cocaine and methamphetamines markedly increase the risk of developing aneurysms or hemorrhage. They damage the vessels and can cause hypertension. When I operate on these patients, the vessels are ragged, very thin, not normal — they lack structural integrity. So yes — avoid cocaine use, avoid methamphetamine use, and avoid excessive alcohol intake if you want to avoid stroke. And throw smoking in there too.

**Andrew Huberman:** It's interesting because for a lot of years there was so much discussion about red wine being good for heart health. Now it's debated.

**Gary Steinberg:** Yes. It used to be considered beneficial to drink red wine, and then any wine in moderation. The latest studies suggest no alcohol is good. My theory is moderation is the key to life, and happiness also promotes longevity. My read of the data is that zero to two drinks per week seems to be the range that everyone agrees is safe at least for non-alcoholic adults, and once you get past two drinks per week it gets into the gray zone. Once you get up past four or five servings of alcohol per week, it's pretty clearly not a good situation. Zero to two drinks per week is seems to be safe — once past that, it gets complicated.

## [00:39:55] Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI), Concussion: Sports, Testing & Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** Anytime we hear about traumatic brain injury or concussion, people immediately think about football. But most head injuries are not from football — they're from construction work accidents, car accidents. What's the going consensus on how to deal with a concussion — sleep more, avoid alcohol, certainly don't get another head injury anytime soon?

**Gary Steinberg:** It's a great question. I got very involved in this back in the '90s — I was the 49ers neurosurgeon for a decade from 1990 to 2000. Steve Young had had some concussions and I actually sent him back to play when he recovered. Steve unfortunately had a bad concussion at one point and ended up retiring, which I think was the smartest thing for him. He's become very involved with studying concussions and trying to figure out better ways to diagnose them, prevent sequelae for football players — including changes in equipment and tackling.

At that time, concussion was not known — even repeated concussion — to cause CTE (chronic traumatic encephalopathy) in football players. CTE was known only in boxers. Soccer players had a high incidence of concussion but it wasn't known if there were long-term sequelae — and usually there are not long-term sequelae as long as you don't get repeated concussions.

Now what we generally recommend: if someone has a concussion, we usually get an MRI if it's severe. MRI scans usually don't show anything — they would show a contusion if there's bruising of the brain — but they don't show the molecular abnormalities that occur with the concussion. The best way to figure out how severe it is and when a person has recovered is more sophisticated neurologic testing. Eye tracking is a very sensitive way to detect problems with the brain after a concussion — many sports like football and hockey are incorporating pre-season eye tracking testing to get a baseline. In general it's good to not stress the brain, but total sensory deprivation for long periods is not a good idea either — you want to make sure the brain still has input but you don't want to overstress it.

## [00:46:45] Statins; TBI & Aspirin; Caffeine & Stroke Risk

**Andrew Huberman:** Statins might be vascular protective even in the absence of high cholesterol?

**Gary Steinberg:** Yes, there's a lot of good evidence for that. In fact some studies have suggested that taking statins reduces the risk of cognitive decline including conditions like Alzheimer's. The information is still emerging.

For traumatic brain injury, in general it's not a good idea to take aspirin — as opposed to stroke or TIA where you would want to take an aspirin — because if you have injury to the brain and there's some traumatic damage, taking a blood thinner might cause that to worsen or cause a hemorrhage.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about caffeine — is there any evidence that caffeine can increase stroke risk?

**Gary Steinberg:** I don't know any relationship unless you're taking so much that your blood pressure is sky-high. Caffeine has lots of benefits evidently in terms of health. It's fine.

## [00:48:31] Exploratory MRI: Benefits & Risks

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on exploratory/preventative MRI scans? I actually did one — a whole body scan. I learned I have a slight L3 or L4 disc bulge, and fortunately I have only one white spot on the brain. But I also know people that go in for these scans and get reports that they have a growth or multiple white spots on the brain.

**Gary Steinberg:** There are benefits and risks. The benefit is that you might pick up something that should be treated — like an early cancer or a large aneurysm in the brain which would have a higher tendency to bleed. But many times I see patients referred for a tiny aneurysm — one or two millimeters — found incidentally on a total body scan. Those aneurysms sometimes we don't even consider as real aneurysms. They don't need to be treated in most cases.

So it's a little controversial — people can be worried about them even if they're reassured. Other examples: you find something in the brain or elsewhere in the body, not sure what it is, and then patients start having more invasive biopsies and tests which can lead to iatrogenic injuries — that's injuries caused by the physicians. So I think you have to be very thoughtful when you interpret the results. For something like a 1.75 millimeter aneurysm — I would recommend: forget about it, get a follow-up scan, but you may very well live and die with this little blister that is of no consequence. Talk with a specialist about it.

## [00:51:53] Blood Pressure, Lifestyle Factors; Tool: Feeling Faint, Hydration; Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** What is sort of a blood pressure cutoff where we could say if you keep your resting blood pressure below a certain number you're doing pretty well?

**Gary Steinberg:** I think it has to be individualized. Over time the standards and guidelines have changed — it used to be if your systolic blood pressure was under 130 that was considered normal. Now the guidelines suggest 120 or lower is better in large studies. But when my blood pressure gets under 120, I feel lightheaded. In fact I had an event about 15 years ago — I was overdoing it, working in two operating rooms, took a run to the Dish in spring, then took a red-eye to Houston for a stroke meeting, ran in 90-degree 85% humidity heat, didn't hydrate well, had more coffee, and as the afternoon session started I felt lightheaded. Next thing I know I'm looking up at the chandelier — they're shouting stroke, cardiac arrest, seizure and starting to pump on my chest. After a $100,000 workup, it was determined I had a faint because I was overdoing it.

Since then I try to get seven to eight hours of sleep at night — that's clearly the bedrock of health. I used to get three to five hours; now I get seven to nine if I can. I cut back on caffeine, I don't push myself to exercise like I used to. I'm on an antihypertensive agent but I actually don't take it every day — for me it's better to have pressure of 125 to 135. If you've got some disease in your arteries, you may not want to have such low blood pressure. But in general, you want to take care of your body.

**Andrew Huberman:** If somebody has naturally low blood pressure and starts to feel a bit woozy in the afternoon — would you recommend they add a bit of salt to their diet?

**Gary Steinberg:** I would recommend they take their blood pressure. You want to correlate any symptoms with vital signs. Take your blood pressure if you're feeling faint — if it's low, one thing you can do easily is hydrate. That was something I used to not do much because I don't want to have to pee in the operating room. I actually had a kidney stone a decade ago — common among surgeons — and since then I hydrate all the time, to the point that my urine is crystal clear, and that helps with some of the brain clarity. Hydration hydration hydration — very important for your blood pressure, your general health, and your kidneys.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a relationship between sleep deprivation and stroke risk?

**Gary Steinberg:** Interestingly, strokes occur more commonly during sleep. It's not known why — one theory is that it's related to circadian rhythms. I don't know if there's a relationship between sleep deprivation and stroke specifically.

## [01:01:27] Chiropractic Neck Adjustment & Arterial Obstruction; Inversion Tables

**Andrew Huberman:** When I was a postdoc living in San Francisco I had a roommate who was a neurology resident and she came back from clinic at UCSF and told me about a patient who had come in experiencing hemi-paralysis of the face — that patient had gone for a neck adjustment at a chiropractor and had a dissection of her artery and essentially had a stroke. How common are these?

**Gary Steinberg:** I agree with you — I recommend patients, if they're going to have a chiropractor, not to have manipulation of their neck because that's what occurs. It's not common, but I see it. What happens is the artery is damaged — the manipulation of moving the bone and soft tissues causes a tear in the wall of the artery — and the blood that's usually in the lumen gets into the wall and causes a false lumen. That blood in the wall pushes part of the wall into the main artery, obstructing flow and sometimes causing a clot to form that can be dislodged and go up to the brain. There's no way to know whether this is going to happen. That's why I recommend not having neck manipulation by a chiropractor — even if it's rare, it's so devastating when it occurs that personally I would avoid that.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about hanging upside down — inversion tables?

**Gary Steinberg:** No evidence that it's bad. As long as you don't stay there for too long, of course.

## [01:05:16] Kids, Tackle Football, Soccer, Boxing; Mild Concussion

**Andrew Huberman:** Would you let your kids play football or rugby?

I think there are a lot of benefits to children playing football or rugby — it's a team sport, a lot of good skills are learned. But there is still risk and we're just learning about it. Even high school players who played many years ago are showing up with chronic traumatic encephalopathy when autopsies are performed. My son — a very good athlete who played four years of high school baseball and soccer — was asked to try out for the quarterback position his senior year and decided, with my encouragement, not to play.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about soccer and heading the ball?

**Gary Steinberg:** There is some evidence that multiple headings can cause some concussions and some long-term injury. Back in the '90s as the 49ers neurosurgeon, there was some evidence that soccer players — particularly female soccer players — had a high incidence of concussion. Now there's much more evidence that heading the ball may lead to some injury.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about somebody who hits their head on a beam in the attic and feels dizzy for a bit?

**Gary Steinberg:** I don't think you need to worry in general, especially if your symptoms resolve within a relatively short period — like a day or two. Even if you have a mild concussion and recover within a day or two, I don't think there's any need to worry or get a scan. It's a commonplace occurrence. As you say, that's why we developed very thick skulls — to protect the most important organ — because after all, the tissue doesn't regenerate, at least not much of it.

## [01:10:49] Nerve Regeneration, Stem Cells, Stroke Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** The prior notion was that once nerve cells in the brain die, they don't regenerate. For a long time, you don't produce any new nerve cells, any new stem cells in the brain. We used to think after an injury or a disease like a stroke — when tissue was damaged and you were paralyzed or couldn't talk — there was no way to recover, those circuits were dead.

**Gary Steinberg:** It turns out that is not true, and we are learning that. In recent years when I trained, there was no hope to restore function in patients who had a stroke, traumatic brain injury, spinal cord injury, ALS, Parkinson's disease. Now we are learning that there is hope.

We know that stem cells do form in the adult brain — that's not controversial anymore. We know that other circuits can take over for circuits that were dead. And we know now — and this is some of the work we're doing with chronic stroke patients who we thought could not recover after 6 months at all — we know there are ways of promoting regeneration or recovery of function.

For instance, we've done studies — still in clinical trial phase — with patients who are years out from a stroke, have been through rehab and physical therapy. 90% or more of recovery after a stroke occurs in the first 6 months; after that patients are not going to recover. And now we are finding in some of our early trials that if you put stem cells into the brain, or with another treatment — the very first FDA-approved treatment for chronic stroke — if you put a stimulator on the vagus nerve in the neck and stimulate, coupled with intensive physical therapy, you can improve arm function in those patients. In multiple trials we're seeing early indications that patients years out from a stroke can start to recover function in their arms, their legs, their speech. The old notion that these circuits are dead is simply not true — they can be resurrected.

**Andrew Huberman:** Where are the stem cells coming from in these experiments?

**Gary Steinberg:** It depends. Some of the studies I've done previously were with cells made from bone marrow donors — so mesenchymal cells — or another group made cells from fetal neural tissue. Just to orient people: inside bone you have the marrow. Cells within the marrow contain a hematopoietic population — potential blood cells — that can become blood cells or other things. Given appropriate factors, you can drive the fate of those stem cells to be neurons or cardiac cells, and then inject them into the brains of patients.

That was the initial notion 20 years ago — that the exogenous cells you inject become neurons, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes. That is not how they work. The way they work primarily is by secreting very powerful proteins, molecules, growth factors that promote native recovery — they promote angiogenesis, native neurogenesis, endogenous gliogenesis, synaptogenesis. But the main benefit may be that they modulate the immune system. By modulating somehow the immune system in the brain, they are able to induce plasticity and recover function.

## [01:17:36] Stem Cells, Immune System, Activity

**Andrew Huberman:** There are dormant stem cell populations in certain compartments of the brain — the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus, the olfactory bulb — that upon hyperoxygenation or increasing blood flow to the brain, largely by virtue of exercise, can be released from dormancy. Is that literature reason enough to suggest that people who've had a stroke should continue to move their body, exercise, maybe even do resistance training?

**Gary Steinberg:** Yes, there's a lot of evidence that activity, physical therapy, even forced activity is very beneficial. It's not just stimulating endogenous stem cells in the brain, but multiple mechanisms — it's recruiting circuits that were not involved before. Studies have been done on stroke patients who make a recovery that show not only is the side of the stroke improving in some cases, but the other side of the brain is showing increased activity — circuits on the other side may be contributing to the recovery on the stroke-affected side.

## [01:21:27] Injury & Recovery, Restraint Therapy

**Andrew Huberman:** I'm thinking of the work of Timothy Schallert and Theresa Jones — the overarching theme was that if somebody has damage on one side of the brain, the tendency is to over-rely on the intact limbs, and the approach to try to recover function was to restrain the good limb to force use of the injured one. This is called constraint therapy.

**Gary Steinberg:** Not only has it been shown in animal studies, but it's been shown in some clinical studies of patients with stroke. One of the trials we did with transplanting stem cells into the brain included restraining the good limb to force use of the other limb. However, some animal studies also suggest that you may have to wait a time — if you force use of the involved limb too soon, it can be detrimental to recovery. There may be an important temporal factor in terms of the timing.

## [01:23:46] Neuroprotection After Injury; Mild Hypothermia

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there anything that people can do or take for neuroprotection after an injury — to essentially try and rescue neurons that would otherwise die?

**Gary Steinberg:** Back in the late 1980s and 1990s, a lot of emphasis was placed on trying to protect the brain against acute stroke. Probably a thousand different drugs were tried — which blocked the pathway leading to cell death. So interestingly, when you deprive the brain and neurons of oxygen and glucose, they don't die immediately — it takes some time and it's actually an active process. There's a release of excitatory amino acids like glutamate — which normally are important neurotransmitters, but after a stroke cause an influx of calcium into the neurons, the final common pathway to dying. Then there are other pathways that lead to release of free radicals, causing apoptotic cell death. With reperfusion — when the artery opens up — you get a lot of inflammation. These pharmacological treatments were found to be very effective in pre-clinical stroke models — we could cure stroke in the lab. But they were never able to be translated to the clinical arena, except for one case.

Another method of protecting the brain was tried: mild hypothermia — a process of reducing the brain temperature and body temperature just a few degrees, from 37 degrees Centigrade to 33. We were one of the first to show that was protective even after a stroke in animals. Cooling neural tissue quiets its electrical activity. Deep hypothermia has a profound effect on shutting down the metabolism — that's why when kids fall into a frozen pond with ice-cold water, they can sometimes survive for half an hour and make a complete recovery, because their body temperature has dropped to very low, like 20 degrees Centigrade.

With mild hypothermia — just a few degrees — there's a slight decrease in metabolic activity, but that doesn't account for all the protection. Mild hypothermia blocks many of the detrimental pathways — it blocks partly the release of excitatory amino acids like glutamate, it blocks the calcium influx, it blocks the inflammation, it even blocks the pathway of programmed cell death. Because it hits all these pathways, it's multifactorial and very effective.

In the early 2000s, in prospective randomized studies, it was finally shown that two types of stroke are benefited by cooling the brain quickly. One is cardiac arrest from ventricular fibrillation — prospective studies published in 2002 showed that if you cool patients who have cardiac arrest and are resuscitated down to between 32 and 34 degrees Centigrade, there are much better neurological outcomes. In 2003, the American Heart Association determined it was a standard of care — a guideline — that you had to cool patients after cardiac arrest.

The other area is neonatal hypoxic-ischemic injury — neonates who have lack of blood flow to the brain when they're born. If you cool them, studies up to 10 years later have shown better cognitive outcomes.

For the kind of stroke we've been talking about — a single blocked artery — it hasn't been proven yet in well-designed prospective trials that cooling works. It works for cardiac arrest where there's global lack of blood flow. Cooling methods include cooling blankets, cooling catheters inserted in the groin, and external devices that cool quickly. People are also working on cooling just the head with helmets.

## [01:34:59] Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP), Stem Cell Therapy

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on platelet-rich plasma (PRP)? We hear so much about it — people get blood drawn, spin down the platelets, and get this infusion. Does it work to help recover or preserve brain tissue?

**Gary Steinberg:** I'm not an expert on platelet-rich plasma, but my reading of the literature cursorily suggests there's not hard evidence that it's beneficial. PRP contains very few, if any, stem cells — it's not legal to assert that PRP is stem cell therapy.

One has to be careful. I get emails every few weeks from people saying should I go to Russia, India, or Mexico and get stem cell therapy. I tell patients no — if you go out of the country, often you don't know what you're getting. We've seen cases of patients going elsewhere, getting injections into the brain or the spinal cord, and developing tumors or other problems.

Even in this country, there was a clinic in Florida injecting stem cells into the eyes of patients with macular degeneration — those patients had their own adipose tissue taken, sorted for mesenchymal stem cells, and reinjected into the eye. Should have been safe — their own cells. But several of them went blind irreversibly. That led the FDA to clamp down on stem cell clinics in the US.

I think this is very important — the dangers of stem cell therapy in general. There's a lot of hope for it — we're just finishing a first-in-human trial at Stanford. We developed these cells in my lab 20 years ago; it took us 20 years to prove that they were safe and effective and didn't cause tumors. The study is looking very promising — it's a phase one study and we're making plans to do a phase two study. In our trial with 17 of the 18 intended patients, almost all have recovered to some extent and many have improved in a meaningful way. We want to be cautious, but some of the results in certain patients are remarkable — people who were largely immobile or aphasic are able to speak or move. We've spent 23 years and more than $46 million in grants and philanthropy getting to this stage.

Some approaches to treat brain diseases are not injecting directly into the brain — they're injecting intravenously or intra-arterially. Those cells don't even get into the brain in many cases, and the idea is that in better studies done in animals, they work by modulating the immune system systemically — the cells get trapped in the lung and spleen, which are described as bioreactors — and modulate the immune system. Even intravenous delivery can be dangerous.

## [01:42:27] Scientific Advancements & Clinical Translation, FDA & Industry

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some things that could accelerate the process of getting these treatments from bench to bedside?

**Gary Steinberg:** Money is a factor. The FDA is appropriately very cautious. I think greater collaboration with industry — promoting more academic-industry relationships — would help. The government agencies don't provide enough money to do the final stage. There's a so-called Valley of Death where you get initial encouraging data even clinically but can't get over the hurdle to FDA approval because of money. I've seen good stem cell therapies not make it because the companies went bankrupt or the board of directors felt the results were good but not good enough.

I haven't formed a company yet, but I'm going to have to — because for the next trial this trial I was fortunate to get a grant from CIRM (California Institute for Regenerative Medicine) of $12 million. But the next trial — with our results as good as they are, we'd probably only need 69 patients now — will cost at least $45 million. Stanford is getting much better at translating basic discoveries into clinical therapies, especially with the proximity to big tech in the Bay Area.

## [01:47:40] Vagal Stimulation

**Andrew Huberman:** A lot of listeners are familiar with the vagus nerve as this very extensive pathway connecting brain and body in both directions. Vagal stimulation is not always about calming — it can be about alerting the brain. What sorts of vagal stimulation are you doing?

**Gary Steinberg:** For stroke — vagal nerve stimulation coupled with very intensive physical therapy was the very first FDA-approved treatment for chronic stroke patients, approved in 2021, three years ago. The study compared stimulation versus non-stimulation — putting the stimulator on but not stimulating, while doing the therapy. Patients did better with stimulation. It was a modest improvement but felt to be meaningful, effective at 90 days. Recently at the last International Stroke Meeting this past February, it was presented that those results hold up for up to a year.

The way it works presumably is that you stimulate the entire vagus nerve in the neck — and it's not the peripheral effects on the heart or other autonomic organs where it's working, it's stimulation that goes back to the brain. When you stimulate a nerve, it doesn't go in one direction. The vagus has lots of connections with brain functions. It's not completely clear which areas are being stimulated to recover from stroke or to improve depression, but it's brain stimulation that somehow resurrects circuits or induces plasticity.

Is the vagal stimulation invasive?

**Gary Steinberg:** You have to put a device in — it requires a small incision in the neck, you wrap a small electrode around the vagus nerve, and it's connected to a battery that can be implanted under the skin. There are also non-invasive ways being investigated — stimulating the ear, which has a branch of the vagus nerve. That's an active area of research. I think not just vagal nerve stimulation but stimulation of the brain is becoming a very important and innovative treatment for many brain diseases and injuries.
