# Episode 69: The Science & Health Benefits of Deliberate Heat Exposure

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman covers the neuroscience and physiology of deliberate heat exposure — how the brain's preoptic area (POA) integrates shell and core temperature signals and orchestrates sweating, vasodilation, and behavioral responses. He details how regular sauna use (80–100°C, 5–20 min per session, 2–7×/week) produces statistically significant reductions in cardiovascular mortality, all-cause mortality, and risk of psychotic disorders, and explains the molecular mechanisms behind these effects: heat shock proteins that prevent protein misfolding, and FOXO3 activation that drives DNA repair and senescent-cell clearance. The episode provides specific protocols for distinct goals — a once-per-week intensive 4×30-minute protocol for 16-fold growth hormone release; the Søberg Threshold (57 min/week sauna + 11 min/week cold) for metabolic improvement; glabrous-skin cooling via palms, soles, and face for rapid core temperature control; and local hyperthermia (41°C to supraclavicular fat, 20 min, 3×/week) to convert white fat to metabolically active beige fat via UCP1 and HSF1. Huberman also explains the endorphin–dynorphin cycle that underlies the mood-elevating effects of heat exposure, and ties all mechanisms together under the concept of mitohormesis.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Shell vs. core temperature are distinct regulatory systems.** The body maintains two separate temperatures — the shell (skin, limbs) and the core (organs, nervous system, spinal cord). Deliberate heat exposure must raise both to produce health effects; dangerous overheating is always a core temperature event. [00:09:31](transcript.md#000931-body-shell-temperature-vs-body-core-temperature)

2. **The preoptic area (POA) is the master thermostat.** Neurons in the POA receive signals from skin → dorsal horn of spinal cord → lateral parabrachial area → POA, then orchestrate vasodilation, sweating, lethargy, and amygdala-driven panic to cool or heat the body. Understanding this circuit is the foundation for designing effective protocols. [00:17:36](transcript.md#001736-heat-removal-circuits-pre-optic-hypothalamus-poa)

3. **Sauna 4–7×/week cuts cardiovascular mortality by 50%.** A 2018 prospective cohort study (n=1,688, mean age 63, 51% women, published in BMC Medicine) found 2–3×/week sauna users were 27% less likely to die of cardiovascular events vs. once-a-week users; 4–7×/week users were 50% less likely. Effects held after controlling for smoking, BMI, and exercise. [00:26:30](transcript.md#002630-protocols--benefits-of-deliberate-heat-exposure)

4. **Core parameters: 80–100°C, 5–20 min per session.** All major studies used this temperature range (176–212°F). Any modality that reaches these temperatures works — dry sauna, steam room, hot tub to the neck, or even jogging in heavy clothing in heat. Infrared saunas typically don't reach this range and are less effective for these outcomes. [00:33:37](transcript.md#003337-tools--conditions-for-deliberate-heat-exposure)

5. **Deliberate heat exposure mimics cardiovascular exercise.** In a hot environment, heart rate rises to 100–150 bpm, plasma volume increases, and stroke volume increases — all without joint loading. This cardiovascular stress is a primary driver of the longevity and heart health benefits. [00:38:47](transcript.md#003847-deliberate-heat-exposure-cortisol--cardiovascular-health)

6. **Four 12-minute sauna sessions at 90°C significantly reduces cortisol.** A study on young adult men used four 12-minute sessions at 90–91°C (194°F) with 6-minute cool-down breaks in ~10°C (50°F) water. Result: significant decrease in cortisol. Testosterone, DHEA, prolactin, and FSH were not significantly changed by this specific protocol. [00:38:47](transcript.md#003847-deliberate-heat-exposure-cortisol--cardiovascular-health)

7. **Heat shock proteins (HSPs) prevent protein misfolding and extend lifespan in animal models.** Heat exposure activates HSPs, which rescue proteins from heat-induced misfolding. In Drosophila, 70 minutes of heat stress extended lifespan by 15% in an HSP-dependent manner (flies without HSPs showed no life extension). [00:44:50](transcript.md#004450-heat-shock-proteins-hsps-molecular-mechanisms-of-heat-regulation)

8. **FOXO3 activation links regular sauna to longevity pathways.** Sauna 2–7×/week in the 80–100°C range upregulates FOXO3, a transcription factor involved in DNA repair and senescent cell clearance. People with naturally hyperactive FOXO3 variants are 2.7× more likely to live to 100. Deliberate heat exposure is one of the few behavioral interventions known to increase FOXO3 activity. [00:47:56](transcript.md#004756-longevity--heat-exposure-foxo3)

9. **Søberg Threshold: 57 min/week sauna + 11 min/week cold for metabolic improvement.** Research by Susanna Søberg established this as the minimum weekly threshold (across multiple sessions, not all at once) for improvements in metabolism and brown fat stores. [00:52:30](transcript.md#005230-deliberate-cold--heat-exposure--metabolism)

10. **16-fold GH increase from 4×30-min sauna — but only done rarely.** A 1986 study ("Endocrine Effects of Repeated Sauna Bathing") found four 30-minute sessions at 80°C in one day produced a 16-fold GH increase on day 1. By day 3 the effect dropped to ~3–4-fold; by day 7 it was ~2-fold. Heat adaptation is the cause — doing this more than once per week (or once every 10 days) will blunt the GH response significantly. [00:54:48](transcript.md#005448-deliberate-heat-exposure--growth-hormone)

11. **Fasted state + no post-sauna cold immersion = maximum GH.** Elevated blood glucose and insulin blunt GH release. To maximize GH: fast 2–3 hours before sauna, avoid immediate post-sauna cold immersion (blunts the GH pulse), and do sauna in the evening so the GH pulse coincides with early slow-wave sleep. Exercise and sauna done on the same day do not add GH — each stimulus resets the same pathway. [01:12:00](transcript.md#011200-heat-exposure--growth-hormone)

12. **Circadian rhythm determines optimal timing for heat vs. cold.** Core body temperature peaks around 4–5 PM then drops toward sleep. Cold exposure early in the day matches the natural temperature rise and supports alertness. Heat exposure later in the day aligns with the cooling phase post-sauna to facilitate sleep onset. Evening sauna also maximizes GH via the slow-wave sleep pulse. [01:08:26](transcript.md#010826-circadian-rhythm--body-temperature-cold--heat-exposure)

13. **Hydrate: at least 16 oz of water per 10 minutes of sauna.** Sweat loss must be replaced with water and electrolytes. Huge individual variation in sweat rate and salt excretion. Electrolytes (especially sodium) are important for high-sweat individuals. [01:16:20](transcript.md#011620-tool-hydration--sauna)

14. **Dynorphin causes discomfort; dynorphin → CAPA receptor binding then upregulates mu-opioid receptors.** When you're in an uncomfortably hot environment, dynorphins are released, bind CAPA receptors, and create agitation and a desire to leave. But downstream, this upregulates the receptors that bind feel-good endorphins — elevating baseline mood and pleasure sensitivity over time. This is why pushing through mild discomfort in the sauna has lasting mood benefits. [01:17:10](transcript.md#011710-heat-endorphins--dynorphins-mood)

15. **Frequent sauna inversely associated with risk of psychotic disorders.** A 2018 prospective cohort study (n=2,000+, no prior psychotic history) found a strong and statistically significant inverse association between frequent sauna use (4–7×/week) and future risk of psychotic disorders, after controlling for confounding variables. [01:17:10](transcript.md#011710-heat-endorphins--dynorphins-mood)

16. **Glabrous skin (palms, soles, upper face) enables fastest core temperature control.** These surfaces contain arteriovenous anastomoses (AVAs) — direct vein-artery connections without capillaries — allowing rapid heat exchange with the blood. Cool (not ice-cold) compresses on these surfaces cool the core far more efficiently than covering the torso. Ice-cold causes vasoconstriction and actually slows heat transfer. Same principle works in reverse to heat someone who is hypothermic. [01:28:44](transcript.md#012844-tool-glabrous-skin-to-heat-or-cool)

17. **Local hyperthermia at 41°C converts white fat to beige fat via UCP1 and HSF1/A2B1.** A Cell 2021 study ("Local Hyperthermia Therapy Induces Browning of White Fat and Treats Obesity") applied 41°C (105.8°F) to supraclavicular fat deposits in mice and humans. This activated HSF1, which upregulated A2B1 (a regulator of glucose and lipid metabolism) and UCP1 (mitochondrial uncoupling protein driving thermogenesis). Beige fat appeared at its natural depots (spine, upper back, clavicles) — not spot reduction at the heated site. [01:35:33](transcript.md#013533-local-hyperthermia-converting-white-fat-to-beige-fat-metabolism)

## Tools & Protocols

### Sauna for Cardiovascular Health & Longevity

- **What:** Regular whole-body sauna exposure targeting reduced cardiovascular mortality, all-cause mortality, and FOXO3/HSP activation.
- **How:** 80–100°C (176–212°F), 5–20 minutes per session, 2–7 sessions per week. Minimum ~57 min/week total. Any heat modality that reaches this temperature range works (dry sauna, steam room, hot tub to neck). Infrared saunas typically don't reach required temperatures.
- **When:** Any time of day; after a workout is convenient. Later in the day is optimal for sleep.
- **Ref:** [00:26:30](transcript.md#002630-protocols--benefits-of-deliberate-heat-exposure), [00:33:37](transcript.md#003337-tools--conditions-for-deliberate-heat-exposure)

### Sauna for Cortisol Reduction

- **What:** Repeated sauna sessions with cool-water breaks to significantly lower cortisol.
- **How:** Four 12-minute sessions at 90–91°C (194°F), each followed by a 6-minute cool-down in ~10°C (50°F) water (or cold shower). Protocol from a published study on young adult men.
- **When:** Any time of day; compatible with regular use.
- **Ref:** [00:38:47](transcript.md#003847-deliberate-heat-exposure-cortisol--cardiovascular-health)

### Sauna for Maximum Growth Hormone Release

- **What:** Intensive once-per-week heat protocol to produce 16-fold pulsatile GH release.
- **How:** Four rounds of 30 minutes at 80°C (176°F), each separated by a 30-minute cool-down (ambient rest, NOT cold immersion — cold immersion blunts the GH pulse). Do this no more than once per week — ideally once every 10 days — to avoid heat adaptation. Avoid eating 2–3 hours before. Do not combine with exercise on the same day if GH maximization is the goal.
- **When:** Evening, in a fasted or post-fast state. Go to sleep shortly after to align with the early-night slow-wave sleep GH pulse.
- **Ref:** [00:54:48](transcript.md#005448-deliberate-heat-exposure--growth-hormone), [01:12:00](transcript.md#011200-heat-exposure--growth-hormone)

### Søberg Protocol: Heat + Cold for Metabolism

- **What:** Combined deliberate heat and cold protocol for metabolic improvement and brown fat activation.
- **How:** Minimum 57 minutes/week of sauna (80–100°C) + minimum 11 minutes/week of deliberate cold exposure (ice bath, cold shower, or cold immersion up to the neck), each divided across multiple sessions — not all at once.
- **When:** Sessions can be on the same or different days; flexible scheduling.
- **Ref:** [00:52:30](transcript.md#005230-deliberate-cold--heat-exposure--metabolism)

### Glabrous Skin Cooling (Rapid Core Temperature Control)

- **What:** Applying cool (not ice-cold) surfaces to palms, soles, or upper face to rapidly lower core body temperature via AVA heat exchange.
- **How:** Use cool (not freezing) wet cloths or submerge palms/soles in cool water. Replace as they warm up. Avoid ice packs — vasoconstriction at these surfaces slows, not speeds, heat transfer. Frozen vegetable bags (e.g., frozen broccoli) under feet work as a field-expedient tool. Cool compress on back of neck and top of head is additionally useful for hyperthermia cases.
- **When:** If someone appears hyperthermic; during or after intense heat exposure; during exercise to extend performance.
- **Ref:** [01:28:44](transcript.md#012844-tool-glabrous-skin-to-heat-or-cool)

### Local Hyperthermia for Fat Browning

- **What:** Targeted heat application to supraclavicular fat deposits to convert white fat to beige fat via UCP1 and HSF1/A2B1 pathways.
- **How:** 41°C (105.8°F) applied locally to the upper back/neck/clavicle area for 20 minutes, 3×/week (Mon/Wed/Fri), for 5 weeks. Human subjects sat upright, head/neck/shoulders unclothed, with thermal imaging monitoring skin temperature. Mechanism: HSF1 binding → A2B1 upregulation (glucose/lipid metabolism genes) + UCP1 activation (mitochondrial uncoupling → thermogenesis). Not spot reduction — beige fat appears at natural depots (spine, clavicles, upper back).
- **When:** Research-stage protocol; practical consumer implementation (e.g., heating pads) is plausible but not yet validated in controlled studies.
- **Ref:** [01:35:33](transcript.md#013533-local-hyperthermia-converting-white-fat-to-beige-fat-metabolism)

### Heat Exposure for Mood (Endorphin/Dynorphin Protocol)

- **What:** Using sauna or hot bath to trigger the dynorphin → endorphin receptor upregulation cycle for sustained mood improvement.
- **How:** Expose yourself to enough heat to feel uncomfortable but not unsafe. The discomfort activates dynorphin (CAPA receptor binding), which in turn upregulates mu-opioid receptors, elevating baseline mood and pleasure sensitivity. Consistency over weeks produces lasting effects.
- **When:** Any time; most relevant for people seeking to improve hedonic baseline, low mood, or anhedonia.
- **Ref:** [01:17:10](transcript.md#011710-heat-endorphins--dynorphins-mood)

### Hydration After Sauna

- **What:** Replacing water and electrolytes lost through sweating during sauna.
- **How:** At least 16 oz (~500 ml) of water per 10 minutes spent in the sauna. Add electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) for high-sweat individuals. Can drink before, during, or after — or some combination.
- **When:** During and/or after each sauna session.
- **Ref:** [01:16:20](transcript.md#011620-tool-hydration--sauna)
