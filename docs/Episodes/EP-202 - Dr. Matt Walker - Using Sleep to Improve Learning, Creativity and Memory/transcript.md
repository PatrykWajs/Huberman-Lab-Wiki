## [00:05:48] Learning, Memory & Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** Welcome back. We have covered a lot of material — in the first episode of this series you gave us an overview of sleep and some actionable items; in the second episode far more actionable items about controlling light, temperature, and when to sleep; and in the third we talked about caffeine, napping, and other things people can do to supercharge their alertness through sleep augmentation in the daytime. Today we are going to talk about sleep, learning, memory, and a topic that I know everybody is very interested in: creativity.

**Matt Walker:** Resplendent pleasure to be back on the show. Thank you for having me.

**Andrew Huberman:** Most people understand that there is some relationship between sleep and learning, but it would still be a good idea for us to zoom out a bit and establish what that relationship is. Many people are familiar with being exposed to new material — cognitive or physical skill material — and not being able to learn it right away, but then having a few days in between and all of a sudden the skill has been embedded. Not obvious in that scenario is that sleep is perhaps the pivotal event that allowed the learning process to take place. So how do you think about sleep as it relates to learning and memory?

**Matt Walker:** I have conceptualized it in three different stages or three different buckets of benefit. The first is that we need sleep *before* learning to prepare the brain to initially imprint and lay those memory traces down. The second is that we need to sleep *after* learning to take those freshly minted memories and save and cement them into the brain so we do not lose them. The third domain is that sleep will then take those new memories and start to collide them with all of the back catalog of information already stored in the brain — it updates the iOS of your informational systems so that you come back the next day with a better ability to understand how the world works. In other words, the difference between knowledge — learning the facts — and wisdom — knowing what it all means when you put it together. That third category is why sleep provides creative insights, and we can double-click on each of those three because the data behind them is utterly fascinating.

## [00:09:32] Memory & Sleep, "All-Nighters", Hippocampus

**Andrew Huberman:** Let us talk about sleeping before learning — essentially establishing the optimal milieu within the brain for learning. What is that about neurochemically and at the level of circuits, and what is the evidence that adequate sleep prior to exposure to new material is beneficial?

**Matt Walker:** That word "optimal milieu" beautifully describes what we found. We started off asking a very simple question at my sleep center: is pulling the all-nighter a wise idea? We took a group of perfectly healthy, smart individuals and assigned them to one of two groups — a sleep group and a sleep-deprivation group. The next day, after sleep or after no sleep, we put them inside a brain scanner and had them try to learn a whole list of new facts while we took snapshots of brain activity, then tested them to see how effective that learning had been. The group that had a full night of sleep had incredibly efficient learning capacity — they had learned and imprinted that information well. The sleep-deprivation group showed a 40% deficit in the ability of the brain to make new memories. We have replicated this with visual information, textbook-like information — the range is somewhere between 20 and 40%.

**Matt Walker:** The structure we focused on is the hippocampus — you have one on the left side and one on the right side of your brain. People can think of it almost like the informational inbox of the brain: very good at receiving new memory files and holding on to them. When we looked at the sleep group during learning, they had wonderful, powerful activation of the hippocampus, as if it was gobbling up all that new information. When we looked at the sleep-deprivation group, we could not find any significant signal whatsoever — sleep deprivation had effectively shut down the memory inbox, and any new incoming files were just being bounced. Subsequent animal studies found that when sleep was restricted, the hippocampal synapses became very stubborn — they just would not form those new synaptic connections, impairing what we call synaptic plasticity.

## [00:13:46] Naps & Learning Capacity

**Matt Walker:** Instead of only dialing sleep down, we wanted to dial it up by way of a daytime nap. We took two groups, had them initially learn a large amount of factual information, then brought them back six hours later at 6 PM and had them learn a whole new set of information. One group spent those six hours awake; the other obtained a 90-minute nap engineered to allow them to cycle through non-REM and REM sleep. When we tested the wake group later, learning capacity had declined. In the nap group, it seemed to restore the brain's capacity to learn — there was no decline in memory; if anything, there was a small boost. The difference between those two groups was about 20%.

**Matt Walker:** When we unpacked the physiology, it was the non-rapid eye movement sleep — and particularly those sleep spindles, those short bursts of electrical activity — that seemed to predict how restored and refreshed your learning ability was. The best analogy: think of the hippocampus like a USB stick that is very good during the day at going around and grabbing new files, but with limited storage capacity. What sleep was doing was shifting those memories from the USB stick of your hippocampus over to the cortex — your hard drive, with a much bigger storage capacity. By doing that, when you woke up after the nap or after a full night of sleep, you had a cleared-out USB stick ready to acquire new files again.

## [00:16:59] Early School Start Times, Performance & Accidents

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you put the 20-40% learning deficits from sleep deprivation in the context of what is happening in education today?

**Matt Walker:** In the United States, the average school start time is somewhere around 7:30 to 7:45. If school buses begin leaving at 5:30 to 5:45 in the morning, some kids are having to wake up at 5:00 AM — maybe even earlier. This is lunacy when you think about it. There is a great study in Edina, a small suburb outside of Minneapolis, Minnesota, where they shifted school start times from 7:25 to 8:30 in the morning. They used SAT scores as the metric, focusing on the top 10% of performing students — the hardest group to expect any benefit from, since they are closest to the ceiling. In the year before the time change, the average score of those top students was around 1288 out of 1600. In the year after the 65-minute delay, it had gone up to around 1500. That is a remarkable shift in a group that should be hardest to move.

**Matt Walker:** In the state of Kentucky, 29 schools over two years shifted start times back by about an hour, and researchers tracked car accident rates in teenage drivers in those districts. In districts where sleep was increased — even by 20-30 minutes — teen car accident rates dropped by somewhere between 16 and 70% depending on the study. Sleep deprivation is the leading cause of traffic accidents in the United States, surpassing drunk driving. The teenage brain is biologically wired to sleep later and wake up later — forcing early start times systematically sleep-deprives an entire generation.

## [00:26:38] Medical Residency & Sleep Deprivation

**Andrew Huberman:** The notion of "we went through it and here I am, so you can go through it too" is very prevalent in medicine.

**Matt Walker:** It is, and it is another good example. Colleagues such as Charles Czeisler at Harvard have really done a great job cataloging exactly why we need to abandon the resident program structure. Young residents are working 30-hour shifts, often without any sleep whatsoever. Residents working a 30-hour shift are almost 460% more likely to make diagnostic errors in the ICU. If you have a surgeon who has had less than six hours of sleep in the previous 24 hours, they are almost 70% more likely to cause a surgical error with non-trivial consequences. And when young residents, after a 30-hour shift, get back in their car and drive home, there is a 168% increased risk that they get into a car accident and end up back in the ER — now as a patient rather than a physician.

## [00:30:49] Tool: Sleep Before Learning; Cramming Effect

**Andrew Huberman:** Are the errors in sleep-deprived students and medical professionals due to errors in memory specifically, or motor skills, or all of the above?

**Matt Walker:** It is all of the above. Learning and memory — your recollection of what you did and what you need to do based on your training — is compromised because recall of that information turns out to be compromised. But it is also decision-making: the frontal lobe is especially sensitive to a lack of sleep, and it is the frontal lobe that takes complex situations, distills them down, and comes up with the correct output scenario of decisions you need to make. Not so much when you are sleep deprived.

**Andrew Huberman:** If I know that tomorrow I am going to take a class or need to perform a recently-learned skill, what should I do the night before?

**Matt Walker:** Think of sleep that night as an investment in tomorrow rather than a cost opportunity of today. Some people will say: "But if I stay awake, I can at least be going over that material for many more hours — does that not compensate?" To a degree it does. But we then brought participants back a month later and tested them again. The group that slept was far better able to have retained that information, whereas the group that did not sleep performed similarly to the sleep group on the day of learning — but a month later, almost none of that material was residing in their brain anymore.

**Andrew Huberman:** So this is the cramming effect.

**Matt Walker:** Exactly right. You can learn a bunch of material, regurgitate it for the exam, and then it is gone — it just never passes from short-term to long-term memory. And that is a beautiful description of then what happens next in the sleep process: it is not just about sleep before learning; you then have to sleep after learning to do exactly what you just described.

## [00:35:09] Tools: Caffeine; Timing Peak Learning; "Second Wind"

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there anything we can do heading into a bout of learning if we are not able to stick to our perfect sleep schedule?

**Matt Walker:** There may be two things. There has been some work suggesting that caffeine may actually enhance the hippocampus — this memory encoding structure — and boost its ability to encode. What they have not yet done is the study where you sleep-deprive someone, give them caffeine, and ask whether caffeine can rescue the encoding deficit. That is entirely possible, but it is equally likely that the sleep-deprived hippocampus is not receptive to the benefit of caffeine. The second strategy: if you have a choice of *when* to learn during a day when you are underslept, think about your chronotype and when you are at your best operating temperature. For someone going to bed around 9 PM and waking at 4:30-5:30, the peak learning window is probably around 10-11 AM. For a later chronotype — 11 PM bedtime, 7:30 AM wake — it is probably closer to midday or 1 PM. The circadian rhythm will come to your rescue as long as you time your learning to your known peak.

**Andrew Huberman:** That gets us back to the two separate systems: sleep pressure from adenosine buildup, and the circadian approximately 24-hour rhythm that causes dramatic shifts in wakefulness and sleepiness independent of adenosine. There is also the post-prandial dip in the afternoon — a natural dip in energy lasting roughly an hour to 90 minutes somewhere between 1 and 4 PM — but after that is another opportunity to learn before the evening downswing.

**Matt Walker:** Right — two windows of opportunity. And there is this strange little blip in the circadian rhythm right before bedtime — a peak that researchers at Harvard have discovered. It makes no sense until you think about evolution: after foraging during the day, what you need is a final spurt to get you home safely to your nest. There is a beautiful little built-in circadian upswing that just briefly boosts alertness so you travel home safely. Many people around 9 PM will experience this as a "second wind" — Wide awake when they expected to be sleepy — and panic. But this is just that transient 45- to 70-minute window that will pass. Dim the lights, use a lights-out alarm, gradually back yourself off, and after that second wind passes, physiological decline will permit sleep.

## [00:44:25] Memory Consolidation in Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** How close to a learning episode does sleep have to arrive in order to maximize the amount of learning that occurs?

**Matt Walker:** You do not need to immediately go to bed — there is a reasonable window. Sleep before learning gets your brain ready to lay down those new memory traces. Sleep after learning takes those freshly minted memories and strengthens them — it essentially hits the "save" button, future-proofing that information within your brain so you do not forget. This goes back as far as a 1929 study by Jenkins and Dallenbach: participants learned nonsense syllables and were tested across an 8-hour period either staying awake or sleeping. In those who stayed awake, there was essentially catastrophic forgetting across those 8 hours. In those who slept, after about two and a half to three hours of sleep, those memories became fixated — like an animal trapped in amber — and they would not decay any further. That finding has been replicated time and time again.

**Matt Walker:** We now have at least two non-mutually exclusive mechanisms for how sleep consolidates fact-based memories. The first is memory translocation: deep non-REM sleep, with its big slow powerful brain waves combined with sleep spindles riding on top like a surfer on a huge amplitude wave, acts like a file transfer mechanism — it moves memories from the short-term, vulnerable reservoir of the hippocampus to the more permanent long-term storage site in the cortex. The second mechanism, perhaps even more fascinating, is memory replay, discovered in the 1990s by Bruce McNaughton at the University of Arizona working with Matt Wilson, then a young researcher, now at MIT. They implanted electrodes in the hippocampal regions of rats learning a maze and listened to the firing patterns of those memory cells. As the rat ran the maze, they could hear the signature pattern of learning. When the rats went to sleep after learning, they kept listening — and they heard that same memory signature replayed at 10 to 20 times faster than during learning. Sleep was compressing and re-engraving those memory circuits, strengthening them through rapid replay.

## [00:55:07] Sleepwalking & Talking; REM-Sleep Behavioral Disorder

**Andrew Huberman:** When I think about sleep and memory replay, I think about sleepwalking and sleeptalking. What is happening there?

**Matt Walker:** Sleepwalking typically occurs during deep non-REM sleep — not during REM sleep, which is an important distinction. During REM sleep we are paralyzed — a mechanism disconnects the motor system from the cortex so we cannot act out our dreams. Sleepwalking happens during deep non-REM, when that paralysis is not engaged, and the person can get up and move around. They are not acting out dreams in the true sense. There is, however, a fascinating and concerning condition called REM Sleep Behavioral Disorder: individuals who have lost the paralysis mechanism during REM sleep. Instead of lying still while dreaming, they physically act out their dreams — often violently, punching, kicking, screaming — and can injure themselves or their bed partner.

**Matt Walker:** What makes this condition particularly sobering is that it is now recognized as one of the earliest prodromal indicators of neurodegenerative disorders like Parkinson's disease and Lewy body dementia. The neurons that generate REM sleep paralysis — in the brainstem — are among the first to be damaged in these conditions. Some studies suggest that more than 80% of individuals with idiopathic REM Sleep Behavioral Disorder will go on to develop one of these diseases within 10 to 15 years. This means we may have a very early window for intervention before the clinical symptoms of neurodegeneration appear.

## [01:00:16] REM Sleep Paralysis, Alcohol, Stress

**Andrew Huberman:** I have experienced sleep paralysis — being asleep, presumably in REM, completely paralyzed but aware of my environment, sometimes with a sense of threat in the room. What is that?

**Matt Walker:** Sleep paralysis is the mirror image of REM Sleep Behavioral Disorder. In RBD, people have lost paralysis during REM — they can move when they should not. In sleep paralysis, you are fully paralyzed during REM but semi-conscious and aware of your environment. The REM paralysis mechanism is running correctly, but consciousness has partially returned before the paralysis has been lifted. It is benign from a medical standpoint, though the experience can be profoundly frightening — often accompanied by hypnagogic hallucinations and a sensation of a presence in the room or a weight on the chest. Across cultures and across history, this has given rise to myths of demons, night hags, and similar figures.

**Andrew Huberman:** What increases the likelihood of sleep paralysis?

**Matt Walker:** Alcohol is a significant disruptor of REM sleep. Because alcohol is a sedative, many people think it helps them sleep, but alcohol fragments sleep and powerfully suppresses REM sleep. When you stop drinking after a period of heavy use, you often get a REM rebound — an intense increase in REM — which can be associated with vivid dreams and increased sleep paralysis. Stress and anxiety also increase the likelihood. And irregular sleep schedules — particularly anything that disrupts the timing of REM sleep, which is concentrated in the latter portion of the night — can increase susceptibility. This is why shift workers, people with jet lag, and those with inconsistent bedtimes experience sleep paralysis more frequently.

## [01:08:46] Skills, Motor Learning & Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** We have been discussing complete paralysis and the inability to move. Let us talk about the ability to move — meaning motor learning. What is the relationship between sleep and the learning of physical skills?

**Matt Walker:** Motor learning — whether it is playing a musical instrument, performing a surgical technique, or executing a sports skill — relies on procedural, non-declarative memory. A canonical study in this field had participants learn a specific sequence of finger taps — a short motor sequence they had to tap out as quickly and accurately as possible. One group then slept before being tested; the other stayed awake before being tested. The sleep group showed approximately 20% improvement in speed and 35% improvement in accuracy — a 20 to 35% gain in motor performance from a single night of sleep, with no additional practice.

**Andrew Huberman:** What stage of sleep is doing that work?

**Matt Walker:** For motor learning it is primarily stage 2 non-REM sleep — specifically the sleep spindles that are especially dense in stage 2, and particularly the sleep spindles that occur in the last two hours of the night. This is important: if you routinely get 6 hours instead of 8, you are disproportionately cutting off the sleep spindle-rich stage 2 that is most concentrated in the second half of the night. You lose a large proportion of your motor memory consolidation benefit even though you have only lost 25% of your time in bed. Sleep spindles during consolidation also appear to be targeted to the specific brain circuits that were active during learning — selective reinforcement of the circuits engaged during practice, not a global blanket strengthening.

## [01:17:03] Tool: Timing Sleep & Learning, Skill Enhancement

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the practical message for someone learning a new physical skill in terms of the timing of sleep relative to practice?

**Matt Walker:** Two messages. First: do not sacrifice the back end of your sleep. The late hours of sleep — what you lose when you set an early alarm — are disproportionately rich in the stage 2 non-REM and REM sleep that consolidate both motor and emotional learning. If you are trying to learn a new physical skill, practice it, then sleep — and do not cut that sleep short. Second: if you have the choice, practice in the evening rather than the morning. Not because of circadian reasons, but because if you practice in the morning you have a full day awake before you get your consolidation sleep — every hour awake after learning is another hour for interference from other experiences. If you practice in the evening, sleep comes relatively soon after learning, minimizing that interference window.

## [01:20:00] Naps; Specificity & Memory Consolidation, Sleep Spindles

**Andrew Huberman:** We talked about naps boosting learning capacity before new learning. Can naps also consolidate motor learning after a learning bout?

**Matt Walker:** Yes — and the data is quite striking. Naps of 60 to 90 minutes that contain sufficient stage 2 non-REM sleep and its associated sleep spindles can provide meaningful motor memory consolidation. Sleep spindle density correlates tightly with the degree of motor memory consolidation. In our studies, we can actually predict how much someone will improve on a motor task based on the density of their sleep spindles during sleep. This has led to exciting work looking at whether we can artificially enhance sleep spindles — through auditory stimulation, transcranial electrical stimulation, or pharmacological means — as a way to amplify motor learning. Early results are promising.

**Matt Walker:** There is also a beautiful smart-targeting phenomenon: if you learn two different motor sequences and then go to sleep, the brain will selectively replay and strengthen the one that was learned less well — as if sleep is a smart teacher that identifies where you are weakest and prioritizes consolidation of that material. Deliberate, focused practice — rather than mindless repetition — tends to produce better motor learning because it recruits stronger, more coherent neural representations that sleep then has more material to work with during replay.

## [01:27:21] Sleep, Motor Learning & Athletes; Automaticity

**Andrew Huberman:** What do we know about elite athletes and sleep, particularly in terms of how sleep affects performance and the automaticity of skills?

**Matt Walker:** The literature is very clear. Sleep deprivation in athletes produces measurable decrements in reaction time, accuracy, decision-making, and physical output — as well as increased injury risk. A study of athletes at Stanford found that when they extended their sleep — sleeping 10 hours per night for several weeks — their performance on virtually every measured metric improved: sprint times got faster, shooting accuracy improved, reaction times quickened. And athletes reported feeling better — better mood, less fatigue.

**Matt Walker:** The concept of automaticity is crucial here. When you first learn a motor skill, its execution requires a great deal of conscious, effortful attention — it is mediated heavily by cortical, conscious circuits. As the skill is mastered, it becomes automated — it moves to subcortical circuits like the basal ganglia and cerebellum, which can execute the skill with less conscious oversight and more speed, consistency, and efficiency. Sleep appears to be important in this transition from effortful cortical control to automated subcortical execution. This is why a skill learned to the point of "I can do it if I concentrate" may — after sleep — feel qualitatively different: smoother, faster, less effortful. Sleep is doing the work of automation that many athletes and musicians describe as the skill "clicking" or "settling in overnight."

## [01:34:10] Can Learning Improve Sleep?

**Andrew Huberman:** We have been talking about how sleep affects learning. Can learning or engaging in certain cognitive activities improve sleep in return?

**Matt Walker:** This is an interesting reciprocal question. There is some data suggesting that more cognitively demanding days, or days involving specific types of learning, can produce measurable changes in sleep architecture that night. After a day of intense learning, slow-wave sleep and sleep spindle activity the following night may be increased or targeted to the brain regions most engaged during learning — as if the brain tags what needs priority consolidation and then dedicates the necessary sleep architecture to that material.

**Matt Walker:** There is also the concept of targeted memory reactivation. If during learning you pair information with a specific cue — say, a particular odor or a soft sound — and then during subsequent sleep you re-expose the brain to that cue at very low intensity (not enough to wake the person, but enough to register in the sleeping brain), you can selectively boost the replay and consolidation of the associated memories. This has been demonstrated in human studies. It opens up the fascinating possibility that we might be able to curate memory consolidation during sleep by strategically reactivating the memories we most want to retain.

## [01:39:13] Tool: Exercise to Improve Sleep; Performance, Injury & Motivation

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there anything people can do behaviorally to improve the quality of sleep that then improves learning?

**Matt Walker:** Physical exercise is one of the most well-established levers for improving sleep quality. Aerobic exercise in particular has been shown to increase the amount of deep slow-wave sleep, decrease sleep onset latency, and increase overall sleep duration. Studies have shown 20-25% increases in slow-wave sleep from regular aerobic exercise. The timing matters somewhat — vigorous exercise very close to bedtime can be stimulating for some people — but for most people, exercise at any time of day improves nighttime sleep. Even a single bout of exercise can improve sleep that night for people who normally sleep poorly.

**Matt Walker:** Exercise also increases growth hormone release, which occurs predominantly during slow-wave sleep — there is a beautiful synergy there. In terms of injury and performance: chronically sleep-deprived athletes show substantially elevated injury rates. One study of adolescent athletes found that those sleeping fewer than 8 hours per night were 1.7 times more likely to have been injured compared to those sleeping 8 or more hours. Sleep deprivation also reduces motivation to exercise, so sleep deprivation and reduced physical activity form a vicious cycle that is hard to break.

## [01:44:38] Pillars of Health; Dieting & Sleep Deprivation

**Andrew Huberman:** How does sleep interact with diet specifically?

**Matt Walker:** Sleep deprivation profoundly dysregulates appetite and the hormones that control it. When you are sleep deprived, levels of leptin — the satiety hormone — go down, while levels of ghrelin — the hunger hormone — go up. The net effect is that sleep-deprived individuals consume on average 300 to 400 additional calories per day compared to when they are well-slept. And it is not just the quantity — sleep deprivation causes preferential desire for high-calorie, sweet, and salty foods, mediated by increased activity in the brain's reward regions in response to food cues. If you are trying to maintain or lose weight and you are chronically sleep deprived, the biology is working strongly against you regardless of how disciplined your dietary intentions are.

**Matt Walker:** There is also the reverse: severely calorie-restricted diets — aggressive dieting — can disrupt sleep. Metabolic state influences sleep architecture, and when the body is in a significant caloric deficit, particularly with low carbohydrate intake, REM sleep can be impaired. The message: sleep and nutrition are deeply interdependent pillars of health, not independent levers. Both are necessary, and they reinforce each other.

## [01:49:35] Performance & Poor Sleep, Belief Effects, "Orthosomnia"

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about the belief effects on sleep — the study where perceived sleep quality, not just actual sleep quality, influenced performance.

**Matt Walker:** There is a study where participants were told either that they had slept well or that they had slept poorly — decoupled from their real sleep quality measured objectively. People who were told they had slept well performed better on cognitive tests than people told they had slept poorly, even when controlling for actual sleep quality. This suggests that the narrative about your sleep can independently influence cognitive performance. It does not mean you can think yourself into performing well after a truly poor night of sleep — but it does mean that catastrophizing about a bad night can independently make things worse.

**Matt Walker:** This has led to discussion of "orthosomnia" — an unhealthy obsession with sleep perfection, often driven by consumer sleep trackers. People become fixated on their data, and the anxiety about achieving perfect sleep scores paradoxically makes their sleep worse. The tools are useful, but treating every night as a performance to be optimized can backfire. There is real value in normalizing some night-to-night variation as part of healthy sleep biology.

## [01:57:03] "Overnight Alchemy", Sleep & Novel Memory Linking

**Andrew Huberman:** Beyond consolidating individual memories, you have described a third function of sleep — a kind of creative integration. Tell us about that.

**Matt Walker:** For a while, we thought sleep was doing just two things for learning and memory: sleep before learning to prepare the brain, and sleep after learning to consolidate. But then data started to emerge showing that sleep is much more intelligent than we ever imagined. Sleep does not simply strengthen individual memories as isolated islands — sleep after learning is almost a form of informational alchemy. Sleep will take these new memories and start to interconnect them and cross-link them — both with other new information learned during the day, and with the back catalog of autobiographical memory. You wake up the next day with a revised, web-like architecture of associations.

**Matt Walker:** A good example: we did a study where participants were presented with a series of what appeared to be unrelated facts, but which actually had a hidden underlying relational structure — a mathematical rule — that linked them together. On the day of learning, very few participants could identify the underlying rule. We then had one group sleep and one stay awake. When we tested them the next morning, the sleep group was nearly three times more likely to have discovered the hidden relational rule compared to the wake group. Sleep had stitched together apparently disparate pieces of information and surfaced the hidden structure connecting them. I think of it as overnight alchemy — sleep is transforming the raw materials of daytime experience into something richer and more connected.

**Matt Walker:** And the stage of sleep most important for this third function — creative integration — turns out to be REM sleep. During REM, the brain is essentially devoid of norepinephrine — the anxiety molecule. In the absence of norepinephrine, the associative, wide-ranging connections between disparate ideas are given free rein. The brain during REM is running loose associative searches across its entire memory database — which is why the content of REM dreams is so bizarre and loosely connected compared to reality. But that bizarreness is a feature, not a bug: it is the engine of associative creativity.

## [02:05:58] Sleep & Creativity

**Andrew Huberman:** So REM sleep is the stage most associated with creativity?

**Matt Walker:** Yes, and there are beautiful examples of this in the history of science and art. Thomas Edison was famously documented as "sleeping on a problem" by literally napping in a chair while holding a steel ball bearing in each hand. As he fell into deeper sleep — into the early stages that border hypnagogia — his muscles would relax, the ball bearings would drop onto metal pans on the floor, the sound would wake him, and he would immediately write down whatever had been going through his mind in that liminal state. He was deliberately harvesting the associative, loosely connected thinking of hypnagogia. Salvador Dali used the same technique. That transitional state between wake and sleep seems to be especially generative for creative problem-solving.

**Matt Walker:** There is also formal experimental evidence. A study published in Nature showed that participants given a mathematical transformation task designed to have a hidden shortcut — a much more elegant solution than the brute-force approach they were taught — were significantly more likely to discover the shortcut after a night of sleep compared to those who stayed awake. And when researchers specifically looked at REM sleep, they found it was specifically REM — not non-REM — that was predictive of whether someone discovered the creative insight.

## [02:11:09] Tools: Waking & Technology; Naps; "Sleep on a Problem"

**Andrew Huberman:** Robert Stickgold at Harvard has done foundational work on sleep and memory consolidation, including targeted memory reactivation studies. What practical tools can people take from all of this research on sleep and creativity?

**Matt Walker:** Several. First: do not immediately pick up your phone when you wake up. That transitional state from sleep to wakefulness — hypnopompia — is another window of loosely associated thinking. Many people report that their best ideas come in the moments just after waking, before the analytical left hemisphere kicks in and narrows cognition back to linear, logical thinking. Protect that transitional state. Keep a notepad next to the bed and write down thoughts, associations, fragments before they dissipate.

**Matt Walker:** Second: naps can access some of this creative benefit, particularly if they include REM sleep. Naps later in the day — after noon — are more likely to contain REM sleep than early-morning naps. A 20-minute nap will typically give you stage 1 and stage 2 non-REM; a 90-minute nap has a reasonable chance of including a REM cycle. If your goal is motor learning consolidation, a 20-minute nap with stage 2 sleep spindles will serve you well. If your goal is creative integration, a longer nap that captures REM is more beneficial. Third — the Edison technique operationalized: if you are stuck on a creative problem, allow yourself to enter the hypnagogic state deliberately. Sit in a chair, hold something that will fall and make noise when you fall deeply asleep — Edison's ball bearings, or modern practitioners use keys or a pen — and let yourself drift toward sleep. The loosely associated, analogical thinking that emerges in hypnagogia is genuinely generative.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the folk wisdom of "sleeping on a problem"?

**Matt Walker:** It has robust empirical backing. The mathematical shortcut studies, the associative learning studies, and the overnight alchemy experiments all support the idea that sleep is actively working on problems you have encountered during the day. More fascinatingly: if you explicitly think about a problem before going to sleep — hold it in mind, engage with it seriously — sleep appears to more specifically work on that material. The brain seems to tag problems as high-priority before sleep, and sleep then directs its associative machinery accordingly. "Sleeping on a problem" is not a metaphor — it is literally what the brain is doing.

## [02:20:51] Creative Insight & Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** Before we close out on sleep and creativity, are the specific contents of REM dreams meaningful in terms of creative processing?

**Matt Walker:** Sometimes, in a qualified way. The dreams most associated with creative insight are not literal replays of the day's events — they are the loosely associative, strange, non-linear dreams characteristic of later REM cycles. The strangeness itself seems to be generative: dream content during REM often recombines elements from different parts of life — different time periods, different contexts, different people — in ways that could not happen in waking reality. That recombination is essentially the brain doing analogical reasoning at high bandwidth.

**Matt Walker:** Some researchers argue we can use dream content as a signal for what the brain is working on — if you are dreaming about loosely related elements of a problem, that may indicate active overnight processing of that domain. But I want to be careful not to overstate the degree to which dreams are directly interpretable as messages. The clearest message is at the systems level: REM sleep as a state, characterized by its unique neurochemistry and broad associative connectivity, is a creative engine. Whether specific dream images are directly meaningful is harder to establish scientifically.

**Andrew Huberman:** So to summarize the entire arc of this episode: sleep before learning prepares the hippocampus to receive and encode new information efficiently; sleep after learning consolidates those memories through translocation from hippocampus to cortex and through memory replay; motor skill learning is specifically served by stage 2 non-REM sleep rich in sleep spindles; and REM sleep performs an entirely different function — associative integration of new memories with existing knowledge, and creative recombination that can surface novel insights. All of these functions are time-sensitive — losing sleep means losing these benefits.

**Matt Walker:** That is a beautiful synthesis. I would add only: please sleep. It is not a luxury. It is a non-negotiable biological necessity, and treating it as such is perhaps the most powerful thing any individual can do for their learning, their memory, their creativity, and their health.
