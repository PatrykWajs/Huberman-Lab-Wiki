## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Hormones & Metabolism

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and Ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. This podcast is separate from my teaching and research roles at Stanford. It is however part of my desire and effort to bring zero cost to consumer information about science and science-related tools to the general public.

Today we are going to talk about two hormones — thyroid hormone and its related pathways, and growth hormone and its related pathways — which arguably are the two hormones and two systems in the body that are most significant for setting your overall level of metabolism. Metabolism is the consumption of energy — not necessarily eating, but the use of energy in the cells of the body for growth of tissues, for repair of tissues, and also just for day-to-day maintenance of function. These two hormones, thyroid hormone and growth hormone, we think of as related to metabolism: keeping body fat low and keeping muscles strong and tendons strong and repairing themselves, but they are also key for brain function, for the ability to maintain cognitive function throughout the lifespan.

The big theme I'd like to introduce is that metabolism isn't just about losing weight. Having a high metabolism — provided it's not too high — is great. It means that you will have more lean tissue, more bone and muscle, and less adipose tissue (fat), and we know that that's healthy.

## [00:01:41] Hypothalamus, Pituitary, Thyroid

There are neurons in your brain in an area called the hypothalamus — which just means it's below the thalamus, "hypo." It sits at the base of your brain in the front; it's part of the forebrain, so it's more or less above the roof of your mouth, maybe about a centimeter or so, and then about a centimeter forward in most people. Neurons in the hypothalamus release hormones that are called releasing hormones. So anytime you hear "releasing," chances are those are neurons in your brain — they extend little wires we call axons into your pituitary, and the pituitary releases a bunch of hormones into the bloodstream. The pituitary releases things that most often have the name "stimulating hormone" because they stimulate organs.

In keeping with the theme of thyroid hormone: you have thyroid releasing hormone in the brain, which tells the pituitary to release thyroid stimulating hormone, and then the thyroid releases thyroid hormones. The thyroid is a little butterfly-shaped gland right around the Adam's apple, and it's got four little bumps behind it called the parathyroid gland. It releases two hormones into the blood to stimulate different tissues and their metabolism — those hormones are called T4 and T3.

If this is already sounding like a lot of information — it's really easy, I promise. Releasing hormone comes from the brain, stimulating hormone comes from the pituitary, and in this case we're talking about the thyroid binding up that stimulating hormone and saying "I need to release something," and it releases T4 and T3. You can basically forget about T4 — it's not completely inactive, it has some roles, but T3 is the one that's more or less active.

## [00:03:22] Thyroid Hormone Functions, Tools: Iodine, Selenium, L-Tyrosine

The main role of thyroid hormone — of T3 — is to promote metabolism, and that doesn't just mean the consumption of energy; it means the utilization of energy, including the buildup of tissues. So it acts on all sorts of target tissues in the body: it acts on muscle, it acts on the liver, it acts on the cartilage, it acts on the bone. It's involved in taking fats and breaking them down into fatty acids and converting those into ATP, which is an important energy currency for cells. It's also involved in taking sugars and turning those into energy. And yes, it goes to adipose tissue — to fat — and it liberates, or helps liberate, some of the fats from those fat cells and uses them for energy. This is why higher thyroid is associated with leaner bodies, and lower thyroid is associated with less lean bodies.

One thing that's absolutely key and actionable is iodine. Iodine is most common in sea salt, in kelp, and in seaweed, and most people can get enough iodine from the food they eat and/or the table salt they consume. Almost all table salt from all over the world, regardless of where you are, contains iodine. The thyroid needs iodine in order to produce thyroid hormone. Iodine combines with an amino acid called L-tyrosine — L-tyrosine comes from meat, from nuts, there are some plant-based sources as well; it is the precursor to dopamine — but in the thyroid, iodine combines with or works with L-tyrosine to produce T3 and T4, the thyroid hormone. So you absolutely need sufficient iodine, you need sufficient L-tyrosine, and then you also need something called selenium.

Selenium is important for thyroid hormone to be made because of the way it allows L-tyrosine and iodine to interact. The thing is, most people aren't getting enough selenium because they don't eat foods that are high in selenium. How much selenium you need will depend on where you live — it actually varies country by country. Some countries say you should get 100 micrograms, some say 200, some say 155. The average from the countries I looked at was about 155 micrograms. People who are trying to increase thyroid levels might want to consume more selenium. If you consume a vitamin that has selenium, you want to make sure you're not overdoing it by also consuming a lot of selenium-rich foods.

Brazil nuts are the heavyweight champion of foods to get selenium from — just six or eight Brazil nuts contain something like 550 micrograms of selenium. Selenium is also present in fish; ham contains a lot of selenium, as does pork. Beef has some selenium. But if you look at sources like pork, beef, turkey, chicken, cottage cheese, eggs, and brown rice, they have something like 30 to 50 micrograms of selenium. So if you're not eating Brazil nuts and you're not eating a lot of animal-based foods, then you're probably not getting enough selenium. You can have these levels measured or you can check what you're consuming and figure out whether you're meeting the amount you need in order to get healthy levels of thyroid.

For children, the daily requirements of selenium are much lower — as low as 30 or 40 micrograms for kids 14 years or younger. Again, that's micrograms, not milligrams. If you're interested in keeping thyroid function healthy and productive, you certainly want to make sure you're getting enough iodine, enough selenium, and enough L-tyrosine. When you start looking at various foods — especially highly processed foods — you start to realize that perhaps many people are not getting adequate amounts of these nutrients.

## [00:07:55] Thyroid Hormone, Glucose & Metabolism; Thyroid Hormone Disorders

If you're curious how thyroid actually increases metabolism and allows you to eat more, it relates to glucose. When you eat something, blood sugar goes up; insulin is secreted from the pancreas and it makes sure that blood glucose doesn't go too high (which can damage tissues) or too low (which can make you hypoglycemic). Thyroid increases glucose uptake by various tissues — in particular muscle and bone. It can actually increase bone mineral density, which is a really good thing as you get older. When I say older, I mean basically 30 and older.

The reason you can recover more quickly from injuries if you have a healthy thyroid is because you can consume energy and that energy is diverted toward bone repair, muscle repair, and cartilage repair. The way it does this again is by increasing ATP. The whole idea here is that iodine, selenium, and L-tyrosine allow thyroid to be at a healthy level so that thyroid can then take glucose in the blood and divert it to tissues for it to be used — in particular your brain. That's why the ability of your brain to use glucose (or ketones, for that matter) is going to be aided by having healthy thyroid.

If you're concerned about having excessively high or excessively low levels of thyroid hormone, look up what the symptoms are and talk to your physician. There are a number of good treatments — prescription drugs that can improve symptoms related to hypo- or hyperthyroid. If you don't make enough thyroid, you can take thyroid by prescription. If you have too much thyroid, sometimes they'll take out the thyroid gland, or they can administer drugs that will either block receptors or interfere with some of the pathways from the brain to the pituitary or from the pituitary to the thyroid. But we're talking here about how to get and maintain thyroid levels in healthy ranges through some straightforward ways — diet and supplementation.

## [00:10:14] Growth Hormone Functions, Prescription Growth Hormone

Next we're going to talk about growth hormone. Growth hormone is pretty straightforward to understand because it follows the exact same logic as thyroid hormone. In fact their functions are so closely overlapping that you're probably going to think, why do you have these two systems? Very briefly: growth hormone releasing hormone comes from the brain and tells the pituitary to release growth hormone, and then growth hormone is released into the bloodstream where it goes and acts on a ton of tissues — muscle, ligaments, bone, fat — to increase metabolism. It sounds just like thyroid hormone, and they do work in parallel. That's why we've lumped them together in the same episode. They increase metabolism and repair and growth of tissues.

Today we're going to talk about the things that anyone can do to increase growth hormone. There are reasons why certain people would want to do that. People who make normal levels of growth hormone might want to do that as they age, because during puberty and development the pituitary is churning out tons of growth hormone — it's responsible for the growth of the body and all its features, height being just one of those. As we age we make less growth hormone, and that is one of the reasons why we recover more slowly from injuries, one of the reasons why we accumulate body fat, and one of the reasons why our metabolism slows.

Growth hormone replacement therapy has been tremendously popular in the last 20 years — which is not to say it doesn't carry problems, because it does. Here's one of the major problems with injecting growth hormone: if it's at levels that are too high, it will cause growth of all tissues — not just muscle and not just reduction in body fat by metabolizing fatty acids, but it will also cause increase in growth of the heart, the lungs, the liver, and the spleen. This is the concern with abuse of growth hormone. We're not going to be talking about abuse of growth hormone. We will however talk about tools that anyone can use to increase levels of growth hormone — some behavioral, some supplement-based, and some that interact with both.

## [00:13:10] Growth Hormone Release, Tools: Sleep & Bedtime Fasting

Growth hormone is released every night when you go to sleep, and it's released in the early part of sleep during so-called slow wave sleep. The two conditions that have to be met in order for growth hormone to be secreted regularly for tissue repair are: you need to get into slow wave sleep (the so-called deep sleep), and you need blood insulin and glucose to be relatively low. Eating within 2 hours of going to sleep is going to suppress growth hormone release — that's very clear.

What is special about this early phase of sleep? In particular, what about slow wave deep sleep allows the pituitary to release growth hormone? The answer is delta wave activity in the brain. Delta waves are these big, giant waves of activity in the brain that correlate with slow wave sleep, as opposed to faster waves of activity that associate with rapid eye movement sleep. It's the delta waves of activity — these sweeping big waves of activity in the neurons — that stimulate the brain to stimulate the pituitary. Once you understand that, you have something to anchor to in terms of thinking about what things you can do in waking that will allow you to release more growth hormone, which for most people is going to be a good thing.

## [00:14:35] Growth Hormone Release, Tools: Meditation; Exercise, Warm-Up, Glucose, Cool Down

The delta wave activity and slow wave activity in the brain being so important for growth hormone release — and growth hormone release being so important for metabolic functions, peeling away unwanted body fat, and repairing tissues — forces us to ask: what other things can we do in waking in order to increase growth hormone release?

Let's start with the ones that have a potentially big effect but are a little bit harder to access. For that I want to point toward a book called "Altered Traits" by Goldman and Davidson — "Science Reveals How Meditation Changes Your Mind, Brain and Body." It's an excellent book for those interested in meditation. What they talk about is that meditation has two separate lines of effects: one changes your state (you're stressed, you sit down, you meditate and relax), and the other produces changes over time — changes in trait. Personality can actually change with long bouts of TM meditation or repeated meditation practice.

The reason we're talking about "Altered Traits" is because certain types of meditation can get people's brains into states that very closely mimic slow wave sleep. What this means is that a meditation practice allowing you to get into slow wave delta-type frequency activity in the brain may be very beneficial for growth hormone release. As mentioned before, that's what's gating growth hormone release — it's not just a circadian phenomenon, it's actually controlled by these brain waves.

Now let's move to the things that have been shown to have pretty enormous effects on growth hormone release in waking, and these are very actionable. Exercise is one of the things that can have a dramatic effect on levels of growth hormone release in waking as well as in sleep the following night. There are hundreds if not thousands of studies measuring growth hormone during or after exercise, or the following night, and the conclusion is that exercise has to be of particular duration and intensity in order to get growth hormone release.

The form of exercise that maximizes growth hormone release is either weight training or endurance training, but both have to be limited to about 60 minutes — not much longer. A proper warm-up seems to accelerate the release of growth hormone once the hard work phase starts. About 10 minutes of warm-up was the number I extracted from all these studies. When I say warm up, it doesn't just mean warming up the limbs and tissues you're going to use so you don't get injured — actually warming the body, getting the body warm as a warm-up, seems to be important because temperature of the body seems to be an important condition or prerequisite for certain patterns of exercise to maximize growth hormone release.

For weight-bearing exercise, getting close to those final repetitions where you can't complete them — but not necessarily going to failure, just getting close — leads to anywhere from 300 to 500% increases in resting growth hormone levels, and 300 to 500% increases in growth hormone the following night when you go to sleep, which is incredible.

One of the other conditions that seemed important again was having relatively low blood glucose — probably not eating too close to exercise or not ingesting a lot of sugars during exercise. That was supported by the fact that ingestion of a sports drink containing caloric sugar immediately flatlined growth hormone levels. There's a really interesting relationship between insulin, glucose, and growth hormone.

Then the other interesting thing was that even after exercise, taking body temperature back down to normal levels relatively quickly seemed to be associated with these big spikes in growth hormone. Otherwise, you might get the big spikes in growth hormone, but if exercise went too long or if body temperature remained too high for too long, then you didn't get the second increase in growth hormone the following night.

## [00:19:30] Growth Hormone Supplements, Arginine

Let's talk about supplements. The amino acid arginine can increase growth hormone levels substantially. The amounts of arginine required to get big growth hormone release are pretty substantial. Some people take arginine before bedtime, some take it before exercise. The prerequisite again is low blood glucose — if blood glucose is high, it's going to quash the effect. The amounts of arginine people take range from 3 grams to 10 grams or sometimes even more, although this is definitely a case where more is not better. There's a threshold at which growth hormone release is actually blunted by taking more than 9 grams of arginine.

Nine grams of arginine orally is a lot of pills — at minimum nine pills — and it can cause some GI disturbance. People can feel nauseous, some will throw it up. What's interesting however is that whether by mouth or by vein, taking arginine can dramatically increase growth hormone release — 400 to 600% above baseline. These are huge increases in growth hormone.

Here is something really important and interesting to note: increasing arginine levels with the specific goal of increasing growth hormone release can actually short-circuit the effects of exercise on growth hormone. Several studies looked at the interaction of taking arginine combined with exercise versus just arginine or just exercise alone. Unfortunately, if growth hormone increases are your goal, you don't get to increase growth hormone 800% by taking arginine and exercising. It always seems to be clamped at about 300 to 500% increases. So hopefully that's clear.

## [00:21:31] Offsetting Age-Related Growth Hormone Decline

Something particularly interesting — as somebody in his 40s — is that it's actually between ages 30 and 40 that the amount of growth hormone you release each night is reduced by two to threefold. Since everybody goes through this age-related decline very dramatically, it seems to me that the things we're supposed to be doing anyway — like exercising, like trying not to eat too close to bedtime, trying to optimize sleep — all of these are wonderful tools that we should be pursuing. They can actually offset the two to threefold decrease. If we're talking about a two to threefold decrease for people in their 30s and 40s, and then we're talking about increases from exercise of 300 to 500%, well then all of a sudden we're in a position to actually offset the age-related decline in growth hormone completely, just through behaviors. I think that's quite interesting and quite powerful.

## [00:22:30] Temperature & Growth Hormone, Tool: Sauna Protocol

I'd like to discuss a way that anyone can increase their levels of growth hormone dramatically — and when I say dramatically, I mean dramatically. We have to remember how growth hormone is released in the first place. Remember, it all starts in the brain in the hypothalamus. The hypothalamus is a brain area that controls things like sexual behavior, temperature regulation, circadian behavior (meaning when you want to be awake and asleep), aggression, and more. It has a rich collection of different neurons involved in all these very basic functions.

As we talked about, the growth hormone releasing hormone comes from neurons in the hypothalamus, those communicate with the pituitary, the pituitary releases growth hormone, and then the growth hormone acts on all these different tissues — muscle, liver, cartilage, body fat — making them use energy. That's why you lose body fat when growth hormone levels are high, and why it makes you grow muscle and strengthens bones.

One of the things that has a profound effect on growth hormone release is temperature. The data on this are very strong — from both animal studies and human studies. You can probably imagine that making animals or people warmer is the way to go if you want to increase growth hormone. Now, anytime you're going to increase temperature, it is risky. I want to be really clear about this: not everyone should engage in the behaviors I'm about to describe. The reason it's risky is it doesn't take much of a temperature increase in the brain to cook neurons, and after that point neurons can't come back, and people can die from hyperthermia.

However, there are really strong data pointing to the fact that sauna — deliberate hyperthermia, but not too high — can increase the release of growth hormone and other hormones. What's so dramatic about this literature is the size of the effects. Entering environments where it's very hot for short periods of time — anywhere from 20 minutes to 30 minutes where the temperature is 80°C to 100°C — has been shown to increase growth hormone release 16-fold. That's right, 16-fold — that's 1,600%.

Now there are also effects on other hormones like prolactin and cortisol. The pattern described in the study — there have been many studies now — was that entering a sauna for 20 minutes, followed by 30 minutes of cooling, followed by 20 minutes in the sauna again, led to a five-fold increase in growth hormone. And by doing that day after day on the third day, you would see these huge increases of up to 16-fold. These increases in growth hormone probably stem from increased activity of neurons within the hypothalamus that stimulate growth hormone release from the pituitary — probably because the growth hormone releasing hormone neurons in the hypothalamus sit very closely and may even be intermixed with some of the neurons in the hypothalamus that regulate heat and body temperature. Remember, metabolism is in part heat — it's like a furnace of how much energy you're consuming and using for building or energy usage purposes.

Sauna can be very, very interesting. Again, it's 20 minutes, 30 minutes cooling, 20 minutes again. Proceed with extreme caution, but nonetheless these are pretty extreme effects in terms of their ability to increase growth hormone levels.

## [00:26:49] Peptides, Sermorelin, Secretagogues, Risk

There's a kind of new area that's developing that deserves our attention — not because I'm encouraging it but because it is happening — and those are peptides. These days you hear a lot about peptides. Peptides is a really huge category of biological compounds. Peptides are just strings of amino acids. We've talked about L-tyrosine, arginine, ornithine — those are individual amino acids — and those are put together into small peptides or polypeptides, which are just longer peptides.

It turns out that for any substance like growth hormone or growth hormone releasing hormone, it's made up of different amino acids in different sequences — just like your genes are made up of A's, G's, C's, and T's (nucleotides) in different sequences. It's like a recipe. Peptides tend to be short sequences of amino acids that resemble a hormone enough — or resemble some other peptide enough — that they can lead to similar or the same effects when injected.

For example, we make growth hormone releasing hormone from our brain, which stimulates growth hormone from the pituitary. But people now will take things like sermorelin — S-E-R-M-O-R-E-L-I-N, sermorelin — which is not the entire peptide sequence of growth hormone releasing hormone, but it's a subset of it that stimulates the release of growth hormone from the pituitary. So this is not taking growth hormone itself; this is taking the stimulating hormone or what's often called a secretagogue, or a mimic. It causes secretion of the hormone one wants. Sermorelin is by prescription.

Do they work? Yes. Do they shut down your natural production of growth hormone releasing hormone? Well, the answer is yes. But some of these peptides actually have the effect of changing gene expression. Recall that big increases in growth hormone that are short-lived — like from exercise, or arginine, or sauna — seem to have these huge effects but they are transient. However, when one is injecting over and over at a constant level, you can put into action gene expression programs that can be long-lived. And let's say you have a particular tumor in the body — tumors will grow when they see growth hormone, even if that tumor is unhealthy for you. You've got growth of tissues all over the body. So again, not promoting their use, but they're definitely out there. Now if you hear about them or someone's talking to you about them, hopefully you have a better understanding about their underlying biology and can think rationally about whether or not they are the right decision for you.
