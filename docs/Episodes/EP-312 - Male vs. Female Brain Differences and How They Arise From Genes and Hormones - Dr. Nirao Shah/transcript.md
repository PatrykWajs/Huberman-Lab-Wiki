# Male vs. Female Brain Differences & How They Arise From Genes & Hormones | Dr. Nirao Shah

## [00:00:00] Nirao Shah

**Andrew Huberman:** Welcome to the Huberman Lab Podcast, where we discuss science and science-based tools for everyday life. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. My guest today is Dr. Nirao Shah. Dr. Shah is a professor of psychiatry, behavioral sciences and neurobiology at Stanford University School of Medicine. Dr. Shah's laboratory studies the neural mechanisms underlying innate behaviors — behaviors that don't have to be learned, they're just hard-wired into us at birth. These include mating behaviors, parenting behaviors, and aggression. And a major focus of the lab is on how those neural circuits differ between males and females, and how hormones — specifically sex hormones — shape the development of those circuits. This is a beautiful and incredibly rich episode. It spans everything from chromosomes to brain circuits to drug targets to the fascinating ways that male and female brains differ in how they process the world.

**Nirao Shah:** Thank you, Andrew. I really appreciate you having me on the show.

## [00:02:11] Mice, Humans & Brain, Biological Conservation

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to start with something that I think listeners need to understand upfront, which is why most of the work in your lab uses mice, and what we can infer from mouse studies about human biology.

**Nirao Shah:** That's a great question. So mice, they have a similar basic brain architecture as humans. The hypothalamus, which is one of the key structures that we study in the context of these behaviors, is remarkably similar between mouse and human. The same neurons that exist in the mouse hypothalamus exist in the human hypothalamus. They express the same genes, they're organized similarly. And the behaviors that we study — mating, aggression, parenting — exist in mice as they do in humans. The neurons and circuits that we identify in the mouse, we can then use cutting-edge genetic and optogenetic tools to manipulate and understand. That's harder to do in humans. And importantly, many of the basic findings in mice have been validated in non-human primates and in humans where studies have been done.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a beautiful concept in biology called evolutionary conservation — meaning that something is so fundamental to life that it was preserved across millions of years of evolution. And what I'm hearing you say is that the hypothalamus and the circuits that regulate mating, aggression, and parenting are among the most conserved structures in all of biology.

**Nirao Shah:** That's exactly right. So the hypothalamus is a very ancient brain region. It regulates the same basic survival and reproductive functions across all vertebrates — fish, frogs, mice, humans. The circuits we study in mice have been evolutionarily conserved across enormous spans of time, which is why those mouse findings are so translationally relevant.

## [00:05:25] Hormones, Nature vs Nurture

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about one of the most contested topics in all of biology and social science, which is nature versus nurture. How do you think about the role of biological factors — specifically genes and hormones — versus environmental and social factors in shaping behavior?

**Nirao Shah:** So I'll answer this from the perspective of innate behaviors. Behaviors like mating and aggression exist across species, they don't have to be learned, they're present even in animals raised in isolation, they're regulated by very specific brain circuits, and hormones modulate those circuits. So in that sense, for these innate behaviors, the biological underpinning is very strong. Now, in humans, the picture is more complex. Humans have a large cortex that allows enormous behavioral flexibility and learning. So a behavior that has a strong biological component in terms of its underlying circuits can still be modulated enormously by experience, culture, social learning. The nature-nurture dichotomy is a false one — biology and environment interact constantly.

## [00:07:13] Biological Sex Differences, Chromosomes & SRY Gene, Hormones

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's start at the very beginning — at conception. What determines biological sex?

**Nirao Shah:** So biological sex in mammals is determined at conception by the chromosomes. Females have two X chromosomes; males have an X and a Y. The Y chromosome carries a gene called SRY — sex-determining region Y. SRY is a transcription factor, meaning it turns on other genes. And the key thing it does is it turns on a cascade that leads to the development of the testes from what would otherwise become ovaries. So SRY is the master switch. You have SRY, you make testes. Without SRY, you make ovaries.

**Andrew Huberman:** So it's one gene.

**Nirao Shah:** It is one gene. Just one. And remarkably, that one gene on the Y chromosome is the thing that sets in motion all downstream sex differences. The testes then make testosterone and anti-Müllerian hormone. Testosterone masculinizes the body — the external genitalia, internal reproductive structures. Anti-Müllerian hormone causes regression of what would otherwise become the female internal reproductive tract, the Müllerian ducts. So you have this cascade from one gene to two hormones to complete bodily sex differentiation.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the default is female.

**Nirao Shah:** The default developmental trajectory, in the absence of SRY, is female. In the absence of testes and testosterone, the embryo develops as a female. So female is in some sense the default developmental program.

**Andrew Huberman:** How does this translate to the brain?

**Nirao Shah:** So the testosterone made by the testes acts on the brain during fetal development and in the neonatal period in rodents. And it permanently changes the structure and wiring of certain brain circuits. This is called the organizational effect of hormones — testosterone organizes the brain during a critical period of development. These effects are largely irreversible. Then later, at puberty, a second wave of hormones — again testosterone in males, estrogen in females — acts on those pre-organized circuits to activate sex-specific behaviors. These are called activational effects. So the brain has been organized by early hormones, and later hormones activate the behaviors encoded by that organization.

## [00:19:09] Androgen Mutations, Feminization & Masculinization

**Andrew Huberman:** There are natural human experiments — genetic conditions — that really illuminate the importance of androgens in sex differentiation. Can you walk us through some of them?

**Nirao Shah:** Absolutely. One of the most informative is androgen insensitivity syndrome, or AIS. These are XY individuals — genetically male — who have a loss-of-function mutation in the androgen receptor. So they make testosterone normally, but their cells can't respond to it. The result is that they develop a completely female external appearance — female body shape, no body hair, female genitalia externally. They have testes internally, not ovaries. But they look and feel female. Most are raised as female and identify as female. So this shows that it's not the Y chromosome per se that masculinizes the body and brain — it's the downstream hormonal action. Without androgen signaling, an XY individual develops as female.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's also a condition going the other direction — XX individuals exposed to excess androgens.

**Nirao Shah:** Yes — congenital adrenal hyperplasia, or CAH. In CAH, there's a mutation in an enzyme in the cortisol synthesis pathway. The body can't make cortisol properly, so precursors are shunted into the androgen synthesis pathway, producing excess androgens — specifically androstenedione. In XX fetuses with CAH, this excess androgen partially masculinizes the external genitalia and the brain. These girls are born with ambiguous or masculinized genitalia. They tend to show more male-typical toy preferences, higher rates of same-sex attraction, and some shift in gender identity compared to XX controls. This is really important evidence that prenatal androgen exposure shapes not just anatomy but behavior and, to some degree, gender identity.

**Andrew Huberman:** How common is CAH?

**Nirao Shah:** The full homozygous condition is not very common — about 1 in 15,000 births. But the heterozygous carrier state — one mutant copy — is quite common, about 1 in 12 people. Carriers generally have normal development but slightly elevated androgen levels.

## [00:22:04] SRY Gene; Animals & Sexual Trans-Differentiation

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there examples in nature where animals change sex?

**Nirao Shah:** Yes, fascinating ones. Some fish species can spontaneously change sex depending on social environment. In certain coral reef fish like clownfish, if the dominant female of a group dies, the dominant male will change sex and become female. This involves changes in gonadal function, hormone production, and ultimately behavior. The brain circuits that regulate sex-typical behaviors rewire. These examples illustrate that in some species, sex is not permanently fixed at conception the way it is in mammals. The SRY-based mammalian system is actually quite different from these more plastic systems.

**Andrew Huberman:** What does this tell us about the relationship between hormones and brain circuits more broadly?

**Nirao Shah:** It tells us that the brain circuits underlying sex-typical behaviors are responsive to the hormonal environment throughout life in some species. In mammals, the early organizational effects are largely irreversible — which is why prenatal hormone exposure has such lasting effects. But even in mammals, adult hormone levels continue to modulate behavioral expression. The organized circuits still need hormonal input to be activated. This is the organizing versus activating distinction.

## [00:27:49] Hormones & Biological Brain Differentiation

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you walk us through the classic experiments that established that hormones organize the brain?

**Nirao Shah:** The landmark study was by Charles Phoenix, David Edwards, and colleagues in 1959. They treated pregnant guinea pigs with testosterone during pregnancy. The female offspring born to those mothers — XX females who had been exposed to excess testosterone in utero — showed masculinized sexual behavior. Instead of displaying lordosis, the typical female receptive posture, they displayed mounting behavior — typical male behavior. And crucially, this behavioral change was permanent. Even if you then removed testosterone in adulthood and gave estrogen, these prenatally masculinized females wouldn't show normal female behavior. The early testosterone had permanently organized their brain circuits in a male-typical way.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the timing matters.

**Nirao Shah:** Timing is everything. There are specific critical periods — windows of development when the brain is especially sensitive to hormonal input. Miss that window and the organizing effects don't happen, or happen incompletely. In rodents, the critical period for brain masculinization is late prenatal and neonatal. In humans, it's thought to be primarily prenatal, during the second trimester when fetal testosterone levels peak in males.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the brain is a lot like the genitalia in this sense — it gets organized early by hormones and those effects are largely locked in.

**Nirao Shah:** Exactly. The same hormonal signals that differentiate the body also differentiate the brain. It's all part of one coordinated developmental program.

## [00:31:22] Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia, Androstenedione; Stress & Pregnancy

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned that in CAH, it's androstenedione specifically — not testosterone — that's the primary androgen being overproduced. Why does that matter?

**Nirao Shah:** So androstenedione is a precursor to both testosterone and estrogen. It's a weaker androgen than testosterone but it still activates androgen receptors to some degree. In CAH, the excess androstenedione is what masculinizes XX fetuses. This is important because it tells us the system is quite sensitive to androgen levels during development — even a partial androgen signal can shift the developmental trajectory.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's also data on psychological stress during pregnancy affecting fetal androgen levels and subsequent behavior in offspring.

**Nirao Shah:** Yes, this is interesting and somewhat controversial. There are reports in rodents that maternal stress during pregnancy elevates stress hormones, which interact with the fetal HPG axis and can affect fetal androgen production. Some studies have found that stressed mothers produce offspring with behavioral changes consistent with altered prenatal androgen exposure. In humans, the evidence is more correlational and harder to disentangle from other factors. But the principle makes biological sense — the fetal endocrine environment is not isolated from the mother's hormonal state.

## [00:35:56] Genes, Brain Differentiation & Sexual Identity; Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia

**Andrew Huberman:** CAH gives us a window into the relationship between prenatal hormones and gender identity. What do the data actually show?

**Nirao Shah:** So CAH females — XX individuals with excess prenatal androgen exposure — show a shift in gender identity compared to non-CAH XX females. A higher percentage of CAH females identify as non-binary or male, or experience gender dysphoria. But the shift is not complete. Most CAH females still identify as female. So prenatal androgen exposure shifts the probability of a male gender identity but doesn't determine it absolutely. This suggests gender identity has a biological component — it's influenced by prenatal hormones — but other factors, including genes, early postnatal hormones, and environmental factors, also contribute.

**Andrew Huberman:** So gender identity is partly biological but not fully determined by prenatal hormones.

**Nirao Shah:** That's the best reading of the current evidence, yes. The data from CAH and from AIS and from 5-alpha reductase deficiency all point toward a biological contribution to gender identity. But it's a contribution, not a complete determination. The biology provides a predisposition; the full expression involves many factors.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you describe 5-alpha reductase deficiency?

**Nirao Shah:** Yes — this is a fascinating condition, sometimes called "penis at 12" syndrome. Individuals are XY, they make testosterone normally, but they lack the enzyme 5-alpha reductase that converts testosterone to DHT — dihydrotestosterone, the more potent androgen that masculinizes external genitalia in utero. So these XY individuals are born with ambiguous or female-appearing external genitalia, and they are typically raised as female. Then at puberty, when testosterone surges and there's now enough testosterone even without DHT conversion, the external genitalia masculinize — they develop a penis and testes descend. And remarkably, the majority of these individuals then adopt a male gender identity and male social roles. This is taken as evidence that prenatal testosterone — despite not producing male-typical genitalia — had organized the brain in a male-typical way, and this becomes expressed at puberty.

## [00:43:37] Testosterone, Estrogen & Brain Circuits

**Andrew Huberman:** How does testosterone actually act on the brain? Does testosterone itself do the work, or is it converted to something else?

**Nirao Shah:** This is one of the most counterintuitive findings in all of neuroendocrinology. In the brain, testosterone is converted to estrogen by an enzyme called aromatase. And it's the estrogen converted from testosterone in the male brain that actually causes brain masculinization. This was discovered by Frank Naftolin at Yale in the 1970s. The brain of a male fetus is being masculinized by estrogen — not testosterone directly.

**Andrew Huberman:** That seems backwards.

**Nirao Shah:** It seems completely backwards, I know. But it's true. The male brain has high aromatase activity, particularly in the hypothalamus. Testosterone gets converted to estrogen locally in the brain. That local estrogen then activates estrogen receptors in the hypothalamus and other regions, and drives the organizational changes that result in male-typical brain wiring.

**Andrew Huberman:** Why doesn't the female brain get masculinized by circulating estrogen?

**Nirao Shah:** Because during the critical period, the female fetus has a protein in its bloodstream called alpha-fetoprotein, which binds estrogen in the bloodstream and prevents it from entering the brain. So even though female fetuses have circulating estrogen, that estrogen is sequestered by alpha-fetoprotein and can't act on the brain. The testosterone in male fetuses is not bound by alpha-fetoprotein — so it gets into the brain, gets converted to estrogen by aromatase, and masculinizes the hypothalamus.

**Andrew Huberman:** Elegant.

**Nirao Shah:** It's very elegant. Evolution found a clever solution to a tricky problem.

## [00:50:36] Intersex Individuals, Castration

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about intersex conditions more broadly. How do we define intersex, and how common is it?

**Nirao Shah:** Intersex is a broad umbrella term for individuals who are born with reproductive or sexual anatomy, chromosomes, or hormone levels that don't fit typical definitions of male or female. That includes conditions like AIS, CAH, 5-alpha reductase deficiency, Klinefelter syndrome (XXY), Turner syndrome (XO), and others. The frequency depends enormously on how broadly you define intersex. If you use a very broad definition including things like late-onset CAH, the estimates can be as high as 1–2% of the population. With a narrower definition focused on cases where sex is ambiguous at birth, it's much less common — around 1 in 2,000 births.

**Andrew Huberman:** What does castration tell us about the role of ongoing hormone levels on behavior in adults?

**Nirao Shah:** Castration experiments in rodents have been fundamental to understanding the activational role of hormones. If you castrate a sexually experienced adult male mouse, his sexual behavior declines over a few weeks and eventually stops. If you then give him back testosterone, his behavior is restored — and it doesn't matter when during the day you give it; the behavioral response is the same. This tells you the adult brain circuits are organized and ready to be activated; they just need the hormonal signal. Contrast this with females, where giving estrogen and progesterone to an ovariectomized female only induces sexual behavior if you give the hormones in the right sequence and at the right time — mimicking the hormonal profile of the estrous cycle.

## [00:52:23] Female Sexual Behavior, Brain, Testosterone & Pheromones

**Andrew Huberman:** What drives female sexual behavior in rodents? Is it estrogen?

**Nirao Shah:** Primarily yes — but the story is more complex. Female sexual receptivity — the willingness to mate — is largely driven by the surge of estrogen followed by progesterone that occurs around ovulation. Estrogen acts on specific neurons in the hypothalamus, including neurons in the ventromedial hypothalamus, to promote receptive behavior. But there's also an interesting role for testosterone in female sexual motivation. Females have low levels of testosterone from the adrenal glands, and this testosterone contributes to libido — the drive to seek out mating opportunities, as distinct from receptivity.

**Andrew Huberman:** And pheromones play a role too.

**Nirao Shah:** Yes, especially in rodents. Mice have a vomeronasal organ that detects pheromones — chemical signals from other individuals. These pheromone signals go to a specialized olfactory pathway that projects directly to the hypothalamus. Male pheromones detected by females promote sexual behavior; female pheromones detected by males promote sexual behavior. The pheromone signals carry information about sex, reproductive state, and even genetic identity. In humans, the vomeronasal organ is vestigial — it's present but largely non-functional. But humans still have olfactory cues that influence social and sexual behavior, even if we're less aware of them.

## [00:57:58] Identify as Heterosexual or Homosexual, Difference in Hormone Levels?

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a biological basis for sexual orientation? Do gay and straight individuals have different hormone levels?

**Nirao Shah:** In terms of circulating adult hormone levels — testosterone, estrogen — no. Gay men do not have lower testosterone than straight men. Lesbian women do not have higher testosterone than straight women. So sexual orientation is not explained by differences in adult circulating hormones.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about prenatal hormone exposure?

**Nirao Shah:** There's more evidence there. Studies of gay men have found subtle differences in anatomical markers of prenatal androgen exposure — things like the ratio of index finger to ring finger length, which is thought to reflect prenatal testosterone levels. Gay men on average show a pattern somewhat shifted toward the female distribution — lower prenatal androgen exposure. And in CAH females, the rate of same-sex attraction is elevated. This is consistent with the idea that prenatal hormone exposure is one factor among many that influences sexual orientation.

**Andrew Huberman:** But it's not deterministic.

**Nirao Shah:** Not deterministic at all. Sexual orientation is influenced by many factors — genetics, prenatal hormones, possibly early postnatal hormones, and perhaps environmental and experiential factors. The prenatal hormone contribution is real but partial.

## [01:00:42] Gender, Sexual Orientation & Hormones; Hormone Replacement Therapy

**Andrew Huberman:** In your view, is gender — as distinct from biological sex — a useful scientific category?

**Nirao Shah:** Gender is a very human-specific construct. It involves self-identity, social roles, and how one presents oneself to the world. We don't have good animal models of gender — you can't ask a mouse how it identifies. The science I can speak to is biological sex — chromosomes, hormones, anatomy, brain circuits. Gender is real and important, but it's a psychological and social construct that maps onto, but is not identical to, biological sex.

**Andrew Huberman:** What does HRT — hormone replacement therapy — do to the brain?

**Nirao Shah:** For trans individuals undergoing gender-affirming HRT, the hormones do affect the brain. Cross-sex hormones produce changes in brain structure and function — MRI studies have shown that testosterone treatment in trans men and estrogen treatment in trans women shift some brain metrics toward the experienced gender. Whether these changes are complete or partial mirrors of the natal sex brain is still an area of active research.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about HRT for postmenopausal women?

**Nirao Shah:** There's substantial evidence that estrogen has neuroprotective effects. Estrogen maintains blood vessel health in the brain, it acts directly on neurons, it supports synaptic plasticity. Postmenopausal women who lose estrogen show an increased risk of cognitive decline and Alzheimer's disease. There is evidence — though contested — that hormone replacement therapy, particularly when started early in the menopause transition, can reduce this risk. The Women's Health Initiative study was initially interpreted as showing HRT increased dementia risk, but subsequent analysis showed that timing matters enormously — starting HRT early after menopause onset appears protective; starting it late may not be.

## [01:10:21] Aromatization; Steroid Hormones & Gene Expression

**Andrew Huberman:** We talked about aromatization — testosterone converting to estrogen in the brain. Can you explain the mechanism by which steroid hormones like estrogen and testosterone act at the cellular level?

**Nirao Shah:** Steroid hormones are lipid-soluble, which means they cross the cell membrane and enter the cell. Inside the cell, they bind to their receptor — the estrogen receptor, the androgen receptor. The hormone-receptor complex then acts as a transcription factor — it goes to the nucleus and directly regulates gene expression. It binds to specific DNA sequences and turns genes on or off. So steroids fundamentally change the gene expression program of the cells they act on. This is why their effects can be so profound and lasting — you're changing which genes are being made into proteins, which changes the entire character of the cell and circuit.

**Andrew Huberman:** And this is different from classical neurotransmitters which act on timescales of milliseconds.

**Nirao Shah:** Completely different timescale. Steroid hormone effects develop over hours to days as new gene expression changes protein levels and ultimately circuit function. This is why testosterone given to a castrated male doesn't immediately restore sexual behavior — it takes days to weeks for the gene expression changes to take effect.

## [01:15:00] Kids & Changing Gender Identity

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a lot of public discussion about gender identity in children — including whether children who express gender dysphoria should be socially transitioned or given puberty blockers. What does the science tell us about gender identity development in children?

**Nirao Shah:** This is an area where the science is still developing and where I'll try to be careful to represent what we know and don't know. What we know is that most young children who express gender dysphoria — the sense of mismatch between their experienced gender and their biological sex — most of them, by adulthood, no longer report gender dysphoria. That's been the finding in studies following children over time. A subset — particularly those with more persistent and intense dysphoria — do continue to identify as transgender into adulthood. The challenge is that we don't yet have reliable biomarkers to predict at a young age which children will persist and which will desist. Puberty blockers, when used to pause puberty, do affect long-term outcomes in ways that aren't fully understood yet. This is an area where more longitudinal research is urgently needed.

## [01:19:05] Sexual Behavior, Refractory Period & Male Brain, Tacr1 Cells

**Andrew Huberman:** Your lab made a remarkable discovery about the neural basis of the male refractory period. Can you walk us through that?

**Nirao Shah:** Yes, this was a very exciting finding. The male refractory period — the time a male needs between successive matings — is regulated by a specific set of neurons in the preoptic area of the hypothalamus. These neurons express a gene called Tacr1, which encodes the receptor for a peptide called substance P. We found about 2,500 of these Tacr1 neurons in the male mouse preoptic hypothalamus. When we optogenetically activated these neurons during a mating event, the refractory period was completely abolished. Males would mate again essentially immediately — within about one second instead of the normal four to five days.

**Andrew Huberman:** One second versus four to five days.

**Nirao Shah:** That's right. The normal inter-mating interval in male mice is four to five days. With these neurons activated, it was about one second — essentially no refractory period. And these neurons are active during mating and during the refractory period. We think they encode the reward value of mating by projecting to the VTA — the ventral tegmental area — which then releases dopamine into the nucleus accumbens. This is the canonical reward circuit.

**Andrew Huberman:** So blocking this reward signal is what creates the refractory period?

**Nirao Shah:** That's our current model, yes. The Tacr1 neurons are active during mating. After mating, something in the circuit maintains them in an inhibited state — and during that inhibited period, the male won't mate. When we artificially reactivate them, the inhibition is lifted and he mates again immediately. The reward signal drives the behavior; the post-reward dampening creates the refractory period.

## [01:23:19] Hypothalamus, Dopamine, Prolactin, Cabergoline, Libido, Dopamine

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned that these Tacr1 neurons project to the VTA and drive dopamine release. Is prolactin involved in the refractory period?

**Nirao Shah:** Prolactin is interesting in this context. After orgasm in men, there's a surge in prolactin — and prolactin is known to suppress dopamine. There's a direct projection from the hypothalamus to dopamine neurons, and prolactin can inhibit that pathway. This has led to the use of cabergoline — a dopamine agonist that also suppresses prolactin — as a way to potentially reduce the refractory period. Some men report that cabergoline shortens their refractory period. Whether this is primarily through dopamine pathways or prolactin pathways isn't entirely clear.

**Andrew Huberman:** And libido more broadly — what regulates the overall drive to seek out sex, independent of the refractory period?

**Nirao Shah:** Libido is driven by testosterone in both men and women — women have lower levels but testosterone is important for female libido too. Beyond hormones, the dopamine system is central — libido is fundamentally a motivational state, and dopamine underlies motivation. Anything that impairs dopaminergic function — opioids, excessive alcohol, certain psychiatric medications — can suppress libido. Conversely, dopamine agonists can enhance libido.

## [01:27:05] Brain Circuits, Aggression & Sexual Behavior

**Andrew Huberman:** There seems to be an intimate relationship between aggression and sexual behavior in the brain. Are they using the same circuits?

**Nirao Shah:** They are deeply intertwined. There's a region in the hypothalamus called the ventromedial hypothalamus, or VMH. David Anderson at Caltech has done beautiful work showing that the same VMH neurons that are active during aggression are also active during mating. If you activate these cells optogenetically, the animal attacks anything in the cage — males, females, even a rubber glove. These cells seem to be encoding a heightened arousal state that can be channeled into either aggression or sexual behavior depending on context.

**Andrew Huberman:** Context determines which behavior emerges.

**Nirao Shah:** Exactly. The same circuit can drive different behaviors. What the animal does with that arousal depends on what stimuli are present, what the animal's internal state is, what the environmental context is. The circuits don't specify behavior — they create a motivational state that interacts with context to produce behavior. This is a theme we see throughout the hypothalamus.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's also the periaqueductal gray — the PAG — which is involved in both pain and sexual behavior.

**Nirao Shah:** Yes, the PAG is fascinating. It's involved in pain modulation — it's the site where opioids and descending analgesia act. But it's also involved in female sexual behavior — it has a role in coordinating the motor aspects of the lordosis reflex, the receptive posture in female rodents. Susanna Lima and colleagues, now at Champalimaud, and others have mapped PAG circuits for both pain and sexual behavior. The overlap between pain and pleasure pathways at the level of the PAG is genuinely interesting.

## [01:32:40] Refractory Period; Age, Testosterone & Libido

**Andrew Huberman:** How does the refractory period change with age in males?

**Nirao Shah:** In rodents, the refractory period increases with age — it takes longer for older males to be ready to mate again. This correlates with declining testosterone levels. When you give old males testosterone, you can partially restore a shorter refractory period. In humans, this is a common complaint — as men age and testosterone declines, the refractory period lengthens. This is thought to be largely testosterone-dependent, though the specific neural mechanisms in terms of Tacr1 neurons and aging haven't been fully worked out.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is this one reason why testosterone replacement therapy tends to improve sexual function in older men with hypogonadism?

**Nirao Shah:** Yes, that's likely part of it. Testosterone acts on the pre-organized circuits to keep them activated. When testosterone declines, those circuits are under-activated and sexual behavior — including libido and the speed of recovery after sex — is impaired.

## [01:36:07] Tacr1 Cells in Females, Periaqueductal Gray & Innate Behaviors

**Andrew Huberman:** You found Tacr1 neurons in the preoptic hypothalamus of males. Are the same neurons present in females?

**Nirao Shah:** Yes — Tacr1 neurons are present in the female preoptic hypothalamus as well. But their function is somewhat different. In females, these neurons are important for regulating female sexual behavior — they contribute to the timing and expression of receptivity. And their number and activity fluctuate across the estrous cycle. So the same basic cell type exists in both sexes, but it's been co-opted for different but related functions.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's elegant — evolution reuses the same building blocks but wires them differently.

**Nirao Shah:** Exactly. And this is a general principle — it's not that males and females have completely different brain cells. Many of the same cell types are present in both sexes. What differs is the number of those cells, their connectivity, and how they respond to hormones. The differences arise from differential gene expression within the same cell types, driven by the different hormonal environments.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the PAG — you mentioned it's involved in female sexual behavior. How does that connect to the Tacr1 neuron story?

**Nirao Shah:** The preoptic area and the PAG are connected. The preoptic area can be thought of as a higher-level command center for sexual behavior, projecting to the PAG which coordinates the motor aspects of the behavior. So in females, the preoptic Tacr1 neurons likely modulate the PAG circuits that drive the actual motor expression of receptive behavior.

## [01:40:00] Parenting Behaviors & Brain Circuits; Pet Dogs

**Andrew Huberman:** Your lab has studied parenting circuits too. What have you found?

**Nirao Shah:** Yes — there are neurons in the same region of the preoptic hypothalamus that regulate parenting behavior. These neurons, when activated optogenetically, can cause animals — including virgin males who normally would attack or ignore pups — to start caring for pups. They'll retrieve them, crouch over them, keep them warm. And conversely, silencing these neurons in a mother impairs her pup care. So there's a specific parenting circuit in the preoptic hypothalamus.

**Andrew Huberman:** Does this overlap with the circuits for mating and aggression?

**Nirao Shah:** There's overlap in brain region but the circuits are somewhat distinct — different cell types regulate different behaviors. The preoptic hypothalamus is a dense cluster of different functional cell types, and mapping which cells do what is an ongoing research program. What's interesting is that many of these cell types are shared between males and females — the parenting circuit, for instance, exists in both sexes, though its expression and activation thresholds differ.

**Andrew Huberman:** I have to ask — do you think dog ownership activates some of these parenting circuits in humans?

**Nirao Shah:** I honestly don't know. What I can say is that some of the same brain regions in humans that are activated during parenting — including the medial preoptic area and the anterior cingulate — are also activated in studies where parents see images of their children. Whether dog-human bonding activates the same circuits is an interesting question that hasn't been rigorously studied.

## [01:43:12] Oxytocin, Pair Bonding, Vasopressin; Biological Redundancy

**Andrew Huberman:** Oxytocin. Everyone talks about it as the bonding hormone. What's the actual story?

**Nirao Shah:** Oxytocin has been studied intensively in prairie voles, which form monogamous pair bonds. After mating, prairie voles form a long-term exclusive bond — they'll reject new potential mates and prefer their partner. Oxytocin and vasopressin were both implicated in this pair bonding. Classic pharmacological experiments — giving oxytocin receptor antagonists — blocked pair bond formation. This established oxytocin as critical for pair bonding.

**Andrew Huberman:** But you've challenged that story.

**Nirao Shah:** Well, we've complicated it. We generated voles with a genetic knockout of the oxytocin receptor — they can't respond to oxytocin at all. And remarkably, these voles still form pair bonds. They're just as monogamous as wild-type voles. So oxytocin receptor signaling is apparently not required for pair bonding in prairie voles.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's stunning given all the previous pharmacological data.

**Nirao Shah:** It is stunning. The most likely explanation is redundancy — vasopressin signaling can compensate. Both oxytocin and vasopressin are short neuropeptides with similar structures; they signal through related receptors. Our next experiment is to knock out both oxytocin receptor and vasopressin receptor and see if pair bonding is now disrupted. But this finding is also a reminder that pharmacological studies — blocking a receptor with an antagonist — don't always tell you the same thing as genetic knockouts. Both approaches are informative but they can give different answers.

## [01:47:22] Libido, Melanocortin, Tacr1 Neurons; GLP-1 Agonists, Clinical Trials; Kisspeptin

**Andrew Huberman:** There's an FDA-approved drug for low libido in women — I believe it targets the melanocortin system. Can you tell us about that?

**Nirao Shah:** The drug is bremelanotide, trade name Vyleesi. It's an injectable that activates melanocortin receptors. Melanocortin signaling from the pituitary — alpha-MSH — is involved in sexual arousal circuits. Removing melanocortin signaling in mice impairs sexual behavior in both males and females. The drug helps a subset of women with hypoactive sexual desire disorder — but the effects are modest and there are side effects, including nausea and hyperpigmentation. Libido is separable from erectile or arousal function — Viagra acts on peripheral vasodilation, not libido. There are very few drugs that actually increase the drive to have sex. There's a significant unmet clinical need there.

**Andrew Huberman:** Could the Tacr1 neuron discovery lead to a libido-enhancing drug?

**Nirao Shah:** It's an interesting possibility. The Tacr1 receptor — neurokinin 1 receptor — does have an FDA-approved antagonist, aprepitant, used for chemotherapy-induced nausea. That antagonist would actually reduce Tacr1 signaling and likely suppress libido. What you'd want is an agonist. There isn't a clinically approved Tacr1 agonist yet, but our discovery has certainly generated interest. Our colleague Mike Eisenberg at Stanford, who studies male sexual health, has reached out about exploring this. The path from discovery to drug is long — you need to verify the neurons exist in human hypothalamus, develop a selective agonist, do preclinical safety testing, then clinical trials.

**Andrew Huberman:** And kisspeptin — that's another hypothalamic peptide people are experimenting with.

**Nirao Shah:** Kisspeptin is a hypothalamic peptide that regulates the onset of puberty — mutations in the kisspeptin receptor prevent puberty. Kisspeptin neurons in the hypothalamus are a major driver of GnRH release, which drives LH and FSH from the pituitary, which drives testosterone and estrogen from the gonads. Because kisspeptin sits at the top of the HPG axis, people have experimented with kisspeptin injections to boost libido. There is a subculture of people doing this with peptides acquired outside of clinical trials. Whether it actually enhances libido safely and reliably hasn't been established rigorously.

**Andrew Huberman:** The GLP-1 agonist story is a good parallel — those peptides were used off-label in the fitness community for years before they became widely accepted.

**Nirao Shah:** Exactly right. And the GLP-1 story shows that peptides that act on the brain can be powerful tools. It's reinvigorating interest in CNS drug development after decades of pharma largely avoiding the brain due to off-target effect concerns.

## [01:56:43] Female Brain Changes, Menstrual Cycle, Pregnancy, Menopause; Estrogen; Men & Hormone Fluctuation?

**Andrew Huberman:** The female brain changes dramatically across the menstrual cycle. What do we know about the magnitude of those changes?

**Nirao Shah:** In rodents, where the estrous cycle is every four to five days, we see approximately three-fold changes in the number of synaptic connections in specific hypothalamic and limbic circuits across the cycle. Dendritic spines — the little protrusions on neurons that receive synaptic input — wax and wane with estrogen. When estrogen is high around ovulation, circuits important for sexual behavior are maximally connected and active. When estrogen falls, those connections partially retract. We also found that the projections from certain hypothalamic neurons to downstream targets change by a similar magnitude. This is enormous structural plasticity on a four-to-five-day cycle in rodents — and there's evidence from MRI and other imaging studies that similar dynamics occur in the human female brain across the monthly menstrual cycle.

**Andrew Huberman:** Does blocking ovulation with hormonal contraception flatten out these changes?

**Nirao Shah:** That's being studied. The short answer is we don't fully know. What we do know is that women on hormonal contraceptives show different brain connectivity patterns than women not on contraceptives, as measured by neuroimaging. Whether this is good, bad, or neutral depends on which circuits and which functions you're looking at.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about during pregnancy?

**Nirao Shah:** Pregnancy involves enormous hormonal shifts — massive increases in progesterone and estrogen, followed by a precipitous drop at birth. There's a beautiful study by Adi Mizrahi, from Larry Katz's lab at Duke, showing that maternal experience — particularly having pups — changes the auditory cortex of mother mice to be more tuned to the ultrasonic vocalizations of their pups. Mothers become better at detecting and responding to their pups' calls. This is a striking example of experience-dependent plasticity driven by the hormonal state of new motherhood.

**Andrew Huberman:** And menopause?

**Nirao Shah:** Menopause involves a profound and irreversible drop in estrogen. We know cognitively that women report changes in memory, mood, and cognitive sharpness with menopause — the "brain fog" of menopause is real. There's a steep increase in Alzheimer's risk in women that begins around menopause. The hypothesis is that estrogen loss is contributing to this — directly through its effects on neurons and synapses, and indirectly through its effects on cerebrovascular health. The circuits whose plasticity we've studied — hypothalamic, hippocampal, frontal — are likely affected when estrogen disappears. It's an active research frontier.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do male hormones fluctuate across meaningful timescales?

**Nirao Shah:** In rodents, giving testosterone to a castrated male at any time of day produces the same effect — the male brain circuits aren't time-sensitive the way female circuits are. In humans, testosterone does show circadian variation — it's typically highest in the morning and lower in the afternoon — but there's no male equivalent of the multi-week hormonal cycle that females have. The male hormonal environment is relatively stable compared to the female hormonal environment.

## [02:04:10] Life Experience Male vs Female, Sex Recognition, Behaviors & Context

**Andrew Huberman:** Do male and female brains experience reality differently?

**Nirao Shah:** We've discovered a set of neurons in the male mouse brain — in the medial amygdala — that fire specifically in response to female odors and signals. These cells represent a sex recognition signal: when they're active, the brain state of the male is essentially "that's a female." The same neurons are present in female mice but are largely silent. So males and females are using different circuits to recognize the sex of other individuals. If we optogenetically activate these male sex recognition neurons for just 90 seconds and then present the male with another male, he tries to mate with the male for the next 15 to 20 minutes — because his internal state is "that's a female," even though all the incoming sensory signals say "that's a male." When we ablate these neurons, the male can no longer discriminate females from males — he loses his preference for female odors and neither mates with females nor attacks males. This is a profound example of internal brain state overriding sensory reality.

**Andrew Huberman:** So male and female brains are literally constructing reality differently at the circuit level.

**Nirao Shah:** At least for this particular computation — the sex recognition computation. Male and female mice use different neural circuits to make the same determination. And because those circuits are different, the phenomenology — the internal experience — of making that sex recognition is presumably different too.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's also the question of how context modulates behavior. The same arousal state can produce mating or aggression depending on context.

**Nirao Shah:** Context is critical. Tinbergen proposed a hierarchy of behavioral motivations — and context determines which behavioral programs are activated at any moment. If you activate VMH neurons — the aggression/mating circuit — in an animal on its home turf, it attacks. Put the same animal in a stranger's cage and activate the same neurons, it may not attack. The contextual input from higher cortical regions can override or modulate the activity of these hypothalamic circuits. This is particularly pronounced in humans, where the prefrontal cortex provides enormous capacity for behavioral flexibility and contextual modulation.

## [02:16:05] Pain Management; Endocrine Disrupters, Gender Identity

**Andrew Huberman:** There's an audience question about sex differences in pain. Do males and females experience and process pain differently?

**Nirao Shah:** There are reports of sex differences in pain thresholds and pain responses — both in rodents and in humans. But this is a challenging area to study because there are many confounds. Women report higher pain sensitivity in many studies, which could reflect real circuit differences or differences in how pain is expressed behaviorally and socially. The PAG, which we discussed in the context of sexual behavior, is also a key site of descending pain modulation. Whether the PAG is wired differently in male and female brains is an interesting open question. I'll be honest that pain neurobiology is not my primary area and I don't have deep expertise here.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about endocrine disruptors — compounds in the environment that mimic or interfere with hormones? Is there concern that environmental exposure is disrupting sex differentiation?

**Nirao Shah:** This is a legitimate concern but the evidence is complex. The principle is clearly established in extreme cases — there are classic studies, like Tyrone Hayes' work at Berkeley on atrazine in frogs, showing that hormone-disrupting chemicals in the water supply can feminize male frogs and disrupt sexual differentiation. In humans, the question is whether the lower levels of endocrine disruptors we're exposed to through plastics, pesticides, and other environmental sources are sufficient to affect fetal development. For that to happen, you'd need exposure at a critical developmental window at sufficient doses. The doses from typical environmental exposure are far lower than the pharmacological doses in those frog studies. That said, microplastics have now been detected in fetal meconium — the first fecal material of newborns — so the compounds do cross the placenta. Whether they're present in sufficient quantities to affect hormonal programming of the brain is an open question that's being actively studied.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's the famous case of Ben Barres — the Stanford neuroscientist who transitioned from female to male and whose mother was treated with an anti-miscarriage drug with androgenic properties during the pregnancy.

**Nirao Shah:** Yes — Ben Barres' case is interesting precisely because he was a thoughtful scientist who himself speculated about the relationship between that pharmacological androgen exposure in utero and his later gender identity. He had an identical twin sister who had no gender dysphoria. So here you have the same genome, same family environment, presumably different in utero hormonal exposure due to the drug, and different gender identities. That's consistent with a prenatal hormonal contribution to gender identity. But it's an anecdote — you can't draw strong causal conclusions from one case.

## [02:21:03] Future Projects

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the most exciting open questions that you're working toward?

**Nirao Shah:** Several big ones. First, we still don't have a complete map of the innate social behavior circuits — we've identified key players but there are many behaviors and many brain regions we haven't characterized yet. How do the circuits for mating, aggression, parenting, and other social behaviors interact with each other and with higher-order circuits in the cortex? Second, the adult plasticity story in the female brain is fascinating and underdeveloped. We've shown three-fold changes in circuit connectivity across the estrous cycle. How widespread is this plasticity? Does the male brain have analogous forms of plasticity, perhaps driven by experience or social context rather than hormonal cycling? Third, the lifespan question — how do these circuits change across puberty, into reproductive adulthood, through late life and menopause? What happens to these circuits when estrogen disappears? This is directly relevant to women's cognitive and neurological health.

**Andrew Huberman:** These are beautiful, important questions and the work you're doing to answer them is remarkable. Thank you, Nirao, for taking the time.

**Nirao Shah:** Thanks, Andrew. It's been a pleasure being here and sharing this with you.
