# Episode 328: Build Your Ideal Physique | Dr. Bret Contreras

## Episode Overview

Dr. Bret Contreras — known as "The Glute Guy" — holds a PhD in sports science and has over 30 years of experience training athletes and everyday people. He invented the hip thrust and pioneered using multiple glute movement vectors as a systematic approach to physique development. In this episode, he and Andrew Huberman cover the full framework for building a resistance training program tailored to individual aesthetic and performance goals, with a particular focus on glute development, lagging muscle groups, progressive overload over a lifetime, training frequency, and body recomposition. The discussion is relevant to both beginners and advanced lifters, men and women, across all age groups.

## Key Takeaways

- **Minimum effective training frequency is twice per week per muscle group.** One day per week can work with brutal intensity and a coach, but twice-weekly full-body training is the practical minimum. Three times per week is theoretically superior but requires careful exercise selection and volume management to avoid overreaching.
- **Progressive overload is the number one principle of resistance training.** Two main forms: same weight for more reps, or more weight for same reps. Without it, you spin your wheels regardless of set count. Tracking in a log or app is essential.
- **Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV) governs everything.** The goal is to do as much volume as possible while still recovering from it. Exercise selection is the most important variable — some exercises (e.g., walking lunges, touch-and-go deadlifts) are too fatiguing for frequent training. Step-ups, cable movements, and shortened-position exercises allow higher frequency.
- **The Big Six lifts develop all major muscle groups.** Squat, bench press, deadlift, military press, chin-up, and hip thrust. Variations count — the movement pattern matters, not the specific implement.
- **Switch exercise focus monthly to avoid injury and sustain progress.** Focus on one movement pattern for PRs each month (e.g., squat month, then deadlift month). This allows continued progressive overload without repeating the same high-stress movements indefinitely.
- **Glutes require training from three vectors (Rule of Thirds).** One-third vertical (squats, deadlifts, lunges — hardest to recover from, builds legs), one-third horizontal (hip thrusts, glute bridges, hyperextensions — easier to recover from, squeeze position), one-third lateral/rotational (hip abduction — easiest to recover from, glute medius and upper glute max).
- **Four movement patterns complete a lower-body program.** Squat/lunge, hinge/pull, thrust/bridge, and abduction. One of each per session, three sets each, three times per week = 36 weekly sets for glutes — manageable and highly effective.
- **Tempo matters far less for hypertrophy than most people think.** A 1-second rep and an 8-second rep produce similar muscle growth. Tempo matters more for injury prevention than for hypertrophy.
- **Rep ranges 6–30 all work for hypertrophy.** All produce similar muscle growth when taken close to failure. Variety across rep ranges is recommended. For lagging body parts trained frequently, lower reps may reduce muscle damage accumulation.
- **Maintaining strength and size is far easier than building it.** As little as one set per exercise per week can maintain months of built-up size (Bickel study). This supports a specialization-maintenance rotation: blast a lagging muscle for 4–6 weeks, then maintain with minimal volume while focusing elsewhere.
- **Loadless training / flexing to assess neuromuscular control.** Before adding load, check if you can flex each muscle without resistance. If you can't consciously contract a muscle, you can't optimally train it — especially important for glutes, which are underactivated in everyday life.
- **Low-load glute activation has legitimate neural benefits.** Isometric glute activation (fire hydrant holds) strengthens the motor cortex-to-glute connection within one week. Use it in warm-ups, but don't let it replace progressive overload work.
- **Muscle memory is real and robust.** Once satellite cell nuclei fuse to muscle fibers, they remain. Returning to training after a layoff restores strength and size rapidly.
- **Psychological readiness and enjoyment drive long-term results.** Training two days per week while excited and recovered produces better results than six days per week dreading the gym. Sustainability over 5+ years beats short-term optimal protocols. Reassess training volume and frequency every five years as recovery capacity changes with age.
- **NEAT (non-exercise activity thermogenesis) suffers with chronic pain and injury.** Nagging injuries cause people to unconsciously move less throughout the day, compounding metabolic and compositional effects. Avoiding injury is a core component of long-term physique management.
- **Body recomposition is real and underutilized.** Simultaneous muscle gain and fat loss is achievable at caloric maintenance with sufficient protein (~1g per lb bodyweight per day). Mini-bulks and mini-cuts (5 lb swings) outperform large bulk-cut cycles for most people. Large surpluses primarily add fat.
- **Spot reduction is a myth; site-specific muscle growth is real.** You cannot target fat loss in a specific area, but you can selectively grow muscles there. Abs look best when body fat is low, not when ab training volume is highest.
- **Hip dips are an anatomical reality when lean.** There is no muscle in the hollow between the glute medius and glute max. Hip dip workouts do not work. Being lean and muscular will produce hip dips — embracing this is more realistic than trying to eliminate them.
- **One set to failure is highly time-efficient.** Two full-body workouts per week with one all-out set to failure per exercise (~45 min each) can produce approximately 80% of maximum possible hypertrophy gains.

## Tools & Protocols

**Training Frequency**
- Beginners: 2x per week full body minimum; 3x per week optimal but harder to recover from.
- Practical split for glute-focused training: Lower, Upper, Lower, Upper, Lower (LULUL) Monday–Friday.
- Women prioritizing glutes: 3 lower body days + 2 upper body days. Men: typically the reverse.
- General rule: find the frequency you can sustain for the next 5 years, train at that, reassess every 5 years.

**Sets per Exercise**
- 2–3 working sets per exercise is sufficient. The first working set provides the majority of the stimulus.
- 4 sets is fine only if effort and progressive overload are applied.

**Progressive Overload**
- Track every workout. Know your personal records.
- Two main forms: same weight for more reps, or more weight for same reps.
- Switch the exercise you're focused on PRing each month.
- Making final 2–3 reps of each set as hard as possible (via mind-muscle focus) is a valid complementary strategy.

**LULUL Split (5 days/week)**
- L: Lower body (squat/lunge + hinge/pull + thrust/bridge + abduction)
- U: Upper body (push + pull)
- Rest: Saturday, Sunday
- Women favor 3L + 2U; men favor 3U + 2L.

**Four Lower Body Movement Patterns**
1. Squat/Lunge — squat, hack squat, leg press, lunge, step-up, Bulgarian split squat
2. Hinge/Pull — RDL, stiff-leg deadlift, good morning, 45-degree hyper, reverse hyper
3. Thrust/Bridge — hip thrust, glute bridge, cable kickback
4. Abduction — seated hip abduction, cable abduction, banded lateral walks

**Rule of Thirds for Glute Training**
- 1/3 vertical: squat, deadlift, lunge variations (hardest to recover from; also builds legs)
- 1/3 horizontal: hip thrust, bridge, hyperextension variations (manageable recovery; squeeze position)
- 1/3 lateral/rotational: abduction movements (easiest to recover from; glute medius and upper glute max)
- 36 total weekly sets (12 per session x 3 days) is achievable and recoverable with this distribution.

**Monthly Focus Rotation**
- Month 1: Squat + bench press PR focus
- Month 2: Deadlift + chin-up PR focus
- Always perform all movement patterns; only the first-exercise PR focus rotates.
- For lagging muscles: 4–6 week specialization block (high frequency + volume), then rotate to maintenance (1–2 sets per week).

**Hip Thrust Setup**
- Bar position: just above the pubic bone for most; shift to upper thighs if pelvic anatomy makes that painful.
- Full range of motion — reach complete hip extension (reverse tabletop) at the top.
- Explosive concentric + controlled eccentric. Full range > partial range.
- Common errors: not reaching full hip extension; feet too far out (hamstring bias); feet too close (quad bias); ego loading with compromised form.

**Growing Glutes Without Growing Legs**
- Eliminate or minimize squats, lunges, stiff-leg deadlifts, RDLs.
- Focus on: hip thrust, cable kickbacks, 45-degree hypers, hip abduction.
- Train 3x per week with 3–4 sets per exercise (recoverable because no stretch-position leg damage).

**Training Lagging Muscle Groups**
- Add frequency: move lagging muscle from 1x to 2–3x per week.
- Add volume to the lagging part; reduce volume for muscles you're already strong in.
- Rotate the specialization each month to allow other muscles to then be maintained.

**Rep Ranges**
- 6–30 reps all produce similar hypertrophy if taken close to failure.
- Lower rep ranges (6–10): joint-friendly for frequent training.
- Higher rep ranges (15–30): effective for single-joint movements and for managing muscle damage.
- Variety across rep ranges is recommended.

**Calves**
- Calves respond best to the stretched position.
- Prioritize full-range standing calf raises or lengthened partials (bottom half only).
- Add extended partials as a superset after full-range sets for additional stretch stimulus.
- Seated calf raises and top-squeeze emphasis have limited evidence.

**Training Layoffs**
- Brief deloads (a few easy days to one week) are beneficial when beat up, psychologically depleted, or healing.
- Muscle memory ensures rapid return of strength and size.
- Avoid full cessation for more than 1–2 weeks without maintaining some movement.

**One Set to Failure Protocol**
- Full body, 1 working set to failure per exercise, 6–10 exercises per session.
- 2x per week = ~90 minutes total training per week.
- Produces approximately 80% of maximum possible hypertrophy gains.
- Rotate exercises and order every few weeks to prevent stagnation and overuse injury.

**Loadless Training / Pre-Training Assessment**
- Before adding weight, assess: can you flex each major muscle without resistance?
- Practice consciously contracting glutes, lats, hamstrings, calves in isolation.
- Low-load glute activation (fire hydrants, bodyweight glute bridges, lateral band walks) in warm-up can strengthen the motor cortex–glute connection rapidly.

**Recomposition**
- Aim for ~1g protein per pound of bodyweight per day.
- Train for progressive strength gain without manipulating diet aggressively.
- Mini-bulks and mini-cuts (5 lb swings) if a surplus/deficit cycle is desired.
- Avoid large bulks unless significantly underweight.

**5-Year Schedule Review**
- Every five years, reassess what training volume and frequency you can sustain consistently.
- Prioritize consistency over optimization.

## Resources Mentioned

- **Bickel Study** — demonstrated that 1/9 of original training volume (one set of squats, leg press, leg extensions per week) maintained most quad size and strength for 6 months after a building phase.
- **Brad Schoenfeld Study** — three sets of three versus three sets of ten on the squat; sets of ten produced more hypertrophy. Also co-authored the detraining (one-week layoff) paper.
- **Helms Study (Eric Helms)** — subjects gained similar muscle in caloric maintenance as with a caloric surplus.
- **Plotkin Study** — hip thrusts grow glutes similarly effectively to squats and step-ups.
- **Mel Siff, Super Training** — foundational strength and conditioning text; coined "loadless training."
- **Louie Simmons** — pioneered max effort / dynamic effort periodization; rotated lifts to allow year-round competition; Westside Barbell.
- **Brian Haycock** — Hypertrophy Specific Training (HST); "strategic reconditioning" (planned deload) concept.
- **Britney Counts Study** — loadless biceps training showed hypertrophy and contralateral triceps growth from isometric resistance.
- **Science paper on metabolism across the lifespan** — showed metabolism stays largely stable across adulthood; reduced NEAT explains apparent metabolic slowdown with age.
- **ACE gene study** — preliminary evidence that some individuals respond better to single-set training while others to multiple-set training.
- *Protocols: An Operating Manual for the Human Body* by Andrew Huberman — available at protocolsbook.com.
