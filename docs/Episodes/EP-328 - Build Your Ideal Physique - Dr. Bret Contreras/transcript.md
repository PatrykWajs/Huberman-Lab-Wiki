## [00:40:41] Low-Load Glute Activation; Life-Long Strength Gains, Avoiding Pain & Injury

**Andrew Huberman:** When you arrive in the gym, perhaps you put more priority on the muscles you have a harder time contracting.

**Bret Contreras:** This was proposed by a researcher, and then Stu McGill, who's been on your show, started talking about gluteal amnesia. It got a lot of backlash — "Really? ## [01:34:49] Glute Function; Abduction vs Adduction; Glute Vectors, Tool: Rule of Thirds

**Andrew Huberman:** When I was growing up, glutes weren't really a thing that was emphasized or selectively trained for. Nowadays you hear "glutes are the new biceps." Setting aside different preferences about glutes, everybody has glutes. Could you describe what are the major functions of the glute and why it's useful to build strength and in some cases hypertrophy for the glutes for men and for women?

**Bret Contreras:** The glutes do three main things: hip extension, hip abduction, and hip external rotation. They also do posterior pelvic tilt, which is like hip extension. If you're sprinting, your leg is out in front of you, you power it down — your foot touches down and you accelerate forward. That's hip extension, front to back. It also does hip external rotation — think of a baseball player about to swing a bat, they're going to rotate and the hip rotates laterally. And then abduction is simply raising your leg out to the side like you're doing a jumping jack. When you're bent over though, you can also do hip abduction — that's like doing the seated hip abduction machine.

**Andrew Huberman:** I think most people are probably not familiar with abduction and adduction. Could you explain?

**Bret Contreras:** Abduction is spreading your legs outwards against resistance. Adduction — think of adduction as adding to the body — is squeezing in. The seated hip abduction machine: if you're perfectly upright and you're squeezing in, that's adduction. If you're pushing out, that's abduction. When you're upright or leaning forward, you involve the gluteus maximus more. The gluteus maximus mainly does hip extension. Hip abduction, especially transverse plane hip abduction like the seated hip abduction machine, is effective too. So it's a very versatile muscle.

Most people in the past have only focused on hip extension — squat, deadlift, lunge. Those are vertical hip extension exercises and they're very effective. But they also grow the legs very well. So if you're a woman that doesn't want a lot of leg growth, or you're just trying to maximize your recoverable volume, you need a different approach.

I started new terminology because we always had the planar terminology — sagittal plane, frontal plane, transverse plane. I said think of vectors instead. You have axial loading — vertical: squats, deadlifts, lunges, step-ups, split squats, good mornings. Then you have anteroposterior loading — front to back: back extensions, glute bridges, hip thrusts, loaded from the front. Then you have your lateral vector — left to right. And then your rotational vector.

I created what I called the Rule of Thirds. A third of your exercise selection should be vertical in nature — these are the hardest to recover from, but they also build your legs and are probably the most efficient. Then you have your horizontal movements — these don't beat you up as much, they work you in a squeeze position: variations of hip thrust, glute bridges, back extensions, reverse hypers, 45-degree hypers, cable kickbacks. They don't beat you up as much. And then the remaining third can be lateral-rotary — these don't beat you up at all: hip abduction movements. They build the glute medius and minimus and also the upper subdivision of the glute max.

With my movement pattern checklist — one squat/lunge, one hinge/pull, one thrust/bridge, and one abduction movement — you could do three sets of each. That's 12 sets. Do that three times a week — that's 36 sets per week. People say that's overkill, but people can recover from this system. Of the 36 sets, 12 are more for the glute medius and minimus, which are different muscles than the glute max. So it's really 24 sets for the glute max with some overlap.

That's actually how I became popular and invented the hip thrust. I saw coaches doing bands and bodyweight stuff for glute activation in the warm-up — why aren't we loading them up? Why are we doing wimpy movements? How can I load up that bridge pattern? And that's how I came up with the hip thrust. Then I said the Rule of Thirds: a third of your movements should be lateral-rotary, a third horizontal, a third vertical. You can recover from it, and you're developing the glutes through all their vectors.

## [01:45:26] Upper vs Lower Glute Maximus Exercises, Frequency

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's say a woman or man comes to you and says, "I want more lower glute max — the area where the glutes meet the hamstring." What movements would you give them?

**Bret Contreras:** I think the reverse lunge is the best for the lower glute max. But it's almost too good — if you're going to train it three times a week, I would say don't do reverse lunge. Do step-ups instead. Step-ups don't tend to get you as sore. The most glute-dominant way to step up is to lean forward and just go to a thigh-parallel position, reaching back, tapping the foot down, then coming up. But also remember: if you train glutes Monday, Wednesday, Friday, you have an extra day for recovery on Friday. So that's where you could do two sets of reverse lunges on the Friday because you have an extra day to recover.

For the lower glute max, the best movement is probably reverse lunge, but it can be too good and too sore. Sometimes it makes people's adductors super sore, and two days later you have a crummy workout. If that's the case, do step-ups instead. Squats, RDLs, leg press — especially single-leg leg press with foot higher up on the platform — work well for that category as well.

For the upper gluteus maximus, we need more research. Based on EMG, hip thrusts are probably the best. EMG doesn't perfectly predict growth, but I think theoretically people feel it more in the upper glute max. For sure the seated hip abduction leaning forward targets mostly upper gluteus maximus — analyzing the fiber directions, but that's just theoretical.

## [01:49:26] Common Mistakes of Hip Thrusts

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the most common mistake that you see people making when they do hip thrusts — on a hip thrust machine or using a barbell over their pelvis?

**Bret Contreras:** We always said the first decade: people are loading up too heavy and not reaching full hip extension. You're not getting into that reverse table pose — in gymnastics they call it a crab pose — where your shoulders are like a tabletop and your knees are at right angles. That's essentially what the hip thrust is. They're not reaching full hip extension. The hip can go into hyperextension — with bent knees, average is probably around 10 degrees. With straight legs you can get about 20 degrees. Some people can get 40 degrees, some people can get zero.

Just think of it like this: you do vertical movements to work the stretch position with the glutes. When you do hip thrust, don't try to make it a stretch position movement — you have better movements for working the stretch. Do full range hip thrusts: make sure you're coming up all the way and reaching full hip extension. Really squeeze at the top. Maybe your last two reps don't quite reach full hip extension, but it shouldn't be where you're only getting a third of the way up.

## [01:52:06] Exercises to Grow Glutes, Women & Men, Hypertrophy

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's say somebody wants to improve their glute development — bigger, rounder, more shaped glutes. Women don't want a giant glute. But when women say "I want rounder glutes" — to get that round shape, you need bigger glute muscles. You need hypertrophy. That means hypertrophy-specific training for the gluteus maximus. Most people don't have really good glute genetics. Most women whose only chance at getting round muscular glutes is to do really optimal glute training and have the diet that matches it.

**Bret Contreras:** Exactly. A lot of the female-specific marketing plays upon women's fears of getting too bulky, too muscular. But for the glutes — how are you going to grow the glutes if you don't get strong? How are you going to grow them if you're just doing wimpy movements? It can't happen. What you're seeking is not glute tone, it's glute hypertrophy.

If somebody wants to grow their glutes, the three types of movements: a vertical movement (squats, hip hinge, RDLs, lunges, reverse lunges, step-ups), hip thrusts, and something for the inner thigh and outer thigh — abduction. Training glutes twice a week might be sufficient. There's not much evidence that three times a week is better than two in the literature, but two times a week is the safer bet. For a man who says, "I want more glute growth" — you're already doing squats or lunges? You're already doing something like stiff-leg deadlifts or RDLs? Great. Just add in two sets of hip thrust twice a week. And if you want to throw in the seated hip abduction machine, do that too. Start light, master the mind-muscle connection, make sure you're using full range, and then gain strength over time. Get to where you can do 315 for 20 reps, or 405 for 10 reps. Trust me, you will notice improvements.

## [02:02:14] Hip Thrust, Barbell, Hip Anatomy; Glute-Focused Hyperextension; Glute Medius Exercises

**Andrew Huberman:** Where should the bar be placed for someone using a barbell — not a hip thrust machine?

**Bret Contreras:** Keep the bar right above the pubic bone — it stays right there throughout the whole range of motion. Probably about two-thirds of people can do it that way. If you have a type of hip anatomy where the bar is hitting your pelvic bones, it's excruciatingly painful. For those people — probably one-third — as they rise up, they push the bar forward until it's on their upper thighs. The men tend to keep it above the pubic bone. We have different pelvic anatomies. Everyone can do hip thrusts — it just takes time. Start with body weight, then the barbell lightweight, and build up tolerance over time. It's the same as when you start doing low bar squats — "Ah, this hurts" — and then three or four weeks later you're fine.

For men who are worried about hip thrust, learn how to do a glute-dominant 45-degree hyper. Round the back, flare your feet out a little — when you round the back, you're shutting the erectors off and then you're using your glutes to pull you up. It's an awesome hip extension exercise.

The most common errors with hip thrusting: not coming up all the way, or the knee angle — if your feet are too far out, you feel it more in your hamstring; feet too close, you feel it more in the rectus femoris and don't feel as much in the glutes. And ego lifting — you watch a video of yourself and realize "I was progressively overloading but now my form looks ugly, I've got to dial it back."

Also, the glute medius exercise — standing plate abduction, working the glute medius of the grounded leg. Adding those in resolved what I thought was a back pain issue completely. Getting the glute medius stronger stabilizes the pelvis and does incredible things.

## [02:08:07] Training Lagging Muscle Groups, Maintaining Strength, Muscle Memory

**Andrew Huberman:** The glutes are lagging for most people. Let's transfer this to essentially any muscle group. If one does a really honest assessment of their development, there almost always is one or two body parts that are lagging. Men in particular tend to focus on their stronger lifts. Could you just say: if I want to bring up my shoulders, for instance — maybe I train them two or three times a week currently instead of once?

**Bret Contreras:** Absolutely. Add volume to the lagging part and take some volume away. You can't just hammer everything. I want people to understand how easy it is to maintain size and strength. There's a classic study by Bickel — they hammered quads like three times a week with three sets of squats, three sets of leg press, three sets of leg extensions. Then one group just stopped training. One group did one-third the volume, so just one day a week. One group did one-ninth the volume — one set of squats, one set of leg press, one set of leg extensions. They maintained most of their size and strength doing just three sets per week for six months. It's really easy to maintain strength and size once you've built it up. It's hard to build, easy to maintain. That could have something to do with muscle memory — the myonuclear theory, where once you've gotten those extra nuclei from satellite cells fused into the muscle fiber, they're there to stay.

For brief periods, if you specialize, you can blast a muscle with high volume and it can grow. There are now five studies showing that over 30 sets a week you get more dose-response — more growth the more volume you do. All of us know that's not sustainable. But what people should realize is: you can blast a muscle with high volume for brief periods and grow. For lagging muscles, it might be a periodic specialization approach — blast it for a month, then maintain.

## [02:14:23] Neck Training; Focused Training & Maintaining Strength

**Andrew Huberman:** Your neck is jacked — how many years have you been training it?

**Bret Contreras:** I've done it for at least 10 years, twice a week. There's a study showing one group did deadlifts, shrugs, rows, and another group did neck flexion and extension. The group doing deadlifts and shrugs grew their traps but not their neck muscles at all. You want to grow the neck muscles, you have to train the neck muscles. The neck is the upper spine. Jeff Cavalier has a great video on how to properly train the neck using a plate wrapped in a towel — mouth closed, nasal breathing, tongue on the roof of the mouth. Bridges are probably not the best because you can slip a disc. Neck training, I think, is vital.

**Andrew Huberman:** How do you work everything together in a comprehensive program? There's a common theme here: short periods of four to six weeks of prioritization training — training a muscle three times a week, different movements, perhaps different rep ranges — and then throttling back on volume for other muscle groups and not panicking that you're going to lose all your development. This allows you to bring everything up to more or less the same level. Most people lack the discipline to throttle back on body parts they're strong in because it feels so good to train the things you're strong in.

**Bret Contreras:** I did that for delts. I started training more volume, more frequency — incorporating a lot of variety, cables, machines, dumbbells, bands, lengthen partials. Just the extra volume grew my delts at age 48. I didn't get stronger at shoulder press or lateral raises — I was just hitting them more frequently and doing more volume. You reach a point where you're sick of it and you naturally throttle back. With grip training, once I focused on my grip for two to three months for a powerlifting competition, I got it super strong. Now I don't have to train it that much. You put in that work up front, then it's easier to maintain.

## [02:23:20] Periodic Training, Strength, Pain, Desire to Train; Tool: Training Layoffs

**Andrew Huberman:** I take layoffs of a week or so deliberately. It keeps things fresh and it hasn't hurt me. But if I go much longer than a week, like ten days, I notice my urge to train actually starts to diminish. What are your thoughts on layoffs of a week or longer?

**Bret Contreras:** In the early 2000s, Brian Haycock had this hypertrophy-specific training with strategic reconditioning — the idea that taking time off might prime you to get newbie gains again. But in practice, a lot of bodybuilders don't tend to do that — they train year round. But you need to de-load. It's really hard to not get nagging pain if you don't. This was recently put to the test in the literature — Brad Schoenfeld was on that paper — and what they found was you didn't see better benefits taking a week off, but you also didn't lose much.

Periodic training — train for a few weeks, take a week off, train for a few more weeks — you do lose strength and some size, but you quickly gain it back due to muscle memory. Here's where layoffs can be beneficial: if it helps your psychological desire to train, or if it helps heal nagging injuries or pain. I don't recommend it to my followers, but I do recommend having times where you chill. Life sometimes forces you into it — you get sick, injured, go on vacation without gym access. But I think just listen to your body. If you're pushing it hard and feel beat up, have a few easy days. It doesn't have to be a week — could just be three training days and you'll come back refreshed. It's a vital, necessary, crucial component of long-term lifting.

You don't always have to do every lift year round. A single-leg squat pattern builds bilateral double-leg strength as well — two studies show this, one on step-ups and one on Bulgarian split squats, where one group did bilateral, one did single-leg, and they both gained the same squat strength. Pick one exercise from a pattern and trust that you're going to maintain most of your strength. Things come back. You get so tied to the lifts themselves, to the routine — "if I stop deadlifting, I'm going to shrink." The body is pretty resilient. If you're training the movement patterns, you will retain the strength.

## [02:34:24] Tool: Rep Ranges for Lagging Body Part; Growing Calves

**Andrew Huberman:** What rep ranges should somebody use to bring up a lagging body part? Let's assume it's arms in this case.

**Bret Contreras:** Classic Brad Schoenfeld paper: three sets of three versus three sets of ten on the squat. Three sets of ten grew more muscle. But in general, lightweight and heavyweight grow similar amounts of muscle — you have to reach at least five to six reps, but it could be all the way up to 30. They grow the same amount. There is some argument as to whether high reps create more muscle damage. If you train a muscle frequently, the lower side of things might be better. Single-joint movements tend to be better done with higher reps, in my opinion. But generally, if you like doing low reps you could do sets of six and grow just fine. If you love higher reps, sets of 20 to 30 grow just fine. Variety is always the best because it's also kind of a different stress on the body to spare the joints.

**Andrew Huberman:** Many people want to grow their calves. I have an asymmetry in calves due to an injury. Could you reiterate your tips for growing calves?

**Bret Contreras:** Calves are so genetic. If you have good calf genetics, you don't even need to train them. The evidence emerging is that there's not much use for seated calf raises or for really focusing on the top squeeze position. Calves respond well to the stretch. You can do full range standing calf raises. You could also just do lengthened partials — just the bottom half. If you do full range calf raises, you might benefit from doing extended partials as supersets — keep going at the end just doing the bottom portion. Focus on the stretch.

## [02:37:35] Can You Build Muscle After 40?, Perimenopause, Menopause; Pregnancy

**Andrew Huberman:** Can a person grow muscle after age 40? And can one build more muscle starting resistance training for the first time in perimenopause or menopause?

**Bret Contreras:** Absolutely — it just matters when you like to start. If you start training in your 80s, there's even evidence in maybe 90-year-olds that they can grow muscle. My point is: if you start training when you're 15 and you train optimally throughout your whole life, there will be a point where you peak with muscle mass. But it's further than people think. People used to think you start losing muscle in your 30s. No — it's probably more like in your 50s you can maintain all of it. If you haven't started lifting, you will absolutely grow muscle. Will a 70-year-old who starts for the first time grow as much as a 20-year-old? No, but you still will grow. Age tends to dull everything, but you can absolutely grow and you should absolutely start.

Strength training during pregnancy is very well researched. It absolutely improves your outcomes with almost everything pregnancy-delivery-related. But if you haven't lifted weights, you might not want to start while you're pregnant. If you're an avid lifter and you get pregnant, you should absolutely continue training. First trimester: morning sickness, it's hard to stay motivated. Second trimester: things return more to normal. Third trimester: day by day, your belly is getting in the way, you'll find movements that work for you. Sumo squats tend to be well tolerated. You can even hip thrust while you're pregnant — just don't let the bar push into your belly. Keep it on the upper thighs, use the Smith machine, and you'll do just fine.

## [02:40:44] Saggy Glutes; Gain Muscle & Lose Fat?, Mini-Bulks & Cuts, Recomp, Hormones

**Andrew Huberman:** Several people asked how to grow lower glutes so they don't droop — presumably about excess body fat and lack of muscle hypertrophy underneath.

**Bret Contreras:** What's sagging? Is it the body fat or the muscle that's sagging? It's the body fat. But if you had big glutes and the same amount of fat, it would look better — fat stretched out over larger muscles. So yes: get strong at lunges, step-ups, squats, RDLs, and hip thrusts. They will all grow your glutes well and hit the lower glutes well. And don't just do the exercise — get stronger at them. Then obviously have an optimal body composition.

**Andrew Huberman:** Can somebody gain muscle while losing fat?

**Bret Contreras:** I've written more on this than anyone. Brad Schoenfeld and Alan Aragon are two of my best friends and they think you should bulk and cut. But the evidence doesn't appear to show you have to be in a caloric surplus. The Helms study showed subjects gained just as much muscle in maintenance as they did with surplus. All these studies on big surpluses just show you gain more fat.

Most of my clients recomp. I say: "Do you get enough protein?" They say yes, around a gram per pound of body weight per day. I say good, I'm not going to touch your diet, let's just get strong. And they recomp. I've had clients train with me for a whole year, stay at 135 pounds, and at the end of the year their waist is dramatically smaller. Same weight, but they lost fat and gained muscle.

If you do want to bulk and cut, do mini bulks and mini cuts — gain five pounds, lose five pounds. You don't have to put on 30 pounds. That's old-school thinking and I think it does more harm than good. Women don't like bulking. You don't have to. Maintenance plus a couple hundred extra calories with mini bulks, mini cuts is fine. Get more familiar with recomping.

**Andrew Huberman:** Kyle Gillett, Dr. Kyle Gillett, clarified that people carrying excess body fat, when they reduce the amount of fat they're carrying, their hormone profiles improve. For men, losing excess body fat increases testosterone. But men who are already really lean and go really sub-maintenance, their testosterone will drop. So these rules about "if you cut calories your testosterone drops" — it depends on where you're starting from.

**Bret Contreras:** Right. If you're in your healthy range — maybe 10 to 20% body fat for men, 15 to 25% for women — just recomp. If you're lower, you need to bulk to be in an optimal hormonal profile. If you're overweight, you need to lose weight. And if you do bulk and cut, do mini, mini bulk, mini cut.

## [02:47:46] Lifting or Pilates for Strength?; Grow Glutes Without Legs; Hip Dips

**Andrew Huberman:** Lifting or Pilates for strength training?

**Bret Contreras:** Lifting.

**Andrew Huberman:** How to grow glutes without growing legs, and can hip dips be induced by lifting?

**Bret Contreras:** How to grow glutes without growing legs — I was the first person to make this a thing, back in 2009, 2010. It's tricky because most of the best glute exercises are leg exercises. But the Plotkin study and subsequent studies show you can grow your glutes similarly effectively through hip thrusts as through squats and step-ups. So you can do hip thrust, kickbacks, 45-degree hypers, abduction — but minimize the squats, lunges, and stiff-leg deadlifts. You can probably do more volume for those isolation lifts. You might do four sets of each because they don't make you very sore. But many women think they don't want leg growth because they have fat on their legs, and once they lean out, they actually like the way their legs look.

Can hip dips be induced through weight training? Hip dips are the hollow point where the glute medius meets the glute maximus. If you get lean, you have to have hip dips — there's no muscle there. When people come up with hip dip workouts, they're full of it. You can't grow muscle in an imaginary space. The solution is body fat stored there, or if you get lean enough, embrace them. The funny thing is Ronnie Coleman from behind flexing his glutes — you see this butterfly shape — and his hip dips don't look bad because you've got these giant outer quads. But with women without those outer quads, it can look unsightly. But that's because we've been programmed to be worried about it. If you're lean, strong, and muscular, lean into it. Can you make them worse through strength training? Yes, in my opinion, because you make them more pronounced as you get leaner. If you're really worried about it, maintain a higher body fat percentage and maybe don't train as much glute medius and frontal-plane hip abduction.

## [02:51:48] Spot Reduction, Abs & Fat Loss; Wide vs Narrow Hips & Training; Grip Strength; Tool: One Set to Failure

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there anything someone can do to accelerate fat loss in their abdominal region?

**Bret Contreras:** We've known that spot reduction is a myth since the '90s. You can do all the crunches you want — it doesn't target fat loss in that area. You can have site-specific muscle growth, but not site-specific fat loss. Fat loss comes off from the entire body through caloric deficit, eating less, moving more, or any combination thereof. Your abs tend to look the best when you're your leanest, not when you're training them the most. That said, it's not to say you shouldn't ever train your abs.

**Andrew Huberman:** Should people train glutes differently depending on whether they have wide versus narrow hips?

**Bret Contreras:** Theoretically, maybe — if you feel like you have V-shaped glutes, maybe do more lower glutes and not as much upper, maybe don't do as much abduction. But most of my clients train glutes the same way regardless. Grip strength: if somebody wants to add grip strength without doing a full specialization phase — if your low back is fine, do farmer's walks, shrugs, and just hold the last rep of your last set of deadlifts for time. If your low back is problematic, do static hangs or progress to one-arm hangs.

**Andrew Huberman:** What is the most unusual but effective training tip that you've never seen widely discussed in social media?

**Bret Contreras:** One set to failure. Two full body workouts a week. One set to failure for each body part, six to ten exercises per day. The workout is done in 45 minutes. So you're training an hour and a half a week and you probably could get 80% of your gains compared to training three times as long. It's very time-efficient. It's very fun because you come in with a goal — you need to try to beat your record. The only thing I would say is: shuffle the lifts around, shuffle the order around, shuffle the variations around, because you can't just keep gaining strength for years on end on the same lift. But train the whole body, one set to failure after a sufficient warm-up for each body part. One, two, or three times a week. This helps more people stick to weight training because they realize they don't have to be in the gym five hours a day.
