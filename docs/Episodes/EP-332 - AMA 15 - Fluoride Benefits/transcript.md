## Why Is Fluoride in Water and Is It Harmful?

The first question is about fluoride. Specifically: why is fluoride in our water, and how much is it harming us?

That might seem like a short question, but there is actually a lot to unpack. The phrasing "how much is it harming us" implies that fluoride is necessarily harming us — and I want to be very clear that the degree to which fluoride can harm or help you depends entirely on how much of it you are consuming. There are clear benefits of fluoride for tooth strength and for warding off cavities. But if you get too much fluoride into your system, it is dangerous — it is a poison at certain levels. As you've probably heard before, the dose makes the poison.

### The Ongoing Legal Debate

To understand why fluoride is in drinking water, it helps to look at what's happening right now in 2024. There is a major lawsuit in the state of California to try and get fluoride removed from the drinking water, filed by individuals who strongly believe that fluoride at any concentration is harmful. Their concern is based on evidence that if fluoride levels in drinking water exceed a certain threshold, it can cause problems with thyroid hormone function and perhaps certain aspects of brain function.

At the same time, there is also a major lawsuit in Buffalo, New York, where citizens are suing the city because they insist there was not enough fluoride in the water — and they are claiming damages based on the dental health needs of their children who did not, in their opinion, get enough fluoride. Whether the fluoride levels were actually as low as claimed is still debated, but the point is this: there is a wide divergence of opinion. Some people want more fluoride in their water and are suing cities because of its absence. Others want none of it and are suing cities because of its presence. This is a genuinely barbed-wire topic.

### Why Fluoride Was Added to Water in the First Place

Your teeth are always in one of two states: demineralization (which I'll call "demin") or remineralization ("remin"). A cavity forms when bacteria — in particular Streptococcus mutans, or strep mutans — feeds on sugars in your mouth and, as a byproduct, creates acids that erode the enamel and deeper layers of your teeth. That is demineralization. The reverse process, remineralization, involves the construction of mineral crystals that stack together in very organized sequences — much like LEGO blocks — and the critical element within those structures is called hydroxyapatite.

Fluoride was not discovered because it is a vitamin or essential nutrient. What was discovered is that fluoride can get inside those crystal chains and form bonds that are actually stronger than the hydroxyapatite bonds that would normally form. In other words, if you consume fluoridated water or use fluoride toothpaste — especially when you are young — the mineralization structure of the teeth becomes supraphysiologically strong, limiting the ability of strep mutans and its acids to erode the enamel and cause cavities.

Every dentist I spoke to — traditional, functional, and periodontists alike — agreed at a chemical and mechanical level that fluoride creates stronger teeth. However, they also all acknowledged that the bonds formed by fluoride are structurally different from the natural hydroxyapatite bonds. Under an electron microscope, you can see the difference. Some dentists noted that it would be preferable for teeth to remineralize through natural hydroxyapatite formation, but they understood and agreed with the public health rationale for fluoridation. Many people do not take adequate care of their teeth, and a low-cost government intervention — adding fluoride to the water supply, which began in earnest in the 1950s — was seen as a practical way to reduce widespread tooth decay.

### How Much Fluoride Is Harming You?

To answer that question meaningfully, you need to know two things: how much fluoride is in your water, and how much of that water you are actually drinking.

There is a tremendous range of fluoride concentrations in tap water depending on what city you live in. The CDC has set a recommended level of 0.7 mg/L of fluoride in drinking water as the concentration deemed safe for preventing tooth decay. However, just because a concentration is deemed safe does not tell you whether you specifically are in a safe zone — that depends on how much water you drink daily and your body weight. A small child consuming the same volume of water as an adult will be exposed to far more fluoride relative to their body mass. Drug dosages are calculated in mg per kilogram of body weight for exactly this reason.

High levels of fluoride have been shown to disrupt thyroid hormone metabolism. It has also been hypothesized — and this remains highly debated — that fluoride can be neurotoxic under certain conditions, meaning it can kill neurons at certain concentrations. Much of that evidence comes from in vitro studies (done in a dish), though there is some in vivo evidence of neurotoxicity at high doses. These are not the concentrations typically present in fluoridated drinking water, but the concern is real enough that people take different stances on the issue.

People tend to fall into three broad categories: those not concerned who simply want fluoride to strengthen their teeth; those mildly concerned who keep an eye on levels but continue drinking tap water; and those very concerned about fluoride at any concentration in their drinking water, hence the lawsuits. Whichever camp you fall into, you should know that if you want to reduce your fluoride intake, water filtration is an option. Many standard filters remove or reduce fluoride, and there are whole-house filtration systems that eliminate it entirely from drinking taps.

Personally, I filter the water I drink. I use either a gravity-fed refrigerator filter or a whole-house filter installed at the drinking water taps. For cooking — boiling rice, pasta, oatmeal — I do not worry about filtering for fluoride. But for direct consumption, I use filtered water. I am not someone who gets hyper-concerned about these things, but given that fluoride filtration is relatively low cost and there are legitimate concerns about excess fluoride, it is a reasonable step to take. If removing fluoride from your water makes you worried about tooth health, I would point you to the upcoming episode on oral health, which covers a range of approaches — including natural remineralization protocols — that dentists agree can maintain strong teeth regardless of your stance on fluoride.

### A Personal Story About Fluoride Toxicity

I want to share a brief personal story that illustrates both the benefit and the danger of fluoride. When I was a child, I had significant dental problems — not from sugar consumption, which my mother kept low, but because my adult teeth came in behind my baby teeth rather than pushing them out. As a result, I had all my baby teeth pulled from the top. This gave me a very poor relationship with dentists from an early age.

The dental office I went to used to administer fluoride treatments using small trays — similar to boxing mouth guards — filled with a sour, jelly-like fluoride paste, placed on both the top and bottom teeth. They would seat me in a wicker chair in front of a television playing cartoons, which did nothing to improve the experience. At around five or six years old, I decided to swallow all the fluoride paste. I sucked it down through the trays, felt it going down my throat. Within about three minutes, I was violently sick and vomited all over the wicker chair. Reflecting back, that was probably deliberate — a five-year-old's protest.

Why did I vomit? Because fluoride at high concentrations is, indeed, a poison. That is not a reason to be afraid of fluoridated toothpaste or fluoridated water at normal levels — it is a reason to understand that dose matters. I have since avoided fluoride toothpaste personally and have maintained very strong gum health and very few cavities, largely through other protocols I will describe in the oral health episode. But I share the story to make the point concrete: fluoride is a compound with genuine benefits at the right dose and genuine toxicity at the wrong dose.
