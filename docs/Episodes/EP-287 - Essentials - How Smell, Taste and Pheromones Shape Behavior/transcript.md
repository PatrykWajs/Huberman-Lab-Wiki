# Essentials - How Smell, Taste & Pheromones Shape Behavior

## [00:00:00] Huberman Lab Essentials; Smell & Taste

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. This podcast is separate from my teaching and research roles at Stanford.

Today we're going to talk about chemical sensing — the sense of smell, our ability to detect odors in our environment. We're also going to talk about taste, our ability to detect chemicals and make sense of chemicals that are put in our mouth and into our digestive tract. And we are going to talk about chemicals that are made by other human beings that powerfully modulate the way that we feel, our hormones, and our health. That last category are sometimes called pheromones — however, whether or not pheromones exist in humans is rather controversial. There actually hasn't been a clear example of a true human pheromonal effect.

What is absolutely clear, what is undeniable, is that there are chemicals that human beings make and release — in things like tears, onto our skin, in sweat, and even breath — that powerfully modulate or control the biology of other individuals. There are things floating around in the environment which we call volatile chemicals. When you actually smell something, like a wonderfully smelling rose or cake, you are inhaling the particles into your nose — little particles of those chemicals are going up into your nose and being detected by your brain.

Other ways of getting chemicals into our system include putting them in our mouth, chewing them, and breaking them down into their component parts — that's how we sense chemicals with the tongue. Sometimes we bring these chemicals into our body through deliberate action, selecting and chewing a food intentionally. Sometimes they come into our body through non-deliberate action, like entering an environment with smoke and smelling it and taking action. And sometimes other people are actively making chemicals with their body — through breath, through tears, or possibly by making what are called pheromones: molecules released into the environment that enter our system through the nose or eyes or mouth and fundamentally change our biology.

## [00:02:04] Tears, Biological Response & Communication

I'll give an example that is very salient and was published about ten years ago in the journal Science, showing that men in particular had a strong biological and hormonal response to the tears of women. What researchers did was have women cry — in this case only women, for whatever reason — and collect those tears. Male subjects either smelled those tears or received a saline control. Men that smelled tears evoked by sadness had a significant reduction in their testosterone levels. They also had a reduction in activity in brain areas associated with sexual arousal.

The researchers recruited subjects who had a high propensity for crying at sad movies — which was not exclusively women. They were really trying to get tears that were authentically cried in response to sadness, as opposed to tears evoked by an irritant placed in the eye. Nonetheless, what this study illustrates is that there are chemicals in tears that are changing the biology of other individuals. I chose this study not because I want to focus on the effects of tears on hormones per se — although I find the results genuinely interesting — but to underscore the fact that chemicals made by other individuals are powerfully modulating our internal state.

Most of us can appreciate that if we smell something putrid we tend to retract, or if we smell something delicious we lean in. But there are many ways in which chemicals are affecting our biology, and interpersonal chemical communication is not something we hear about often but is extraordinarily interesting. So let's talk about what smell is and how it works.

Here is how smell works: smell starts with sniffing. No volatile chemicals can enter our nose unless we inhale them. If our nose is occluded or if we're actively exhaling, it's much more difficult for smells to enter, which is why people cover their nose when something smells bad. The nose has a mucosal lining designed to trap things and get them stuck near the base of your brain. Right about 2 cm up is your olfactory bulb — a collection of neurons that actually extend out of the skull into the mucosal lining of the nose. You have neurons that extend their little dendrites and processes out into the mucus to respond to different odorant compounds.

## [00:05:05] Smell, Innate vs Learned Response, Memory

The olfactory neurons also send a branch deeper into the brain, and they split into three different paths. One path is for what we call innate odor responses — hardwired aspects of how you smell the world that were there from the day you were born and will be there until you die. These are pathways and neurons that respond to things like smoke, which has a highly adaptive function: detecting burning things generally signals lack of safety or impending threat. These neurons project to the amygdala, which is often discussed in terms of fear but is really about fear and threat detection.

You also have neurons in your nose that respond to odorants or combinations of odorants that evoke desire and what we call appetitive behaviors — approach behaviors that make you want to move toward something. When you smell a delicious cookie or a savory dish you really enjoy, that is because of these innate pathways that require no learning whatsoever. Some pathways from the nose into the brain are involved in learned associations with odors. Many people have the experience of remembering the smell of their grandmother's home or particular items baking — typically evoking a kind of nurturing feeling of safety and protection.

One of the reasons why olfaction is so closely tied to memory is that it is the most ancient sense we have. We have a pathway for innate responses and a pathway for learned responses. In humans, it is a little bit controversial as to whether a third pathway sits truly separate from the standard olfactory system or is its own system embedded within it — but they call this the accessory olfactory pathway.

## [00:08:31] Accessory Olfactory Pathway, Pheromones, Vandenbergh effect

The accessory olfactory pathway is what in other animals is responsible for true pheromone effects. In rodents and in some primates, including mandrills — if you've ever seen a mandrill they have these big beak-like noses; look them up if you haven't, M-A-N-D-R-I-L-L-S — there are strong pheromone effects. One example: if you take a pregnant female rodent or mandrill, remove the father who created those fetuses, and introduce the scent of the urine or fur of a novel male, she will spontaneously abort or miscarry. It's a very powerful effect.

Another example is called the Vandenbergh effect, named after the person who discovered it. If you take a female of a given species that has not entered puberty and expose her to the scent or urine of a sexually competent — meaning post-pubertal — male, she spontaneously enters puberty earlier. Something about the scent triggers a response through this accessory olfactory system, creating ovulation or, in rodents, an estrus cycle. This is not to say the exact same things happen in humans. In humans, as mentioned earlier, there is chemical sensing between individuals that may operate independently of the nose — but those are basically the three paths by which odors impact us.

## [00:10:41] Smell & Alertness, Smelling Salts, Tool: Nasal Breathing & Learning

I want to talk about the act of smelling. If you are not somebody who is particularly interested in smell, but you are interested in making your brain work better, learning faster, and remembering more things — this segment is for you. It turns out that how you smell, meaning the act of smelling rather than how good or bad you smell, powerfully impacts how your brain functions and what you can and cannot learn.

Noam Sobel's group, originally at UC Berkeley and then at the Weizmann Institute, has published a number of papers I'd like to discuss. One of them — "Human non-olfactory cognition phase locked with inhalation" — was published in Nature Human Behaviour. What this paper shows is that as we inhale, the level of alertness in the brain goes up. This makes sense because olfaction is the most primitive and primordial sense by which we interact with our environment. Inhaling is a cue for the rest of the brain to pay attention to what's happening — not just to the odors. As the paper's title suggests: human non-olfactory cognition is phase locked with inhalation. The act of inhaling itself wakes up the brain.

Sniffing — inhaling — as an action has a powerful effect on your ability to be alert, to attend, to focus, and to remember information. When we exhale, the brain goes through a subtle but nonetheless significant dip in its level of arousal and ability to learn. How should you use this knowledge? Focus on nasal breathing while doing any kind of focused work that doesn't require you to speak or eat or ingest something. There is a separate paper published in the Journal of Neuroscience showing that if subjects are restricted to breathing through their nose, they learn better than if they have the option of breathing through their mouth or a combination. There are other ways to wake up the brain more as well — for instance, the use of smelling salts.

## [00:14:06] Tool: Increase Sense of Smell; Brain Health, Olfactory Neurons

Inhaling through your nose and doing nasal breathing is going to be a more subtle version of waking up your system and alerting your brain. For those interested in having a richer, deeper connection to the things they smell and taste, practicing and enhancing your ability to sniff might sound like a ridiculous protocol, but it is actually a fun experiment you can do. Simply take an object — say, an orange — smell it, do ten or fifteen nasal inhales, and then smell it again. You'll notice that your perception of that smell, the richness of it, will be significantly increased. You can actually have a heightened experience, and this will also be true for the taste system.

No other system in our body that I'm aware of is as amenable to behavioral training shifts that happen so quickly. In fact, how well we can smell and taste things is actually a very strong indication of our brain health. Our olfactory neurons — the neurons in our nose that detect odors — are unique among other brain neurons because they get replenished throughout life. They don't just regenerate when damaged; they are constantly turning over throughout our lifespan, dying off and being replaced by new ones. Other neurons in your cortex, retina, or cerebellum do not do this — but these olfactory neurons are special.

A number of things seem to increase the amount of olfactory neuron neurogenesis. There is evidence that exercise and increased blood flow can increase olfactory neuron neurogenesis, although those data are fewer relative to evidence for social interactions and actually interacting with odorants of different kinds. Enhancing your olfactory abilities and taste abilities by interacting with odors — preferably positive ones — and sniffing more, inhaling more, is grounded in the real mechanistic biology of how the brain wakes up and responds to these chemicals.

## [00:16:54] Traumatic Brain Injury & Olfactory Dysfunction

Speaking of brain injury, olfactory dysfunction is a common theme in traumatic brain injury for the following reason. These olfactory neurons extend wires into the mucosa of the nose but also extend a wire up into the skull through what's called the cribriform plate — a Swiss cheese-type plate. If you get a hit to the head, that bone, the cribriform plate, can shear those little wires off and those neurons die. Eventually they will be replaced, but there is a phenomenon by which the severity of concussion and recovery from a head injury can actually be gauged in part by how fully one recovers their sense of smell.

If you have unfortunately suffered a concussion, your sense of smell is one readout by which you might evaluate whether or not you're regaining sensory performance — along with other readouts like balance, cognition, and sleep. I'd like to refer you to a paper entitled "Olfactory Dysfunction in Traumatic Brain Injury: The Role of Neurogenesis." The first author is Marin. It was published in Current Allergy and Asthma Reports in 2020. It is a peer-reviewed review article.

What the paper discusses is that olfactory functioning disturbances are common following traumatic brain injury (TBI) and can have a significant impact on quality of life. Although there is no standard treatment for post-injury loss of smell, olfactory training has shown promise for beneficial effects. This means that if you've had a head injury or repeated head injuries, enhancing your sense of smell is one way to create new neurons. And now you know how to enhance your sense of smell: by interacting closely with things that have an odor and by focusing on the inhale to wake up the brain and attend to the nuances of those smells.

## [00:19:07] Smell, Alertness, Smelling Salts, Tool: Peppermint

As a last point about specific odors and compounds that can increase arousal and alertness through sniffing rather than ingestion — there are good data on peppermint. Minty-type scents, whether you like them or not, will increase attention and can create the same sort of arousal response, although not as intensely or dramatically as ammonia salts. Please don't go sniff real ammonia: you could damage your olfactory epithelium if you do so too close to the source. If you're going to use smelling salts, be sure you work with someone or know exactly what you're getting and how to use them. You can damage your olfactory pathway in ways that are quite severe, and you can also damage your vision — tearing up because you inhaled something noxious means you have irritated the mucosal lining and possibly the surfaces of your eye.

Scents like peppermint, and ammonia-based smelling salts, wake you up because they trigger specific olfactory neurons that communicate with specific centers of the brain — namely the amygdala and associated neural circuitry — triggering alertness of the same sort that a cold shower, an ice bath, a sudden surprise, or a stressful text message would evoke. The systems of your body that produce arousal, alertness, and attention and cue you for optimal learning involve very general mechanisms with basic molecules like adrenaline and epinephrine. The number of stimuli that can evoke that adrenaline response — peppermint, ammonia, a loud blast — is near infinite. That is the beauty of your nervous system: it was designed to take any variety of stimuli, place them into categories, and evoke different categories of very general responses.

## [00:21:15] Taste Modalities & Functions; Taste & Digestive System

Now you know a lot about olfaction and how the sense of smell works. Let's talk about taste — how we sense chemicals in food and drink. There are essentially five, and scientists now believe there may be six, things that we taste alone or in combination. They are sweet, salty, bitter, sour, and umami. Most of you have probably heard of umami by now — U-M-A-M-I — which is the name for a particular receptor expressed on your tongue that detects savory tastes. Each one has a particular group of neurons in your mouth and tongue that respond to particular chemicals and chemical structures.

It is a total myth — complete fiction — that different parts of your tongue harbor different taste receptors. You know that high school textbook diagram where sweet is in one part of the tongue, sour in another, and bitter in another? All of these receptors are completely intermixed along your tongue. These receptors in your tongue give rise to the gustatory nerve, which runs from the tongue to the nucleus of the solitary tract, then to the thalamus and insular cortex. It is in the insular cortex — this region of the cortex — where we sort out and make sense of and perceive the various tastes. Taking a little bit of sugar or something sour like lemon juice and touching it to the tongue: within 100 milliseconds you can immediately distinguish sweet, sour, bitter, or umami. That assessment is made by the cortex.

What do these five receptors encode for? Sweet tastes signal the presence of energy — sugars — and the ability to sense whether a food is a rapid energy source or can give rise to glucose is essential. Salty receptors are trying to sense whether or not there are electrolytes in a given food or drink; electrolytes are vitally important for the function of our nervous system and entire body. Bitter receptors are there to make sure we don't ingest things that are poisonous — they create what we call a labeled line, a unique trajectory to neurons in the brain stem that control the gag reflex. If we taste something very bitter, it automatically triggers the gag reflex, and putrid smells will also evoke these same neurons.

The umami receptor isn't sensing savory simply because the body loves savory — it is because savory is a signal for the presence of amino acids. The presence of amino acids in our gut and digestive system, along with fatty acids, is essential. The sour receptor is there to detect spoiled or fermented food: fermented fruit can be poisonous, and alcohols are poisonous in many forms to our system. Sour-receptor-bearing neurons communicate to an area of the brain stem that evokes the pucker response — closing of the eyes and shutting of the mouth and cringing away.

What is the putative sixth taste? There are now data to support the idea — although more work needs to be done — that we also have receptors on our tongue that sense fat. Because fat is so vital for the function of our nervous system and other organs, we may be sensing the fat content in food. You have to understand that your tongue and mouth are an extension of your digestive tract. We are essentially a series of tubes, and that tube starts with your mouth and heads down into your stomach. The mouth's job is to sense the chemical constituents of what you might bring into your body — is it putrid? Is it sour and fermented and going to poison you? Is it so bitter it could poison you? Is it so savory you want more and more? If so, you would trigger dopamine release. All of that starts in the mouth.

Those little bumps on your tongue called papillae are not your taste buds. Surrounding those little papillae like little rivers are dents and indentations that allow more surface area and allow you to pack more receptors. Down in those grooves are where all the little neurons and their processes sit, with receptors for sweet, salty, bitter, umami, sour, and maybe fat as well. Remember, even though we can enjoy food and evolve our sense of what's tasty or not depending on life decisions and environmental changes, the taste system — just like the olfactory system and the visual system — was laid down for the purpose of moving towards things that are good for us and moving away from things that are bad for us. That is the core function of the nervous system.

## [00:27:30] Pheromones, Coolidge Effect, Humans & Chemical Communication

Now I'd like to return to pheromones. As mentioned, true pheromonal effects are well established in animals. One of the most remarkable pheromone effects ever described is the Coolidge effect. The Coolidge effect is the effect whereby a male of a given species — in most cases a rodent or a rooster — mates and at some point reaches exhaustion or the inability to mate again. The Coolidge effect establishes that if you swap out the hen for a new one or the female rat or mouse for a new one, the rat or rooster spontaneously regains the ability to mate. Somehow their vigor is returned. The refractory period after mating that normally occurs is abolished, and they can mate again.

It turns out that females also exhibit this: female rodents will mate to exhaustion and at some point refuse to mate any longer, unless you swap in a new male — after which they spontaneously regain the desire to mate. How do we know it's a pheromonal effect? The recovery of the desire and ability to mate, both in males and females, can be evoked completely by the odor of a new male or female. It doesn't even have to be the actual animal's presence. That is how you know it's not a visual or some other interaction — it is a pheromonal interaction.

Pheromonal effects in humans have been debated for a long time. We are thought to have a vestigial — a kind of shrunken-down miniature — accessory olfactory structure called Jacobson's organ or the vomeronasal organ. Some people don't believe Jacobson's organ exists in humans; some do. There is evidence of something vomeronasal tucked into some of the divots of the nasal passage. Even if that organ is not there or is not responsible for the chemical signaling between individuals, there is chemical signaling between human beings. As mentioned earlier, the effect of tears in suppressing the areas of the brain involved in sexual desire and lowering testosterone in males is a concrete result published by an excellent group.

There is also evidence both for and against chemical signaling between women in terms of synchronization of menstrual cycles. The original paper on this was published in the 1970s by McClintock, which essentially said that when women live together in group housing like dormitories, their menstrual cycles were synchronized, hypothesized to be due to pheromonal effects. Over the years that study has been challenged many times. The more recent data point to the idea that there is chemical signaling between women in ways that impact the timing of the menstrual cycle. By the strict definition of a pheromone — a molecule released from one individual that impacts the biology of another — yes, this qualifies, though what the specific chemical compound is remains unclear.

None of this should surprise us. It is very clear that hormones have a profound effect on a large number of systems in our biology, and that smell and taste and the ability to sense the chemical states of others — either consciously or subconsciously — have a profound influence on whether or not we might want to spend time with someone, whether this is somebody we are pair-bonded with, whether this is somebody we just met and don't yet trust. Given what is at stake in terms of reproductive biology, it makes so much sense that much of our biology is wired toward detecting and sensing whether things and people are things we should approach or avoid.

You and every other human, from the time you're born until the time you die, are actively seeking out and sensing and evaluating the chemicals that come from other individuals. A remarkable study done by the Weizmann Institute — I believe also from Noam Sobel's group — looked at human interactions when people meet for the first time. What they found was that people would reach out and shake hands, and almost every time — within just a few seconds of having shaken hands with a new individual — people will touch their eyes. They are taking chemicals from the skin contact and placing them on a mucosal membrane of some sort, typically their eyes. You are marking other people when you shake their hand, and they are then taking your mark and rubbing it on themselves subconsciously. We are evaluating the molecules on people's breath, evaluating the molecules on people's skin by actively rubbing them on ourselves, and actively sensing the chemicals they are emitting — their hormone status, how they smell, possibly even their pheromones. People are shaping each other's biology all the time by way of these chemicals being traded from one body to the next through air, skin-to-skin contact, and tears.
