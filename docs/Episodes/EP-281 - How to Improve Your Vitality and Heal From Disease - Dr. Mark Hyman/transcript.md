## [00:19:48] Medicine & Research; "Exposome", Impediments & Ingredients for Health, Whole Foods

## [00:26:30] Seed Oils, Starch & Sugar, Ultra-Processed Foods; Obesity Rise

**Andrew Huberman:** There's so much debate about which foods are bad. Where do you stand on seed oils?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** Look, I think there's a hierarchy here. And I think the evidence is quite clear now that the primary driver of our metabolic crisis is starch and sugar — refined carbohydrates. Insulin resistance from excess carbohydrate intake is the root of most chronic disease. When you eat too much starch and sugar, you spike insulin, you become insulin resistant over time, you gain fat around your organs, you develop inflammation, you develop metabolic syndrome, which leads to diabetes, heart disease, Alzheimer's, cancer — it's all connected.

Now, seed oils — these are polyunsaturated omega-6 fats found in soybean oil, corn oil, canola oil, sunflower oil — they've increased dramatically in our diet over the last century as industrial food processing has expanded. And there are legitimate concerns about them, particularly when they're heated, which creates oxidized lipids and aldehydes that are quite toxic. But I don't think they're as acutely harmful as the starch and sugar issue.

What's really the most toxic combination is fat plus starch — which is what you find in most ultra-processed foods. Potato chips, french fries, pastries — they combine the inflammatory effects of excessive starch with the problematic fat. That combination is uniquely fattening and inflammatory.

**Andrew Huberman:** And ultra-processed foods are everywhere. How do you define ultra-processed?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** The NOVA classification defines ultra-processed foods as industrial formulations made mostly or entirely from substances derived from foods, with little or no whole food, and with added preservatives, colorings, flavorings, emulsifiers and other additives designed to make them hyperpalatable. Things like chips, breakfast cereals, soda, packaged bread, hot dogs, flavored yogurts, instant noodles, protein bars. Things that contain ingredients you would never find in a home kitchen.

And what's terrifying is that these foods now make up about 60% of calories in the American diet — even higher in children. And the data is very clear: ultra-processed food consumption is associated with higher rates of obesity, diabetes, heart disease, depression, dementia, and cancer. And part of the mechanism is the gut microbiome — these foods destroy the microbiome, which then drives inflammation systemically.

**Andrew Huberman:** The rise in obesity is striking. What are the major drivers?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** It's really the food environment. We've engineered food to be hyperpalatable, cheap, convenient, and ubiquitous. Food scientists have used enormous resources — billions of dollars — to figure out exactly the combination of salt, sugar, fat, and texture that hits what they call the bliss point, making the food essentially addictive. And then we've made it available everywhere — gas stations, pharmacies, hardware stores — and made it incredibly cheap relative to real food. And we've done this over 50 years, so we've basically changed the food environment completely. And people's biology responds to that environment. It's not a moral failing — it's biology responding to a food environment that it was not designed for.

## [00:46:25] Essential Supplements, Omega-3s, Vitamin D3, Multivitamin, Iodine, Methylated B12

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about supplements. What do you consider the baseline — the things that almost everyone should be taking?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** If I had to boil it down to the essential core, I would say: omega-3 fatty acids, vitamin D3, a high-quality multivitamin, magnesium, and iodine. Let me explain each.

Omega-3s — EPA and DHA — are absolutely critical. Most people are deficient because we've dramatically reduced our intake of fatty fish and increased our intake of omega-6-rich seed oils, creating an inflammatory imbalance. The research on omega-3s is extensive — cardiovascular benefits, brain benefits, anti-inflammatory effects, mood benefits. I recommend one to two grams of EPA and DHA per day. Get a product that's been third-party tested for purity — mercury contamination in fish oil is a real concern.

Vitamin D3 — this is not just a vitamin, it's actually a hormone that regulates thousands of genes. The deficiency is epidemic in modern society because we spend almost no time outdoors, and when we do, we often wear sunscreen. Most people need 2,000 to 4,000 IU per day, though some people need much more. Get your 25-hydroxy vitamin D tested — you want to be around 60 to 80 ng/mL. And take it with K2, because they work together.

A high-quality multivitamin — because even if you eat a good diet, you can't get all the micronutrients you need from food alone given the nutrient depletion of our soils. Look for one with methylated B vitamins — methylated folate and methylcobalamin, not cyanocobalamin.

Magnesium — most people are deficient. It's involved in over 600 enzymatic reactions in the body. It's critical for sleep, for muscle function, for glucose metabolism, for DNA synthesis. The best forms are magnesium glycinate for sleep and anxiety, magnesium threonate for brain health, magnesium citrate for bowel function.

Iodine — this is a hugely underappreciated nutrient. It's critical for thyroid function, which controls your metabolism. But it's also important for breast tissue, for prostate tissue, for immune function. Most people don't get enough because we've reduced our consumption of iodized salt as "fancy" salts without iodine have become popular, and because soil iodine depletion means foods have less. You can get it from seaweed and iodized salt, or supplement with it.

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** And methylated B12 — this is critical for energy, nerve function, brain health, detoxification. And a huge percentage of the population has variants in the MTHFR gene that impair their ability to methylate. So you need the already-methylated form — methylcobalamin — not cyanocobalamin, which the body has to convert.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about magnesium forms for sleep specifically?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** Magnesium glycinate and magnesium threonate are the best for sleep and anxiety. I take both. Glycinate is well-absorbed and calming. Threonate crosses the blood-brain barrier particularly well, which is why it's especially good for brain function and cognitive health. The research on magnesium threonate for Alzheimer's prevention is actually quite promising.

## [00:56:54] Supplements & Traditional Medicine; Limited Budget & Nutrition

**Andrew Huberman:** How does traditional medicine view supplements? There's often a lot of skepticism.

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** Traditional medicine tends to dismiss supplements as "you just make expensive urine." And there's some truth to that if you're taking the wrong forms or if you're not deficient. But the reality is that micronutrient deficiencies are rampant in the modern world, and they have profound effects on health. The research on vitamin D alone is extraordinary — deficiency is associated with virtually every chronic disease you can name. But conventional medicine has been slow to adopt supplementation as a standard practice.

Part of the problem is the business model — you can't patent vitamins, so there's no incentive for pharmaceutical companies to fund large randomized trials. And the FDA requires pharmaceutical standards for drug claims, which means supplement companies can't make disease claims even when the evidence is strong. So we have this bizarre situation where the evidence for many supplements is quite good, but they're not incorporated into mainstream practice.

**Andrew Huberman:** What if someone has a very limited budget? What's the priority?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** If I had to prioritize with a very limited budget, I would say: vitamin D3 and omega-3s. You can get both for very little. Vitamin D3 is incredibly cheap. But the most important thing — more important than supplements — is food. And good food doesn't have to be expensive if you make the right choices. Eggs are extraordinarily nutritious and cheap. Canned sardines and mackerel are rich in omega-3s and cheap. Frozen vegetables are just as nutritious as fresh. Dried legumes are incredibly nutritious and very cheap. ## [01:26:03] Toxins, Food Additives, Generally Recognized As Safe (GRAS)

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about toxins in our food supply. The GRAS designation — generally recognized as safe — seems like a significant loophole.

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** It's one of the biggest problems in the regulatory system. Under the Delaney Clause and the GRAS system, food companies can introduce new food additives — new chemicals — without any prior FDA review, as long as they determine that the substance is "generally recognized as safe." They can hire their own scientists, do their own review, and add the chemical to food without the FDA ever knowing about it.

There are over 10,000 chemicals in our food supply, and the vast majority have never been independently tested for safety. Many of them were approved in the 1950s and '60s under very limited safety testing. And we know now that many of them — artificial colors, preservatives, emulsifiers, artificial sweeteners — have real effects on the gut microbiome, on hormonal signaling, on inflammation, on behavior in children.

The European Union has a much more precautionary approach. They require chemicals to be proven safe before they can be used. We do the opposite — we assume something is safe unless proven otherwise, and by the time the harm is proven, the chemical has been in the food supply for decades.

## [01:36:58] Big Food, Company Consolidation, Nutrition Labels

**Andrew Huberman:** What about nutrition labels? Are they useful?

**Dr. Mark Hyman:** Nutrition labels are intentionally confusing. They show you calories, macronutrients, a few vitamins — but they don't tell you about the quality of the food. A "low-fat" food might be loaded with sugar. A food with 300 calories might be from whole ingredients or from industrial chemicals. The whole system is designed to obscure rather than illuminate.

The better approach is Robert Lustig's rule: if it comes from a plant, it's probably okay; if it's made in a plant, be careful. Or Michael Pollan's: eat food, not too much, mostly plants. Or my version: if your great-grandmother wouldn't recognize it as food, don't eat it.

**Andrew Huberman:** Robert F. Kennedy had a big meeting with the consumer brand association, basically telling the food industry to clean up its act.
