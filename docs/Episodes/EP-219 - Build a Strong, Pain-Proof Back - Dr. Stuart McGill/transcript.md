## [00:06:23] What Causes Back Pain?; Genetics, Dog Breed Analogy

**Andrew Huberman:** And now for my discussion with Dr. Stuart McGill. Welcome — I'm a big fan of your work. I've watched a lot of your other content, read your books, and I'm excited to discuss what makes for a really strong, resilient back, what causes back pain and how to relieve it, and what all of that allows for in terms of mobility and functionality not just in sport but in everyday life. So to kick things off: what causes back pain?

**Stuart McGill:** Let me give context before I define it as tightly as I can. Back pain is a symptom. Can you imagine asking someone for an exercise or prevention strategy for "leg pain"? That sets the stage — we're talking about a symptom for which there are a hundred or more different pathways and mechanisms. So we've got to have a comprehensive assessment to focus on the type of back pain and then match an appropriate intervention. What causes back pain? Genetics loads the gun, exposure pulls the trigger, and the psychosocial milieu around the individual influences how they respond to the pain. We can break it down in those three categories.

**Andrew Huberman:** When you say genetics loads the gun — I have a right shoulder that sits slightly lower than my left, my dad has the same thing, I tend to pronate one foot more when I run. If I told you I occasionally have lower right side back pain, would you immediately have an idea about the origin or a remedy, or do you need to drill deeper and understand more about my lifestyle and structure?

**Stuart McGill:** My thought would not go to one or the other but to both, and I'd start that conversation with an analogy. Let's talk about breeds of dogs. A St. Bernard is a big, heavy-set, robust-boned animal — can it run the 100 meters? Of course. But should a St. Bernard train like a Greyhound? No — its joints are not built for that style of movement. If you put a St. Bernard on a Greyhound track and train it for speed, you will injure the dog. The same logic applies to human body types and spines. You must know your architecture before you prescribe a program.

## [00:12:55] Tool: Skeleton & Body Type; Spine Flexibility & Discs

**Andrew Huberman:** Can we use external surrogate markers to assess our spine type without imaging — wrist circumference, knee width, hip width?

**Stuart McGill:** Absolutely. Knee width, bi-iliac crest width, and hip width are all good surrogates indicating general skeletal heaviness. But there's more to the genetics story. The shape of the disc matters: if you take a group of top golfers and look at a transverse MRI scan, their discs are more ovoid. Someone built to bear compressive load has limacon-shaped discs — more like a lima bean. The discs are repeating joints throughout the spine. They are not ball-and-socket joints — they are a fabric of layer upon layer of collagen fibers oriented at alternating angles, like a Michelin tire cross-section. Between each bony vertebra, this disc is simultaneously pliable and compressible, allowing motion while transmitting load. There really is no better mechanical architecture.

**Stuart McGill:** The end plate thickness also matters: people with thicker end plates tend to have more compressive strength but less mobility; those with thinner end plates are more mobile but more vulnerable to compressive loading. The shape and dimensions of your spine at birth set the range within which you can safely train for life.

## [00:20:25] Flexibility & Exercises; Discs & Collagen

**Andrew Huberman:** My sister is naturally very flexible — can bend her fingers back easily, remarkable shoulder extension. Would naturally hypermobile people be wise to avoid certain activities if their goal is to remain pain-free?

**Stuart McGill:** That's a huge question with many variables. The naturally hypermobile person's joints move freely, but their muscles are being asked to provide stabilization around joints that are already at the end of their range. The collagen in the disc is organized to be prestressed — meant to be loaded. When you repeatedly bend a flexible spine to its end range under load, you delaminate the collagen layers. You can increase mobility with training, and you can increase disc collagen toughness with strength training — but those two goals trade off against each other. The hypermobile person should prioritize building stability and control, not seeking ever-greater range of motion.

**Stuart McGill:** Repetitive bending under load accumulates fatigue damage — the disc shows micro-failure with sub-maximal cyclic loading. This is the mechanism behind many disc injuries: not a single traumatic event but hundreds of sub-failure loading cycles that accumulate over time until the annulus finally tears. Understanding this is why spine hygiene — learning to move without repeatedly bending the lumbar spine under load — is the foundation of back pain prevention.

## [00:27:32] Stress & Tipping Point; Athletic Tradeoffs, Triathletes

**Stuart McGill:** Every system in the body requires stress for optimal health — the cardiovascular system, the musculoskeletal system, the endocrine system, even the psychological system. It needs stress to create adaptation for robustness. But you cannot cross what's known as the tipping point, because if you do, you start building cumulative trauma — emotional or psychological, or cumulative stress at the tissue level. We have to define where the tipping points are, try to expand them through adaptation, but never cross them.

**Andrew Huberman:** So athletic tradeoffs are real — your genetics define what you're best suited for. Consider the triathlete: a sport with three very separate physiological demands. The swimmer benefits from broad shoulders and a long trunk to reduce drag. The cyclist needs specific hip flexor and quad architecture. The runner tends toward a smaller, compact lower body with a particular hip and foot structure. The elite triathlete is a compromise of all three — and compromise is the key word. Nobody wins all three legs.

**Stuart McGill:** That's exactly right. For general robustness and a pain-free life, the person with a more willowy, flexible spine does well to build strength in the muscles around the spine — not just abdominals but obliques, quadratus lumborum, and erectors — to give the spine the stability its discs lack. The person with the thicker, stiffer spine can afford to push heavier loads because the architecture is already there. Both types need balance across strength, mobility, and endurance — trained in proportions that match their architecture and goals.

## [00:36:17] Back Pain, Goals & Training Program

**Andrew Huberman:** Should we train against our natural predisposition to offset imbalances, or lean into our strengths?

**Stuart McGill:** The answer comes through assessment — not through a generic prescription. Our assessment finds the movement triggers for pain. Every person who comes to us has back pain, and initially we find the movements that cause pain and modify them. Then we tune the body strategically with stability, mobility, and eventually endurance. The goal of training should always be defined first: what do you want to be able to do in ten or twenty years? Only then can you build the right tool set. Anyone who gives a one-size-fits-all answer to this question is oversimplifying.

**Andrew Huberman:** What does "spine hygiene" mean practically?

**Stuart McGill:** Spine hygiene means learning how to move through daily life without repeatedly loading your spine in the ways that harm it. It means learning the hip hinge — getting objects off the floor by bending at the hips rather than the lumbar spine. Learning to lunge to get to the floor using ball-and-socket joints rather than twisting lumbar vertebrae. Learning to roll out of bed without a loaded spinal flex. We teach people the baby crawl, which eliminates torso twisting while transferring load to the extremities. Each of these small movements, done hundreds of times a day, is either a deposit or a withdrawal from your spine's tissue bank.

## [00:45:57] Spine Hygiene, Back Pain, Powerlifting

**Stuart McGill:** If pain is causing sensitization, here is the analogy: you stub your toe once and the pain goes away quickly. But if every day you stub your toe, you increase sensitivity so that eventually you don't even need to stub it — just a light touch on that sensitized toe produces a maladaptive, heightened response. If we keep creating pain, we keep the nervous system sensitized and the tissue will never heal. We have to start a desensitization wind-down — which is simultaneously tissue-based and neurologically-based.

**Stuart McGill:** We assess sitting posture: slouched posture causes pain, upright posture eliminates it. So I give them a lumbar support — which I'm actually using right now. I sat on an airplane for five hours yesterday coming here, and this support prevents back pain during prolonged sitting. If I sit flexed for five hours I'll have a grumpy back, and I won't feel like going for a walk at the hotel — which is exactly the kind of stress below the tipping point that optimizes health. So the program starts with: know your pain triggers and eliminate them in the short term while building capacity to tolerate them long term.

## [00:53:33] Genetics & Running

**Andrew Huberman:** When someone walks into your clinic — before they say anything about their pain — what do you observe, and what does it tell you?

**Stuart McGill:** I watch them get out of the car if I can — that's when the assessment starts. The way people walk, whether they flex forward at the trunk, whether they have a natural lumbar arch or a flat back — all of these are telling. A great sprinter typically has a pronounced lumbar lordosis and walks with an upright pelvis. That architecture allows hip flexors to act as springs, creating stiffness for explosive forward propulsion. A distance runner tends to have a flatter lumbar curve, distributing load over more joints for a longer, lower-energy stride. There is remarkable variation in human spines that we tend to ignore. We keep acting as if everyone should move the same way and achieve the same range of motion — but the genetics are as varied as dog breeds. Train an Irish Wolfhound like an English Bulldog and you will injure one of them.

## [01:00:46] Rehabilitation & Reducing Volume; Injury

**Andrew Huberman:** For someone with a history of back pain building a long-term sustainable training program — what does the overall rehabilitation arc look like?

**Stuart McGill:** Phase one: pain reduction. Find the pain triggers and remove them completely — not forever, but for now. The spine gets a chance to heal and the nervous system desensitizes. Phase two: building base resilience — tuning the body with strategic mobility for joints that need it, and strategic stability to create control where the spine needs it. Phase three: training for the person's goal — returning to the gym, to sport, or simply being able to carry grandchildren without pain.

**Stuart McGill:** How do you know when you've gone over the tipping point? The most reliable signal is not pain during exercise — exercise often suppresses pain through endogenous analgesics. The signal is your pain level the next morning. If your back is grumpier than before you trained, you went over. If it feels the same or better, you stayed below. Simple rule, but most people ignore it because the pain arrives with a delay and they've disconnected the training from the consequence.

## [01:07:42] Tool: Training for Lifelong Fitness, Injury & Joints

**Andrew Huberman:** My view is that one of the best strategies for lifelong fitness is to train consistently, train reasonably hard, and not get hurt. What do you think?

**Stuart McGill:** Don't get hurt — getting hurt is tremendously asymmetric. Think of it economically: invest $100 and gain 50%, you have $150. Lose 50%, you have $50. The loss hurts far more than the gain helps. Injury in training is exactly that asymmetric. Push too hard and you might gain a little short-term resilience, but the risk of a serious setback is far more damaging than the potential gain.

**Stuart McGill:** And people talk about muscles but don't talk about their joints. The key to a long life of fitness is: don't mess up your joints. Cartilage does not regenerate like muscle does. When you damage a joint surface, you live with that damage for the rest of your life. The strategy is to identify loads just below your tipping point, build consistency there, and let adaptation gradually raise the ceiling — rather than always pushing to or beyond the limit. The most joint-protective training tends to be moderate load, moderate volume, with excellent technique maintained throughout every single repetition.

## [01:17:40] Pain Types, Biopsychosocial Model of Pain

**Andrew Huberman:** We should discuss the biopsychosocial model of pain. Dr. Sean Mackey, head of the pain division at Stanford, is a big proponent. It incorporates psychological and physiological elements and points to seven or more pathways to dealing with pain — emotional state, stress, sleep, and more.

**Stuart McGill:** I love that framework because it is so consistent with what we find. If someone's pain is purely mechanical — they exceeded their tipping point and have tissue damage — the biopsychosocial components are less dominant. We tune the body mechanically and they recover. But consider someone traumatized at the time of injury, or with a history of abuse. When I start to palpate, they recoil. That's a very characteristic response — the pain has a much deeper root than a damaged disc. For these people, the biopsychosocial model is not just accurate, it is the primary framework.

**Stuart McGill:** Sleep is the most underappreciated variable. Chronic sleep deprivation dramatically lowers the pain tipping point — tissues become more sensitized, the nervous system less capable of modulating pain signals. Address the sleep, and sometimes back pain begins to resolve without changing anything else. Chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol, increases systemic inflammation, and lowers the pain threshold substantially. We start every assessment simultaneously biomechanical, neurological, and psychosocial — how they move, what provokes their pain, and what is going on in their life.

## [01:26:15] Coaching, Explosivity & Endurance

**Andrew Huberman:** What broad correlations have you observed between training style and personality? Do endurance athletes have different mental profiles from strength athletes or sprinters?

**Stuart McGill:** I haven't seen good science on this, but after working with people for forty years and seeing the extremes of the phenotype, I have opinions. The elite sprinter tends to be impulsive, ballistic, and extraordinarily focused for very short windows — explosive in personality as well as in movement. The elite endurance athlete has extraordinary mental endurance — they can stay in discomfort for very long periods. The powerlifter tends to be methodical, patient in a concentrated way, capable of generating enormous internal intensity for a brief moment. These are broad strokes with enormous individual variation.

**Stuart McGill:** What I can say with more confidence: the training style you commit to changes you. If you train ballistically for years, your nervous system adapts not just physically but in the way it recruits motor patterns. That ballistic pattern becomes your default in life, not just in the gym. The endurance athlete develops a different default — a different set point for rate of force development, for sustained output, for pain tolerance. The training shapes the organism.

## [01:32:43] Virtual Surgery & Rest, Pain Recovery

**Stuart McGill:** We had a group of patients who had failed every previous treatment — chiropractor, physiotherapist, osteopath, surgical consult, psychologist — and had been basically conditioned to fail. For this group we tried a process called virtual surgery. I make a production of it: I anoint them like a knight, touch them on the shoulder, look into their eyes and say — that's your surgery. Now you've had surgery. Tomorrow is your first post-surgical recovery day. You're going to rest, get up for short shuffles every few hours. Each day we add a little more, following a full post-surgical recovery protocol.

**Stuart McGill:** Long before they completed the protocol, we had these patients walking in pain-free — with no surgery performed. The ritual of surgery, the permission it gives people to truly rest and stop fighting through pain, is as powerful as the operation itself for certain types of mechanical back pain. It is a striking demonstration of how much the psychosocial component contributes to the duration of pain. The general rule for working with pain: early in rehabilitation, work below the pain threshold — find all the movements you can do without pain and build the program around those. As tissue heals and the nervous system desensitizes, gently expand the envelope. Don't charge into pain — court the edge of it.

## [01:41:25] Tool: McGill's Big 3; Building Back Strength & Stability

**Andrew Huberman:** An enormous number of people have written in about how much the Big 3 have helped them. To recap: the bird dog, the modified curl-up, and the side plank — designed to build strength and stability around the spine. If you were to add a fourth exercise to the Big 3, what would it be?

**Stuart McGill:** It depends on the assessment. For someone with a willowy spine, unilateral lower back pain worse after sitting and then standing, with pain shooting down one leg — that presentation suggests discogenic involvement. For that person, I would add a gluteal activation exercise that teaches the glute to fire before the lumbar multifidus during hip extension. When the glute fires first, it unloads the facet joints on the affected side and creates the stiffness the disc needs to stop its irritating movement pattern.

**Stuart McGill:** The Big 3 in proper form build the core cylinder: the diaphragm as the top, pelvic floor as the bottom, obliques and transverse abdominis as the walls. When that cylinder is stiff and pressurized, the spine has the mechanical support it needs to tolerate load and transfer force from legs through trunk to arms. Without that cylinder, every breath and heavy lift recruits the stabilizers as breathing muscles instead — which is why training the Big 3 often improves running endurance. You stop using your core stabilizers as breathing muscles, freeing them to do their stabilizing job.

## [01:46:39] Inversion Tables & Spine Deloading, Disc Bulge, Tool: Lumbar Support

**Andrew Huberman:** What is your view on inversion tables and spinal deloading for disc problems?

**Stuart McGill:** We do spinal deloading through traction in our clinic, but it is applied by trained clinicians because it needs to be tailored. For a patient with a posterior disc bulge, laying prone and exhaling allows the low back to sink into the table and increase lordosis — we measured this in the lab: that maneuver actually vacuums the disc bulge back in. If it immediately reduces pain down the leg, we have them lay prone with someone pulling on their legs — about five or six pounds per leg along the plane of the table. But the next patient might find that same maneuver hurts them. So we play Jazz — adjusting the approach in real time to what that individual spine responds to. No decompression table does this. They're more like a blunt instrument, and the people who come to us have already failed ten previous treatments, so we need nuanced skills.

**Stuart McGill:** For people with discogenic back pain who must sit for long periods, the lumbar support — a foam or inflatable support placed in the curve of the lower back — is one of the simplest and most effective tools available. It is not glamorous, but it works.

## [01:51:09] Tool: Daily Walking; Sitting

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a recommended daily walking dose for spine health — duration, speed, frequency?

**Stuart McGill:** Don't walk to pain. If your tipping point is forty minutes, don't go for a forty-minute walk — you've guaranteed a bad back day. But if you can walk twenty minutes pain-free, walk twenty minutes three times a day. Now you've got a full hour of pain-free walking. It's about dosing the exposure throughout the day rather than one long bout.

**Stuart McGill:** For the person with discogenic back pain, any single position held for too long becomes painful. The key is to keep changing posture. A sit-stand desk is a good idea, and the magic is in the timing: sit for twenty minutes, stand for thirty, walk for ten — then repeat that cycle. The disc has no blood supply; it depends on the pumping action of movement to stay hydrated and receive nutrients. Walking and postural variety drive that pumping mechanism. Something like a prolonged airplane flight without lumbar support and without periodic movement is one of the most stressful things you can do to a discogenic spine.

## [01:55:33] Deadlift & Bone Density, Glute-Ham Raise

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on the deadlift and squat for back health across different ages and body types?

**Stuart McGill:** At the highest level, every exercise is a tool to reach a specific goal. The deadlift is an extraordinary exercise — I don't know of anyone more involved with world-class deadlifters than I have been through the back pain relationship. On one hand I love the deadlift; on the other, I hate the deadlift. If you take consulting clients under thirty years of age, I will say half of them will report that it started with a deadlift — that is approaching epidemic proportions. And yet I will still tell you I love the deadlift.

**Stuart McGill:** The deadlift is a tremendously neurologically dense exercise. Every single muscle of the body should be involved — there are no pure agonists or antagonists; the full fascial complex is tightened to take the slack out before the bar leaves the ground. Strength starts as a thought. You densify that thought, then densify the pulse train down the nerves, teaching them to carry that amount of electrical current, and teach the muscles to utilize it. A great deadlift is not a muscle grinding — it is a cascade of neurological events. It teaches the nervous system to organize the entire body into a single stiff unit at precisely the right moment, which has massive carryover to almost every athletic movement.

**Stuart McGill:** The glute-ham raise is particularly valuable for people who have lost the ability to feel their hamstrings as distinct from their lumbar erectors — very common in chronic back pain. It isolates the hamstrings and trains the posterior chain as a relay: force originating at the glute, transmitted through the hamstring, absorbed at the knee — rather than defaulting to lumbar extension as the primary motor pattern.

## [02:06:20] Training & Age, Osteoporosis, Tool: Deadlift Alternatives

**Andrew Huberman:** As we age, how does the approach to back training need to change? And what about people with osteoporosis?

**Stuart McGill:** There was interesting pushback after my deadlift statements — older men saying that when they stop deadlifting, their back pain actually increases. I get it — for them, the deadlift is the right tool. But those people tend to be somewhat unidimensional in their athleticism. Ask them to throw a football or swing a golf club and they won't do well, because those movements require a rotational snap that a grinding deadlift does not develop.

**Stuart McGill:** For osteoporosis, the loading principle is actually very important — bone responds to mechanical load by depositing more mineral. So the prescription is not to avoid loading; it is to load safely given the current bone density. The trap bar deadlift, where the load is more centered on the body's axis, can provide the osteogenic stimulus with a lower risk profile for someone with fragile bones. For those who cannot deadlift due to disc damage, severe osteoporosis, or other contraindications, alternatives include the suitcase carry, farmer's carry, single-leg deadlift with light load, and the supine bridge with loaded hip thrust. Each can deliver significant posterior chain training and spinal loading without the specific risk profile of the conventional deadlift.

## [02:16:47] Tools: Biblical Training Week; Spine Stability & McGill's Big 3; Shrinking & Age

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell me about the Biblical Training Week.

**Stuart McGill:** The name came from the idea that every major religion has a Sabbath — a day off where the cumulative work of the week is allowed to adapt and settle. In its basic form: six days to train, one day of complete rest. Two days I strength train. Two days I work on mobility — the things that have become sticky as I've gotten older. Two days I work on cardiovascular fitness. I never do the same thing two days in a row — always alternating, allowing recovery and adaptation.

**Stuart McGill:** One critical piece: don't do heavy spinal loading first thing in the morning. When you wake up, the disc has been unloaded during sleep and is fully hydrated — paradoxically making it more vulnerable. The collagen fibers are stretched and the disc is under more internal pressure in the morning. Wait 30 to 60 minutes after waking before any loaded spinal exercises. This is one of the most important and most ignored pieces of spine hygiene advice I give. And as we age, the disc loses height — you will literally shrink over the decades, more rapidly if you load the spine heavily and repeatedly without adequate recovery. The Biblical Training Week's built-in recovery days are partly a counter to that shrinkage process.

## [02:24:16] Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP); Disc Damage

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there any evidence for compounds or injectables — like PRP — that can restore tensile strength and hydration to discs?

**Stuart McGill:** I haven't seen any evidence of PRP restoring disc height or tensile strength. I don't condemn PRP across the body — I swear by it for stubborn muscle tears, and for ball-and-socket articular joints it can make a measurable difference. But not injected into the disc.

**Stuart McGill:** The key context: if a young person who has never traumatized their disc trains mobility like yoga or ballet, they can probably increase range of motion. If they strength train, they can toughen the collagen in the end plate and build bone. They can do both — increase constituent strength and create mobility. But once you have an injury to the disc and lose disc height, the world changes. Most people with disc damage can be managed toward one goal — mobility or loading strength — but not both simultaneously. Trying to pursue both often further compromises disc height, and we tell them: we can manage you to achieve your goal, but you're going to have to back off the deadlifts and heavy compressive loads.

## [02:27:56] Tools: Biblical Training Week & Strength Exercises, Neck Strength

**Andrew Huberman:** Walk me through what a strength day actually looks like for you.

**Stuart McGill:** I start with the Big 3. Bird dogs first — I work on form and then add some dynamism. I don't lift the leg so much as push the heel away, which locks the core and really engages the glutes and hamstrings. Then I draw small squares with the hand and foot toward the midline and back up, creating a little dissociation through the ball-and-socket joints while keeping the core quiet. We did an experiment with the Pensacola Fire Department: half the firefighters trained with a coach who explained the principles behind each exercise form; the other half had cheerleader-style trainers encouraging more reps. Both groups got fit. Then we measured them on actual fireground tasks — advancing a loaded fire hose, chopping a hole in a burning roof, putting up a fire ladder. The group trained with principled coaching had dramatically fewer injury markers, including less valgus knee collapse, which strongly predicts ACL injury risk.

**Stuart McGill:** Neck strength is often overlooked but important. The neck is an extension of the spine, and weakness in the deep cervical flexors and suboccipitals contributes not just to neck pain but to headaches, upper back pain, and reduced shoulder control. I include specific neck exercises — not just surface muscles but deep stabilizers — in every strength day.

## [02:35:24] Tools: Sword Play, Distal Limb Loading, Training for Symmetry

**Stuart McGill:** One of my favorite tools is what I call sword play. I take an iron bar about this long and put a two-pound weight on the end. Then I do figure eights with it — over here, around there. You wouldn't believe the athletic gains. NHL hockey players tell me it transformed their wrist shot and slap shot. Tennis players say they've never had such power and finesse off the racket. Sword play trains the distal chain — wrist, forearm, shoulder — to create and transmit force rapidly, while requiring the core to absorb and redirect that force in all planes of motion. It is among the most neurologically rich exercises you can do.

**Andrew Huberman:** I came across an old book called Heavy Hands from the 1970s — the entire concept was carrying small dumbbells during walking and other exercise, getting the weights out away from the body. Is there something real to distal limb loading?

**Stuart McGill:** Absolutely. We know from neuroimaging that innervation of the distal muscles and fine control of the digits — fingers, toes — are reliable markers of upper motor neuron pathway integrity. Calf size and atrophy are also markers. Loading the most distal portions of the body to maintain that neuromuscular connectivity is going to return to the modern sphere of fitness and longevity — and there is real neurological basis for it. The training for symmetry principle also applies: years of one-sided sports — throwing, kicking, racket sports — create accumulated structural and neuromuscular asymmetries that eventually become pain generators. Sword play and bilateral distal loading are among the best tools to address that.

## [02:42:38] Tools: Biblical Training Week, Mobility & Cardiovascular Exercises, Athletic Panel

**Andrew Huberman:** What do the mobility days look like in the Biblical Training Week?

**Stuart McGill:** Given my history and my injuries, I do thoracic spine extension, a little neck work, and hip mobility within my capacity. Once I've done the targeted mobility work, I go through the motion of every joint without load. Then I do footwork — with my background I'll do some shadow boxing and Southpaw footwork patterns. This keeps the nervous system engaged with quick, coordinated movement even on non-strength days.

**Stuart McGill:** For cardiovascular days: in winter I cross-country ski, or if I can't get outside I ride a stationary bike in the clinic. In summer I ride outside, kayak, swim, canoe, or walk hard in the hills with my dog Tico. I've also had my genetic athletic panel done — they gave me a panel of ten athletic attributes, and I would have gotten every single one right if asked to self-assess. I tested ultra for grip strength — I always knew that. And ultra for the ability to generate power in short bursts. Knowing your genetic athletic profile allows you to invest training time in the domains that are both important to you and achievable given your architecture.
