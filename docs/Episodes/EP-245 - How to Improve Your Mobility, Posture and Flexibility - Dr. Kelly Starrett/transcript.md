## [00:05:46] Movement; Tool: Daily Floor Sitting

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. Kelly Starrett, welcome. You've pioneered so many areas of health and fitness. Let's start with the big M — movement. When you look at how most people sit, walk, and do their exercise, what are the most common problems you see? Is it imbalance, asymmetry, bodies trained into dysfunction?

**Kelly Starrett:** Let me borrow a couple of analogies. The first is this notion of mechanotransduction — at a cellular level, your tissues need mechanical input to express themselves. If you want a strong tendon, you have to load it. Katie Bowman points out that if you put an orca into captivity, its fin will start to fold — that's because you've altered the environment and removed the loading. The same principle applies to us: what is it that we need in our daily lives to maintain the integrity of our tissue systems?

**Kelly Starrett:** If we have a movement language made up of words, most of us are only using a few words: I sit, I stand, I walk slowly, I sit again. We go to the gym and use those same few words on an elliptical that never asks for hip extension. Our bodies are adaptation machines — they begin to adapt to those limited positions, and the brain may start to perceive certain ranges as unsafe, minimizing the movement options it offers. One of the first things I recommend is sitting on the ground for 20 to 30 minutes in the evening — cross-legged, squatting, long-sitting, side-sitting — anything that gets you down on the floor and back up.

**Kelly Starrett:** Research shows that cultures where people toilet on the ground, sleep on the ground, and sit on the ground have near-zero fall risk in elderly populations and lower rates of hip and lower back osteoarthritis. At a basic level, you're using and touching shapes that your body has essentially abandoned, and your tissues will start to say: let's keep that range around, let's normalize what the hip should be able to do. Muscles and tissues are like obedient dogs — at no age do you stop adapting, at no age do you stop healing.

**Andrew Huberman:** Would you extend that to lying on a hardwood floor with a low-pile rug and doing, say, cobra or updog type positions while watching TV in the evening?

**Kelly Starrett:** Absolutely. Any kind of ground-based movement — squatting, stretching, rolling around — all of that counts. You start to accumulate exposure to the things you're trying to change or restore, and that's the first-order intervention in most movement problems.

## [00:50:35] Tool: Foam Rolling, Uses, Types & Technique

**Andrew Huberman:** You've become synonymous with foam rolling. What's the deal — is there utility to it, is there a wrong way to do it?

**Kelly Starrett:** There's no wrong way, but there are ways that are not a great use of your time. If I'm using soft tissue mobilization with a roller or ball, I need to ask: what's my goal? The research is very clear that it can help with pain and restore range of motion. Brent Brookbush at the Brookbush Institute has incredible research summaries on this. If you're in pain before exercise, a quick two to three minute desensitization of the tissues creates a window of opportunity to move — that's a really excellent use of soft tissue work. No physical therapist required, nobody went blind, you didn't dislocate.

**Kelly Starrett:** You can also use rolling to restore range of motion, and to decrease DOMS — delayed onset muscle soreness. Whether it's blood flow, non-threatening neural input, massage, or parasympathetic downregulation, the bottom line is: does it help? Yes. Are all techniques on the roller the same? No. If you're rolling up and down your calf without variation, you're not doing much. Here's the key: find a place on the tissue that's uncomfortable, stiff, or asymmetrical, and stop there. Take a big four-second inhale — you need to be able to breathe in the position, because if you can't breathe in a position you don't own that position. Then contract the muscle under load for four seconds, then slowly relax and soften on a long exhale.

**Kelly Starrett:** Repeat that cycle two or three times. Your brain desensitizes, changes in range of motion follow, and the brain says "that's not a problem anymore." This is just moving slowly through range under load — what we'd call tempo work. As for foam roller size: I'm a bigger fan of smaller diameter rollers, which fit your body better. Those big white pool-noodle sized rollers are great for some things but sometimes you need the precision of an elbow, a forearm, or a thumb. A lacrosse ball, a softball, a harder smaller roller — all have their place. Sometimes you need a specific tool for a specific tissue.

**Andrew Huberman:** When I use a roller it feels localized and uncomfortable — is that normal?

**Kelly Starrett:** If you push on a tissue, we expect it to be painless or at most mildly uncomfortable to compression. What you're feeling is sensitized tissue — your brain is interpreting stiffness as a threat and reading it as pain. Quality tissue should feel like layers of warm silk sliding over steel springs. If it doesn't, you've found a place to work. The technique I described — breathe, contract, relax — is how you retrain the nervous system's relationship to that tissue. The goal is to find your minimum: can you compress that tissue and have it feel neutral? If yes, you're done there. Move on.

## [01:01:30] Injury vs. Incident, Pain

**Kelly Starrett:** What I'm really interested in is inputs and outputs — what did you do to make yourself feel better, and did you just hope it would go away? Let me define a couple of things. An injury has a clear mechanism of mechanical trauma: bone sticking out, a snap and a pop, night sweats, fever, vomiting, dizziness, unaccounted-for weight loss or gain, changes in bladder or bowel function. Those are red flags — that's an emergency medical problem.

**Kelly Starrett:** Everything else I want to call an incident — pain, loss of range of motion, numbness, tingling. I came across this framing in Charles Perrow's book *Normal Accidents*, which looks at complex systems: a "normal accident" is just an expression of the system given enough time. Your stiff shoulder isn't a problem until you fall on ice and suddenly can't handle the load overhead and you tear your rotator cuff. That's not a black swan event — it's a normal expression of that shoulder system given enough time. So an incident is a signal: your body is asking for change. Pain is a request for change.

**Kelly Starrett:** When I survey 100 high school athletes and ask how many are pain-free, two hands go up. Pain is very much part of the athletic condition, the human experience. The question is: are you using fitness and training as scaffolding to understand nutrition, hydration, soft tissue work, desensitization, and reperfusion of the tissues? Are you empowering yourself to say what's going on in my body and why? And when you run out of ideas, get some help.

## [01:05:54] Managing Pain & Stiffness, Tool: D2R2 Method

**Andrew Huberman:** Can we use the roller as a diagnostic tool? If I lie down on it and something really hurts, does that mean something's wrong?

**Kelly Starrett:** It means that for whatever reason those tissues have become sensitized and your brain is interpreting that stiffness as a threat and reading it as pain. Some people don't have that — they just don't feel pain even when they have the same underlying stiffness — but that's not normal tissue either. Quality tissue should feel like layers of silk over steel springs. Your training and loading can make tissues stiff, high-fiber, high-density, which creates problems because your training shouldn't change your range of motion adversely.

**Kelly Starrett:** I came up with something called the D2R2 model. The first D is Desensitize: if something hurts, desensitize it. You can do that with scraping, isometrics, rolling, blood flow restriction — many techniques. Pain prevents you from generating the same force or wattage, and your brain will start truncating movement solutions if you're in pain. The second D is Decongest: tissues that are swollen become more sensitized, and congested tissues don't heal as fast. The lymph system is the sewage system of the body, and muscle contraction drives lymphatic drainage. Decongested tissues typically express less pain.

**Kelly Starrett:** The first R is Reperfuse: get blood flow into the tissue. "Once I warm up I feel great" — that's the power of blood flow. Sometimes I'll spend a few minutes getting a huge quad pump on the leg extension machine, then go squat heavy. The second R is Restore: do you have full, normal range of motion in that joint? That's the last thing we address, because you may still be able to perform your sport at a high level while not having full range of motion, but that restricted range may be limiting your movement choices and potentially overloading tissues in compensatory ways.

**Kelly Starrett:** A great framework comes from Serge Gracovetsky's book *The Spinal Engine* — the trunk is a driver of power, not just a chassis for the bigger engine. That means there are positions where I'm highly effective and positions where I'm less effective — if my spine can't flex, extend, rotate, and get into complex shapes, that's a red flag. How are you training that thing beyond just your six-pack?

**Kelly Starrett:** Dave Durante, an Olympic gymnast from Stanford, has a free ab workout that shows how many fun and effective ways there are to play with trunk work. Adding rotation — bringing your right foot to your left hand during hanging pike variations — suddenly reveals deficiencies. I love Stu McMillan's concept of "spinal engine work": PVC pipe side bending, playing with different shapes, David Weck ropes, medicine ball throws. You need to train the functional trunk beyond straight curl-ups because at the end of the day you can't surf with power, run with power, or punch with power from a six-pack built exclusively from cross crunches.

## [01:43:51] Dynamic & Novel Movements; Endurance & Strength Propensities

**Kelly Starrett:** Westside Barbell has been doing movement variability forever — squatting with this bar, then that bar, changing height, changing stance. The squat pattern is the thing we're training, but how do we add another twist to the pretzel? That's sophistication. In many sports we're shifting toward "all-terrain vehicle" thinking — you can't afford to just be good at one thing. The more dynamic range people express, the more evolution you see of any sport.

**Andrew Huberman:** You're more of an endurance athlete by propensity, which I think is worth mentioning.

**Kelly Starrett:** I'm not very strong. I've been training for 30 years and I still can only deadlift what I deadlift — that's embarrassing. If you put a 100-pound backpack on me and we went backpacking, I'd be fine for days. My primary sport is trying to keep up with my wife on the mountain bike. I'm a disciple of Joel Jamieson and I spend huge proportions of my training in aerobic zones. There are just mutants out there — people with enormous aerobic engines or extraordinary strength genetics — and we've told a little lie on the internet that if you eat this way and do these things you'll be elite. You'll have a training effect, for sure, but it's not the same as being a mutant.

**Kelly Starrett:** The important thing is to figure out what you like to do and what your natural leanings are. The most important thing is that everyone needs to do real heavy weightlifting — not pink dumbbells — but the question is how much do you need to do to be better at your sport or to minimize bone density loss. We need to divide this into "aesthetics and staying intact" versus "I want to go to the Olympics" because those are very different conversations.

## [01:50:29] Tool: Workout Intensity; Consistency & Workout Longevity

**Andrew Huberman:** A coach named Kenny Kane taught me something I've found immensely useful. He said: 80% of your workouts will be at 80% of what you could do that day, 10% at 90% intensity, 5% at 95%, and 5% across the year are maximum 100% do-or-die workouts.

**Kelly Starrett:** Let's start with a simple idea: be consistent before you be heroic. If your intensity causes you to not be able to show up to the gym for three days, the recovery response to that isn't great. I'd much rather you be more consistent and not blow yourself out. There was a phase where people said you shouldn't be sore when you leave the gym — leave some reps in reserve, show up the next day, grease the groove. That's really good advice, especially since most people are not 20.

**Kelly Starrett:** What we know now from all this data is that we can make better progress without burning it to the ground every session. The best practices of elite athletes show a lot of 70–80% heart rate — recovery in Joel Jamieson's language — 80–90% conditioning, 90%+ overload. There are some days where touching 78 or 80% is hard because I'm sleep-deprived, stressed, traveling. As my early coach Mike Bergner said: "When the frying pan's hot, let's cook." I need to know myself and know my athletes — if someone looks great today, let's go chase something. But the frying pan is not always hot, and if you're jacked up on stimulants you can't hear inputs and outputs at all.

## [01:57:41] Hip Extension, Tools: Couch Stretch, Bosch Snatch

**Andrew Huberman:** I love the assault bike for high-physiology low-skill training. Let's talk about hip extension. When I squat and stand up, I'm extending the hip, but I think what you mean by hip extension is a different thing?

**Kelly Starrett:** The squat and lunge are cousins — long lever versus short lever, different torso position, different way of holding the weight. When I'm squatting down and standing up, people are like "I'm working on extension." Sure. But now continue that extension conversation and bring that knee behind your hip into a lunge. That's hip extension. The one thing I'm seeing erode across almost all populations I work with is this fundamental expression of power. The only ones I don't see it in are elite Olympic sprinters.

**Kelly Starrett:** The couch stretch is the key test and tool. Kneel on the ground facing a wall, then turn away from it. Place one knee in the corner where the floor meets the wall, foot going straight up the wall toward your butt. That's position one. A lot of people will struggle to even squeeze their glute in that position — it's positionally inhibited. Your knee is barely behind your torso, and already many people can't breathe or recruit their glutes there. That's how significant the hip extension deficit is.

**Kelly Starrett:** Position two: come up into high kneeling — rear knee in the corner, rear foot up the wall, front leg at a right angle in front. Position three: more upright torso, increasing the hip demand. The real test is the front foot elevated on a 12 to 18 inch box — a hip lock position — where the front leg's knee is pulling the pelvis into posterior tilt and you're fully testing hip extension range. This mimics the sprint position with the rear leg extended behind.

**Kelly Starrett:** How do you load hip extension in training? Rear-foot elevated split squats (Bulgarian split squats), flipping a tire where you buck your hips forward at the top, walking lunges, any movement where the trailing leg is in full extension in a long lever position. France Bosch has excellent material on this — his "Bosch snatch" technique takes a hip hinge with a plate or dumbbell, and as you drive the weight overhead you step your front foot up onto a box, going from a C-shape hip hinge to full upright extension with that trailing leg behind. It adds speed to the extension demand. You can do the same thing with an overhead press: front foot on a box, back foot flat, press from that position. It'll immediately expose your hip extension deficit — you won't even be thinking about the weight.

## [02:09:38] Fundamental Shapes & Training, Hip Extension, Movement Culture

**Kelly Starrett:** Let me give you a simple framework for exercise selection. The shoulder does a limited number of things: goes overhead, out to the side, forward, backward. The hip has flexion, extension, and different squat widths. In those fundamental bookend shapes — what I call archetypes — you can ask: how are you loading each? If you only ever press with a barbell or do lat pulldowns, you're technically overhead, but you're not in the fullest expression of overhead — arms straight up, parallel by ears, hands over the top of your head.

**Kelly Starrett:** You can also look at distraction versus compression in these movements. Are you pressing (compressing the joint) or pulling (distracting the joint)? Are you starting from a snatch position or a front rack position? What are the start and finish positions? These are simple ways of understanding root movements and positions that help improve performance, predict future performance, and help get through pain.

**Kelly Starrett:** The resistance training world has moved beyond "get bigger and stronger" toward functional movement. The reaction against purely bodybuilding-based training gave rise to movement culture — portal, Ido Portal, etc. — which showed that the gym doesn't necessarily beget better movers. If we took sport-specific movement skills out of training and just stayed in the gym, some people lost elegance, grace, rhythm, and movement solutions. The answer isn't to abandon the gym; it's to put play back in, explore new shapes, vary the tools — kettlebells, dumbbells, bands, ropes, medicine balls — and make sure you're training the full expression of the movement, not just a narrow slice of it.

## [02:21:06] Training for Life & Fun

**Andrew Huberman:** I use resistance training to run better, faster, farther, and without pain. That's my purpose for it.

**Kelly Starrett:** That framework gives you a third-party objective measure: does my running get better with my training? It's a really great way to evaluate your training. And it keeps you from the gravitational pull of just adding more weight to the hack squat. Apply a longevity and durability lens — as Juliet says: don't you want to just be able to pop off the couch and go on adventures?

**Kelly Starrett:** My weekly structure: one long rucking or running piece, one shorter run, one sprint session, leg day, torso day (neck, abs, back), and distal muscles (calves, biceps, triceps, forearms, grip) on Saturday. That combination meets the demands of life — I can sprint for an airplane with luggage, go backpacking, go to the gym and move respectable weight, hang from a bar, and feel good. The moment I've gone to the extreme in any one kind of training, I've ended up injured or sick.

**Kelly Starrett:** Notice what Andrew said: I train so I can have fun. We've sucked the joy and play out of fitness and replaced it with metrics. The whole point is so you can go spend some credits. Nicole Christenson says "we don't nature for time — stop naturing for time." We train so we can surf all day, ski, hike, ride bikes, play with our kids, do the work we care about. When we're we're looking at societal health, the first questions should be: what do you want to do, and who are your friends that will do it with you? Let's start there. Sport is the last place where people genuinely congregate — everyone knows what a push-up is, everyone knows what a deadlift is, it's the universal language.

## [02:30:20] Aging with Range of Motion & Control; Mental State & Training

**Andrew Huberman:** When I see elderly people moving extremely well on Instagram — 80 to 100 years old — it tends to be gymnastics-type movement or sprinting, rarely just heavy gym work. Is that what got them there or is it an expression of their genetics?

**Kelly Starrett:** What's noticeable is that those disciplines require greater range of motion, skill in body control, and high power output. In our programming for adults I make people sprint once a week — if they don't have the tissue tolerance or range of motion for track sprints, they do it on a bike, finding peak wattage. What I really need people to maintain is their ability to move quickly with control through great ranges of motion. That's a recipe for staying vital. Yoga plus sprints — that's pretty badass. People who just do the elliptical with light dumbbells are doing a lot of busy work: they feel involved in a program but they can't progress those pink dumbbells forever. Show me progression.

**Kelly Starrett:** As for gym environment and mental state: I find that my best thinking happens under enormous aerobic load. If I'm distracted by social media during a workout I can't hear what's going on and that distraction carries through into the rest of the day. But there are times when I want distraction — getting ready for a four-day backcountry ski trip, I have two- and three-hour sessions where I need headphones. It's okay to be amused. Use training as a concentration time, as an interaction time. The gym shouldn't be the loneliest place in the world — if you're not making eye contact, talking, high-fiving, get a different gym.

## [02:35:38] Fascia, Myofascial Mobilization

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd be remiss if I didn't ask about fascia. You and Jill Miller were some of the first people I heard talk about it in an elaborate way. To what extent does tight fascia restrict movement, and what can myofascial mobilization do for us?

**Kelly Starrett:** If you pull fascia out of the human movement equation, the human fails to stop moving — so we've always known it was important. Gil Hedley did free YouTube dissections really describing fascia as this incredible connective tissue network that envelops, wraps, stores energy, and communicates. Ida Rolf was one of the first people to talk about mobilizing fascia with touch — Rolfing was her modality.

**Kelly Starrett:** When I'm trying to help someone think about pain or restore position, I ask these layered questions in order: Is this an environmental problem — poorly hydrated, inflamed, poor sleep? Is this a movement problem — do they just need cueing and better technique? Is this a joint capsule problem — joint capsule stiffness can account for huge chunks of range of motion limitation? Is this a muscle dynamics problem — high tone, stress, fear, trigger points, fibrosis? And lastly: are the sliding surfaces sliding? Do things that should slide actually slide?

**Kelly Starrett:** Grab the skin on your forehead and slide it in all directions — it should move freely over the underlying structures. Now grab the skin over your Achilles. In many people it doesn't budge — it's like they have an exoskeleton. That fascial compartment has seized and adhered to underlying surfaces, creating tissue restriction and higher tension. When you're mobilizing tissues you're trying to keep those sliding surfaces sliding. Nerves run through nerve tunnels; taking huge breaths keeps all those trunk structures moving. If you went to ART and it didn't solve the problem, it may not have been a fascial problem. If you went to a chiropractor and it didn't solve it, it may not have been a joint restriction. What we want to do is recognize that if rolling solved all the problems we would have solved all the problems by now — so put fascia equally alongside joint, muscle, and movement factors as an important part of the system.

## [02:41:17] Rolfing, Tool: Tissue Mobilization & Reducing Discomfort

**Kelly Starrett:** Let me pull Rolfing to the side and just say: mobilizing your tissues does not have to be painful. You will experience discomfort, but here are two guideposts. Number one: you always have to be able to take a full breath. If I'm mobilizing you and you stop breathing, you're going too deep. Number two: I like to have volitional contraction — you should be able to flex out of the mobilization, you should have control over that tissue. If the pain or depth is so much that you literally lose normal muscular control, we've gone too far.

**Kelly Starrett:** We tend not to work on a single tissue longer than five minutes. We prefer to do soft tissue work before bed. We found better adherence because no one's doing anything productive in the 10 minutes before they go to bed, and like a child's bedtime routine, your brain starts to associate the sequence: rolling, then sleep. People who've engaged in self-massage tend to be very relaxed — as Jill Miller says, you're "switching on the off switch." Start anywhere on the leg that's stiff or asking for attention. Five minutes per body part. As long as you can breathe and contract, you're doing it right.

## [02:45:14] Deliberate Heat & Cold, Training, Injury & Healing

**Andrew Huberman:** You were one of the first people to tell me that for injuries, it's often better to perfuse the tissue rather than ice it, and that heat can be more favorable. What's your overall view of deliberate heat and cold?

**Kelly Starrett:** Cold water immersion can attenuate training adaptations if done in the six to eight hours after hypertrophy and strength training, because of its potent anti-inflammatory properties — it prevents some of the inflammation that would prompt the adaptation response. If your goal is bigger muscles and getting stronger, don't do immersion-based cold exposure in the six to eight hours after training. It's fine to do it before training, fine on other days, fine not to do it at all. For someone who just wants to be a decent recreational athlete, the timing of the plunge relative to training isn't going to matter much. Put it as far from training as you can if there is a performance concern.

**Kelly Starrett:** For injuries specifically: your body heals at the rate of a human being, or it heals slower — because we do dumb things that rate-limit healing. Sleep and nutrition are the biggest factors. Icing suppresses prostaglandin release, which cuts off some of the chemical signals that call circulating stem cells to the injured tissue. An old study showed Australian military athletes given ibuprofen after bad ankle sprains returned to activity faster than those who didn't, but they had chronic ankle instability because they didn't have a sufficient healing response. Your body will warm back up eventually, but if you think you're going to drive angiogenesis and modulate healing by applying a non-specific ice pad for a non-specific time, you're working against yourself.

**Kelly Starrett:** Ice can work for pain control — once you're numb you can't feel anything. But the question is what happens when it wears off. We now have much better tools to manage congestion: non-fatiguing muscle contraction, NMES devices like the H-Wave, controlled movement to decongest and keep the lymphatics moving. These produce better clinical outcomes. For general sauna use: I do sauna whenever I can, it's a great way to chill out, and hot-cold contrast is something I try to do once a week. Sauna is also incredible for social bonding — some of my best friendships have been forged in the sauna.

## [02:54:35] Desire to Train, Physical Practice

**Kelly Starrett:** One thing I love about heat and cold is that they inform my readiness state. My CO2 tolerance, my cold tolerance, and my heat tolerance all drop dramatically when I'm stressed and under-recovered. If I've been in the cold plunge for 30 seconds and I'm already shivering, that's another data point: maybe this is a 70% day in the gym.

**Kelly Starrett:** Juliet and I believe in this thing called "desire to train." We wake up every day thinking about when we're going to exercise, what we're going to do. On some days that desire just isn't there, and it's remarkable how consistently that correlates with poor heat tolerance, poor CO2 tolerance, and poor cold tolerance. It's a nice third-party objective measure — especially once you get good at listening to it.

**Andrew Huberman:** Assessing your forward center of mass for effort — are you back on your heels, flat-footed, or forward — is a useful daily check-in. Great to push hard sometimes, great to back off at others, but just exploring the full emotional and physical range of motion is the goal.

**Kelly Starrett:** Ultimately all of this is supposed to be additive. The sauna is social glue — my male friends and I get together once a week, and because it's so hot we're all super vulnerable and we talk the truth, share stories, talk about our lives. My wife and I feel more connected after a sauna together. I don't need a device to tell me when I need more fruits and vegetables and sleep and when I've had enough cookies. The brain exists to be around other brains and to do rad things in the world together — and once you throw that filter on everything, it becomes a lot more sustainable.

## [02:58:54] Balanced Nutrition; Eating Behaviors & Social Media

**Andrew Huberman:** You seem very balanced about nutrition. Most people feel overwhelmed by the nutrition conversation now. Why do you think it's gotten so contentious, and what does balanced nutrition look like to you?

**Kelly Starrett:** My day job is high performance, which means I have to talk about body composition and fueling — do you have enough carbs on board, are you eating to recover, to minimize session cost? But in my personal life I think the functional unit of change is the household. Sitting down with your kids two or three times a week is powerful. Cooking is beautiful. I found that putting food on a table with a tablecloth increased calorie intake — that's a useful tool when athletes are under-fueling.

**Kelly Starrett:** For my own nutrition: I aim for 0.8 to 1 gram of protein per pound of body weight and prioritize protein at every meal across a variety of protein sources. I tend toward leaner proteins because my genetics don't support very fatty ones — keto gives me issues. Then I try to hit 800 grams of fruits and vegetables per day, based on Erin Hanson's work at Optimize.me Nutrition — this is the "800 gram challenge." When I started doing that I was stuffing myself with fruits and vegetables, getting enough protein, and there was simply no room for junk food. The 800 gram target is agnostic about dietary identity — works for vegans, vegetarians, carnivores. A banana is 80 to 100 grams, a big salad might be 200 to 300 grams, so you're actually working hard to hit the 800 gram target.

**Kelly Starrett:** Social media has become everyone's personal reality TV show, and the gravitational pull toward certain body types or behaviors is real. When someone asks if they "won" by getting super shredded with dysfunctional eating patterns, I ask: did you win? You've got another 60 to 70 years on this planet — how does that work? We don't really diet; we manipulate macros to support performance. When athletes come to me saying "I think I should lose four pounds this week," I'm like: let's talk about body composition after the season, not mid-competition. Getting people to understand that food can enrich relationships, that cooking is fun, that serving others is fun — that creates a fundamentally different relationship with fueling.

## [03:10:23] Sustainable Nutrition & Training; Tool: 3 Vegetable Rule

**Kelly Starrett:** The nutrition piece is especially dangerous for young athletes. Juliet and I are very focused on youth sports right now and what we call relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S) — where kids aren't eating enough to fuel both activity and growth simultaneously. Stacy Sims was one of the first people to put that on my radar. Elite women athletes often battle this — they have this tension between what their body needs to win world championships and what they think their Instagram audience wants to see.

**Kelly Starrett:** At home we have a three vegetable rule at dinner, from a woman we work with, Margaret Garvy: she cooks a protein plus three vegetables, and that's where dinner starts. With my pickiest daughter Caroline, who thinks anything brown is automatically not edible, suddenly she might eat one of the three vegetables and we've made progress. The idea is to crowd out less nutritious foods by starting with what's most important: protein plus three vegetables. What your teenagers eat when they leave the house is on them — you might as well stuff them with the good stuff at home while you can.

## [03:14:30] Supplements
