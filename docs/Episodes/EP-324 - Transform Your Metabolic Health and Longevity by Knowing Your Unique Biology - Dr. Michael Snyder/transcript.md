## Healthy Glucose Range, Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGM), Hemoglobin A1c
*(00:03:33)*

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd like to start by just getting oriented around what we should know about glucose and blood glucose management. Maybe let's start with what are the healthy ranges of blood glucose, and how do those relate to the blood tests that most of us know, like hemoglobin A1c?

**Michael Snyder:** So, we've been studying glucose now for a long time. My first major study was around 2012 to 2015, so we've been at this for over a decade. And when you look at glucose values, for a healthy person, the typical range you want is in the 70-to-140 mg/dL range. Below 70 is hypoglycemia, and above 140 is hyperglycemia. And you spend a lot of time, actually, in the normal range if you're a healthy person.

The hemoglobin A1c test just looks at the average glucose on your red blood cells over 90 days. So that gives you a kind of time-averaged view of your glucose. And if you look at the continuous glucose monitors, you can see the dynamics — how do they vary over the course of the day? When do you spike? How long do you stay high? And that information, the time-in-range information, I think is much more informative than just the hemoglobin A1c.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what is the hemoglobin A1c cutoff for being diagnosed as diabetic, pre-diabetic, or normal?

**Michael Snyder:** For diabetic, it's 6.5% and above. Pre-diabetic is 5.7 to 6.4%. And normal is below 5.7%. But the problem with the average is it doesn't tell you about the dynamics. You could have somebody who has an average that looks great, but they're spiking dramatically and then dropping dramatically, and that would average to normal. That's not healthy. So looking at the dynamics is, I think, very important.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the continuous glucose monitor, something like a Dexcom or a Libre — these devices allow you to track that.

**Michael Snyder:** Right. And time-in-range is the key metric. You want to spend as much time as possible between 70 and 140. But it's not just about whether you spike — it's about how high you go and how long you stay elevated.

## Individual Variability & Food Choice, Glucose Spikes & Sleepiness
*(00:09:02)*

**Andrew Huberman:** There's been a lot of work from your lab and others showing that people respond very differently to the same foods. Can you walk us through what was discovered about individual variability in glucose responses?

**Michael Snyder:** Sure. So this is one of the most important findings, I think, from our research. We call them glucotypes. Some people are low variability — they tend to not spike much with food. Some people are high variability, what we call spikers. And what was really surprising to us is that the same food can cause very different glucose responses in different people.

The classic example is rice versus bread. In some people, rice causes a huge spike. In others, bread causes a bigger spike. Tomatoes — you wouldn't expect tomatoes to be a bad food, but some people spike to tomatoes. So this individual variability is really important and tells us that a one-size-fits-all diet is probably not the right approach.

**Andrew Huberman:** And is there any way to predict ahead of time — without wearing a CGM — what kind of glucotype you are?

**Michael Snyder:** Unfortunately, not very well. The CGM is really the gold standard for figuring that out. Genetics plays some role, your microbiome plays a role. But the only way to really know is to measure. And that's one of the reasons I'm such a big advocate for people actually trying a continuous glucose monitor, even for a couple of weeks, just to understand your own response.

**Andrew Huberman:** When people eat and get a glucose spike, they often report feeling sleepy. Is there a connection there?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes, there's definitely a connection between post-meal glucose spikes and that feeling of sleepiness — the so-called post-prandial dip. When you eat and your glucose spikes, your body releases insulin to bring it back down. If it comes down quickly or even overshoots, you can end up in a low glucose state that drives that sleepiness. Sustained high glucose also correlates with fatigue. So managing your glucose spikes matters for energy as well.

## Glucose Spikes, Tools: Post-Meal Brisk Walk; Soleus "Push-Ups"; Exercise Snacks
*(00:15:16)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about what people can do to reduce glucose spikes after a meal.

**Michael Snyder:** So, the most powerful intervention, I think, is the post-meal walk. Even 15 to 20 minutes of a brisk walk after a meal can dramatically reduce the glucose spike. Walking uses your muscles, and your muscles are the main glucose sponge in your body. When your muscles are active, they take up glucose from the bloodstream without needing insulin, through GLUT4 transporters. So you're basically using your muscles to absorb that glucose before it can build up.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's also something called soleus push-ups that has gotten some attention in recent years.

**Michael Snyder:** Right. The soleus muscle, which is a calf muscle, is interesting because even though it's only about 1% of your body weight, it has a very high oxidative capacity. So when you activate it — even while seated, by doing what some call soleus push-ups or seated heel raises — it can draw a meaningful amount of glucose. The data on this are actually quite interesting. You can do this at your desk, at a restaurant, and it seems to have a measurable effect on post-meal glucose.

**Andrew Huberman:** Exercise snacks — just brief bouts of movement throughout the day — also have an effect?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. Things like doing 10 air squats before a meal, or even just standing up and moving around briefly. These small movement breaks throughout the day keep your muscles primed to take up glucose. You don't need to do a 45-minute workout. Short bouts are remarkably effective.

## Glucose Dysregulation, Diabetes & Sub-Phenotypes, Tool: Larger Morning Meal
*(00:21:06)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you talk about the sub-phenotypes or subtypes of diabetes? Because I think many people think of it as one disease, but your work and others have been showing it's more heterogeneous.

**Michael Snyder:** Right. So, when we looked at this carefully, using the kinds of deep data profiling we do, we found at least four or five subtypes. Muscle insulin resistance — your muscles become resistant to insulin, so they don't take up glucose well. Then there's beta cell defect — the beta cells in your pancreas that produce insulin aren't working well. Then you have incretin defects — the gut hormones that signal to your pancreas aren't working. And hepatic insulin resistance — your liver is overproducing glucose even when it shouldn't be.

These subtypes respond differently to different interventions. If you have muscle insulin resistance, resistance training and post-meal exercise is particularly important. If you have a beta cell defect, you may need medications that support insulin secretion. And so on.

**Andrew Huberman:** And one dietary thing that came up in your research was the timing of when you eat — specifically eating a larger meal in the morning.

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. There's a concept called chrono-nutrition — the timing of food affects your glucose response. One of the most consistent findings is that the same meal consumed in the morning causes a smaller glucose spike than the same meal consumed at night. So front-loading your calories — bigger breakfast, smaller dinner — is better for glucose management. People who eat their biggest meal at dinner tend to have worse glycemic control, even if the total calories are identical.

## Exercise Timing, Muscle Insulin Resistance
*(00:28:34)*

**Andrew Huberman:** How does exercise timing interact with glucose management?

**Michael Snyder:** This is an area that doesn't get enough attention. Exercise actually makes your muscles insulin-sensitive, but that effect is not permanent — it wanes over time. So, if you exercise in the morning and then sit at a desk all day, by the time you eat dinner you may have lost some of that benefit. Distributing exercise throughout the day, or at least doing some movement after meals, is probably better than one big exercise bout in the morning if your goal is glycemic control.

The post-meal walk of 15-20 minutes is actually, in some ways, more effective for glucose management than longer exercise sessions done far from meal times, because the timing matters enormously.

## Diabetes Subtyping, Weight, Glucose Control; Incretins
*(00:30:49)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the role of body weight in diabetes, and also incretins. What are incretins?

**Michael Snyder:** Incretins are hormones released from your gut when you eat. The main ones are GLP-1, glucagon-like peptide-1, and GIP — glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide. They signal to your pancreas to release insulin in anticipation of and during glucose absorption. Some people have a defect in this signaling — their incretins don't work well — and that's one of the subtypes of diabetes.

Weight is obviously a major factor. Excess body fat, especially visceral fat — the fat around your organs — causes insulin resistance. But it's not just the total weight. Where you carry it matters. And some people can have a normal BMI but have significant visceral fat, which we can now detect with MRI.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there are people with normal weight who have type 2 diabetes.

**Michael Snyder:** Exactly. So, diabetes is not just an obese person's disease. Thin people can have type 2 diabetes, especially the beta cell defect subtype. That's actually what happened with me — I'm relatively lean, but I developed type 2 diabetes after a viral infection because of a beta cell defect. So, not fitting the stereotypical profile doesn't mean you're safe.

## GLP-1 Agonists, Diabetes, Tool: Muscle Maintenance & Resistance Training
*(00:35:41)*

**Andrew Huberman:** GLP-1 agonists like semaglutide — Ozempic, Wegovy — and tirzepatide — Mounjaro — have become enormously popular. What's your take on them?

**Michael Snyder:** These are remarkable drugs. They work by mimicking or enhancing GLP-1 signaling, which suppresses appetite, slows gastric emptying, and increases insulin secretion. The weight loss effects are dramatic — we've never had drugs that work this well for obesity. And beyond weight loss, they have cardiovascular benefits and now we're seeing data suggesting cognitive benefits.

I'm on semaglutide myself because of my type 2 diabetes and the beta cell defect subtype. And it's been very helpful.

The big concern I have is muscle mass. When people lose weight rapidly, they tend to lose significant muscle mass along with fat. And that's a problem, especially as we age, because muscle mass is one of the most important predictors of longevity and health span. So, anyone on a GLP-1 agonist really needs to prioritize resistance training.

**Andrew Huberman:** What kind of resistance training protocol would you recommend?

**Michael Snyder:** At minimum, two to three times a week, compound movements that work large muscle groups — squats, deadlifts, rows, presses. And make sure you're getting adequate protein, because you need the building blocks to maintain muscle. The current evidence suggests around 1.6 to 2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day for people trying to maintain or build muscle.

## Metformin, Berberine, Headaches
*(00:38:40)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Metformin is one of the most commonly prescribed diabetes medications. And berberine has been called "nature's metformin." Can you speak to both?

**Michael Snyder:** Metformin works primarily by reducing glucose production in the liver. It's been around for decades and has a very good safety profile. There's also interest in it as a longevity drug — the TAME trial is looking at this. One thing people don't appreciate about metformin is that it can cause hypoglycemia, especially if you take it and then skip meals or exercise a lot.

Berberine is a plant compound that acts through similar mechanisms, primarily AMPK activation. The evidence suggests it does lower blood glucose, and some studies show it's comparable to metformin. I've seen it cause headaches in some people, which is worth knowing. Neither is as powerful as the GLP-1 agonists for weight management, but they have their place.

## GLP-1 Agonists, Cognition, Longevity, Tool: Habits Support Medication; Cycling
*(00:41:01)*

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned cognitive benefits from GLP-1 agonists. What's the evidence there?

**Michael Snyder:** There are epidemiological studies showing that people on GLP-1 agonists have lower rates of Alzheimer's and cognitive decline. The mechanism isn't fully understood — it could be through improved glucose metabolism in the brain, reduced inflammation, reduced weight. It's an active area of research.

There's also data on longevity more broadly. GLP-1 agonists reduce cardiovascular events significantly. They reduce fatty liver disease. They may have direct anti-inflammatory effects. So, the benefits seem to extend far beyond weight loss.

One thing I want to emphasize: these drugs are not a replacement for lifestyle. They work best when combined with good habits. If you use a GLP-1 agonist to lose weight and then re-adopt the same behaviors that caused the problem, you'll gain it back. The medication should be a bridge to better habits, or a complement to them.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there are people who cycle on and off these medications?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. Some people go on them, lose weight, improve their metabolic health, build better habits, and then taper off. That can work. The concern is that the underlying biology hasn't changed — your set point is still there — and many people regain weight when they stop. For someone like me with a structural defect in beta cells, I'll probably be on it long-term. But for others, cycling might be a strategy.

## Subcutaneous vs Visceral Fat, Organ Stress
*(00:47:41)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Can you explain the difference between subcutaneous and visceral fat and why visceral fat is so much more dangerous?

**Michael Snyder:** Subcutaneous fat is the fat under your skin — you can pinch it. It's metabolically less active and, in moderate amounts, it's not particularly harmful. It actually serves as a kind of buffer — it can store energy without dumping it directly on your organs.

Visceral fat is the fat packed around your abdominal organs — your liver, intestines, kidneys. This fat is metabolically very active. It secretes inflammatory cytokines, free fatty acids, and other molecules directly into the portal circulation, which feeds straight to your liver. This causes hepatic insulin resistance, inflammation, and contributes to a whole cascade of metabolic problems. The relationship between visceral fat and cardiometabolic risk is much stronger than the relationship with total body fat or BMI.

## Meal Timing & Sleep, Tools: Post-Dinner Walk, Routines, Bedtime Consistency
*(00:51:58)*

**Andrew Huberman:** What about the interaction between meal timing and sleep quality?

**Michael Snyder:** Eating close to bedtime tends to elevate glucose during sleep, which disrupts sleep quality. We see this clearly in the CGM data. When you eat a large meal within one to two hours of bedtime, your glucose rises during what should be the early part of your sleep, and sleep is much more disrupted. So, having a cutoff — not eating within two to three hours of bedtime — is really important.

**Andrew Huberman:** And post-dinner walking again is relevant here?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. A walk after dinner, even a 15-minute walk, blunts that post-meal glucose spike and helps you go to bed with lower, more stable glucose. It makes a real difference to sleep quality.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about bedtime consistency?

**Michael Snyder:** Consistency of bedtime is one of the strongest predictors of sleep quality. Going to bed and waking up at the same time — even on weekends — anchors your circadian rhythm. We see this in the wearable data very clearly. People with consistent sleep timing have much better HRV, better glucose control the next day, better cognitive function. The variability in sleep timing, what some call "social jet lag," is actually quite harmful metabolically.

## Microbiome, Immune System & Gut; Diet & Individual Variability
*(00:57:16)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about the microbiome. How does the gut microbiome influence glucose and metabolic health?

**Michael Snyder:** The microbiome is a huge contributor. From our studies, roughly 20 to 30 percent of the variability in glucose response between people can be explained by their microbiome composition. And we know roughly 20 percent or so is attributable to genetics. The rest is lifestyle — diet, exercise, sleep, stress.

The microbiome affects glucose through several mechanisms. It processes dietary fiber and produces short-chain fatty acids like butyrate, which improve insulin sensitivity. It influences how quickly food is absorbed. It modulates the immune system — and importantly, there's a strong gut-immune axis. About 70% of your immune cells are in or around your gut. Changes in your microbiome can cause systemic inflammation that impairs insulin signaling.

**Andrew Huberman:** And diet is the primary lever for modulating the microbiome?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. Diet has a major effect, and it works quickly. Within days of changing your diet, your microbiome shifts. A diet rich in diverse plant foods, fiber, fermented foods tends to support a healthy microbiome. Ultra-processed foods, high sugar intake, and low fiber tend to dysregulate it.

## Fiber Types, Cholesterol & Glucose, Polyphenols
*(01:02:52)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Not all fiber is the same. Can you break down the different types?

**Michael Snyder:** Right. The two main categories are soluble and insoluble fiber, but there are many subtypes within those. Two that we've studied carefully are arabinoxylan and inulin.

Arabinoxylan is found in Metamucil — psyllium husk — and in many vegetables and grains. It's a viscous, soluble fiber. It has a very impressive effect on LDL cholesterol — in our crossover study, it reduced cholesterol by about 25%. That's comparable to a low-dose statin. It didn't have a direct effect on glucose in our study, interestingly.

Inulin is found in chicory root, garlic, onions, peas, asparagus. It's a prebiotic fiber — it feeds specific beneficial gut bacteria. Again, in our study, it didn't directly reduce glucose, but it shifts the microbiome in ways that may be beneficial over time.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's individual variability in how people respond to these fibers as well.

**Michael Snyder:** Absolutely. Some people respond strongly to arabinoxylan for cholesterol reduction; others don't. It depends on your microbiome, your genetics, your baseline levels. The people with the highest baseline LDL tended to get the biggest reductions. So, again, personalization matters.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about polyphenols?

**Michael Snyder:** Polyphenols are plant compounds — found in berries, dark chocolate, olive oil, tea, coffee — that have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. They also act as prebiotics, feeding beneficial gut bacteria. The data on polyphenols and metabolic health are generally positive, though the mechanisms are still being worked out. They're generally very safe and the food sources are delicious, so this seems like a low-risk intervention.

## Food As Medicine; Fiber, Microbiome & Individual Variability; Probiotics
*(01:09:50)*

**Andrew Huberman:** What's your take on probiotics?

**Michael Snyder:** The evidence on probiotics is mixed. The general idea is sound — you're trying to add beneficial bacteria. But most probiotics don't successfully colonize. They pass through. The exceptions may be fermented foods — like yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut — which contain live bacteria along with the fermentation substrates they need, and there's some evidence these actually do make a difference.

The real insight from our microbiome research is that the microbiome is extraordinarily individual. Two people can eat the same probiotic, and one person's microbiome will shift and the other's won't. So, the future is probably personalized probiotics — formulated based on your specific microbiome profile.

**Andrew Huberman:** And food really is medicine in this context.

**Michael Snyder:** Absolutely. The old Hippocratic saying — let food be thy medicine — turns out to be deeply true. We see it in the cholesterol data with arabinoxylan. We see it in the inflammation data. The challenge is that it's personalized. What works for me won't necessarily work for you. That's the frontier.

## Profiling Healthy Individuals, Genomes, Wearables
*(01:20:35)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about the work you're doing profiling healthy individuals — the deep omics profiling work.

**Michael Snyder:** This is really the core of what we do. We recruit healthy individuals and we profile them deeply — genome sequencing, RNA-seq to see which genes they're expressing, proteomics, metabolomics, microbiome, wearables. We call this precision health. The idea is that by deeply profiling healthy people, we can find early signals of disease before symptoms appear, and we can understand the relationship between lifestyle factors and health outcomes.

From this work, we've found that a lot of healthy people actually have actionable findings. Things like high cholesterol that wasn't known, blood pressure issues, glucose issues. In one study of healthy Stanford employees, we found that a large fraction had findings that warranted medical follow-up. So, the more you look, the more you find — in a good way, because you can act on it.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the wearables give you the longitudinal data that snapshots can't?

**Michael Snyder:** Exactly. A single blood draw gives you one data point. Wearables give you thousands of data points over time. And patterns emerge from that data. For instance, we've found that drops in heart rate variability can precede illness by days to weeks. We've found that abnormal sleep patterns can be early signs of metabolic dysregulation. The continuous nature of the data is what makes it powerful.

## Whole-Body MRIs, Nodules, Healthy Baseline, Early Diagnosis
*(01:26:31)*

**Andrew Huberman:** You've been involved with whole-body MRI as a tool for health assessment. What are the pros and cons?

**Michael Snyder:** The pros are that you get a comprehensive look at your anatomy — you can detect tumors, aneurysms, organ abnormalities — things that would otherwise be invisible until symptoms appear. We have examples of cancers caught at early stages through this, where treatment is much more effective.

The challenge is what we call incidentalomas — you find things that look abnormal but turn out to be benign. These can cause anxiety and lead to follow-up procedures that have their own risks. The answer to this, I think, is establishing a baseline early. If you scan someone at 40 and find a liver lesion that's 5mm, and you scan them again at 42 and it's still 5mm, that's reassuring. If it's 15mm, that's concerning. Context and longitudinal tracking make the data actionable.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there are now commercial services offering this?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. Q Bio, which is a company I was involved with, offers whole-body MRI scans, and there are others. The cost has come down significantly. These are roughly 35 to 40 minute scans. For people in their 40s and older especially, I think this is a very valuable investment.

## Sensors, CGM, Sleep, Heart Rate Variability (HRV), Tools: Mindset Effects, Increase REM
*(01:34:07)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about wearables more specifically — which sensors are most useful?

**Michael Snyder:** The CGM is extremely valuable, especially for understanding your unique glucose response. For general health tracking, a smartwatch or ring that measures heart rate, HRV, sleep stages, activity — these are now quite good. The Oura ring and the WHOOP are popular and reasonably accurate.

HRV is one of the most information-rich metrics available from wearables. It reflects the state of your autonomic nervous system. Low HRV correlates with stress, illness, poor recovery. High HRV, generally, correlates with good fitness, good recovery, and lower disease risk.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's interesting research from Ali Crum's group at Stanford about mindset and sleep — that even your perception of how well you slept affects your biology.

**Michael Snyder:** Right. The idea that you can shift what your body does just by believing you slept well or poorly is remarkable. It's a good reminder that the data from a wearable isn't everything — how you feel and your subjective sense of restoration also matters. Don't let a bad reading on your wearable ruin your morning.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there are some practical tools for improving REM sleep specifically?

**Michael Snyder:** Matt Walker has pointed out that warming the sleeping environment in the final two hours of sleep can increase REM sleep. The idea is that as you warm up slightly, you're more likely to cycle into REM. Keeping the room cooler overall but then allowing some warming in the early morning may increase the REM-rich portion of sleep. It's a simple intervention worth trying.

## HRV, Sleep, Exercise, Tool: Long Exhales; Next-Day Excitement & Sleep
*(01:39:30)*

**Andrew Huberman:** What's the best way to improve HRV?

**Michael Snyder:** Exercise is the most powerful lever — especially aerobic exercise. Consistent exercise over weeks to months significantly elevates baseline HRV. Sleep quality is also critically important. Beyond those, certain breathing techniques — specifically extended exhales — can acutely raise HRV by activating the vagal pathway. Taking a breath in and extending the exhale to be roughly twice as long as the inhale activates the vagus nerve and increases parasympathetic tone.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's the interesting phenomenon where excitement about the next day can disrupt sleep?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. If you're very excited about something happening the next day — a big event, a trip, an important meeting — that anticipatory arousal can interfere with sleep, particularly the falling-asleep phase. It's not necessarily a problem with your sleep architecture; it's a problem with the arousal state. Managing that, whether through journaling about what you're excited about before bed, or some form of relaxation practice, can help.

## Organ Aging, "Ageotypes"; Biological Age vs Chronological Age
*(01:42:48)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about ageotypes — this concept from your lab.

**Michael Snyder:** So, the idea is that people age in different ways. Chronological age is just how many years you've been alive. Biological age tries to capture how old your body actually is physiologically. But the insight from our work is that even biological age isn't one number — different organs age at different rates in different people.

We identified four main ageotypes: cardiovascular, metabolic, liver, and immune. Some people are predominantly cardiovascular agers — their heart and vasculature age faster than their other systems. Others are metabolic agers — their glucose regulation and metabolic pathways age faster. Others are liver agers, and some are immune agers.

Why does this matter? Because it's actionable. If you know you're a cardiovascular ager, you really should be aggressive about cardiovascular risk factors. If you're a metabolic ager, glucose management becomes a primary focus.

**Andrew Huberman:** How does this compare to the Horvath epigenetic clocks and other measures of biological age?

**Michael Snyder:** The Horvath clocks and other DNA methylation-based age measures are impressive scientifically. They do predict health outcomes at a population level. But at an individual level, a single number for biological age isn't very actionable — it doesn't tell you what to do. The ageotype approach is more granular and therefore more actionable. You know which system is aging faster and can target interventions there.

## Longevity, Health Span, Genetics, Blue Zones
*(01:49:41)*

**Andrew Huberman:** How much of longevity is genetic versus lifestyle?

**Michael Snyder:** For the average person, genetics accounts for about 16% of lifespan variation. That's much less than people think. It means that lifestyle — diet, exercise, sleep, stress, social connection — is the dominant factor. For people with exceptional longevity genes, like APOE2 and certain other variants, genetics plays a bigger role. But for most of us, it's mostly in our hands.

The Blue Zones research is interesting here. The people in Okinawa, Sardinia, Loma Linda — the common features are not just diet. It's strong social networks, sense of purpose, regular moderate physical activity, and mostly whole-food plant-rich diets. The social component is probably more important than people appreciate.

**Andrew Huberman:** And the Mediterranean diet keeps coming up as evidence-based.

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. The PREDIMED trial and others have established the Mediterranean diet as genuinely longevity-promoting. Olive oil, fish, legumes, whole grains, lots of vegetables, moderate wine. The polyphenols, the omega-3s, the fiber — it all converges on the same mechanisms we've been discussing.

## Epigenetics, Viral Infection & Disease
*(01:52:19)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Tell us about epigenetics and how viral infections can trigger disease.

**Michael Snyder:** Epigenetics refers to changes in gene expression that don't involve changes to the DNA sequence itself — things like DNA methylation and histone modifications that turn genes on or off. These can be heritable, and they can be altered by environmental exposures, including viral infections.

What we found, which I think is really important, is that viral infections can cause lasting epigenetic changes that alter disease risk. The example I'm personally invested in is RSV — respiratory syncytial virus — which I contracted, and we documented that it triggered my type 2 diabetes by causing epigenetic changes that impaired my beta cell function. Not just transiently, but persistently.

**Andrew Huberman:** And COVID seems to be another example of this.

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. Two to four percent of people who get COVID develop diabetes after the infection. The virus can directly infect pancreatic beta cells, and there seem to be lasting epigenetic effects. This is a major concern for long COVID — the persistent symptoms may in part be explained by these durable epigenetic changes that alter organ function.

## ALS, Heritability; Neuroprotection, Nicotine
*(01:58:54)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Your lab has also been working on ALS. What have you found?

**Michael Snyder:** ALS is traditionally thought of as poorly heritable — only about 10% of cases are familial. But when we did a deep genetic analysis, we found 690 genes associated with ALS. The previous count was something like 7. So, the genetic landscape is vastly more complex than appreciated, and genetics contributes far more than the 10% figure suggests.

**Andrew Huberman:** And there's an interesting connection to nicotine and neuroprotection?

**Michael Snyder:** Yes. This is one of those counterintuitive findings. Nicotine — not smoking, but nicotine itself — has shown neuroprotective effects in animal models, with reduced rates of Parkinson's disease and Alzheimer's disease in rodents. There are epidemiological data in humans showing lower rates of these diseases in smokers, though that's confounded by the fact that smokers die of other things first.

I actually heard Richard Axel, the Nobel Prize winner, talk about this once — how nicotine might be genuinely neuroprotective. The mechanism likely involves nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. This is not a recommendation to smoke or use nicotine products, but it is an active research area.

## Air Quality, Allergies, DEET & Pesticides, Inflammation, Mold; Microplastics
*(02:03:47)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Environmental factors — air quality, pesticides, microplastics. What should people know?

**Michael Snyder:** Air quality is hugely underappreciated. PM2.5 — fine particulate matter — is one of the most significant environmental contributors to cardiovascular and metabolic disease. There are now cheap air quality monitors — $50 to $100 — that can measure PM2.5 in your home and environment. If you live near a highway or in an area with frequent wildfires or agricultural activity, your indoor air quality may be much worse than you realize. HEPA filters make a real difference.

We also found, interestingly, that DEET — the insect repellent — is ubiquitous. We measured it in essentially everyone we profiled, including people who reported never using DEET. So, environmental exposures that you're not aware of are happening. Near agricultural areas, pesticide levels are elevated. These things cause low-grade inflammation.

Mold is another one — people who have certain genetic susceptibilities can have severe inflammatory reactions to mold that can be misdiagnosed as everything else.

**Andrew Huberman:** And microplastics?

**Michael Snyder:** Microplastics are being found everywhere now — in blood, in breast milk, and recently in brain tissue. The health implications are genuinely unclear at this point. They're there. We know that. Whether they're causing harm, and at what concentrations, is still being worked out. It's a legitimate concern, and reducing plastic exposure seems prudent — glass containers, filtering water, reducing consumption of highly packaged foods.

## Single-Drop Blood Test & Biomarkers, Wearables, Observational Trials
*(02:15:02)*

**Andrew Huberman:** Two Stanford faculty talking about measuring lots of biomarkers from a single drop of blood screams of Theranos. Let's just call it what it is and not dance around it. Which was a spectacular failure that involved, apparently, a lot of corruption and lying. However, that was some years ago, and you were not involved with that. So the technology has now evolved to the point where my understanding is that you are able to measure lots of biomarkers from a single drop of blood.

**Michael Snyder:** Right. Thousands. And the idea is different from what Theranos was doing. They were trying to replace standard clinical assays — measuring LDL, cholesterol, things like that — from a microvolume. That's actually quite hard. We're not trying to replace clinical values. We're doing deep data profiling — metabolomics, lipidomics, proteomics — from a small sample. The key was finding the right collection format that keeps the analytes stable. We spent seven years testing different approaches. The old approach of using paper — cellulose — doesn't work well because analytes oxidize. We ultimately identified formats that work and validated them.

I did experiments where I took a sample every hour for seven straight days while also wearing a CGM and a smartwatch and doing food logging. We found thousands of correlations between biochemistry and behavior. For instance, my insulin rises 10 minutes after a glucose spike. We found that alpha-synuclein — involved in Parkinson's — seems to fluctuate with stress. We're pursuing that. These are observational trials — deep data measurements on individuals to generate hypotheses that can then be tested.

## Immersive Events & Mental Health Benefits
*(02:26:40)*

**Andrew Huberman:** I want to ask about something that, at least on the face of it, might seem unusual for a Stanford geneticist — your laboratory's involvement in looking at data from people who attended a Tony Robbins event. Could you describe the contour of this study?

**Michael Snyder:** It started when a postdoc joined my lab who was very interested in mental health. We realized that mental health is very poorly measured. Surveys are the gold standard, and how do you know if you're getting better? You do more surveys? That's unsatisfying to me. We thought wearables and microsampling could give us better biomarkers.

We ran a study with Byron Katie — who runs immersive reframing programs — and then a pilot study with Tony Robbins. We profiled participants before, immediately after, and at follow-up time points. The results surprised me — by standard survey measures, these people showed real improvements in anxiety, depression, burnout. The Byron Katie group, we now have some of the omics data back, and they did show improvements in inflammatory markers as well.

We then ran a larger study — about 600 to 700 people who went through Tony Robbins, compared to a similar-sized control group who didn't. Same thing: the Tony Robbins group improved significantly on virtually all wellness measures. Anxiety, depression, burnout. The control group didn't. And the effect persisted — we tracked them for up to a year. The caveat is that it wasn't randomly assigned, so the people who signed up for Tony Robbins may differ in some ways from those who didn't. But the signals are there.

We've now written up the psychological paper and are still analyzing the omics data from the microsampling. It should be published as a preprint shortly.
