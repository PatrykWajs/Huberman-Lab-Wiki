# Episode 92: The Effects of Cannabis (Marijuana) on the Brain & Body

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Andrew Huberman delivers a comprehensive solo deep-dive into cannabis (marijuana), covering strains (sativa, indica, hybrid), the three-type THC:CBD ratio classification system, and the endocannabinoid system — explaining how THC and CBD hijack CB1 and CB2 receptors with thousandfold greater potency than the brain's own anandamide and 2-AG. He examines how cannabis affects specific brain regions (hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, amygdala, basal ganglia, cerebellum, hypothalamus) to produce its characteristic effects on memory, mood, focus, appetite, and motor control. The episode explores cannabis's divergent effects on creativity, libido, speech patterns, and hormone systems (prolactin, testosterone, estrogen, GnRH axis), distinguishing acute from chronic use throughout. Huberman gives particular emphasis to three high-risk populations: pregnant and breastfeeding mothers (CB1 receptors are critical for fetal neural wiring), adolescents and young adults under 25 (accelerated prefrontal cortical thinning, 4x psychosis risk from a *Lancet Psychiatry* systematic review of 4,000+ studies), and individuals with personal or family history of psychosis or schizophrenia. Therapeutic applications — chemotherapy-related nausea, glaucoma, pain — are acknowledged alongside the independent harms of smoking and vaping.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cannabis has 70+ psychoactive compounds; THC and CBD are primary** — The plant contains over 70 psychoactive and 400 biologically active compounds. THC (delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol) drives the high; CBD (cannabidiol) has largely non-psychoactive biological effects; CBN (cannabinol) is a third, less-studied compound. Most research focuses on THC and CBD. [00:15:08](transcript.md#001508-cannabis-strains-psychoactive-compounds-thc--cbd)

2. **Sativa vs. indica: divergent subjective profiles, unpredictable individually** — Sativa varieties produce head-centered stimulant effects (elevated mood, alertness, talkativeness, narrowed focus); indica produces full-body sedation and relaxation ("in-da-couch"). Both suppress amygdala threat detection. Individual response — including anxiety and paranoia — is unpredictable regardless of strain and cannot be reliably predicted from personality or anxiety baseline. [00:19:34](transcript.md#001934-sativa-vs-indica-stimulant-vs-relaxation-effects)

3. **Type 1/2/3 classification describes the THC:CBD ratio within any strain** — Type 1 = THC-dominant, Type 2 = roughly equal THC and CBD, Type 3 = CBD-dominant. This ratio is the primary modulator of psychoactive intensity. Higher CBD partially moderates THC-driven effects; all strains still produce memory impairment and motor effects. [00:25:55](transcript.md#002555-hybrid-cannabis-strains-type-1-2--3-strains)

4. **THC and CBD outcompete endogenous cannabinoids by ~1,000-fold, causing dependence** — When cannabis enters the brain, it parks in CB1 receptors with such affinity that the body's own anandamide (EAE) and 2-AG are effectively sidelined. Without external THC, the endocannabinoid system cannot compensate, producing rebound anxiety, disrupted mood, and impaired brain states — the mechanism of dependence. [00:40:35](transcript.md#004035-thc-cbd-vs-endogenous-cannabinoids-dependence)

5. **Endocannabinoids are retrograde messengers that bi-directionally tune synaptic gain** — Released from postsynaptic neurons and traveling backward to modulate presynaptic release, endogenous cannabinoids can cause long-term potentiation or long-term depression depending on circuit and context. CB1 receptors are enriched in the nervous system; CB2 receptors dominate in immune tissue, liver, and genitals. [00:43:14](transcript.md#004314-endogenous-cannabinoids-cannabinoid-receptors--nervous-system-function)

6. **Cannabis acts on multiple brain regions producing a constellation of effects** — THC/CBD bind CB1 receptors across the hippocampus (short-term memory deficits, universal across strains), prefrontal cortex (sativa: focus increase; indica: planning shutdown promoting sleep), amygdala (threat-detection suppression), basal ganglia and cerebellum (motor slowing, less physical activity, speech changes), and hypothalamic arcuate nucleus (munchies — dual mechanism: cognitive food-craving plus gut blood-sugar signaling). CB2 receptors in mouth and eyes explain dry mouth and red eyes. [01:09:41](transcript.md#010941-brain-areas-affected-by-thc--cbd-side-effects)

7. **Cannabis and creativity: the link is personality-mediated, not direct** — Cannabis raises dopamine in thinking/planning circuits, facilitating divergent thinking. But a key study (LaFrance et al., *Consciousness and Cognition*) found the cannabis–creativity link is "largely a spurious correlation driven by differences in personality" — specifically openness to experience. Cannabis enhances creativity only in those for whom it reduces anxiety, enabling greater divergent thinking. High-dose or anxiety-inducing cannabis impairs creativity; non-users do not gain creative capacity from cannabis. [01:26:41](transcript.md#012641-does-cannabis-increase-creativity)

8. **Chronic use alters speech patterns even when sober** — Vogel et al. (*Drug and Alcohol Dependence*) found chronic users (≥2x/week) show measurably reduced spectral tilt (vocal effort and intensity) and altered verbal timing when not under the influence — reflecting lasting changes to motor circuits (basal ganglia, cerebellum) that govern speech. Neurosurgeon Dr. Eddie Chang (UCSF) reports he can predict cannabis use from neural recordings of the laugh reflex alone. [01:35:08](transcript.md#013508-chronic-cannabis-use--changes-in-speech-patterns)

9. **Cannabis's libido effect is individual and prolactin-mediated** — A 2017 fMRI study (*Psychopharmacology*) found the key predictor of sexual arousal response to cannabis is prolactin reactivity, not baseline levels. Individuals whose prolactin rose under THC showed no nucleus accumbens activation to erotic stimuli; those whose prolactin did not rise showed elevated activation and greater arousal. Since prolactin is mutually inhibitory with dopamine, elevated prolactin suppresses the dopamine-driven sexual desire circuit. [01:46:46](transcript.md#014646-cannabis--libido-dopamine--prolactin)

10. **Chronic smoking suppresses the HPG axis, reducing testosterone and raising estrogen** — THC (not CBD) suppresses GnRH from the hypothalamus, reducing LH and FSH from the pituitary, lowering testosterone and disrupting ovulation. Smoking cannabis ≥2x/week significantly elevates prolactin and aromatase enzymes, converting testosterone to estrogen (observed as gynecomastia in ~35% of male chronic users). Edible cannabis appears to have a smaller prolactin-elevating effect. Cannabis also impairs sperm motility and health at high THC doses chronically. [01:56:55](transcript.md#015655-cannabis--hormones-prolactin-testosterone-estrogen--fertility)

11. **Smoking and vaping cannabis carry independent vascular and respiratory harms** — Regardless of substance, combustion products and vaping chemicals damage endothelial cells lining blood vessels and capillaries throughout the brain and body — reducing cognitive capacity, raising stroke risk, impairing lung function, and causing sexual dysfunction through reduced blood flow. These harms are separate from THC/CBD pharmacological effects; vaping is not safe, only different in harm profile from smoking. [02:06:53](transcript.md#020653-smokingvaping-tobacco-or-cannabis--negative-health-consequences)

12. **Cannabis during pregnancy disrupts fetal brain wiring — 15% of US pregnant mothers self-report use** — CB1 receptors are expressed on every neuron in the developing brain and are essential for cell proliferation, axon outgrowth, axon steering, and synapse formation. Endogenous cannabinoids are at their highest concentrations in fetal development. THC and CBD cross the blood-placental barrier and outcompete these critical endogenous signals. ~15% of US pregnant mothers report cannabis use. All contacted OB/GYNs and pediatricians strongly discourage any form (smoked or edible, including CBD) during pregnancy and breastfeeding. [02:10:06](transcript.md#021006-avoiding-cannabis-during-pregnancybreastfeeding-fetal-neural-development)

13. **Chronic use drives escalating anxiety and depression, not the reverse** — For chronic users (≥2x/week), over 12+ months the CB1 receptor's G protein-coupled cascade downregulates: fewer receptors become available, downstream signaling weakens, and anxiety relief diminishes. Users require higher doses for diminishing effect. Anxiety eventually worsens even during use. Cannabis use makes individuals four times more likely to develop chronic major depression; depression does not strongly drive people to seek cannabis. [02:18:13](transcript.md#021813-negative-health-consequences-of-cannabis-anxiety--depression-tolerance)

14. **Adolescent use accelerates prefrontal thinning and quadruples psychosis risk** — A *Translational Psychiatry* Bayesian causal network study found cannabis use between ages 14–25 accelerates prefrontal cortex gray matter thinning dose-dependently. A *Lancet Psychiatry* systematic review of 4,000+ studies found heavy use (≥2x/week) is associated with 4x the risk of psychosis (schizophrenia, bipolar-like episodes) later in life; starting at age 12–14 more than doubles psychosis risk. The 16–24 age bracket has the highest adoption rates (~20% using daily), making this Huberman's most urgent public health concern. [02:25:57](transcript.md#022557-cannabis-use--adolescenceyoung-adulthood-predisposition-to-psychosis)

15. **Family history of psychosis or bipolar disorder is a hard contraindication** — Bipolar disorder carries a 30x familial inheritance rate. Cannabis use during adolescence adds a 4x compounded psychosis risk on top of that genetic predisposition. Individuals with personal or family history of schizophrenia or bipolar disorder should avoid cannabis entirely — the risk is not dose-dependent in this population and may manifest from minimal use. [02:34:36](transcript.md#023436-adolescent-cannabis-use-brain-development--mental-health-disorders)

16. **Established therapeutic uses exist — chemotherapy nausea, glaucoma, pain** — Cannabis has documented utility for chemotherapy-induced nausea, reducing intraocular pressure in glaucoma, and pain management. Huberman notes these are best addressed in dedicated condition-specific episodes. Lower-THC, higher-CBD (Type 2–3) edible formulations carry less risk in adults over 25 using occasionally. [02:41:44](transcript.md#024144-cannabis--pain-management-divergent-effects-of-cannabis)

## Tools & Protocols

### Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR)
- **What:** Behavioral practice guiding the brain and body into deep relaxation while remaining awake; umbrella term for yoga nidra variants and scripted relaxation. Restores dopamine in motivation and motor-control circuits and improves ability to fall back asleep.
- **How:** Follow a guided audio script. Huberman's free 10-minute version is on YouTube (Virtusan channel — search "Huberman NSDR"). Involves long-exhale breathing and a perceptual shift from thinking/planning to pure sensation. Science-based; no mystical component.
- **When:** First thing in the morning after poor sleep, any time during the day for restoration, or during a nighttime waking.
- **Ref:** [00:07:03](transcript.md#000703-tool-non-sleep-deep-rest-nsdr-sleep--anxiety)

### Cannabis Harm-Reduction Decision Framework
- **What:** Seven-variable checklist Huberman outlines for evaluating whether cannabis use is appropriate for a given individual.
- **How:** Assess: (1) Age — avoid entirely under 25; (2) Genetic predisposition — personal or family history of psychosis, schizophrenia, or bipolar disorder is a hard stop; (3) THC:CBD ratio — prefer Type 3 (high CBD, low THC) to reduce psychosis and anxiety risk; (4) Frequency — occasional (< 2x/week) is lower risk than chronic (≥ 2x/week); (5) THC potency — lower potency carries significantly less psychiatric risk; (6) Route of administration — edibles/tinctures preferred over smoking/vaping to avoid endothelial damage; (7) Profession and cognitive demands — high-precision work warrants greater caution.
- **When:** Before initiating, continuing, or adjusting cannabis use patterns.
- **Ref:** [02:41:44](transcript.md#024144-cannabis--pain-management-divergent-effects-of-cannabis)

### Cannabis Abstinence During Pregnancy and Breastfeeding
- **What:** Firm, evidence-based recommendation to abstain from all cannabis products — including edibles and CBD — during pregnancy and the breastfeeding period.
- **How:** Complete abstinence from THC, CBD, CBN, and any other cannabinoids in any delivery form (smoked, vaped, edible, tincture, transdermal). CB1 receptors are critical to every stage of fetal neural development; even edible CBD crosses the blood-placental barrier and outcompetes endogenous cannabinoids essential for fetal brain wiring.
- **When:** Throughout pregnancy and the full breastfeeding/lactation period.
- **Ref:** [02:10:06](transcript.md#021006-avoiding-cannabis-during-pregnancybreastfeeding-fetal-neural-development)
