## [00:01:01] Eyes, Lens, Eyelashes

What is vision? Well, vision starts with the eyes. We have no what's called extraocular light perception. While it feels good to have light on our skin, while it feels good to be outside in the sunlight for most people, the only way that light information can get to the cells of your body is through these two little goodies on the front of your face. Your eyes, in particular your neural retinas, are part of your central nervous system. They are part of your brain. They are the only part of your brain that sits outside the cranial vault — in other words, you have two pieces of your brain that deliberately got squeezed out of the skull during development and placed in these things we call eye sockets.

The eyes have a lot of other important structures in them. There's a lens to focus light precisely to the retina. There are also other pieces of the eye that are designed to keep the eye lubricated. You also have these things that we call eyelashes. Most people don't know this, but eyelashes are there to trigger the blink reflex — they aren't just aesthetically nice. Eyelashes are there so that if a piece of dust or something starts to head towards the cornea, the eye blinks very, very fast. It's the fastest reflex you own.

We also have eyelids. Now, eyelids might seem like the most boring topic of all, but they are incredibly fascinating. Today we're going to talk about how you can actually use your visual system to increase your levels of alertness based on the neural circuits that link your brain stem with your eyelids.

## [00:02:40] Retina, Photoreceptors & Brain

The entire job of the eyes is to collect light information and send it off to the rest of the brain in a form that the brain can understand. No light actually gets in past those neural retinas — it gets to the neural retina, and we have specific cells in the eye called photoreceptors. They come in two different types: rods and cones. Cones are mainly responsible for daytime vision, and the rods are mainly responsible for vision at night or under low light conditions generally speaking.

These photoreceptors — the rods and cones — have chemical reactions inside them that involve things like vitamin A. That chemical reaction converts the light into electricity. Within the eye, within the retina, there is then a series of stages of processing, and that information eventually gets sent into the brain by a very specific class of neurons called retinal ganglion cells.

Here's what's incredible — everything you see around you, you're not actually seeing those objects directly. What you're doing is making a best guess about what's there based on the pattern of electricity that arrives in your brain. Let's take an example of a color like green. You have cones in your eye that respond best to the wavelength of light that is reflected off, say, a green apple. You don't actually see the green apple — what you see is the light bouncing off that green apple going into your eye. Your brain actually compares the amount of green reflection coming off that apple to the amount of red and blue around it. What the brain is receiving is a series of electrical signals, and it's comparing those signals in order to come up with what we call perceptions.

The brain is doing these incredible things. It's also creating a sense of depth. Even though what arrives from the retina is essentially a readout of a two-dimensional flat image, your eyes are slightly offset from one another. The image of a face lands on one eye in a slightly different location than it does in the other eye. The brain then does the equivalent of geometry and trigonometry and essentially figures out how far away you are — which is just incredible. The brain uses about 40 to 50% of its total real estate for vision. That's how important vision is.

## [00:07:25] Time of Day & Retina, Tool: Morning Sunlight Exposure

As amazing as eyesight is, it actually did not evolve for us to see shapes and colors and motion and form. The most ancient cells in our eyes — and the reason we have eyes — is to communicate information about time of day to the rest of the brain and body. Remember, there's no extraocular photoreception. There's no way for light information to get to all the cells of your body, but every cell in your body needs to know if it's night or day.

There is a particular category of retinal ganglion cell: the so-called melanopsin retinal ganglion cells, named after the opsin they contain. They are essentially photoreceptors in their own right. These cells communicate to areas of the brain when particular qualities of light are present in your environment, and signal to the brain that it's early day or late in the day. They regulate when you'll get sleepy, when you'll feel awake, how fast your metabolism is, your blood sugar levels, your dopamine levels, and your pain threshold.

These melanopsin ganglion cells have been shown to set the circadian clock and to respond best to the contrast between blue and yellow light of the sort that lands on these cells when you view the sun at low solar angle — when it's low in the sky either in the morning or in the evening. The most central and important aspect of our biology is to anchor ourselves in time. We know time at a biological level based on where the sun is.

What does this mean for a protocol? Get that light in your eyes early in the day and anytime you want to be awake. Try and get as much sunlight in your eyes during the day as you safely can. You need a lot of this light in order to trigger these melanopsin cells, which will then trigger your circadian clock — which sits above the roof of your mouth — which will signal every cell in your body including temperature rhythms and so on. Get that bright light early in the day. Absolutely essential. Two to ten minutes.

## [00:10:25] Tool: Reduce Nearsightedness & Outdoor Time

Getting two hours a day of outdoor time without sunglasses has a significant effect on reducing the probability that you will get myopia. Myopia, or nearsightedness, has to do with the way that the lens focuses light onto the retina. Your eye is an optical device. You have lenses in your eyes and those lenses need to move. It's not a rigid lens like a glass lens — it's a dynamic lens.

## [00:10:56] Accommodation, Focus, Tools: Panoramic Vision; Upward Gaze

The eye can dynamically adjust where light lands by moving the lens and changing the shape of the lens through a process called accommodation. If you understand this process of accommodation, you not only can enhance the health of your eyes in the immediate and long term, but you also can work better — you'll be able to focus better on physical and mental work, and you will be able to concentrate for longer. So much of our mental focus, whether or not it's for cognitive endeavors or physical endeavors, is grounded in where we place our visual focus.

Accommodation is our ability to accommodate to things that are up close or further away. The iris and the musculature and a structure called the ciliary body move the lens. When you look far away, your lens actually relaxes — it can flatten out — and you'll notice that it actually is relaxing to look at a horizon. Whereas if you look at something up close, like a pen or phone or computer screen, it takes effort. The muscles have to contract such that the lens actually gets thicker in order to bring the light to the retina.

These days we're spending a lot of time looking at things up close — mainly our phones and computers — and we are indoors. In other words, you are not giving your lens the opportunity to flatten out and for these muscles to relieve themselves of this work, but you are also training your eyes to be good at looking at things up close and not far away. As a consequence, you are reshaping the neural circuitry in your brain. You want to get outside not just to lighten the load on your mind, but to maintain the health of your visual system. Exercise these muscles by letting the lens move and relax.

What's the protocol? For every 30 minutes of focused work, you probably want to look up every once in a while, relax your face and eye muscles including your jaw muscles — because all these things are closely linked in the brain stem — and allow your eyes to go into so-called panoramic vision where you're just not really focusing on anything. Then refocus on your work.

If you are feeling tired, it can actually be beneficial to the wakefulness systems of the brain — including the locus coeruleus and areas that release norepinephrine — to actually look up, toward the ceiling. You don't want your chin all the way back, but to look up, raise your eyes toward the ceiling, and try to hold that for 10 to 15 seconds. It actually triggers some of the areas of the brain that are involved in wakefulness. When things are up, we tend to be alert. When everything's focused down, including our eyes, it tends to have a more suppressive or sedative type signaling to the deeper centers of the brain.

## [00:14:43] Improve Vision, Tools: View Distances; Smooth Pursuit; Accommodation

How can you improve your vision? How can you get better at seeing things? One way is to make sure that you spend at least 10 minutes a day total viewing things off in the distance — well over half a mile or more. Try and see a horizon. Try and get your vision out to a location that's beyond the four walls of your house or apartment. Try and see at a distance because it's good for your eyesight. It'll keep the lens nice and elastic and the muscles nice and strong that move the lens.

Our visual system is exquisitely tuned to motion — not just our self-generated motion, but the motion of things around us. One of the things that it does is something called smooth pursuit. Smooth pursuit is our ability to track individual objects moving smoothly through space in various trajectories. You can actually train or improve your vision by looking at smooth pursuit stimuli. Remember, the brain follows the eye — it follows the movements of the eye. The neural circuits within the brain have to cope with changes in smooth pursuit. If you're doing a lot of reading up close, you're not viewing horizons, and you're not getting a lot of smooth pursuit stimulation, your vision will get worse.

The tool is: spend two to three minutes doing smooth pursuit. There are some programs on YouTube — you can look up "smooth pursuit stimulus." Practice accommodation for a few minutes maybe every other day. Just bring something in close, feel the strain of your eyes doing that, move it out, feel the relaxation point, move it past that relaxation point where you will have to do a vergence eye movement to maintain focus as it moves out, and bring it back in. Practice accommodation.

Be sure to give your eyes some rest. Get outside, look at a horizon, or do nothing — just let your eyes go soft, what the yogis would call soft gaze. Practice a little bit of smooth pursuit. You don't have to be neurotic about this, but if you do this every other day or every third day or so, you'll maintain the visual and motion tracking systems of the brain and the eye working in a really nice coordinated fashion.

## [00:17:36] Binocular Vision, Lazy Eye, Children

The young brain — up until about age seven, but maybe even extending out until about age 12 — is extremely vulnerable to differences in ocular input between the two eyes. My scientific great-grandparents won the Nobel Prize for discovering so-called critical periods: periods of time in which the brain is more plastic, more able to change. Those two guys, David Hubel and Torsten Wiesel, forever changed the face of visual neuroscience and forever changed the way we think about treatment of the young brain.

It used to be thought that you wouldn't want to do surgery on a young kid because of risk of anesthesia in young individuals, but we now know that you need to repair these imbalances — that even a few hours of occluding one eye early in life can lead to permanent, unless something's done, permanent changes in the way that the brain perceives the outside world. When that eye is opened up again, the brain actually can't make sense of anything coming through it. It shuts down that visual pathway.

What does this all mean in terms of protocols? If you're a young person, do your best to get really good binocular vision — not just at the level of your phone or your tablet, but also at distance. You will build strong binocular visual machinery in the brain and at the level of the eyes and the eye musculature. If you did have an occlusion, what's needed is to cover up the other eye to create an imbalance so that the weak eye — the so-called lazy eye, sometimes referred to as amblyopia — has to work harder.

Almost everybody has a dominant eye. It usually doesn't relate to your dominant hand, although it can. If you do have strong imbalances between the two eyes — which can be caused by cataracts, lens issues, or neuromuscular issues — try and get those dealt with as early as possible by contacting a really good ophthalmologist and ideally a neuro-ophthalmologist. It is very common for young children and babies to have an eye with strabismus that either deviates out or deviates in. It is important to correct that if you would like to have balanced vision between the two eyes and high-fidelity quality vision.

## [00:20:25] Hallucinations & Visual System

Hallucinations are a property of the visual system, and it was always thought that hallucinations arise because of overactivation or activation of certain aspects of the visual system. There's a paper published by a good friend and phenomenal scientist and physicist, Chris Neil up at the University of Oregon in Eugene. They studied LSD-like compounds and discovered that hallucinations actually occur because portions of your brain become underactive. The visual portions of your brain are understimulated.

This is probably why when people go into cave retreats where it's completely black, pretty soon they start hallucinating — they start seeing things even though there's nothing there. The visual system is desperate to make guesses about what's out in the world. It turns out that hallucinations are an underactivation of the visual system and then a compensation by which the visual system creates activity and generates hallucinations. So if you're in the dark long enough, you start to hallucinate and see things.

## [00:21:38] Improve & Test Vision, Tool: Snellen Chart

One of the things that you can do to improve your vision — and it's also kind of fun — is to put a Snellen chart in your home. A Snellen chart is that list of letters that at the Department of Motor Vehicles they have you cover up an eye and read — the letters of course get smaller and smaller. This is something that's not often mentioned, but your performance on the Snellen chart will vary depending on time of day, because your level of fatigue and your ability to control accommodation and other mechanisms of the eye muscles will vary. So you can take it as an average.

It's also a good thing, if you're going to get your vision tested for corrective lenses or maybe thinking about laser surgery, to really get it measured by a professional — get your vision tested by somebody who really understands vision like an ophthalmologist or a really good optometrist. If you put a Snellen chart in your home, you can do that as part of your visual training.

What is more important than your eyesight? Eyesight is so vital. It's right up there with movement and our ability to move, to get up out of chairs and to walk and to run and to take care of ourselves. Eyesight and movement are the main ways that we are able to take care of ourselves and take care of others. When you start having compromised eyesight or compromised movement, people need to take care of us. So while it might seem nerdy to have a Snellen chart in your home or to do a smooth pursuit exercise a couple times a week, preserving your eyesight is one of the most life-enhancing things that you can do.

## [00:23:44] Support Vision, Tool: Vitamin A & Vegetables

Now of course there are genetic factors and injury-related factors that can compromise eyesight and our ability to see. The things discussed today aren't going to solve all those issues, but they can have a tremendous positive impact if you're willing to do just a little bit of work. There are a few other things that can perhaps improve vision, and I want to dispel a few myths about supplementation.

You understand a lot about the biology of vision: light has to arrive at the retina and get converted into electrical signals. That process requires things like vitamin A — a fat-soluble vitamin. It requires things like the carotenoids. That metabolic cascade, the biochemical cascade, is essential for vision. This is why you've been told that carrots help you see better, because they're high in vitamin A.

There are a few simple things you can do to support your vision. First, it is true that eating dark leafy vegetables and things like carrots that have vitamin A in abundance, and eating them in close to their raw form — naturally occurring foods that contain a lot of vitamin A in their raw form — can help support vision. Does that mean that if you ingest super-physiological amounts of that stuff it's going to make your vision that much better? ## [00:25:11] Supplements, Lutein, Astaxanthin

There's a lot of excitement nowadays about supplementation to help support the health of the visual system. I want to talk about a molecule that's in a lot of supplements to support vision and for which there are some really good data: that's lutein. What is lutein? Lutein is in the pathway that relates to vitamin A and the formation of the opsin — the photopigment that captures light in the back of your eye, literally absorbs light, and converts it into electrical signals allowing you to see.

There is some evidence — I spoke to our chair of ophthalmology — through quality peer-reviewed studies that supplementing with lutein can help offset some of the detrimental effects of age-related macular degeneration. However, only for individuals with moderate to severe macular degeneration. For people that have normal vision or with just a low degree of macular degeneration, these studies did not see a significant improvement of vision from supplementing with lutein.

The other compound is astaxanthin. What is astaxanthin? It's a really interesting compound — it's the red-pink pigment found in various seafoods and also in the feathers of flamingos. It's structurally similar to beta carotene, so it's very pro-vitamin A, but it has some chemical differences which may make it safer than vitamin A. Remember, vitamin A is a lipid-soluble vitamin, so it can be stored in our body for long periods of time. The most notable effect of astaxanthin for the purposes of this episode is on ocular blood flow. It does seem to increase the amount of ocular blood flow — the blood supply to the eyes. It's also been shown to have positive effects on things like skin elasticity, skin moisture, and skin quality — probably due to its effects on blood flow.
