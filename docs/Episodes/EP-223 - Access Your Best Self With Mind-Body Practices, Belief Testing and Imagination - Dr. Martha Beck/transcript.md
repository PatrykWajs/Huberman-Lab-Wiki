## [00:37:22] Accessing the Subconscious; Compassionate Witness Self

**Andrew Huberman:** You use the term "essential self" a lot. Can you define it precisely? What is the essential self, and how is it different from the social self?

**Dr. Martha Beck:** The social self is the part of us that was shaped by culture, family, religion, peer pressure — all the forces outside us that told us who we should be. The social self is the persona we learned to perform. It's not bad — it's essential for navigating society — but it's not our deepest identity. The essential self is what was there before all of that programming. It's the part of you that had preferences as a child before you knew you were supposed to have different preferences. It shows up in what fills you with energy versus what drains you. It shows up in the things you gravitate toward when no one is watching, when there's no performance required. The essential self is often quieter than the social self — the social self is loud and anxious and opinionated — but it's deeper and it's more consistent.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** And here's the thing — the essential self never learns to lie. The social self learns to lie very early: white lies, social lies, lies of omission. But the essential self always tells the truth. It just tells it in sensations rather than in words.

**Andrew Huberman:** So how do we access it? Given that we're socialized from birth to suppress it, how do we hear it?

**Dr. Martha Beck:** The most direct path is through the body. The essential self speaks in body sensations. Certain things open the body — a warmth, a relaxation, an expansion. Other things close it — a tightening, a contraction, a sinking. Learning to read those signals is the foundation of everything I teach. And the doorway to that is what I call the Compassionate Witness Self.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** The Compassionate Witness Self is a way of relating to yourself that comes from depth rather than from either the impulsive "I want" part or the controlling "you should" part. Here's an exercise: Hold your hands out in front of you, palms up. In your left hand, imagine the part of you that just wants to do whatever it wants, eat whatever it wants, say whatever it feels, follow every impulse — your wild impulsive self. In your right hand, hold the part of you that criticizes and controls and drives — the part that says "you're not doing enough, you're not being good enough." Now hold those two out. That is what some traditions call the True Self, or what I call the capital-S Self. That is what we're trying to get back to.

**Andrew Huberman:** This resonates deeply with the Internal Family Systems (IFS) model developed by Richard Schwartz — the idea that there are multiple parts, and the goal is not to eliminate any of them, but to bring them into relationship with a Self that can hold them all with compassion.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** Exactly, and I've met Richard Schwartz and I love his work. ## [00:59:14] Suffering & Compassionate Attention

**Andrew Huberman:** You've developed a practice around what you call "letting stay" rather than "letting go." Can you describe that?

Second, you pay compassionate attention to the suffering without trying to fix it or change it. Third — and this is the magic — you follow the compassion itself back to its source, and you find the capital-S Self. You find that the compassion was coming from somewhere — from a self that is not identical with the suffering.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the compassion is both the path and the destination.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** Yes. Exactly that. The compassion leads you to the compassionate self. And from there, suffering has a different quality — it's still real, it's still painful, but it doesn't feel like annihilation anymore.

## [01:02:10] Challenging Internal Thoughts, Understanding Truth, Body & Mind

**Andrew Huberman:** You've described a practice of testing beliefs — specifically internal thoughts that cause suffering. Byron Katie does something similar with "The Work." What's your version of this practice?

**Dr. Martha Beck:** My version is simpler than Byron Katie's — though I love her work and have trained with her. The basic practice is this: take any thought that is causing you suffering and ask, "Is this true?" Not in a dismissive way, but in a genuinely curious way. Then notice what happens in your body when you hold the thought. Does your body open or close? Contract or expand? The body is a truth detector in a way that the verbal mind is not. The verbal mind can generate any narrative whatsoever and make it seem convincing. But the body doesn't lie. When you hold a true belief, the body tends to relax and expand. When you hold a false belief, even one you've told yourself thousands of times, the body tends to contract and tighten.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** Now this isn't infallible — some true things are frightening, and the body will contract at them initially. But there's a qualitative difference between the contraction of encountering a challenging truth and the contraction of holding a false belief. The challenging truth has a quality of "yes" even in the discomfort. The false belief has a quality of "no" — a wrongness that doesn't resolve. You learn to read the difference.

**Andrew Huberman:** And this is something you can train? Like any other sensory skill?

**Dr. Martha Beck:** Absolutely. It's a sensory skill exactly like any other. ## [02:06:55] Tool: True Empathy

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about empathy, because you've distinguished between what most people call empathy and what you call "true empathy." What's the difference?

**Dr. Martha Beck:** Most of what people call empathy is actually what psychologists call emotional contagion — you feel what the other person feels, you catch their emotional state. This can be very intense, especially for highly sensitive people. But it's not actually helpful. When you're flooded with another person's pain, you lose your capacity to think clearly or act effectively. You become part of the problem rather than part of the solution.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** True empathy has four components. First is self-other awareness — the clear recognition that I am not you. We are separate beings with separate realities. This isn't coldness — it's clarity. Second is emotion regulation — the ability to be present with another person's distress without being dysregulated by it. You stay in your own nervous system while witnessing theirs. Third is perspective-taking — the genuine effort to understand what the experience is like from inside their reality, not from inside yours. And fourth is compassionate action — doing something that is actually useful for them, not just what would make you feel better.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** The critical distinction is: true empathy happens from inside your own skin, not from inside theirs. You witness their experience from your own stable ground. This is why highly empathic people — in the emotional-contagion sense — often end up completely overwhelmed and useless to the people they're trying to help. And it's why the most effective helpers I've ever seen have a quality of grounded calm — they're fully present, they're moved, but they're not swept away.

**Andrew Huberman:** This connects to the oxygen mask principle — you have to be stable yourself before you can help someone else stabilize.

**Dr. Martha Beck:** Exactly. And it's another form of what we've been talking about all along: maintaining contact with your own essential self even in the presence of another person's distress. The Compassionate Witness Self is the place from which true empathy is possible.
