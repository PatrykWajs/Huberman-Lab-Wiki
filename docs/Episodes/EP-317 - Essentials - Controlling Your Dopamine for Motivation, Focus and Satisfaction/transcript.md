## Dopamine & Drive

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. I'm Andrew Huberman, and I'm a professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford School of Medicine. Today we are going to talk all about dopamine and what drives you to do the things that you do. We're going to talk about motivation and desire and craving, but also how dopamine relates to satisfaction and our feelings of well-being.

If ever you've interacted with somebody who just doesn't seem to have any drive — they've given up — or if you've interacted with somebody who seems to have endless drive and energy, what you are looking at in those two circumstances is without question a difference in the level of dopamine circulating in their system.

## Neuromodulator; Dopamine Effects, Parkinson's Disease; Brain Circuits

Dopamine is what we call a neuromodulator. Neuromodulators are different than neurotransmitters. Neurotransmitters are involved in the dialogue between neurons, whereas neuromodulators influence the communication of many neurons in the nervous system. What this means is that dopamine release changes the probability that certain neural circuits will be active and that other neural circuits will be inactive.

Dopamine is not just responsible for pleasure. It is responsible for motivation and drive, and also for craving. Those three things are sort of the same. It also controls time perception — this turns out to be important both for avoiding addiction and for sustaining effort and being a happy person over long periods of time. Dopamine is also vitally important for movement.

In diseases like Parkinson's or Lewy body dementia, there is a depletion or death of dopamine neurons at a particular location in the brain, which leads to shaky movements, challenges in speaking, and challenges in initiating movement. Because dopamine is depleted elsewhere too, people with Parkinson's and Lewy body dementia also experience drops in motivation and affect — they tend to get depressed. When those people are properly treated, they can recover some fluidity of movement and they feel better psychologically, not just because they can move but also because dopamine impacts mood and motivation.

You have two main neural circuits in the brain that dopamine uses in order to exert all its effects. One is mainly for movement — the substantia nigra to dorsal striatum pathway. And there is the other pathway, the so-called mesocorticolimbic pathway, that is for reward, reinforcement, and motivation. Dopamine can be released in two ways: local synaptic release and volumetric release, where dopamine is distributed more broadly across many neurons. This means dopamine is incredible because it can change the way that our neural circuits work at a local scale and at a very broad scale.

If I were to put a really simple message around dopamine, it would be: there's a molecule in your brain and body that when released tends to make you look outside yourself, pursue things outside yourself, and to crave things outside yourself. Dopamine is a universal currency in all mammals, but especially in humans, for moving us toward goals. And how much dopamine is in our system at any one time compared to how much dopamine was in our system a few minutes ago — that dictates your so-called quality of life and your desire to pursue things.

## Motivation & Dopamine Levels

Your experience of life and your level of motivation and drive depends on how much dopamine you have relative to your recent experience. This is why when you repeatedly engage in something that you enjoy, your threshold for enjoyment goes up and up and up. If you understand the schedules and kinetics around dopamine, you will be in a terrific position to modulate and control your own dopamine release for optimal motivation and drive.

All of us have different baseline levels of dopamine. Some of this is surely genetic. Some people just simply ride at a level a little bit higher. Some people are a little less excitable. Even subtle fluctuations in dopamine really shape our perception of life and what we're capable of and how we feel.

## Subjective Experience & Dopamine, Activities that Increase Dopamine

Dopamine colors the subjective experience of an activity to make it more pleasurable and something that you want more of. What sorts of activities increase dopamine and by how much?

Chocolate will increase your baseline level of dopamine 1.5 times — it's a pretty substantial increase in dopamine, though it is transient, going away after a few minutes or even seconds. Sex — both the pursuit of sex and the act of sex — increases dopamine two times. Nicotine, in particular nicotine that is smoked, increases dopamine two and a half times above baseline, very short-lived. Cocaine will increase the level of dopamine in the bloodstream two and a half times above baseline. Amphetamine will increase the amount of dopamine in the bloodstream ten times above baseline — a tremendous increase.

Exercise will have a different impact on dopamine depending on how much somebody subjectively enjoys that exercise. If you're somebody who loves running, chances are it's going to increase your levels of dopamine two times above your baseline — not unlike sex. People who dislike exercise will achieve less dopamine increase or no increase in dopamine from exercise. The same applies to yoga, weightlifting, swimming, or any form — it's going to vary by your subjective experience.

The prefrontal cortex — the area of your forebrain involved in thinking, planning, and assigning a rational and subjective experience to something — plays a key role in this. Certain chemicals have a universal effect and make everybody's dopamine go up. But the subjective coloring of an experience by dopamine explains why the same activity can produce vastly different dopamine responses in different people.

## Dopamine Highs, Lows & Baseline; Evolutionary Context, Addiction

Let's really drill into what dopamine peaks versus baseline means. Why would we have a dopamine system at all? We have to remember what our species' primary interest is. Like all species, we have a main interest: to make more of ourselves. It's about foraging for resources — food, water, salt, shelter, social connection. Dopamine is the universal currency of foraging and seeking.

It makes perfect sense that dopamine would have a baseline level and that it would have peaks, and that the baseline and peaks would be related in a direct way. Let's say you were alive 10,000 years ago and you woke up with minimal water and minimal food left. You need to generate the energy to go seek those things. Dopamine drives you to go out and look for things. Maybe you hunt an animal and kill it. You experience a dopamine release — you found the reward. But then it needs to return to a lower level, because if you just stayed there you would never continue to forage for more.

Critically, it doesn't just go back down to the level it was before. It goes down to a level below what it was before you went out seeking that thing. We all have a sort of dopamine set point. If we continue to indulge in the same behaviors — or even different behaviors — that increase dopamine in big peaks over and over and over again, we won't experience the same level of joy from those behaviors or from anything at all. That has a name: addiction.

When somebody pursues a drug or an activity that leads to huge increases in dopamine, the baseline drops because of depletion — the readily releasable pool of dopamine in synaptic vesicles is literally depleted. People feel pretty lousy and many make the mistake of pursuing the dopamine-releasing substance again, thinking mistakenly that it's going to bring up their baseline. Not only does it not give them a peak, their baseline gets lower and lower. Eventually they will stop getting dopamine release from that activity at all and drop into serious depression.

Fortunately, there is a way to work with the dopamine system such that we can constantly stay motivated while also keeping that baseline of dopamine at an appropriate, healthy level.

## Dopamine Reward Prediction Error, Tool: Intermittent Rewards

The key lies in intermittent release of dopamine. The real key is to not expect or chase high levels of dopamine release every time we engage in these activities. Intermittent reward schedules are the central schedule by which casinos keep you gambling, by which elusive partners keep you texting and pursuing, and by which the internet and social media keep you motivated and pursuing.

There is something called dopamine reward prediction error. When we expect something to happen, we are highly motivated to pursue it. If it happens, great — we get the reward in various chemical forms including dopamine, and we are more likely to engage in that behavior again. This is the basis of casino gambling. You can transplant that example to any number of different pleasureful activities. Some activities naturally have this intermittent property woven into them.

Your ability to experience motivation and pleasure for what comes next is dictated by how much motivation and pleasure and dopamine you experienced prior.

## Caffeine & Dopamine; Tool: Yerba Mate & Protecting Dopamine Neurons

There is one exception to this pattern of peaks depleting the baseline: caffeine. Caffeine upregulates D2/D3 dopamine receptors, which actually makes whatever dopamine is released by an activity more accessible and more functional within the biochemistry and pathways of your brain and body.

While coffee, tea, or other forms of caffeine will have this effect of increasing dopamine receptors, yerba mate has some particularly interesting properties. First, it contains caffeine. It is also high in antioxidants and contains GLP-1, which is favorable for management of blood sugar levels. Yerba mate has also been shown to be neuroprotective specifically for dopaminergic neurons — ingestion of yerba mate and some of the compounds within it can actually serve to preserve the survival of dopamine neurons in both the movement-related pathway and the motivation pathway.

If one were going to consume caffeine, you might consider consuming that caffeine in the form of yerba mate — both for the sake of upregulating dopamine receptors and getting more of a dopamine increase, for the stimulant properties of caffeine, and additionally because yerba mate does appear to have neuroprotective and in particular dopamine neuron-protective properties.

## Amphetamine, Cocaine & Challenges for Learning

There is also evidence that two substances that greatly increase dopamine — namely amphetamine and cocaine — can cause long-term problems with the dopaminergic pathways. This is largely based on a study published in 2003. The title of the paper pretty much tells the story: "Amphetamine or cocaine limits the ability of later experience to promote structural plasticity in the neocortex and nucleus accumbens."

The neocortex is the outer shell of the brain, and the nucleus accumbens is part of the mesolimbic dopamine pathway for motivation, drive, and reinforcement. Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to change in response to experience — it is the basis of learning and memory and essentially remodeling of our neural circuitry in positive ways of all kinds. This study was one of the first to show that ingesting amphetamine and cocaine — because of the high peak in dopamine that they create and the low dopamine state they create afterwards — limits plasticity and learning subsequent to taking those substances.

This should serve as a serious cautionary note: amphetamine and cocaine not only cause a drop in baseline dopamine but can actually put the brain into a state in which it cannot learn and modify itself to get better, at least for some period of time.

## Tool: Increase Dopamine & Deliberate Cold Exposure

There are activities that we can do that will give us healthy, sustained increases in dopamine — both peaks when they happen and to maintain or even increase our baseline levels. One of these is deliberate cold exposure. Cold showers, ice baths, or exposing oneself to cold water of various kinds can in fact increase our levels of dopamine as well as the neuromodulator norepinephrine.

First, safety parameters: getting into very very cold water — 30°F or even low 40s°F — can put somebody into a state of cold water shock. People can die doing that, so approach this with caution. But for most people, getting into 60°F or 50°F water can have tremendously beneficial results on your neuromodulator systems including dopamine.

A study on human physiological responses to immersion into water of different temperatures found that upon getting into cold water, changes in adrenaline and noradrenaline were immediate. What was interesting is that dopamine levels started to rise somewhat slowly and then continued to rise, reaching levels as high as 2.5 times above baseline — a remarkably high increase. Why would that be good when I've basically said increases in dopamine are detrimental to your baseline? This does appear to raise the baseline of dopamine for substantial periods of time. Most people report feeling a heightened level of calm and focus after getting out of cold water.

Cold water exposure turns out to be a very potent stimulus for shifting the entire milieu of our brain and body, allowing many people to feel much better for a substantial period of time. It can be very stimulating, so typically doing it early in the day is better. Some people do it seven days a week, others three days a week, others every once in a while. Once you become cold water adapted, once it no longer has the same impact of novelty, then it will no longer evoke this release.

## Hard Work & Motivation, Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Rewards, Tool: Growth Mindset

I would like to talk about the positive and negative aspects of rewards for our behavior, and from that suggest a protocol by which you can achieve a better relationship to your activities and to your dopamine system — a way to tune up your dopamine system for discipline, hard work, and motivation.

Hard work is hard. Most people work hard in order to achieve some end goal. End goals are terrific and rewards are terrific, whether monetary, social, or any kind. However, because of the way that dopamine relates to our perception of time, working hard at something for the sake of a reward that comes afterward can actually make the hard work much more challenging and make us much less likely to lean into hard work in the future.

There is a classic experiment done at Stanford in which children in nursery school and kindergarten drew pictures — and they drew because they liked to draw. The researchers took kids who liked to draw and started giving them a reward for drawing, generally a gold star or something a young child would find rewarding. Then they stopped giving them the gold star, and what they found is the children had a much lower tendency to draw on their own. Remember, this was an activity that prior to receiving a reward the children intrinsically enjoyed and selected to do.

This relates to intrinsic versus extrinsic reinforcement. When we receive rewards — even if we give ourselves rewards for something — we tend to associate less pleasure with the actual activity itself that evoked the reward. If you get a peak in dopamine from a reward, it's going to lower your baseline. And the cognitive interpretation is that you didn't really do the activity because you enjoyed it — you did it for the reward.

It is also important to understand that dopamine controls our perception of time. When we engage in an activity and have a reward waiting at the end, we are extending the time bin over which we are analyzing or perceiving that experience. Because the reward comes at the end, we start to dissociate the neural circuits for dopamine and reward that would have normally been active during the activity. We have the experience of less and less pleasure from that activity while we're doing it.

This is the antithesis of growth mindset — that striving to be better, being in the mindset of "I'm not there yet, but striving itself is the end goal." Growth mindset consistently delivers people to tremendous performance. People that have growth mindset end up performing very well because they're focused on the effort itself. All of us can cultivate growth mindset. The neural mechanism of cultivating growth mindset involves learning to access the rewards from effort and doing. That's hard to do because you have to engage the prefrontal component of the mesolimbic circuit. You have to tell yourself: "This effort is great. This effort is pleasureful." What you find over time is that you can evoke dopamine release from the friction and the challenge that you happen to be in.

The beauty of the mesolimbic reward pathway is that it includes the forebrain. So you can tell yourself the effort part is the good part. "I know it's painful. I know this doesn't feel good, but I'm focused on this. I'm going to start to access the reward." You will find the rewards — meaning the dopamine release — inside of effort. If you repeat this over and over again, it starts to become reflexive for all types of effort. When we focus only on the trophy, only on the grade, only on the win as the reward, you undermine that entire process.

So how do you do this? In those moments of the most intense friction, you tell yourself that in that moment you are doing it by choice and you're doing it because you love it. The ability to access pleasure from effort through our dopaminergic circuitry is without question the most powerful aspect of dopamine and our biology of dopamine. And the beautiful thing is it is accessible to all of us.

To really underscore what can interfere with and prevent you from getting dopamine release from effort itself: don't spike dopamine prior to engaging in effort, and don't spike dopamine after engaging in effort. Learn to spike your dopamine from effort itself.

## Experiences & Shifting Perception, Dopamine Balance

Along the lines of how dopamine schedules and our perception of things shape the way we experience pleasure, there are beautiful studies mainly looking at sugar appetite and our sense of pleasure from sweet things. If you ingest something that you like — it tastes good to you — but then you ingest something that's even sweeter, and then you go back to the food you ate previously, well, you don't like it as much. And that shift in perception can be blocked by blocking the shift in dopamine. This really speaks to the peaks and valleys in dopamine and how your experience of anything is going to depend on your prior experience of other things that evoke dopamine. Big dopamine release makes it more challenging to experience more big dopamine release.

So dopamine is one of those things that you don't want too high or too low for too long. It's all about staying in that dynamic range, and that's going to be different for everybody.

## Compounds to Increase Dopamine: Wellbutrin, L-Tyrosine, PEA, Alpha-GPC

There are circumstances in which increasing levels of dopamine is desirable, advantageous, and clinically helpful. A good example is the drug Wellbutrin, also called bupropion, which increases dopamine and norepinephrine. Wellbutrin was developed as an alternative treatment for depression because some people who take SSRIs — selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors — suffer from serotonin-related side effects like decreased appetite, decreased libido, or other side effects they don't want. Wellbutrin seems to avoid the sexual side effects but can blunt appetite, and because of the increase in norepinephrine and dopamine it increases levels of motivation and craving but can also create a state of elevated alertness that can sometimes get in the way of healthy eating. One has to work with their clinician as it is a prescription drug in order to find the correct dosage.

For those seeking to increase baseline levels of dopamine without prescription pharmaceuticals, many people have turned to L-tyrosine. L-tyrosine is an amino acid precursor to L-dopa — it lies further up the dopamine synthesis pathway. It is sold over the counter in the United States. People take L-tyrosine as a way to get more energized, alert, and focused. It is typically taken in capsule or powder form, anywhere from 500 to 1,000 milligrams. The time scale for increasing dopamine is about 30 to 45 minutes after ingestion — that's when dopamine levels start to peak. If you have a pre-existing dopaminergic condition — schizophrenia, psychosis of any kind, bipolar, anxiety — L-tyrosine will not be good for you. If you don't have such conditions, you should expect it to lead to an increase in dopamine. For some people that feeling may be agitating; for others it will be pleasureful. There will be a crash afterwards. L-tyrosine can be used from time to time for enhancing focus and motivation, but not regularly — you don't want to experience the drop in dopamine that inevitably occurs some period of time afterwards.

There is one compound that you have probably actually taken without realizing it that increases dopamine: PEA, phenylethylamine — technically beta-phenylethylamine. PEA is found in various foods; chocolate just happens to be enriched in PEA and can increase synaptic levels of dopamine. PEA can be taken from time to time as a focus and work aid for intense bouts of work. Again, not too often. A useful combination is 500 milligrams of PEA and 300 milligrams of alpha-GPC. This leads to a sharp but very transient increase in dopamine that lasts about 30 to 45 minutes and tends to be more regulated and even than something like L-tyrosine.

## Social Connection; Recap & Key Takeaways

While it's useful to think about pharmacology and underlying neural circuitry and cold water baths and all these different things related to dopamine schedules and reward mechanisms, I would be remiss if I didn't include that social connections — close social connections in particular — are essential to stimulating the dopamine pathways. So the take-home message there is quite simple: engage in and pursue quality, healthy social interactions.

The most important things to understand are that these dopamine pathways really are under your control, and the locus of control resides in the fact that your previous levels of dopamine are influencing your levels of dopamine right now — and your current levels of dopamine and where you take them next will influence your dopamine levels in the days and weeks to come. Thank you for your interest in science.
