# Episode 355: Essentials: How to Optimize Your Hormones for Health & Vitality | Dr. Kyle Gillett

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Kyle Gillett, dual board-certified physician in family medicine and obesity medicine, joins Andrew Huberman for a comprehensive discussion on hormone optimization across the lifespan for men and women. Topics include the Big 6 lifestyle pillars for hormone health, how to get useful bloodwork ordered, the roles and interactions of testosterone, DHT, estrogen, progesterone, growth hormone, prolactin, and DHEA, PCOS diagnosis and treatment, TRT risks and sleep apnea, the effects of cannabis and alcohol on the HPG axis, peptides (BPC-157, melanotan/PT-141), and the underappreciated role of spiritual health in overall hormone function.

## Key Takeaways (10-12 bullets with [HH:MM:SS] links)

- **[00:02:35]** The **Big 6 lifestyle pillars** for hormone health are: diet, exercise, stress optimization, sleep, sunlight/outdoor exposure, and spirit — consistency over years beats intensity over weeks (law of diminishing returns).
- **[00:04:32]** Bloodwork should be done **every 3–6 months** preventatively; always get both a fasted and a non-fasted panel to identify metabolic trends before they become clinical problems.
- **[00:05:35]** **Caloric restriction improves testosterone in obese or metabolically unhealthy individuals** but decreases it in young, lean, healthy men — the intervention must match the patient's metabolic status.
- **[00:06:51]** Intermittent fasting at **caloric maintenance** is not deleterious to hormone health and actively enhances the overnight growth hormone pulse; longer fasting windows yield slightly more GH output (law of diminishing returns applies).
- **[00:09:20]** The three most common hormone-sleep disruptions: (1) true growth hormone deficiency, (2) vasomotor symptoms of menopause/andropause (low progesterone in women; low testosterone in men), (3) TRT-induced sleep apnea — risk scales dose-dependently.
- **[00:11:43]** Women have significantly more **total testosterone than estradiol** (measured in different units); SHBG binds androgens in order: DHT > testosterone > DHEA > estradiol — understanding SHBG is essential for interpreting any androgen panel.
- **[00:13:34]** **DHT** is a potent androgen driving motivation, effort, and libido; dietary polyphenols (e.g., bioavailable turmeric + black pepper extract) inhibit 5-alpha reductase and reduce DHT — relevant for those with androgenic hair loss or low DHT symptoms.
- **[00:16:47]** **PCOS** affects 10–20% of women and is frequently underdiagnosed; Rotterdam criteria require only 2 of 3 features (androgen excess, insulin resistance, polycystic ovaries); first-line tools include body composition optimization, myo-inositol, and metformin if insulin resistance is confirmed.
- **[00:20:13]** **Smoked cannabis** increases aromatase → raises estrogen → suppresses LH/FSH → reduces testosterone, acting similarly to opiates on the HPG axis; **alcohol** and all GABA agonists (benzos, barbiturates) directly suppress the HPG axis and lower testosterone dose-dependently.
- **[00:23:04]** **Prolactin and dopamine must stay balanced** (the "wave pool" model); estrogen directly upregulates prolactin synthesis; dietary casein and gluten act as mu-opioid receptor agonists in the gut and can meaningfully raise prolactin in sensitive individuals.
- **[00:26:22]** **BPC-157** increases VEGF (blood vessel growth) and is most useful short-term for cartilage and ligament injuries; contraindicated with active cancer or high cancer risk; non-pharmacy-grade peptides frequently contain LPS, causing inflammation — not a sign the peptide is working.
- **[00:34:05]** The **sixth pillar — spirit** — has measurable physiological consequences regardless of religious belief; spiritual alignment affects hormone health, mental and physical outcomes; interdisciplinary care (body + mind + spirit) consistently improves clinical results.

## Tools & Protocols

### Big 6 Hormone Health Pillars
**What:** Six lifestyle levers ranked by hormonal impact: diet, exercise, stress optimization, sleep, sunlight/outdoor exposure, spirit.
**How:** Prioritize resistance training within exercise; aim for 150–180 min/week zone 2 cardio minimum; optimize sleep continuity; manage cortisol through community and practices; daily outdoor exposure; intentionally address spiritual health.
**When:** Ongoing foundation — applicable for both men and women from puberty onward.
**Ref:** [00:02:35]

### Hormone Testing Protocol
**What:** Regular bloodwork to track hormones and metabolic markers over time.
**How:** Every 3–6 months; include a fasted AND non-fasted draw. For men: total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, LH, FSH, estradiol, DHEA-S, prolactin, cortisol, CBC, metabolic panel. For women: add progesterone; time draws to cycle phase.
**When:** Any age; particularly important during hormonal transitions (puberty, perimenopause, andropause). Report symptoms (low energy, poor focus, reduced performance) to physician to justify ordering labs.
**Ref:** [00:04:32]

### Zone 2 Cardio for Hormone Health
**What:** Low-intensity sustained aerobic exercise supporting metabolic and hormonal baseline.
**How:** 150–180 min/week minimum at a conversational pace; distribute across 3–5 sessions.
**When:** Year-round baseline; not a replacement for resistance training.
**Ref:** [00:05:35] — Higher zone 2 volume reduces necessity of caloric restriction for hormonal benefit.

### Intermittent Fasting for Growth Hormone
**What:** Time-restricted eating to enhance the overnight GH pulse without sacrificing testosterone or other hormones.
**How:** Stop eating 2–3 hours before sleep; maintain caloric maintenance (not restriction) if metabolically healthy; 16:8 or similar windows are effective.
**When:** Daily habit; particularly beneficial for GH and IGF-1 optimization in middle-aged and older adults.
**Ref:** [00:06:51]

### PCOS Management Protocol
**What:** Multi-tool approach addressing androgen excess, insulin resistance, and ovulatory dysfunction.
**How:** Optimize body composition first; consider myo-inositol (insulin sensitizer) and D-chiro-inositol (weak anti-androgen); use metformin only if insulin resistance is confirmed; diet and exercise are primary levers.
**When:** Upon diagnosis or suspicion of PCOS; labs + clinical evaluation before initiating supplements or medications.
**Ref:** [00:16:47]

### Prolactin Reduction via Diet
**What:** Removing dietary mu-opioid receptor agonists that elevate prolactin.
**How:** Eliminate or reduce casein (dairy protein) and gluten; assess subjectively over 4–8 weeks.
**When:** When prolactin is elevated or dopamine-prolactin ratio feels imbalanced (low motivation, reduced libido).
**Ref:** [00:23:04] — Simple, no-cost intervention; most relevant for prolactin-sensitive individuals.

### BPC-157 Short-Term Injury Protocol
**What:** Peptide enhancing vascularization of injured tissues, particularly cartilage and ligaments.
**How:** Prescribe through a physician; source from a compounding pharmacy to ensure LPS removal; use short-term only; avoid entirely with cancer history or high risk.
**When:** Acute musculoskeletal injury targeting poorly vascularized tissues (cartilage, ligaments, tendons).
**Ref:** [00:26:22] — VEGF-increasing mechanism; LPS contamination is the primary risk of non-pharmaceutical sources.

## Resources Mentioned

- Rotterdam criteria — diagnostic framework for PCOS (2 of 3 features required)
- SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin) — androgen binding hierarchy: DHT > T > DHEA > estradiol
- HPG axis (hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal) — central hormone regulation pathway
- 5-alpha reductase — enzyme converting testosterone to DHT; inhibited by plant polyphenols
- Myo-inositol / D-chiro-inositol — insulin sensitizers used in PCOS management
- Metformin — biguanide insulin sensitizer; first-line for metabolic component of PCOS
- BPC-157 (Body Protective Compound 157) — VEGF-upregulating peptide for tissue repair
- Bremelanotide / PT-141 — FDA-approved for hypoactive sexual desire disorder in women
- Tesamorelin (Egrifta) — FDA-approved GH-releasing hormone analogue for lipodystrophy
- LPS (lipopolysaccharide) — inflammatory contaminant in non-pharmacy-grade peptides
- VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor) — targeted by BPC-157; inhibited by Avastin in oncology
- Avastin (bevacizumab) — VEGF inhibitor on WHO essential medications list for cancer
- Dutasteride mesotherapy — topical/localized scalp injections for androgenic hair loss without systemic DHT suppression
