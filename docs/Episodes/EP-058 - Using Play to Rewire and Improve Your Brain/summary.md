# Episode 58: Using Play to Rewire & Improve Your Brain

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Play is not a childhood luxury — it is the primary biological mechanism through which the nervous system rewires itself at every stage of life, homeostatically regulated by ancient brain circuits that never disappear. Andrew Huberman explains the precise neurochemistry of genuine play (elevated endogenous opioids from the periaqueductal gray, low epinephrine), why high adrenaline from stress or trauma blocks access to the play state entirely, and how contingency testing in low-stakes environments expands the prefrontal cortex's behavioral repertoire. The episode traces play's function from the infant-toddler transition through adolescent social development, introducing the concept of personal play identity — four dimensions (play style, personality, sociocultural environment, economics/technology) that form in early adolescence and predict adult behavioral tendencies. Trauma's suppression of play circuits is addressed alongside recovery strategies including dance and movement therapies. The episode closes with a concrete protocol: at minimum one hour per week of novel, low-stakes play to sustain adult neuroplasticity.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Play is homeostatically regulated** — Like hunger or sleep, play is driven by ancient circuitry. Animals restricted from playing will play more when given the opportunity, placing play alongside other fundamental biological drives. ([00:13:57](transcript.md#1357-homeostatic-regulation-of-play))

2. **The neurochemistry of play is specific: high opioids, low epinephrine** — Genuine play requires elevated endogenous opioids released from the periaqueductal gray (PAG) and low levels of adrenaline/epinephrine. Any elevation of epinephrine — from stress, competitive anxiety, or trauma — blocks the play circuits entirely. ([00:50:39](transcript.md#5039-neurobiology-of-low-stakes-play))

3. **Play is the primary portal to neuroplasticity** — Across all animal species, those that play longest throughout life have the greatest capacity for brain plasticity. Play is not a hack; it is the evolutionarily conserved mechanism by which the nervous system rewires itself at any age. ([01:00:03](transcript.md#10003-play-is-the-portal-to-neuroplasticity))

4. **Contingency testing is play's core function** — Play allows the brain to explore different roles, outcomes, and rules in a low-stakes environment. This expands the prefrontal cortex's repertoire of behavioral algorithms, making people more creative, flexible, and effective in real-world contexts. ([00:29:21](transcript.md#2921-contingency-testing))

5. **Play postures actively shift neurochemistry** — Animals and humans use specific body postures — lowered head, soft eyes, tongue out, partial stances — to signal low-stakes interaction. These postures actively suppress adrenaline and enable the play state. The head tilt with open eyes is the universal human play signal. ([00:36:13](transcript.md#3613-body-postures))

6. **Rule testing and graceful loss-acceptance build social cognition** — Healthy play involves testing and sometimes breaking the rules of play itself. Children who cannot accept losing or who violate social rules of play show impaired social development; play that includes rule-breaking and recovery builds resilience. ([00:44:03](transcript.md#4403-rule-testing--breaking))

7. **Trauma and chronic stress suppress play circuits** — Elevated adrenaline from childhood trauma or chronic stress directly suppresses the neural circuits for play. This is not permanent; exposure to safe, low-stakes play environments — including dance and movement therapy — can restore access to plasticity. ([01:18:03](transcript.md#11803-trauma--play-deficits--recovery))

8. **Reading on smartphones suppresses physiological sighs and impairs learning** — A study (Honma et al., *Scientific Reports*) found that reading on smartphones suppresses physiological sighs, causes prefrontal cortex hyperactivation, and reduces comprehension. The narrow visual aperture of a phone screen likely suppresses brainstem parafacial nucleus neurons that generate sighs. ([00:02:23](transcript.md#0002-tool-reading-on-smart-phones-sighing--learning))

9. **Personal play identity predicts adult behavioral tendencies** — How a child plays — their competitiveness, tolerance for role-switching, rule rigidity, preference for solo vs. group play — forms a "personal play identity" (coined by Güneş, 2021) that directly predicts adult behavioral patterns in work, relationships, and learning. ([01:32:52](transcript.md#13252-personal-play-identity))

10. **Chess and role-based games produce outsized neuroplastic effects** — In chess, each player must mentally inhabit multiple pieces with different rules simultaneously, forcing the prefrontal cortex to run more behavioral algorithms in parallel. This multi-role demand produces broader neuroplasticity than single-role games or linear exercise. ([01:27:36](transcript.md#12736-chess-mental-roles-novelty))

11. **The playful mindset is about expanding outcome tolerance, not about fun** — A playful mindset means deliberately expanding the outcomes you are willing to entertain and reducing attachment to any specific result. It does not require smiling or silliness — it requires putting yourself into scenarios where you are not the top performer. ([00:32:17](transcript.md#3217-the-power-of-playful-mindset))

12. **Tinkering is the adult expression of the play drive** — Companies like NASA historically hired childhood tinkerers. Feynman, Rodney Mullen, and Banksy all exemplify the same pattern: those who continue to evolve their fields maintain a low-stakes exploratory stance alongside serious pursuit of goals. ([00:54:22](transcript.md#5422-expanding-capabilities-through-tinkering))

13. **Dynamic, multi-directional movement is especially potent for plasticity** — Play involving jumping, lateral movement, and variable speeds engages the vestibular system and cerebellum in ways linear exercise (e.g., running in a straight line) does not, opening broader plasticity portals. ([01:23:25](transcript.md#12325-competition--dynamic-movement))

14. **Developmental pruning establishes who we become — play directs that pruning** — From birth to roughly age 25, approximately 40% of neural connections are actively pruned by glial cells. Play is the primary mechanism that determines which connections are strengthened and which are removed. Adult play continues to shape the remaining circuits. ([01:10:14](transcript.md#11014-fire-together-wire-together))

15. **One hour of novel play per week is the minimum effective dose** — Play should be novel (something you are not already expert at), low-stakes, and focused on contingency exploration. Once proficiency is achieved, the activity becomes performance — switch to something new. ([01:40:55](transcript.md#14055-recommendations-for-play))

16. **Play circuits never disappear in the adult brain** — Unlike circuits pruned in development, the neural machinery for play persists throughout the entire lifespan. Biology does not waste resources; the fact these circuits remain is evidence play is essential in adulthood. ([01:40:55](transcript.md#14055-recommendations-for-play))

## Tools & Protocols

### Weekly Play Practice

- **What:** Dedicated engagement in a novel, low-stakes activity for the explicit purpose of neuroplasticity and behavioral flexibility — distinct from performance-mode exercise or work.
- **How:** Choose something you are not proficient at. Keep adrenaline low — avoid making outcomes high-stakes. Explore freely. If you become skilled at it, switch to something new. Mild competition is acceptable; outcome fixation that spikes epinephrine is not.
- **When:** At minimum 1 hour per week, as a dedicated session separate from goal-oriented work or exercise.
- **Ref:** [01:40:55](transcript.md#14055-recommendations-for-play)

---

### Physiological Sigh During Smartphone Reading

- **What:** Deliberate use of the double-inhale physiological sigh to counteract sigh-suppression caused by reading on a narrow-aperture screen, restoring oxygen delivery and reducing prefrontal hyperactivation.
- **How:** Every ~5 minutes while reading on a smartphone or small screen, perform a physiological sigh: inhale fully through the nose, add a second smaller inhale on top, then exhale slowly and completely through the mouth. Alternatively, switch to printed paper or a larger screen for material requiring retention.
- **When:** Any time you are reading or studying on a smartphone.
- **Ref:** [00:02:23](transcript.md#0002-tool-reading-on-smart-phones-sighing--learning)

---

### Playful Mindset Cultivation

- **What:** A deliberate cognitive practice of expanding the outcomes you are willing to entertain in any situation — at work, in relationships, in learning — by reducing attachment to specific results and entering scenarios where you are not the top performer.
- **How:** Put yourself in unfamiliar games or tasks. Adopt a tinkering stance — low-stakes, free-form exploration. Allow yourself to not be good at something. Focus on the process of contingency exploration. Watch how others behave (cheating, rigidity, cooperation) as information about yourself and them.
- **When:** Apply in any context where you feel rigid, outcome-fixated, or creatively stuck.
- **Ref:** [00:32:17](transcript.md#3217-the-power-of-playful-mindset)

---

### Role-Based Play for Prefrontal Expansion

- **What:** Engaging in games or activities that require you to assume multiple distinct roles with different rules — chess, role-playing games, improvisational theater, strategy games — to force the prefrontal cortex to run multiple behavioral algorithms simultaneously.
- **How:** Choose an activity that requires you to mentally inhabit different characters or rule sets within a single session. Each role forces a different set of predictions and contingency evaluations, producing broader neuroplastic effects than single-role activities.
- **When:** As part of weekly play practice, especially for adults seeking cognitive flexibility and creativity benefits who prefer non-athletic play.
- **Ref:** [01:27:36](transcript.md#12736-chess-mental-roles-novelty)

---

### Tinkering as Low-Stakes Neuroplasticity

- **What:** Free-form, outcome-uncoupled exploration of a skill or craft at the boundary of current ability — deliberately avoiding performance mode to access the high-opioid, low-epinephrine neurochemical state.
- **How:** Take something you know reasonably well and explore it without any specific goal. Remove the performance constraint entirely. Do not even aim to get better — just observe what happens when you vary your approach. The neurochemical goal is elevated endogenous opioids and low epinephrine.
- **When:** When formal play activities feel contrived; as a lower-barrier alternative to structured play for tinkerer-personality types.
- **Ref:** [00:54:22](transcript.md#5422-expanding-capabilities-through-tinkering)

---

### Dynamic Movement Play for Vestibular Plasticity

- **What:** Play or exercise involving multi-directional movement, variable speeds, jumping, lateral shuffling, or ducking — engaging the vestibular system and cerebellum to open broader plasticity portals beyond what linear exercise achieves.
- **How:** Choose activities like dance, soccer, martial arts, or gymnastics that require movement in multiple planes. The key is generating novel motor patterns, not repeating the same linear motion. Even adding backward walking to a running routine is a step in this direction.
- **When:** As part of weekly play practice; especially valuable for people whose default exercise is strictly linear (running, cycling, lap swimming).
- **Ref:** [01:23:25](transcript.md#12325-competition--dynamic-movement)

## People Mentioned

**Jaak Panksepp** — Neuroscientist; pioneered research on the neurobiological substrates of play in mammals, including the role of the periaqueductal gray and endogenous opioids in play behavior; known as "The Rat Tickler" for discovering ultrasonic laughter in rodents. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaak_Panksepp))

**Charles Darwin** — Naturalist; studied play postures across animal species, documenting the universality of play signaling behaviors including the canine play bow. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin))

**Jack Feldman** — Professor of Neuroscience at UCLA; world expert in breathing and respiration; discovered the parafacial nucleus neurons responsible for generating physiological sighs. ([UCLA profile](https://www.neuroscience.ucla.edu/jack-feldman))

**Allan Schore** — Clinical psychologist and neuropsychoanalyst at UCLA; extensive work on how early caregiver-infant interactions shape the brain's affect regulation systems, bridging infant play and emotional development. ([UCLA profile](https://www.uclahealth.org/providers/allan-schore))

**Gökhan Güneş** — Turkish researcher; coined the term "personal play identity" and defined its four dimensions (play style, personality, sociocultural environment, economics/technology). ([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/scientific-contributions/Gokhan-Gunes-2183929929))

**Carla Shatz** — Neuroscientist at Stanford; coined the phrase "fire together, wire together," describing the Hebbian synaptic strengthening that underlies neural plasticity during development and play. ([Stanford profile](https://neuroscience.stanford.edu/people/carla-j-shatz))

**Richard Feynman** — Nobel Prize-winning physicist at Caltech; cited as a lifelong tinkerer whose playful mindset — lock-picking, bongo drumming, pranks — fueled scientific creativity into old age. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman))

**Rodney Mullen** — Professional skateboarder; cited as an example of a tinkerer who evolved skateboarding by spending hours in free-form exploration of board physics rather than drilling fixed techniques. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodney_Mullen))

**Beth Stevens** — Neuroscientist at Harvard Medical School; referenced for research showing that glial cells actively prune synapses not functionally engaged — the cellular mechanism underlying play-directed neural pruning. ([Harvard profile](https://www.hms.harvard.edu/dms/neuroscience/fac/stevens.php))

**Charlie Hoehn** — Author of *Play It Away: A Workaholic's Cure for Anxiety*; advocate for play as a treatment for anxiety and burnout. ([charliehoehn.com](https://charliehoehn.com))

**John Ratey** — Associate Clinical Professor of Psychiatry at Harvard Medical School; author of *Spark: The Revolutionary New Science of Exercise and the Brain*; covers play's role in neuroplasticity. ([Harvard profile](https://hms.harvard.edu/faculty-staff/john-ratey))

**Tim Ferriss** — Entrepreneur, author, and podcaster; Huberman credits Ferriss's blog for surfacing the *Play It Away* book recommendation. ([tim.blog](https://tim.blog))
