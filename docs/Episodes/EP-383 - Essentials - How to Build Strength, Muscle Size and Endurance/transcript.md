## [00:00:39] 9 Exercise Adaptations; Progressive Overload

**Andy Galpin:** There are about nine different adaptations you can get from exercise. The first one is what we'll just call skill — improving anything from a golf swing to a squatting technique to running. From there we get into speed, which is moving as fast as possible. The next one is power, which is a function of speed multiplied by strength. Once you get past strength, the next one down the list is hypertrophy — muscle size, growing muscle mass. After hypertrophy, you get into the endurance categories: first is muscular endurance, which is your ability to do high-rep output like push-ups in a minute.

Past muscular endurance, you're into more energetic or cardiovascular fatigue — you've left the local muscle and you're now into the entire physiological system. I call this anaerobic power, your ability to produce a lot of work for say 30 seconds to two minutes. Then there's VO2 max — your ability to sustain work over three to twelve minutes at max heart rate. Finally, long-duration endurance: sustaining work for 30-plus minutes without a break. Some of these adaptations cross over, and some are actually a little bit contrarian to each other, so pushing toward one may sacrifice another.

One thing you have to do to make all of these work is what's functionally called progressive overload. Adaptation physiologically happens as a byproduct of stress — you have to push a system. If you continue to do the exact same workout over time, you're not going to get much improvement. Progressive overload can come from adding more weight, adding more repetitions, training more often in the week, or adding complexity to the movement.

## [00:03:53] Progressive Overload, One-Rep Max, Soreness, Tool: Modifiable Variables

**Andy Galpin:** What are the progressive overload principles most effective over time for strength and hypertrophy? You have what we call modifiable variables — a short list of everything you can change in your workout to produce a different outcome. The very first is choice: exercise selection. If you want to get stronger but do the wrong set range, rep range, or speed, you won't get strength — you'll get muscular endurance. So exercise selection matters, but it's the application that determines the adaptation.

The second variable is intensity — not perceived effort, but literally a percentage of your one-rep max or a percentage of your maximum heart rate or VO2 max. The third is volume: how many total reps and sets you're doing. Next is rest intervals, the amount of time between sets. Then there's progression — changing weight, reps, rest intervals, or movement complexity. For beginners especially, you want to make sure the movement pattern is correct before worrying about intensity or rep ranges, because the body needs time to develop tissue tolerance.

Soreness is a terrible proxy for exercise quality. It's a bad way to estimate whether it was a good or bad workout. If you're sore to the point where you can't sit without pain, you've gone past the point of benefit — you'll have to skip training sessions and your total monthly volume will actually be lower. You want to walk a pretty fine line, and most people should hedge a little on the side of less soreness rather than more, because frequency is very important for almost all adaptations.

**Andy Galpin:** Frequency is the last modifiable variable — how many times per week you train a specific movement or muscle group. Those are the global variables you can play with to get one outcome versus another. Some folks want to get stronger without putting on muscle mass. Some want both. If you manipulate those variables correctly, you can get exactly that — very little development of muscle size with a lot of strength development.

## [00:08:39] Full-Body Workout, Exercise Selection

**Andy Galpin:** One of my other laws of strength and conditioning is that the default is all joints through their full range of motion. The ankle should go through its full range, the knee, the hip, the elbow — all of them. Science is fairly clear that strength development and hypertrophy are generally enhanced with a larger range of motion. Caveats aside — always maintain good position and don't injure yourself.

For exercise selection, you want to balance across movement areas. That means upper body press (pushing away from you — bench press), upper body pull (pulling toward you — bent row, pull-up), and lower body movements. The pressing should include both horizontal (perpendicular to your body) and vertical (overhead). The pull version includes horizontal rows and vertical pull-ups. If you're doing a single workout, choosing one of each — one upper body press, one upper body pull, one lower body hinge, one lower body squat — gives you a decently well-rounded session.

**Andrew Huberman:** Got it. So regarding intensity for strength development?

**Andy Galpin:** There's a certain recruitment threshold needed for neurons to fire in order to develop strength. We have fast-twitch and slow-twitch muscle fibers; you activate slow-twitch ones first because they're associated with low-threshold motor neurons. The only way to activate the higher-threshold neurons — the big fast-twitch chunks — is to demand the muscle to produce more force. These fibers are incredibly important for aging; one of the major problems with age-related muscle loss is preferential loss of fast-twitch fibers.

## [00:11:11] Training Intensity, Repetitions, Sets, Rest Intervals, Supersets

**Andy Galpin:** To develop strength you need to impose a demand of strength, not just repetitions. The load has to be very high — generally above 85% of your one-rep max if you're moderately trained; maybe 75% if you're newer. Because the intensity demand is so high, by definition true strength training is going to be in the five repetitions per set or less range.

In terms of sets, three work sets per exercise is probably enough. But get fully warmed up — build up to that 85%. A classic warm-up: a set of 10 at 50%, a set of eight at 60%, a set of eight at 70%, then a set of five at 75%, then go into your two or three working sets. Rest intervals are critical for strength: you need to rest two to four minutes between sets so you can maintain that intensity on the next set. If fatigue incurs and you have to reduce the reps or the load, you've lost the primary driver.

That rest time doesn't mean sitting on your phone. You can use supersets — do your bench press, then while your chest rests, go do your deadlifts. We've studied this and supersets will reduce strength gains slightly, but only by a tiny amount. For the average person, superset. For someone trying to break a world record in powerlifting, don't superset.

**Andrew Huberman:** How often can and should one train a muscle? And how do you know if a muscle has recovered locally versus systemically?

**Andy Galpin:** The first question is: what are you training for? If you're training for hypertrophy, you need to hedge toward recovery. Hypertrophy requires a massive insult to the muscle and then allowing protein synthesis — the building of new tissue — to occur, which takes 48 to 72 hours at minimum. Strength training, on the other hand, doesn't cause a lot of soreness because intensity is the driver, not volume. So you can train for strength every single day on the same muscle if you want.

For hypertrophy, if you go back into a muscle too soon, you'll blunt the growth response and cut off the protein synthesis process. The general guideline is that if you're below a 3-out-of-10 soreness level, you can train again. The optimal window for hypertrophy is probably every 72 hours — so if you train shoulders Monday, wait until Wednesday or Thursday before training them again.

## [00:14:51] Hypertrophy vs Strength Training Recovery

**Andrew Huberman:** What if someone's schedule means they end up waiting five or six days between training the same muscle group?

**Andy Galpin:** You've not lost gains — you've just lost the opportunity to make more progress in that window. You can train a muscle once every five or six days if you want, as long as you match the total volume in that one session. The research shows frequency can vary as long as total volume stays the same. The challenge is just that it becomes difficult to get enough volume done in a single workout when you compress it into fewer sessions per week.

Volume-wise, the more recent meta-analyses say you're looking at around 10 working sets per muscle group per week as a minimum threshold. But you probably want 15 to 20, and well-trained folks may need 20 to 25 sets per week. That becomes very challenging to fit into a single workout — so splitting across more frequent sessions becomes practically necessary.

## [00:17:06] Training Volume & Frequency for Hypertrophy

**Andy Galpin:** What are the repetition ranges effective for hypertrophy? The quick answer is anywhere between five and 30 reps per set — the literature shows pretty much equal hypertrophy gains across that entire range. But I want to refine the frequency answer first: for strength, even twice per week per muscle is a good number to get most people really strong. You don't need to train a muscle at 85% every single day — two times per week is very effective.

For hypertrophy, the programming is almost idiot-proof. The range is five to 30 reps. The work is hard, but you can't really screw up the rep range. The only caveat is you have to take each set to muscular failure (or very close), and you need enough rest for protein synthesis and adaptation to occur.

## [00:18:59] Hypertrophy Rep Ranges, Frequency & Recovery; Cell Mechanisms

**Andrew Huberman:** Should people experiment with different rep ranges?

**Andy Galpin:** Yes — use the rep range as a way to have variation because most people don't want to do the exact same three sets of 10 every time. The three most likely drivers of hypertrophy are: metabolic stress, mechanical tension, and muscular damage. You don't need all three — one is sufficient, and a combination enhances outcomes. Muscular damage is the soreness mechanism; more damage is not better, but a little is a reasonable proxy. Mechanical tension is more like the strength end — even sets of five to eight will produce some muscle growth, though not optimally.

Metabolic stress — the burn — is an area of scientific contention, but something is clearly happening. You want to train to near-failure but not extreme failure. You don't need a partner to lift the barbell off your chest, but you have to get close. The programming has a lot of freedom: five to eight reps through 30 reps is all in the zone, and shifting across that range keeps training interesting and still drives adaptation through different cellular mechanisms.

## [00:22:31] Tool: 3 x 5 Protocol; Power vs Strength

**Andy Galpin:** A fast, flexible guideline I call the three-to-five concept: pick three to five exercises, do three to five reps, for three to five sets, with three to five minutes rest in between, three to five times a week. That can be as little as three sets of three for three exercises three times a week — a 20-minute workout. It can be as high as five sets of five for five exercises five days a week. This keeps you in the strength and power domains while contouring toward your lifestyle, soreness, and time constraints.

The only differentiator between power and strength training is intensity. For strength: 85% or above of your one-rep max. For power: lighter, because you need to move toward the velocity end of the spectrum. Power equals strength multiplied by speed, so while getting stronger helps power, you want to spend more time in the 40-to-70% range for power development. Everything else — exercise selection, rep and set structure, frequency — can still follow the three-to-five framework; just shift the intensity depending on the target outcome.

## [00:24:08] Mental Awareness in Training; Mind-Muscle Connection

**Andrew Huberman:** The nervous system plays an important role at every level. This takes us into the realm of where the mind is at during a particular movement. For strength training, am I focusing on moving weights? For hypertrophy, am I focusing on challenging the muscle? Does that subtle mental shift change the pattern of nerve fiber recruitment?

**Andy Galpin:** Intentionality matters for both. Research on power and speed development shows that the intent to move is actually more important than the actual movement velocity. If you're doing a power or strength exercise and just doing enough to get the bar up, you'll get less strength improvement than if you're intending to move that bar as fast as possible — even if the net bar velocity is identical. This is one reason why good coaching matters enormously.

The same concept applies to hypertrophy through the mind-muscle connection. Recent studies looking at things like a bicep curl show that simply watching your bicep and thinking about contracting it harder — even while executing the same repetitions at the same intensity — produces greater growth. The initial indication from this research strongly suggests mind-body connection results in more gains than simply going through the motions.

**Andy Galpin:** The better option is always a shorter, higher-quality training session over a longer, distracted one. If you're not feeling it that day, cut 15 minutes out and go do 20 minutes of high-quality, intentional work — that beats a 45-minute zombie workout every time.

## [00:27:10] Activating Muscle Groups, Awareness, Tool: Eccentric Overload

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there ways people can learn to engage particular muscle groups more effectively?

**Andy Galpin:** This is very common — there's always some muscle group that people just can't seem to get going. Whenever I'm diagnosing movement quality, the very first thing I check is awareness. You'd be surprised how many people, once you simply identify the muscle group and give them a tactile prompt — touching the lat during a pull-down and saying "squeeze here" — will suddenly activate a muscle they weren't accessing at all.

Outside of awareness, eccentric overload is a very effective tool for activation of a difficult-to-target muscle. Take a pull-up as an example: if you have poor lat activation, step on a box to reach the top position and then simply lower yourself under control. You're breaking the movement down into smaller pieces that let you focus on execution. Eccentrics are great for strength development, great for hypertrophy, and teach you to focus on control. A huge percentage of people who've never felt a sore lat despite doing pull-ups will wake up sore after doing eccentric-only pull-ups. That's the signal — keep going down that path and build up to full concentric-eccentric-isometric execution over weeks or months.

## [00:29:26] Tool: Resistance Training Breathing & Post-Training

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a prescriptive breathing protocol that applies to 75% of people 75% of the time?

**Andy Galpin:** In general, a decent strategy is to maintain a breath hold during the lowering or eccentric phase — the most dangerous part of the movement — and then exhale on the concentric portion. For a bench press: breathe in, brace, lower under control, then begin to exhale during the latter half of the concentric push. For a single rep, don't worry about breathing at all. For sets of three to eight, you need some breathing strategy. A common one: exhale on every third rep, reset, rebreathe. If you feel you need to breathe after every rep, that's okay but takes time.

**Andrew Huberman:** How about breathing between sets and after the workout?

**Andy Galpin:** After the workout, we're not just going to high-five, drink water, and walk out. There will be a downregulation strategy involving light control and breath control. The easiest prescription: move toward nasal breathing as much as possible, with a double exhale length relative to the inhale. If you take a four-second inhale, exhale for eight seconds. Box breathing is also fine: equal inhale, hold, exhale, hold — four seconds each. Do this for five minutes after every workout.

**Andrew Huberman:** I started doing this and it completely changed my recovery rate. I noticed two things: I recovered more quickly workout to workout, and I no longer had the energy dip three to four hours after a hard workout. I used to think that dip was about the post-workout meal, but it turned out it was the adrenaline ramp-up from training not being downregulated at the end. Clamping that with the breathing protocol allowed me to work through the afternoon.

**Andy Galpin:** You're saving neural energy. Good fighters learn to do this between rounds. Sprinters do it between events. Even one minute of downregulation breathing after any high-intensity cognitive or physical effort will pay dividends — after important social interactions, after a deep work sprint, after a workout. Just give yourself one minute.
