# Episode 95: The Science of Learning & Speaking Languages | Dr. Eddie Chang

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Eddie Chang, chair of neurosurgery at UCSF and co-director of the Center for Neural Engineering & Prostheses, joins Andrew Huberman to explore the neuroscience of speech and language from first principles through cutting-edge clinical translation. They cover how the infant brain tunes itself to native-language phonemes during a postnatal critical period — and what continuous white noise exposure may do to that process — then trace how the auditory cortex and speech motor cortex collaboratively encode the ~12 articulatory features that underpin all human words. The conversation examines the real neuroscience behind Broca's and Wernicke's areas (and how the textbook picture is partly wrong), lateralization of language, bilingualism, dyslexia, stutter, and foreign accent syndrome. A major arc covers Dr. Chang's BRAVO clinical trial, in which a man paralyzed and unable to speak for 15 years communicated sentences decoded directly from his speech motor cortex — a world first — and the lab's next frontier of decoding facial expressions to build avatar-based communication for locked-in patients. The episode also covers epilepsy types (absence, temporal lobe, nocturnal), the ketogenic diet for seizure control, BCI augmentation ethics, and closes with Dr. Chang's personal practices — aerobic exercise, alcohol avoidance, and treating the operating room as a meditative sanctuary — for sustaining peak performance under extreme cognitive and surgical demands.

## Guest: Dr. Eddie Chang

**Dr. Edward Chang, MD** is the Chair of the Department of Neurological Surgery and the Joan and Sanford Weill Chair in Neurological Surgery at the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF). He is also co-director of the UCSF Center for Neural Engineering & Prostheses. His laboratory (Chang Lab) studies how the human brain produces and comprehends speech and language, combining intraoperative neurophysiology in awake patients with computational and AI methods to decode the neural code for speech. Clinically, he specializes in the surgical treatment of epilepsy and movement disorders. His landmark 2021 *New England Journal of Medicine* paper demonstrated that a fully paralyzed, anarthric man could communicate sentences through a brain-computer interface — a first in human history.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Critical periods for language are real and mechanistic.** Any infant can tune its auditory cortex to the phonemes of any language — but only during a postnatal sensitive period shaped by the statistics of ambient sound, not genetics alone. Chang's white-noise rearing experiments in rat pups showed the critical period can be held artificially open for months — representing delayed maturation, not a bonus learning window. [00:07:19](transcript.md#000719-neuroplasticity-learning-of-speech--environmental-sounds)

2. **Continuous white-noise machines for infants carry a theoretical risk.** Masking environmental speech sounds around the clock may deprive the developing auditory cortex of the structured input it needs to close its critical period normally. Chang does not use them for his own children. Evidence comes from rodents; human studies are absent, making the caution speculative but mechanistically grounded. [00:13:10](transcript.md#001310-white-noise-machines-infant-sleep--sensitization)

3. **Speech and language are neurologically distinct systems.** Language is meaning, grammar, and intent — a distributed cortical computation. Speech is the physical motor act of shaping exhalation through the larynx, pharynx, jaw, lips, and tongue into consonants and vowels. Stutter is a speech motor-coordination disorder: ideas and grammar are fully intact; only the output machinery breaks down. [01:01:18](transcript.md#010118-speech-vs-language-signal-transduction-from-ear-to-brain)

4. **The textbook account of Broca's and Wernicke's areas is about 50% wrong.** Classic neurology placed speech production in Broca's area (left inferior frontal gyrus). Chang's intraoperative mapping across hundreds of patients shows that resecting posterior frontal lobe often leaves speech fully intact — it is the precentral gyrus (speech motor cortex) that is truly critical. Wernicke's area has held up better; damage there produces aphasia with word-salad output and comprehension failure. [00:41:08](transcript.md#004108-brain-areas-for-speech--language-brocas--wernickes-areas-new-findings)

5. **The brain encodes speech as ~12 articulatory features, not words.** Electrocorticography from the superior temporal gyrus reveals millimeter-scale salt-and-pepper maps of phonetic features — the specific tongue, lip, jaw, and larynx movements generating consonant classes (plosives: b/d/g/p/t/k; fricatives: sh/s/th/f) and vowels. These ~12 features, combined and sequenced, generate every word in every human language — analogous to DNA's four base pairs encoding all life. [01:20:26](transcript.md#012026-plosives--consonant-clusters-learning-multiple-languages)

6. **Language is strongly but not absolutely lateralized.** In right-handed people, language is left-hemisphere 99% of the time. In left-handed people, it is still left-dominant ~70% but 20–30% show bilateral or right-sided language. After a left-hemisphere stroke, the right hemisphere can acquire language through plasticity. The symmetric anatomy of both hemispheres suggests latent machinery on the right that normally goes unused. [00:53:23](transcript.md#005323-lateralization-of-speechlanguage--handedness-strokes)

7. **Bilinguals share core language circuits; live human interaction matters more than exposure alone.** Both languages map largely to the same cortical regions. Early simultaneous acquisition during the critical period establishes finer phonological maps. Critically, social live-human interaction drives specialization far more effectively than recorded audio — a key constraint on language-learning apps and taped instruction. [00:59:05](transcript.md#005905-bilingualism-shared-language-circuits)

8. **Reading and writing co-opt the speech sound system — they are not independent.** Reading is a human invention too recent to have evolved dedicated brain regions. A visual word form area in the posterior temporal lobe feeds into the auditory speech cortex: skilled readers map visual word forms onto phonological representations. Dyslexia in many children is a phonological-processing deficit (the visual-to-phonology mapping is impaired), not purely a visual disorder. Early phonological-awareness intervention produces substantially better outcomes than visual-only approaches. [01:28:33](transcript.md#012833-reading--writing-dyslexia--treatments)

9. **The BRAVO trial: a paralyzed man speaks through thought alone.** Participant "Pancho" — unable to speak for 15 years after a brainstem stroke — had ECoG arrays implanted over his speech motor cortex and connected via a transcutaneous port to an AI decoder. The decoder translated brain-activity patterns into words and sentences on a screen from a 50-word vocabulary, reaching ~50 words per minute versus ~1 word per minute by pecking a keyboard with a neck-mounted stick. The 2021 NEJM paper was the first demonstration of sentence-level BCI speech decoding in a paralyzed human. [01:45:26](transcript.md#014526-paralysis-als-locked-in-syndrome--brain-computer-interface-bci)

10. **Avatar-based BCI is the next frontier for restoring full communication.** Decoding words from speech cortex restores only the verbal channel. Chang's lab is developing systems that simultaneously decode intended facial expressions from motor cortex signals and render them on a personalized animated avatar in real time — restoring expression, lip movements, and nonverbal communication lost in locked-in syndrome, while providing embodied feedback that may accelerate BCI learning. [02:10:21](transcript.md#021021-non-verbal-communication-facial-expressions-bci--avatars)

11. **BCI augmentation ethics are unresolved.** Industry involvement has commercialized research once confined to labs. Chang distinguishes medical restoration from true cognitive augmentation and notes that society has not adequately thought through questions of access, invasiveness, and whether enhancement is desirable. He views augmentation as incremental — not sudden photographic memory — and notes the brain's evolved speech machinery already far outperforms any existing BCI in bandwidth. [02:02:14](transcript.md#020214-neuralink-bci-superhuman-skills--augmentation)

12. **Stutter is a speech motor-coordination failure, not an anxiety disorder.** Anxiety triggers and worsens stuttering but is not its cause. The failure is a breakdown in millisecond-precise coordination of larynx, lips, jaw, and tongue. Auditory feedback plays a role: changing what a stutterer hears while speaking modulates fluency, implicating a feedback loop between speech production and auditory cortex. Fluency-shaping therapy targets motor coordination directly and is the primary evidence-based route. [02:17:35](transcript.md#021735-stutter-anxiety--treatment)

13. **Foreign accent syndrome after stroke is real but gain of a new language is not.** Focal damage to the precentral gyrus speech-motor area can distort prosody and phonological output so that speech sounds like a foreign accent — without the person acquiring any vocabulary or grammar of another language. A complete new-language gain-of-function after stroke has no scientific basis and no documented cases known to Chang. [01:37:54](transcript.md#013754-stroke--foreign-accent-syndrome)

14. **Long-term motor and auditory memories are distributed, not localized.** Patients with Broca's or Wernicke's lesions can still sing familiar songs, count, and recall long-practiced phrases, because such memories are encoded redundantly across multiple cortical regions. This distributed architecture is why focal resections rarely produce total amnesia for established skills or autobiographical memories. [01:30:31](transcript.md#013031-auditory-memory-long-term-motor-memory)

15. **Epilepsy can perfectly mimic psychiatric conditions.** A young woman treated for anxiety disorder for years turned out to have amygdala seizures generating spontaneous, trigger-free panic attacks. This was undetectable on MRI; only intracranial electrode monitoring confirmed the diagnosis. Seizures hijack whatever function the affected area normally performs — temporal lobe seizures can produce metallic tastes, burning smells, and déjà vu for the same anatomical reason. [00:24:26](transcript.md#002426-emotion-anxiety--epilepsy)

16. **Aerobic exercise and surgical focus share the same restorative mechanism.** Chang's primary mental-regulation tool is daily running or swimming — not for fitness but to disconnect from all external demands and enter singular attentional focus. The operating room functions identically: no phone, rote motor sequences, total immersion in one problem. He notices measurable deterioration in operating performance and interpersonal quality after even one or two missed exercise days. [02:22:55](transcript.md#022255-tools-practices-for-maintaining-calm-under-extreme-demands)

## Tools & Protocols

### Aerobic Exercise for Mental Regulation
- **What:** Daily aerobic exercise — running or swimming — used primarily for mental and state-regulation benefit, not physical fitness.
- **How:** Daily or near-daily sessions. Chang notices measurable change in operating room performance and interpersonal quality after even 1–2 missed days.
- **When:** Consistent daily habit; especially important before high-demand cognitive or surgical work.
- **Ref:** [02:22:55](transcript.md#022255-tools-practices-for-maintaining-calm-under-extreme-demands)

### Deep Focus / Attentional Disconnection
- **What:** Extended periods of total attentional immersion with zero external interruptions that function as a nervous-system reset.
- **How:** Chang leaves his phone outside the operating room and treats surgery as complete disconnection from the outside world — the same quality of focus as running. The common thread is singular demanding attention with no multitasking.
- **When:** Built into daily routine (exercise) and each surgical case.
- **Ref:** [02:22:55](transcript.md#022255-tools-practices-for-maintaining-calm-under-extreme-demands)

### Alcohol Avoidance
- **What:** Complete avoidance of alcohol as a sustained cognitive and surgical performance strategy.
- **How:** Ongoing lifestyle choice framed explicitly as a performance tool, not a moral stance. Mentioned alongside caffeine avoidance.
- **When:** Ongoing.
- **Ref:** [02:22:55](transcript.md#022255-tools-practices-for-maintaining-calm-under-extreme-demands)

### White Noise Machine Caution for Infants
- **What:** Limiting or avoiding continuous all-night white noise in infant sleep environments to preserve auditory critical-period development.
- **How:** White noise masks the structured speech sounds the infant brain needs to tune native-language phoneme maps. Chang chose not to use white noise for his own three children, opting instead for more natural, structured sounds. Shorter, lower-volume, or intermittent use is less likely to be disruptive but remains unstudied in humans.
- **When:** During infancy — the primary postnatal critical period for language sound acquisition.
- **Ref:** [00:13:10](transcript.md#001310-white-noise-machines-infant-sleep--sensitization)

### Fluency-Shaping Therapy for Stutter
- **What:** Behavioral speech therapy that directly retrains the speech motor coordination system, not just anxiety management.
- **How:** Techniques target slowing articulation rate, managing airflow, controlling initiation, and building new motor patterns. Auditory feedback modification (changing what the person hears while speaking) can also modulate fluency. Delivered by a speech-language pathologist.
- **When:** Best started early in childhood while neuroplasticity is robust; adult therapy also effective.
- **Ref:** [02:17:35](transcript.md#021735-stutter-anxiety--treatment)

### Phonological Awareness Intervention for Dyslexia
- **What:** Reading interventions targeting the auditory-phonological system rather than the visual system alone, based on the understanding that dyslexia in many children reflects a deficit in mapping visual word forms to speech sounds.
- **How:** Orton-Gillingham-based and multisensory phonemic awareness programs that explicitly link letter shapes to phoneme sounds. Skilled readers develop a direct visual-to-meaning route over time, but this phonological foundation must be established first.
- **When:** Early childhood intervention (before age 8) produces substantially better outcomes.
- **Ref:** [01:28:33](transcript.md#012833-reading--writing-dyslexia--treatments)
