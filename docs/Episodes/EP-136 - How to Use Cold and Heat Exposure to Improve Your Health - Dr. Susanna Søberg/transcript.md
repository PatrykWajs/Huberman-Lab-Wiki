## [00:07:40] Physiology in Uncomfortably Cold Environments

**Andrew Huberman:** Welcome — thank you, so great to have you here. I feel like I should give a little bit of the backstory of how we got connected. For many years I've been interested in cold thermogenesis — it was the topic of my senior thesis in college — and I've followed the popularity of Wim Hof. We've had Dr. Craig Heller from the biology department at Stanford who works on cold and its impact on physiology and sports performance. But there's been a real lack of new, high-profile, quality scientific information about how cold plunges and sauna impact human physiology. My first question: what is happening when we get into an uncomfortably cold environment?

**Susanna Søberg:** Thank you for that question. It's really good to address what actually happens in our physiology when we get cold. You can get cold in many ways — the most potent stressor is submerging into cold water, but you could also go outside in the cold wind, which is also going to activate your sympathetic nervous system and get all these neurotransmitters going in your body. If you are very hot before you go into cold water, it's going to feel less stressful because your core temperature is warmer, but the temperature difference from your skin to the cold water will still give you a shock. That's why when people go into a sauna first and then into cold water, they can do it more easily than if they were cold beforehand.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned the sympathetic nervous system — that's the branch of our nervous system responsible for creating accelerations in heart rate, feelings of alertness, and the stress response. And you mentioned the catecholamines — dopamine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine. Maybe a little later we'll talk about those individual neurotransmitters, but you raised an important point...

## [00:12:05] Tool: Water Temperature, "Cold Shock" & Discomfort

**Andrew Huberman:** ...which is something I get asked about a lot: how cold should the water be? I know it's very hard to give a straight prescription for that because it boils down to what you just said — the difference between your current skin temperature and the temperature of the water. Is there any way to gauge what is the best approach? Should it feel uncomfortable?

**Susanna Søberg:** That's a really good question and I definitely think this could be the subject of future studies to really unravel which protocols are the best and for which outcomes. If the temperature is very cold and you feel very cold, you should stay in the water a little bit longer. But as long as you get uncomfortably cold, it's cold enough — you get what we call the cold shock, which is activation of your sympathetic nervous system and the catecholamines.

**Andrew Huberman:** Does the shock mean that I'm having trouble controlling my breathing? Is that a good gauge?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes, you can say so, because that's kind of how we define it — you hyperventilate, you have a faster breathing rate. That increases because you activate your gasping reflex if you are new to this. But if you are adapted, it kind of subsides with time. You can train this cold exposure and get adapted to it so you don't have this hyperventilating response every time. Building up your resilience and adaptation is going to make this shock subside. It's always harder in the beginning. You should do hard things — cold water immersion should not be comfortable. If you enjoy it, then I think something is not right. It should be hard, because that's the point of it.

**Andrew Huberman:** This is an important point. Many people shy away from deliberate cold exposure because it's uncomfortable in a way that is very different from the discomfort of exercise. With exercise you can slow down or reduce the weight. With deliberate cold exposure it's kind of all or none. I've done some work with military special operations personnel and these are very tough individuals. When asked to do cold water exposure training, it was remarkable — about a third just went straight in and looked stoic, about a third talked a lot and were agitated but made it through, and about a third would not get in past their knees or thighs. You couldn't tell from their physical appearance who would have which response. It seems like people vary tremendously in their ability to embrace the discomfort of cold. Is that your experience as well?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes. Some people just feel better in the cold and some dread it even more. Some of those soldiers might have already been adapted to the cold so they embrace it more easily. It could also be that some people have a more sensitive nervous system. When you avoid the cold, you will also feel the cold more painfully when you finally do go in — so the more you avoid the cold, the more painful it will feel when you expose yourself to it.

## [00:17:37] Cold Showers vs. Immersion in Water, Brown Fat

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned being outside in a t-shirt versus cold immersion to the neck versus cold showers. What are the differences in terms of impact, short-term and long-term?

**Susanna Søberg:** There are different outcomes because they are very different exposures of cold to your cold receptors in the skin. The more you cover your body in cold — which you do in cold water because the water molecules are closer to your skin — the more potent activation of all your cold receptors. That will definitely activate your autonomic nervous system more rapidly compared to going out in a t-shirt in cold wind. But going for a walk in cold air is also going to activate your sympathetic nervous system and increase norepinephrine and activate brown fat — a healthy kind of fat tissue that we have in our body that, when activated, increases your metabolism.

Cold showers are somewhere in between. If we had more studies on cold showers we would learn more about how they activate metabolism and increase neurotransmitters in the brain. But what we do know from rodent studies and in humans is that as soon as we get cold on our skin we activate our brown fat. Brown fat is our first responder to keep our temperature up, and muscles are the second tissue that can increase thermogenesis — they shiver secondarily to start increasing temperature. So as soon as you go into a cold shower, you will activate your brown fat immediately, which should be good for increasing metabolism. The reason there are fewer studies of cold showers than cold immersion is partly methodological — it's just harder to control cold shower conditions in a laboratory setting.

## [00:22:11] Cold Receptors, Brown Fat & Temperature Homeostasis

**Andrew Huberman:** It sounds like any form of cold to the skin that people register as uncomfortable or jarring activates the brown fat. Do we know what the pathway is from cold receptor on the skin to the brown fat?

**Susanna Søberg:** The cold receptors send a signal to our temperature-regulating center in the brain — the hypothalamus — and that's going to take in this message very rapidly. As you immerse the body into cold water, there is a rapid increase in neurotransmitters: noradrenaline, adrenaline, and a little bit of cortisol. The noradrenaline will then immediately activate the brown fat — it's the most potent activator of brown fat. There is also a direct pathway from the cold receptor in the skin directly to the brown fat, showing that this tissue was developed in our evolution to keep us warm and save us whenever skin temperature varies. Brown fat is also activated when we get warmer on our skin, so it may also function as a temperature regulator in both directions. There is also a third pathway: from the muscles to the brown fat. So we have three parallel pathways — cold skin to hypothalamus to brown fat, cold receptors directly to brown fat, and shivering muscles to brown fat — all working together to keep us from suffering too much in cold water.

## [00:25:22] Shiver, "After Drop", Healthy Stress

**Andrew Huberman:** I've heard that shivering causes the release of succinate, which then activates brown fat. Is it important to induce shivering, and does it matter when shivering happens?

**Susanna Søberg:** Shivering is good because that increases your metabolism and burns some calories. You shouldn't be afraid of shivering — as long as you don't get too hypothermic by staying in too long. When you shiver after you get out, that is because of the "after drop." The after drop is when your core temperature decreases even after you get out of cold water. It always does this: as soon as you get into cold water, your blood vessels constrict to keep blood in your core and keep your vital organs warm. As soon as you get up, those blood vessels open again, the warm blood flows out, gets cooled at the surface, and then flows back into the core — and that's going to decrease your core temperature. That's what induces shivering after you get out.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the shiver then activates brown fat, which warms you back up?

**Susanna Søberg:** Exactly. That's why you should end on the cold — but we can get back to that.

Shivering is just like training. It's training for all your cells, your muscles, your metabolism — it increases what's called insulin sensitivity. Think of shivering the same way you think of going to the training center. It's going to hurt a little bit, but it's a different way of training your cells in your body. It creates what is called healthy stress — hormesis in the cells. The more you expose your muscle cells or your brown fat cells to healthy stresses — exercise, cold and heat exposure — the more robust they become, increasing heat shock proteins and cold shock proteins to make you more resilient for next time. It's the same process as exercise, just different practices.

## [00:31:08] Long-Term Health Benefits of Deliberate Cold Exposure

**Andrew Huberman:** What are some of the longer-term changes that are known to occur in the hours and day after deliberate cold exposure?

**Susanna Søberg:** Every time you go in, you get a little bit more adapted — just like exercise makes you a little bit stronger. The brown fat becomes more activated, the mitochondria in the brown fat cells become more numerous and more efficient at heating you up because the body expects you to do this again. The capillaries in your skin also become better at constricting, giving you a better shield for the next exposure. Over time, your stress response will also subside — you'll have a less intense increase of your catecholamines with repeated exposures.

You'll also have an increase in metabolism from the activation of brown fat and muscles, which then makes your insulin sensitivity better. There's an interesting study from Keppler Storck from around 2016 where they measured insulin sensitivity in middle-aged men and women during one winter swimming season of four to five months. They saw that they had lower blood pressure after the season, a lower heart rate, and better insulin sensitivity. I figured that brown fat could be the missing link explaining why we see less inflammation in the body. The long-term outcomes include lower blood pressure, lower heart rate, better insulin sensitivity, and better glucose balance.

## [00:38:26] Brown Fat, Insulin Sensitivity & Metabolism

**Andrew Huberman:** I'd love to drill into brown fat at a deep level. My understanding is that it's located in specific areas of the body — at the clavicles, the back of the neck, the upper back — is rich in mitochondria, and is more widespread in children. Can you tell us more about the biology, distribution, whether it can expand as adults, and what makes it so special?

**Susanna Søberg:** You are not wrong, but it's true that there are more locations of brown fat than we previously thought. Brown fat is very plastic — it can grow and it can decrease. This is proven in studies involving pheochromocytoma, a specific type of benign tumor on the adrenal gland. People with this tumor have a huge continuous increase in noradrenaline, which causes a continuous activation of brown fat — and their brown fat grows substantially throughout the body in six different locations. They also have high blood pressure, and they lost a lot of weight because of the metabolic activation. When the benign tumor is removed, the brown fat decreases again to normal size, blood pressure normalizes, and they regain weight. This is proof of concept that brown fat can grow. It's plastic.

Studies also show that after age 40, there is an association with having less brown fat and increased obesity. Brown fat is an insulin-sensitive organ — just as muscles become less insulin-sensitive with obesity, so does brown fat. There are studies showing that people who work outside — like farmers — tend to keep more brown fat because they're exposed to natural temperature variation. Insulin sensitivity is a very good thing: you want your cells to be sensitive to insulin. Insulin insensitivity is type 2 diabetes.

## [00:45:07] Temperature Regulation, Brown Fat vs. White Fat

**Andrew Huberman:** Do we need an extreme stressor like cold shock to activate brown fat, or is just staying active enough?

**Susanna Søberg:** You can activate brown fat with just a little bit of exposure to cold. Cold is the most potent activator of brown fat because it's our temperature-regulating organ. Studies have shown that if you just put your hand in cold water, you can activate your brown fat — it just shows that a temperature change on your skin is sufficient. Going outside in a t-shirt, wearing cooling vests — studies have shown that 10 days of this will also grow your brown fat. You can also sleep in the cold: a study from Hansen et al. from 2017 showed that subjects who slept in a room at 19°C for one month had increased insulin sensitivity. The next month sleeping at 24°C, they measured less activation, and at 27°C even less. The brown fat is so sensitive to noradrenaline that it responds very rapidly to mild cold stimuli.

Brown fat grows mitochondria — these small energy factories that take up glucose and fatty acids from the bloodstream to generate heat. In that way, brown fat can decrease unhealthy white fat. White fat is our energy storage and is located on our thighs and around inner organs. We just don't need too much of it. The key is that exposing yourself to various temperatures activates brown fat because it evolved to keep us in perfect homeostatic balance — to keep us alive.

Regarding the 19°C sleeping study — I think it's the cold on the face that's activating the increase in brown fat, even though subjects were under a comforter. You have so many cold receptors in your face. I did an experiment myself where subjects put their hand in 4°C water for four minutes and I measured the brown fat with an infrared thermography camera. After just a few minutes I could see an increase in temperature from brown fat activation. Brown fat is very close to the skin surface, which is also why it's there — to heat your inner organs rapidly.

## [00:52:26] Cold Resilience, Scandinavia

**Andrew Huberman:** When I started working in a cold physiology laboratory as an undergraduate, my mentor Harry Carlile — who came from a lineage including Rothwell and Stock, who discovered non-exercise-induced thermogenesis — said to wear a t-shirt in there for two or three days and you'll cold-adapt. After just two days, I could come back on the third day and be perfectly comfortable. I also got very lean during that time. There's a saying in Scandinavia about wearing fewer layers approaching winter so you prepare yourself for the cold, and more layers in spring approaching summer so you're better at cooling. Do you know that saying?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes, we say you should wear less before winter and more before summer. And you're completely right. Around the 1950s, the Russian government wanted to address tuberculosis and increase immune resilience, so in Scandinavia — and also in Russia — people put their babies outside to sleep in prams. We still do that in Denmark. My two boys have been sleeping outside in winter for their first few years because it's very good for them — they get a better immune system and get resilient to the cold. They run around in t-shirts when it's super cold. I didn't fully understand it at the time, but we have inherited this way of doing things through our culture.

When we were in Copenhagen last June, I noticed that people in Copenhagen are dramatically fitter than in the United States. Everyone is bicycling, not wearing sunglasses to extract as much sunlight as possible, and the winter swimmers ranged from fit triathlete-looking types to young kids to people probably in their 70s, 80s, maybe even 90s — vastly different from what you see at the ocean in Los Angeles.

## [01:00:16] Winter Swimmers & Brown Fat; Discomfort

**Andrew Huberman:** Could you tell us about your study — what motivated it, what you did, who the subjects were, what you measured, and what you discovered?

**Susanna Søberg:** When I started this research in 2016, I was very interested in preventive medicine. I read up on all the brown fat literature and got hired in a fantastic research group that mostly did cell studies but really wanted someone to do a human study. I found the sleeping-in-cold paper by Hansen et al. very fascinating and thought, well, this has already been done — but I wanted something that also had people moving, doing something together. Winter swimming came up, maybe as a bit of a joke at first, but when I read the literature I couldn't find anything about activation of brown fat with cold water other than the hand-in-a-bucket study. So cold water immersion should be a very potent activator of brown fat — but nobody had directly studied it.

Because we didn't have the funding for a large study, we did a proof-of-concept study with a smaller number of participants but dug deeper into different mechanisms. I also took white fat biopsies to see if there were any differences between groups. The first year was a field study — I was not a winter swimmer when I started. I was always cold, wearing big socks and sweaters. But I started thinking, well, if this is so healthy in theory, I should start exposing myself. The winter swimmers on the jetty joked: "You cannot study this unless you have tried it." So I tried it. The first few times were not comfortable — it felt painful, like running too long after a long break and regretting taking that extra mile.

**Andrew Huberman:** Was the discomfort when you got in, or also afterward?

**Susanna Søberg:** It was very uncomfortable only at the moment of being in. Afterwards, as soon as I got out, I felt fantastic. We went into the sauna and I did three rounds because I loved the feeling. You have all these neurotransmitters going in your brain — you feel more positive, invigorated, with so much energy. I could totally see why people do this to get energy throughout the day. I often now do one or two dips per week, and that is enough to get that energy and positive feeling. That is also why I set up my study to look at the lowest dose — the lowest amount you can get away with and still see health benefits.

Some of the subjects I observed stayed in for very long times — 20 minutes or half an hour. To me that seemed extreme. If you're not adapted, that's not a good idea. It's going to exhaust your cells and make them age too fast. When you pass that hormetic stress level, the healthy stress level, the quite opposite happens.

## [01:10:28] Sex Differences & Brown Fat, Cold-Adapted

**Susanna Søberg:** I found that if I wanted to get this protocol through an ethical committee, I really needed to keep things very controlled and make sure participants were healthy. What I did was recruit winter swimmers who had already been swimming for two or three seasons — already adapted to the cold — and compare them to a matched control group matched on diet and BMI. We chose one gender in this study — specifically to eliminate confounding factors — because women have more brown fat than men.

**Andrew Huberman:** Really? Why is that?

**Susanna Søberg:** I think it's interesting because women are also smaller in size and mass, but they also have a lower peripheral temperature, especially on hands, ears, and feet, compared to men. Men have bigger hearts than women and can pump out more blood peripherally, which may explain warmer extremities in men. The thermocomfortable state is also different between genders: men are thermocomfortable at 22°C and women at 24°C.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the thermostat wars at home have now been validated.

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes! It's a 2-degree difference. This is also one reason we chose one gender for the proof-of-concept study — with limited funding, having four groups was not feasible. So I recruited winter swimmers who had been swimming two to three seasons, doing two to three times per week, and who only stayed in the water for one to two minutes.

## [01:15:21] Diving Reflex & Parasympathetic Activation

**Susanna Søberg:** After one or two minutes of cold water immersion, the initial cold shock response subsides and you get activation of your rest-and-digest system — the parasympathetic nervous system. You get that activation because when you submerge into cold water you have activation of the diving response, which slows down oxygen consumption in your body and slows your heart rate.

**Andrew Huberman:** I've heard that when we put our face in cold water or go underwater, the parasympathetic response is activated — a calming, relaxation response — whereas the initial sympathetic response comes from the cold shock. Does this bring up whether submerging your head changes the physiological outcome?

**Susanna Søberg:** The diving reflex is activated when you submerge into cold water even just to the neck — you don't necessarily have to get your face under. When you activate your diving response, you slow down your oxygen consumption because the body tries to preserve oxygen so you won't get hypothermic too fast — it's a survival system. After maybe one to two minutes you will have full activation of both the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems. That's going to activate serotonin in your brain as well, which is good for mental balance — this corresponds to what people report feeling after they get up. We need more studies on these neurochemical effects.

## [01:18:44] Tool: Deliberate Cold & Sauna Protocol

**Andrew Huberman:** So the winter swimmers are climbing down a ladder into cold water outdoors, up to their neck, staying in for one to two minutes, and then getting out. How many times a week, and what does the full protocol look like?

**Susanna Søberg:** They do this two to three times per week. For each session they take three rounds — three cold dips and two sauna sessions. They start in the cold and they end in the cold.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the sequence is: cold water for one to two minutes, then into the sauna?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes. The sauna was about 80°C. They stayed in the sauna for 10 to 15 minutes. Then back into the cold for up to two minutes, then back into the sauna for another 10 to 15 minutes, then back into the cold for a third round, ending on cold.

**Andrew Huberman:** How cold was the water on average?

**Susanna Søberg:** It's a very uncontrolled environment — outdoor winter swimming — but I measured the temperature every time they went. The water starts around 12°C in October, goes down to about 2°C on average in January, then back up. So it was within a spectrum of 2°C to about 15°C. It doesn't have to be that cold to be good enough to activate metabolism.

**Andrew Huberman:** What time of day did participants do this?

**Susanna Søberg:** I didn't control for time of day — I wanted them to go whenever they had time. The most important message to give people is: do it when you have time. If 6 p.m. after work is your only window, try it — if it doesn't impact your sleep then fine, but you have to find out what works for you. Just like coffee or exercise, cold water immersion activates the sympathetic nervous system and can make it hard to fall asleep for some people, though not everyone.

## [01:23:11] Winter Swimmers, Shiver; Circadian Rhythm & Brown Fat

**Andrew Huberman:** What did you observe in terms of changes in brown fat, how quickly did it occur, and what about insulin sensitivity and metabolism?

**Susanna Søberg:** Besides measuring skin temperature as an outcome for brown fat activity, we did PET-MRI scanning of the brown fat — the gold standard. PET-CT or PET-MRI scanning of brown fat is very expensive and not feasible for most people, but we used it to see brown fat activation during cold stimulation and also to see what kind of circadian rhythm we have in brown fat activity. I also wanted to see if the brown fat was activated during thermoneutral conditions — how comfortable are they away from the cold and sauna? I used a visual analog scale, asking subjects where they are from 1 to 10 on comfort with the temperature (1 = very cold, 10 = burning hot, 5 = thermocomfortable).

I also measured electromyography to see if subjects were shivering during the cooling day, because sometimes people shiver before they consciously know they're shivering. What I found was that the winter swimmers were shivering less — or having less vigorous shivering — even when their perception of cold was similar to the control group. The adapted subjects' shivering was so small they may not even notice it, because the mitochondria in their muscle cells are so dense they don't need to shiver as vigorously to generate thermogenesis.

What we saw was that winter swimmers had increased insulin sensitivity — they produced less insulin on all experimental days. When we gave subjects a glucose drink — a standard glucose tolerance test — the winter swimmers had faster glucose clearance in the bloodstream. After two hours their blood glucose level was lower and the curve went down faster than in the control group. Despite having lower insulin release, they had better blood glucose clearance, which is really what you want. Excessive insulin is bad, and high blood glucose is very neurotoxic — it can damage brain cells. The range in which neurons are happy is not a tremendously large range, and incidentally neurons survive better when getting cold than when getting too hot.

## [01:31:14] Tool: Minimum Threshold for Cold & Heat; Sauna & Cardiovascular Health

**Andrew Huberman:** If I do the math — three rounds of cold for one to two minutes, two to three times a week — what were the thresholds that you discovered were important for getting the positive changes in brown fat, insulin sensitivity, and metabolism?

**Susanna Søberg:** When we calculated the numbers, this ended up being 11 minutes of cold exposure in total per week, divided across two to three visits. That corresponds to being in cold water for one to two minutes at a time. For the sauna it was 57 minutes in total per week — 10 to 15 minutes at a time.

This is very similar to what we see in observational studies from the Finnish cohort study by Laukkanen et al. published in 2015, which showed that up to 19 minutes in the sauna has a dose-response relationship with decreasing risk of cardiovascular disease. The threshold for meaningful benefit was around 19 minutes per session, and going further than 30 minutes per session showed no additional health benefits. Beyond 30 minutes you may even exhaust the cells and increase cardiovascular risk. So there's a window of healthy stress: roughly 10 minutes per session seems to be enough, and you don't have to expose yourself very much — either to heat or to cold — to get meaningful health benefits.

The key message is that you don't have to go extreme. You don't have to swim for half an hour in cold water. One to two minutes per session, reaching 11 minutes total per week for cold, and 57 minutes total per week for sauna, is the minimum effective dose.

## [01:35:19] Tool: Maintaining Stimulus when Cold-Adapted; Shorter Sessions

**Andrew Huberman:** As one becomes more cold-adapted, do the benefits start to wear off? I understand the threshold is 11 minutes total per week divided into two or three sessions of one to three minutes. Is it the case that if somebody did one 11-minute session per week it might not be as beneficial as dividing it up — because the hormetic response depends on having that cold shock? Once you're deep into a session, you've already had the autonomic stimulus.

**Susanna Søberg:** Exactly. The goal is not to get so cold-adapted that you can sit in for the full 11 minutes in one session. If the goal is to improve these health metrics, the idea is to keep the stimulus a stimulus — short exposures, more frequent — so that every single time it acts as a meaningful stimulus. Having watched the landscape of this on social media and in books, I think I'm touching on something important: the longer sessions might not just not be beneficial but might actually be detrimental. There is practical feasibility too — a couple of minutes in cold immersion is far less challenging for most people than spending a full morning at a winter swimming club.

## [01:38:09] Cold Exposure, Sleep Quality, Clothing

**Andrew Huberman:** Were there any other observations that didn't make it into the paper — psychological benefits, or anything that stood out?

**Susanna Søberg:** I regret that I didn't measure sleep quality in a formal way. But anecdotally, the winter swimmers told me they had really good sleep. The control group also said they slept well, but mentioned they sometimes woke up briefly during the nights I had them in the lab — whereas the winter swimmers reported really good, uninterrupted sleep.

I also observed that winter swimmers were very comfortable being cold and less scared of showing their skin — a kind of reduced social anxiety. One winter swimmer, after eight hours in the study, walked out completely naked without caring at all — he just went out into the hallway. That's a side effect of getting very comfortable with the cold.

**Andrew Huberman:** In your book you dedicate some pages to naked winter swimming versus with a bathing suit. Is there any data on clothed versus unclothed cold exposure?

**Susanna Søberg:** If you have a skinny bikini on, it's not going to make any difference to your cold exposure, adaptation, or benefits. But I think there's something else — a sense of freedom in skinny dipping. In Denmark, the oldest winter swimming clubs have men and women swimming naked and sharing saunas. It has something to do with how you absorb your surroundings and feel in touch with nature. Newer clubs tend to require bathing suits. There is no physiological requirement for one or the other.

## [01:47:37] "Brown Fat Negative" & Shiver

**Susanna Søberg:** One observation that's in the back of the paper — one of the winter swimmers had zero brown fat when we scanned him. We see this occasionally in previous studies too. He was not obese. What I observed during the cooling experiment was that before I knew he had no brown fat, he was unable to control his shivering the way the other winter swimmers could. He got very cold very easily, and his temperature was all over the place — we had to keep adjusting. We all noticed something was different about him. When we scanned him and didn't find any brown fat, I initially blamed the technology. The PET-CT team reanalyzed the scan and confirmed it was correct — he simply had no brown fat. He was what we call "brown fat negative" — a natural knockout.

His blood samples also looked more like the control group: his insulin levels were like the control group, slightly higher than the other winter swimmers, and his glucose clearance was not as fast. He was an outlier in the analysis. The implication is that if you shiver early and struggle to regulate your temperature, you may have less brown fat — or you haven't adapted to the cold yet, and you should build that up through regular exposure.

## [01:52:13] Cold & Heat, Inflammation Reduction

**Andrew Huberman:** Were there any markers related to immune function or broader benefits that you examined?

**Susanna Søberg:** We looked at inflammation — we measured IL-6, an anti-inflammatory marker, and IL-10 as well. Both went up and follow each other, as is well known in the literature. It's very important to think about cold and heat exposure as things that lower inflammation in the body, because if we can do that we open a door to preventing lifestyle diseases — type 2 diabetes, but also some mental diseases, including depression, anxiety, and Alzheimer's disease, all of which are associated with inflammation in the research literature.

The beautiful thing is that we can go out in nature and use these natural stressors — just exposure to temperature, cold or heat — to tweak our body into a more natural state. Our modern sedentary lifestyle, with better-insulated houses and less outdoor activity, has made us increasingly temperature-comfortable and temperature-neutral. Obesity increases started in the 1980s, when more comfortable lifestyles became widespread. Cold exposure, heat exposure, exercise, and even a bit of fasting all increase hormetic stress in the body — natural stresses that could keep us in that natural balance.

## [01:55:40] Tool: "Søberg Principle": End on Cold, Metabolism

**Andrew Huberman:** Could we talk about what I refer to as the Søberg Principle — ending on cold? In reviewing your paper I noticed that subjects end on cold, and this has been a longstanding debate: should you warm up with a warm shower afterward or end on cold? The Søberg Principle says to end on cold, in order to force your body to heat itself back up and thereby increase metabolism further still. Is that right?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes. When you end on cold, you force your body to heat up by itself. That will require that you keep your brown fat activated and also your muscles — a good collaboration to keep your thermogenesis up. In that way, you don't have to think of your cold dip as just a one-to-two-minute exercise — if you end on cold you have an exercise for your body going on for hours afterward. That's not only for your metabolism but also for keeping your neurotransmitters activated, because your body still needs them to activate the brown fat. That is going to make your brown fat cells and muscle cells more efficient and increase mitochondria in the cells, which will then generate heat very fast.

After three to five times of doing this — even if you're new — you will notice a switch where you get warmer more easily. I showed in my study that the winter swimmers were physically warmer on the skin compared to the control group, even when resting. They had a more activated brown fat, higher heat loss from the body — probably because they have more vascular skin due to the contrast of cold and heat — but that's not a bad thing. It actually means their body has to work to keep them warm, which keeps the system tuned. I would also hypothesize this contributes to subjectively reported improvements in sleep, because in order to fall asleep you need your core body temperature to drop by about 1 to 3°C. By forcing your body to heat itself up naturally, you may also make it better at cooling down later when needed.

## [01:59:39] Cold Exposure: Fed or Fasted?

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any known benefits of doing deliberate cold exposure fasted versus after a meal?

**Susanna Søberg:** I measured glucose on the cooling days as well — specifically fasting and on cold exposure days. But I don't think I've seen studies specifically on the question of fed versus fasted during cold exposure. More science is needed on this specific topic.

## [02:00:32] Raynaud's Syndrome; Hand/Feet Protection in Cold

**Andrew Huberman:** A lot of people ask whether cold exposure could help people with Raynaud's syndrome — which leads to very poor blood flow to the extremities and very cold, sometimes blue fingertips. Could cold exposure improve the elasticity and plasticity of small capillaries and vessels?

**Susanna Søberg:** I haven't seen any studies directed specifically at this outcome and measuring Raynaud's syndrome. Logically, exposing the hands and feet to cold and heat alternation to make the vessels more vascular would help, and I have heard from people saying it has helped them — but I've also heard from others saying it didn't help them. Studies are needed on this specific topic.

As a practical tip: I hurt my hands when I go into the cold, so I keep my hands above the water. If people suffer from pain in the fingers, just take the hands up a bit from the water — that's going to help you stay in long enough to get the full exposure. Neoprene boots can also help for feet pain. There is no problem with keeping hands out or feet in neoprene booties if that's what removes the barrier to doing cold exposure — because your full body still has cold receptors providing activation. Hands and feet are very potent places to get fast activation of your nervous system, but your full body coverage in cold water provides sufficient activation regardless.

## [02:05:21] Tool: Headache & Cold Exposure; Head Submersion & Head Coverings

**Andrew Huberman:** Sometimes I or others will get a back-of-the-head headache at the water interface during cold immersion to the neck. Do we know the origin of these headaches?

**Susanna Søberg:** I haven't specifically heard about that one, but one reason to keep your head out relates to blood flow. Studies from Bispebjerg Hospital in Denmark have shown that when you submerge into cold water up to the neck at 0°C, you will have a decreased blood flow to the brain of around 30 to 40%. This makes sense — the sympathetic nervous system activates, blood flow is maintained to the heart and muscles to keep you alive, but it is taken away from the brain. That's also why it's hard to think when you're stressed.

If you then also dunk your head, you increase that decrease in blood flow to the brain even further. A research group in Canada has collectively looked at papers comparing head-submerged versus neck-only immersion, and found that submerging to the neck results in 11% heat loss from the body core, but dunking the head increases that heat loss rate by 36%. So dunking the head brings you significantly closer to hypothermia than neck-only immersion.

As for wearing a cap or beanie — I have slightly sensitive ears, and in Denmark we have a lot of wind. When you get out of cold water with wind, you can get dizzy or get an immediate brain freeze. The beanie keeps your head a little warmer so you can stay for one to two minutes without the additional discomfort and dizziness from wind chill.

An interesting related point: in hot saunas, people from Eastern Europe or Finland or Russia often wear wool caps — not to make it hotter, but to insulate the brain from the heat. The sense of urgency to get out of a hot sauna is a brain-driven mechanism. Insulating the brain from heat lets you stay in the sauna longer without triggering that urgent exit signal — which is why putting a towel over your head in the sauna also helps you stay in more comfortably and for longer.

## [02:11:29] Children & Hypothermia Risk

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any data on children doing deliberate cold exposure and sauna? And are there protocols that parents should know about?

**Susanna Søberg:** Children are smaller than adults, so we cannot completely transfer all information, benefits, and protocols to children. A study compared heat loss in 12-year-old boys versus adults during one to two minutes of cold immersion to the neck. The boys could defend their core temperature in a similar way to adults, but they had to use their muscles much faster — meaning they couldn't stay as long and used more energy to defend their core temperature. They will also be colder when they come out because of their larger surface-to-mass ratio. Children can generally handle up to about one minute, but they will reach hypothermia faster than adults. Simply put: they are smaller and can't defend their temperature for very long.

**Andrew Huberman:** This is a real concern. I once brought a 16-year-old intern — lean, excellent swimmer — on a Pacific Ocean swim without wetsuits. He became hypothermic. He didn't report feeling overly cold, but he started slurring his words, became semi-euphoric, was drooling a bit — classic hypothermia. Fortunately we got him to shore and heated him up. This is why you always want to ocean swim with a buddy. Hypothermia is no joke, and smaller-bodied people are at greater risk. A good reason to approach deliberate cold exposure with caution — start with cold showers, then cold immersion, and always be very careful in natural bodies of water with currents.

## [02:17:16] Gender Differences & Cold Exposure

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any additional male-female differences we should be aware of for deliberate cold exposure protocols?

**Susanna Søberg:** There are differences. If we replicated my study in women, it could be that they would see enough benefit with just 9 minutes per week — because women apparently run colder and have more brown fat and increased brown fat metabolism. But I honestly don't know, and I wouldn't want to give out a specific protocol for women without data. Women also do cold exposure and winter swimming with the 11-minute protocol and feel good about it. For very brief cold exposures — the "micro-stressing" approach — the physiology is similar enough that women can look at my protocol as a good starting point.

I always say that if you really dread the cold and don't like it, you are a perfect candidate for deliberate cold exposure — your sympathetic stress response will be greater and the adaptation from that short one-to-two-minute exposure will be much greater. For people who are perfectly comfortable in the cold, it's harder to get the same degree of adaptive response — similar to how very strong people need heavier weights to evoke the same adaptive stimulus.

## [02:19:57] Tool: Brief, Repeated Temperature Changes; Circadian Rhythm & Temperature

**Andrew Huberman:** If somebody only had two days a week to do cold exposure and no sauna, would you suggest they get in for one to two minutes, get out, get back in for another couple of minutes, to get multiple temperature change stimuli — rather than one longer single session?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes. The changes in temperature are what strengthen your cells. If you can do a short amount of exposure, get out, and get back in, that challenges your cells to adapt to changing temperatures — it's going to strengthen them. During one session you can do this variation. In nature, like in Denmark with four seasons, the temperature varies for us automatically. If you have a cold plunge, I would say varying the temperature up and down is what creates the hormetic stress and keeps your cells on their toes. The body treats it as something slightly toxic — a small piece of toxicity that you're touching — and adapts accordingly.

**Andrew Huberman:** This brings up circadian time. In your study you didn't control for time of day. Our body temperature shifts dramatically throughout the day — heating up as we wake, reaching a peak in the afternoon, then dropping as we prepare for sleep. Could doing deliberate cold exposure at different times of day by itself be beneficial — because your body will be at different base temperatures?

**Susanna Søberg:** Yes, that is what I keep saying coming through in all these beautiful studies — mine and others. It's not really about getting cold. It's about going from warm to cold and from cold to warm. Biology is not an event, it's a process. These metabolic and thermoregulatory processes are like the turning of a knob — a verb, not a noun. People will have a lot more flexibility, fun, and benefit if they think of it that way, rather than "I need to get into X degrees of water for X amount of time on X number of days" in a rigid way.

Logically, just changing the temperature up and down is what matters. It could be all the way up to 12°C water temperature — you're going to activate your brown fat anyway. Even 19°C cold air is enough to activate brown fat. Maybe we don't have to go as cold as many people think, and you don't have to put ice in your plunge all the time. Just vary it — keep the system off balance, keep it tuned.

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned a study that is more recent or ongoing and not yet published — if you're willing, could you share anything about it?

**Susanna Søberg:** We haven't analyzed all the data yet, so I don't really have results I can share. But what we looked at was both men and women — so that's coming.

Your study stands as a landmark in the landscape of exploring deliberate cold exposure and heat. There just haven't been that many high-resolution, detailed modern studies of this. Thank you for the incredible public education efforts on social media and with your book — we'll put links to all of those in the show note caption. We certainly need more scientists who are both experienced with doing hardcore research and who actually do the practices themselves.
