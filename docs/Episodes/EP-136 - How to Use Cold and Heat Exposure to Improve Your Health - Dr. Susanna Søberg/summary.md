# Episode 136: How to Use Cold & Heat Exposure to Improve Your Health | Dr. Susanna Søberg

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Susanna Søberg, PhD (University of Copenhagen), joins Andrew Huberman to discuss the science and protocols for deliberate cold and heat exposure. The conversation centers on Dr. Søberg's landmark 2021 Cell Reports Medicine study, which established minimum effective thresholds for cold and sauna exposure to increase brown fat thermogenesis and improve metabolism. Topics span brown fat biology, the cold shock response, diving reflex and parasympathetic activation, insulin sensitivity, cardiovascular health, the "Søberg Principle" of ending on cold, circadian temperature variation as a health principle, practical protocols, and special populations including children and people with Raynaud's syndrome.

## Guest: Dr. Susanna Søberg

Dr. Susanna Søberg completed her doctoral research at the Center of Inflammation and Metabolism and the Center for Physical Activity Research at the University of Copenhagen in Denmark. Her work focuses on how deliberate cold and heat exposure can be used to enhance human metabolism and overall health. She is the first author of a pivotal study defining minimum thresholds for cold and sauna exposure to activate brown fat thermogenesis. ## Key Takeaways

1. **Cold shock is the goal, not just cold.** The beneficial stimulus is an uncomfortably cold exposure that triggers the cold shock response — hyperventilation, sympathetic activation, and catecholamine release. If you're comfortable, it's not cold enough. Hyperventilating breath rate and a gasping reflex are signs you're in the effective range. As cold adaptation builds, this shock response subsides; the goal is to keep each exposure acting as a true stimulus. [00:12:05](transcript.md#001205-tool-water-temperature-cold-shock-discomfort)

2. **Brown fat is the key metabolic organ activated by cold.** Three parallel pathways converge on brown fat: (1) cold skin → hypothalamus → brown fat; (2) cold skin receptors → brown fat directly; (3) shivering muscle → brown fat. Noradrenaline is the most potent activator. This redundancy reflects the evolutionary importance of this tissue. Brown fat is also activated by warmth, functioning as a two-directional temperature regulator. [00:22:11](transcript.md#002211-cold-receptors-brown-fat-temperature-homeostasis)

3. **Shivering is not to be avoided — it's the training.** Shivering burns calories, improves insulin sensitivity, and creates hormetic (healthy) stress in cells. After getting out of cold water, the "after drop" causes core temperature to fall further, inducing shivering that activates brown fat to rewarm the body. Heat shock proteins and cold shock proteins accumulate with repeated exposure, making cells more resilient. Well-adapted winter swimmers shiver less visibly because their mitochondrial density is higher. [00:25:22](transcript.md#002522-shiver-after-drop-healthy-stress)

4. **Minimum effective dose: 11 min cold + 57 min sauna per week.** Dr. Søberg's study showed these thresholds — divided across 2–3 sessions — produced improved insulin sensitivity, lower blood pressure, improved glucose clearance, and increased brown fat activity. More is not necessarily better; the Laukkanen Finnish cohort data shows that beyond ~30 min per sauna session there is no additional benefit and potentially adverse effects. [01:31:14](transcript.md#013114-tool-minimum-threshold-for-cold-heat-sauna-cardiovascular-health)

5. **The Søberg Principle: always end on cold.** Ending a cold/heat session on cold forces the body to generate its own heat for hours afterward, maximizing brown fat and muscle thermogenesis, extending the neurotransmitter boost, and improving mitochondrial efficiency. Do not take a warm shower immediately after. This also likely improves sleep by training the body's natural heat-dissipation mechanism — falling asleep requires core temperature to drop ~1–3°C. [01:55:40](transcript.md#015540-tool-soeberg-principle-end-on-cold-metabolism)

6. **Frequent short sessions beat rare long sessions.** Keeping sessions short (1–2 min cold, 10–15 min sauna) maintains the shock stimulus. As cold adaptation occurs, the catecholamine spike and cardiovascular activation attenuate — so a single long weekly session will lose stimulus value faster than multiple short sessions. The goal is to keep every exposure acting as a meaningful stimulus. [01:35:19](transcript.md#013519-tool-maintaining-stimulus-when-cold-adapted-shorter-sessions)

7. **Brown fat is plastic and trainable — and declines with age and obesity.** Brown fat can grow and shrink depending on cold exposure habits. After age 40, less brown fat is associated with increased obesity. Brown fat is an insulin-sensitive organ; insulin insensitivity (type 2 diabetes) reduces its function. Sleeping in a 19°C room for one month (Hansen et al. 2017) increased brown fat activity and insulin sensitivity. Farmers who work outdoors tend to retain more brown fat than sedentary individuals. [00:45:07](transcript.md#004507-temperature-regulation-brown-fat-vs-white-fat)

8. **Cold immersion is more potent than cold air or cold showers.** Water molecules in direct contact with skin activate cold receptors more rapidly and completely than cold air. Cold showers are intermediate but still activate brown fat immediately upon skin contact. Controlled studies on showers are limited due to methodological challenges, but rodent and human data confirm that any cold to skin activates brown fat immediately. [00:17:37](transcript.md#001737-cold-showers-vs-immersion-in-water-brown-fat)

9. **After 1–2 minutes, the diving reflex activates the parasympathetic system.** In addition to the initial sympathetic cold shock, submerging into cold water (even to the neck) activates the diving reflex, slowing oxygen consumption and heart rate. After one to two minutes, both sympathetic and parasympathetic systems are fully engaged. This dual activation likely triggers serotonin release, contributing to the mental balance and positive mood reported after getting out. [01:15:21](transcript.md#011521-diving-reflex-parasympathetic-activation)

10. **Cold and heat reduce inflammation and may protect against modern lifestyle diseases.** Markers including IL-6 and IL-10 both increased and tracked each other in Dr. Søberg's study. Inflammation links cold/heat exposure to potential benefits for type 2 diabetes, depression, anxiety, and Alzheimer's disease. Our modern thermally neutral, sedentary lifestyle has removed the natural temperature stressors our bodies evolved with; obesity rates rose in the 1980s alongside greater thermal comfort. [01:52:13](transcript.md#015213-cold-heat-inflammation-reduction)

11. **Temperature variation itself — not just the depth of cold — is the key hormetic signal.** Biology is a process, not an event. Going from warm to cold and back is more important than hitting any specific water temperature. Even 12°C water or 19°C cold air activates brown fat. Varying your plunge temperature across days, doing multiple short in-and-out cycles within a session, or doing cold exposure at different times of day (leveraging the natural circadian shift in core body temperature) all create the hormetic stress that strengthens cells. [02:19:57](transcript.md#021957-tool-brief-repeated-temperature-changes-circadian-rhythm-temperature)

12. **Women likely have more brown fat than men and run colder peripherally.** Thermocomfort baseline differs by ~2°C (men: 22°C, women: 24°C), and women have colder hands, feet, and ears, possibly due to lower cardiac output to extremities. This may mean women could see benefits with slightly less cold exposure per week (potentially ~9 min), but this is not yet confirmed by controlled data. A forthcoming study from Dr. Søberg will include both men and women. [01:10:28](transcript.md#011028-sex-differences-brown-fat-cold-adapted)

## Tools & Protocols

### Deliberate Cold Immersion Protocol (Søberg Study)

- **What:** Cold water immersion (outdoor or plunge) to the neck, alternating with sauna.
- **How:** 3 rounds per session: cold (1–2 min) → sauna (10–15 min) → cold (1–2 min) → sauna (10–15 min) → cold (1–2 min). Always start and end on cold. Water temperature between ~2–15°C. Sauna at ~80°C.
- **When:** 2–3 sessions per week, at any time of day that fits your schedule. Avoid late evening if it disrupts sleep.
- **Ref:** [01:18:44](transcript.md#011844-tool-deliberate-cold-sauna-protocol)

---

### Minimum Weekly Thresholds

- **What:** Minimum effective doses for meaningful health benefit from cold and heat exposure.
- **How:** Cold: 11 minutes total per week, divided across 2–3 sessions (1–2 min each). Sauna: 57 minutes total per week (10–15 min per session). Do not consolidate all minutes into one session — frequency matters for maintaining the cold shock stimulus. Beyond 30 min per sauna session shows no additional benefit and may be harmful.
- **When:** Ongoing as a baseline protocol.
- **Ref:** [01:31:14](transcript.md#013114-tool-minimum-threshold-for-cold-heat-sauna-cardiovascular-health)

---

### The Søberg Principle (End on Cold)

- **What:** The practice of ending any cold/heat session on cold, not on heat.
- **How:** After your final sauna round, do one final cold immersion (1–2 min) and then exit without warming up via shower or sauna. Allow your body to rewarm itself naturally. This extends thermogenesis and neurotransmitter activation for hours and likely improves sleep by training the body's natural cooling mechanism.
- **When:** Every cold/sauna session, as the final step.
- **Ref:** [01:55:40](transcript.md#015540-tool-soeberg-principle-end-on-cold-metabolism)

---

### Brief Repeated Temperature Changes

- **What:** Multiple short cold-warm transitions within a single session to maximize hormetic stress.
- **How:** Get in for 1–2 min, get out, allow partial rewarming, get back in — repeat. Works for those without sauna access. Temperature variation itself (not just depth of cold) is the key signal. Cold plunge temperature can be varied over days/weeks rather than kept constant. Varying the time of day you do cold exposure leverages the natural circadian shift in core body temperature as an additional variation stimulus.
- **When:** During each session, or between sessions if access permits.
- **Ref:** [02:19:57](transcript.md#021957-tool-brief-repeated-temperature-changes-circadian-rhythm-temperature)

---

### Maintaining Brown Fat Year-Round (Low-Intensity)

- **What:** Low-intensity cold exposure to maintain brown fat activity outside of formal cold plunge sessions.
- **How:** Wear fewer layers during autumn/winter acclimation (Scandinavian principle: wear less before winter, more before summer). Sleep in a cooler room (~19°C). Spend time outside in cold air in a light layer. Even cold air on the face activates brown fat via abundant facial cold receptors. These stimuli are sufficient to maintain and even grow brown fat between formal cold sessions.
- **When:** Daily/seasonal lifestyle habits.
- **Ref:** [00:45:07](transcript.md#004507-temperature-regulation-brown-fat-vs-white-fat), [00:52:26](transcript.md#005226-cold-resilience-scandinavia)

---

### Protecting Hands/Feet During Cold Immersion

- **What:** Strategies for people with cold-sensitive extremities (e.g., Raynaud's symptoms or general hand pain).
- **How:** Keep hands above the water surface during immersion. Use neoprene boots for foot pain. Briefly dip hands to activate hand cold receptors, then raise them. Full body immersion still provides complete cold receptor activation even without extremity immersion — there is no physiological requirement to keep hands or feet submerged.
- **When:** As needed, any cold immersion session.
- **Ref:** [02:00:32](transcript.md#020032-raynauds-syndrome-handfeet-protection-in-cold)

---

### Head Protection in Cold & Sauna

- **What:** Using a cap or beanie during cold immersion to reduce heat loss and dizziness; using a wool cap in sauna to extend session duration.
- **How:** Wear a wool or neoprene beanie during cold immersion to prevent excessive brain heat loss (head submersion increases total core heat loss by 36% vs. neck-only and reduces cerebral blood flow by 30–40%), reduce wind-driven dizziness, and allow staying in for the target duration. In sauna, wearing a wool cap insulates the brain from heat, reducing the urge to exit early and allowing longer sessions.
- **When:** Cold immersion in windy outdoor conditions; sauna for extending session duration.
- **Ref:** [02:05:21](transcript.md#020521-tool-headache-cold-exposure-head-submersion-head-coverings)
