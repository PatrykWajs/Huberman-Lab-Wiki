## [00:06:30] Stress: Short & Long-Term, Good & Bad

**Andrew Huberman:** Robert, thank you for joining us today. I've been looking forward to this for a very long time and I appreciate it. There is an enormous range of topics that we could drill into, but just to start off, I want to return to a topic that is near and dear to your heart, which is stress. One of the questions I get most commonly is: what is the difference between short and long-term stress in terms of their benefits and drawbacks? And the reason I say benefits is that stress and the stress response can keep us alive, but stress can also sharpen our mental acuity. So how should we conceptualize stress — in the short-term and in the long-term?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Well, basically there are two graphs that one would draw. The first is just all sorts of beneficial effects of stress in the short-term, and then once we get into chronicity, it's just downhill from there. Short-term it saves you from the predator, short-term because you're giving a presentation and you think more clearly or your focus is better — all sorts of aspects of that. And what winds up being an argument is: how long does it take to go from short-term to long-term? That's somewhat arbitrary, but the sorts of chronic stressors that most people deal with are just undeniably in the chronic range — like having spent the last 20 years in daily traffic jams, or an abusive boss, or some such thing.

The other curve that's sort of perpendicular to this is dealing with the fact that sometimes stress is a great thing. Our goal is not to cure people of stress, because if it's the right kind, we love it. We pay good money to be stressed that way — by a scary movie or a rollercoaster ride. What you wind up seeing is, when it's the right amount of stress, it's what we call stimulation. The basic curve there is: here is an optimal level of stimulation, and too little causes function to go down — what we would call boredom.

## [00:11:00] Testosterone: Common Myths vs. Actual Truths

**Andrew Huberman:** I heard you say once before that among all the brain areas that bind testosterone — where testosterone can park and create effects — the amygdala is among the most chockablock full of these parking spots, these receptors. How should we think about the role of testosterone in the amygdala, given that the engagement of the amygdala is fundamental in this transition point between an exhilarating positive response and a negative stressful response? Or maybe just broadly, how should we think about testosterone and its effects on the brain?

**Robert Sapolsky:** And pertinent to the transition from whether this is a stressor evoking fear or evoking aggression — also because the amygdala is in the center of all four points on those axes. Basically, almost everybody out there has a completely wrong idea as to what testosterone does, which is: testosterone makes you aggressive because males in virtually every species out there have more testosterone, and seasonal measures have testosterone surging at the time of year they're competing over territory. And you take testosterone out of the picture — you castrate any mammal out there, including us — and levels of aggression will go down. The easy thing then tends to conclude that testosterone causes aggression. And the reality is testosterone does no such thing. It doesn't cause aggression. You can see this both behaviorally and in the amygdala.

What does testosterone do? It lowers the threshold for the sort of things that would normally provoke you into aggression so that it happens more easily. It makes systems that are already turned on, turn on louder — rather than turning on aggressive music or some such thing. What does that look like behaviorally? You take five male monkeys, put them together, they form a dominance hierarchy. You now triple the testosterone level of the most submissive individual. He doesn't now suddenly start challenging the dominant male. What he does instead is he just gets more submissive faster. It's just making him more of whatever he already was. It is not turning on new behaviors.

## [00:15:15] Behaviors that Affect Testosterone

**Andrew Huberman:** And in terms of status and the relationship between individuals — either nonhuman primates or humans — can we say that relative levels of testosterone between individuals is correlated to status within the hierarchy?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Yes, but in a way that winds up being totally uninteresting. If you go back to the endocrinology texts, there were two totally reliable findings. Higher levels of testosterone predict higher levels of aggression in humans and other animals. Higher levels of testosterone predict higher levels of sexual activity. And when you look closely, we've got cause and effect backwards: sexual behavior raises testosterone levels, aggression raises testosterone levels. Your levels before were barely predictive of what's going to happen — it's a response rather than a cause. When you look at that in terms of individual differences, they don't matter a whole lot. You can spend an entire career on the social circumstances that produce 3.5% more testosterone in the circulation, and expect to see all sorts of interesting implications, but that's not really the case. It's somewhat of a yes-or-no modulator of the much more subtle social stuff that's already there.

**Andrew Huberman:** Very interesting. I think that there are a lot of misconceptions about human biology, but testosterone seems to be one area where, at least from what I can find on the internet, it's sort of at the peak of misunderstanding.

## [00:24:05] Testosterone: The Challenge Hypothesis

**Andrew Huberman:** So how should we conceptualize testosterone? I realize there isn't a single sentence that can capture a hormone in all its effects. But as I've heard you talk about testosterone today and over the years, I start to get the impression that, as the most misunderstood molecule in human health, it's clearly doing something very powerful — it's shifting the way that certain neural circuits work, adjusting the gain on the amygdala, and certainly other things as well. Is there any truism about testosterone and its relationship to effort or resilience that might help people think about it more accurately?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Yeah. Maybe three separate answers to that. The first one is, I think it's a fair summary to think that when it comes to motivated strong behaviors, what testosterone does is make you more of whatever you already are — in terms of sexual arousal, libido, aggressiveness, spontaneous and reactive aggression. It's upping the volume of things that are already strongly there.

The second way to think about it is here's my favorite finding about testosterone. This was wonderful work by a guy, John Wingfield, who's one of the best behavioral endocrinologists out there. About 20 years ago he formulated what was called The Challenge Hypothesis of Testosterone in Action. What does testosterone do? Testosterone is what you secrete when your status is being challenged, and it makes it more likely that you'll do the behaviors needed to hold onto your status. Okay, so that's totally boringly straightforward if you are a baboon. If somebody is challenging your high rank, the appropriate response is going to be aggression.

Now if you're a human, the same logic applies — testosterone goes up when your status is challenged — but the behavior needed to hold onto your status might be anything. It might be getting more aggressive, or it might be working harder, or it might be acting more compassionately, or it might be giving a better speech. Whatever the socially appropriate behavior is in that context for holding onto status, testosterone makes it more likely to come out. That's the Challenge Hypothesis. It's not causing aggression. It's making you do more of what you need to do in your particular social context.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's a beautiful framing and it explains so much. It explains why testosterone goes up in chess players before and during a match, and why it's associated with ambition and drive more broadly.

**Robert Sapolsky:** Exactly. And a third framing: testosterone is also about confidence. Studies show that people with higher testosterone are more willing to look somebody in the eye, are less likely to back down, are more willing to accept a challenge. And this isn't necessarily about being more aggressive — it's about not being more afraid of the consequences of acting boldly.

## [00:29:20] How Dopamine Impacts Testosterone & Motivation

**Andrew Huberman:** I think about testosterone and dopamine being close cousins in the brain — not just because of their relationship through the pituitary and hypothalamus — but also because of dopamine's salient role in creating this bias toward exteroception. When somebody takes a drug that increases dopamine, or they're chockablock full of dopamine, they tend — and I want to highlight 'tend' because I'm really generalizing — but they tend to focus on outward goals, things beyond the boundaries of their skin. And testosterone seems to do a bit of the same. Is there anything we can do to better conceptualize the relationship between testosterone and dopamine and motivation?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Well, I think it's got lots to do with the massive revisionism about dopamine. Everyone, since the pharaohs, got brought up being taught that dopamine is about pleasure and reward. It turns out it isn't — it's about anticipation of reward, and it's about generating the motivation, the goal-directed behavior needed to go get that reward. And before you know it, you're using elevated dopamine your entire life to motivate you to do whatever is going to get you entry into heaven in the afterlife. It's really about the motivation.

And what testosterone does — even in individuals who are not aggressive, and why testosterone replacement is often a very helpful thing for aging males — is it increases energy, it increases a sense of thereness, a presence of alertness that increases motivation. So that's a whole aspect, which then takes us into: is your motivation to get up and hand out soup in a soup kitchen for homeless people, or is it to get up and go elbow people out of the way? And that's where all the social context stuff comes in, and that's where personality and prior experience is going to determine what you're motivated to do with that elevated energy.

## [00:32:32] Estrogen: Improves Brain & Longevity BUT TIMING IS KEY

**Andrew Huberman:** We don't hear about estrogen as often, and it's always interesting to me now doing some public-facing education that testosterone is this very controversial molecule — just to say it is almost controversial — but estrogen doesn't seem to hold the same controversial weight, and yet estrogen has very powerful effects on both the animal brain and on the human brain of males and females. Men do not want their estrogen to go too low. Terrible things happen — they will lose cognitive function, libido can drop. So men need estrogen as well. Are there any general themes of estrogen that people should be aware of or that you think are generally misunderstood?

**Robert Sapolsky:** No, and it's once again very context dependent. And if estrogen after giving birth is playing a central role in you wanting to shred the face of somebody getting too close to your kittens, we know it's not just warm, fuzzy, empathic kind of stuff. Estrogen in lots of ways could be summarized by: if you've got a choice between having a lot of estrogen in your bloodstream or not, go for having a lot of estrogen. It enhances cognition, it stimulates neurogenesis in the hippocampus, it increases glucose and oxygen delivery, it protects you from dementia, it decreases inflammatory oxidative damage to blood vessels — which is why it's good for protecting from cardiovascular disease, in contrast to testosterone, which is making every one of those things worse.

This springs up the minefield with a question: what about post-menopausal estrogen? All sorts of lab studies with non-human primates suggested that if you keep estrogen levels high after a monkey's equivalent of menopause, you're going to keep brain health a lot better — decreasing the risk of dementia, stroke, all of that. And there was enormous enthusiasm for giving post-menopausal women estrogen supplementation. Then the Women's Health Initiative study came out, and it showed an increased risk of breast cancer, an increased risk of cardiovascular disease — and it was basically the opposite of what was expected.

The field was in shock. People were upset. What has slowly come out since, though, is the critical issue of timing. If you give estrogen during or immediately around menopause, you get the protective effects on the brain and cardiovascular system. If you wait 10 years after menopause and then give estrogen, you lose those benefits and start getting the downsides. The field has called this the "critical window hypothesis" or the timing hypothesis. And this is enormously important for women and for their physicians to understand. The benefits of estrogen are real, but the timing of when you start it matters enormously.

## [00:42:15] Stress Mitigation & Our Sense of Control

**Andrew Huberman:** You described a study once about two rats: one running on a wheel voluntarily, one who is basically stuck in a running wheel and is forced to run anytime rat number one runs. In one case the rat is voluntarily exercising; in the other case, the rat is being forced to go to PE class, so to speak. And you see divergent effects on biology. I'd like to use this as a case study for stress mitigation in general. What do you think about stress mitigation, and what should we do as individuals and as families and as a culture to try to encourage people to mitigate their stress, but in ways that are not going to turn us into rat number two — where we're being forced to mitigate our own stress and therefore it becomes more stressful?

**Robert Sapolsky:** What you see is rat number one gets all the benefits of exercise. Rat number two gets all the downsides of severe stress with the same exact muscle expenditure and movements going on. Perfectly yoked. Great example that it's the interpretation in your head. And I'll bet rat number two is having a whole lot more activity in its amygdala than is rat number one.

Okay, so stress mitigation. Anything I say here I should preface with: I'm reasonably good at telling people what's going to happen if they don't manage their stress, but I'm terrible at actually managing stress or advising how to manage it — I'm much better with the bad news aspect of it. But what you see is, by now, a classic literature half a century old showing what are the building blocks of stress.

The building blocks are: lack of control over a stressor, lack of predictability that the stressor is coming, lack of social support, and the inability to interpret the stressor as anything other than worsening over time. And critically, the two biggest factors are the sense of control and the sense of predictability. And here's the thing: the perception of control is what matters. You don't actually have to have control — you just have to believe you do. The classic study: put a rat in a box with a lever. Every so often it gets a shock. If it learns that pressing the lever terminates the shock, it has less stress physiology than a rat that gets the exact same shocks but has no lever. Even if the lever doesn't actually work — the rat just thinks it does — you still see reduced stress response. The perception of control is a massive buffer against stress.

And predictability works similarly: if a rat gets ten seconds warning before a shock, it has less stress physiology than a rat that gets the same shocks with no warning — because that ten seconds of warning is also ten seconds of being sure you're safe. Predictability creates an island of safety.

## [00:51:35] How Best to Buffer Stress

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on stress mitigation from the standpoint of, we don't want to be rat number two, we want to select something for ourselves? In terms of more head-centered, cognitive approaches to stress mitigation versus going at the core physiology — cold showers, exercise, breathing?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Cold showers make some sense physiologically — preconditioning for when the real stressors come. In terms of what you bring up: transcendental meditation, mindfulness, exercise, prayer, reflecting on gratitude — collectively they work, on average. They can lower heart rate and cholesterol levels and have all sorts of good outcomes. But they come with caveats. One is exactly the caveat from the running wheel study: it doesn't matter how many of your friends swear by a stress management technique. If doing it feels like an obligation, like you have to do it, it's going to stop working as well or may even become a stressor itself.

The second caveat is individual differences. There are people for whom talking about their stress with other people is extraordinarily helpful — they get a lot of social support from it, they feel less alone. And there are other people for whom talking about their stress with others makes them feel worse, because it reinforces it, or because the social interaction itself is stressful. And this is enormously individual.

The third caveat is about cognitive reframing. If you genuinely can reframe a stressor — convince yourself it isn't as bad as you thought, or that it has benefits — this really does change the physiology. But you can't just will yourself to reframe. You need to be in the right state and have the right distance from the stressor. Which is why sometimes the most effective approach is first doing something physiological — a run, a cold shower, some breathing — to get the amygdala quieted down a bit, and then doing the cognitive work.

## [01:00:32] Context-Setting, Prefrontal Cortex & Hierarchy

**Andrew Huberman:** What's remarkable to me is how areas of the brain like the hypothalamus and the amygdala are sort of like switches. **Robert Sapolsky:** Or a mixed, horribly ambivalent one that is changing by the millisecond and could be mutually contradictory. No, that's absolutely the case in the prefrontal cortex. I more than once have regretted having wasted 30 years of my life studying the hippocampus when I should have studied the prefrontal cortex, because it's so much more interesting what it does. And it's all this contextual stuff. It's all the ways in which it's not okay to lie in this setting, but it's a great thing in another. It's not okay to kill unless you do it to them, and then you get a medal. All of this social context and moral relativity and situational ethics — that's the prefrontal cortex that's got to master that.

And that winds up meaning that's the place in your brain more than anywhere where your perception of things can powerfully shape physiological outputs. The hierarchy of the brain in terms of who's regulating whom — and the prefrontal cortex is ultimately at the top, in terms of contextual modulation — means that what you believe to be true about a situation has enormous downstream physiological consequences. And this is both the promise and the peril of having such a big prefrontal cortex. The promise: you can talk yourself into seeing things differently and genuinely change your stress physiology. The peril: you can also talk yourself into catastrophizing, into hierarchical thinking that dehumanizes others, into rumination. The same machinery. It goes both ways.

**Andrew Huberman:** And our hierarchies themselves are relevant here, because where we sit in a hierarchy — or where we perceive ourselves to sit — matters greatly to our stress physiology, to our health.

**Robert Sapolsky:** Enormously. The literature on this is vast. Across dozens of species, across dozens of types of social hierarchies, where you sit in the hierarchy is one of the most powerful predictors of health outcomes. And the key is — again — perception. If you believe you're low status, you have the physiology of low status, regardless of what you'd be objectively measured at. And some of the saddest data in the literature is on humans who have internalized low status — who have given up, who have learned helplessness. That's where you see some of the most severe chronic disease outcomes. Because for them, every stressor confirms what they already believe: that they have no control, no predictability, and no social support.

## [01:13:17] Do We Have Free Will?

**Andrew Huberman:** Along the lines of choice, I'd like to shift gears slightly and talk about free will — about our ability to make choices at all.

**Robert Sapolsky:** Well, my personal way-out-in-left-field inflammatory stance is: I don't think we have a shred of free will, despite 95% of philosophers and probably the majority of neuroscientists saying that we have free will in at least some circumstances. I don't think there's any at all.

The reason for this is: you do something, you behave, you make a choice, whatever. And to understand why you did that, where did that intention come from? Part of it was due to the sensory environment you were in the previous minute. Some of it is from the hormone levels in your bloodstream that morning. Some of it is from whether you had a wonderful or stressful last three months and what sort of neuroplasticity happened. Part of it is what hormone levels you were exposed to as a fetus. Part of it is what culture your ancestors came up with, and thus how you were parented when you were a kid. All of those are in there, and you can understand where behavior is coming from by incorporating all of those factors. At that point, not only are there all of these relevant factors, but they're ultimately all one factor. If you're talking about what evolution has to do with your behavior, by definition you're also talking about genetics. If you're talking about what your genes have to do with behavior, by definition you're talking about how your brain was constructed. If you're talking about your mood disorder now, you're talking about the sense of efficacy you were getting as a five-year-old. They're all intertwined. And when you look at all those influences, where is the room for free will?

**Andrew Huberman:** This brings up a very practical question, which is: if there's no free will, why bother? Why bother trying to change? Why bother trying to make better decisions?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Because it doesn't mean change is impossible. It doesn't mean that what you do doesn't matter. What it means is that when change happens, it happens through mechanisms — through the same biological, psychological, social, and environmental mechanisms that drive all behavior. So trying to change is itself one of those mechanisms. Being exposed to a compelling argument is one of those mechanisms. Having a good teacher is one of those mechanisms. Living in a society with good institutions is one of those mechanisms. The absence of free will doesn't mean passivity. It means that the causes of behavior are real and knowable, and therefore potentially manipulable. That is actually more hopeful, not less. Because it means change has real levers.

## [01:20:50] How to Apply Knowledge & Learning

**Andrew Huberman:** You're saying that even if it seems close to impossible, that circumstances can change, and yet that striving to be better human beings is still a worthwhile endeavor. Do I have that correct?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Absolutely. Because simply the knowledge — either from experience or from making it to the end of the right neurobiology class — has taught you that change can happen within a framework of a mechanistic neurobiology. You are now more open to being made optimistic by the good news in the world around you. You are more likely to be inspired by this or that. You are more resistant to getting discouraged by bad news, simply because you now understand it's possible.

**Andrew Huberman:** Somebody who spent much of his career working on the hippocampus — I have to assume that you are a believer in neuroplasticity, that neural circuits can change in response to experience, and that some of the same top-down mechanisms of prefrontal cortex that we were talking about can play a role there.

**Robert Sapolsky:** Yeah. And not only can prenatal hormone exposure change the way your brain is being constructed, but learning that prenatal hormone exposure can change the construction of your brain will change your brain right now — and how you think about where your intentions came from. Wow, maybe that had something to do with it. The knowledge of knowledge is an effector in and of itself.

**Andrew Huberman:** That's such an important and powerful statement to hear. I think many people think that if a tool doesn't involve a pill or a protocol, it's useless. And certainly there are pills and protocols that are very useful in a variety of contexts for a variety of things. But the idea that knowledge itself — as you put it, knowledge of knowledge — is itself a tool is a very important concept for people to embed in their minds.

## [01:23:44] Robert's New Book: "Determined: The Science of Life Without Free Will"

**Andrew Huberman:** Are you willing to tell us a little bit about that book and where you are in that process and what we can look forward to?

**Robert Sapolsky:** Yeah, it's going really slow. Title is "Determined: A Science of Life Without Free Will." Essentially the first half of the book is trying to convince a reader — if not that there's no free will whatsoever, but at least that there's a lot less than is normally assumed. I'm going through all the standard arguments for free will, and why they don't make sense with 21st century science. That has led to reading a lot of very frustrating philosophers who are basically willing to admit that stuff is made out of atoms and molecules — they're not just relying on magic — but who believe in free will for magical reasons, where it doesn't make sense. So the first half of the book is to hopefully convince people that there's much less free will than we used to think.

And then the second half is this gigantic juncture built around the fact that I haven't thought there's any free will since I was like an adolescent. And despite thinking that way, I still have absolutely no idea how you're supposed to function with that belief. How are you supposed to go about everyday life if anything you feel entitled to isn't true? If any angers and hatreds you feel aren't justified, if there's no such thing as appropriate blame or punishment or praise or reward, and none of it makes any sense, and somebody even compliments you on your haircut and you've been conditioned to say "oh, thanks" as if you had something to do with it? How are we supposed to function with that? So the second half is wrestling with that, and the punchline is: it's going to be incredibly hard. And if you think it's going to be hard to subtract a notion of free will from making sense of serial murderers, it's going to be a thousand times harder making sense of when somebody says "good job" to you.
