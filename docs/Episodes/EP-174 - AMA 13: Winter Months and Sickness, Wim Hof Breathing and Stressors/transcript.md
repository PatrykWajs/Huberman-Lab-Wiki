## [00:01:46] Does Cold Weather Make You Sick?

The first question is about colds and flus. The question specifically is: why is it that we get more colds and flus in the winter months? Is it the temperature outside? Is it the fact that people are spending more time indoors? Or is it a myth that we get more colds and flus in the winter months? Well, the first thing is that the research very clearly shows that in winter months there is a greater prevalence of colds and flus.

Now, of course, the words "in the winter months" means many things. For instance, in most areas of the world unless you live directly near the equator, winter months means that days are going to be shorter and nights are longer than in the summer months. In general, that's true. It's especially true if you live very far from the equator. So imagine somebody living up in Tromsø, Norway, which is very close to the North Pole. In winter, days are very, very short in Tromsø.

And conversely in the summer months, days are very, very long in Tromsø, Norway. Now, Tromsø, Norway represents an extreme of day length variation according to time of year. The closer you get to the equator, the less variation there is in day length and therefore night length across the year. However, in most locations on Earth, days will be significantly shorter — by about an hour or two at least — for certain parts of the year. And in general, shorter days correlate with colder temperatures. That's what's really important to know: shorter days generally correlate with colder temperatures regardless of where you live on planet Earth.

So in the winter months, as we're calling it, involves at least two things: shorter days, which means less sunlight and either more artificial light or more darkness depending on how much artificial light you rely on; and colder temperatures. Colder temperatures, of course, doesn't necessarily mean that you are exposed to those colder temperatures — because what's the third thing? Shorter days and colder temperatures outside mean that people are spending more time indoors.

So what the research clearly shows is that one of the reasons — not the only, but one of the reasons — for the greater prevalence of colds and flus in the short-days a.k.a. winter months is that people are spending more time indoors and therefore at closer proximity. This raises a whole bunch of really interesting questions that have been explored in the peer-reviewed research about proximity and cold and flu transmission. There have been studies where people who are suffering from cold and flu come into the laboratory and are asked to sneeze at different proximities to other people — obviously, people are doing this under consent, agreeing to participate in these particular experiments. And there does seem to be a very direct relationship between physical distance between you, the uninfected person, and the sneezing, coughing, nose-wiping, eye-wiping person that is suffering from the flu. In other words, the closer you are to somebody who's sneezing or coughing, the higher the probability that you will contract that cold or flu.

So we've already established two things. First, physical proximity is a key variable in terms of transmission of cold and flu — and at some level that's obvious. If you're in the next room, down the hallway, or across the street from somebody with a cold or flu, you don't expect to contract it from them. Whereas if you're in the same vehicle with them, sitting next to them on an airplane, on the same bus, or in the same classroom and they are sneezing and coughing, the probability that you would contract that cold or flu is increased.

That's just obvious. But the studies that have explored the relationship between physical proximity and transmission have actually analyzed things down to the range of: if you're standing one foot apart, or three feet apart, or six feet apart — what's the probability that you will contract that cold or flu? And it follows a very direct relationship where the closer the proximity, the more likely you're going to contract it. And of course, anything like exchange of skin contact or exchange of saliva — because obviously in romantic relationships people kiss, and in familial relationships or friendships people hug — any contact further increases transmission. Not just from stuff emitted from the nasal passages and mouth when people sneeze and cough, but also when people wipe their eyes, or wipe their skin, because oftentimes they have cold or flu virus on their skin. It doesn't always survive terribly long on the skin, but if they wipe their face or nose or use tissues and then don't wash their hands thoroughly afterwards, they can transmit cold and flu simply through a handshake.

Now, this particular point about proximity and people being indoors more during the winter months raises a whole bunch of interesting questions and, in fact, protocols that I plan to cover in a Huberman Lab podcast episode solely devoted to colds and flus and how to avoid them. But one brief telegraph of what I plan to cover in that episode: if people are telling you that they are sneezing and coughing due to a cold or flu but that they are "not contagious" — either because they are early in the cold or flu or late in the cold or flu — keep in mind that any time someone is sneezing or coughing they are in fact contagious. I'll get back to that in the full-length episode on colds and flus. But I felt it was too important not to mention right now: people's theories about when they are or aren't contagious are rarely substantiated by the actual data. The actual data point to the fact that when people are sneezing and coughing due to a cold or flu, they are still contagious.

So we've established that cold outside means people tend to be indoors more, which increases physical proximity, which is one of the reasons why there's more cold and flu transmission. One of the other reasons why being indoors more tends to increase cold and flu transmission is that in the cold months of winter, people tend to be not under air conditioning or at neutral temperatures, but rather in heated rooms. And depending on the type of heating used, the heated air tends to be drier air.

Now, this is a little bit counterintuitive because if you ever go outside on a really cold winter day, you will realize that the cold weather outdoors is extremely dry — you can almost feel the dryness of the air. Think of yourself outdoors on a very cold winter day taking a brisk walk, then think about yourself taking that same brisk walk on a very humid summer day. Humidity is the concentration of water in the ambient air, whereas dryness is the lack of humidity. That simple thought experiment will remind you just how dry the cold air is outdoors during the winter months.

Whereas indoors, heating that air has the property of drying the nasal and oral passages — but especially the nasal passages. So another key reason why there's more transmission of colds and flus in winter is because people are spending more time indoors and the way those indoor environments are heated is drying out the nasal passages. And the nasal passages represent a primary site of defense for viral infections like colds and flus, as well as bacterial and fungal infections.

I've talked before on this podcast about the importance of nasal breathing under any conditions where you don't have to breathe through your mouth — if you are not eating, not speaking, or not exercising hard (which by the way often requires mouth breathing to bring in enough oxygen, and there's nothing wrong with that under those conditions). But for the most part, it's best to be a nasal breather. Why? Because the nasal passages contain a number of physical barriers, including the hairs within your nose. Those hairs actually serve as a barrier toward infection — this is well established.

The mucosal lining of the nose acts as its own physical barrier and physical trap for incoming viruses, fungal infections, and bacterial infections. They literally get trapped in the nasal passages and therefore can't enter deeper into your physiology. Keep in mind — as covered in the episode with Noam Sobel and the episode on olfaction — that your brain sits not far behind your nasal passages. There's a bony barrier there called the cribriform plate, but it's not far from your nostrils to your brain, and it is not far from your nostrils to the rest of your respiratory pathway. The hairs of your nostrils, the mucus itself, and the microbiome — the trillions of micro bacteria that thrive in the mucosal lining of your nasal passages — actually serve to protect against many incoming infections.

This is why it's so important to keep the mucosal lining of your nasal passages thriving and intact. One of the best ways to do that is to make sure that the air you're breathing is sufficiently humidified. So while there are many different claims out there about how to avoid colds and flus, if you suffer from them and are going to spend a lot of time in a particular indoor environment, you might be wise to use an air humidifier — something that brings more moisture into the air that you're breathing, especially at night while you're sleeping. That won't prevent the person in your environment who's sneezing actively from transmitting their cold or flu, but it will keep your nasal passages and the rest of your respiratory pathways as healthy and as resistant as possible.

This brings up another key point: regardless of whether it's winter or any other time of year, all day long you're combating different types of infections. Different viral, fungal, and bacterial infections are bombarding your system and your immune system counters that through physical, neurochemical, hormonal, and pure immune mechanisms. There's a whole discussion of this in the episode on the immune system. But keeping the air you breathe — especially at night — sufficiently humidified is one great way to offset colds and flus you might be fighting off, perhaps because you brought one home from work.

If you are having a hard time grasping how you could bring home a cold or flu and then ward it off — do you have it, do you not? — this is some of the gray area around cold and flu and viral transmission generally. We don't know, for instance, if you got a mild sniffle as opposed to a really bad cold, whether it would have been much worse had you slept less, had you not humidified your air better, et cetera. Probably it would be. And if you've ever not slept well while you're sick, you've experienced just how much worse that sickness feels. The symptoms go from very mild to moderate or from moderate to very severe when you are sleep deprived. That said, it's also clear that some strains of the cold or flu can be more mild than others. So this can be pretty nuanced.

Nasal breathing whenever possible, day and nighttime, is extremely important for encouraging the overall health and resistance of your respiratory pathways to incoming cold and flu and other viruses. Similarly, humidifying the air you breathe — especially at night but perhaps also during the day in your office or home environment — can be very beneficial for warding off colds and flus. And if you'd like to read more about the nasal versus mouth breathing components of colds and flus, I want to mention that this is a bidirectional effect: people who deliberately breathe through their nose tend to get fewer colds and flus, while people who tend to breathe through their mouth more tend to get more colds and flus. Now, you might think that's the same thing or just two sides of the same coin — but actually it's not. There's what we call a double dissociation, whereby if people are mouth breathers, they are far more susceptible to colds and flus and other types of respiratory illnesses.

I'll put a link in the show note captions to one paper I like a lot, entitled "Association of oral breathing with dental malocclusions and general health in children." This paper explores a lot of different things about nasal versus mouth breathing — all of which, by the way, point to the fact that nasal breathing whenever possible is better for health, aesthetics, and dental health than mouth breathing. But one of the key components here, which is why I bring it up in this context: "oral breathing is related to a significantly higher prevalence of allergies and a significantly more likely chance of getting sick and taking medication for being sick."

So to recap: being a nasal breather, humidifying your air, and paying attention to whether or not you're indoors with people who are coughing and sneezing because it's cold outside — all of those things are going to be relevant. Perhaps keep a little bit of distance, maybe a lot of distance, from those people; or encourage them to wash their hands after they wipe their nose. I think it's entirely reasonable to ask somebody who's sick to try not to get you sick. There's actually not many things ruder than someone who knows they're sick showing up to an event because they didn't want to miss it and getting everybody else sick. That's my opinion — and I think that opinion is shared with many of you.

Now, as I mentioned earlier, when it's cold outside, the air tends to be drier. Not always — you could live in an environment that's very cold and very rainy, and rainy is maximum humidity. But if you are going to spend time outdoors on cold winter days — which by the way is a really good thing for other reasons that I'll get into in a moment, because they relate directly to why there's such a prevalence of colds and flus — there are two things to keep in mind. First, the things about proximity still hold. There have been studies of people with colds and flus sneezing in indoor versus outdoor environments. You can get a cold or flu from somebody sneezing outdoors next to you just as well as indoors, though there's a slightly higher probability of transmission if it happens indoors at the same distance. Obviously: sneeze and cough in the other direction, cover your mouth and nose. Sneezing into your elbow is probably the next best thing if you don't have a tissue — as opposed to into your hand, which then you touch other things and yourself.

Without getting too deep into the mechanics of sneezing and coughing: if you're going to be outside in the cold winter months and you'd like to avoid colds and flus, when you run, exercise, walk, or are in conversation, try to nasal breathe unless you have to mouth breathe. And if you can sense your nasal passages or your mouth drying out because of hard breathing — exercise during the winter months is still important, I'm not discouraging it as long as you can do it safely — just be aware that when you take those dried-out air passages indoors and you're around other people who might have colds and flus, you are going to be more susceptible. That's cause for taking a hot shower; and if you have access to a steam room or humidifier, rehydrating those nasal and oral passages.

Now, if you're outside and running and your nasal passages are drying out, does that mean you're more susceptible to colds and flus that are just out in that environment if no one else is around? Chances are no. However, what we were all told as kids — if you go outside and get a chill, it's a good idea when you come back inside to take a hot shower, hot bath, or if you have access, a sauna to heat back up — that's also true. It does seem that there's a relationship between core body temperature and susceptibility to cold and flu.

Keep in mind that fevers in response to a cold or flu are an attempt of your immune system to heat up that virus and destroy it. That's worth keeping in mind if you're considering taking medication to lower your fever. Of course, highly elevated body temperatures can be very dangerous to the brain and body — there's a fairly limited upper range of temperatures we can tolerate before we start damaging ourselves. But also keep in mind that fever is an adaptation designed to destroy viruses. So if you blunt the fever, you are actually letting a cold or virus proliferate more readily than it would if you allowed a slight fever to combat it. There's a whole discussion to be had there — obviously you don't want people cooking their brains due to elevated fever and not treating it. But you also don't want to reduce your core body temperature too much.

Which raises the question of things like cold plunges. Should you be taking cold showers and doing cold plunges in the winter months when it's already cold and you might be coming down with something? Well, that segues directly into the next question I'm going to answer for this AMA, which relates to cold exposure and so-called cyclic hyperventilation breathing — sometimes associated with Wim Hof breathing, but other forms of deliberate breathing as well — and how specific forms of deliberate breathing and deliberate cold exposure can indeed be used to offset or even prevent entirely infection due to bacteria or viruses. But also, if you are already contracting or have already contracted a cold or virus, why cyclic hyperventilation breathing or deliberate cold exposure may actually be the worst thing you can do.

So we'll answer that question next. In the meantime, just know that yes, indeed in the winter months you are more susceptible to colds and flus because there's more of them going around. We talked about some of the reasons why they're going around, and some of the things you can do to protect yourself. There are a bunch of other things that are very valuable that we'll explore in the full-length episode on colds and flus. And I'm hoping you all stay clear of colds and flus these winter months — and now you have some tools to try and reduce the duration and severity of those colds and flus should you get one.
