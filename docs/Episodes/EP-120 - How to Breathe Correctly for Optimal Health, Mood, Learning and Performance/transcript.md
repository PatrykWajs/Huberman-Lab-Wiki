## [00:09:36] Respiration, Oxygen & Carbon Dioxide

The main players in today's discussion are going to be oxygen and carbon dioxide. Now, a common misconception is that oxygen is good and carbon dioxide is bad. That's simply not the case. When we breathe in, we are largely breathing in air in order to bring oxygen into our body. Oxygen can't diffuse through our skin into the deeper cells of our body. Other single cell and very simple organisms can actually bring oxygen into their system without the need to breathe. But we have to breathe in order to bring oxygen to the cells that reside deep in our body. In particular, our brain cells, which are the most metabolically active cells in our body, require a lot of oxygen.

We also need a system — which turns out to be the breathing or respiratory system — that can offload or remove the gas that we call carbon dioxide, not because carbon dioxide is bad but because too much of it in our system is not good. In fact, much of today's discussion will also center around the common misconception that carbon dioxide is something that we want to get rid of. You don't want to get rid of too much carbon dioxide or else you can't actually get oxygen to the cells and tissues of your body in an efficient way. So you need oxygen and you need carbon dioxide in your body.

When thinking about the respiration system, it's useful to categorize things as either mechanical mechanisms or chemical mechanisms — a common theme in biology. Any time there's a critical bodily function, it's very unlikely that just one type of information is going to be used. Almost always, it's going to be mechanical and chemical information in parallel. When we're thinking about the respiration system, we also need to look at the mechanical system and the chemical systems of the lungs and the bloodstream.

If you can understand the mechanical and chemical aspects of breathing, even just at a top contour, then the various tools I discuss during today's episode — such as the ability to calm yourself down most quickly by doing what's called a physiological sigh — will make sense. The physiological sigh is two very deep inhales through the nose followed by a full exhale through the mouth to lungs completely empty. That double inhale through the nose followed by a long exhale through the mouth works to reduce your levels of stress and lower your levels of autonomic arousal very fast in real time. It works better than any other known approach. This is actually something that your body has specific neural circuits to do, and it performs during sleep on a regular basis and even throughout the day.

If you understand the mechanical and chemical aspects of breathing, you will also understand why most people are overbreathing. They're breathing too often. They are blowing off or removing too much carbon dioxide. And if you understand that carbon dioxide is critical for the way that oxygen is delivered from the bloodstream to the tissues of the body, including the brain, it will make very good sense as to why people who are breathing too much don't actually experience all the effects of elevated oxygen — but rather they're putting their body into what's called a hypoxic state. They're not getting enough oxygen to the tissues of their body, in particular their brain.

## [00:18:18] Breathing Mechanics

The mechanical aspects of breathing are really straightforward. You can breathe through your nose or through your mouth. It goes down through the larynx — a rigid tissue or pipe that brings the air from the nose and mouth down to the lungs. The rigidity of the larynx is crucial: it can't collapse while you're bringing air in and out, the way a flimsy tube would.

Your lungs are basically two big bags that can fill with air or squeeze air out. What most people don't realize is that inside the lungs are hundreds of millions of little sacs called the alveoli. By having those hundreds of millions of little sacs, you increase the surface area of the lungs and allow more oxygen to pass from the air into the bloodstream than would be possible otherwise. So those alveoli are an important part of the mechanical aspect of breathing.

The lungs do not have any muscles themselves, so we need muscles that can either squeeze or expand them. There are two general groups of muscles: the diaphragm and the intercostal muscles. The diaphragm is a thin muscle that sits below the lungs and above the liver. When we inhale using diaphragmatic breathing, that diaphragm contracts and moves down, which allows more space for the lungs to inflate with air. The intercostal muscles — the muscles between our ribs — also contract on inhale, allowing the rib cage to move up and expand a bit. Your ribs are not fixed in place; they can actually get further and closer apart from one another.

Because muscles can't move themselves, they are controlled by nerves. There is a specialized nerve called the phrenic nerve, which comes out of the neck, goes close to the heart, and forms synapses with the diaphragm. When those neurons release neurotransmitters, the diaphragm contracts and moves down. Interestingly, the phrenic nerve is not just a motor nerve — it also has sensory neurons that dive deep into the diaphragm and close to the liver, sending information back to the brain about where the diaphragm is.

Diaphragmatic breathing is just one mode by which the brain and the phrenic nerve can control muscle to manage the mechanical aspects of the lungs. You also have intercostal muscles and a separate set of nerves that allow your rib cage to expand and create more room for the lungs. There is no data showing that diaphragmatic breathing is inherently better than rib cage breathing. When you're exerting yourself very hard, you tend to use both. However, being able to control those systems independently or in combination gives tremendous power toward regulating your mental and physical states.

Breathing in through the nose offers more resistance to airflow than through the mouth, and that resistance is actually beneficial — it allows you to draw more force and bring more air into your lungs. The increased resistance through the nose allows you to inflate your lungs more, not less. This increased resistance also wipes away a common misconception: if you have challenges breathing through your nose, you should breathe through your nose more, not less, because that extra resistance is helping you bring more oxygen into your system.

## [00:31:23] Chemistry of Breathing, Hyperventilation

The two major players in the chemical side of breathing are oxygen and carbon dioxide. Carbon dioxide plays critical roles in delivering oxygen to your cells. Without carbon dioxide, you're not going to get enough oxygen to the cells and tissues of your body. That said, if carbon dioxide levels are too high, that is very problematic — in fact, one of the ways that one can reliably induce panic in anybody is to have them breathe air that contains too much carbon dioxide. Even in people who completely lack amygdalas on both sides of the brain — people who are otherwise completely unafraid of things that ought to be feared — breathing excess carbon dioxide immediately triggers a panic attack. This tells us there are parallel mechanisms and redundancy in the system to protect ourselves from too much carbon dioxide.

When we breathe in, our lungs inflate. Oxygen can actually move from the air into those alveoli and then from those alveoli into the vasculature — the capillaries, veins, and arteries — because the walls of those little alveoli are exceedingly thin and are surrounded by capillaries. That oxygen gets bound up by hemoglobin, which then delivers it to the various cells and tissues of the body. However, oxygen can't just hop on hemoglobin and cruise until it reaches its destination and hop off. You require carbon dioxide in order to liberate oxygen from hemoglobin. Carbon dioxide has this incredible property of being able to change the shape of hemoglobin — opening up the cage-like structure around oxygen molecules and allowing them to be liberated and delivered to tissues.

Carbon dioxide is also strongly related to how acidic or basic your body is overall. If carbon dioxide levels go too low, your blood pH increases — you become more alkaline. The body ideally stays at about pH 7.4. If carbon dioxide levels go too low, the pH increases in a way that temporarily allows more oxygen to be available to tissues, but that is not sustainable or desirable.

When you hyperventilate, you inhale more than usual but you also exhale more than usual, removing more carbon dioxide from your body than normal. This removal of excess carbon dioxide — hypocapnia — creates vasoconstriction: the capillaries, veins, and arteries of the brain start to constrict, cutting off blood flow. You feel lightheaded, tingly in the periphery, more alert but also anxious. While hyperventilation brings in more oxygen, the removal of carbon dioxide prevents that oxygen from being liberated from hemoglobin and delivered to tissues. So high oxygen intake combined with low carbon dioxide results in a paradoxical hypoxia — not enough oxygen actually gets to the brain and body cells.

## [00:40:35] High Altitudes, Oxygen & Breathing

When you ascend to high altitude — say 6,000, 10,000, or 11,000 feet above sea level — it becomes harder to breathe for a mechanical reason. At sea level, the air pressure outside your body is high relative to the low pressure inside your lungs when they're empty, so air rushes in readily. At high altitude, that steep pressure gradient doesn't exist. The air pressure outside is lower, so you have to put a lot more effort into breathing — really engaging the diaphragm and intercostal muscles vigorously, sometimes even lifting your shoulders with each breath. Inhaling is always an active process, and exhaling is generally passive; but at altitude, the active process of inhaling becomes far more demanding.

When you arrive at altitude, you'll feel lightheaded, may have a headache, and have more buildup of carbon dioxide in your system — disrupting the normal balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide. Over days to weeks, the body adapts through changes in hemoglobin-oxygen carrying capacity and the interactions between carbon dioxide and hemoglobin that allow more oxygen to be delivered to tissues. The respiratory muscles — the diaphragm and intercostals — also get trained up, becoming stronger and more efficient.

The converse is also true. If you've been training at 8,000 or 10,000 feet and then come down to sea level, for about two to five days you'll feel like an absolute beast — able to deliver far more oxygen to your muscles per breath, because the hemoglobin adaptations and the stronger respiratory muscles now operate in an easier pressure environment.

If you find yourself at altitude with a headache or trouble catching your breath, spending time consciously drawing in larger breaths — as fatiguing as that may seem — will allow you to adapt more quickly.

## [00:47:16] Tool: Sleep Apnea, Nasal Breathing

When we go to sleep at night, a large fraction of the population underbreathes — not taking deep enough or frequent enough breaths — experiencing what's called sleep apnea, becoming hypoxic. People carrying excess weight are more prone to nighttime sleep apnea, but many people who are not overweight also experience it. Signs include excessive daytime sleepiness, daytime anxiety combined with sleepiness, and snoring. Sleep apnea is a very serious health concern that greatly increases the probability of cardiovascular events, heart attack, stroke, and is a precursor or direct cause of sexual dysfunction in both males and females, cognitive dysfunction during the daytime, and can exacerbate dementia.

One of the major treatments for sleep apnea is a CPAP device, but one of the more common behavioral approaches now being used is deliberately shifting to nasal breathing during sleep. Taping the mouth shut using medical tape prior to sleep is one way that people can learn to nasal breathe during sleep and can greatly offset sleep apnea, snoring, and sleep-related issues. People who don't feel safe taping their mouth can practice pure nasal breathing during exercise, walking, or while working during the daytime. This trains the system to become a better nasal breather during the daytime, which is known to transfer to nighttime breathing patterns — allowing people to become nasal breathers during sleep, snore less, and have less sleep apnea.

An important distinction: a CPAP device adjusts airflow going into your system but doesn't really teach you how to breathe properly. When you shift from mouth to nasal breathing during sleep, you are actually learning and training your system to breathe properly. For people with minor or emerging sleep apnea, shifting to becoming a nasal breather will be far more beneficial and cost effective than a CPAP.

## [00:51:50] Brain Centers & Breathing Rhythm

There are basically two areas of the brain that control breathing. The first is called the pre-Bötzinger complex, named after a bottle of wine and discovered by the great Jack Feldman, a professor of neuroscience at UCLA. This is one of the most fundamental discoveries in all of neuroscience in the last hundred years. This brain area controls all aspects of breathing that are rhythmic — inhales following exhales following inhales. It's a small set of neurons in the brainstem, and it may have a role in sudden infant death syndrome when its function is disrupted. It is also why opioid overdoses kill: exogenous opioids like fentanyl bind to opioid receptors on the pre-Bötzinger complex and shut it down, stopping rhythmic breathing.

The other brain center controlling breathing is the parafacial nucleus. This area is involved in patterns of breathing where there is a doubling up of inhales or exhales, or a deliberate pause in breathing — such as box breathing (inhale, hold, exhale, hold). The parafacial nucleus also activates during speech, when you need to make sure you still get enough oxygen while talking. Most of the time, you're using both these circuits in parallel, which makes sense biologically — for something so critical that failure to do it would cause death, there are two different brain structures that control it.

## [00:57:23] Brain, Hyperventilation & "Over-breathing"

Breathing is incredible because it represents the interface between conscious and subconscious control over not just the body but brain state. By using your brain consciously to control your breathing, you are using your brain to control your brain. The best way this has ever been described is from a 1988 paper by Balestrino and Somjen in The Journal of Physiology: "The brain, by regulating breathing, controls its own excitability." Excitability is the threshold at which a given neuron can be active or not.

When people overbreathe — hyperventilate at rest — they expel too much carbon dioxide. The neurons of the brain fire at low levels as background noise all the time. But when you want to focus, learn, or exercise, certain circuits need to be far more active than that background noise. A beautiful paper entitled "Effects of Voluntary Hyperventilation on Cortical Sensory Responses" showed that when people hyperventilate and become hypocapnic — low carbon dioxide in the blood — they experience about a 30 to 40 percent reduction in the amount of oxygen being delivered to the brain. The lack of carbon dioxide also prevents normal vasodilation, reducing blood flow. Most critically, the brain overall becomes hyperexcitable — as if starved of oxygen and blood flow, all neurons increase their firing levels nonspecifically. The background noise gets louder and louder. As a consequence, the signal from a sound or touch or other event in the world doesn't get above the noise. We actually know that there's an increase in anxiety, and people become less efficient at detecting and learning from the environment.

This also explains why hyperventilation is used to evoke seizures in seizure-prone patients — seizure is an excitability storm, and low carbon dioxide is one of the major triggers.

## [01:03:53] What is Healthy Breathing?

Normal healthy breathing is breathing about six liters of air per minute. Most of us don't think in terms of liters, but basically, if you're just sitting and working or walking slowly, six liters of air per minute is about 12 shallow-ish breaths. Studies examining large populations have found that most people breathe far too much — anywhere from 15 to 20 or even 30 shallow breaths per minute, vastly overbreathing relative to what's healthy.

The correct pattern of healthy breathing involves two things. First, nasal breathing — because of the resistance it provides — delivers more oxygen into your system and allows you to generate more air pressure to fill your lungs, meaning each breath takes longer to exhale. Second, people breathing in the proper healthy manner are also taking pauses between breaths. These pauses are extremely important: they prevent overbreathing and maintain proper oxygen-to-carbon dioxide ratios.

As a consequence of overbreathing, the brain becomes hyperexcitable at the level of background noise, and people are less efficient at detecting and learning information, at focusing, and so on. There are also hundreds, if not thousands, of studies showing that without enough carbon dioxide in the tissues, all bodily tissues — liver, lungs themselves, stomach — are affected, largely through shifts in pH.

The best way to attend to healthy breathing is to spend about a minute each day paying attention to how quickly you're breathing per minute when simply at rest — making coffee, sitting down to read, on social media. Chronically holding your breath is bad, but so is overbreathing. Across studies, the consistent finding is that most people underbreathe at night and overbreathe during the day.

## [01:09:44] Tool: Train Healthy Breathing, Carbon Dioxide Tolerance Test & Box Breathing

To determine whether you're breathing in a healthy or unhealthy way, perform the carbon dioxide tolerance test. Please don't do this while driving or operating heavy machinery or near water. Sit down, breathe normally through your nose for a few seconds. Then inhale through your nose as deeply as you can — fill your lungs as much as you can. Then start a timer and measure how long it takes to perform a controlled, slow exhale through the nose until your lungs are completely empty. You're not holding your breath with lungs empty — you're timing how long you can control the exhale itself.

If it took 20 seconds or less to expel all your air, you have low carbon dioxide tolerance. If it took 25 to 45 seconds, you have moderate tolerance. If you were able to go 50 seconds or longer, you have high carbon dioxide tolerance. Assign yourself a number: low = 3, moderate = 5 to 6, high = 8 to 10.

Now perform box breathing using that number as your interval duration in seconds. Box breathing is equal-duration inhale, hold, exhale, hold, repeated. If you scored in the low category, your inhale, hold, exhale, and hold are each 3 seconds. Moderate: 5 to 6 seconds per phase. High: 8 to 10 seconds per phase. Do this for about two minutes, two to three times per week.

This exercise greatly increases your neuromechanical control over the diaphragm through the phrenic nerve, via neuroplasticity — training the relationship between your brain and the normally subconscious breathing musculature. It also trains you to pause between breaths and take full mechanically-driven breaths using the diaphragm and nasal pathways. Over two to three weeks, you'll find the box breathing becomes easier, and when you retest, your carbon dioxide discard rate will have extended — you'll move up a category. The ultimate benefit is deeper, less frequent breathing when at rest, even when you're not consciously thinking about it, which translates to lower brain hyperexcitability, less overbreathing, better sleep, lower resting heart rate, and improved mood.

## [01:22:39] Tool: Breathwork & Stress Reduction; Cyclic Sighing

A study from my laboratory in collaboration with Dr. David Spiegel, associate chair of psychiatry at Stanford School of Medicine, explored box breathing, cyclic sighing, cyclic hyperventilation, and mindfulness meditation as means to reduce stress around the clock. The study was published as "Brief Structured Respiration Practices Enhance Mood and Reduce Physiological Arousal." We used WHOOP straps donated by WHOOP to measure heart rate variability and physiological parameters on more than a hundred subjects living their real lives, not in a lab.

The basic takeaway was twofold. First, deliberate breathwork practices done for about five minutes per day across about a month led to greater reductions in stress than did a five-minute-per-day meditation practice. Of the three breathwork types explored — box breathing, cyclic sighing, and cyclic hyperventilation — cyclic sighing produced the most robust and pervasive reductions in stress, improvements in mood, and improvements in sleep.

Cyclic sighing is performed as follows: inhale through the nose as deeply as you can, then immediately take a second sharp inhale through the nose to maximally inflate the lungs — this second inhale re-inflates alveoli that have partially collapsed — then perform a long, extended exhale through the mouth until the lungs are completely empty. Repeat for five minutes. The time of day doesn't matter.

One single physiological sigh — that same pattern done once — is also the fastest physiologically verified way to reduce stress and reintroduce calm in real time. It shifts the autonomic nervous system from heightened sympathetic arousal toward parasympathetic calm faster than any other known approach. It is not a hack — physiological sighs were discovered by physiologists in the 1930s, who observed that people spontaneously perform them when carbon dioxide builds up from underbreathing, while asleep and while awake. Animals also perform spontaneous physiological sighs before going to sleep.

So the two applications are: one physiological sigh in the moment for real-time stress reduction, and five minutes of cyclic sighing daily for chronic stress reduction around the clock.

## [01:33:56] Tool: Physiological Sighing & Exercise Side Cramp

Physiological sighs also have a very useful application in exercise: eliminating the side stitch. If you've ever felt a cramp on your right side while running, swimming, or exercising, it is not a cramp of the muscle in the traditional sense. Recall that the phrenic nerve — emanating from cervical nerves C3, 4, and 5 — courses down behind the heart and innervates the diaphragm, which sits directly on top of the liver. The phrenic nerve has sensory neurons that dive deep into the diaphragm and close to the liver. The right-side side stitch is most likely referred pain from those sensory fibers detecting spasming or inefficient firing of the phrenic nerve and sensing the liver and diaphragm beneath it.

Performing the physiological sigh two or three times while still running, swimming, or cycling will typically alleviate that right-side side stitch. The changes in phrenic nerve firing — and particularly the changes in sensory feedback from the sensory component of the phrenic nerve back to the brain — produce an alleviation of the referred pain. If you experience a left-side side stitch, that tends to relate to excess air or fluid in the stomach and has to do with the bilateral branching of the phrenic nerve picking up sensory input from organs on the left side.

## [01:39:16] Breathing & Heart Rate Variability

The relationship between breathing and heart rate is an absolutely lockstep one. When you inhale, your diaphragm moves down, creating more space in the thoracic cavity. The heart temporarily enlarges, and the blood moving through it slows slightly because it's in a larger volume. Your nervous system detects this and sends a neural signal to speed the heart up. So inhales increase heart rate. When you exhale, your diaphragm moves up, the rib cage compresses inward, and the heart becomes slightly smaller. Blood flow through it accelerates, and your nervous system sends a signal to slow the heart down. So exhales decrease heart rate. Your heart rate follows your breathing — inhales always increase it, exhales always decrease it.

This process — called respiratory sinus arrhythmia — is the basis of heart rate variability (HRV). It involves the vagus nerve, the 10th cranial nerve, which is a parasympathetic nerve that slows your heart rate through extended exhales. If you look at any breathing practice — Wim Hof, Tummo, Kundalini, Pranayama, physiological sighing, cyclic sighing — by measuring the ratio of inhales to exhales, you can accurately predict the net effect on heart rate. Practices emphasizing inhales (like cyclic hyperventilation) increase heart rate. Practices emphasizing exhales (like cyclic sighing) decrease heart rate. Box breathing creates an equilibrium.

This is why marksmen in the biathlon exhale just before taking a shot — their heart rate spikes during cross-country skiing, then they exhale to bring it down for the precision shot. It's why boxers and martial artists exhale forcefully on strikes. Learning to actively exhale and extend your exhales is a skill that allows you to rapidly control your heart rate on demand — invaluable for managing anxiety, recovering between exercise rounds, and preventing panic attacks.

## [01:46:21] Tool: How to Stop Hiccups

Hiccups are caused by a spasm of the phrenic nerve. The phrenic nerve, emanating from C3, 4, and 5, innervates the diaphragm and also has sensory fibers that sense deep within the diaphragm and the liver. A hiccup has that occasional painful sensation because of rapid sensory feedback from a sharp contraction in the diaphragm relayed back to the brain.

Many common remedies — breathing into a bag to reinhale carbon dioxide, drinking from the opposite side of a glass — work poorly because they're very indirect methods of trying to adjust phrenic nerve activity.

The reliable method is to inhale three times in a row through the nose without exhaling between inhales: a big deep inhale, then a second inhale through the nose however brief, then a third even shorter inhale through the nose. Then hold the breath for 15 to 20 seconds, and then slowly exhale through the nose or mouth. This triple inhale hyperexcites the phrenic nerve in a very unnatural pattern, which causes it to undergo a hyperpolarization — a much lower probability of getting activated again for some period afterward. Return to normal cadence of breathing after performing this. A second repetition is fine if needed. As far as we know, this is the most efficient and science-supported way to eliminate hiccups.

## [01:51:17] Tool: Stress Management & Cyclic Hyperventilation, "Wim Hof Method"

Cyclic hyperventilation involves a bout of 25 or so breaths — deeply inhaling through the nose and either passively or actively exhaling through the mouth, repeated for 10 to 25 breaths. This greatly increases levels of autonomic arousal by deploying adrenaline from the adrenals and epinephrine from the locus coeruleus. In the study described earlier, participants then expelled all their air and held for 15 to 30 seconds, then repeated for five minutes. This did lead to interesting and positive physiological changes in stress mitigation, although not as significant as cyclic sighing.

The Wim Hof method combines cyclic hyperventilation with either lungs-full or lungs-empty breath holds, depending on the variant, and separately incorporates deliberate cold exposure and a mindfulness component. An important safety note: never perform cyclic hyperventilation in or near water, as it greatly increases the risk of shallow water blackout. The reason: cyclic hyperventilation depletes carbon dioxide, and because the trigger to breathe is an increase in carbon dioxide — not a depletion of oxygen — you can lose consciousness without feeling the urge to breathe. Your brain monitors carbon dioxide level as a threat signal; with carbon dioxide depleted, the impulse to breathe is suppressed, making blackout possible in water.

What cyclic hyperventilation really offers is self-induced stress inoculation. You are deliberately triggering the adrenaline release that normally happens when life events stress you. During the breath holds and subsequent rounds, you explore what it is to have a lot of adrenaline circulating while trying to maintain a calm state of mind. Some people even do mental tasks — math problems, structured thinking — during the breath holds to practice managing the mind under hormonal stress. Done once or twice a week, this can help you learn to manage high-adrenaline states. That said, people with anxiety attacks or panic disorders should avoid this practice.

## [01:57:11] Deliberate Cold Exposure & Breathing

For deliberate cold exposure — cold showers, ice baths, cold immersion — there is no single best way to breathe, but a useful approach is to actively control rhythmic breathing: inhales follow exhales follow inhales. When you get into very cold water, there is a robust decrease in the activation of the prefrontal cortex — the area of the brain behind the forehead controlling structured thinking and reasoning — and it won't work well for the first 20 to 30 seconds. The default response to this kind of stressful circumstance is for rhythmic breathing to stop and for the parafacial nucleus to take over with a panicky doubling-up-of-inhales mode.

By consciously maintaining rhythmic breathing — even if it's fast inhale-exhale, inhale-exhale — you can navigate what would otherwise be a very stressful circumstance and make it less stressful. That skill translates to other stressful situations in life: when hit with something stressful, you'll notice your breathing depart from rhythmic; by quickly returning to rhythmic breathing and extending your exhales, you can calm down rapidly.

## [01:59:54] Tool: Inhales & Learning; Exhales & Movement

How your specific patterns of breathing relate to learning and memory is a fascinating and highly useful area of neuroscience, recently reviewed in detail by Jack Feldman in a paper entitled "Breathing Rhythm and Pattern and Their Influence on Emotion."

When you inhale — and particularly when you inhale through your nose — your brain functions differently than when you exhale. During inhalation, your pupil gets slightly larger, your reaction time to events in the environment is significantly faster, your ability to remember things and learn new information is significantly greater. During exhalation, those advantages reverse.

Why would this be? The olfactory system is the most ancient sensory system — before vision, before audition, before touch. To get chemical information from the environment into the brain, terrestrial animals must inhale. Inhalation activates areas of the piriform cortex (ancient neocortical areas) and increases activity of the hippocampus, the brain area involved in learning and memory.

A particularly important study was published in The Journal of Neuroscience in 2016: "Nasal Respiration Entrains Human Limbic Oscillations and Modulates Cognitive Function." This study — using subjects with intracranial electrodes embedded for epilepsy monitoring, allowing direct brain recording — showed that when people inhale through their nose as a stimulus is presented, their reaction time to detect it is much faster, and the ability to detect novel or surprising stimuli in the environment is significantly enhanced. This held not only for fear-inducing stimuli but for surprising stimuli of all kinds.

Tool takeaway: if you are sitting down to read, study, or listen to information you want to retain, it makes sense to increase the duration or intensity of your inhales relative to your exhales. The more you're inhaling, the more your brain is in a focused mode capable of accessing and retrieving information.

Conversely, the ability to generate fast, voluntary movements is greatly enhanced during exhale. Baseball and softball coaches tell players to exhale on the swing for maximum power. In boxing and martial arts, forceful exhalations accompany strikes — because exhales, when made active rather than passive, engage the full musculature in ways that maximize power output. So: inhales for learning and memory, exhales for voluntary, powerful movement.

## [02:09:15] Mouth vs. Nasal Breathing, Aesthetics

Whenever possible — unless you're speaking, eating, exercising very hard, or otherwise requiring a shift in breathing pattern — you should be striving to breathe through your nose. This is beautifully described in the book Jaws: The Story of a Hidden Epidemic, by Paul Ehrlich and Sandra Kahn of Stanford School of Medicine, with an introduction and foreword from Jared Diamond and Robert Sapolsky.

The increased resistance of nasal breathing allows you to inflate your lungs more, not less. Nasal breathing also warms and moisturizes the air coming into the lungs, which is more favorable for lung health than breathing through the mouth. Chronic mouth breathing can be quite damaging to respiratory function over time.

Another benefit of nasal breathing: the gas nitric oxide is created in the nasal passages. Nitric oxide causes relaxation of smooth muscles of the vasculature — not just in the nose but in the brain and throughout the body. This is why nasal breathing helps relieve congestion: it dilates the vasculature, increases blood flow, and delivers nitric oxide to all the tissues of the body, allowing more efficient delivery of nutrients and removal of waste products from tissues.

The aesthetic effects of mouth versus nasal breathing are striking. When people mouth breathe chronically, there is an elongation of the jaw, drooping of the eyelids, and the entire jaw structure changes in unfavorable ways. When people switch to becoming nasal breathers by default — whether through mouth taping, keeping the mouth closed during cardiovascular exercise, or box breathing exercises — the aesthetic changes are very dramatic and favorable: elevation of the cheekbones, sharpening of the jaw, improvements in dental and jaw structure.

A simple test from the book Jaws: at rest, you should be able to close your mouth, place your tongue on the roof of your mouth behind the teeth, and breathe only through your nose. Many people won't be able to do this yet, but deliberately nasal breathing over time — as described throughout this episode — will expand the available space within the palate and make it increasingly easy.
