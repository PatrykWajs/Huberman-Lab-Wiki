## [00:08:29] Neural Circuits for Aggression, "Hydraulic Pressure Model"

Let's talk about aggression. There are instances where aggression is adaptive — a mother protecting her children if they are being threatened. Most people would agree that maternal aggression of that sort is a great thing. Of course, other forms of aggression, like unprovoked proactive aggression — somebody simply being violent to somebody else even when unprovoked — most of us cringe when we see that kind of behavior.

A more important reason to think about aggression is that by understanding the biology and psychology of aggression, you will be in a much better position to understand how all emotional states come to be. Many people have heard from pop psychology that aggression is just sadness — a form of sadness amplified. But when we look at the underlying biology and the peer-reviewed literature, nothing could be further from the truth. We have distinct circuits in the brain for aggression versus grief and mourning — those are non-overlapping.

The formal study of aggression really began at the start of the last century. One of the names most associated with it is Konrad Lorenz. Some of you may be familiar with that name. Konrad Lorenz studied so-called imprinting behaviors and fixed action pattern behaviors. He's most famous, at least in scientific circles, for getting geese to believe that he was their parent. He discovered fixed action patterns — patterns of behavior that could be evoked by a single stimulus. The idea that you could get a huge category of different behaviors triggered by just the presence of one person is remarkable, because what it suggested is that there are neural circuits — not just individual brain areas, but collections of brain areas that work together — to engage a pattern of behaviors.

This is the first fundamental principle we need to define today: when we talk about aggression, we are talking about activation of neural circuits, not individual brain areas, but neural circuits that get played out in sequence like keys on a piano. Aggression is a verb. It has a beginning, a middle, and an end. It's a process, not an event. That turns out to be very important in terms of how one can halt aggression, prevent it from happening before it's initiated, or even prolong aggression if that's what's needed.

Lorenz thought about what underlying processes could drive aggression, and he talked about this notion of pressure — almost like a hydraulic pressure. Just think about fluid pressure in a small container being pushed, pushed, pushed until the container is ready to explode. Multiple variables could impinge on that and create that pressure. It turns out that's exactly how the system works. There is no single brain area that flips the switch for aggression, although there is a brain structure that generally houses the propensity and the output of aggression.

This notion of hydraulic pressure is very important because it really captures the essence of how neural circuits work when talking about primitive behaviors generally. You can start to notice this in yourself and in others — when you are veering toward aggression, verbal or physical. That veering is the buildup of this hydraulic pressure that Lorenz referred to, and it really does have an underlying biological basis.

It was some years later that the first experiments came along which started to identify the brain areas and the biological pressures that can induce aggressive behavior. The person who really gets credit for this is Walter Hess, who was working on cats. What Hess did was lower a stimulating electrode into their brain — the brain does not have any pain sensors, so after a small hole is made in the skull, electrodes can be lowered into the brain. He stimulated different brain areas, and eventually his electrode landed in a site that caused an otherwise passive, purring, relaxing cat to suddenly go into an absolute rage — arched back, hissing, piloerection where the hairs go up, drooling, even spitting, trying to attack him or anyone else. He observed that when he turned off the stimulation of this brain area, the cat very quickly went back to being passive calm kitty. This happened in every cat he looked at.

## [00:15:50] Brain Regions Controlling Aggression, Ventromedial Hypothalamus (VMH)

For those of you that are regular listeners of this podcast, today we're going to talk about some new neural circuits. We're going to talk a lot about the so-called VMH, or ventromedial hypothalamus. The ventromedial hypothalamus is a nucleus — a small collection of neurons, nerve cells — that is truly small. It's only about 1,500 neurons on one side of your brain and a matching 1,500 neurons on the other side. That combined ~3,000 neurons is sufficient to generate aggressive behavior of the sort that Hess observed in the cat. When you see somebody who's in an act of rage or verbal aggression or defensive aggression, almost certainly those neurons are engaged in that behavior — perhaps even generating it.

Experiments done by David Anderson's lab at Caltech were really the first to parse the fine circuitry and show that the ventromedial hypothalamus is both necessary and sufficient for aggressive behavior. They identified where the ventromedial hypothalamus was in the mouse, then analyzed which genes — which DNA — which proteins are expressed in particular cells of the ventromedial hypothalamus. It turns out there is a particular category of neurons in the ventromedial hypothalamus that make an estrogen receptor, and it is those neurons in particular that are responsible for generating aggressive behavior.

They used a tool developed by Karl Deisseroth — optogenetics — that allows people to control the activity of neurons by shining light on them. In the context of an experiment on a mouse, the beautiful experiments of Dayu Lin (now in her own laboratory at New York University), a little fiber optic cable was put down into the hypothalamus of the mouse. The mouse can move around in its cage freely, and the experimentalist was able to stimulate the turning on of a little bit of blue light that activated only those estrogen receptor neurons in only the ventromedial hypothalamus.

When that mouse is in a cage with another mouse, depending on who the other mouse is — a female mouse — the male mouse will attempt to mate with the female mouse provided he's gone through puberty. About halfway through the mating behavior, Dayu turned on the light to stimulate these estrogen receptor-containing neurons only in the male mouse. The male mouse ceased from trying to mate and immediately tried to kill the female mouse. He started attacking her. Then she turned off the light, the male stopped, and went back to trying to mate with the female mouse. These are such dramatic shifts in behavior triggered only by the activation of only the small set of neurons within the ventromedial hypothalamus.

The next experiment was to put the male mouse in a cage alone, but with a rubber glove filled with air or water. She stimulated the ventromedial hypothalamus neurons, and the mouse immediately tried to kill the glove. It went into a rage, attacking the glove as if it were another mouse. She stopped the stimulation and the mouse immediately went back to being completely calm. A number of different variations were done — stimulating the VMH in female mice as opposed to male mice, putting female mice with other female mice or with male mice. No matter what variation was carried out, stimulation of the ventromedial hypothalamus in a male or female mouse evokes this very dramatic, almost instantaneous aggressive behavior.

Subsequent experiments done by Dayu Lin and other laboratories have shown that the ventromedial hypothalamus is connected with a bunch of other brain areas. One that's important is the so-called PAG, the periaqueductal gray nucleus — a large structure in the back of the brain that houses neurons that can create endogenous opioids, chemicals that can cause pain relief. Even if one is the aggressor, it's likely that they may incur some physical damage and want some pain relief. The PAG also is connected to neural circuits that eventually arrive at things like the jaws. Stimulation of the ventromedial hypothalamus can evoke biting and aggressive biting behavior.

## [00:22:14] Psychiatric Disorders & Aggression

Many categories of psychiatric disorders, developmental disorders, and psychological challenges — schizophrenia, PTSD, depression, borderline personality disorder, and even certain forms of autism — can include elements of aggression and even violence. Now, aggression and violence are certainly not present in all people who suffer from these conditions. However, it can be a feature of those, and it's a well-described feature in terms of understanding the constellation of challenges people suffer from.

## [00:23:36] Stimulation of the VMH, Estrogen Receptors & Aggression

Many of you are probably puzzled because there's this highly specialized brain area, the ventromedial hypothalamus, with this highly specialized subcategory of neurons — the neurons that make estrogen receptors — and yet the activation of those cells triggers dramatic and immediate aggression, both in males and in females.

Most of us think about estrogen and don't immediately think of aggression. Most of us hear testosterone and might think about aggression. To understand this, I want to refer back to a conversation I had on a previous episode with my colleague Robert Sapolsky of Stanford, who studied testosterone and its impacts on behavior as well as estrogen and other hormones.

To dispel a still very common myth: testosterone does not increase aggressiveness. Testosterone increases proactivity and the willingness to lean into effort in competitive scenarios. Sometimes this is referred to as the challenge hypothesis. If people are given testosterone, or if you look at people who have different levels of testosterone that they naturally make, what you find is that testosterone tends to increase competitiveness, but not just in aggressive scenarios. If somebody is already aggressive, giving them testosterone will tend to make them more aggressive. If somebody is very benevolent and altruistic, giving them testosterone will make them more benevolent and altruistic, at least up to a point.

Robert Sapolsky described an interesting experiment: if you look at testosterone levels or administer additional testosterone to people who are doing philanthropy — giving money to organizations — increased testosterone makes people more willing to compete, to give more money than the other person in the room in order to "alpha out" the other person by giving more. This is an act of altruistic philanthropy, not an act of aggression. The point is that testosterone itself does not make people more aggressive.

In the experiments we've been talking about, it's actually the activation of estrogen receptor-containing neurons that makes these animals more aggressive. And it turns out there's evidence that in certain contexts, estrogen can make people more aggressive.

What's going on is that testosterone can be converted into estrogen through a process called aromatization. There's an enzyme called aromatase — anytime you have a word ending in A-S-E, in biology it's almost always an enzyme. The aromatase enzyme converts testosterone into estrogen, and it is actually testosterone aromatized into estrogen and then binding to these estrogen-containing neurons in the ventromedial hypothalamus that triggers aggression.

To repeat that: it is not testosterone itself that triggers aggression. It is testosterone aromatized into estrogen within the brain and binding to these estrogen receptor-containing neurons in the ventromedial hypothalamus that evokes aggression — and dramatic aggression at that.

This effect of estrogen causing aggression in the brain is very robust — so much so that if you take a mouse that lacks the aromatase enzyme (or a human that lacks it, and they do exist), there is a reduction in overall aggression despite high levels of testosterone. And if the aromatase enzyme is blocked, it doesn't matter how much you increase testosterone — you do not observe this aggression.

There are beautiful data showing that whether or not estrogen stimulates aggression can be powerfully modulated by whether or not days are short or days are long — that is, whether or not there's a lot of sunshine or not. The brain is encased in skull, so it doesn't directly know if there's sunshine, but day length is converted into hormonal signals. In long days, melatonin levels are reduced (melatonin tends to produce states of sleepiness), dopamine is increased, and the stress hormones — especially cortisol — are reduced. If estrogen levels are increased experimentally under long-day conditions, it does not evoke aggression. However, in short days, if estrogen is increased, there's a heightened predisposition for aggression.

That makes perfect sense. In short days, the melatonin signal goes up. Stress hormones circulate more. Short days tend to be associated with winter, when we are bombarded with more bacterial viruses — viruses survive better in cold. Shorter days are conducive to aggression not because days are short per se, but because stress hormone levels are higher and dopamine levels are lower.

Under conditions where cortisol is high and where the neuromodulator serotonin is reduced, there is a greater propensity for estrogen to trigger aggression. Estrogen in long days doesn't increase aggression; estrogen in short days — when cortisol is elevated and serotonin lower — increases aggression. This is all converging on a very clear biological and psychological picture, and indeed a clear set of tools we can use.

Serotonin, the neuromodulator associated with feelings of wellbeing, when low, creates a further shift toward aggressive tendency. So returning to Lorenz's hydraulic pressure model, external stimuli — things we hear or see — plus our internal state — stress levels, feelings of contentment — are all converging to create this pressure of either more aggressive or less aggressive tendency.

When we are well fed — in particular well fed with carbohydrates — serotonin tends to be released. The precursor to serotonin is tryptophan. There are nice studies exploring diets that can reduce aggressive behavior in children and adults. Tryptophan-rich diets — things like white turkey meat, and many carbohydrates — contain the precursor to serotonin. Increasing levels of tryptophan either by supplementation or food, or drugs that increase serotonin like fluoxetine (Prozac), Zoloft, or any of the other SSRIs, tend to reduce aggressive behavior. Similarly, if cortisol levels are reduced, the tendency for aggressive behavior is reduced.

## [00:31:57] Neural Circuits Mediating Physical Acts of Aggression, Biting

Dayu Lin's lab has shown that activation of the ventromedial hypothalamus triggers a downstream circuit in the periaqueductal gray that then triggers a whole other set of circuits of fixed action patterns — including swinging of the limbs, punching, and also biting behavior. We have circuits in our brain that can evoke violent use of our mouth or violent use of our limbs.

In humans and especially in human children, biting is something that while young children might do as a form of aggression, tends to disappear pretty early in childhood. If it doesn't, it's often seen as a mark of pathology. There is general agreement in the psychology and psychiatric community that past a certain age, using one's teeth to impart aggression is a particularly primitive and troubling behavior.

The key point is that neural circuits — not individual brain areas — evoke the constellation of behaviors that we call aggression.

## [00:35:56] Testosterone & Competitiveness/Estrogen & Aggression

So we've established that it's not testosterone but testosterone converted into estrogen that activates these circuits for aggression. Testosterone has the net effect of making effort feel good, or at least increasing the threshold at which effort feels bad or unsustainable. It does that by changing the activity or the threshold for activation of brain structures like the amygdala.

There are some studies carried out in humans that have evaluated the effects of testosterone and how levels of testosterone correlate with aggressiveness in the short term. There is a study that explored levels of testosterone in men of different professions: ministers, salesmen, firemen, professors, physicians, and NFL players. What they discovered was that testosterone levels were essentially in that order from low to highest — minister, salesman, fireman, professor, physician, NFL player. One never knows whether these men went into a particular profession because they had a given testosterone level, or whether the work itself altered their testosterone levels — it's probably both.

Cortisol and testosterone are generally in somewhat antagonistic push-pull mode because they derive from the same precursor — when cortisol is high, testosterone tends to be lower. Each of these professions involves different levels of competitiveness, and competitive interactions can cause increases in testosterone — particularly in the winners of competitive interactions.

Studies that analyzed salivary testosterone in female prisoners — incarcerated individuals — found that higher levels of salivary testosterone were related to those who had arrived in prison because of conviction of a violent crime as opposed to a nonviolent crime. Likewise, when examining prison rule violations, the testosterone levels of women with no prison violations tended to be lower than those with even one or more aggressive violations of prison rules.

There was also a very interesting study from Goetz et al., published in 2014, that looked at serum blood levels of testosterone 30 minutes after application of a gel-based testosterone that goes transdermal. Brain imaging showed increased activity of neurons in the so-called corticomedial amygdala — an area of the amygdala complex associated with aggressive-type behaviors, linked as part of the larger circuit that includes the ventromedial hypothalamus and the PAG.

Just 30 minutes after application of this testosterone gel, there was a significant increase in corticomedial amygdala activation. Testosterone can have acute, immediate effects on the pathways related to aggression. While steroid hormones can pass through cell membranes and change gene expression slowly (as in puberty), they can also have very fast-acting effects. The amygdala harbors both corticosteroid receptors and testosterone receptors, and they each adjust amygdala activity differently — testosterone tends to activate amygdala circuitry for inducing states that are more action-based. In animals and humans, testosterone application and activation of this corticomedial amygdala pathway makes organisms lean into effort.

So: estrogen is activating aggression pathways in the ventromedial hypothalamus, but testosterone is acting to accelerate or bias states of mind and body toward those that will lead to aggression. Think of testosterone as increasing the pressure toward an aggressive episode, and then estrogen actually triggering that aggressive episode in the ventromedial hypothalamus.

## [00:43:00] Sunlight, Melatonin & Aggression

## [01:09:27] Caffeine, Alcohol & Impulsivity

Now, there are some things that tend to bias certain social contexts toward being more aggressive. Those generally come in two forms: alcohol and caffeine.

Why would caffeine increase aggressive impulsivity? The general effects of caffeine are to increase autonomic arousal — the activity of the so-called sympathetic arm of the autonomic nervous system — which in plain language is the alertness arm of your nervous system. That is, it creates a sense of readiness in your brain and body, activating the sympathetic chain ganglia, which run from about the base of your neck until the top of your pelvis, deploying chemicals that activate adrenaline release. There's a parallel increase in adrenaline in your brain, creating the state of alertness and readiness. That state of readiness can be for all sorts of things, not just aggression. However, when in a state of increased sympathetic tone after drinking caffeine, we bias all those brain and body systems toward action as opposed to inaction. So put simply, caffeine can increase impulsivity.

On the opposite end of things, alcohol tends to decrease activity in the sympathetic arm of the autonomic nervous system, making us feel less alert. Initially it can create a state of alertness because of its effects in inhibiting the forebrain — our prefrontal cortex in particular has what's called top-down inhibition, exerting a quieting effect on circuits of the hypothalamus, such as the ventromedial hypothalamus. Your forebrain is able to rationalize, think clearly, suppress behavior, and engage the no-go pathways — telling you, "Don't say that mean thing. Don't do that violent thing."

Alcohol initially tends to increase our level of overall activity by reducing inhibition — not just in that forebrain circuit, but in other circuits — tending to make us more active and talkative. But very shortly thereafter it starts acting as a sedative by reducing activity in the forebrain, releasing some of the deeper brain circuits involved in impulsivity, and causing a somewhat sedative effect.

So caffeine and alcohol are at two opposite ends of the spectrum: caffeine increasing arousal and readiness and the tendency for impulsivity, and alcohol also increasing impulsivity, but through a different mechanism.

They looked at people who ingested non-caffeinated alcohol drinks at a frequency of 9.18 drinks per week, and those drinking at least one caffeinated alcoholic beverage per week — some as high as 7.87 caffeinated alcohol beverages per week. The basic outcome was that the more alcohol someone tended to consume, the more likely it was that they would engage in indirect aggressive-type behaviors. This means that even though alcohol can bias certain individuals to be more aggressive, and even though certain individuals already have a disposition toward being more aggressive, there was an effect independent of both — above and beyond alcohol and predisposition — meaning someone consuming caffeinated alcoholic beverages had a particularly high likelihood of engaging in indirect aggressive behavior.

The combination of caffeine and alcohol is really acting as a two-pronged system to bias people toward more impulsivity — less self-regulation. The study title is "Efficacy of carnitine in the treatment of children with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder." What they focused on was whether or not acetyl-L-carnitine supplementation could adjust the behavioral tendency of these kids with ADHD — and to make a long story short, indeed it did. There was a very significant effect of acetyl-L-carnitine supplementation on improving some of the symptomology of ADHD.

A few details: This was a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled double crossover study, done as an outpatient study. These were six to thirteen-year-old kids diagnosed with ADHD. They received either acetyl-L-carnitine or placebo — the researchers made sure both had similar look and taste. It was consumed twice daily after meals. The carnitine dosage was 100 milligrams per kilogram of body weight, with a maximum dosage of four grams per day. This was a fairly long-term study exploring behavioral and psychological outcomes at week eight, sixteen, and twenty-four. They also monitored blood markers — hemoglobin, hematocrit, red blood cell count, white blood cell count — to ensure blood levels of carnitine were increasing and no negative effects were showing up.

The basic outcome: "Given twice daily, carnitine appeared to be effective and well-tolerated treatment for a group of children with ADHD." They showed significant reductions in their "Total Problem Score" — a well-established measure of behavioral problems in kids with ADHD. Reductions in intentional problems, overall reductions in delinquency, and most importantly for today's discussion, significant reductions in aggressive behavior.

What's especially nice about this study is that they were able to confirm the shifts in L-carnitine within the bloodstream of these kids — correlating the physiology with the psychological changes. This increases our confidence that things like L-carnitine can be used perhaps in concert with things like omega-3 supplementation, diets biased toward increasing tryptophan and therefore serotonin, obviously avoiding alcohol, and as it appears from this study, reducing intake of caffeinated alcoholic beverages.
