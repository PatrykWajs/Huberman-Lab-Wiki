# Episode 212: How to Navigate Menopause & Perimenopause for Maximum Health & Vitality | Dr. Mary Claire Haver

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Mary Claire Haver, a board-certified OB/GYN and menopause specialist, joins Andrew Huberman to explain the biology of perimenopause and menopause — from the hormonal "zone of chaos" that drives chaotic estrogen swings during the perimenopausal transition, to the systemic effects on brain health, cardiovascular function, body composition, bone density, gut microbiome, and mental health. The episode dismantles decades of misinformation stemming from the misinterpreted 2002 Women's Health Initiative trial and establishes that the timing of HRT initiation — not its use per se — is the key variable determining cardiovascular risk and benefit. Dr. Haver details a full suite of interventions: high-protein anti-inflammatory nutrition (the Galveston Diet), resistance training, weighted vest walking, creatine and Verisol collagen supplementation, and transdermal estradiol plus micronized progesterone and testosterone. The discussion also covers PCOS, GLP-1 medications, genitourinary syndrome of menopause, sleep disruption, libido, and how the medical establishment failed a generation of women after the WHI press conference halted menopause education for over two decades.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Menopause is one day; perimenopause is the real transition — lasting 7–10 years.** The clinical definition (one year after the final menstrual period) fails women with IUDs, hysterectomies, ablations, or PCOS who may not track periods. Perimenopause begins when egg supply falls below a critical threshold and the ovary grows resistant to FSH/LH signals, typically 7–10 years before that final period. [00:06:26](transcript.md#002604-menopause-age-of-onset) / [00:09:50](transcript.md#005009-perimenopause-hormones--zone-of-chaos)

2. **Average US menopause onset is 51–52; normal range is 45–55, with wide genetic variation.** Genetics is the single largest predictor. Smoking accelerates onset by years; hysterectomy with ovaries retained shortens ovarian lifespan by ~4 years; tubal ligation by ~1.5 years. African-American women go through menopause ~1.5 years earlier; Asian women tend to go later. Knowing when your mother and maternal aunts went through menopause is clinically meaningful. [00:06:26](transcript.md#002604-menopause-age-of-onset) / [00:20:04](transcript.md#002004-perimenopause-symptoms-tool-lifestyle-factors--ovarian-health)

3. **Perimenopause creates a "zone of hormonal chaos."** The ovary becomes resistant to FSH/LH pulses — the brain keeps sending larger and larger signals — resulting in estradiol surges far above normal reproductive levels followed by deep troughs. This volatility drives brain fog, anxiety, palpitations, joint pain, dysfunctional uterine bleeding, vertigo, gut changes, skin crawling, and fatigue. [00:09:50](transcript.md#005009-perimenopause-hormones--zone-of-chaos)

4. **Mental health effects of perimenopause are profound and underrecognized.** SSRI use doubles across the menopause transition; Australian data shows a ~4-fold increase in depression and anxiety disorders during perimenopause. One in five women leaves her job due to menopause symptoms (UK data, mirrored in US). Emerging research shows giving estrogen to perimenopausal women with new-onset depression outperforms SSRIs. [00:14:42](transcript.md#001442-perimenopause-estrogen--mental-health)

5. **The 2002 Women's Health Initiative (WHI) trial was catastrophically misinterpreted.** The trial enrolled women averaging 63 years old — many with pre-existing cardiovascular disease — more than a decade after menopause onset, and excluded women with hot flashes. A press conference at the Watergate Hotel declared "estrogen causes cancer" before the data were published. The absolute increased risk of breast cancer in the estrogen+progestin arm was 1 extra case per 1,000 women per year; the estrogen-only arm actually showed a 30% *decrease* in breast cancer risk. [01:06:29](transcript.md#010629-hot-flashes-estrogen-hormone-replacement-therapy-hrt-breast-cancer-risk--cognition)

6. **The timing hypothesis: HRT initiated within 10 years of menopause onset or before age 60 is cardioprotective.** American Heart Association (2020) analysis found women who started HRT between ages 50–59 had a 50% decreased risk of cardiovascular disease, death from CVD, and all-cause mortality. Starting HRT on top of established atherosclerotic plaques may loosen plaques — increasing short-term stroke risk — which is where the WHI's older cohort ran into trouble. [01:06:29](transcript.md#010629-hot-flashes-estrogen-hormone-replacement-therapy-hrt-breast-cancer-risk--cognition) / [01:15:36](transcript.md#011536-estrogen-hrt-cardiovascular-disease-blood-clotting-meno-posse)

7. **Body composition, not weight, is the key metric in menopause.** Women do not gain weight faster than the secular aging curve, but they lose muscle and gain visceral fat. A premenopausal woman has ~8% of body fat as visceral fat; after the transition it rises to ~23% with no changes in diet or exercise. Waist-to-hip ratio (WHR) measured relaxed in the morning is a better cardiometabolic predictor than BMI: <0.70 = low risk; >1.0 = elevated risk for women. [00:48:31](transcript.md#004831-tools-body-composition-muscle--menopause-protein-intake) / [00:51:42](transcript.md#005142-menopause-genetics-symptoms-tools-waist-to-hip-ratio-gut-microbiome)

8. **Protein intake is the single most modifiable dietary lever.** Most women consume 50–60 g/day; WHI data shows women with 1.5–1.7 g/kg lean body mass/day have substantially lower frailty scores. Practically, 80–120 g/day depending on body composition. Distribute protein across all meals; most women stack protein at dinner while skipping it at breakfast. Collagen supplements total intake but is not a complete protein. [00:48:31](transcript.md#004831-tools-body-composition-muscle--menopause-protein-intake) / [00:58:22](transcript.md#005822-galveston-vs-mediterranean-diet-fasting-tool-building-muscle)

9. **Fiber intake should reach 25–32 g/day; most women get 10–12 g.** Fiber feeds the gut microbiome, slows glucose absorption, and co-delivers vitamins, minerals, and phytochemicals. The Zoe Nutrition study of 1,100 women documented gut microbiome changes through the menopause transition — shifting from a typical female microbiome profile toward a male one as sex hormones decline. Fermented foods and Mediterranean-pattern eating correlate with better menopause symptom scores. [00:45:01](transcript.md#004501-tool-diet-preparing-for-perimenopause-visceral-fat) / [00:51:42](transcript.md#005142-menopause-genetics-symptoms-tools-waist-to-hip-ratio-gut-microbiome)

10. **HRT is titrated by symptom resolution, not blood estradiol levels.** There are no established therapeutic estradiol ranges for HRT; two women at identical serum estradiol concentrations may have entirely different symptom burdens. Transdermal estradiol plus micronized progesterone (women with a uterus) is the preferred formulation. Oral micronized progesterone taken at bedtime improves sleep via GABAergic mechanisms. [01:24:00](transcript.md#012400-estrogen--testosterone-starting-hrt--ranges)

11. **Transdermal testosterone in physiologic doses (target ~50–70 ng/dL) supports libido, mood, muscle, and bone.** There is no FDA-approved testosterone product for women in the US; it is compounded transdermally. Women in the highest quartile of testosterone have better bone density and stronger muscles. HSDD (hypoactive sexual desire disorder) is the most common sexual dysfunction in menopausal women; testosterone is first-line when relationship issues and pain have been ruled out. [01:24:00](transcript.md#012400-estrogen--testosterone-starting-hrt--ranges) / [02:09:09](transcript.md#020909-male-support-rekindle-libido)

12. **Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM) affects all women eventually and is undertreated.** Low-dose vaginal estradiol or vaginal DHEA (Intrarosa/prasterone) treat local tissue atrophy, painful intercourse, urinary urgency, and recurrent UTIs with minimal systemic absorption. Safe for most women with breast cancer history per oncologist guidance. Vaginal estrogen is also the best treatment for recurrent UTIs in menopausal women — not repeat antibiotics. [01:30:36](transcript.md#013036-other-hormones-thyroid--dhea-local-treatment-urinary-symptoms)

13. **Osteoporosis is a preventable condition — intervention must start in the 30s and 40s.** 50% of women will have an osteoporotic fracture before they die; hip fracture after age 65 carries 30% one-year mortality with surgical repair (79% without). Dr. Haver's "Osteoporosis Prevention Pack": creatine monohydrate 5 g/day, Verisol bioactive collagen ~5 g/day, weighted vest walking (start at 10% body weight), resistance training, vibration platform, vitamin D3+K2, and HRT when indicated. [01:41:30](transcript.md#014130-supplements-fiber-tools-osteoporosis-prevention-pack) / [01:46:53](transcript.md#014653-collagen-cellulite-bone-density)

14. **Women over 60 who have never used HRT can still benefit, with cardiovascular screening.** If coronary calcium scoring and carotid intimal-medial thickness ultrasound show no established atherosclerotic disease, late starters retain benefits for bone, skin, and genitourinary health. No arbitrary cutoff: "I will probably die with my estradiol patch on if I don't develop a reason to take it off." [02:01:55](transcript.md#020155-post-menopause--hrt-sustained-hrt-usage)

15. **PCOS is primarily driven by insulin resistance; GLP-1 medications are resolving it in obese patients — and causing unintended pregnancies.** Dr. Haver had PCOS throughout her reproductive life despite being thin. GLP-1 receptor agonists help obese PCOS patients resume ovulation, producing a surge of unintended pregnancies. Women on both HRT and GLP-1s show ~30% greater weight loss than GLP-1 alone. GLP-1s may also reduce impulsive behaviors (alcohol, gambling, gaming) via hypothalamic reward pathway modulation. [01:55:27](transcript.md#015527-polycystic-ovary-syndrome-pcos--treatment-glp-1-addictive-behaviors)

16. **Only 10% of symptomatic menopausal women are offered any therapy; most get antidepressants 4:1 over HRT.** Less than 0.03% of NIH funding goes to menopause research. Statins — routinely prescribed for the elevated LDL that appears in 80% of women during the menopause transition — have never been shown to reduce *primary* heart attack risk in women (AHA, 2020). The American Board of OB/GYN exam had no menopause category for over a decade after the WHI. [01:15:36](transcript.md#011536-estrogen-hrt-cardiovascular-disease-blood-clotting-meno-posse) / [01:37:57](transcript.md#013757-obgyn-medical-education--menopause)

## Tools & Protocols

### Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition (Galveston / Mediterranean Pattern)
- **What:** High-fiber (25–32 g/day), protein-adequate (80–120 g/day), colorful-phytonutrient-rich diet minimizing refined carbohydrates and pro-inflammatory foods. The Galveston Diet is an Americanized, menopause-focused implementation of Mediterranean principles created by Dr. Haver after completing her culinary medicine certification.
- **How:** Prioritize legumes, vegetables, and whole grains for fiber; diverse plant colors for phytochemicals; quality protein at every meal and snack (healthy fat + good carb + protein per sitting). Pair with a time-restricted eating window (e.g., 16:8) only if protein targets can still be met. Fermented foods (yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut, miso) support gut microbiome health.
- **When:** Start in the 30s — before perimenopause — to reduce baseline inflammation and build metabolic resilience heading into the transition.
- **Ref:** [00:45:01](transcript.md#004501-tool-diet-preparing-for-perimenopause-visceral-fat); [00:58:22](transcript.md#005822-galveston-vs-mediterranean-diet-fasting-tool-building-muscle)

### Resistance Training for Muscle & Bone Preservation
- **What:** Progressive resistance exercise targeting all major muscle groups, minimum 2–3 sessions per week with progressive overload. Compound lifts prioritized.
- **How:** Dr. Haver does 3–4 days/week. She reduced marathon running and aerobics in favor of heavy resistance training. Hiring a trainer to design a program and avoid injury is recommended for those new to lifting.
- **When:** Ideally before perimenopause; continue through and after menopause indefinitely. Primary non-hormonal stimulus for preserving both muscle and bone.
- **Ref:** [00:58:22](transcript.md#005822-galveston-vs-mediterranean-diet-fasting-tool-building-muscle); [01:41:30](transcript.md#014130-supplements-fiber-tools-osteoporosis-prevention-pack)

### Protein Intake Optimization
- **What:** Targeted daily protein to preserve lean mass and reduce frailty risk; 1.5–1.7 g/kg lean body mass/day (80–120 g/day for most women).
- **How:** Distribute across all meals; include protein with breakfast (most women skip it), lunch, and dinner. Avoid stacking all protein at the evening meal. Collagen powder can supplement totals but does not replace complete proteins (missing one or two essential amino acids). Fasting windows should not compress protein intake below targets.
- **When:** Ongoing from the 30s forward; especially critical around and after perimenopause as anabolic resistance increases.
- **Ref:** [00:48:31](transcript.md#004831-tools-body-composition-muscle--menopause-protein-intake); [00:58:22](transcript.md#005822-galveston-vs-mediterranean-diet-fasting-tool-building-muscle)

### Waist-to-Hip Ratio (WHR) Monitoring
- **What:** Simple tape-measure proxy for visceral fat and cardiometabolic risk; better than BMI or scale weight for tracking menopause-related metabolic changes.
- **How:** Measure the smallest part of the waist (or use the navel if no natural waist definition); measure the widest part of the hips. Both measurements relaxed, first thing in the morning with bladder empty. Divide waist by hips. For women: <0.70 = low visceral fat risk; >1.0 = elevated visceral fat. Track monthly.
- **When:** Quarterly or after body composition changes. InBody impedance scanner or DEXA are gold-standard alternatives if accessible.
- **Ref:** [00:51:42](transcript.md#005142-menopause-genetics-symptoms-tools-waist-to-hip-ratio-gut-microbiome)

### Hormone Replacement Therapy — Estrogen + Progesterone
- **What:** Transdermal estradiol (patch, gel, spray, or vaginal ring) plus micronized progesterone (for women with an intact uterus) to restore estrogen to a symptom-free level.
- **How:** Start low, titrate by symptom resolution — not by targeting specific blood estradiol levels. Transdermal preferred over oral to avoid hepatic first-pass effect on clotting factor synthesis. Oral micronized progesterone taken at bedtime exploits GABAergic sleep-promoting properties. Avoid synthetic progestins (e.g., medroxyprogesterone) when micronized progesterone is tolerated.
- **When:** Perimenopausal onset (low dose to dampen hypothalamic chaos) or early post-menopause. Initiate within 10 years of menopause onset or before age 60 for full cardiovascular benefit. Women >10 years post-menopause or >60 require coronary calcium scoring and carotid IMT ultrasound before initiating.
- **Ref:** [01:06:29](transcript.md#010629-hot-flashes-estrogen-hormone-replacement-therapy-hrt-breast-cancer-risk--cognition); [01:24:00](transcript.md#012400-estrogen--testosterone-starting-hrt--ranges)

### Testosterone Replacement Therapy (Women)
- **What:** Transdermal testosterone in physiologic doses to support libido, mood, muscle retention, cognition, and bone density.
- **How:** Compounded transdermal cream; target ~50–70 ng/dL, checking levels after titration. Signs of excess (>90 ng/dL): temporal hair loss, voice deepening, acne, new chin hair. No FDA-approved product for women in the US; cost ~$30/month compounded.
- **When:** Any stage of the menopause journey when HSDD, persistent low drive, mood issues, osteopenia, or sarcopenia are present. Rule out relationship issues and pain/arousal disorders first. Two FDA-approved libido medications exist (bremelanotide/Vyleesi; flibanserin/Addyi) but testosterone is preferred for cost and multi-benefit profile.
- **Ref:** [01:24:00](transcript.md#012400-estrogen--testosterone-starting-hrt--ranges); [01:30:36](transcript.md#013036-other-hormones-thyroid--dhea-local-treatment-urinary-symptoms); [02:09:09](transcript.md#020909-male-support-rekindle-libido)

### Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM) — Local Treatment
- **What:** Topical/vaginal estrogen and DHEA to treat local tissue atrophy, urinary urgency, painful intercourse, and recurrent UTIs with minimal systemic HRT exposure.
- **How:** Low-dose vaginal estradiol cream, ring, or tablet; or vaginal DHEA (Intrarosa/prasterone — converts locally to both testosterone and estradiol). Topical estriol facial cream slows the ~30% collagen loss seen in the first five years post-menopause. Minimal systemic absorption at vaginal doses; safe even for most women with breast cancer history per oncologist guidance.
- **When:** Any post-menopausal woman with GSM symptoms. Can be used independently of or in addition to systemic HRT.
- **Ref:** [01:30:36](transcript.md#013036-other-hormones-thyroid--dhea-local-treatment-urinary-symptoms)

### Osteoporosis Prevention Pack
- **What:** Multi-modal bone-preservation protocol to begin in the 30s–40s, not after fracture.
- **How:**
  - **Creatine monohydrate** 5 g/day — supports muscle and has emerging bone density data
  - **Verisol bioactive collagen peptides** ~5 g/day — 5-year RCT in menopausal women showed bone density improvements vs. placebo
  - **Weighted vest** (start at 10% of body weight) — worn during walks, household tasks, or added to leg-day workouts; applies osteogenic loading to the axial skeleton
  - **Resistance training** — non-negotiable primary stimulus for bone remodeling
  - **Vibration platform / shake plate** — adjunct in limited-mobility populations
  - **Vitamin D3 + K2** — 80% of Dr. Haver's patients are deficient; monitor 25-OH-D levels
  - **HRT when indicated** — preserves bone density at any age
- **When:** Begin in the 30s; intensify around perimenopause. Do not wait for an osteoporosis diagnosis.
- **Ref:** [01:41:30](transcript.md#014130-supplements-fiber-tools-osteoporosis-prevention-pack); [01:46:53](transcript.md#014653-collagen-cellulite-bone-density)

### Visceral Fat Reduction
- **What:** Multi-modal approach targeting visceral (metabolically active) fat specifically — not just cosmetic subcutaneous fat.
- **How:** Anti-inflammatory diet + time-restricted eating + resistance training + systemic HRT when indicated (women on HRT accumulate significantly less visceral fat than untreated controls). Track via WHR or InBody scan, not scale weight. Women on HRT + GLP-1 show ~30% greater weight loss than GLP-1 alone.
- **When:** Proactively before perimenopause; aggressively during the transition when visceral fat rises from ~8% to ~23% of total body fat.
- **Ref:** [02:12:46](transcript.md#021246-hrt-rash-side-effect-acupuncture-visceral-fat)
