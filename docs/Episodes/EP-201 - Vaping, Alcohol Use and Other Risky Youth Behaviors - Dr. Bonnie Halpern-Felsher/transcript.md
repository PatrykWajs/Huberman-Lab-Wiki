## [00:05:38] Adolescence

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. Halpern-Felsher, welcome, thank you so much for having me. We're going to talk about a very important and sometimes troubling period of life — not always troubling, but I think for everyone, adolescence and the teen years, youth essentially, is a tricky landscape. As our brains and bodies mature, we have more autonomy in where we take them, but that means also more exposure to the ideas, suggestions, actions, and peer pressure of others, and that's sometimes where the problems arise. What is adolescence in the teen years like, what's going on psychologically that we might not be aware of?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Sure. So first of all, ages — adolescence can be anywhere from starting roughly around age ten; some people would say ending around age 18, maybe 21 if you want to go into young adulthood, then maybe mid-twenties. And really adolescence is a wonderful time of, as you said, exploration. It's a time when, first of all, marked by pubertal changes, onset of menses for girls, and really the pubertal and physical development — secondary sexual characteristics are coming out. We also have a lot of emotional development going on during this time. Height changes are occurring. But we're getting a lot of social changes as well, and peer pressures.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** One misconception is parents think that they don't matter during adolescence — they still really matter. But peers also come in and matter quite a bit. Teens are really trying to figure out who they are: "Who am I? Where am I going in life? What do I want to do when I grow up? What's important to me? How do other people feel about me?" A lot of the social and psychosocial development is happening. You also get asynchronous development — if a young person starts puberty at a younger age, say ten, where they're physically looking older and more mature, but emotionally and psychosocially they still might be young. It's really this wonderful time of exploration for an adolescent, and a time of really wanting autonomy and wanting to make a lot of decisions that we should like them make, but there are some risks that we have to be careful about at the same time.

## [00:09:19] Household Conflict, Parents; Smart Phones

**Andrew Huberman:** In the last 20–30 years in this country there's been a marked increase in the frequency of divorce. Is there any direct evidence that single-parent homes or divorced homes are creating more challenges in terms of risk-taking behavior in adolescents?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** What you're saying is pretty accurate to what I've seen. The literature would say it's not the divorce per se — it's the conflict that is happening. If parents actually get divorced and the conflict resolves, teenagers and children generally do well, particularly within about two years. You can have two parents who are married and living in the same home in a difficult relationship, but if there's no conflict, then generally teens will do well. The problem comes in whether parents are living at home together, separated, or divorced — if there's conflict. That conflict tends to result in social issues, emotional issues, a lot of social anxiety, depression, and with depression can come self-medication. But we're seeing adolescent angst regardless of parenting.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** What we really need is parenting that's important — parents being good monitors, being involved in their kids' lives. Not "you're 16, you have a car, you can go wherever you want." We still need parents to monitor, to pay attention, to find out their kids' friends, where they're going after school. That discretionary time when parents are working and teenagers come home between 3 and 5 or 3 and 6 tends to be the most risky couple of hours — it's called discretionary hours where there's no parent around. Knowing where their children are and what they're doing, and that there's some adult presence and some monitoring, is more important than whether or not the parents are still in a relationship.

## [00:18:25] Vaping, E-Cigarettes, Nicotine & Cannabis

**Andrew Huberman:** Maybe now would be a good time to talk about risky behaviors or even just behaviors that are known to have some detriment to health — smoking and vaping and e-cigarettes primarily. What are the statistics on smoking, vaping, and e-cigarettes?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** The good news is smoking rates — conventional cigarette smoking rates — have gone down pretty dramatically in the last couple of decades with teenagers, to well below 5% of teenagers. That's the good news. E-cigarette use, which I prefer over the term "vaping" because they're aerosols, not vapes — has gone up pretty dramatically. E-cigarettes came on the market in the U.S. in 2007. It was really in 2017 to 2019 that we saw a dramatic increase — upwards of 27 to 29% of teens using e-cigarettes, and something like a 78% increase in high school student use and a 48% increase in middle school use over those couple of years.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Since 2019 it's gone down. In 2020, during COVID, initiation of e-cigarette use really occurs socially and teens were not at school. The latest data show rates under 10%, but I actually don't think it's true. I'm in schools doing curriculum presentations all the time — we've never been busier than we are right now with schools crying for help. Schools are telling me it's 40 to 60% of their students using e-cigarettes. We have some national data suggesting it's more in the 20 to 30 to 40% range as well. Whatever it is, it's too many teens who are inhaling nicotine and cannabis as well.

## [00:23:46] Adolescent Nicotine Use: Marketing, Flavors

**Andrew Huberman:** Nicotine — known cognitive enhancer, also known to dramatically increase blood pressure and vasoconstriction, not healthy for the body, highly addictive. By my observation, very few people can use nicotine occasionally. What do we know about why they're actually starting and why they're continuing?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Why does a teenager try nicotine? There are a few reasons. One has to do with marketing — no doubt. The devices themselves are cool-looking, easy to hide. They look like USB devices, like highlighters. In fact, there's a new brand called Highlight that is a working highlighter — but take the cover off and it's actually a nicotine e-cigarette device. Clearly marketed toward students. You have products that look like boba tea, Star Wars shapes, cartoon shapes that are clearly being targeted to not just teenagers but children — young children. I am getting elementary school teachers calling us for help. They are catching second- and third-graders using nicotine e-cigarettes. We actually created an elementary school curriculum because so many schools across the country called us.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** The other reason is flavors — and flavors and marketing go hand in hand. These products smell and taste like sugar, like sweets, like dessert, like candy. The names are things like "unicorn poop" and "sugar booger" and "honey doo." That's not for adults — those names are squarely for kids. The marketing around them are these animated, Pizzazz images of pineapples dancing, strawberries dancing. There are juice-box-style vaping devices that look like juice boxes. That's not targeting you and me — that's targeting a kid.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Teens also like the rush. They like the buzz. Another reason is stress and coping — right now teens are so stressed out. They've been stressed out for years, but they're particularly stressed with the pandemic and even a couple years out, teens are still having socialization issues and social-emotional learning issues. We know there's a pretty strong relationship between stress, not being able to cope, and using any drug, including nicotine. It's not peer pressure per se — it's more like a lot of friends are using it. I've talked to teens who say, "I wasn't intending on it, but I tried it and I really like the taste, I like the flavor."

## [00:32:13] Nicotine Initiation, Freebase vs. Salt-Based Nicotine, Concentration

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about adult use of e-cigarettes by comparison, and then about what type of nicotine these devices use.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** The difference with teenagers is they're initiating with e-cigarettes — they're not substituting from cigarettes. In 2015 when newer products came on the market, it was a salt-based nicotine. Cigarettes and earlier e-cigarettes have a freebase nicotine. Freebase nicotine is very caustic — on the basic side of the litmus test — and if you're a nicotine-naive youth, when you start, you don't want that caustic throat-hit feeling. Teens will say they don't like it, they cough, it tasted bad.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** JUUL came on the market in 2015 with a salt-based nicotine, which uses benzoic acid to move that litmus test needle toward neutral. So when you use it, it's smooth — you don't have that throat hit, you don't cough, you don't feel sick from it. Teens will say they like it. Coupled with the flavors, it's also more absorbent, and there's some suggestion and early evidence that it's also more addictive.

**Andrew Huberman:** So the amount of nicotine in one of these pods is significantly greater than the amount in one pack of cigarettes in many cases?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Correct. Before earlier e-cigarettes had 0 to 36 milligrams of nicotine. Suddenly we jumped up to 59 milligrams per milliliter, which is generally about 40–41 milligrams. You're looking at anywhere from the nicotine in one to two packs of cigarettes per device per pod. Some teens were using a pod a week — about two or three cigarettes a day — to one to four pods a day. One to four pods means one to eight packs of cigarettes worth of nicotine. The newer e-cigarettes have probably four times that. They're equivalent to about 300 to 500 cigarettes worth of nicotine. And this is the first nicotine product you can use 24/7. I've talked to teens who say they wake up in the middle of the night to take a hit — they're hiding them under their pillows, in their nightstands.

**Andrew Huberman:** So just to put this in context: some adolescents and teens between ages 10 and 21 are going through as many as four pods per day, which could be the equivalent of four to eight packs of cigarettes per day in terms of nicotine concentration?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Correct. Now not all teenagers are using four pods a day — those are extremely highly addicted teens. Typically teens are using maybe a pod a day. But it's really concerning that a lot of this is happening, including while they're sleeping.

## [00:41:35] Addiction & Withdrawal; E-Cigarette Access

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** What we're finding when we talk to teens is that pretty rapidly they're going from "I like it" to "I need it." We actually published studies showing that teens who have been using e-cigarettes in the past 30 days — the majority are showing signs of addiction, pretty rapidly too, within a few weeks. It's such high levels of nicotine. A study by a colleague of mine showed that a greater percentage of teens who use e-cigarettes are doing so in the first five minutes of waking — that is a sign of addiction. Even though initiation may go down, the percentage of teens who are using daily has gone up, and I attribute that a lot to the type of nicotine, the salt-based nicotine, and the huge amount of nicotine on the market.

**Andrew Huberman:** Where are 12-year-olds getting the money to buy vape cartridges?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** It's a great question. In terms of money — some of the newer products were about a dollar or two per pod, compare that with a pack of cigarettes which is $10 to $15. They're cheap. There's also sharing — pod parties where somebody buys the device and you bring your own pod, pop it in, and share it around. We've heard stories of teenagers buying them and then selling for a few cents a puff — "meet me in the bathroom for 50 cents or a dollar." We've unfortunately seen parents buying e-cigarettes for their teens. Students using their lunch money to buy vapes.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** In December 2019 the legal age to be allowed to purchase nicotine products across the U.S. became 21. But a lot of vape shops don't know it's 21, or they're still selling to 18-year-olds anyway because there's not enforcement. Teens are getting them from vape shops really easily, getting them online really easily. We really need parents, educators, and police officers to enforce the age restriction. We have a product that is appealing to teens in a very unregulated market right now. The FDA is not regulating it, local shops are not regulating it — it is the wild west.

## [00:48:48] Vaping Health Hazards, Aldehydes, Flavors

**Andrew Huberman:** What do we know about the health hazards of vaping per se? Does it increase lung disease, cancer rates?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** First of all, start with the brain and nicotine — absolutely, these high levels of nicotine, and really any nicotine, is harmful to the developing brain. Our brains continue to develop until we're around 25. In that process of your brain developing and changing, if you introduce nicotine, you're changing the brain chemistry and you're so much more likely to become addicted as an adolescent. The tobacco industry knows this — that's why they target teens. If we target a teen, we're going to have them for life.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Now, e-cigarettes do not have tar, but they do have aldehydes. Aldehydes have been linked to cancer. There's also lead, cadmium, propylene glycol, and glycerin. Formaldehyde — the same chemical we use in laboratories to fix tissues — these cross-link proteins and basically kill tissue. When you inhale these things into your lungs and bloodstream, that can't be good. We're seeing significant effects on heart and lungs: lung collapses, pneumonia, asthma. One teen I know who was using four pods a day was having seizures — which makes sense because nicotine is a stimulant and can cause runaway excitability in the brain.

**Andrew Huberman:** When I was in high school we had to run laps. I can't imagine doing that if your lungs don't function properly.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Exactly. Teens will say it's much harder, and adults too. When you tell a teen, "I'm worried about lung cancer in 20 years," they don't care. But when you tell a teen it's harder to run, they're more likely to listen.

**Andrew Huberman:** I remember when I was a kid it was the "Just Say No to Drugs" era. There must be a lot of data indicating what messages kids respond to. That was effective.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** First of all, "Just Say No" is not effective — simply having someone say "just say no" around any behavior to a teenager, whether it's tobacco, cannabis, sex, does not work. And for many reasons. First of all, teens are curious. When you say "just say no," and they say "why? well because it's bad for you," and they tried the e-cigarette and they didn't have immediate lung problems and instead they actually liked it — then we look bad. **Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** What we talk to teens about — based on decision-making research I've been doing for 25 years — we have to help teens weigh the benefits and the risks. ## [01:14:11] Quitting Nicotine & Cannabis; Physical & Social Withdrawal Symptoms

**Andrew Huberman:** How difficult is it for adolescents and teens to quit vaping, e-cigarettes, and cannabis?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** It's really difficult. Both are addictive. Interestingly, not a lot of people realize that cannabis is addictive — about one in six people, particularly under the age of 25, do become addicted. Nicotine is probably the most difficult-to-quit drug out there. We have so many teens who are addicted to nicotine through e-cigarettes and really struggling to get off of it.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** There's not a lot that we have in terms of help. There are some inpatient programs, but those are expensive or require insurance coverage, and they take young people out of their natural environment. For outpatient programs, we don't have the best recommendations because we don't have great research. Nicotine replacement therapy — the patch — is not approved by the FDA for anyone under 18. But a lot of doctors are using nicotine patches off-label and prescribing them for someone under 18. If a teenager is using 40 milligrams of nicotine in a day and a patch is only 21 milligrams, one approach is to use one patch and supplement with gum, suckers, and lozenges as a form of treatment — to wean them off by reducing the dosage over time.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Withdrawal symptoms last about three to four minutes at a time. One teenager told me they have a playlist where each song is three to four minutes — when they start to feel the withdrawal, they pop the music in their ears and go do something, and by the time the song ends, that uncomfortable feeling is over. Others say they'll run. Chewing on a regular toothpick. But we also have to know that with adults and cigarettes it can take 7 to 11 tries to quit, so we can't expect a teenager to quit overnight, especially with the social pressure.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** One of the things we do in our program to help teens quit is we talk about social withdrawal — not just physical. Our youth action board says we need to talk about social withdrawal, because teens may not be able to go to that party on Saturday night where they know their friends are vaping, because seeing or smelling or witnessing someone using an e-cigarette will cue up that craving. They actually have to isolate themselves from the friend group who was using. We tell parents: it's not the time to get pissed off with your kids for using — it's the time to really help them. Let's be in this together.

If we can get young people to rally around the environmental impact of vaping, we can also shift some of the culture.

## [01:30:08] Teen Activities, Social Media, Autonomy

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there evidence that the kids who are vaping nicotine or cannabis are less likely to be engaged in other activities? Is this becoming a closed loop of reward that bypasses the effort-reward reinforcement cycle?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** When we were talking about cigarettes back until probably 2014, I would say yes — it was generally teens who would say "I'm bored" or "I'm not an athlete" or "this was my social outlet." I haven't seen that as much with e-cigarettes as a cause of using. E-cigarettes are so ubiquitous now — it doesn't matter how old you are, male or female. We used to see more males using because some of the earlier devices were more tech-oriented, but now we're seeing more of an equal if not slightly more female using. It's not your so-called "bad kids" — it's everywhere, independent of location, race, ethnicity.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** As a result of e-cigarettes, I do see teens who become more isolated, more bored, more likely to sit home and vape. But we're not seeing them out in parks as much — and I think that's because of social media and just the whole change, and parents being afraid of letting their kids out due to predatory behavior concerns. The pendulum has almost swung too much — now parents have to keep their kid inside. But teens have to get into a little bit of trouble; they have to have that natural amount of getting together and hanging out and being crazy at the park. And I think that has stopped or slowed down a lot.

## [01:36:28] Risky Behaviors, Alcohol, Driving, Sexual Behavior

**Andrew Huberman:** What's going on now in terms of risk-taking behavior — drunk driving, doing dangerous stuff? Is it still true that males are doing more of that physical danger stuff than females?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** A lot of what I've seen is balanced out between the sexes — maybe different kinds of risk behaviors. We're still seeing teens drinking, still seeing teens going to parties and getting drunk. The big difference now is, from Mothers Against Destructive Driving — which includes drunk driving, racing, and driving under the influence of cannabis — at least we've gotten that word out. They're still drinking and still doing stupid stuff when they drink, but they're not getting behind the wheel as much. Uber and Lyft have certainly been a game-changer in the landscape. I've heard not just about a designated driver but a designated sober sitter — someone who goes to a party to make sure you're not going home with someone you don't want to go home with, not falling downstairs, not falling out of a window.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** More and more teens are not driving — they're delaying driving. Whether that's because of ride-sharing, it's less expensive for parents to pay for a ride share than for insurance for someone under 25. We're definitely still seeing risk behavior amongst teenagers, and part of it has to do with impulsivity. Until around age 16–17, teens are still very impulsive — the back of the brain, the amygdala, the emotional center and motor coordination, develops faster and first. The front of the brain — executive functioning, planning for the future, being able to think of risks and benefits — develops later. So you certainly see impulsive decision-making.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Rates of sexual behavior are stabilizing and going down, and certainly rates of risky sexual behavior have also gone down. We are getting the message across around condom use, STI testing, and birth control. And teen pregnancy, I believe, is down as well, though I'd need to check the latest numbers.

## [01:43:27] International E-Cigarette Use, Regulation

**Andrew Huberman:** Is what we're talking about today mostly within the United States, or does it carry over to other countries?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** It depends on the behavior. E-cigarette nicotine use has not been as high in a lot of other countries. For example, the UK has a nicotine standard — a maximum amount of nicotine you're allowed to have, around 1.7%. In the U.S. we have no nicotine standard, which is a major issue with regulation. Some products in the U.S. are at 5% or 10% nicotine — there's no regulation about how much nicotine you can have. The marketing was also not as directed at teens in other countries. That has changed in the last year or two. Countries like the UK that used to say they didn't have the same problem are now saying they're seeing a pretty significant increase. Even in countries that didn't allow certain e-cigarettes on the market — they've now infiltrated. And even in this country, certain e-cigarettes are illegal and they're coming in through illicit trading across borders.

## [01:46:10] Nicotine Pouches, Health Risks; Tolerance

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about Zyn pouches — no vaping, no e-cigarettes, no smoking, but a little nicotine pouch. Presumably there are risks including the addictive nature and blood pressure increase, but what do we know?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** The popular press is actually ahead of the scientific press on this one, and probably ahead of the science. We're seeing a very sharp increase in the market share of Zyn compared to other nicotine products. We published a study showing around 20 to 25% of adolescents and adults — and about 11 to 15% of teens — are using a pouch. The CDC showed about 1.1% to 1.5% of teens admitting to using pouches, which is not a huge increase, but a few hundred thousand teens are using, as opposed to 2.5 million-plus using e-cigarettes.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** What's most concerning is it seems like these are not existing e-cigarette users adding pouches — these are new initiates. So Zyn may be an on-ramp to nicotine. Each pouch of Zyn is 3 or 6 milligrams. There's no tobacco in the pouch itself — it's white powdered nicotine. My concern with smokeless tobacco has been oral cancer for years — you're putting this at the mucosal line and are we going to start seeing more oral cancer? What else is in there besides the nicotine? We really lack the research. Our study showed that many teens are using multiple pouches throughout the day, and some social media shows teens putting in several pouches at the same time. So you might be getting as much nicotine as a pack of cigarettes.

**Andrew Huberman:** A lot of people start with one or two pouches a day and quickly move to four or five, and the typical ceiling seems to be quickly reaching a canister a day — which I think is 15 to 20 pouches. At 3 to 6 milligrams each, that's up to 60 to 120 milligrams of nicotine — at least two packs of cigarettes worth of nicotine.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** That's right. We're getting so much nicotine now — not in the form of combustion, maybe in the form of e-cigarettes or pouches — but it's still a huge amount that very young brains are using. The brain doesn't do well developing with high, artificially high levels of any neuromodulator. And getting addicted so young means potentially a lifetime of addiction.

## [01:53:25] Tools: Vaping Interventions, Decision Making, Harm Reduction

**Andrew Huberman:** What sorts of messaging work? There's quitting, there's just saying no, there's harm reduction, there's replacement behaviors. What works — not just at the level of public health discourse, but parent to child, peer to peer, sibling to sibling?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** The most important thing is — and I've said this for years — have a conversation. Some people think that having a conversation about tobacco, cannabis, alcohol, or sex will get young people curious. That is not at all the case. There's nothing we can talk to a young person about that they don't already know. We're kidding ourselves to say we can't mention drugs to a 16-year-old because we're going to get them curious — they've known about drugs since they were eight years old.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** I often say to parents: start that conversation young, when your kids are really young. When your kids are four or five, the conversation might be about having a cookie or some grapes — "do you want to go to bed now or in five minutes?" — so they feel like they're making the decision. You need to start having conversations around decision-making and healthy decision-making very young. Not scheduling it — not "Saturday at 3 o'clock we're going to sit down and talk about sex or drugs." That does not work. You need organic conversations. See something on TV — "Oh, let's have a conversation about that." Just making it a natural part of life. I'm the queen of organic conversation.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** The other is harm reduction — or as I prefer to say, "comprehensive education." The most important part is of course not to use — not to have risky sex, not to use tobacco, not to use drugs at all. But how do we do that with a young person when you go into a classroom and 10–20% have already started using? If you shut them down, they don't feel seen — "they're not talking to me, why should I listen?" So the conversation has to acknowledge a spectrum of use from no use all the way up to addicted. The message is: best if you don't use, but if you do, let's help you cut back or quit. If you are continuing to use, let's keep you safe. All the research says those comprehensive messages are way more effective than just "don't use." Like a parent saying, "I hope you're not drinking, but if you are, pick up the phone and I'll come get you." That's harm reduction. That's putting safety first.

## [02:02:37] Fentanyl, Drug Testing, Recreational Drug Use

**Andrew Huberman:** You mentioned fentanyl. About once every four to eight months I hear about someone's kid or close relative who died of a fentanyl overdose.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** It was more in the twenties and thirties before, but now it's really getting into the teens and young adults. And some of the teens I know of who have died were not drug users. They needed a pill because they were in pain, or they needed something, and they were not told: "Don't buy something off the internet. If you do, test it. And make sure that you're not using alone — because if you're using alone, we can't give you naloxone." I carry naloxone in my backpack all the time, for anyone who might need it. I think every school should have naloxone, every library should have naloxone, every bar should have naloxone.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Fentanyl test strips — I believe in this concept of comprehensive drug education, and I've been working with some groups to test whether, if we put both naloxone and fentanyl test strips in schools, would teens get them? Even though I grapple with the message — if I saw a teenager taking a fentanyl test strip, my first inclination as a parent and scientist is to say "are you crazy, don't do that." But at the same time, if I know there's a chance a teen is going to go to a party and pick up a drug and not know what's in it, I'd rather that they're safe. The problem with fentanyl test strips is they're not perfect — if you're testing one side of the pill but it's the other side that has fentanyl, you could still die. This is not a one-stop fix. But if you have a group of youth who are going to use, I would still rather arm them with that information.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Fentanyl is making its way into all pharmaceuticals — benzos, MDMA — and teens are getting it not necessarily knowingly, as drug manufacturers and sellers are cutting drugs with fentanyl. Combining cannabis or nicotine and fentanyl — we're still learning about the biological mechanisms. Some teens are having fentanyl overdoses from cannabis or vaping. There's so much more research needed. I'm optimistic in seeing a change in the landscape of education and in seeing more teens willing to say no. But I'm not optimistic when it comes to the pharmacology — more and more drugs are infiltrating our youth.

## [02:13:45] Tool: Organic Conversations & Risky Behavior

**Andrew Huberman:** It seems like having conversations about these things is important — not turning a blind eye, not shying away from those conversations. And you said don't formalize the conversation — not "Saturday at 3 p.m. we're going to have a discussion about drugs." Make it part of the landscape to create ease and facilitate talking about these things.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Totally agree. I often say to parents or educators: if they're listening to this podcast or any podcast, it's actually fine to say "hey, I learned something — can we have a conversation about what I learned?" Not a confrontation but a conversation. Let's normalize drug discussions and behavior discussions. Not normalize the use or the behavior itself, but normalize talking about it. Talk to your teens. "This is what I just learned — what do you know? Can we find out some information together? Go on our REACH websites, go on other websites, learn information together." Not lecture but have a conversation.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** A teen may not tell you right away "yes I'm using" — that's okay. You're building trust. I often say: I'm not going to bust you, I certainly won't be happy about it, but let's have a conversation so we can build the trust. And then I can help you either not use, get help, stop using, be safer in using, or help you prevent starting at all. Meet youth where they're at — whether they're using already or not. Don't come at them with your preconceived notions as an adult because it will backfire. We need to use their strengths — young people are strong, smart. We need to use their strengths, take their lead, and then use our adult wisdom and experiences to turn that into the proper conversation.

## [02:17:20] Long-Term Goals & Teens; Vaping, Pornography & Teens

**Andrew Huberman:** One of the top questions from social media followers: Would love to learn more about how to get teens to see the longer-term implications of the choices they make. Is there any way to get them to understand how now leads to later?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** We've done studies where we ask teens about their goals — "I want to be a dancer," "I want to be a doctor" — and then connect their current behavior and current risks and keeping themselves healthy to how that plays into their goals. That tends to work a lot. Teens set boundaries: "I don't want to get pregnant because I want to be a dancer." Really linking what they're doing now to their ultimate goals is one way to help them think that through. If you can supersede these short-term behaviors with an even larger goal, they can see how those things link up.

**Andrew Huberman:** There's a question about positive news about teenagers — "every discussion is around risk, as if they're all dysfunctional." Can you highlight some ways in which the adolescent brain is exceptional?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Teenagers are fundamentally fantastic. They're creative, they're passionate. Teens care about the environment — probably more than adults do right now. Teens care about social justice. Teens care about our future and our planet. We should be capitalizing on that. We have 40 teenagers and young adults who work with us all the time on our work because we trust them. Some people are afraid of teens — I embrace them. I think they're fantastic.

**Andrew Huberman:** Do we know how vaping shapes the teen brain — any known biological changes as a consequence of vaping nicotine?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** During adolescence, nicotine changes the brain wiring. We're actually born with the nicotinic receptors — we're born with the ability to become addicted to nicotine. During adolescence, we're pruning away and getting rid of the connections and neurons that we don't need. During that process, anything that we don't use and don't reinforce goes away. But if we introduce nicotine into the brain, it solidifies and keeps that receptor. I think of it as cups — those receptors that were already there — filling those cups with nicotine. You then take away that nicotine and your cups say "I need more." So you're rewiring your brain, wiring it to be more likely to become addicted. That's what happens during adolescence and young adulthood that we don't see in adults — that's why we really want to keep young people away from nicotine as long as possible.

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there negative effects of pornography on the developing brain?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** I don't know as much about the developing brain specifically, but there's clear evidence about viewing pornography around just not having good, healthy sexual relationships. Porn is not normal — it is not a normal relationship between two people. You're really not necessarily developing a healthy relationship with your partner because you're assuming some gold standard out there that may or may not be able to be achieved. It's also a problem with body shaming. There's a great quote — if you don't teach teens, they're going to seek out information. Having teens learn about sex from porn is like having them learn physics from the Transformers or learn how to drive from Fast and Furious. We need to give teens the information.

## [02:24:08] Mental Health Crisis & Substance Use

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there any information about potential causality between the mental health crisis in youth and substance use?

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** There are plenty of studies showing the bidirectional relationship between anxiety, depression, mental health, and substance use. A lot of teens are self-medicating by using various substances to reduce anxiety and stress — also for social lubrication, going into a party and pregaming or using drugs to make themselves less stressed in that situation. But we also know that drugs lead to suicidal ideations, suicide attempts, psychosis, and other mental health issues. Drugs also lead to reduced academic achievement. There's also a lot of co-use — not just using multiple products but using them together. Many teens chase cannabis and tobacco because it enhances the high, or use cannabis with alcohol — mixing drugs that enhance the high but in a very scary way. And a lot of teens don't even realize — with blunts, which use both nicotine and cannabis together — that they're actually becoming addicted to both products simultaneously.

**Andrew Huberman:** I find great optimism in the message. It's cloudy out there for our youth, but there's also great hope in everything you're conveying. Why would you be trying so hard to fix these problems if you didn't believe they could be fixed? What's clear is it's going to be a community effort. Kids know what's happening — we have to have these conversations. They're hard and uncomfortable for adults to have, but until we normalize the conversation, it's unlikely we're going to solve these problems.

**Bonnie Halpern-Felsher:** Thank you so much. That is exactly the right message. I'm incredibly optimistic about the human capital and the social capital. Meet youth where they're at. Use their strengths. And normalize these conversations — not normalize the use, but normalize the conversation.
