## [00:02:11] Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

**Andrew Huberman:** Dr. John Kruse, welcome. Let's talk about ADHD. Probably best if we start off by laying out what it is — is the hyperactivity component always in there? Childhood ADHD, adult ADHD — maybe give us the top contour of this and then we can get into ways to combat ADHD depending on different circumstances and different needs.

**Dr. John Kruse:** I'll just start out by saying that, like most things in neuroscience and psychiatry, we have some definitions and we have lots of different thoughts and frameworks to approach things. So I'll start with our diagnostic category — how we diagnose ADHD. There are 18 different symptoms: nine of them are hyperactive-impulsive and nine of them are inattentive. The inattentive ones are things like forgetting to follow through on things, losing items, being easily distracted. The impulsive and hyperactive ones are cutting people off in conversations, blurting things out, running around, fidgeting.

The definitions themselves were designed with a child population in mind because until roughly the mid-1990s it was dogma that this was a neurodevelopmental disease of childhood and that every child who had it outgrew it. That is dramatically wrong. Some kids do, but most kids don't. The latest work suggests that most adults fluctuate in time with the severity of their ADHD symptoms.

As an adult, you need to have at least five of the 18 symptoms. And when we say "have them" — all of these are things that normal people can display at any time. Any of us might interrupt someone, might have trouble sitting, might have trouble attending to a task. But to meet the criteria, these have to be displayed to an excessive degree, to the extent that they're causing some dysfunction or distress, and they have to be displayed in multiple realms of life. If it's only at work that you have trouble completing a task, that might have something to do with your boss or an uncomfortable chair. These have to be traits that are pervasive.

## [00:05:37] Genetics & Environment; COVID Pandemic & ADHD Diagnoses

**Andrew Huberman:** One thing I and a lot of people wonder about — is it the case that when somebody with ADHD is in their home environment, where typically there are more options of things to do, that the symptoms get worse? As opposed to when they go to a restaurant or school or work, where the environment is more constrained?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Like our other mental health or psychiatric conditions, there's clearly both a biological and social-environmental component. The nature-and-nurture question isn't "which is it?" — it's always both. We know there's a very strong genetic component to ADHD. The heritability factor is around 0.8, which is about as strong as the heritability for height or for schizophrenia. This tends to run in families, but again, it has a social dimension — you're not just a brain in the world, you're a brain interacting with the world.

With ADHD, we like to frame it as: both structure is important and demands are important. One of the aspects of ADHD we most often understand today is as a problem with executive functions of the brain — how the brain's working memory works, how selective attention works or doesn't work, how emotional regulation works. And one of the things we know most clearly is that ADHD is structure-dependent. Give an ADHD brain a high-structure environment where there are exactly the right number of demands, interesting and challenging but not overwhelming, and they can perform extraordinarily. Give them insufficient structure or overwhelmingly too much and they perform much worse.

The COVID pandemic provides a remarkable natural experiment. We had many adults, many of whom already had ADHD and many who are borderline or predisposed, being put into environments where there was essentially zero structure. No commute enforcing a wake-up time, no boss walking around the office, no peers to watch. You decide what you're doing and when, for how long and to what extent — and that can be a train wreck for someone with an ADHD brain.

There was a huge increase in ADHD diagnoses during COVID because suddenly people had to manage with zero external structure. Some of that was unmasking of pre-existing ADHD, some of it was the environment exacerbating people who are genetically closer to the boundary. With the COVID pandemic there was a huge increase in ADHD diagnoses, and in many places prescriptions for stimulants essentially doubled in the year or two after COVID hit.

**Andrew Huberman:** It makes sense given the lockdowns. It's as if the structure that was externally imposed was suddenly stripped away, and it revealed what the brain's architecture actually is when left to its own devices.

**Dr. John Kruse:** Exactly. And what's interesting is that some people with ADHD actually thrived. The ones who thrived tended to be the ones who had already figured out what their best environment is — they had organized their lives around that. But for the vast majority, losing external structure was devastating.

The other big COVID factor that I saw clinically was that we know people with ADHD often have comorbid anxiety disorders — maybe 50% or more. The sustained stress and uncertainty of COVID escalated anxiety enormously in those people, and anxiety itself makes attention and concentration much worse. So you had the double whammy of less structure and more anxiety hitting this population particularly hard.

## [00:14:26] ADHD, Interest & Careers

**Andrew Huberman:** In your clinical observation, can you point us to clusters of professions where people with ADHD tend to gravitate toward — because they have sufficient or even hyper proficiency there?

**Dr. John Kruse:** I'll frame this in two different ways. First: one of the most insightful observations about ADHD is that non-ADHD brains are importance-driven. If you know you have to move your car because you'll get a parking ticket, you go out and move it. If you know taxes need to be paid in April, you take care of it. The ADHD brain, in contrast, is an interest-driven brain. They know they should be doing their taxes, but — look at how the Warriors are doing in the basketball playoff, or look at this cool cat video. So regarding career and work, the most important thing is that the work is interesting to you.

ADHD is almost invariably a relative disability — how it impacts someone depends enormously on what they do and the environment they do it in. In what way is it a disability? In the sense that you have less choice over when, where, and how long your attention goes to any given thing. If you've built your life to create an environment where what you're forced to attend to is what captivates you, you have far less disability.

Some of the best matches, at least in my clinical experience, are people who gravitate toward work that is fast-paced, crisis-based, or has high novelty — emergency medicine is a great example. These aren't necessarily people who like crises, but they perform at their best in them. Entrepreneurial work can be great because the early stages of building something are inherently engaging and exciting. Highly creative work — theater, improvisational performance — where being in the moment is actually an asset.

The environments that tend to work less well are long-horizon, repetitive, detail-oriented tasks with no inherent interest — accounting, certain legal work, data entry. The classic 9-to-5 structured office job can be challenging not because the hours are bad but because the work itself may not generate the internal interest required to focus.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about people who have ADHD but end up in those more regimented professions?

**Dr. John Kruse:** They often find compensatory mechanisms — usually a very specific niche within that profession that happens to be interesting to them. Or they thrive in deadline-driven aspects of the work. Deadlines are essentially artificial crises, and ADHD brains often mobilize very effectively around genuine urgency. The problem is when there's no urgency, no deadline, and the work is not intrinsically compelling. That's where they struggle most.

## [00:20:40] Social Media & Distractibility; ADHD & Lifespan Effect

**Andrew Huberman:** We hear quite often that social media and scrolling — Twitter, Instagram, TikTok — is quote-unquote giving people ADHD. Are there data suggesting that the practice of looking at thousands of different contexts — 15 videos in a minute — is creating more distractibility in other domains of life?

**Dr. John Kruse:** There's a lot of good neuropsychological data that the more time you spend immersed in social media — it's the constant barrage of information, and not just the volume but the fact that you are constantly being interrupted, and most of these interruptions are intentionally designed to attract your attention — yes, the more people practice thinking that way or being in the world that way, it's harder to sustain attention for long periods. You train yourself to overreact to any new distraction. The core elements of some of the executive functions that are impaired in ADHD — we are all becoming more ADHD-like.

That's actually the thesis of a book I've been working on, which I call "Attention Deficit World." One of the things that's been frustrating is there's been a huge disconnect. There are people writing about the neuroscience — are our brains getting more distracted? And it's not just that concentration is worse. Immersion in social media and the cell-phone world — it's not all bad. People do multitask more quickly and switch in and out of tasks better than people who don't immerse themselves a lot in the internet. They're still not doing as well as if they had no distractions and just focused solely, but they're multitasking better.

What's important is that the more the world is pulling at our attention in all these different ways, the more ADHD is not a discrete condition but is on a spectrum. And those of us who don't have a formal diagnosis are being pushed further in that direction by the environment. The issue is a widening of the distribution — more people are being pushed to have functionally significant symptoms even without the underlying biology.

**Andrew Huberman:** And what about the lifespan trajectory of ADHD? We mentioned that the old dogma was that children outgrow it.

**Dr. John Kruse:** What we see now is that it is not outgrown in a binary sense. Many adults have more capacity to manage it — partly because they have more life experience, partly because they've often figured out what works for them. But the symptoms don't disappear; they change in presentation. The gross hyperactivity of a seven-year-old bouncing off the walls becomes an adult who can't sit through a long meeting, who feels internally restless, who takes up multiple hobbies simultaneously. The core biology remains but the expression adapts over time. And the latest longitudinal work suggests that the symptoms actually fluctuate considerably — there can be periods of life where it's well-managed and others where it flares again, particularly during life transitions that disrupt structure.

## [00:27:39] Hyperfocus, Flow States

**Andrew Huberman:** My understanding is that people with ADHD have the ability to focus quite intensely on things that really capture their interest. Is it true that people with ADHD can in fact focus — but that the neural or neurochemical thresholds needed to drop into focus are just much higher? It takes more fear of a deadline, or more excitement about an activity?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Many people think ADHD is a horrible title because it focuses on attention deficit and hyperactivity disorder. It's very clear that it's not a deficit of attention — if it's a deficit at all, it's a deficit of control over attention. And with attention there are at least three different realms where we're controlling it: one, we direct attention — if something important is going on we have to be able to shift to it; two, we have to be able to sustain it; and three, we need to shift out of it if it's inappropriate to stay in it. In all three of those realms, people with ADHD have less volitional control over their attention.

So many people with ADHD describe hyperfocus as one of their superpowers — where they're so absorbed in their work that they are losing track of time, losing track of hunger, losing track of needing to go to the bathroom, losing track of the appointment they had an hour ago. They're just locked in. And it can be extraordinarily productive. I've had patients who've written their best work in those episodes. The downside is they can't turn it on at will — it happens to them. And they often can't turn it off at will either, which creates its own problems.

**Andrew Huberman:** The flow state literature suggests that the conditions for flow are very specific — challenge must be well-matched to skill, the activity must be intrinsically motivating, distractions must be eliminated. Is ADHD hyperfocus essentially the same thing as flow, or is it distinct?

**Dr. John Kruse:** There are clearly overlaps. Flow as described by Csikszentmihalyi has that element of being so absorbed that time distorts, that effort feels effortless. Hyperfocus in ADHD shares that phenomenology. The difference may be that in classical flow there's a sense of the challenge being well-calibrated — not too easy, not overwhelming. In ADHD hyperfocus, the trigger seems to be primarily interest and novelty, which can occur at any difficulty level. Someone with ADHD can hyperfocus on a video game that is trivially easy simply because it's engaging their interest-driven system. So hyperfocus is a broader, less calibrated version of flow — driven more by raw interest than by optimal challenge.

## [00:33:45] Tools: 4 Essential Behaviors for ADHD; Regular Meal Schedule

**Andrew Huberman:** What are your thoughts on people — with ADHD or not — trying to identify sort of best conditions for them? How important is circadian time here?

**Dr. John Kruse:** So many people come to me as a psychiatrist for ADHD primarily focused on medications. And we still know that the stimulants are the single most powerful tool — in terms of extent of symptom reduction overall and in terms of the percentage of people they help. But the medicine isn't going to change everything. You need to be focusing on your life as well. I always start with scheduling.

Many people with ADHD find scheduling anathema — like the slaves on the galley ship being told you have to row, stroke, stroke, stroke. What I tell them is that the part of you that's going to help make the schedule that works for you isn't some evil taskmaster trying to make you do what you don't want to do. It's actually the wisest, smartest, nicest, kindest part of you that's identified your bigger goals and is trying to match what you're doing minute-to-minute to line up with those bigger goals.

Before you slot in your work or school or externally-derived tasks, I tell people you need to have the four basics in place. Sleep is by far the biggest basic — particularly critical for ADHD, and there are particular reasons why it's a particular challenge for people with ADHD. The four essentials I tell people are: sleep, eating, exercise or some amount of movement, and a miscellaneous category of me-time, relaxation, or meditation. All of those need to be in place before you start scheduling work tasks.

About the eating component: I had two different real-life diagnostic tests in my practice. My office was at the end of a steep driveway. The only people who ever showed up at the front house door rather than the correct office entrance were people with ADHD. Not everyone with ADHD got it wrong, but never did someone coming in for OCD or depression or PTSD show up at the wrong door — even though I gave the same instructions to everyone. The other real-life diagnostic test: if someone said "it was 4:00 yesterday and I just realized I hadn't eaten all day" — that's a strong signal. It's not that everyone with ADHD does that, but either not getting the right interoceptive cues from your body or not paying attention to them is something measurably seen in ADHD.

So having a regular meal schedule is important. People with ADHD often start a meal, take a few bites, go back to work, and the meal never really ends — it just fragments into the rest of the day. Having defined mealtimes and eating them through to completion is part of the structural scaffold.

## [00:42:21] Tool: Regular Sleep Timing; Stimulants & Sleep

**Andrew Huberman:** Before we start talking about medications and other factors that modulate ADHD, maybe we could step through each of the four essentials and you could share some of your favorite tools. Let's start with sleep.

**Dr. John Kruse:** Regular to-bed and wake-up times. Matthew Walker in his book on sleep really emphasizes that quality sleep isn't just about eight hours — it's not just duration. The timing of your sleep is every bit as important as the duration. If you're used to sleeping midnight to 8 a.m. and you stay up until 4 a.m. on a weekend and sleep until noon — you may still get those eight hours but they're not restorative to the same extent as if you'd slept at your regular time. My PhD research was on circadian rhythms and we had reams of data 30-plus years ago that the timing of sleep was as important as duration. And yet every public service announcement just says "get your eight hours." Why are we leaving out this other piece we've known for decades?

People with ADHD have a strong propensity to being night owls — a different chronotype where they may be more effective or functional later in the day. There's a strong genetic component to this, with genetic markers affecting sleep timing that are over-represented in the ADHD community. Some of it is a genuine push toward evening chronotype; some of it is the nature of ADHD — if you procrastinate, you're going to push things off till the end of the day; fewer distractions at night when everyone else is asleep.

Getting regular sleep doesn't mean it has to be what I'd call "normal." If it works for you — if you can build your career and social life around sleeping from 2 a.m. to 10 a.m. every day — go for it, as long as you can be consistent with it.

One thing paradoxically for many people is that actually being on stimulant medications can help with getting regular sleep. Stimulants do have insomnia as a side effect for some people, but more people with ADHD find that stimulants help — either because the drug is wearing off at the end of the day and there's a crash in alertness that makes them more tired, or because they're expending more productive energy during the day and are more tired at the end, or because stimulants help synchronize circadian clocks by creating a consistent, alerting start early in the day.

**Andrew Huberman:** On the days when I take a small amount of Wellbutrin, I notice a significant improvement in my sleep and an increase in rapid eye movement sleep. I reverse-engineered the major effect as being norepinephrine-driven, and when I do something else that raises epinephrine — a cold plunge first thing in the morning for one to three minutes — I also see an increase in REM sleep quality later that night. Does that logically hold for you?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Both of those have some science background. What's hard to sort out is why it's working. One: potential placebo effect. Two — and maybe even more important than placebo — is that the days you're deciding to do this, there's something different about those days to begin with, because you're not doing it every day.

## [00:48:06] Insomnia; Tools: Bedtime Structure, Exercise, Phones, Breathing

**Dr. John Kruse:** Maybe the biggest finding in insomnia sleep medicine in the last 20 years is that almost everyone who has a problem with insomnia doesn't actually have a problem with sleep. What I mean is: the sleep system is intact. It's there, it's waiting to arrive and put you to sleep each night. The problem — and this is from the sleep researchers — with at least 90% of people who have insomnia problems, is a failure of the daytime arousal system to shut off properly. Normally we have two mutually inhibitory systems: a wakefulness-arousal system and a sleep-sedation system. When one turns on, the other turns off. With most insomnia, it isn't that sleep is weak or insufficient — it's sitting there, waiting. It just can't land on the landing pad because you're too aroused.

So the non-medication approaches to helping with sleep are not about forcing sleep — that doesn't work very well. It's about getting rid of arousal, dampening arousal. For people with ADHD: first, decide on a reasonable bedtime and think about it ahead of time. Second, eliminate stimulation. Exercise — I'm a marathon runner — exercising too late in the evening can elevate body temperature and disrupt falling asleep. Emotional, intellectual, and cognitive arousal too — and the biggest single tool in modern life is: do not have your phone in your bedroom. Studies have shown that even if you're not checking it, if it's there, you're thinking about it. Just having it out of sight is better than having it visible and turned off.

Using a partner or family members to help reinforce bedtime structure can also be helpful — if both people are on the same page and it's done supportively rather than as a nag.

One of my favorite tools for falling asleep is cyclic sighing. We know cyclic sighing engages the parasympathetic nervous system. One of the things that happens normally in the transition to sleep each night is going from primarily sympathetic tone to primarily parasympathetic tone — so anything that puts you there already makes it easier. In my own experience, not only does cyclic sighing help me fall asleep better, it actually helps me stay asleep throughout the night better.

## [00:52:30] Nighttime Waking Up; Cyclic Sighing

**Andrew Huberman:** Many people including myself have very little trouble falling asleep but then consistently wake up around 3 a.m. The idea that our bladders get smaller as we age and that's why people wake up — that's complete nonsense, right?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Some people it may be a prostate issue, but that clearly isn't accounting for half the population. I think it's much more the neurologic integration of our bladder — all our nerves start functioning not quite as well, and they're getting the signal that they really need to urinate right now when clearly most of those people don't. They could wait. But the signal is arriving and it's believable.

**Andrew Huberman:** How much cyclic sighing are you doing before sleep and how long before sleep?

**Dr. John Kruse:** When I read your paper with Spiegel and others, I said I can do five minutes a day of cyclic sighing. I wound up just consistently doing it — partly for general health, partly because I have slightly elevated blood pressure. I do about five minutes, and I count by reps — about 20 or 25 reps. If you lose track, just go back to the lowest number, because everything we're trying to do is decrease arousal. If you have a timer and you check it, you're reversing some of the benefit. My recommendation is do it for five minutes, but do it by counting reps. Much more than five minutes — I tell people there's not much additional benefit beyond that.

The effect was clear for me. Before trying cyclic sighing at nighttime, I was waking up virtually every single night to urinate. In the 18 to 20 months since I've been doing it, I think it's been a total of four times that I've woken up during the night.

**Andrew Huberman:** Remarkable. Prior to trying cyclic sighing I was waking up virtually every single night — and I've found the same dramatic reduction.

## [00:56:35] Exercise; Addiction, Risk, Kids & Stimulants; Catecholamines & Focus

**Andrew Huberman:** Are there any data about specific types of exercise being better for ADHD, independent of effects on sleep?

**Dr. John Kruse:** There do seem to be some acute effects of measurably improving some of the executive functions associated with better attention from acute aerobic exercise, and some more general longer-term benefits from consistently exercising — being able to concentrate longer, being able to switch attention more appropriately. There's a huge body of clinical literature of patients reporting they feel much more alert on days they get their workout in. But in terms of identifying what's the most valuable type, what's the best duration — I would say we can't make any solid conclusions. Try it and see what works for you.

**Andrew Huberman:** Is there a relationship between ADHD and addiction because of the impulsivity component?

**Dr. John Kruse:** The ballpark figure is that about 20% of Americans run into some substance addiction problem in their lifetimes. For people with ADHD, that's roughly doubled — close to 40%, maybe more depending on the population. The impulsivity connection is real: impulsivity is a core feature of ADHD and is also one of the features that most strongly predicts addiction. There's also a self-medication hypothesis: some people with undiagnosed ADHD are drawn to substances that make them feel more normal — stimulants, nicotine, even cannabis in some cases.

There's a counterintuitive finding: appropriately treating children with stimulants seems to reduce, not increase, the likelihood of later addiction. The old fear that putting kids on stimulants was creating future addicts has been significantly undermined by longitudinal data showing the opposite — that untreated ADHD is a much bigger risk factor for addiction than treated ADHD.

**Andrew Huberman:** What are catecholamines and how do they relate to focus?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Catecholamines are a family of neurotransmitters and hormones: dopamine, norepinephrine, and epinephrine. They're all structurally related, derived from the amino acid tyrosine. In the brain, the relevant ones for attention and focus are primarily dopamine and norepinephrine. Dopamine is critical for motivation, reward, and the drive to engage with tasks. Norepinephrine drives arousal, alertness, and the ability to filter signals from noise — it's the signal-to-noise ratio amplifier in the prefrontal cortex. Both are depleted or dysregulated in ADHD, and virtually all effective ADHD medications work by increasing one or both of these neurotransmitters in relevant brain circuits.

## [00:1:04:32] Ritalin, Stimulants, Amphetamines; Amphetamine-Induced Psychosis & Risks

**Andrew Huberman:** Let's talk about medications. Let's start with Ritalin. How often is Ritalin used nowadays and what is it doing neurochemically?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Ritalin — generic methylphenidate — has dozens of slow-release versions now and even a skin patch. Our definition of what a stimulant is is really vague in its broadest sense — any drug that has an effect in the body like the sympathetic nervous system. By the loosest criteria, caffeine is a stimulant. Wellbutrin is a stimulant even though we classify it as an antidepressant. But when we're talking ADHD medicines, stimulant more specifically refers to amphetamine-based products like Adderall and Vyvanse.

Amphetamine is a strong dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake blocker — it prevents what's already been released from being taken back up, so more is available for longer. But in addition to that, amphetamine is a potent vesicle manipulator — it's actually forcing a bigger release from the vesicles when they're synaptically released. So it's not just that the signal lasts longer — it's a bigger signal to begin with.

Methylphenidate, on the other hand, is actually a pretty weak vesicular manipulator, and some studies don't find any impact there at all. That means methylphenidate is basically a norepinephrine and dopamine reuptake inhibitor — which is what Wellbutrin is. Most ADHD experts lump amphetamine and methylphenidate together, but if you look at the efficacy data closely, separating them out, methylphenidate is actually closer to the pack of non-stimulant options. Amphetamine products are head and shoulders above everything else. There's a clear decision point in the data.

In terms of some of the side effects, the one I worry most about — not common at all but horrible when it happens — is amphetamine-induced psychosis. With amphetamine-Adderall products, it's probably dose-dependent, but it occurs in close to one out of 500 people. That's uncommon, but it's a really bad condition. Amphetamine-induced psychosis is a schizophrenic-like picture — usually the person is really paranoid, really worried that friends are manipulating them or police are spying on them. With alcohol intoxication, you can be out of touch with reality, but you fall asleep at the end of the night and wake up the next morning no longer psychotic. With amphetamine-induced psychosis, on the other hand, it can continue for days, weeks, or months after stopping the medication. About 20% of those people, if you look at 20 years out, are in a permanent psychotic state.

In my population in San Francisco I actually had a rate higher than one in 100. Part of that was working with a lot of HIV-positive men, as HIV can affect the brain and create vulnerability. Part of it was a high incidence of methamphetamine use — methamphetamine is chemically different from amphetamine, with an extra methyl group, and it has higher rates of psychosis. And part of it was that I was known in the city as someone comfortable with a broader range of stimulant dosages, so I had people who had previously had psychotic episodes — sometimes hidden from me — restart and return to the hospital.

The key take-home: if you have a family history of schizophrenia or psychosis, or you've had any experience of it yourself — including any psychosis from marijuana — I would absolutely not prescribe an amphetamine-based stimulant.

## [00:01:18:03] Adult ADHD & Medications; Stimulants & Cardiovascular Risk?

**Andrew Huberman:** Someone comes in as an adult, they meet the criteria for ADHD — do you go to amphetamines first? And what about cardiovascular risk?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Some of this is individual clinical style. My style is to listen as closely as I can to what the patient wants — explain in as much detail as I can the risks and the likelihood of those, and what I perceive the benefits to be. For years, statistically, I had many more people on non-stimulants than stimulants compared to the general ADHD population.

With the stimulants, amphetamine-based products really are more powerful. And unlike many other conditions, with stimulants there's more of a linear relationship: if a little bit of Wellbutrin helps, a lot is likely to help more. With amphetamines, you might start getting more side effect issues at higher doses, but there's a meaningful dose-response curve.

The cardiovascular risk question — for years there was concern that these are sympathomimetic drugs, they raise heart rate and blood pressure, so they must be bad for the heart. The data on that actually looks reassuring. There have been a number of large epidemiological studies and the signal that emerges consistently is that properly prescribed stimulants do not significantly increase cardiovascular risk in the vast majority of adults, including adults with known hypertension. There are people in whom it would be contraindicated — severe arrhythmias, very labile hypertension — but for the general population of adults with ADHD, the cardiovascular risk is less than feared.

## [00:01:26:06] Adult ADHD Medication Choices, Psychosis, Cannabis

**Andrew Huberman:** For an adult who has never touched stimulants — would you start them on Ritalin, Wellbutrin, or something in the Adderall/Vyvanse cluster?

**Dr. John Kruse:** My presentation is usually: Adderall is likely to be the most strongly effective — and I more often use Vyvanse now. These are the other options, but Adderall also has a greater — rare but real — risk for these bad problems. I try to find out if they've tried these things before, what dose it was, what it did for them. And I try to find out what scares them. Some people are petrified and won't go anywhere near amphetamines; some say they're not that concerned about the rare risks.

On cannabis and psychosis: I took heat for stressing this on the podcast and social media, and then the traditional press started putting out information that says essentially the same thing. There's clearly some risk from cannabis, even at low THC. It's much more potent than it was 70 years ago. CBD may actually have some antipsychotic effect, so strains that were more balanced may have had less of a risk. But now that you can get pure high-THC products — and even in states with regulation, a majority of labels don't correlate with what you're actually getting — this is a real concern.

If someone has had a psychotic episode on marijuana and also has ADHD — as a friend recently referred to me — I would absolutely not prescribe an amphetamine-based stimulant. The likelihood of recurrence is too high. My clinical approach would be first to explore what the marijuana is doing for them and whether clearing it out of the picture might itself be the most helpful step before adding anything new.

## [00:01:33:49] ADHD Symptoms, Nicotine; Caffeine, Energy Drinks, L-Theanine

**Andrew Huberman:** What happens when somebody with ADHD starts dabbling in nicotine use — pouches, gums — in ways that don't cause cancer?

**Dr. John Kruse:** There's some well-done research showing nicotine is helpful for improving some executive functions — sustained attention particularly. A major pharmaceutical company was actually developing a nicotine receptor product specifically for ADHD and abandoned it several years ago, for reasons I haven't been able to determine.

What's worth throwing out is that nicotine, although it acts like a stimulant in many ways, is unusual in that it both arouses people and reduces anxiety simultaneously. Most of our stimulants bang away at the sympathetic nervous system — good arousal and bad arousal together. Nicotine seems to be both calming and helping alert people. So for people who feel more anxious on traditional stimulants, nicotine may be of interest, as long as they're taking it in a way that's not detrimental to health — meaning not smoking, vaping, or dipping, which clearly are harmful. I have some people who prefer it to any other medication.

**Andrew Huberman:** What about caffeine and energy drinks?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Caffeine is the most widely used psychoactive substance on the planet. If you used it at an equivalent dose to our stimulants, it's actually a pretty lousy stimulant. It's working primarily on adenosine and only indirectly on dopamine, but it's associated with higher levels of anxiety and cardiac effects at high doses. Most people are using it at a substantially lower equivalent dose.

The ADHD experts historically have fallen into two camps: some say it's going to interact with stimulant medications, stay off it; the other half say as long as you know it's part of the picture and you try to be consistent with your dosage, it's fine — and maybe it helps you get away with a lower prescription dose.

One issue: people think they know their caffeine dose, but they often don't. A University of Florida study went into a Florida Starbucks and bought the same drink every day for three weeks — the highest caffeine day compared to the lowest was a threefold difference. So you may think you know what you're getting, but the variation is enormous.

There's also caffeine plus L-theanine. There's some research — mostly in kids — showing measurable beneficial effects. L-theanine seems to take the jitteriness off caffeine, and may have some direct beneficial cognitive effects of its own. Adding L-theanine to caffeine-containing drinks because it seems to dampen anxiety while potentially adding direct cognitive benefits — the evidence is small but points in a positive direction.

## [00:01:43:28] Fish Oil, Cardiac Effects & ADHD, Tool: Fish Oil Dose, EPA vs DHA

**Andrew Huberman:** What about fish oil for ADHD in particular, and what threshold dosages are relevant?

**Dr. John Kruse:** The ADHD fish oil story is actually the opposite of the cardiac story. For cardiac benefits, the first large studies showed benefits, more recent ones haven't — and the cardiology community looks at the more recent ones and says that's where the story ends. With ADHD and fish oil, the first few studies in kids didn't show any benefits at all, and then subsequently several studies did show benefits. The field has moved toward a more positive view of fish oil for ADHD — though there are methodological differences that haven't been fully reconciled.

Unless you're taking such a massive dose that you risk heavy metal poisoning — which is a possible issue with extremely high doses — most recommendations are in the range that seems safe and possibly beneficial. Depression has been the most consistent field for a mental health benefit from fish oil. There the recommendation is usually to target about 1,000 milligrams of EPA — eicosapentaenoic acid — per day. If you're seeing some benefit but it feels like there's room for improvement, you could probably reasonably double that. Above 2,000-3,000 milligrams per day you'd want to be careful, but below that the safety profile is good.

The EPA versus DHA distinction matters. EPA is the one that shows benefits for mood and attention; DHA is critical for brain structure and development. Most fish oil supplements have both, but for mood and attention applications you want to look at the EPA content specifically.

## [00:01:52:56] ADHD & Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Tool: Task List System

**Andrew Huberman:** Are any of these brain-training games to get people better at focusing known to be worthwhile according to real data or clinical observation?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Let me start by broadening it a bit. One of the effective approaches that helps with symptomatic reduction in ADHD is cognitive behavioral therapy — CBT. My quick overview: it focuses on actions, thoughts, and feelings. Humans can have direct control of their actions and thoughts, not too much over their feelings — but all three affect each other. CBT was developed by Aaron Beck to treat depression, probably in the late 1960s.

On the surface CBT is a horrible match for ADHD because it requires lots of repetitive, boring homework — doing the same thing over and over. But there have been at least two groups — Mary Solanto in New York and a Harvard/Pennsylvania group involving Ramsay and Safren — who developed approaches using CBT techniques specifically designed for people with ADHD to help overcome some of those hurdles. Both approaches encourage the use of medications in combination because many people with ADHD are too unfocused to sit down and do the CBT work otherwise.

Both approaches, devised completely independently, are amazingly similar. At the core of both is having a system of scheduling each day — not micromanaging each minute, but having the essentials in place, having blocks of time where you know what you're going to do, and having a task list in combination with that.

With ADHD being interest-driven rather than importance-driven, you may have a task list but you choose the easy interesting item — buying shoelaces — rather than the important urgent item — moving your car or doing taxes. The task list system uses simple triaging: things that are both urgent and important go in the A category and get done first. Things that are important but not as urgent go in B. Everything else is C. One consolidated list — not 17 scattered ones, because if it's everywhere, it's nowhere.

That CBT approach can also work on decreasing procrastination, structuring your workspace, eliminating distractions, and extending concentration time through structured modules.

## [00:01:57:52] Video Games, Neurofeedback, ADHD Benefit?, Tool: Technology Restriction

**Dr. John Kruse:** On video games — there is one product that's been FDA-approved for use in ADHD. The important thing to remember is that the FDA's system for medications requires you to demonstrate it really works. For devices, basically they're saying it's not going to kill anyone. Having FDA approval for a device doesn't guarantee strong efficacy.

The product — from researchers including Adam Gazzaley's group at UCSF — can clearly show you get better at their product and at tests that look exactly like their product. But in terms of real-world impact on ADHD symptoms day-to-day, there's not a lot of data. That doesn't mean it doesn't work, but the evidence is limited.

Similarly with neurofeedback: there are lots of companies making lots of money, and a recent article in the American Journal of Psychiatry reported another failure to find a significant impact from neurofeedback. I'm not saying it has no effect, but I've had lots of people ask whether they should keep spending hundreds of dollars each week for more sessions.

**Andrew Huberman:** What behavioral intervention has worked the most consistently?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Restricting and managing technology. I've made it a point to put social media on an old phone — those apps are only on that phone, which I don't even know the number to. If I need to post something I have to go get it, and the friction of that alone substantially reduces impulsive social media checking. That deliberate friction design — making the technology you want to use less requires some effort — is one of the most reliably effective behavioral interventions I've seen.

## [00:02:02:26] Guanfacine, Clonidine, Hypertension, Effects & Timeframe

**Andrew Huberman:** What is guanfacine and why is it sometimes used for ADHD?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Guanfacine and a related drug called clonidine are both alpha-2 agonists — they work on a subset of the norepinephrine system. They're both originally antihypertensives for lowering blood pressure. It was actually in clonidine studies first that it was discovered these could be helpful for people with ADHD — I think it was serendipitous.

One key difference between the two: clonidine leaves the norepinephrine alpha-2 receptor really quickly, and for people who skip a dose or stop abruptly, it's not uncommon to have rebound hypertension — not just mild, but dangerously high. Guanfacine leaves the receptors more slowly, and studies have shown the rebound hypertension is far less common. Given that people with ADHD often forget their medication, this is part of why research has moved more toward guanfacine.

There's extensive work by Amy Arnsten at Yale showing that guanfacine's effects seem to deal with strengthening synaptic connections in prefrontal circuitry — unlike most drugs that are just boosting dopamine or norepinephrine acutely. This may be why guanfacine has a slower onset — weeks rather than days. That's actually a sign it's doing something different, building up prefrontal connectivity rather than simply boosting neurotransmitter availability.

One important implication: almost everyone says stimulants work quickly and drugs like Wellbutrin and Strattera work slowly because they're antidepressants. But the neuroscience of this is wrong from first principles. How quickly is dopamine reuptake blocked by Wellbutrin? It's within an hour of taking it. The reason people respond more slowly isn't because the neurochemical effect is slow — it's because what's being fixed is a different process. Guanfacine is an excellent example of this: it works by slowly building up prefrontal synaptic strength, which takes weeks but can produce durable changes that outlast the medication itself.

## [00:02:10:13] Modafinil, History & Forms, Dependence

**Andrew Huberman:** What is modafinil, what does it do, and how might it be useful for ADHD?

**Dr. John Kruse:** Modafinil was developed by a French company and approved there for maybe a decade before it came to the US roughly 35 years ago. At the time all the research showed it was an orexin receptor agonist — working on the hypocretin/orexin system. Now most of the pharmacology literature refers to it as a dopamine-acting drug. There's ongoing debate about whether it's primarily orexin or primarily dopamine that it works via. I stick with orexin because that's where I was taught and I haven't seen a clear consensus otherwise.

The orexin system is the more natural normal waking system — as opposed to the sympathetic-nervous-system-driven alarm response. It's waking you up but not agitating you. The original claim was that modafinil was waking you up without overstimulating you. That's why it was called the "non-stimulant stimulant." The entity that spent the most research money on what this does or doesn't do was the US military, because they have a large interest in wanting people to be alert and ready but not hyperactive or trigger-happy — the way amphetamines can make people.

Modafinil has been shown to produce genuine cognitive effects — improved attention, reduced sleepiness — but tends to be less effective for classic ADHD symptoms than amphetamine. It's probably more useful for the inattentive, "brain fog," can't-get-started-in-the-morning presentation than for the high-energy-but-can't-focus presentation.

As for dependence: the withdrawal from modafinil is subtle compared to amphetamines — the drug doesn't produce the same level of dopamine surge and crash. But regular users who try stopping notice they feel foggy and sluggish. It's a softer physical dependence than traditional stimulants, but the psychological dependence — "I need this to be my sharp self" — can be just as real.

## [00:02:19:02] Drug Holidays; Short- vs Long-Acting Drugs, Addiction, Vyvanse

**Andrew Huberman:** What are the general trends about preference for long-acting versus shorter-acting drugs? And what about drug holidays — taking weekends off?

**Dr. John Kruse:** For decades, particularly with kids, the dogma has been that taking breaks from stimulants is a good idea because it will decrease the likelihood of developing addictions and decrease tolerance. One of the known side effects of stimulants for kids is growth suppression — height winds up being about 2 cm less, consistently found in kids on stimulants throughout their growth years. Taking long breaks — like summers — seems to overcome that decrement. But as for lower rates of addiction or tolerance from short breaks? I've looked and there's nothing that clearly shows that clinically.

For short-acting versus long-acting: the advantages of immediate-release drugs are that they work quickly, you can feel them going in, you know when they're on and when they're off. If you forget your medicine in the morning but have a presentation at 3 p.m., you could take it at 2 and still sleep that night. The flexibility is useful. The big downside is that it tends to go off quickly, and most people experience some withdrawal as it wears off — energy crash, mood crash, being tearful or irritable.

Extended-release versions go in more gradually and go out much more gradually. The one I like most for a long-acting amphetamine product is Vyvanse. Vyvanse was designed specifically to be unattractive to drug abusers — it chemically links a dexamphetamine molecule to lysine, one of the amino acids. If you snort or inject it, you have an inactive drug. Your red blood cells need a specific enzyme to cleave the lysine and release active dexamphetamine. That's the slow-release mechanism: how quickly your red blood cells can do it. They have limited capacity, which is why above a certain dose, adding more tends to extend the duration rather than increase the peak.

The result is one of the most consistently even delivery systems. People who switch from Adderall to Vyvanse almost invariably say it's smoother — not as jarred, not as revved up, without the same crash at the end of the day.

## [00:02:28:56] Time Perception, ADHD, Circadian Rhythm Disruption, Phototherapy

**Andrew Huberman:** People with ADHD don't seem to track time the way non-ADHD people do. They know the difference between "now" and "not now" but they're not tracking time itself. What do we know about time perception in ADHD?

**Dr. John Kruse:** There are two different angles. The one easier to objectively measure is putting people in a lab with a time perception test. People with ADHD are consistently inconsistent — they're not perpetually underestimating or overestimating, but they are estimating incorrectly much more often than people without ADHD. So there's something at a basic time-processing level that's absent. There's also the real-world aspect of not paying attention to cues — not noticing other people who left the room, being distracted — which compounds the situation.

I often ask — even though it's not one of the 18 diagnostic symptoms — are you chronically late? I had a patient who was receiving threatening notices from her boss because she'd come in two hours late one day. In a subsequent session I asked: when is the expectation you arrive? Nine o'clock. When do you usually show up? Oh, usually 9:15 or 9:20. In her mind, that wasn't late.

As for whether stimulants measurably improve time perception in the laboratory sense — I should know that but I don't have it at the top of my mind.

The more global question: there's a Danish research group that believes ADHD is primarily a circadian rhythm disruption — that that's the central neurologic issue. There's interesting work on bright light therapy. I did work in the 1980s on bright light therapy for winter depression, which has an effect as strong as medication. There are one or two studies done on individuals with ADHD without any seasonal depression or any depression at all — just those same bright lights shown to them early in the morning — and measurably improved a broad range of ADHD symptoms. The claim was that it was helping re-synchronize internal rhythms that are out of sync in ADHD.

Even though we have a master clock in the suprachiasmatic nucleus, we also have clocks throughout the body — and they need to talk to and interact with each other to be synchronized. For many people with ADHD, they may not be well-synchronized. Getting that synchronization working could be essential, and morning bright light therapy is one of the simplest, most accessible tools to try.
