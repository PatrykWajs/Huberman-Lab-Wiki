## 00:00:00 Sense of Self, Interoception

Welcome to Huberman Lab Essentials, where we revisit past episodes for the most potent and actionable science-based tools for mental health, physical health, and performance. Today's topic is our sense of self — what scientists call interoception — which is our sensing of our internal landscape: things like our heartbeat, our breathing, and our gut. Of all the topics one could cover, this sense of self has perhaps the most foundational level of importance for everything we feel, everything we do, and everything we are capable of doing. Learning a little about the mechanisms of self-sensing, of understanding what's going on in your internal milieu, will position you to do some very simple things that can lead to outsized positive effects on everything from sleep to body composition to mental focus, mood, stress regulation, and even your ability to heal and recover from injuries of different kinds.

## 00:01:25 Brain & Body Communication, Vagus Nerve, Mechanical & Chemical Information

We have a system in the body that connects the brain to all of our bodily organs and connects those organs back to the brain — a bidirectional communication that either positions us to do things well or positions us to do things poorly. The system most often associated with this is the 10th cranial nerve, called the vagus nerve. The word vagus comes from the Latin for "vagabond," meaning to wander, and indeed the vagus is a vast, wandering network of nerve fibers — not a single axon but many. These fibers leave the brain stem and send information into the body to control heart rate, breathing, digestion, and even whether the spleen secretes immune killer cells to ward off bacteria. Critically, there are two fundamental types of information that the organs send back up to the brain: mechanical information (whether the gut is full or empty, whether the heart is beating fast or slow) and chemical information (whether the gut's acid-alkaline balance feels right, whether toxins are present in the blood). Your sense of self — feeling good or feeling bad — is dependent on this continuous read of mechanical and chemical signals from every organ except the brain itself, which has no pain or touch receptors and functions purely as a command center.

## 00:04:43 Lungs & Diaphragm, Tool: Breathwork for Alert or Calm

The lungs contain millions of tiny air sacs called alveoli, and the diaphragm — a skeletal muscle sitting below the lungs — is the engine of breathing. Because the diaphragm is skeletal muscle, just like a bicep or quadricep, it can be controlled voluntarily, making breathing one of the few visceral functions under conscious command. When you inhale, the diaphragm drops, the thoracic cavity expands, and the heart gets a little more physical space — it dilates slightly, slowing the rate of blood flow inside it. Neurons on the heart called the sinoatrial node register this change and signal the brain, which sends back an instruction via the vagus nerve to speed the heart up; so inhales actually increase heart rate. On the exhale, the diaphragm rises, the heart is squeezed into a smaller space, blood flows faster, and the brain responds by slowing the heart down. This means you have direct mechanical control over your alertness: emphasizing long or vigorous inhales speeds the heart and raises alertness, while emphasizing long exhales slows the heart and creates calm. The simplest calming protocol is the physiological sigh — two quick inhales through the nose followed by a long exhale — which maximally fills the alveoli with the double inhale and then expels as much carbon dioxide as possible on the slow exhale, rapidly lowering heart rate. Conversely, 25–30 cycles of deep inhale followed by a short exhale will flood the body with adrenaline and produce an alertness comparable to a couple of espressos, all through purely mechanical means.

## 00:09:47 Brain & Gut Communication, Tool: Reduce Sugar Cravings

The gut — stomach, intestines, and esophagus — is part of what can be called the body's series of tubes, and it communicates with the brain through both mechanical pressure and chemical signals. When the gut fills, stretch-sensitive pressure receptors signal the brain to stop eating; when it empties, those same neurons drive seeking behavior, sending you to the refrigerator in a near-manic state. Researcher Steven Liberles at Harvard Medical School identified a category of neurons called GLP1R neurons that extend branches down into the intestines to sense stretch and, via a separate branch traveling up through the neck, signal the brain to either seek more food or stop. Beyond stretch, these same neurons detect actual nutrients — fatty acids, amino acids, and sugars — arriving from digested food, and they fire signals to the brain saying, in effect, "do more of that behavior." Crucially, these neurons care nothing about taste: experiments using gavage (tube feeding that bypasses the mouth entirely) or a numbed mouth show the same nutrient-triggered brain activation. This is why replacing sugar cravings with high-omega-3 or amino acid-rich foods can reduce those cravings — the gut neurons respond to the nutrients, not to sweetness, and once satisfied with fats or proteins they stop driving the sugar-seeking signal. Pausing for 10–20 seconds after eating to consciously attend to the sense of fullness in the gut can help override the mechanical emptiness signals that otherwise drive overeating.

## 00:15:00 Brain, Gut Chemistry, Inflammation & Gut Microbiome, Tool: Fermented Foods

Beyond mechanical signaling, the chemical environment of the gut exerts profound influence on brain function and immune health. The gut needs to maintain a relatively acidic pH — lower on the scale, more acidic — in order to function properly, and gastric juices are powerful modulators of brain state. Within all mucosal-lined tissues of the body live microbiota: microorganisms from our food and environment that colonize us and can be either beneficial or harmful depending on whether the local pH provides the right conditions for good bacteria to thrive. When healthy microbiota dominate, inflammatory cytokines — signaling proteins that can degrade brain and body function — are reduced, and when they are reduced, cognition improves, sleep quality improves, infection resistance improves, and wound healing accelerates. A landmark study by Justin Sonnenberg's lab at Stanford compared a high-fiber diet against a diet supplemented with a few daily servings of fermented foods; the fermented-foods group showed dramatically lower inflammatory markers and better immune function. The clear practical takeaway: eat a variety of fermented foods (yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut, kefir, etc.) on a daily basis to maintain gut microbiome diversity and keep inflammatory cytokine levels in check.

## 00:18:53 Vomiting, Brain; Tool: Reduce Nausea

Vomiting is the gut contents running in reverse, and its trigger mechanism reveals a beautiful and fundamental relationship between blood chemistry and brain. The brain is protected by the blood-brain barrier (BBB) — a selective fence that prevents most substances from reaching neurons, because 99.9999% of neurons do not regenerate and must be protected from damage. However, one small region of the brain stem lacks a full BBB by design: the area postrema, which sits adjacent to the chemoreceptor trigger zone. Neurons in the area postrema act like a crossing guard, sampling blood chemistry directly and firing when they detect pathogens, excess acidity, or other toxins. When activated, they trigger abdominal wall motor reflexes that produce vomiting — a protective mechanism to expel whatever caused the contamination. Even the memory or mental image of nausea-inducing stimuli can activate area postrema neurons because they are sensitized by prior negative experiences. To suppress nausea, the most well-supported intervention is ginger: eleven independent peer-reviewed studies document that 1–3 grams of ginger produces a notable reduction in nausea, likely by modulating area postrema firing threshold. Cannabis — both THC and CBD — has also been shown to reduce nausea through a similar mechanism.

## 00:23:52 Fever, Brain Chemistry, Tool: Cooling Body

Fever is an increase in body temperature orchestrated by the brain in response to pathogens or toxins detected in the bloodstream. Like the vomiting circuit, the fever circuit relies on circumventricular organs — neurons lining the fluid-filled ventricles of the brain that sit outside the full blood-brain barrier. A cluster called the OVLT (organum vasculosum of the lateral terminalis) senses foreign molecules in the cerebrospinal fluid and communicates that information to the preoptic area of the hypothalamus, which then cranks up body temperature to "cook" the invader. This is an elegant adaptive mechanism, but it becomes dangerous above about 102–104°F because neurons do not regenerate — once they are damaged by excess heat they are gone permanently. A common but wrong instinct is to place a cold compress on the back of the neck to cool the brain; doing so cools the blood going to the brain, which causes the preoptic area to compensate by raising temperature further, potentially worsening the situation. The correct approach is to cool the palms of the hands, the soles of the feet, and the upper part of the face — surfaces that have specialized blood vessel structures (arteriovenous anastomoses) that efficiently transfer heat out of the body — while also creating systemic, whole-body cooling rather than targeting just the neck or head.

## 00:28:20 Vagus Nerve, Emotions, Mood, Tool: Heart Awareness

The vagus nerve is often described in popular culture as purely calming, but that is a misconception — it is primarily a communication and motor system, carrying bidirectional signals between body and brain that can be stimulating (as when detecting nutrient-rich food and triggering dopamine release), distressing (as when transmitting nausea), or calming depending on context. What the vagus does aggregate is the combined state of the gut, the heart, and the diaphragm-lungs, and this aggregate signal is what the brain translates into emotion and mood. Emotions are not purely cognitive events; they are generated through the heart's response to a thought, the change in breathing triggered by a relationship event, the chemical state of the gut. The face, including pupil size, skin tonality, and degree of flushing or smiling, is a visible read-out of this internal aggregate — an expression of gut, heart, and lung chemistry. Remarkably, studies show that when we know someone well, our heart rate and breathing begin to unconsciously mirror theirs, meaning our interoceptive sense extends to reading the internal states of others. Interoceptive awareness can be deliberately strengthened through a simple practice: close your eyes, reduce external sensory input, and direct attention to sensing your own heartbeat. Just a minute or two of this periodically trains the vagal connections between body and brain, sharpens the ability to notice when something feels off in a situation or relationship, and builds what was historically called "vagal tone" but is better understood as general interoceptive sensitivity. Pushing and pulling on the levers of this system — breathwork, diet, fermented foods, heartbeat awareness — compounds into meaningful improvements in how well both brain and body function.
