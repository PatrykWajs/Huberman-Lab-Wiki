# Episode 56: Science of Mindsets for Health & Performance | Dr. Alia Crum

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Alia Crum, tenured Associate Professor of Psychology at Stanford and Director of the Stanford Mind & Body Lab, joins Andrew Huberman to discuss how mindsets — the core beliefs and assumptions we hold about stress, food, exercise, sleep, and medicine — measurably alter our physiological responses to those things. The episode covers landmark experiments (the milkshake study, the hotel housekeeper study, the UBS stress study, Navy SEAL mindset research) and delivers concrete frameworks for adopting mindsets that work for rather than against your health and performance. Crum's lab has shown that what you believe about what you're doing shapes the biological outcome of doing it, often as powerfully as the behavior itself.

## Guest: Dr. Alia Crum

**Dr. Alia Crum** is a tenured Associate Professor of Psychology at Stanford University and the founder and Director of the [Stanford Mind & Body Lab](https://mbl.stanford.edu). She earned her PhD in clinical psychology (Yale), completed clinical training in stress and trauma, and previously saw patients before transitioning fully to research. A former Division I ice hockey player and competitive gymnast growing up, she bridges elite athletic experience with rigorous experimental psychology. Her father was a martial artist and teacher of meditation, giving her early exposure to mind-body principles that later became her scientific focus. Her research program spans mindsets about stress, food, exercise, medicine, and side effects — all with the unifying thesis that the total effect of any health behavior is a combined product of the objective input and what you believe about that input.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Mindsets are core belief filters, not just attitudes.** Crum defines mindsets as core beliefs or assumptions about a domain that orient us toward a particular set of expectations, explanations, and goals — they simplify reality and actively shape physiological responses. ([00:08:26](transcript.md#000826-what-is-a-mindset--what-does-it-do))

2. **The milkshake study: belief changes ghrelin response.** Participants who drank an identical ~380-calorie shake but believed it was either "indulgent" (620 cal) or "sensible" (140 cal) showed dramatically different ghrelin curves — the hunger hormone dropped three times more after the "indulgent" label. The objective nutrient content was held constant; the mindset did the physiological work. Counterintuitively, the "sensible" label left participants physiologically hungry — suggesting that a restraint mindset may counteract the objective benefits of dietary restriction. ([00:14:45](transcript.md#001445-mindsets-change-our-biological-responses-to-food))

3. **Placebo vs. nocebo vs. belief effects.** Placebo effects are composed of social context, mindsets/beliefs, and the body's natural physiological processes. Nocebo effects are the mirror image: negative beliefs cause real negative consequences, including priming people to experience side effects they were only told to expect. Crum prefers the term "belief effects" to capture these mechanisms outside the drug-trial context. ([00:25:57](transcript.md#002557-placebo-vs-beliefs-vs-nocebo-effects))

4. **Hotel housekeeper study: mindset about exercise changes weight and blood pressure.** Hotel housekeepers informed that their daily work already exceeded surgeon-general exercise guidelines showed significant reductions in weight, BMI, waist-to-hip ratio, and blood pressure over four weeks compared to a control group — with no change in actual activity. The intervention was purely informational, demonstrating that the brain's recognition of activity as exercise is part of what triggers the physiological benefit. ([00:28:57](transcript.md#002857-mindset-dramatically-impacts-the-effects-of-exercise))

5. **Comparative exercise mindset predicts mortality.** In three nationally representative datasets, how much exercise people believed they got relative to others predicted death rates over 21 years — even after controlling for objective activity levels. In one sample, people who thought they exercised less than peers had a 71% higher risk of death, regardless of actual exercise. Public health guidelines that make people feel perpetually insufficient may thus independently harm health by instilling a negative exercise mindset. ([00:33:44](transcript.md#003344-motivational-messaging--mindset-about-fitness))

6. **Sleep mindset effects: sham feedback alters cognitive performance.** One study gave participants false EEG feedback about their prior night's sleep quality. Participants told they had worse sleep than they actually had showed deficits on cognitive tasks — decoupled from their self-reported actual sleep quality. This suggests mindsets about sleep quality can push around cognitive functioning independently of objective sleep, with direct implications for interpreting wearable sleep scores. ([00:42:03](transcript.md#004203-mindsets-about-sleep-tracking-sleep))

7. **Stress mindset is a paradox — not purely debilitating or purely enhancing.** The research shows stress can narrow focus, speed information processing, and trigger physiological toughening (catabolic hormones activating anabolic ones). Post-traumatic growth literature shows even severe chronic stressors can produce enhanced connection to values and others. People with a stress-is-debilitating mindset default to two maladaptive extremes: freak out (catastrophize) or check out (disengage/denial). Checking out from stress also means checking out from the things you care about — a pathway that often leads to substance abuse. ([00:45:00](transcript.md#004500-making-stress-work-for-or-against-you))

8. **Navy SEALs are the only population to average a stress-enhancing mindset.** In every other group tested, the average mindset about stress falls on the debilitating side of the scale. SEALs in BUD/S training who scored higher on stress-enhancing mindset were more likely to complete training (the program holds a ~15% completion rate regardless of class size), had faster obstacle course times, and were rated more positively by peers. ([00:45:00](transcript.md#004500-making-stress-work-for-or-against-you))

9. **Nine minutes of video shifted physiology at UBS during the 2008 financial crisis.** Employees randomly assigned to watch stress-is-enhancing films for nine minutes over one week reported fewer physical symptoms (backaches, insomnia, racing heart) and rated their work performance higher versus those shown stress-is-debilitating films. The debilitating films did not make people worse because that message was already ambient — it was the enhancing perspective that was new and actionable. ([00:45:00](transcript.md#004500-making-stress-work-for-or-against-you))

10. **Mindsets link conscious thought to subconscious physiology.** Ghrelin, cortisol, DHEA, adrenaline, and testosterone are regulated by brain structures considered subconscious, yet mindset shifts measurably change their levels. Crum frames mindsets as a "portal between conscious and subconscious processes": they operate as default background settings that can be accessed and reprogrammed consciously, then run automatically afterward without requiring moment-to-moment conscious direction. ([01:01:50](transcript.md#010150-mindsets-link-our-conscious--subconscious))

11. **Four sources shape every mindset: upbringing, culture/media, influential others, conscious choice.** Social media influencers and top-grossing movies present nutrition content that would fail UK advertising standards in 70–90% of cases — and the majority of this is not paid advertising but organic content choices. Unhealthy foods are described with language conveying excitement, sexiness, and danger; healthy foods are described with deprivation language (boring, bland, nutritious-but-not-fun). ([01:10:40](transcript.md#011040-4-things-that-shape-mindsets-influencers--mindsets))

12. **Reframing side effects as mechanistic signals improves pediatric allergy treatment.** Children undergoing peanut oral immunotherapy (OIT) who were told their side effects were a sign the treatment was working — mechanistically linked to the body learning to tolerate the allergen — moved through the protocol faster, experienced fewer symptoms at high doses, had reduced anxiety, and showed better immune-marker outcomes. Crum's lab is extending this approach to cancer treatment and COVID-19 vaccine symptom management. ([01:19:40](transcript.md#011940-mindsets-about-medicines--side-effects))

13. **Teaching mindsets: the meta-mindset protocol.** Step 1 — become aware you hold a mindset (beliefs are interpretations, not objective reality). Step 2 — evaluate whether the mindset is helpful or harmful, not whether it is correct. Step 3 — actively seek evidence and experiences that support a more useful mindset. Avoid dogmatism: treat yourself like a scientist, observe which mindsets serve you, and update accordingly. ([01:26:25](transcript.md#012625-how-to-teach-mindsets))

14. **Potency + indulgence framing beats restriction framing for food.** A satisfaction/nourishment mindset produces more appropriate hormonal satiety signaling. Framing food as fueling your immune and endocrine systems — rather than as something to restrict — aligns body and brain toward appropriate satiety. Crum shifted her own eating approach after the milkshake study: eating healthy foods with a mindset of indulgence and enjoyment rather than restraint. ([00:39:30](transcript.md#003930-the-power-of-a-potency--indulgence-mindset))

## Tools & Protocols

### Rethink Stress: Acknowledge → Welcome → Utilize

- **What:** A three-step framework developed by Crum's lab to adopt a stress-enhancing mindset and channel the stress response toward goal-directed action rather than avoidance or overreaction.
- **How:**
  1. **Acknowledge** — Notice and name that you are stressed. Do not suppress or deny it.
  2. **Welcome** — Recognize that the stress exists because something you care about is at stake. Use it as a signal pointing back to your values.
  3. **Utilize** — Direct the energy of the stress response toward the goal that matters. Ask: what can I do here to learn, grow, or act?
- **When:** Any moment of perceived stress — acute or chronic. Works in real time or as a reflective practice. Designed to replace the default freak-out or check-out responses.
- **Ref:** [Rethinking Stress Toolkit — Stanford SPARK](http://sparqtools.org/rethinkingstress/) | [transcript 01:04:50](transcript.md#010450-3-best-ways-to-leverage-stress)

---

### Food Mindset Shift: From Restraint to Potency & Nourishment

- **What:** Replacing a restriction/diet mindset with one that emphasizes the potency, richness, and nourishing function of food to produce more appropriate satiety hormonal responses.
- **How:** When eating, frame what you are eating in terms of what it does for you — fuels immune and endocrine systems, provides building blocks for muscle and neurons — rather than as calories to minimize or indulgence to feel guilty about. For foods you choose to enjoy, allow yourself to experience them as genuinely satisfying. The goal is not to abandon healthy eating but to do so with a mindset of indulgence rather than restraint.
- **When:** At every meal or food decision. Especially relevant when breaking out of chronic restrictive diet cycles.
- **Ref:** [Milkshake study, Crum et al.](https://mbl.stanford.edu/publications) | [transcript 00:39:30](transcript.md#003930-the-power-of-a-potency--indulgence-mindset)

---

### Exercise Mindset Reframe: Recognize What You Already Do

- **What:** Reframing physical activity you perform anyway (walking, household work, daily movement) as genuine exercise — because the psychological recognition of the activity activates the associated physiological benefits.
- **How:** Take stock of all physical activity in your life, not just formal workouts. Understand that the body's response to exercise is partially contingent on whether the brain registers the activity as exercise. Cultivate a sense of "enoughness" rather than chronic insufficiency — the data suggest that perceived exercise inadequacy may independently predict mortality.
- **When:** Applicable to anyone who underestimates their activity levels; especially relevant as a public health framing.
- **Ref:** [Hotel housekeeper study, Crum & Langer 2007](https://mbl.stanford.edu/publications) | [transcript 00:28:57](transcript.md#002857-mindset-dramatically-impacts-the-effects-of-exercise)

---

### Meta-Mindset Protocol: Conscious Mindset Change

- **What:** A structured approach (developed by Crum's lab with Chris Evans) for deliberately identifying and changing an unhelpful mindset across any domain (stress, food, exercise, medicine, etc.).
- **How:**
  1. Become aware that you hold a mindset — your beliefs are interpretations filtered through expectations, not objective reality.
  2. Examine the effects: how is this mindset making you feel, what is it leading you to do, is it helpful or harmful? (Not: is it right or wrong?)
  3. Seek out experiences, information, and framing that support a more useful alternative mindset.
- **When:** Useful as an ongoing reflective practice; most impactful during periods of significant behavioral change or health intervention. Apply without dogmatism — mindset is one piece of the puzzle, not the whole picture.
- **Ref:** [transcript 01:26:25](transcript.md#012625-how-to-teach-mindsets)

---

### Side Effect Reframing for Medical Treatments

- **What:** Informing patients that side effects of a treatment are mechanistic signals of the treatment working — not merely collateral damage — reduces subjective suffering and improves adherence and outcomes.
- **How:** Before or during a treatment with expected side effects, provide a clear explanation of why those side effects occur and how they are evidence the treatment is active. Shift the frame from "endure this" to "this means it's working." This works through the mindset about what the symptom means — not through simple positive expectancy alone.
- **When:** Any medical or behavioral treatment with a significant side effect profile. Particularly studied in pediatric food allergy oral immunotherapy; being extended to cancer treatment and COVID-19 vaccine symptom management.
- **Ref:** [Lauren Howe et al. — OIT mindset study](https://bit.ly/3FT4Irh) | [Crum et al. — mindsets and side effects](https://bit.ly/3AnUkGY) | [transcript 01:19:40](transcript.md#011940-mindsets-about-medicines--side-effects)

---

### Stress Mindset Assessment

- **What:** A validated self-report scale measuring whether an individual's core belief about stress leans toward "stress is enhancing/useful" or "stress is debilitating/harmful." Used in research and as a self-diagnostic.
- **How:** Rate agreement with statements such as "stress enhances my performance and productivity" and "stress heightens my vitality and growth." Available through the Stanford SPARK toolkit.
- **When:** Use as a baseline assessment before a mindset intervention; also useful as an individual self-check.
- **Ref:** [sparqtools.org/rethinkingstress](http://sparqtools.org/rethinkingstress/) | [transcript 00:45:00](transcript.md#004500-making-stress-work-for-or-against-you)
