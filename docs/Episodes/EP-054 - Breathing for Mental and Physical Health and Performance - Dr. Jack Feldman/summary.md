# Episode 54: Breathing for Mental & Physical Health & Performance | Dr. Jack Feldman

> 📄 [View Full Transcript](transcript.md)

## Episode Overview

Dr. Jack Feldman — the neuroscientist who discovered the Pre-Botzinger Complex and the Parafacial Nucleus — joins Andrew Huberman for a deep exploration of the science of respiration. The conversation covers the neural circuits generating breathing rhythms, how breathing directly modulates brain states and emotions through multiple pathways (olfactory, vagal, central, and descending), and the role of physiological sighs in lung health. Dr. Feldman describes landmark research from his own lab showing that slowed breathing for 30 minutes daily dramatically reduces fear responses in mice (comparable in magnitude to pharmacological amygdala manipulation), Kevin Yackle's work connecting the Pre-Botzinger Complex to the locus coeruleus, Gordon Mitchell's research on episodic hypoxia for stroke and spinal cord rehabilitation, and the cognitive-enhancing properties of magnesium L-threonate. The episode concludes with practical breathwork protocols — from box breathing and cyclic hyperventilation to deliberately variable breathwork — that Dr. Feldman himself uses and recommends.

## Guest: Dr. Jack Feldman

**Dr. Jack Feldman** is a Distinguished Professor of Neurobiology at the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA). He is the pioneering neuroscientist who discovered the **Pre-Botzinger Complex** — the brainstem region that generates the inspiratory rhythm — and the **Parafacial Nucleus**, which drives active expiration. His work established the foundational understanding of how the brain controls breathing. He received his early training as a physicist before transitioning into neuroscience.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The Pre-Botzinger Complex drives inspiration; the Parafacial Nucleus drives active expiration.** These two brainstem oscillators are distinct. At rest, expiration is passive (elastic recoil of the lungs). During stress, exercise, or high CO2 demand, the parafacial nucleus activates to force air out via abdominal muscles and internal intercostals. [00:20:11](transcript.md#002011-two-breathing-oscillators-pre-botzinger-complex--parafacial-nucleus)

2. **Mammals are unique in having a diaphragm — enabling an extraordinary alveolar surface area.** Reptiles and amphibians actively expire and passively inspire. The diaphragm moves only two-thirds of an inch downward but expands a ~70 square meter alveolar membrane (about one-third of a tennis court), making it extraordinarily mechanically efficient and enabling the lung branching and surface area that supports large mammalian brains. [00:26:20](transcript.md#002620-how-we-breathe-is-special-compared-to-non-mammals)

3. **Physiological sighs occur automatically every ~5 minutes and are essential for lung health.** Alveoli (400–500 million in humans) slowly collapse from surface tension. A normal breath cannot re-inflate them — only a double-inhale sigh provides sufficient pressure. Bombesin-related peptides released during stress act on Pre-Botzinger neurons to increase sigh rate. Ablating those Bombesin-responsive neurons eliminated sighing in rats and led to severe lung deterioration, ultimately requiring sacrifice. [00:36:23](transcript.md#003623-physiological-sighs-alveoli-re-filling-bombesin)

4. **Slow breathing for 30 minutes/day (10x slower than normal) dramatically reduces fear — without drugs.** Dr. Feldman's lab (with Michael Fanselow) developed a protocol to slow awake mouse breathing by a factor of 10 for 30 min/day over 4 weeks. Those mice froze significantly less to conditioned foot-shock stimuli — a fear reduction comparable in magnitude to major amygdala manipulation, and without any placebo confound. This is distinct from Kevin Yackle's work; the fear-extinction study is from the Feldman/Fanselow collaboration. [01:05:34](transcript.md#010534-meditating-mice-eliminating-fear)

5. **Kevin Yackle's work identified the Pre-Botzinger → Locus Coeruleus pathway linking breathing to arousal.** A subpopulation of Pre-Botzinger neurons project to the locus coeruleus — the brain's norepinephrine "sprinkler system." Ablating those projections (via Lindsey Schwarz in Liqun Luo's lab) made animals calmer with EEG changes indicating reduced arousal. Faster breathing activates this pathway and raises arousal; slower breathing suppresses it. [01:19:00](transcript.md#011900-locus-coeruleus--alertness)

6. **Breathing modulates brain state through at least four distinct pathways.** (1) Olfactory: airflow through the nose creates oscillatory signals in the olfactory bulb that spread throughout the forebrain. (2) Vagal: stretch receptors in the lungs send respiratory-modulated signals up the vagus nerve (vagal stimulation can also relieve refractory depression). (3) CO2/pH: even small shifts in breathing change CO2 and blood pH — chronic low CO2 from hyperventilation links to anxiety; high CO2 can trigger panic attacks. (4) Descending volitional command: deliberate breathing changes send motor cortex signals to Pre-Botzinger with collaterals throughout the brain. [01:19:00](transcript.md#011900-locus-coeruleus--alertness)

7. **Locked-in syndrome reveals separate volitional and emotional pathways for breathing control.** A stroke to the ventral pons eliminates all volitional movement except lateral eye movement, yet patients continue breathing normally and their breathing changes spontaneously when they laugh — demonstrating that emotional control of breathing uses a pathway entirely separate from volitional motor control. Referenced from *The Diving Bell and the Butterfly* (Jean-Dominique Bauby). [01:11:00](transcript.md#011100-brain-states-amygdala-locked-in-syndrome-laughing)

8. **Nasal breathing enhances memory and hippocampal activation vs. mouth breathing.** Journal of Neuroscience studies showed improved memory recall during nasal breathing, with greater hippocampal activity. Mechanistically, airflow through the nose creates respiratory-oscillatory signals in the olfactory bulb that are absent with mouth breathing. Right vs. left nostril breathing may differentially activate brain hemispheres (plausible but lacking controlled data). [01:40:41](transcript.md#014041-nasal-breathing-memory-right-vs-left-nostril)

9. **Nearly every brain and body function is coordinated with the breathing cycle.** Heart rate (respiratory sinus arrhythmia), pupil size, fear response amplitude (Solano paper: fearful faces rated differently during inspiration vs. expiration), reaction time, and cortical oscillations all show respiratory modulation. During inhalation, the brain is more alert and better at encoding information than during exhalation (Noam Sobel's group). [01:44:50](transcript.md#014450-breathing-coordinates-everything-reaction-time-fear-etc)

10. **Episodic hypoxia — repeated brief exposures to 8% oxygen — dramatically increases motor neuron strength and aids rehabilitation.** Gordon Mitchell's research showed that episodic hypoxia (3 min hypoxia / 5 min normal air, repeated) produces lasting elevation of motor strength for hours. A stroke patient showed dramatic ankle extension strength gains post-exposure. CO2 stays relatively stable in true episodic hypoxia, unlike breath holds where both O2 drops and CO2 rises simultaneously. [01:35:22](transcript.md#013522-stroke-muscle-strength-tbi)

11. **Cyclic hyperventilation (Wim Hof / Tummo style) partially mimics episodic hypoxia.** One minute of rapid deep breathing followed by a full-exhale breath-hold of 15–60 seconds, repeated for ~5 minutes, produces heightened alertness, lower stress reactivity, and improved cognitive focus. True episodic hypoxia uses 8% O2 — breathwork approximates but cannot fully replicate this. [01:38:08](transcript.md#013808-cyclic-hyperventilation)

12. **Transitions between breathing states may be as powerful as the specific patterns themselves.** Dr. Feldman's hypothesis: deliberately cycling through different breathing patterns and consciously experiencing transitions may drive neuroplasticity in breathing control. The disruptive effect on entrenched neural circuits — like slowly filling in a well-worn rut — is likely the primary mechanism underlying all sustained breathwork benefits. [02:02:05](transcript.md#020205-deliberately-variable-breathwork-the-feldman-protocol)

13. **Magnesium L-threonate crosses the blood-brain barrier and may reverse age-related cognitive decline.** Developed by Guosong Liu (former Feldman lab student) and chemist Fay Mow, magnesium threonate uses threonate (a vitamin C metabolite) to supercharge the magnesium transporter across gut, blood-brain, and cell membranes. A double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in mild cognitive impairment patients (biological age 51, cognitive age 61 by Spearman's G) showed the placebo group improved 2 cognitive years while the threonate group improved 8 years on average after 3 months. Dr. Feldman uses it personally (at half dose, confirmed by blood testing). [02:06:29](transcript.md#020629-magnesium-threonate--cognition--memory)

14. **One breath can shift brain state; sustained practice shifts it lastingly.** A single physiological sigh or a few slow exhales can transiently calm arousal by disrupting the current neural circuit. Sustained breathwork — 30 min/day over weeks — gradually weakens entrenched circuits (fear loops, depression loops) through repeated disruption before the circuit can reconsolidate, analogous to electroconvulsive shock but milder and self-administered. [01:57:13](transcript.md#015713-dr-feldmans-breathwork-protocols-post-lunch)

## Tools & Protocols

### Physiological Sigh (Double Inhale + Long Exhale)
- **What:** A double inhale through the nose followed by a long, slow exhale through the mouth — the body's built-in mechanism for re-inflating collapsed alveoli and rapidly reducing autonomic arousal.
- **How:** Inhale fully through the nose → immediately sniff again to further expand the chest → exhale slowly and completely through the mouth.
- **When:** Any time rapid stress reduction is needed. One or two repetitions is usually sufficient. Happens automatically every ~5 minutes; can also be triggered deliberately.
- **Ref:** [00:36:23](transcript.md#003623-physiological-sighs-alveoli-re-filling-bombesin)

### Box Breathing (Dr. Feldman's Daily Protocol)
- **What:** Equal-duration inhale, hold, exhale, hold cycle. Dr. Feldman's personally used practice for post-lunch performance recovery or daily calm-focus maintenance.
- **How:** Inhale for 5 seconds → hold for 5 seconds → exhale for 5 seconds → hold for 5 seconds. Can extend to 10-second cycles when comfortable. Use a timer app.
- **When:** Post-lunch performance dip, pre-task focus, or general daily maintenance. Start with 5 minutes; work up to 20 minutes. Dr. Feldman uses 5–10 minutes.
- **Ref:** [01:57:13](transcript.md#015713-dr-feldmans-breathwork-protocols-post-lunch)

### Cyclic Hyperventilation (Wim Hof / Tummo Style)
- **What:** Rapid deep inhales followed by a full-exhale breath-hold, repeated in cycles. Partially mimics episodic hypoxia; produces heightened alertness and reduced stress reactivity.
- **How:** Breathe rapidly and deeply for ~1 minute → exhale fully and hold breath for 15–60 seconds (as long as comfortable) → repeat for ~5 minutes total (~5 cycles).
- **When:** Morning or pre-performance when seeking heightened alertness and autonomic resilience. Avoid near water or while driving. Not recommended during pregnancy or with cardiac conditions.
- **Ref:** [01:38:08](transcript.md#013808-cyclic-hyperventilation)

### Slow Breathing Practice (Fear-Reduction / Emotional Regulation Protocol)
- **What:** Deliberate slow breathing well below spontaneous resting rate, sustained for 30 minutes daily. Modeled on the Feldman/Fanselow lab mouse study that produced amygdala-level fear reduction.
- **How:** Target a rate roughly 10x slower than normal resting rate (from ~15 breaths/min down to ~1–2 breaths/min). Use nasal breathing throughout. Maintain for 30 minutes.
- **When:** Daily breath practice. Long-term use may structurally reduce amygdala-driven fear reactivity and break entrenched emotional circuit loops.
- **Ref:** [01:05:34](transcript.md#010534-meditating-mice-eliminating-fear)

### Deliberately Variable Breathwork (The Feldman Protocol)
- **What:** Intentionally cycling through multiple different breathing patterns within a single session, consciously experiencing the transitions between each state.
- **How:** Cycle through patterns — e.g., box breathing (5 sec) → cyclic hyperventilation (1 min fast + hold) → slow nasal breathing → box breathing at longer durations — attending to the felt transitions between each state.
- **When:** Advanced breathwork sessions. Particularly valuable for developing adaptive breathing control, interoceptive awareness of state transitions, and potentially driving broader neuroplasticity than single-pattern practice.
- **Ref:** [02:02:05](transcript.md#020205-deliberately-variable-breathwork-the-feldman-protocol)

### Nasal Breathing for Memory and Cognitive Consolidation
- **What:** Breathing exclusively through the nose during learning, recall, or any mentally demanding task to maximize olfactory-hippocampal respiratory oscillations.
- **How:** Practice nasal breathing during study, reading, or memory-encoding tasks. Mouth taping during sleep is a complementary practice for maintaining nasal breathing overnight.
- **When:** During and immediately after learning. Supported by Journal of Neuroscience studies showing improved hippocampal activation and memory recall vs. mouth breathing.
- **Ref:** [01:40:41](transcript.md#014041-nasal-breathing-memory-right-vs-left-nostril)

### Magnesium L-Threonate Supplementation
- **What:** A form of magnesium engineered to cross the blood-brain barrier via the threonate transporter, improving synaptic signal-to-noise ratio, LTP, and cognitive function.
- **How:** Typically 1.5–2 g magnesium L-threonate per day (commercial product: Magtein). Dr. Feldman uses half the recommended dose based on blood magnesium testing. ~5% of people experience GI intolerance — stop if this occurs.
- **When:** Daily. Also reported to improve sleep transitions and depth. Consider blood magnesium testing to titrate dose. Sold commercially through various supplement companies under the Magtein brand.
- **Ref:** [02:06:29](transcript.md#020629-magnesium-threonate--cognition--memory)

### Episodic Hypoxia (Experimental / Clinical)
- **What:** Repeated brief exposures to low-oxygen air (8% O2) to upregulate motor neuron output and support neurological rehabilitation after stroke, TBI, or spinal cord injury.
- **How:** In clinical settings: 3 minutes of hypoxic air (8% O2) alternating with 5 minutes of normal air, repeated multiple times. Gordon Mitchell's lab at the University of Florida is the leading research center.
- **When:** Under clinical supervision for stroke, TBI, or SCI rehabilitation. Cyclic hyperventilation breathwork is a partial home approximation (not equivalent — breathwork cannot achieve 8% O2 levels).
- **Ref:** [01:29:40](transcript.md#012940-breath-holds-apnea-episodic-hypoxia-hypercapnia) / [01:35:22](transcript.md#013522-stroke-muscle-strength-tbi)
