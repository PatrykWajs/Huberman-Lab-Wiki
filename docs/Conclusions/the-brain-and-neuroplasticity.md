# The Brain and Neuroplasticity

<!-- topic-intro-added -->
> **Overview**
>
> Neuroplasticity is a fundamental principle in neuroscience that highlights the brain's remarkable capacity to enhance existing neural pathways, establish new connections between neurons, and even produce new neurons through a process known as neurogenesis. This dynamic ability is activated in response to various stimuli and experiences, such as acquiring new knowledge or skills, engaging in novel or challenging activities, or undergoing rehabilitation following a brain injury like a concussion or stroke. Previously, it was believed that significant neuronal plasticity was limited to childhood and adolescence, with the adult brain seen as relatively static. However, current research has demonstrated that the potential for neuroplasticity persists across an individual's lifespan, allowing the brain to adapt and rewire itself at any age.
> 
> While younger brains can readily undergo structural adaptations with minimal engagement, older adults must actively participate in stimulating experiences—such as reading, social interactions, or learning new abilities—to promote neuroplastic changes. This intentional involvement is crucial for harnessing the brain's adaptive capabilities in later life. Understanding these mechanisms not only sheds light on cognitive development and recovery but also underscores the importance of lifelong learning and engagement in activities that challenge the brain, ultimately enhancing mental resilience and overall well-being.
