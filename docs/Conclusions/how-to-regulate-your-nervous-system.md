# How to Regulate Your Nervous System

<!-- topic-intro-added -->
> **Overview**
>
> The nervous system functions as an extensive and ongoing communication network, encompassing both the central nervous system, which includes the brain and spinal cord, and the peripheral nervous system, consisting of all other nerves throughout the body. This intricate system operates through a vast array of neurons, neurotransmitters, and stress hormones, playing a crucial role in regulating our thoughts, emotions, and physical actions. The network is shaped by various external and internal stimuli, including different stressors, which are defined as any demands that challenge the nervous system and require mental or physical resources. Throughout a typical day, individuals encounter numerous stressors, such as navigating traffic, preparing for important meetings, engaging in physical exercise, or interacting with challenging family members.
> 
> Stressors themselves are not inherently positive or negative; rather, their physiological effects are significantly influenced by how we perceive and interpret them. Developing the ability to reinterpret the body's natural stress response in a more constructive manner can be an effective strategy for enhancing resilience to stress. This shift in perspective can lead to numerous health benefits, positively impacting immune function, hormonal balance, mental well-being, and overall health. Understanding these dynamics and how to manage them effectively can play a vital role in daily life, enabling individuals to navigate stress more effectively and improve their overall quality of life.

- Cognitive reappraisal (consciously reinterpreting the meaning of a triggering situation) works upstream of the emotional response and produces less physiological cost than suppression; it is one of the most evidence-backed regulation strategies. — *from [How to Better Regulate Your Emotions - Dr. Marc Brackett](../Episodes/EP-387 - How to Better Regulate Your Emotions - Dr. Marc Brackett/summary.md)*

- The brain and nervous system have two parallel pathways driving sugar consumption: one based on sweet taste perception and another based on the nutritive (glucose-raising) properties of foods, both hardwired into all mammals. — *from [EP-390 - Control Sugar Cravings & Metabolism with Science-Based Tools | Huberman Lab Essentials](../Episodes/EP-390 - Control Sugar Cravings and Metabolism with Science-Based Tools/summary.md)*
- Fructose reduces hormones that suppress ghrelin (hunger hormone), making you feel hungrier regardless of calorie intake; high fructose corn syrup (50%+ fructose) is particularly problematic compared to fruit (1-10% fructose). — *from [EP-390 - Control Sugar Cravings & Metabolism with Science-Based Tools | Huberman Lab Essentials](../Episodes/EP-390 - Control Sugar Cravings and Metabolism with Science-Based Tools/summary.md)*
- Neuropod cells in the gut detect sugar and send signals via the vagus nerve to trigger dopamine release and increase food-seeking behavior, even for foods with "hidden sugars" that don't taste sweet. — *from [EP-390 - Control Sugar Cravings & Metabolism with Science-Based Tools | Huberman Lab Essentials](../Episodes/EP-390 - Control Sugar Cravings and Metabolism with Science-Based Tools/summary.md)*
